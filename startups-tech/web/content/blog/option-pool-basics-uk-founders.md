<p>Before a startup hires its first senior engineer or closes its seed round, investors and advisers often ask about the employee option pool. The questions that land on accountants are rarely about pool size (that is a commercial and dilution decision for investors and legal advisers) but they are frequently about the tax mechanics: what scheme to use, what HMRC needs to know, and what happens if the company grows past the qualifying tests. This guide covers the compliance side only.</p>

<h2>What an option pool is, in compliance terms</h2>

<p>An option pool is a reserved block of shares set aside by the company to grant to employees, directors and sometimes advisers as options. The pool is a cap-table reservation, not a distinct legal entity or account. Shares typically remain unissued until an option is exercised, at which point new shares are issued (diluting existing holders). In the UK, where the company qualifies, the most tax-efficient way to grant from the pool is as <a href="https://www.gov.uk/tax-employee-share-schemes/enterprise-management-incentives-emis">Enterprise Management Incentives (EMI) options</a>, which allow up to £250,000 of unexercised option value per employee and £3m across the company, and must be reported to HMRC via the Employment-Related Securities (ERS) regime.</p>

<p>This page covers tax and compliance only. It does not advise on how large the pool should be as a proportion of equity: that is a commercial and dilution decision to make with your investors, legal advisers and board.</p>

<h2>Sizing the pool: framing only, not investment advice</h2>

<p>Founders typically reserve a percentage of the fully diluted share capital for the option pool before or at a funding round, often at an investor's request. The specific percentage is a negotiated, commercial decision that depends on hiring plans, investor dilution preferences and the stage of the company. This page gives no view on pool size and does not model dilution scenarios as advice. The right percentage to reserve is a matter for your company's legal and financial advisers, not a tax rule.</p>

<p>The same line holds for dilution. When options are exercised, new shares are issued and existing holders are diluted, but how much is a function of pool size, exercise price and timing, all cap-table matters for your board and investors. The tax rules (the EMI limits and ERS reporting) bite on the number and value of options granted, not on the dilution percentage, so staying inside the £250,000-per-employee and £3m-company caps is a compliance job while the dilution maths sits with your legal and financial advisers.</p>

<p>What is a tax and compliance matter is how the reserved pool is subsequently granted to employees, how those grants are valued, and what must be reported to HMRC. Those are the questions this guide addresses.</p>

<h2>Granting from the pool as EMI options</h2>

<p>For qualifying UK companies, EMI is the standard tax-efficient way to grant from the option pool. <a href="https://www.gov.uk/tax-employee-share-schemes/enterprise-management-incentives-emis">HMRC's EMI guidance</a> sets out the tests that must be met at the time of each grant.</p>

<h3>Company-level tests</h3>
<ul>
  <li>Gross assets of <strong>£30m or less</strong> at the date of grant (see the <a href="https://www.gov.uk/hmrc-internal-manuals/employee-tax-advantaged-share-scheme-user-manual/etassum52030">gross-assets test detail</a>).</li>
  <li>Fewer than <strong>250 full-time-equivalent employees</strong>.</li>
  <li>The company must not carry on excluded activities (broadly: financial activities, property development, farming, legal services and certain other trades).</li>
  <li>Total unexercised EMI options across the company must not exceed <strong>£3m</strong> in value.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Employee-level tests</h3>
<ul>
  <li>Each employee or director must meet the <strong>working-time requirement</strong> (broadly, EMI is their main employment or they commit at least 25 hours per week, or 75% of their working time, to the company).</li>
  <li>The unexercised value of that employee's EMI options must not exceed <strong>£250,000</strong> over a rolling three-year window.</li>
</ul>

<p>If any of these tests are breached at or after grant, EMI status can be lost on the options affected. That is a <a href="/blog/share-schemes-and-emi/emi-qualifying-company-rules">qualifying-company rules</a> issue covered in the sibling guide. The <a href="/calculators/emi-vs-unapproved-calculator">EMI vs unapproved calculator</a> lets you model the tax difference between an EMI grant and an unapproved option.</p>

<h2>Valuation and documentation</h2>

<p>EMI option grants must be made at an agreed valuation. HMRC Shares and Assets Valuation (SAV) is the HMRC team that agrees valuations before or at grant. Agreeing the valuation with SAV before granting is standard practice and protects against later disputes about the market value used.</p>

<p>The process and requirements are set out in <a href="https://www.gov.uk/tax-employee-share-schemes/enterprise-management-incentives-emis">HMRC's EMI guidance</a>. The standalone VAL231 form once used to submit valuation requests is no longer live at its old address, so start the valuation through HMRC SAV or the <a href="https://www.gov.uk/guidance/submit-your-employment-related-securities-ers-return">ERS online service</a> instead.</p>

<p>Alongside the valuation, the company should hold:</p>
<ul>
  <li>A board resolution approving the option grant.</li>
  <li>An option agreement signed by the company and the employee.</li>
  <li>A record of the option plan rules under which the grant is made.</li>
</ul>

<p>The <a href="/blog/share-schemes-and-emi/emi-option-valuation">EMI option valuation guide</a> covers the valuation process in detail.</p>

<h2>Reporting the pool to HMRC</h2>

<p>Three reporting obligations apply once a company sets up an option pool using EMI or any other employment-related securities scheme.</p>

<ol>
  <li><strong>Register the scheme.</strong> Before the first grant, the EMI scheme must be registered with HMRC via the <a href="https://www.gov.uk/guidance/submit-your-employment-related-securities-ers-return">Employment Related Securities online service</a>. Registration generates the scheme reference number used in all subsequent returns.</li>
  <li><strong>Notify grants.</strong> Each individual EMI option grant must be notified to HMRC. The deadline for grant notification is <strong>6 July</strong> following the end of the tax year in which the grant was made. From the 2022/23 tax year onwards, grant notification runs through the same ERS return process as the annual return.</li>
  <li><strong>File the annual ERS return.</strong> An annual return must be filed for every registered scheme by <strong>6 July</strong> following the tax year end. This applies even if nothing happened in the year: nil returns are required. Missing the return triggers automatic penalties.</li>
</ol>

<p>All three obligations are governed by <a href="https://www.gov.uk/guidance/submit-your-employment-related-securities-ers-return">HMRC's ERS return guidance</a>. The <a href="/services/emi-scheme-setup">EMI scheme setup service</a> covers registration, documentation and the first return filing.</p>

<h2>When EMI does not fit the pool</h2>

<p>Not every company or every grant will qualify for EMI. The most common reasons are exceeding the gross-asset or headcount tests, carrying on an excluded activity, or an individual employee not meeting the working-time requirement. When EMI is not available, two main fallbacks exist.</p>

<h3>CSOP: the scale-up fallback</h3>

<p>A <a href="https://www.gov.uk/tax-employee-share-schemes/company-share-option-plan">Company Share Option Plan (CSOP)</a> allows up to <strong>£60,000</strong> of options per employee. CSOP has broader company eligibility than EMI (no gross-asset or headcount limit, though excluded-activity and share-class tests apply) and is the standard route for companies that have grown past the EMI thresholds. The tax treatment is less favourable than EMI at exercise, but CSOP remains a tax-advantaged scheme. The <a href="/blog/share-schemes-and-emi/emi-vs-csop">EMI vs CSOP guide</a> covers the comparison.</p>

<h3>Growth shares and unapproved options</h3>

<p>Where neither EMI nor CSOP fits, the company can grant growth shares or unapproved (non-tax-advantaged) options under <a href="https://www.gov.uk/hmrc-internal-manuals/employment-related-securities/ersm30450">general employment-related-securities rules</a>. These are taxed as income (and NIC where relevant) on the value acquired above any amount paid, rather than as a capital gain. They are flexible but carry a heavier tax burden for the employee at exercise. The <a href="/blog/share-schemes-and-emi/growth-shares-explained">growth shares guide</a> covers the mechanics. For companies issuing shares at funding rounds, the <a href="/blog/share-schemes-and-emi/section-431-elections">section 431 election guide</a> addresses the restricted-securities rules that often apply alongside growth share grants.</p>
