<p>A CQC consultant and an accountant do different jobs when you register. The consultant helps you meet the compliance and quality side of the application: policies, statement of purpose, readiness, and interview preparation. The accountant owns the financial leg, principally the <a href="/blog/cqc-and-financial-compliance/cqc-financial-viability-statement-walkthrough">financial viability statement CQC requires on its own template</a>, plus projections, funding evidence, and structure. Many founders use both, because the two roles cover genuinely separate ground (<a href="https://www.cqc.org.uk/guidance-providers/registration">CQC registration guidance</a>; <a href="https://www.cqc.org.uk/guidance-regulation/providers/registration/supporting-documents-provider/document/financial-viability-template">CQC FVS template</a>).</p>

<h2>Two different jobs, one application</h2>

<p>CQC registration is mandatory before any regulated activity begins. Trading before registration is a criminal offence under the Health and Social Care Act 2008. The application is not a single document but a submission with a compliance strand and a financial strand, and the skills required for each are different.</p>

<p>The compliance strand covers your statement of purpose, your policies and procedures, evidence that your registered manager meets the fit-person criteria, and your readiness to meet the fundamental standards. The financial strand covers whether your proposed service is financially viable from day one: opening capital, cash-flow runway to break-even, and a cost base built on current statutory rates.</p>

<p>CQC assesses both strands. A strong compliance submission alongside a weak financial submission is still a failing application, and vice versa. The two sides of the application are separate, and so are the professionals best placed to help with each (<a href="https://www.cqc.org.uk/guidance-providers/registration">CQC registration guidance</a>).</p>

<h2>What a CQC consultant typically does</h2>

<p>CQC consultants are specialists in the compliance and quality side of the registration process. Their core work sits on the non-financial side of the application and covers the practical readiness steps that determine whether CQC is satisfied that the proposed service will operate safely.</p>

<p>Typical consultant support includes:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Drafting or reviewing the statement of purpose, which describes the service type, client group, location and regulated activities</li>
  <li>Developing or auditing the policies and procedures CQC expects to be in place before registration is granted</li>
  <li>Assessing the registered manager's fit-person application and preparing them for the interview with CQC</li>
  <li>Reviewing the overall application for completeness and regulatory consistency before submission</li>
  <li>Advising on which regulated activities to register for and in what sequence</li>
</ul>

<p>None of this work involves producing the financial viability statement or preparing financial projections. A consultant who flags that the FVS is required is doing their job correctly. Preparing it is the accountant's job.</p>

<h2>What the accountant owns: the financial leg</h2>

<p>The accountant's role in a CQC registration is specific and load-bearing. CQC requires new providers to submit a <a href="https://www.cqc.org.uk/guidance-regulation/providers/registration/supporting-documents-provider/document/financial-viability-template">financial viability statement on CQC's own template</a>, and this is normally prepared or signed by an accountant. The detailed walkthrough of what the FVS must demonstrate is covered separately in the <a href="/blog/cqc-and-financial-compliance/cqc-financial-viability-statement-walkthrough">step-by-step FVS guide</a>; the summary here is that the document must prove the proposed service has the financial resources to operate safely and sustainably from opening.</p>

<p>Beyond the FVS itself, the accountant's input during registration typically covers:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Cash-flow projections from launch through to break-even, modelled on realistic occupancy or hours ramp-up</li>
  <li>Funding evidence: confirmed opening capital, committed loan facilities, or investor contributions (indicative terms are not sufficient)</li>
  <li>A cost base that uses current statutory rates, including the National Living Wage of £12.71 per hour for workers aged 21 and over from 1 April 2026, and employer NIC at 15% on earnings above £5,000 per year</li>
  <li>The business structure decision, in particular whether a propco/opco split (one company owns the property, another operates the care service) makes sense, and how the associated-company rules affect the corporation tax rates that apply to each entity</li>
</ul>

<p>On structure: the corporation tax rates are 19% on profits up to £50,000 and 25% above £250,000, with marginal relief between those thresholds. Where a propco/opco structure is used, the associated-company rules reduce the thresholds proportionately, which can accelerate the point at which the group reaches the 25% rate. This is a pre-registration decision with long-term tax consequences (<a href="https://www.gov.uk/corporation-tax-rates">gov.uk corporation tax rates</a>).</p>

<h2>Where the two roles overlap and where they hand off</h2>

<p>The overlap is narrower than many founders expect. The clearest handoff point is the financial viability statement itself: the consultant identifies it as a requirement and the accountant produces it. Beyond that, the two professionals are working largely in parallel on separate parts of the submission.</p>

<p>There is one area where the two roles must be coordinated. The statement of purpose describes the regulated activities and the scale of the proposed service. The financial projections in the FVS must be consistent with that statement: the number of registered places, the types of care provided, and the planned occupancy all feed directly into the revenue and cost model. If the statement of purpose changes late in the process, the FVS may need to be reworked. Early alignment between the consultant and accountant avoids this.</p>

<p>Using both professionals is legitimate and common. It is not a sign that either is doing the other's job; it reflects the genuine two-sided nature of the application.</p>

<h2>Choosing what you actually need</h2>

<p>The right combination depends on the complexity of the application and the applicant's own experience. The financial complexity is the clearest determinant of how much accountancy input is needed.</p>

<p>The financial complexity is the clearest guide. A straightforward single-home application from an experienced operator may need less consultant input than a first-timer. The FVS, however, is always required, and the accountant's role in the financial leg is constant across application types.</p>

<h2>The costs behind the decision</h2>

<p>There are three separate cost categories involved in a CQC registration, and confusing them is a common source of budget surprises.</p>

<p>First, the CQC registration fee. CQC charges a regulated fee for the registration itself. The fee is banded by the scale of the regulated activity, based on registered places or service-user capacity. It is a real, recurring cost. The current fee amounts are published on <a href="https://www.cqc.org.uk/guidance-providers/registration/fees">CQC's fee-scheme page</a>. You can also use the <a href="/calculators/cqc-fee-calculator">CQC fee calculator</a> to work out the banded figure for your proposed service. This fee is paid to CQC and is entirely separate from anything paid to a consultant or accountant.</p>

<p>Second, consultant fees for compliance and quality support. These vary by scope, service type and provider. No figures are given here because pricing is specific to the engagement.</p>

<p>Third, accountant fees for the financial leg. Again, these depend on the complexity of the FVS, the projections required, and any structural advice around the business entity. No figures are given here for the same reason.</p>

<p>All three categories belong in the pre-launch budget. Founders who plan only for the CQC registration fee and discover the professional fees later are carrying an unbudgeted cash call at the worst possible moment.</p>
