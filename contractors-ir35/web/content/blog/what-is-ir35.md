<h2>What IR35 Is and Why It Exists</h2>

<p>IR35 takes its name from the Inland Revenue press release 35, published in March 1999. The legislation, now consolidated in ITEPA 2003 Chapters 8 and 10, targets contractors who work through a limited company or other intermediary but whose actual working relationship with the end client looks like employment. HMRC calls these workers disguised employees.</p>

<p>The concern is straightforward. An employee and a contractor doing identical work for the same client can pay very different amounts of income tax and National Insurance if the contractor routes income through a personal service company. IR35 removes that advantage for arrangements that are, in practice, employment.</p>

<p>Whether the rules apply to you depends not on the wording of your contract alone, but on the actual working relationship between you and your client. HMRC can look past the contract language to the reality on the ground. That is why getting the practical arrangements right, and documenting them, matters as much as what the contract says.</p>

<h2>The Three Core Status Tests</h2>

<p>Employment status case law has produced three tests that carry the most weight in any IR35 determination. Courts and tribunals treat them as the primary indicators, though they are not the only factors.</p>

<h3>Substitution</h3>

<p>Can you send a substitute to do the work if you are unavailable, without the client's approval being required? A genuine right of substitution is one of the strongest indicators of self-employment. If your contract says the client must approve any substitute, or if in practice you have never been able to send one, this right is weakened. For substitution to carry weight, it should be a real option that you have exercised or that the client knows they cannot prevent.</p>

<h3>Control</h3>

<p>Does the client control how you do the work, not just what the end result should be? An employee is typically told when to start, where to sit, which tools to use, and how to approach the task. A contractor is usually told what outcome is needed and left to decide the method, timing and approach. If your client dictates your working hours, requires you to attend their premises during set hours, or directs your day-to-day methods in detail, that points towards employment.</p>

<h3>Mutuality of Obligation</h3>

<p>Is the client obliged to keep offering you work, and are you obliged to accept it? In an employment relationship there is an expectation on both sides. The employer keeps providing work; the employee keeps turning up. A genuine contractor relationship ends when the project ends. There is no expectation that the client will find something else for you to do, and no expectation that you will hang around waiting. If your engagement has rolled over month after month with no clear project scope, this test starts to point towards employment.</p>

<h2>The Off-Payroll Working Rules: Who Decides in 2026</h2>

<p>Before April 2021, every contractor decided their own IR35 status (except for public sector engagements, where the public body took responsibility from April 2017). Since April 2021, the rules changed for medium and large private-sector clients.</p>

<p>Under the current off-payroll working regime (ITEPA 2003 Chapter 10), the determination works like this.</p>

<p>The client (the organisation you are ultimately working for) must produce a Status Determination Statement before the engagement starts. The SDS sets out their conclusion on your status and the reasons for it. They must give a copy to you and, if there is an agency in the chain, to the agency.</p>

<p>If the client is small, applying the small-company threshold test under Companies Act 2006 (two or more of: turnover under 10.2 million, balance sheet under 5.1 million, fewer than 50 employees), the old rules still apply and you as the contractor make the determination. In practice this means many contractors working directly with small technology start-ups or owner-managed businesses still self-assess.</p>

<p>If the client is medium or large, they carry the risk. If they get the determination wrong and fail to deduct the right tax, HMRC can pursue the client rather than you, provided you have not given them false information about your working arrangements.</p>

<h2>What Happens If You Are Inside IR35</h2>

<p>An inside determination means the fee-payer (the agency if there is one, otherwise the client directly) must apply Pay As You Earn to the deemed employment payment. They deduct income tax at the relevant band rates and employee National Insurance at 8 per cent (2026/27 rate) from the gross assignment income, minus a 5 per cent allowance for business expenses which was removed from April 2021.</p>

<p>Your limited company receives the net amount. You draw that as salary. The dividend-plus-salary extraction strategy that makes outside IR35 contracting efficient no longer applies to that income. Your effective marginal rate on assignment income rises significantly, in most cases to 40 per cent or higher once employer National Insurance (15 per cent in 2026/27) is factored into the cost to the fee-payer.</p>

<p>One cost many contractors miss: employer National Insurance is deducted from the assignment income before you see it, not added on top. That means the gross amount your company appears to receive is already reduced by the employer NI before income tax and employee NI are applied. The combined effect on take-home is larger than many contractors expect when they first encounter the numbers.</p>

<h2>Your Right to Challenge an SDS</h2>

<p>If you receive an SDS you disagree with, you have a statutory right to challenge it. The process is set out in ITEPA 2003 section 61T. You must submit your disagreement in writing to the client, setting out why you believe their determination is wrong. They have 45 days to respond with a revised SDS or a written explanation of why they are maintaining their original view.</p>

<p>If the client does not respond within 45 days, they become liable for any unpaid PAYE and National Insurance from the point the response was due. This is one of the few areas where the off-payroll rules actively protect the contractor.</p>

<p>A challenge is worth making if you can point to specific evidence. The most persuasive evidence is substitution in practice (you actually sent a substitute, or your contract records that you offered one), clear project scope that ended when the deliverable was complete, use of your own equipment, and simultaneous work for other clients during the same period.</p>

<h2>CEST: HMRC's Own Status Tool</h2>

<p>HMRC publishes a free online tool called Check Employment Status for Tax (CEST). It asks a series of questions about your engagement and returns a determination. HMRC has said it will stand behind CEST results, provided you answer accurately and the result is not "unable to determine".</p>

<p>CEST has limitations. It does not ask about mutuality of obligation directly, which tax tribunals have criticised. A number of published tribunal cases have reached different conclusions from CEST for the same factual situation. Use CEST as one data point, not as a definitive answer. If CEST says outside but your actual working practices look like employment, you have a problem regardless of the tool output.</p>

<h2>Next Steps</h2>

<p>If you are starting a new engagement, get your contract reviewed by a specialist before signing. A contract review from a firm experienced in IR35 work typically costs between 150 and 400 pounds and will identify clauses that need strengthening. Combine this with IR35 insurance, which covers the cost of an HMRC enquiry and any underpaid tax if a determination goes against you.</p>

<p>If you are already on an inside engagement, check whether the SDS was properly produced and whether the reasons given were adequate. Many early SDS documents issued after April 2021 were blanket determinations with no case-specific reasoning, which does not meet the statutory requirements.</p>
