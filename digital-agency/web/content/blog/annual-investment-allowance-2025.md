<p>If you run a marketing agency, a digital agency, or a creative agency in the UK, you buy equipment. Laptops for your design team. Servers for your development work. Office furniture for your studio in Shoreditch or the Northern Quarter. The Annual Investment Allowance (AIA) is the most straightforward way to get tax relief on those purchases.</p>

<p>For the 2025/26 tax year, the AIA remains at £1 million per year for most qualifying plant and machinery <sup><a href="#ref-1" id="cite-1">[1]</a></sup>. That is a generous limit. Most agencies will never hit it. But knowing how the allowance works, what qualifies, and what the 2025 Budget changed means you can time your spending to reduce your corporation tax bill.</p>

<h2>What is the Annual Investment Allowance?</h2>

<p>The AIA lets you deduct the full cost of qualifying assets from your taxable profits in the year you buy them. Instead of spreading the relief over several years through writing-down allowances, you get 100% relief immediately <sup><a href="#ref-2" id="cite-2">[2]</a></sup>.</p>

<p>For a limited company paying corporation tax at 19% or 25%, that £10,000 server rack saves you £1,900 to £2,500 in tax. For a sole trader paying income tax at 40%, the same purchase saves you £4,000. The maths is simple. The timing matters.</p>

<h2>The £1 million limit in 2025</h2>

<p>The AIA limit has been set at £1 million since 1 January 2019. That figure is confirmed for the 2025/26 tax year and remains in place for the June 2026, December 2026, and June 2027 examination cycles <sup><a href="#ref-3" id="cite-3">[3]</a></sup>.</p>

<p>If your accounting period is shorter than 12 months, the limit is pro-rated. A 9-month period gives you £750,000 of AIA <sup><a href="#ref-1" id="cite-1">[1]</a></sup>. If your business closes, you cannot claim AIA for items bought in the final accounting period <sup><a href="#ref-1" id="cite-1">[1]</a></sup>.</p>

<h2>What qualifies for the AIA?</h2>

<p>The AIA covers most plant and machinery used in your trade <sup><a href="#ref-4" id="cite-4">[4]</a></sup>. For an agency, that typically includes:</p>

<ul>
<li>Computers, laptops, monitors, and tablets</li>
<li>Servers, networking equipment, and data storage</li>
<li>Office furniture, desks, chairs, and shelving</li>
<li>Fixtures such as lighting, heating, and air conditioning</li>
<li>Kitchen and break-room equipment</li>
<li>Tools and equipment used by your team</li>
</ul>

<p>The asset must be used wholly and exclusively for the purposes of your trade <sup><a href="#ref-4" id="cite-4">[4]</a></sup>. If you buy a laptop that you also use for personal browsing, you can only claim relief on the business-use proportion.</p>

<h2>What does NOT qualify?</h2>

<p>Several categories are explicitly excluded from the AIA <sup><a href="#ref-4" id="cite-4">[4]</a></sup>:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Cars</strong>, no AIA on any car, regardless of cost or emissions</li>
<li><strong>Assets used for leasing</strong>, if you buy equipment to lease to others, it does not qualify</li>
<li><strong>Assets provided for the use of a director or employee</strong>, unless the asset is used wholly and exclusively for the trade</li>
<li><strong>Assets bought before your business starts trading</strong>, you cannot claim AIA on pre-trading expenditure</li>
<li><strong>Assets bought in the period your business ceases to trade</strong>, no AIA in the final accounting period</li>
</ul>

<p>If you are unsure whether a specific purchase qualifies, ask your accountant before you buy. Our <a href="/services">ICAEW qualified team</a> can review your planned expenditure and confirm the relief available.</p>

<h2>When is an asset "bought"?</h2>

<p>The date you buy an asset determines which accounting period the AIA claim falls into. HMRC defines the purchase date as <sup><a href="#ref-1" id="cite-1">[1]</a></sup>:</p>

<ul>
<li>The date you sign the contract, if payment is due within less than 4 months</li>
<li>The date payment is due, if it is due more than 4 months after the contract</li>
</ul>

<p>For hire purchase contracts, you can claim AIA on the full cost when you start using the asset, even though you are still making payments <sup><a href="#ref-1" id="cite-1">[1]</a></sup>.</p>

<p>This timing rule is useful for year-end planning. If you sign a contract for £50,000 of new workstations on 31 March 2026, with payment due within 30 days, the AIA claim falls into your 2025/26 accounting period. If you sign the same contract on 2 April 2026, it falls into 2026/27.</p>

<h2>What changed in the 2025 Budget?</h2>

<p>The 2025 Budget introduced two significant changes to capital allowances that affect agency founders <sup><a href="#ref-5" id="cite-5">[5]</a></sup><sup><a href="#ref-6" id="cite-6">[6]</a></sup>:</p>

<h3>Writing-down allowance rate reduction</h3>

<p>The main rate of writing-down allowance for plant and machinery is reducing from 18% to 14%. This change takes effect from 1 April 2026 for corporation tax and 6 April 2026 for income tax <sup><a href="#ref-5" id="cite-5">[5]</a></sup>.</p>

<p>This matters because any expenditure that does not qualify for the AIA, or that exceeds your AIA limit, will be written down at 14% instead of 18% from April 2026. The government expects this change to raise £1.5 billion by 2029-30 <sup><a href="#ref-6" id="cite-6">[6]</a></sup>.</p>

<h3>New 40% first-year allowance</h3>

<p>From 1 January 2026, a new 40% first-year allowance will apply to main rate assets. This covers new expenditure on eligible assets, including most plant and machinery for leasing. It excludes second-hand assets, cars, and assets leased overseas <sup><a href="#ref-5" id="cite-5">[5]</a></sup>.</p>

<p>For most agency founders, the AIA at 100% will remain the better option. The 40% first-year allowance is relevant if you have already used your full £1 million AIA limit, or if you are buying assets that do not qualify for the AIA.</p>

<h3>Electric vehicle charge points and zero-emission cars</h3>

<p>The 100% first-year allowance for zero-emission cars and electric vehicle charge points remains in place until 31 March 2027 for corporation tax and 5 April 2027 for income tax <sup><a href="#ref-5" id="cite-5">[5]</a></sup>. If you are considering electric vans or pool cars for your agency, the relief is generous.</p>

<h2>How to claim the AIA</h2>

<p>You claim the AIA through your annual tax return or company tax return. For a limited company, the claim goes on the CT600 form. For a sole trader or partnership, it goes on the self-assessment return.</p>

<p>You can only claim AIA in the period you bought the item <sup><a href="#ref-1" id="cite-1">[1]</a></sup>. You cannot go back and claim it later. If you miss the claim, you lose the relief for that asset.</p>

<p>Keep detailed records of every qualifying purchase. Your accountant will need the invoice, the contract date, the payment date, and evidence that the asset is used wholly and exclusively for the trade.</p>

<h2>Practical planning for agency founders</h2>

<p>Here is how to use the AIA to reduce your tax bill:</p>

<p><strong>Time your spending.</strong> If your agency has a profitable year and you need new equipment, buy it before your year-end. The AIA relief reduces your taxable profit in that year.</p>

<p><strong>Group purchases.</strong> If you are planning a studio fit-out or a full hardware refresh, do it in one accounting period to maximise the AIA claim. Spreading purchases across two years means you lose the benefit of the full £1 million limit in each year.</p>

<p><strong>Watch the writing-down allowance change.</strong> With the main rate dropping to 14% from April 2026, there is an incentive to bring qualifying expenditure forward into the current year. Every £10,000 of assets you buy before April 2026 attracts 100% AIA relief. After April 2026, anything above your AIA limit is written down at 14% per year instead of 18%.</p>

<p><strong>Consider a holding company structure.</strong> If you own multiple agencies, a holding company can route dividends and manage capital allowances across the group. Our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-structure">incorporation and structure guide</a> covers this in more detail.</p>

<h2>Common mistakes agency founders make</h2>

<p><strong>Claiming AIA on cars.</strong> This is the most common error. No car qualifies for the AIA, regardless of cost or emissions. Use the 100% first-year allowance for zero-emission cars instead.</p>

<p><strong>Claiming AIA on assets used personally.</strong> If you buy a laptop for the business but your teenage son uses it for gaming at weekends, HMRC will challenge the claim. Keep a log of business use.</p>

<p><strong>Missing the timing rule.</strong> The AIA claim is based on the contract date, not the payment date. If you sign a contract in one accounting period but pay in the next, the claim goes in the period you signed.</p>

<p><strong>Forgetting the cessation rule.</strong> If you close your agency, you cannot claim AIA on assets bought in the final accounting period. Plan your equipment purchases accordingly.</p>
