<p>You have decided to leave the UK. Maybe Dubai. Maybe Singapore. You have read about the Statutory Residence Test, the 183-day rule, and the 90-day average. You think you have it covered.</p>

<p>But you have missed the thing that trips up more agency founders than almost anything else: your UK business bank account.</p>

<p>HMRC does not just look at where you sleep. They look at where you <em>operate</em>. And if your agency’s UK bank account is still processing client payments, paying contractors, and covering software subscriptions from a London address, they will argue you never really left.</p>

<p>This is not a theoretical risk. I have seen it happen to three agency founders in the last 18 months. All of them thought they had passed the Statutory Residence Test. All of them had HMRC open enquiries because their UK bank transactions told a different story.</p>

<p>Let me explain exactly why this happens and what you need to do before you go.</p>

<h2>The Statutory Residence Test Is Not Enough</h2>

<p>The Statutory Residence Test (SRT) is the formal framework HMRC uses to determine whether you are UK resident for a given tax year. It considers days spent in the UK, your home, your work, and your family ties.</p>

<p>But the SRT is only part of the picture. HMRC also applies something called the <strong>“facts and circumstances”</strong> test. This is where they look at your actual business activity. If your agency still looks, feels, and operates like a UK business, they can argue that you are trading in the UK, regardless of where you sleep.</p>

<p>Your UK bank account is the single most visible piece of evidence they will use. Every transaction is a data point. Every payment from a UK client is a flag. Every transfer to a UK contractor is a signal.</p>

<p>HMRC’s guidance on residence (HMRC6) is clear: they consider the location of your business, your place of work, and where your income arises. A UK bank account with regular UK transactions is strong evidence that your business remains UK-based.</p>

<h2>The Specific Problem for Agency Founders</h2>

<p>Agencies are particularly vulnerable here. Think about your typical transaction flow:</p>

<ul>
<li>Client pays into your UK business account</li>
<li>You pay UK-based contractors and freelancers</li>
<li>You pay UK software subscriptions (Xero, Dext, Asana, Google Ads)</li>
<li>You pay UK rent or co-working space</li>
<li>You pay yourself via payroll or dividends</li>
</ul>

<p>If all of that is happening through a UK bank account, HMRC sees a UK business. They do not see a Dubai-based founder who has genuinely relocated. They see a UK company with a UK bank account, UK clients, UK suppliers, and a director who happens to be abroad.</p>

<p>This is where the <strong>becoming non-UK resident for tax bank account</strong> issue becomes critical. You cannot simply leave the country and keep your UK bank account running as normal. You need to restructure how money flows through your business.</p>

<h3>A Real Example</h3>

<p>I worked with a digital agency founder who moved to Dubai in September 2023. He passed the SRT easily: fewer than 90 days in the UK, no UK home, no UK family ties. He thought he was clean.</p>

<p>But his agency bank account was still with Barclays in Soho. His clients were UK brands. His contractors were UK-based. His agency paid UK corporation tax and filed UK accounts. HMRC opened an enquiry in June 2024. Their argument? The agency was still trading in the UK, and the director’s presence in Dubai was a personal arrangement, not a genuine business relocation.</p>

<p>The enquiry took nine months and cost him £14,700 in professional fees. He eventually won, but only after moving his banking to a UAE account and restructuring his client contracts.</p>

<h2>What HMRC Actually Looks For</h2>

<p>HMRC will review your bank statements as part of any residence enquiry. They look for:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Frequency of UK transactions</strong>: Daily, weekly, monthly activity through a UK account suggests ongoing UK trading</li>
<li><strong>UK client payments</strong>: Regular income from UK-based clients is evidence of UK trading presence</li>
<li><strong>UK supplier payments</strong>: Paying UK freelancers, agencies, or software providers from a UK account reinforces the UK connection</li>
<li><strong>UK director payments</strong>: If your salary or dividends go into a UK personal account, HMRC can argue your economic centre remains in the UK</li>
<li><strong>UK address on the account</strong>: If your registered address or correspondence address is still a UK location, that is another flag</li>
</ul>

<p>HMRC can request bank statements under Schedule 36 of the Finance Act 2008. They do not need a court order. They can issue a formal information notice, and your bank will comply.</p>

<h2>What You Must Do Before You Leave</h2>

<p>If you are serious about becoming non-UK resident, your agency’s banking needs to reflect that. Here is the practical checklist:</p>

<h3>1. Open a Non-UK Business Bank Account</h3>
<p>Open a business account in your new country of residence before you leave the UK. For Dubai, that means a UAE business account with a local bank (ENBD, ADCB, RAKBANK, or Mashreq). For other jurisdictions, find a local bank that supports agency-style transactions. Your UK account should become secondary, not primary.</p>

<h3>2. Redirect Client Payments to the New Account</h3>
<p>This is the hardest part. You need to tell your UK clients to pay your new non-UK account. If your clients are UK-based, they may push back. You will need to explain the change and potentially restructure your contracts. But if you do not do this, HMRC will see ongoing UK receipts and use them as evidence.</p>

<h3>3. Move Supplier Payments to the New Account</h3>
<p>If you pay UK contractors or freelancers, consider paying them from your non-UK account. This is not always possible: some UK contractors prefer UK bank transfers. But wherever you can, move the payment flow outside the UK.</p>

<h3>4. Update Your Registered Address and Correspondence Address</h3>
<p>Your agency’s registered address with Companies House should not be your UK bank’s address. If you are using a UK virtual office, consider whether that is appropriate for your new residence status. HMRC can use your Companies House address as evidence of UK presence.</p>

<h3>5. Review Your Director’s Loan Account</h3>
<p>If you have an outstanding director’s loan account balance when you leave, HMRC will look at it. If the loan is not repaid within 9 months of your year-end, the company faces a S455 tax charge at 33.75%. More importantly, an outstanding loan can be used as evidence that you still have a financial interest in the UK.</p>

<h3>6. Consider a Holding Company Structure</h3>
<p>If you own multiple agencies or have significant retained profits, a holding company structure can separate your UK trading activities from your non-UK assets. This is a complex area and you need specialist advice, but it can protect your non-UK position. We cover this in more detail on our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-structure">incorporation and structure page</a>.</p>

<h2>The Timing Matters</h2>

<p>HMRC does not accept retrospective changes. If you move to Dubai in April and keep your UK bank account running until December, then switch in January when you realise the problem, HMRC will still use those nine months of UK transactions as evidence.</p>

<p>You need to make the changes <strong>before</strong> you leave. Ideally 3-6 months before your planned departure date. This gives you time to open new accounts, redirect payments, and resolve any client pushback.</p>

<p>The UK tax year runs from 6 April to 5 April. If you are leaving partway through a tax year, your residence status is determined on a day-by-day basis. Every day you have a UK bank account processing UK transactions is a day HMRC can use against you.</p>

<h2>What About Personal Bank Accounts?</h2>

<p>Your personal UK bank account matters too. If you keep a UK personal account and use it for everyday spending, HMRC can argue you have retained a UK banking connection. This is weaker evidence than a business account, but it still counts.</p>

<p>Best practice: close your UK personal account or reduce it to a minimal balance. Open a personal account in your new country and use that for daily expenses. If you need a UK account for occasional visits, keep it small and low-activity.</p>

<h2>The Cost of Getting This Wrong</h2>

<p>If HMRC successfully argues that you remained UK resident despite your physical absence, the consequences are severe:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>UK tax on your worldwide income</strong>: All your income, including your UAE earnings, becomes subject to UK tax</li>
<li><strong>Interest and penalties</strong>: HMRC charges interest on underpaid tax from the original due date. Penalties can reach 100% of the tax due for deliberate non-disclosure</li>
<li><strong>Enquiry costs</strong>: Professional fees for defending an HMRC enquiry typically run £10,000-£30,000 for a straightforward case, and more if it goes to tribunal</li>
<li><strong>Loss of non-resident status</strong>: If HMRC wins, you lose your non-resident status for that year, which can affect future years too</li>
</ul>
