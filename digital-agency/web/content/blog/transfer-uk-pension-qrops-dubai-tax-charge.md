<h2>Why This Question Matters for Agency Founders Moving to Dubai</h2>
<p>You have built a successful agency in the UK. You have a pension pot sitting there, perhaps £150,000 or £250,000, built up over years of dividends and retained profits. Now you are moving to Dubai. You have heard that you can transfer your UK pension to a Qualifying Recognised Overseas Pension Scheme (QROPS) and avoid UK tax. You want to know if you can <strong>transfer UK pension to QROPS Dubai</strong> without a 25% tax charge.</p>
<p>The short answer is: almost certainly not. And attempting it could cost you far more than 25%.</p>
<p>Let me explain why, and what you can actually do instead.</p>

<h2>What Is a QROPS and Why Do People Want One?</h2>
<p>A QROPS is a pension scheme based outside the UK that HMRC has approved for receiving transfers from UK pension schemes. The attraction is straightforward: once your pension is in a QROPS, it sits outside the UK tax system. You can draw it down in a lower-tax jurisdiction, or take it as a lump sum with less UK tax deducted.</p>
<p>For an agency founder moving to Dubai, where there is no income tax, the logic seems obvious. Move the pension to a Dubai-based QROPS, draw it tax-free, and avoid UK inheritance tax on the pot. Who wouldn't want that?</p>
<p>The problem is that the UAE has no QROPS-friendly scheme. None. HMRC does not recognise any scheme based in the UAE as a valid QROPS.</p>

<h2>The 25% Unauthorised Payment Charge Explained</h2>
<p>If you transfer your UK pension to a scheme that is not a genuine QROPS, HMRC treats it as an unauthorised payment. The consequences are severe.</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Unauthorised payment charge: 25%</strong> of the transfer value.</li>
<li><strong>Unauthorised payment surcharge: 15%</strong> if the total unauthorised payments exceed 25% of the fund value.</li>
<li><strong>Scheme sanction charge:</strong> the receiving scheme may also face a charge, which they will likely pass back to you.</li>
</ul>
<p>So a £200,000 pension transferred to a non-QROPS in Dubai could trigger a £40,000 unauthorised payment charge, plus a £30,000 surcharge. That is £70,000 gone before you have touched a penny. And that is before any tax on growth or withdrawals.</p>
<p>Most "Dubai QROPS" schemes marketed online are not recognised by HMRC. They are often set up in jurisdictions like Malta, Guernsey, or the Isle of Man, and then marketed as "Dubai-friendly" because the provider has a Dubai office. But HMRC looks at the scheme's registered location, not the provider's marketing base.</p>

<h2>Why the UAE Has No QROPS-Friendly Scheme</h2>
<p>HMRC sets strict conditions for QROPS status. The scheme must be established in a country that has a Double Taxation Agreement (DTA) with the UK, or meets specific information-sharing requirements. The UAE has a DTA with the UK, but it does not cover pensions in the way HMRC requires.</p>
<p>More fundamentally, the UAE does not have a pension system that mirrors UK pension rules. There is no requirement for schemes to provide benefits broadly equivalent to UK pension schemes. There is no mechanism for HMRC to enforce reporting or recovery of tax charges. So HMRC simply does not recognise any UAE-based scheme as a QROPS.</p>
<p>If a provider tells you they can set up a QROPS in Dubai, ask them for the HMRC reference number of the scheme. Then check it against the HMRC QROPS list published on GOV.UK. If it is not there, the transfer is unauthorised.</p>

<h2>What About Transfers to Malta or Gibraltar-Based Schemes?</h2>
<p>Some providers offer a workaround: transfer your UK pension to a QROPS in Malta or Gibraltar, then move that QROPS to a Dubai-based arrangement. This is where it gets dangerous.</p>
<p>HMRC introduced anti-avoidance rules in 2017 that specifically target these structures. If you transfer to a QROPS and then move it to a non-recognised jurisdiction within five years, the original transfer can be retrospectively treated as unauthorised. The 25% charge applies from the date of the original transfer, plus interest.</p>
<p>So even if you successfully transfer to a Malta QROPS today, and then move it to Dubai in three years, HMRC can come back and hit you with the charge plus penalties. The five-year rule is strict. And HMRC is actively pursuing these cases.</p>

<h2>What Agency Founders Moving to Dubai Should Actually Do</h2>
<p>If you are moving to Dubai and have a UK pension, here is the safe approach.</p>

<h3>Leave the Pension in the UK</h3>
<p>For most agency founders, the simplest and most tax-efficient option is to leave your pension where it is. You can still access it from Dubai. You can take your 25% tax-free lump sum (up to £268,275 under current rules) while you are a non-UK resident. The remaining 75% is taxable in the UK at your marginal rate when you draw it, but if you have no other UK income, you may pay little or no tax on it.</p>
<p>There is no requirement to be a UK resident to hold a UK pension. You can keep it there indefinitely.</p>

<h3>Consider a UK SIPP Instead</h3>
<p>A Self-Invested Personal Pension (SIPP) held with a reputable UK provider gives you flexibility. You can invest in a wide range of assets, including property and commercial property, which might be relevant if you are planning to return to the UK or invest in UK assets. You can manage it from Dubai without any tax issues.</p>
<p>If you later decide to return to the UK, your pension is already in the right place. No transfer fees, no tax charges, no hassle.</p>

<h3>Get Specialist Cross-Border Advice</h3>
<p>If you have a pension pot over £250,000, or if you are considering transferring to a QROPS in any jurisdiction, you need advice from a specialist cross-border pension adviser who is authorised by the Financial Conduct Authority (FCA) in the UK. Do not use a Dubai-based adviser who is not FCA-regulated. The FCA has issued multiple warnings about unauthorised overseas pension transfers.</p>
<p>As ICAEW qualified accountants, we work with agency founders who are moving to Dubai or already based there. We can help you understand the tax implications of your pension choices, but we will always recommend you speak to a regulated pension transfer specialist before making any decision. The cost of getting this wrong is simply too high.</p>

<h2>What If You Have Already Transferred?</h2>
<p>If you have already transferred your UK pension to a scheme that is not a genuine QROPS, you may be able to correct the position. HMRC has a process called "remediation" where you can transfer the funds back to a UK pension scheme and potentially reduce or avoid the unauthorised payment charge. But this is time-sensitive and complex. You need to act quickly.</p>
<p>Contact a specialist pension adviser or your accountant immediately. Do not ignore letters from HMRC about the transfer. They will catch up with you eventually.</p>

<h2>The Bottom Line on Transferring a UK Pension to Dubai</h2>
<p>You cannot <strong>transfer UK pension to QROPS Dubai</strong> without a 25% tax charge because there is no HMRC-recognised QROPS in the UAE. Any provider claiming otherwise is either misinformed or selling you a product that will cost you tens of thousands in penalties.</p>
<p>Leave your pension in the UK. Use a SIPP. Take your 25% tax-free cash when you are a non-resident if it makes sense. And if you have more than £250,000 in your pension, get proper cross-border advice from an FCA-regulated firm.</p>
