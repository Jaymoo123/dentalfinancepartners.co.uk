<h2>Your UK Agency Owns a US Subsidiary. Now What?</h2>
<p>You set up a US subsidiary because your agency needed a stateside presence. Maybe you have US clients who prefer paying in dollars. Maybe you needed a local entity to win contracts in New York or LA. Either way, the subsidiary has generated profits and you want to bring some of that money back to your UK holding company.</p>
<p>The question is simple: what tax do you pay on a dividend from your US subsidiary to your UK agency?</p>
<p>The answer is not simple. But it is knowable. This article walks through the UK tax treatment of a dividend from a US subsidiary of your agency, covering the rates, the reliefs, and the practical steps your accountant needs to handle.</p>
<p>Working exclusively with agency founders, we deal with these cross-border structures regularly. Here is what you need to understand before you declare that dividend.</p>

<h2>The Core Principle: UK Corporation Tax Applies</h2>
<p>When your UK agency receives a dividend from its US subsidiary, that dividend is not automatically tax-free. UK corporation tax applies to the dividend income of your UK company, regardless of where the dividend originates.</p>
<p>The rate depends on your total profits. For the 2025/26 tax year:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Small profits rate:</strong> 19% on profits up to £50,000</li>
<li><strong>Main rate:</strong> 25% on profits above £250,000</li>
<li><strong>Marginal relief:</strong> applies between £50,000 and £250,000, giving an effective rate between 19% and 25%</li>
</ul>
<p>So if your UK agency has profits of £200,000 and receives a £50,000 dividend from the US subsidiary, your total profits become £250,000. The corporation tax on that combined figure will be calculated at the marginal rate, not the small profits rate.</p>
<p>That is the starting point. But it is rarely the final position, because the US has already taken its own tax on that dividend before it reaches you.</p>

<h2>US Withholding Tax on Dividends</h2>
<p>When a US subsidiary pays a dividend to a foreign parent company, the US imposes a withholding tax. The standard rate under US domestic law is 30%.</p>
<p>However, the UK-US Double Taxation Treaty reduces that rate significantly. For a UK company that owns at least 10% of the voting stock of the US subsidiary, the withholding rate drops to 5%. If your UK agency owns 80% or more of the US subsidiary, and certain conditions are met, the rate can drop to 0%.</p>
<p>In practice, most agency founders we work with hold 100% of their US subsidiary. That means the 0% rate is achievable, but only if you meet the treaty conditions. These include:</p>
<ul>
<li>The UK company is the beneficial owner of the dividend</li>
<li>The UK company is resident in the UK for tax purposes</li>
<li>The UK company is not a pass-through entity</li>
<li>The US subsidiary has not claimed certain tax benefits that trigger limitation on benefits clauses</li>
</ul>
<p>To claim the reduced rate, your US subsidiary must file IRS Form W-8BEN-E with the paying agent before the dividend is paid. If you miss this step, the US will withhold at 30%, and you will need to file a claim for refund with the IRS. That process takes months.</p>

<h2>Double Taxation Relief: How It Works</h2>
<p>Here is where the UK tax treatment of a dividend from a US subsidiary of your agency gets interesting.</p>
<p>The UK gives relief for foreign tax paid on dividends through two mechanisms: <strong>unilateral relief</strong> and <strong>treaty relief</strong>. In practice, you use the Foreign Tax Credit (FTC) system.</p>
<p>Suppose the US withholds 5% on a $100,000 dividend. You receive $95,000. The US keeps $5,000. When you report the dividend on your UK corporation tax return, you declare the gross dividend of $100,000 (converted to sterling at the spot rate on the date of receipt). You then claim a credit for the $5,000 US tax paid.</p>
<p>The credit is capped at the UK corporation tax due on that foreign income. If your UK corporation tax rate is 25%, the maximum credit you can claim is 25% of the gross dividend. If the US withholding rate is 5%, you can fully offset that 5% against your UK liability. You pay the difference: 20% to HMRC.</p>
<p>If the US withholding rate were higher than the UK rate (unlikely in your situation, but possible in other jurisdictions), the excess credit cannot be refunded. It can only be carried forward or back, subject to strict rules.</p>

<h3>Worked Example</h3>
<p>Let us run a real example. Your UK agency holds 100% of a Delaware LLC that has elected to be taxed as a C corporation (the standard structure for agency subsidiaries). The US subsidiary makes a profit of $200,000 and pays US federal corporation tax at 21% plus state tax. It declares a dividend of $150,000 to your UK parent.</p>
<p>You file Form W-8BEN-E before payment, so the US withholds at 0% under the treaty. You receive $150,000. Converted at $1.25/£, that is £120,000.</p>
<p>Your UK agency has other profits of £180,000. Total profits: £300,000. Corporation tax at 25% on the full amount: £75,000. But you claim a foreign tax credit of $0 (because no US tax was withheld). Your UK tax on the dividend is £30,000 (25% of £120,000).</p>
<p>Now suppose you had not filed the W-8BEN-E. The US withholds at 30%. You receive $105,000. Converted: £84,000. You declare gross dividend of £120,000 and claim a credit for the $45,000 US tax (£36,000). The credit is capped at the UK tax on that income: £30,000. You can only claim £30,000 credit. The remaining £6,000 of US tax is lost. You pay £0 UK tax on the dividend, but you lost £6,000 of tax credit permanently.</p>
<p>Filing the paperwork matters.</p>

<h2>Reporting Requirements: What Goes on Your Tax Return</h2>
<p>The UK corporation tax return (CT600) includes supplementary pages for foreign income. You will need to complete:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>CT600 main return:</strong> include the dividend in the "other income" section</li>
<li><strong>CT600J:</strong> the supplementary page for foreign tax credits</li>
<li><strong>Computations:</strong> your accountant will prepare a detailed computation showing the gross dividend, exchange rate used, foreign tax paid, and credit claimed</li>
</ul>
<p>You also need to consider the <strong>controlled foreign company (CFC) rules</strong>. If your US subsidiary earns passive income (interest, royalties, dividends from third parties) rather than active trading income, HMRC may charge a CFC charge on your UK company. For most agency subsidiaries earning fee income from clients, this is not an issue. But if your US subsidiary holds cash balances that generate interest, or if it licenses intellectual property, the CFC rules need a review.</p>

<h2>Exchange Rate: A Common Trap</h2>
<p>The dividend is declared in US dollars. You must convert it to sterling at the spot rate on the date the dividend is received, not the date it is declared. HMRC publishes monthly exchange rates, but you can use the actual spot rate from your bank or XE on the receipt date.</p>
<p>If the exchange rate moves between declaration and receipt, the sterling value changes. Your accountant needs to use the correct date. Using the wrong rate can trigger a HMRC enquiry.</p>

<h2>Structuring the US Subsidiary: LLC vs C Corporation</h2>
<p>Most UK agencies set up their US subsidiary as a Delaware LLC. But an LLC is a pass-through entity for US tax purposes unless it elects to be taxed as a C corporation. If your LLC does not make that election, the US treats the profits as flowing directly to your UK company, and the dividend treatment changes entirely.</p>
<p>For the dividend treatment described in this article to apply, your US entity must be treated as a C corporation for US tax purposes. If it is a transparent entity (disregarded entity or partnership), the US income is taxed differently, and the UK tax treatment also differs.</p>
<p>If you are unsure which structure your US subsidiary uses, ask your US tax adviser. The UK tax treatment of a dividend from a US subsidiary of your agency depends entirely on the US classification of that subsidiary.</p>

<h2>Practical Steps Before Declaring the Dividend</h2>
<p>Before your US subsidiary declares a dividend to your UK parent, do these four things:</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Confirm the US subsidiary's tax classification.</strong> Is it a C corporation or a pass-through? If it is a pass-through, do not declare a dividend. Restructure first.</li>
<li><strong>File Form W-8BEN-E.</strong> Your US subsidiary gives this to its bank or paying agent. It claims the 0% or 5% treaty rate. Do this before the dividend payment date.</li>
<li><strong>Check your UK agency's profit level.</strong> If the dividend pushes you into the marginal relief band or the main rate, the effective tax rate changes. Model the numbers before you declare.</li>
<li><strong>Document the exchange rate.</strong> Note the spot rate on the receipt date. Save the bank statement or XE confirmation.</li>
</ol>

<h2>When to Speak to Your Accountant</h2>
<p>If you are reading this and thinking "my US subsidiary is an LLC and I have not elected C corporation status," stop. Do not declare a dividend. Speak to your accountant first.</p>
<p>If your US subsidiary has been operating for more than a year and you have not filed UK corporation tax returns reflecting the US income, you may have a disclosure obligation. HMRC's worldwide debt rules require you to report all foreign income, even if it is retained in the US subsidiary and not distributed.</p>
