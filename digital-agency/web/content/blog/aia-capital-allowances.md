<h2>What Is the Annual Investment Allowance (AIA)?</h2>

<p>The Annual Investment Allowance (AIA) is a tax relief that lets your agency deduct the full cost of qualifying plant and machinery from your profits before you pay tax <sup><a href="#ref-1" id="cite-1">[1]</a></sup>. In plain English: if you buy a piece of equipment for your agency, you can write off the entire cost in the same tax year, rather than spreading the relief over several years.</p>

<p>For most agency founders, this is the single most valuable capital allowance available. The current AIA limit is £1 million per year <sup><a href="#ref-1" id="cite-1">[1]</a></sup>. That means a 12-person digital agency billing £800k per year could buy £50,000 of new laptops, monitors, and office furniture and claim the full £50,000 as a tax deduction in the same period.</p>

<p>The AIA is effectively a 100% first-year allowance for business expenditure on qualifying plant or machinery <sup><a href="#ref-2" id="cite-2">[2]</a></sup>. It applies to sole traders, partnerships, and limited companies <sup><a href="#ref-1" id="cite-1">[1]</a></sup>.</p>

<h2>How Much Can You Claim Under the AIA?</h2>

<p>The AIA limit is £1 million per 12-month accounting period <sup><a href="#ref-1" id="cite-1">[1]</a></sup>. This limit has been permanent since the Growth Plan 2022 confirmed it would not revert to £200,000 <sup><a href="#ref-3" id="cite-3">[3]</a></sup>.</p>

<p>If your accounting period is shorter than 12 months, the AIA is pro-rated. For example, if your agency has a 9-month accounting period, your AIA limit would be 9/12 x £1,000,000 = £750,000 <sup><a href="#ref-1" id="cite-1">[1]</a></sup>.</p>

<p>Between 2008 and 2022, there were seven different AIA limits, with some decreases as well as increases <sup><a href="#ref-3" id="cite-3">[3]</a></sup>. The current £1 million level is now permanent, which gives agency founders certainty when planning capital expenditure <sup><a href="#ref-3" id="cite-3">[3]</a></sup>.</p>

<p>For the great majority of UK businesses, the £1 million limit will significantly exceed their annual expenditure on plant and machinery, meaning they can claim full tax relief up front <sup><a href="#ref-3" id="cite-3">[3]</a></sup>.</p>

<h2>What Qualifies for AIA Capital Allowances?</h2>

<p>Qualifying plant and machinery includes most tangible assets your agency uses in its trade <sup><a href="#ref-2" id="cite-2">[2]</a></sup>. Common examples for agency founders include:</p>

<ul>
<li>Computers, laptops, and monitors</li>
<li>Servers and networking equipment</li>
<li>Office furniture and desks</li>
<li>Fixtures and fittings (shelving, lighting, air conditioning)</li>
<li>Motorcycles, vans, lorries, and trucks used for business</li>
<li>Tools and equipment used by your team</li>
</ul>

<p>Motorcycles, vans, lorries, and trucks are not considered cars, so they can be included in the AIA <sup><a href="#ref-2" id="cite-2">[2]</a></sup>. This is useful if your agency runs a delivery service or has field-based staff.</p>

<h3>What Does NOT Qualify?</h3>

<p>AIA is not available on <sup><a href="#ref-2" id="cite-2">[2]</a></sup>:</p>

<ul>
<li>Assets not used immediately in the trade</li>
<li>Business cars (passenger cars are excluded)</li>
<li>Assets bought in the chargeable period when the qualifying activity is permanently discontinued</li>
<li>Transactions with a connected person</li>
</ul>

<p>If you buy a car for your agency, you cannot claim AIA on it. Instead, you claim capital allowances at the standard writing-down allowance rates (18% or 6% per year depending on CO2 emissions).</p>

<h2>When Can You Claim the AIA?</h2>

<p>You can only claim AIA in the period you bought the item <sup><a href="#ref-1" id="cite-1">[1]</a></sup>. The date you bought it is when you signed the contract, if payment is due within less than 4 months <sup><a href="#ref-1" id="cite-1">[1]</a></sup>.</p>

<p>This timing rule matters. If you sign a contract for £30,000 of new equipment on 31 March but don't take delivery until 10 April, the AIA claim belongs to the earlier tax year. Plan your purchases accordingly.</p>

<p>For agency founders using the cash basis of accounting (sole traders and partnerships only), you can only claim capital allowances on business cars <sup><a href="#ref-4" id="cite-4">[4]</a></sup>. If you use accruals accounting, you claim on all qualifying assets.</p>

<h2>How to Claim AIA Capital Allowances</h2>

<p>Claiming AIA is straightforward. You include the qualifying expenditure in your capital allowances computation when completing your tax return or company tax return <sup><a href="#ref-4" id="cite-4">[4]</a></sup>.</p>

<p>For limited companies, the claim goes on the CT600 company tax return. For sole traders and partnerships, it goes on the self-assessment return (SA100 or partnership return).</p>

<p>You need to keep records of the purchase, including invoices, contracts, and proof of payment. Your accountant will need these to prepare the computation.</p>

<p>If you use accounting software like Xero, QuickBooks, or FreeAgent, your accountant can tag qualifying purchases and run the capital allowances calculation from your fixed asset register.</p>

<h2>AIA and Other Capital Allowances</h2>

<p>You can claim first year allowances in addition to the AIA; they do not count towards your AIA limit <sup><a href="#ref-2" id="cite-2">[2]</a></sup>. This means if you buy assets that qualify for a separate first-year allowance (like certain energy-saving equipment), you can claim both.</p>

<p>Full expensing and 50% first-year allowance can be claimed on qualifying plant and machinery investments from 1 April 2023 <sup><a href="#ref-4" id="cite-4">[4]</a></sup>. Full expensing gives 100% relief on main pool assets, similar to AIA but with no cap. However, full expensing only applies to companies, not sole traders or partnerships.</p>

<p>From 1 January 2026, a 40% first-year allowance can be claimed for qualifying plant and machinery purchased after that date <sup><a href="#ref-4" id="cite-4">[4]</a></sup>. This is a new relief to watch for if you are planning significant capital expenditure.</p>

<h2>Special Rules for Partnerships</h2>

<p>If your agency operates as a partnership, HMRC has clarified that the AIA is available to each individual partner, not the partnership as a whole <sup><a href="#ref-5" id="cite-5">[5]</a></sup>. This means each partner can claim up to £1 million of AIA on their share of qualifying expenditure.</p>

<p>This is a significant advantage for larger partnerships. If you have three partners and the partnership buys £2.5 million of qualifying assets, each partner can claim their share of the expenditure against their own AIA limit.</p>

<h2>Practical Examples for Agency Founders</h2>

<h3>Example 1: Small Creative Agency</h3>
<p>A 5-person creative agency in Bristol Harbourside turns over £350k per year. They spend £18,000 on new iMacs, £4,000 on a server, and £6,000 on office furniture. Total qualifying spend: £28,000. They claim the full £28,000 as AIA, reducing their taxable profit by £28,000. At 19% corporation tax, that saves them £5,320.</p>

<h3>Example 2: Growing Digital Agency</h3>
<p>A 20-person digital agency in Manchester Northern Quarter turns over £1.8m. They invest £65,000 in new laptops, monitors, and a network upgrade. They claim the full £65,000 under AIA. At 25% corporation tax (main rate), the tax saving is £16,250.</p>

<h3>Example 3: Sole Trader Web Designer</h3>
<p>A sole trader web designer turning over £65k buys a new laptop for £2,400 and a monitor for £600. Total: £3,000. They claim the full amount under AIA on their self-assessment return, reducing their taxable profit by £3,000. At 20% basic rate tax, they save £600.</p>

<h2>Common Mistakes to Avoid</h2>

<p>One common error is claiming AIA on assets that are not used immediately in the trade <sup><a href="#ref-2" id="cite-2">[2]</a></sup>. If you buy equipment and store it for six months before using it, HMRC may reject the claim.</p>

<p>Another mistake is forgetting to claim AIA on assets bought in the final months of your accounting period. The timing rule means you claim in the period you signed the contract, not when you paid or took delivery <sup><a href="#ref-1" id="cite-1">[1]</a></sup>.</p>

<p>If you are a sole trader or partnership using cash basis, remember you can only claim capital allowances on business cars <sup><a href="#ref-4" id="cite-4">[4]</a></sup>. All other assets are expensed through the cash basis instead.</p>

<p>Finally, do not assume the AIA applies automatically. You must actively claim it on your tax return. If you miss it, you can amend the return within 12 months of the filing deadline.</p>

<h2>Should You Plan Capital Expenditure Around the AIA?</h2>

<p>Yes, but with caveats. The £1 million limit is generous enough that most agencies will never hit it. A report by the Office of Tax Simplification in 2018 found that 97% of UK businesses did not exceed the permanent AIA level of £200,000 <sup><a href="#ref-3" id="cite-3">[3]</a></sup>. With the limit now £1 million, that figure is even higher.</p>

<p>However, timing still matters. If you are planning a major equipment purchase, consider whether it makes sense to bring it forward or delay it to align with your accounting period. The AIA is a use-it-or-lose-it relief in each period.</p>

<p>If your agency is approaching an exit, capital allowances planning becomes more important. Assets bought before a sale can reduce taxable profits in the final trading period. Speak to your accountant about the interaction between AIA and <a href="/blog/growth-and-exit">exit planning strategies</a>.</p>
