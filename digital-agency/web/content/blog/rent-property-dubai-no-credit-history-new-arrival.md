<h2>Why Your UK Credit History Does Not Count in Dubai</h2>

<p>If you have just moved to Dubai from the UK, you will quickly discover that your Experian or Equifax credit file means nothing here. Dubai has its own credit bureau, Al Etihad Credit Bureau (AECB), and it starts from zero the moment you arrive.</p>

<p>That creates a practical problem. Landlords and real estate agents in Dubai use your credit history to assess whether you will pay rent on time. Without a local file, you are an unknown quantity. And in a market where rents have risen sharply over the last two years, landlords are not taking risks.</p>

<p>The result? Most new arrivals cannot <strong>rent property Dubai no credit history</strong> without offering something substantial upfront. That something is usually a full year's rent in advance.</p>

<h2>The Standard Solution: One Cheque for 12 Months</h2>

<p>In the UK, you pay rent monthly. In Dubai, the default expectation for new arrivals without a credit history is a single post-dated cheque (or bank transfer) covering the entire 12-month tenancy.</p>

<p>Let me give you a real example. A 12-person digital agency billing £800k per year might relocate a senior director to Dubai. That director needs a two-bedroom apartment in Dubai Marina. Annual rent: AED 180,000 (roughly £38,000 at current exchange rates). The landlord asks for one cheque upfront. That is AED 180,000 leaving your bank account before you have unpacked a single box.</p>

<p>This is not unusual. It is the standard arrangement for anyone who cannot show a local credit history or a strong UAE salary certificate.</p>

<h3>What About Post-Dated Cheques?</h3>

<p>Some landlords accept 4, 6, or even 12 post-dated cheques spread across the year. But that concession is typically reserved for tenants with an established UAE credit file or a salary transfer letter from a recognised local employer. As a new arrival, you are unlikely to get that flexibility in your first tenancy.</p>

<p>If you can negotiate, aim for 4 cheques (quarterly payments). That gives you some cash flow breathing room. But be prepared to hear "no" and fall back to the single cheque option.</p>

<h2>Understanding the Ejari System</h2>

<p>Ejari is Arabic for "my rent". It is the government-run system that registers all tenancy contracts in Dubai. Every rental agreement must be registered with Ejari through the Real Estate Regulatory Authority (RERA). Without an Ejari certificate, your tenancy is not legally recognised.</p>

<p>Here is what that means for you practically:</p>
<ul>
<li>You cannot connect utilities (DEWA) without an Ejari registration.</li>
<li>You cannot get a tenancy visa (which many employers require for residency) without it.</li>
<li>You cannot register your tenancy dispute if something goes wrong.</li>
</ul>

<p>The agent or landlord typically handles the Ejari registration. The cost is around AED 220 plus a knowledge and innovation fee (AED 10). Some agents include this in their service fee. Others charge it separately. Ask before you sign.</p>

<h2>Agency Fees: What You Will Actually Pay</h2>

<p>Dubai real estate agents charge a commission, usually 5% of the annual rent. That is standard and non-negotiable in most cases. But there are other fees that catch new arrivals out.</p>

<p>Here is a full breakdown for that AED 180,000 apartment:</p>
<ul>
<li>Annual rent: AED 180,000</li>
<li>Agent commission (5%): AED 9,000</li>
<li>Security deposit (typically 5%): AED 9,000 (refundable at end of tenancy, minus any damage deductions)</li>
<li>DEWA connection fee: AED 550</li>
<li>Ejari registration fee: AED 230</li>
<li>Housing fee (charged by DEWA, 5% of annual rent on your bill): AED 9,000 spread monthly</li>
</ul>

<p>Total upfront cash needed before you move in: roughly AED 198,780 (around £42,000). That is a significant cash requirement. Plan for it.</p>

<h2>Can You Avoid Paying a Full Year Upfront?</h2>

<p>There are a few routes, but they are not available to everyone.</p>

<h3>Corporate Leases</h3>
<p>If your agency is relocating you, ask whether they will sign a corporate lease. Many companies in Dubai rent properties directly from landlords for their senior staff. The landlord sees a blue-chip company as the tenant, not an individual with no credit history. That changes the risk calculation completely. You might get monthly payments or a much lower upfront commitment.</p>

<h3>Salary Certificate from a UAE Employer</h3>
<p>Once you have a UAE employment contract and a few months of salary transfers into a local bank account, you can approach your bank for a tenancy contract letter. Some banks, like Emirates NBD and ADCB, offer tenancy finance or will issue a guarantee to the landlord. This effectively replaces the need for a full year upfront. But you need 3-6 months of salary history first.</p>

<h3>Guarantor Arrangements</h3>
<p>A UAE-based guarantor with a strong credit history can sometimes help. But this is rare in Dubai's rental market. Most landlords simply prefer the cash upfront.</p>

<h2>How to Budget as a New Arrival</h2>

<p>If you are moving your agency or yourself to Dubai, here is a realistic budget for the first 12 months:</p>
<ul>
<li>Rent: 12 months upfront (AED 180,000 in our example)</li>
<li>Agent fee: 5% of annual rent</li>
<li>Security deposit: 5% of annual rent</li>
<li>Utilities connection: AED 550</li>
<li>Furniture and appliances: AED 30,000-60,000 depending on standard (many apartments come unfurnished or semi-furnished)</li>
<li>Visa and medical: AED 5,000-7,000 for your residency visa and health insurance</li>
<li>Car purchase or lease: AED 30,000+ for a reasonable used car (Dubai is not walkable)</li>
</ul>

<p>Total first-year cash requirement: roughly AED 250,000-300,000 (£53,000-£64,000). That is before you spend a dirham on food, school fees, or entertainment.</p>

<h2>Practical Steps to Secure a Tenancy</h2>

<p>Here is the sequence that works for most new arrivals:</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Get your residency visa sorted first.</strong> You cannot sign a tenancy contract without a valid UAE residence visa. Your employer or a PRO (Public Relations Officer) service handles this.</li>
<li><strong>Open a UAE bank account.</strong> You need a local account to issue cheques or transfer rent. Most banks require your residence visa and Emirates ID to open an account. Some, like Mashreq Neo, offer digital onboarding for new arrivals.</li>
<li><strong>Find a property through a RERA-registered agent.</strong> Check the agent's RERA number on the Dubai REST app. Do not deal with unregistered brokers.</li>
<li><strong>View the property in person.</strong> Photos in Dubai listings are often heavily edited. See the actual unit, check for maintenance issues, and ask about chiller fees (air conditioning charges) which can add AED 1,000-2,000 per month in summer.</li>
<li><strong>Negotiate the payment plan.</strong> Ask for 4 cheques. If the landlord says no, ask for 2 cheques (6 months each). Every concession reduces your upfront cash requirement.</li>
<li><strong>Sign the tenancy contract and register Ejari.</strong> The agent should do this within 48 hours. Do not hand over the full rent until Ejari is registered.</li>
<li><strong>Connect DEWA.</strong> You need your Ejari certificate, Emirates ID, and passport copy. DEWA connection usually happens within 24 hours.</li>
</ol>

<h2>What Happens After Year One?</h2>

<p>Once you have a full year of rent payments on your AECB credit file, you are in a different position. At renewal, you can negotiate monthly payments. Many tenants switch to 12 post-dated cheques after the first year. Your credit score improves with every on-time payment, making future rentals easier.</p>

<p>If you are running an agency from Dubai, this matters. Your ability to secure property without tying up huge cash reserves improves significantly after 12 months. Plan your cash flow accordingly in year one, and you will have more flexibility in year two.</p>
