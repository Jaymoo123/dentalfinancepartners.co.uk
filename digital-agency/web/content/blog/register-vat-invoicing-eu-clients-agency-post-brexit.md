<p>If your UK agency invoices clients in the European Union, the VAT rules changed on 1 January 2021 and they haven't gone back. You cannot simply treat EU invoices the same way you treat UK invoices. Get it wrong and you could undercharge VAT, face HMRC penalties, or leave your client unable to reclaim the VAT you charged them.</p>

<p>This is a practical guide for agency founders who need to know exactly what to do. Not a textbook on cross-border VAT. Just the steps, the rules, and the traps to avoid.</p>

<h2>Do You Need to Register for VAT to Invoice EU Clients?</h2>

<p>Short answer: it depends on where your services are "supplied" for VAT purposes, and whether your UK turnover has crossed the £90,000 threshold.</p>

<p>If your UK taxable turnover (from UK clients, EU clients, and rest of world) exceeds £90,000 in any rolling 12-month period, you <strong>must</strong> register for VAT regardless of where your clients are based. That is compulsory registration under the VAT Act 1994.</p>

<p>If your turnover is below £90,000, you can <strong>voluntarily register</strong>. Many agencies serving EU clients choose to do this because it allows them to reclaim VAT on their own costs (software subscriptions, equipment, professional fees) and because it simplifies their invoicing process. More on that below.</p>

<p>For most UK agencies, the question is not "should I register?" but "once I'm registered, how do I invoice EU clients correctly?"</p>

<h2>The Key Rule: Place of Supply for Agency Services</h2>

<p>Under post-Brexit VAT rules, the "place of supply" for most agency services (marketing, advertising, PR, web design, consulting, creative work) to EU business clients is <strong>where the client belongs</strong>, not where you are based.</p>

<p>This matters because it determines who charges VAT and at what rate.</p>

<p>If your EU client is a VAT-registered business (which most are), the supply is treated as taking place in their country. You do not charge UK VAT. Your client accounts for VAT in their own country through the "reverse charge" mechanism. Your invoice should show "VAT: Reverse charge" or "VAT: 0% - reverse charge applies."</p>

<p>If your EU client is a <strong>non-business customer</strong> (a private individual, not a registered business), the supply is treated as taking place in the UK. You <strong>do</strong> charge UK VAT at 20%. This is much less common for agencies, but it happens if you do direct-to-consumer work or work for unincorporated associations without a VAT number.</p>

<h3>What the Reverse Charge Means for Your Invoice</h3>

<p>The reverse charge is not a zero rate. It is a specific mechanism that shifts the VAT accounting from you (the supplier) to your client (the recipient). Your client declares both the output VAT and the input VAT on their own VAT return. The net effect is zero for them, but HMRC still gets its money.</p>

<p>For you, the agency, it means you do not collect VAT from the EU client and you do not pay that VAT to HMRC. Your invoice is "outside the scope" of UK VAT (or more accurately, it is a supply subject to the reverse charge).</p>

<p>Here is what a correct invoice to an EU business client looks like:</p>

<ul>
<li>Your UK VAT number (e.g. GB123456789)</li>
<li>Your client's VAT number (including their country code, e.g. FR12345678901 for France, DE123456789 for Germany)</li>
<li>Your client's name and address</li>
<li>A clear description of the services (e.g. "Digital marketing services, social media campaign management")</li>
<li>The net value of the services in GBP or agreed currency</li>
<li>A line stating: "VAT: Reverse charge, the customer must account for VAT under Article 44 of the EU VAT Directive" or simply "Reverse charge: VAT to be accounted for by the customer"</li>
<li>No UK VAT charged, the total equals the net value</li>
<li>Your invoice number, date, and payment terms</li>
</ul>

<p>Do not put "VAT 0%" or "zero rated" unless you are certain that is the correct treatment. The reverse charge is not the same as zero rating. Using the wrong wording can confuse your client's accounts department and delay payment.</p>

<h2>When You Must Charge UK VAT to EU Clients</h2>

<p>There are two scenarios where you <strong>do</strong> charge UK VAT on invoices to EU clients:</p>

<p><strong>1. The client is not VAT-registered.</strong> If you are selling to a private individual, a sole trader without a VAT number, or a non-business entity, the place of supply is the UK. You charge 20% UK VAT. This is rare for agency services but worth knowing.</p>

<p><strong>2. The services are "electronically supplied services" (ESS) to non-business customers.</strong> This is a separate set of rules under the VAT Mini One Stop Shop (MOSS) regime. If you sell digital services (e.g. SaaS, downloadable content, online courses) to EU consumers, you must register for MOSS and charge the VAT rate of the customer's country. Most agency services are not ESS, but if your agency sells digital products directly to EU consumers, check this carefully.</p>

<h2>Do You Need to Register for VAT in an EU Country?</h2>

<p>Generally, no. If you only supply services to EU business clients and those services fall under the reverse charge, you do not need to register for VAT in the client's country. The reverse charge handles everything.</p>

<p>However, there are exceptions:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>If you have a physical presence</strong> in an EU country (an office, a branch, a warehouse, or employees working there), you may have a fixed establishment and need to register locally.</li>
<li><strong>If you sell goods</strong> to EU customers (e.g. promotional merchandise, physical products), the rules are different and you may need to register.</li>
<li><strong>If you attend events or exhibitions</strong> in the EU and provide services there, the place of supply may shift.</li>
</ul>

<p>For a standard UK agency with no EU office, selling services to EU business clients, the reverse charge is your friend. Use it correctly and you avoid the administrative burden of multiple VAT registrations.</p>

<h2>How to Check Your Client's VAT Number</h2>

<p>Before you issue a reverse charge invoice, you must verify that your client's VAT number is valid. HMRC provides a free online tool called <strong>VIES (VAT Information Exchange System)</strong> at ec.europa.eu/taxation_customs/vies. Enter the client's VAT number and country code. If it comes back as valid, you are safe to treat the supply as B2B.</p>

<p>If the number is invalid or the client cannot provide one, treat them as a non-business customer and charge UK VAT. Do not guess. A reverse charge invoice with an invalid VAT number can leave you liable for the VAT if HMRC challenges it.</p>

<p>Keep a record of the VIES check for each invoice. Screenshot it or save the PDF. This is your evidence that you took reasonable steps to verify the client's status.</p>

<h2>Currency and Exchange Rates on EU Invoices</h2>

<p>You can invoice in any currency you agree with your client. Many agencies invoice in GBP because it is simpler for their accounting. Others invoice in EUR because the client prefers it.</p>

<p>If you invoice in a foreign currency, you must convert the net value to GBP for your VAT return. Use the HMRC published exchange rate for the last day of the VAT period, or the rate on the invoice date. Be consistent. Pick one method and stick with it.</p>

<p>Your invoice can show the total in EUR (or USD, or any currency) but your VAT return must be in GBP. If you use accounting software like <strong>Xero</strong> or <strong>QuickBooks</strong>, set up a multi-currency bank account and let the software handle the conversion. Most modern platforms do this automatically if you enter the exchange rate or link to a live feed.</p>

<h2>EC Sales Lists: The Form You Probably Forgot</h2>

<p>If you are a UK VAT-registered agency supplying services to EU business clients, you must file an <strong>EC Sales List (ESL)</strong> with HMRC. This is a periodic return (usually quarterly) that lists all supplies of services to VAT-registered customers in EU member states.</p>

<p>The ESL is separate from your VAT return. You file it online through your HMRC VAT account. It asks for:</p>

<ul>
<li>Your client's VAT number (including country code)</li>
<li>The total value of supplies to that client in the period</li>
<li>The currency and exchange rate used</li>
</ul>

<p>You must file an ESL even if you made no supplies to EU clients in the period (a nil return). HMRC can issue penalties for late or missing ESLs. The penalty is £5 per day for the first 2 months, then £15 per day after that, up to a maximum of 30 days. After 30 days, penalties increase significantly.</p>

<p>Set a reminder in your calendar. Your accountant can handle this for you, but if you manage your own VAT, do not forget it. It is one of the most common compliance gaps we see in <a href="/agencies/marketing-agencies">marketing agencies</a> that start selling to EU clients.</p>

<h2>What About Services You Buy from EU Suppliers?</h2>

<p>This works in reverse. If you buy services from an EU business (e.g. freelance design work, software subscriptions, consulting), you must account for the reverse charge on your UK VAT return. You declare both the output VAT (what you would have paid if the supplier were UK-based) and the input VAT (what you can reclaim). The net effect is zero, but you must include both sides.</p>

<p>In <strong>Xero</strong> or <strong>QuickBooks</strong>, set up a specific tax code for "Reverse charge on services from EU" (often labelled "20% EC VAT" or similar). Your accountant can configure this. Do not simply ignore the invoice or treat it as a non-VAT purchase. HMRC expects to see the reverse charge on your return.</p>

<h2>Practical Steps for Your Agency</h2>

<p>Here is a checklist to follow if you are already VAT-registered or planning to register:</p>

<ol>
<li><strong>Confirm your turnover.</strong> If you are approaching £90,000 in UK and EU sales, register for VAT. If you are below but want to reclaim VAT on costs, consider voluntary registration.</li>
<li><strong>Verify each EU client's VAT number</strong> via VIES before your first invoice. Keep the evidence.</li>
<li><strong>Set up your accounting software</strong> with the correct tax codes for reverse charge supplies. Most modern platforms have a "VAT 0% EC Services" or "Reverse charge" option.</li>
<li><strong>Draft an invoice template</strong> that includes the reverse charge wording. Show it to your accountant before you send it to a client.</li>
<li><strong>File your EC Sales List</strong> quarterly. Set a recurring task in your calendar.</li>
<li><strong>Check your software settings</strong> for multi-currency if you invoice in EUR or other currencies.</li>
<li><strong>Review your purchases from EU suppliers</strong> and ensure you are accounting for reverse charge on those too.</li>
</ol>

<h2>Common Mistakes Agency Founders Make</h2>

<p><strong>Mistake 1: Charging UK VAT to EU business clients.</strong> We see this frequently. An agency invoices an EU client at £1,200 plus 20% VAT = £1,440. The client cannot reclaim UK VAT because they are not registered in the UK. The client disputes the invoice, payment is delayed, and you have to issue a credit note and re-invoice. Avoid this by using the reverse charge.</p>

<p><strong>Mistake 2: Not filing EC Sales Lists.</strong> An agency starts selling to a handful of EU clients, files VAT returns correctly, but forgets the ESL. HMRC sends a penalty notice 6 months later. The penalty can be hundreds of pounds for a simple omission.</p>

<p><strong>Mistake 3: Treating all EU clients the same.</strong> A German GmbH with a VAT number is different from a French sole trader without one. Check every client individually. Do not assume.</p>

<p><strong>Mistake 4: Ignoring currency conversion.</strong> If you invoice in EUR and use a different exchange rate each quarter without recording it, your VAT return will not reconcile with your bank statements. Use a consistent method and keep a record.</p>
