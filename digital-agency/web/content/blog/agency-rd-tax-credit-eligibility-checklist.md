<p>If you run a digital agency, a creative agency, or a software development shop, you have probably heard other founders talk about R&D tax credits. You have probably also assumed they do not apply to you. That assumption costs agency owners real money every year.</p>

<p>HMRC approved over £7 billion in R&D tax relief in the most recent published year. A significant portion went to companies that do not look like laboratories. They look like agencies.</p>

<p>This article is a straight checklist. Work through it with your actual projects in mind. If you tick enough boxes, you need to speak to your accountant before your next corporation tax return goes in.</p>

<h2>What HMRC Actually Means by R&D</h2>

<p>Let us kill the misconception immediately. R&D does not mean white coats, petri dishes, or university partnerships. HMRC defines R&D as work that seeks to achieve an advance in science or technology by resolving scientific or technological uncertainty.</p>

<p>For an agency, that translates to: did you build something where the outcome was not certain at the start? Did you have to test, fail, iterate, and solve a problem that had no off-the-shelf answer?</p>

<p>If you did, you may have agency R&D tax credit eligibility even if you never thought of yourself as doing research.</p>

<h2>The Core Eligibility Test: Five Questions</h2>

<p>HMRC uses a five-part test. Every qualifying project must satisfy all five. Here is how they apply to agency work.</p>

<h3>1. Was There a Qualifying Project?</h3>
<p>A project can be a client engagement, an internal tool build, or a product you developed for resale. It does not matter whether it succeeded commercially. What matters is what you attempted technically.</p>
<p>Example: a web design agency in Manchester Northern Quarter spent eight months building a custom booking engine for a chain of clinics. No existing SaaS product handled the specific compliance requirements. That is a project.</p>

<h3>2. Was There an Advance in Science or Technology?</h3>
<p>This is where most agencies trip up. The advance must be in the field of science or technology, not in your business model or your client's commercial position.</p>
<p>Building a standard WordPress site with a new theme is not an advance. Building a machine learning model that predicts customer churn from unstructured social media data probably is.</p>
<p>The advance is measured against the publicly available knowledge at the time. If a competent professional in your field could have done it without uncertainty, it is not R&D.</p>

<h3>3. Was There Technological Uncertainty?</h3>
<p>This is the key question. At the start of the project, was it not obvious whether the solution was achievable? Did you have to experiment, model, simulate, or prototype to find out?</p>
<p>Agencies routinely encounter this when integrating APIs that were never designed to work together, building real-time data visualisation tools from incompatible data sources, or developing proprietary algorithms for media buying optimisation.</p>

<h3>4. Did You Try to Overcome That Uncertainty?</h3>
<p>You need evidence of systematic work. Emails, Slack threads, technical specs, test results, meeting notes. HMRC does not expect a laboratory notebook, but they do expect a paper trail showing you tried things, some failed, and you iterated.</p>

<h3>5. Was the Work in a Qualifying Field?</h3>
<p>Software development qualifies. So do engineering, materials science, biology, chemistry, and physics. Pure arts, humanities, and social sciences do not. If your advance is in software engineering or data science, you are in scope.</p>

<h2>Specific Agency Scenarios That Qualify</h2>

<p>Let me give you real examples from agencies we have worked with at Agency Founder Finance. Names changed, numbers accurate.</p>

<h3>Custom Software Development Agency</h3>
<p>A 15-person agency in Bristol Harbourside builds bespoke CRM systems for recruitment firms. One client needed a system that could parse CVs in seven languages, extract structured data, and match candidates to roles using weighted criteria that changed per sector. No off-the-shelf product handled multilingual CV parsing at the required accuracy. The agency spent 400 hours building and training a natural language processing pipeline. That is qualifying R&D. The claim was worth £47,300 in corporation tax reduction.</p>

<h3>Digital Marketing Agency with Proprietary Tech</h3>
<p>A 40-person SEO agency in Soho built their own bid management tool for Google Ads. The tool had to adjust bids in real-time based on 23 variables including weather data, stock levels, and competitor pricing scraped from public sources. The uncertainty was whether the real-time processing could keep latency under 200 milliseconds at scale. It took six months to solve. Qualifying. The claim reduced their tax bill by £82,000.</p>

<h3>Creative Agency Building Interactive Experiences</h3>
<p>A creative agency in Shoreditch won a contract to build an augmented reality installation for a museum. The technical challenge was getting AR tracking to work in low-light conditions across irregular surfaces. Standard AR frameworks failed. They had to write custom computer vision code. Qualifying. The claim was £21,400.</p>

<h2>What Does Not Qualify</h2>

<p>Let me save you time on the claims that will not work.</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Routine website builds.</strong> Even complex ones, if the technical approach is standard. A headless CMS with a React front end is not R&D unless you are doing something genuinely new with the architecture.</li>
<li><strong>Client work that is purely creative.</strong> Designing a brand identity, writing copy, producing video content. These are not technological advances.</li>
<li><strong>Business process improvements.</strong> Restructuring your team, changing your pricing model, improving your sales process. These are commercially valuable but not R&D.</li>
<li><strong>Work funded by someone else.</strong> If a client paid you to solve a problem and retained the IP, the R&D claim belongs to them, not you. Check your contracts carefully.</li>
</ul>

<h2>The Financial Mechanics: How the Relief Works</h2>

<p>There are two schemes. Which one applies depends on your company size.</p>

<h3>Merged R&D Scheme (from 1 April 2024)</h3>
<p>For accounting periods beginning on or after 1 April 2024, a single merged scheme replaces the old SME and RDEC regimes. Under the merged scheme, all companies (regardless of size) receive a taxable expenditure credit of 20% of qualifying R&D spend. After 25% corporation tax on the credit, the net benefit is approximately 15% of qualifying costs. Loss-making R&D-intensive SMEs (qualifying R&D at least 30% of total costs) may instead claim under Enhanced R&D Intensive Support (ERIS), giving an 86% enhanced deduction and a 14.5% payable credit.</p>

<h3>The old SME scheme and RDEC (pre-1 April 2024)</h3>
<p>For accounting periods beginning before 1 April 2024, the old two-scheme structure applied. SMEs (under 500 employees and either turnover under €100m or a balance sheet under €86m) could claim an enhanced deduction of 186% of qualifying R&D expenditure. Loss-making SMEs could surrender the loss for a cash payment of up to 14.5% of the surrendered amount. Larger companies used the RDEC scheme at a 20% credit rate. These rates do not apply to post-1-April-2024 periods.</p>

<h2>What Costs Qualify</h2>

<p>For agencies, the big categories are usually:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Staff costs.</strong> Salaries, employer NI, and pension contributions for employees directly working on qualifying projects. If someone splits their time between R&D and client work, you apportion.</li>
<li><strong>Software licences.</strong> If you used specific software to conduct the R&D, the licence costs qualify. AWS compute time for training ML models is a common example.</li>
<li><strong>Subcontractors.</strong> If you paid another company to do qualifying R&D work, up to 65% of the payment qualifies under the SME scheme. If you used externally provided workers (individual contractors on your payroll), 100% qualifies.</li>
<li><strong>Consumables.</strong> Materials used up in the R&D process. Cloud credits, data sets, API usage fees.</li>
</ul>

<h2>The Documentation You Need</h2>

<p>HMRC does not require a specific format, but they do require a competent report. If you are challenged, and around 10% of claims are now selected for compliance checks, you need to show:</p>

<ul>
<li>A technical narrative describing the advance, the uncertainty, and how you overcame it</li>
<li>Project records: timelines, test results, iteration logs</li>
<li>Financial records showing the costs allocated to each project</li>
<li>Evidence of the technological uncertainty at the project start</li>
</ul>

<p>We recommend building the technical narrative as you go. A monthly note in a shared document that says "this week we tried X and it failed because Y" is worth far more than a retrospective reconstruction two years later.</p>

<h2>The Risks: What Happens If You Get It Wrong</h2>

<p>R&D claims are increasingly scrutinised. HMRC launched a compliance taskforce specifically targeting what they see as overclaimed relief. If your claim is rejected, you face:</p>

<ul>
<li>Repayment of the relief plus interest</li>
<li>Penalties of up to 30% of the overclaimed amount for careless errors, up to 100% for deliberate errors</li>
<li>Increased scrutiny on all future claims</li>
</ul>

<p>The way to avoid this is straightforward: only claim for genuinely qualifying work, document it properly, and have a qualified accountant review the claim before submission. Working exclusively with agency founders, we review every claim against the five-part test before it goes anywhere near a CT600.</p>

<h2>The Checklist: Should You Investigate Further?</h2>

<p>Print this out. Go through it with your technical lead or head of delivery.</p>

<ol>
<li>In the last two years, have you built any software, tool, or system that required solving a problem with no known solution?</li>
<li>Did your team spend time testing approaches that did not work before finding one that did?</li>
<li>Do you have records of that process? Emails, tickets, code commits, meeting notes?</li>
<li>Is the work in software, data science, engineering, or another technical field?</li>
<li>Does your company have under 500 employees and under €100m turnover?</li>
<li>Did you pay salaries, software costs, or subcontractor fees directly related to that work?</li>
<li>Did you retain the IP, or does the contract allow you to claim R&D on the work?</li>
</ol>

<p>If you answered yes to four or more of these, you should have a conversation with your accountant before you file your next corporation tax return. The claim must be made within two years of the end of the accounting period in which the expenditure was incurred. Miss that window and the relief is gone permanently.</p>
