<h2>Why Your Dubai Move Might Not Be as Tax-Free as You Think</h2>

<p>You sell your agency shares for £1.2 million. You move to Dubai in April 2025. You think the gain is outside UK tax because you are non-resident. You are wrong if you come back before April 2030.</p>

<p>That is the temporary non residence rule in action. It is one of the most commonly missed planning points for UK agency founders moving to the UAE. And it is expensive when it catches you.</p>

<p>As ICAEW qualified accountants working exclusively with agency founders, we see this scenario more often than you would expect. A founder moves to Dubai, sells their agency or a significant asset, then circumstances change. A family issue. A business opportunity. A partner who does not settle. They return to the UK within five years, and HMRC sends them a tax bill for a gain they thought was safe.</p>

<p>This article explains the <strong>temporary non residence rule five years</strong> threshold in plain English, with real numbers, so you can plan your exit properly.</p>

<h2>What Is the Temporary Non-Residence Rule?</h2>

<p>The temporary non residence rule is a piece of UK tax legislation that applies when you leave the UK, become non-resident, and then return within five complete tax years.</p>

<p>If you do, certain types of income and gains that arose while you were abroad are treated as if they arose in the year you return. HMRC essentially "claws them back" and taxes them as UK income or gains.</p>

<p>The key number is five complete tax years. Not five calendar years. Five tax years (6 April to 5 April).</p>

<p>Here is the critical distinction:</p>

<ul>
<li>If you are non-resident for fewer than five complete tax years, the rule applies. Your gains and certain income are taxable in the UK.</li>
<li>If you are non-resident for five complete tax years or more, the rule does not apply. Your gains and income are outside UK tax (subject to other rules).</li>
</ul>

<p>That is the <strong>temporary non residence rule five years</strong> threshold. Miss it by one day, and you lose the tax advantage.</p>

<h2>What Gets Clawed Back?</h2>

<p>The rule does not catch everything. But for an agency founder, the items that matter are:</p>

<h3>Capital Gains</h3>
<p>If you sell shares in your agency, or any other chargeable asset, while you are temporarily non-resident, the gain is treated as arising in the UK in the tax year you return. You pay UK capital gains tax on it at the rates that apply in that year.</p>

<p>This is the big one for agency founders. If you sell your agency shares after moving to Dubai but before the five-year window is up, and then return early, you owe CGT.</p>

<h3>Income</h3>
<p>Certain types of income are also caught. This includes:</p>
<ul>
<li>Employment income (if you work for a UK employer while abroad)</li>
<li>Trading income (if you continue to run a UK business)</li>
<li>Pension income (in some cases)</li>
</ul>
<p>For most agency founders, the capital gains clawback is the primary concern. But if you maintain any UK income streams, check whether they are caught too.</p>

<h3>What Is Not Caught?</h3>
<p>Some income types are exempt from the clawback. For example, dividend income and bank interest are generally not caught by the temporary non residence rule. But do not assume anything. Check with your accountant before relying on an exemption.</p>

<h2>How the Five-Year Clock Works</h2>

<p>This is where most people get confused. The clock does not start on the day you leave the UK. It starts on the first full tax year of non-residence.</p>

<p>Here is a worked example:</p>

<p>You leave the UK on 1 August 2025 and become non-resident. Your first full tax year of non-residence is 2026/27 (6 April 2026 to 5 April 2027).</p>

<p>To avoid the temporary non residence rule, you must remain non-resident for five complete tax years:</p>

<ul>
<li>Year 1: 2026/27</li>
<li>Year 2: 2027/28</li>
<li>Year 3: 2028/29</li>
<li>Year 4: 2029/30</li>
<li>Year 5: 2030/31</li>
</ul>

<p>You can return to the UK on 6 April 2031 without triggering the clawback. Return on 5 April 2031, and you are inside the window. The gain is taxable.</p>

<p>Notice that your departure date (1 August 2025) is not the start of the clock. The clock starts on 6 April 2026. So your actual time away from the UK is closer to five years and eight months, not five years exactly.</p>

<p>This is the <strong>temporary non residence rule five years</strong> nuance that trips up founders who think they can move to Dubai for "about five years" and come back when they feel like it.</p>

<h2>Real Numbers: What the Clawback Costs</h2>

<p>Let us put numbers on this.</p>

<p>You own 100% of a digital agency based in Shoreditch. You sell the shares in January 2027 for £2.1 million. Your base cost is £100,000. The gain is £2 million.</p>

<p>You moved to Dubai in September 2025. You are non-resident from 2026/27 onwards. You plan to stay for six years.</p>

<p>In 2029, your father becomes ill. You return to the UK in March 2030 to care for him. You have been non-resident for four complete tax years (2026/27, 2027/28, 2028/29, 2029/30). You have not reached five.</p>

<p>The £2 million gain is now treated as arising in the 2029/30 tax year. You owe UK capital gains tax.</p>

<p>Assuming you are a higher rate taxpayer, the CGT rate on carried interest or business asset disposals is 24% (for non-BADR gains) or 18% (if BADR applies and you qualify). If BADR applies, you pay 18% on the first £1 million and 24% on the remaining £1 million. Total tax: £380,000.</p>

<p>If BADR does not apply, you pay 24% on the full £2 million. Total tax: £480,000.</p>

<p>That is a £380,000 to £480,000 tax bill you thought you had avoided. All because you returned six months too early.</p>

<h2>What About BADR?</h2>

<p>Business Asset Disposal Relief (formerly Entrepreneurs' Relief) is available on the first £1 million of qualifying gains. The rate is 18% for disposals from 6 April 2025, rising to 18% from 6 April 2026.</p>

<p>If you are temporarily non-resident and return within five years, you can still claim BADR on the clawed-back gain, provided you met the qualifying conditions at the time of disposal. You need to have held at least 5% of the shares and been an officer or employee for the two years before the sale.</p>

<p>But the rate you pay is the rate in force in the year you return, not the year you sold. So if you sell in 2027 (when the BADR rate might be 18%) but return in 2030 (when the rate is 18%), you pay 18% on the first £1 million.</p>

<p>This is another planning point. The timing of your return affects the tax rate applied to the clawed-back gain.</p>

<h2>How to Avoid the Temporary Non-Residence Rule</h2>

<p>There are three main strategies:</p>

<h3>1. Stay Away for Five Complete Tax Years</h3>
<p>This is the simplest. Do not return to the UK within five complete tax years. If you need to visit, keep visits to fewer than 183 days per tax year, and ideally fewer than 90 days on average over the five years (to maintain non-resident status under the Statutory Residence Test).</p>

<p>If you must return for a family emergency, consider whether you can do so without becoming UK resident again. Short visits are generally fine. Moving back permanently is not.</p>

<h3>2. Sell Before You Leave</h3>
<p>If you sell your agency shares while you are still UK resident, the gain is taxable in the UK anyway. There is no clawback to worry about. You pay CGT at the prevailing UK rates, and then you can move to Dubai with no UK tax exposure on that gain.</p>

<p>This is often the cleanest approach. You know the tax cost upfront. There is no uncertainty about future rates or your return date.</p>

<h3>3. Sell After the Five-Year Window</h3>
<p>If you are already non-resident and planning to sell, wait until you have completed five complete tax years of non-residence before disposing of the asset. Then the gain is outside UK tax (subject to other rules like the UK property exemption).</p>

<p>This requires patience. If you need the cash sooner, it may not be an option.</p>

<h2>What If You Already Returned Early?</h2>

<p>If you have already returned to the UK within the five-year window and you had a capital gain while abroad, you need to report it to HMRC. The gain is treated as arising in the tax year of return. You must include it on your Self Assessment tax return (SA100) for that year.</p>

<p>Failure to report it can lead to penalties and interest. HMRC has access to data from the UAE under the Common Reporting Standard. They will find out.</p>

<p>If you have not yet filed the return, do so as soon as possible. If you have filed and omitted the gain, you can amend the return within 12 months of the filing deadline. After that, you need to notify HMRC of a discovery.</p>

<p>This is not a DIY situation. Speak to an accountant who understands the temporary non residence rule and the UK-UAE tax position.</p>

<h2>Is There Any Relief Available?</h2>

<p>In limited circumstances, you can claim relief under a Double Taxation Agreement (DTA). The UK-UAE DTA contains provisions that may prevent the UK from taxing certain gains, depending on the nature of the asset and your residency status.</p>

<p>But the DTA does not override the temporary non residence rule in all cases. It depends on the specific wording of the treaty and the type of gain. Shares in a UK company, for example, are often taxable in the UK regardless of the DTA.</p>

<p>Do not assume the DTA protects you. Check with your accountant.</p>

<h2>Planning Your Exit Properly</h2>

<p>If you are considering a move to Dubai, the temporary non residence rule should be at the top of your planning checklist. Here is what to do:</p>

<ul>
<li>Confirm your departure date and calculate your five complete tax years of non-residence. Work out the earliest date you can return without triggering the clawback.</li>
<li>Decide whether to sell your agency before leaving, after the five-year window, or during the window (with the risk of clawback if you return early).</li>
<li>If you sell during the window, build in a contingency for early return. Keep a cash reserve to cover the potential CGT bill.</li>
<li>Document your non-residence carefully. Keep records of days spent in the UK, your accommodation arrangements, and your ties to the UAE. HMRC may challenge your status.</li>
<li>Review your share structure. If you hold shares in a holding company, the position may be different. Holding company structures can sometimes provide additional planning options.</li>
</ul>

<h2>The Bottom Line</h2>

<p>The temporary non residence rule five years threshold is not optional. It is a hard statutory test. Miss it, and you pay UK tax on gains you thought were safe.</p>

<p>Plan for five complete tax years away. Or sell before you leave. Or wait until the window closes. But do not assume you can move to Dubai, sell your agency, and come back whenever you want. That assumption has cost agency founders hundreds of thousands of pounds.</p>

<p>If you are already in the UAE and thinking about returning, check your dates before you book the flight. And if you are planning a move, map out the five-year window before you go.</p>
