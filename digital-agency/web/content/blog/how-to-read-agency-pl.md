<p>Most agency founders I meet have never been shown how to read their own P&L. They look at the bottom line, see a positive number, and assume everything is fine. Then they wonder why the bank balance doesn't match the profit figure.</p>

<p>That gap between profit and cash is where agencies fail. And it starts with not understanding what the P&L actually tells you.</p>

<p>This is agency finance basics. The kind of knowledge that separates founders who build sellable businesses from those who burn out running expensive jobs.</p>

<p>I'm going to walk through a real P&L for a 12-person digital agency billing £800,000 per year. Every line. What it means. What to look for. And what the numbers tell you about the health of your agency.</p>

<h2>What a P&L Actually Is</h2>

<p>The profit and loss statement (P&L) measures performance over a period. Usually a month, a quarter, or a year. It shows revenue, costs, and the resulting profit or loss.</p>

<p>It does <strong>not</strong> show cash. That is the single most common misunderstanding I see.</p>

<p>Your P&L might show a £50,000 profit for the year while your bank account shows £5,000. That is normal if you have unpaid invoices, prepaid expenses, or loan repayments. The P&L and the balance sheet work together. One without the other is a half picture.</p>

<p>For agency finance basics, start with the P&L. Learn it first. Then add the balance sheet and cash flow statement later.</p>

<h2>The P&L Structure: Top to Bottom</h2>

<p>Every P&L follows the same structure. Revenue at the top. Costs deducted in order. Net profit at the bottom. The detail between those points is where the story lives.</p>

<h3>Revenue (Turnover)</h3>

<p>This is the total value of work you invoiced during the period. Not cash received. Invoiced.</p>

<p>For our example agency:</p>

<ul>
<li>Retainer income: £480,000 (60% of total)</li>
<li>Project income: £240,000 (30%)</li>
<li>Pass-through costs (ad spend, print, licences): £80,000 (10%)</li>
<li><strong>Total revenue: £800,000</strong></li>
</ul>

<p>Pass-through costs are money that flows through your agency to third parties. Media spend. Print production. Software licences you buy and resell. You should separate these from your own revenue because they inflate your turnover without adding profit.</p>

<p>An agency billing £800,000 with £200,000 in pass-through costs is really a £600,000 agency. The P&L should show pass-through costs as a separate line or within cost of sales, not buried in revenue.</p>

<h3>Cost of Sales (Direct Costs)</h3>

<p>These are costs directly tied to delivering the work. For an agency, that means:</p>

<ul>
<li>Staff salaries for delivery teams</li>
<li>Freelancer costs</li>
<li>Subcontractor fees</li>
<li>Software tools used directly on client projects (e.g. Figma, Adobe Creative Cloud, SEMrush)</li>
<li>Pass-through costs (if not shown separately)</li>
</ul>

<p>Our example agency:</p>

<ul>
<li>Delivery team salaries (8 people): £320,000</li>
<li>Freelancers: £48,000</li>
<li>Direct software costs: £12,000</li>
<li>Pass-through costs: £80,000</li>
<li><strong>Total cost of sales: £460,000</strong></li>
</ul>

<h3>Gross Profit and Gross Margin</h3>

<p>Gross profit is revenue minus cost of sales. Gross margin is that number expressed as a percentage of revenue.</p>

<p><strong>Gross profit: £800,000 - £460,000 = £340,000</strong><br>
<strong>Gross margin: £340,000 ÷ £800,000 = 42.5%</strong></p>

<p>A 42.5% gross margin is low for a digital agency. Most healthy agencies sit between 50% and 65%. Below 45% and you are either undercharging, over-staffing projects, or carrying too many non-billable people in your delivery team.</p>

<p>If your gross margin is below 50%, look at three things:</p>

<ul>
<li>Are your project scopes tight enough? Scope creep kills margins.</li>
<li>Are you using too many freelancers at rates that wipe out your markup?</li>
<li>Are you including pass-through costs in your revenue calculation? If so, recalculate without them.</li>
</ul>

<p>Excluding pass-through costs, our example agency's gross margin is £260,000 ÷ £600,000 = 43.3%. Still low. There is work to do here.</p>

<h2>Overheads (Operating Expenses)</h2>

<p>These are the costs of running the agency that are not tied to specific projects. Every agency has them. The question is whether they are proportionate.</p>

<p>Our example agency overheads:</p>

<ul>
<li>Management salaries (director, ops, finance): £120,000</li>
<li>Office rent (Manchester Northern Quarter): £24,000</li>
<li>Software (Xero, Dext, Float, Slack, Notion, Google Workspace): £18,000</li>
<li>Marketing and business development: £15,000</li>
<li>Professional fees (accountant, legal, insurance): £12,000</li>
<li>Travel and entertainment: £8,000</li>
<li>Training and development: £5,000</li>
<li>Other (IT, phones, subscriptions, bank fees): £10,000</li>
<li><strong>Total overheads: £212,000</strong></li>
</ul>

<p>Overheads as a percentage of revenue: £212,000 ÷ £800,000 = 26.5%. That is reasonable for a 12-person agency. Most well-run agencies sit between 20% and 30%. If you are above 35%, you have structural costs that need addressing.</p>

<p>Look at your own overheads and ask: does every line item directly support revenue generation or compliance? If not, cut it.</p>

<h2>Operating Profit (EBITDA)</h2>

<p>This is gross profit minus overheads. It tells you what the agency earns from its core operations before interest, tax, depreciation, and amortisation.</p>

<p><strong>Operating profit: £340,000 - £212,000 = £128,000</strong><br>
<strong>Operating margin: £128,000 ÷ £800,000 = 16%</strong></p>

<p>A 16% operating margin is decent but not great. For a well-run agency, I want to see 20% or higher. That gives you room to invest, save for tax, and build a buffer.</p>

<p>If your operating margin is below 10%, your agency is fragile. One bad month or one client loss could tip you into a loss.</p>

<h3>Other Income and Costs</h3>

<p>Below operating profit, you will see:</p>

<ul>
<li>Interest income (bank interest on savings)</li>
<li>Interest expense (loan or overdraft costs)</li>
<li>Depreciation (spreading the cost of assets like laptops and office furniture over their useful life)</li>
<li>Amortisation (same for intangible assets like goodwill or software development costs)</li>
</ul>

<p>For our example:</p>

<ul>
<li>Interest income: £1,200</li>
<li>Interest expense: £3,800</li>
<li>Depreciation: £15,000</li>
<li>Amortisation: £0</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Profit before tax: £128,000 + £1,200 - £3,800 - £15,000 = £110,400</strong></p>

<h3>Corporation Tax</h3>

<p>For the 2025/26 tax year, corporation tax rates are:</p>

<ul>
<li>19% on profits up to £50,000</li>
<li>25% on profits above £250,000</li>
<li>Marginal relief between £50,000 and £250,000</li>
</ul>

<p>Our example agency has profits of £110,400. That falls in the marginal relief band. The effective tax rate will be somewhere around 21-22%, not the headline 25%.</p>

<p><strong>Estimated corporation tax: £24,000 (roughly 21.7%)</strong></p>

<p><strong>Net profit after tax: £110,400 - £24,000 = £86,400</strong></p>

<h2>The Key Ratios Every Agency Founder Should Track</h2>

<p>The raw numbers matter. But the ratios tell you whether your agency is improving or declining month on month.</p>

<h3>Revenue Per Head</h3>

<p>Total revenue divided by total headcount. For our example: £800,000 ÷ 12 = £66,667 per head.</p>

<p>For a digital or marketing agency, £66k per head is below average. Most healthy agencies target £80k-£120k per head. If you are below £70k, you either have too many non-billable people or your rates are too low.</p>

<p>Revenue per head is a blunt measure but it is useful for benchmarking against similar agencies. If you are at £55k per head and your competitor is at £95k, there is a structural problem to solve.</p>

<h3>Utilisation Rate</h3>

<p>Billable hours divided by total available hours for your delivery team. If each of your 8 delivery staff works 40 hours per week for 46 weeks per year, that is 14,720 available hours. If they bill 11,000 of those, utilisation is 74.7%.</p>

<p>Target utilisation for delivery teams: 75-85%. Below 70% and you are carrying too much bench. Above 90% and you are heading for burnout.</p>

<h3>Gross Margin Percentage</h3>

<p>Already covered above. Track this monthly. If it drops by more than 3 percentage points in a single month, investigate immediately. Something changed in your cost base or your pricing.</p>

<h3>Overhead Ratio</h3>

<p>Total overheads divided by total revenue. Our example: 26.5%. Target: 20-30%. If you are above 35%, your cost base is too high for your revenue.</p>

<h3>Net Profit Margin</h3>

<p>Net profit after tax divided by revenue. Our example: £86,400 ÷ £800,000 = 10.8%. Target: 10-20% for a healthy agency. Below 5% and you are not building real value.</p>

<h2>Common P&L Mistakes Agency Founders Make</h2>

<p>I see the same errors repeatedly. Here are the ones to watch for.</p>

<p><strong>Mistake 1: Including VAT in revenue.</strong> Your P&L should show net revenue (excluding VAT). If your accountant has included VAT, the numbers are inflated. Check your software settings. Xero and QuickBooks both allow you to report net or gross. Use net.</p>

<p><strong>Mistake 2: Not separating pass-through costs.</strong> If you buy £50,000 of Google Ads spend for a client and invoice them £50,000, that is not your revenue. It is pass-through. Show it separately or exclude it from your margin calculations.</p>

<p><strong>Mistake 3: Treating all staff costs as overheads.</strong> Delivery staff are cost of sales. Management, sales, and admin are overheads. If you lump everyone together, you cannot calculate gross margin accurately.</p>

<p><strong>Mistake 4: Ignoring directors' salaries.</strong> If you pay yourself through payroll, that salary is an overhead. Many founders exclude their own salary from the P&L because it feels like personal income. Include it. The P&L needs to reflect the true cost of running the agency.</p>

<p><strong>Mistake 5: Looking at annual numbers only.</strong> Monthly P&Ls tell you the story. Annual numbers hide the volatility. If you only look at year-end accounts, you miss the months where margins collapsed and recovered. Run a monthly P&L. Review it within two weeks of month-end.</p>

<h2>What a Healthy Agency P&L Looks Like</h2>

<p>Here is a rough target for a 10-15 person agency turning over £750k-£1.2m:</p>

<ul>
<li>Gross margin: 55-65%</li>
<li>Overhead ratio: 20-30%</li>
<li>Operating margin: 20-30%</li>
<li>Net profit margin: 12-20%</li>
<li>Revenue per head: £80k-£120k</li>
<li>Utilisation: 75-85%</li>
</ul>

<p>If your numbers fall outside these ranges, you have a clear area to work on. Start with gross margin. That is the lever that has the biggest impact on everything below it.</p>

<p>A 5 percentage point improvement in gross margin on £800k revenue is £40,000 of additional gross profit. That flows almost entirely to the bottom line if overheads stay flat.</p>

<h2>How to Start Reading Your Own P&L</h2>

<p>Pull up your latest monthly P&L. If you use Xero or QuickBooks, run a report for the last 12 months. If you use FreeAgent, same thing.</p>

<p>Go through each line. Compare this month to last month. Compare this quarter to the same quarter last year. Look for trends, not single data points.</p>

<p>If something does not make sense, ask your accountant. A good ICAEW qualified accountant will explain the numbers, not just file them. That is the difference between compliance and advice.</p>

<p>If you do not have a monthly P&L, ask your accountant to set one up. It should take them less than an hour once your bookkeeping is clean. If they push back, find a different accountant.</p>
