<p>You've been running a freelance agency on the side for 18 months. Maybe you're a web designer billing £38k a year while holding down a part-time role. Or a copywriter turning over £52k from three retainer clients. And you're asking yourself: should I incorporate?</p>

<p>The standard advice is simple: incorporate once your profits hit a certain threshold. But that advice misses the real question. The question isn't "when do I save tax?" It's "what does the full cost picture look like for <em>my</em> agency?"</p>

<p>This guide walks through the actual numbers for a side hustle agency founder considering incorporation in 2026/27. We'll cover the tax arithmetic, the hidden costs most guides ignore, and the practical triggers that tell you when it's time to make the switch. As ICAEW qualified accountants working exclusively with agency founders, we see this decision made well and made badly every month.</p>

<h2>The Starting Point: Sole Trader vs Limited Company Tax in 2026/27</h2>

<p>Let's get the headline numbers on the table first. For the 2026/27 tax year:</p>

<p><strong>Sole trader:</strong> You pay income tax and Class 4 National Insurance on all your profits above the personal allowance. Your tax bill is roughly 20% on profits between £12,571 and £50,270, then 40% on profits between £50,271 and £125,140. Plus Class 4 NIC at 9% on profits between £12,570 and £50,270, then 2% above that.</p>

<p><strong>Limited company:</strong> Your company pays corporation tax on its profits. At 19% for profits up to £50,000, then marginal relief up to £250,000 where the main rate of 25% kicks in. You then pay yourself a salary (typically up to the NI threshold of £12,570) and take the rest as dividends. Dividends are taxed at 10.75% (basic rate), 35.75% (higher rate), or 39.35% (additional rate). The dividend allowance is £500.</p>

<p>On paper, the limited company looks cheaper for profits above roughly £30k. But the paper doesn't tell you the full story.</p>

<h2>The Three Hidden Costs of Incorporation</h2>

<h3>1. Accountancy fees double (at least)</h3>

<p>As a sole trader, your annual accounts are straightforward. A good accountant will charge you somewhere between £400 and £800 per year for a sole trader return. Maybe £1,200 if you have multiple income streams.</p>

<p>Once you incorporate, you need annual accounts filed at Companies House, a corporation tax return (CT600), a personal self-assessment (SA100) covering your salary and dividends, and ongoing bookkeeping throughout the year. Your accountancy fees will jump to £1,800-£3,500 for a small agency. For a growing agency with multiple contractors, VAT returns, and management accounts, expect £4,000-£6,000.</p>

<p>That's an extra £2,000-£4,000 per year before you save a penny in tax.</p>

<h3>2. Your time cost</h3>

<p>Running a limited company means filing confirmation statements at Companies House (£34 per year). It means maintaining a statutory register. It means filing your own RTI payroll submissions every month (or paying your accountant extra to do it). It means dealing with HMRC queries that come addressed to "The Company Secretary" instead of you personally.</p>

<p>If you value your time at £75 per hour (which most agency founders should), and incorporation adds 20 hours of admin per year, that's £1,500 of hidden cost.</p>

<h3>3. The cash flow trap</h3>

<p>As a sole trader, you pay tax once a year on the profits you actually earned. As a limited company, you pay corporation tax 9 months after your year end. But you also owe personal tax on your dividends, which falls due on 31 January following the tax year. And if you're new to dividends, you might not have put aside enough.</p>

<p>Worse: many new company directors take dividends monthly, forgetting that the tax on those dividends isn't due for 22 months. They spend the money. Then HMRC's bill arrives and they don't have it.</p>

<p>We see this pattern every spring. It's painful.</p>

<h2>When Does Incorporation Actually Save You Money?</h2>

<p>Let's run three real scenarios. These are based on actual agency founders we work with.</p>

<h3>Scenario A: The £35k side hustle</h3>

<p>You're a freelance copywriter. Your side hustle turns over £42,000. After expenses (software, home office, travel), your profit is £35,000.</p>

<p><strong>Sole trader:</strong><br>
Profit: £35,000<br>
Personal allowance: £12,570<br>
Taxable: £22,430<br>
Income tax at 20%: £4,486<br>
Class 4 NIC at 9% on £22,430: £2,019<br>
Total tax: £6,505<br>
Net take-home: £28,495</p>

<p><strong>Limited company:</strong><br>
Company profit: £35,000<br>
Corporation tax at 19%: £6,650<br>
Available for distribution: £28,350<br>
Take as salary £12,570 (no tax or NI) and dividends £15,780<br>
Dividend tax at 10.75% on £15,280 (after £500 allowance): £1,337<br>
Total tax: £7,987<br>
Net take-home: £27,013</p>

<p><strong>Result: You're £1,482 worse off as a limited company, before accounting for the extra accountancy fees and your time.</strong></p>

<p>At this profit level, incorporation is a net loss. Don't do it.</p>

<h3>Scenario B: The £75k growing agency</h3>

<p>Your side hustle is becoming a real business. Turnover is £95,000. After expenses, profit is £75,000.</p>

<p><strong>Sole trader:</strong><br>
Profit: £75,000<br>
Personal allowance: £12,570<br>
Taxable: £62,430<br>
Income tax: 20% on £37,700 (£7,540) + 40% on £24,730 (£9,892) = £17,432<br>
Class 4 NIC: 9% on £37,700 (£3,393) + 2% on £24,730 (£495) = £3,888<br>
Total tax: £21,320<br>
Net take-home: £53,680</p>

<p><strong>Limited company:</strong><br>
Company profit: £75,000<br>
Corporation tax at 19% on first £50k (£9,500) + marginal relief on £25k (effective rate ~26.5% = £6,625) = £16,125<br>
Available for distribution: £58,875<br>
Salary: £12,570 (no tax or NI)<br>
Dividends: £46,305<br>
Dividend tax at 10.75% on first £37,700 (£3,299) + 35.75% on £8,105 (£2,735) = £6,034<br>
Total tax: £22,159<br>
Net take-home: £52,841</p>

<p><strong>Result: You're £839 worse off as a limited company. But the gap is closing.</strong></p>

<p>At £75k profit, the tax arithmetic is roughly neutral. The deciding factor becomes whether the extra admin and accountancy cost is worth it for the protection limited liability offers and the ability to reinvest profits tax-efficiently.</p>

<h3>Scenario C: The £150k established agency</h3>

<p>Your side hustle is now your main gig. You've got three staff, a proper office, and turnover of £210,000. Profit is £150,000.</p>

<p><strong>Sole trader:</strong><br>
Profit: £150,000<br>
Personal allowance: £12,570<br>
Taxable: £137,430<br>
Income tax: 20% on £37,700 (£7,540) + 40% on £87,400 (£34,960) + 45% on £12,330 (£5,549) = £48,049<br>
Class 4 NIC: 9% on £37,700 (£3,393) + 2% on £99,730 (£1,995) = £5,388<br>
Total tax: £53,437<br>
Net take-home: £96,563</p>

<p><strong>Limited company:</strong><br>
Company profit: £150,000<br>
Corporation tax at 25% on full £150k (marginal relief has tapered out) = £37,500<br>
Available for distribution: £112,500<br>
Salary: £12,570<br>
Dividends: £99,930<br>
Dividend tax at 10.75% on first £37,700 (£3,299) + 35.75% on next £62,230 (£21,003) = £24,302<br>
Total tax: £61,802<br>
Net take-home: £88,198</p>

<p><strong>Result: You're £8,365 better off as a limited company, before accounting for fees.</strong></p>

<p>At £150k profit, the tax saving is substantial. Even after paying an extra £3,000 in accountancy fees, you're still £5,000+ better off. And you have limited liability protection, which matters when you have staff and client contracts.</p>

<h2>The Non-Tax Reasons to Incorporate</h2>

<p>Tax is not the only factor. Here are three reasons to incorporate even when the tax arithmetic is neutral:</p>

<h3>Limited liability</h3>

<p>If you're a web designer building sites for clients, and one of your sites crashes a client's ecommerce platform costing them £50k in lost revenue, they can sue you personally as a sole trader. As a limited company director, your personal assets are protected (assuming you haven't given personal guarantees).</p>

<p>Once your agency has more than a few clients, or you're handling significant project budgets, the liability protection alone can justify incorporation.</p>

<h3>Reinvestment and growth</h3>

<p>A limited company lets you reinvest profits at the corporation tax rate (19-25%) rather than your personal tax rate (up to 45%). If you want to hire staff, buy equipment, or invest in software, you can do that with pre-tax money. As a sole trader, you'd have to earn the money, pay tax on it, then spend the remainder.</p>

<p>For a growing agency planning to invest £20k in new software and training next year, the difference is significant.</p>

<h3>Exit readiness</h3>

<p>You cannot sell a sole trade in the same way you sell a limited company. If you ever want to exit your agency, having a limited company structure makes the transaction cleaner and more attractive to buyers. And if you hold the shares for at least two years, you qualify for Business Asset Disposal Relief (BADR), which means 18% capital gains tax on the first £1 million of gain.</p>

<p>That's a £390k tax saving compared to the 24% residential property rate. It's worth planning for even if your exit is five years away.</p>

<h2>The Practical Trigger Points</h2>

<p>Based on the numbers above, here are the practical triggers that tell you it's time to talk to an accountant about incorporating your agency:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Your profit exceeds £80k.</strong> Below this, the tax saving is marginal and may be eaten by extra fees.</li>
<li><strong>You're hiring staff.</strong> Having employees as a sole trader is possible but administratively messy. A limited company gives you a cleaner payroll structure.</li>
<li><strong>You're working with enterprise clients.</strong> If your clients are large companies, they may prefer (or require) working with a limited company rather than an individual.</li>
<li><strong>You want to reinvest profits.</strong> If you're planning significant investment in the business, incorporation lets you do that tax-efficiently.</li>
<li><strong>You're thinking about an exit.</strong> Even if it's 3-5 years away, the clock on the BADR two-year holding period starts when you acquire the shares.</li>
</ul>

<h2>What About the Admin Burden?</h2>

<p>Let's be direct about this: running a limited company is more work than being a sole trader. But it's not <em>that</em> much more work if you set it up properly from day one.</p>

<p>You'll need:</p>
<ul>
<li>Accounting software (Xero or QuickBooks are standard for small agencies. We recommend Xero for most agency founders because of its project tracking and reporting capabilities.)</li>
<li>A separate business bank account (you'll need this anyway once your turnover hits a certain level)</li>
<li>A payroll system (your accountant can handle this for £20-40 per month)</li>
<li>A process for quarterly VAT returns if you're registered for VAT (which you probably should be once you incorporate)</li>
</ul>

<p>Most agency founders we work with find the transition takes about 3-4 months to feel normal. After that, the routine becomes second nature.</p>

<h2>One More Thing: The Flat Rate VAT Trap</h2>

<p>If you're a sole trader below the £90k VAT threshold, you don't charge VAT. Once you incorporate, many founders voluntarily register for VAT to claim back input tax on their expenses. That's fine.</p>

<p>But some accountants recommend the Flat Rate VAT scheme to new company directors. For a marketing agency, the flat rate is 13.5% (or 14.5% for some sectors). The idea is you charge 20% VAT to clients but pay HMRC a lower percentage, keeping the difference.</p>

<p>The trap: if you're a "limited cost trader" (you spend less than 2% of your turnover on relevant goods), you're forced onto the 16.5% flat rate. Most digital agencies fall into this category because their costs are mostly labour and software (services, not goods). At 16.5%, the flat rate scheme saves you almost nothing compared to standard accounting. And it prevents you from reclaiming VAT on your expenses.</p>

<p>Before registering for VAT as a new limited company, speak to your accountant about whether the flat rate scheme actually works for your agency model. For most digital and creative agencies, it doesn't.</p>

<h2>The Bottom Line</h2>

<p>Incorporating your side hustle agency is not an automatic win. For profits under £80k, the tax saving is small or negative once you account for extra fees and your time. For profits above £80k, the arithmetic starts working in your favour, and the non-tax benefits (limited liability, reinvestment, exit readiness) become real advantages.</p>

<p>If your agency is turning over £40k-£60k and you're not planning to grow aggressively, stay as a sole trader. Save the complexity for later. If you're at £80k+ and thinking about hiring, investing, or eventually selling, incorporation is the right move.</p>

<p>The worst decision is incorporating too early and creating unnecessary admin and cost. The second worst is incorporating too late and missing out on years of tax-efficient reinvestment and the BADR clock.</p>

<p>If your profit is above £60k and you're unsure which path fits your situation, speak to an accountant who works with agency founders. The decision is specific to your numbers, your growth plans, and your risk tolerance. A good accountant will walk you through the scenarios with your actual figures, not generic thresholds.</p>
