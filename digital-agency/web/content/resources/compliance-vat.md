<p>UK VAT-registered businesses must account for VAT on their supplies. Most businesses use standard VAT accounting: they charge VAT at 20 per cent, reclaim input VAT on purchases, and pay the net difference to HMRC. Digital agencies can alternatively apply to use the Flat Rate Scheme (FRS), which simplifies administration but does not always produce a lower VAT bill.</p>

<p>This guide explains how both schemes work, when the limited cost trader (LCT) test applies to agencies, and how to use the VAT scheme comparison model to determine which approach produces a better outcome for your agency.</p>

<h2 id="standard-vat-accounting">Standard VAT accounting</h2>

<p>Under standard accounting, a VAT-registered agency:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Charges clients 20 per cent VAT on taxable supplies (typically design, development and consultancy services).</li>
  <li>Reclaims input VAT on business purchases (software subscriptions, equipment, professional services) by recording it on VAT returns.</li>
  <li>Pays HMRC the difference between output VAT charged and input VAT reclaimed.</li>
</ul>

<p>For an agency with £180,000 in net fee income and £8,000 in VAT-recoverable inputs, standard VAT produces a net VAT payment of £28,000 per year (£36,000 output minus £8,000 input).</p>

<h2 id="flat-rate-scheme">The Flat Rate Scheme</h2>

<p>Under the FRS, an agency charges clients 20 per cent VAT as normal but pays HMRC a fixed percentage of its gross (VAT-inclusive) turnover instead of the input/output difference. The percentage varies by business type; the standard rate for advertising and marketing businesses is 11 per cent.</p>

<p>However, for most digital agencies the relevant rate is not 11 per cent but either 12.5 per cent or 16.5 per cent, depending on the limited cost trader test.</p>

<h2 id="limited-cost-trader">The limited cost trader test</h2>

<p>HMRC introduced the limited cost trader (LCT) rate to prevent service businesses with low goods purchases from gaining an excessive FRS advantage. An agency is a limited cost trader in any VAT period where its expenditure on goods (excluding food, fuel, vehicles, and capital assets) is either:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Less than 2 per cent of its gross (VAT-inclusive) turnover, or</li>
  <li>Less than £1,000 per year (£250 per quarter).</li>
</ul>

<p>As a reminder, VAT registration is compulsory once taxable turnover exceeds £90,000 in any rolling 12 months, and the deregistration threshold is £88,000.</p>

<p>Most digital agencies, which provide services rather than selling physical products, have very low goods spend relative to turnover. They typically qualify as limited cost traders and pay the LCT flat rate of 16.5 per cent rather than the sector rate.</p>

<p>At 16.5 per cent of gross turnover, the FRS typically produces a higher VAT bill than standard accounting for agencies with reasonable input VAT to reclaim. The Excel model calculates the LCT test automatically based on your goods spend and shows the resulting flat rate.</p>

<h2 id="comparison-example">Comparison example</h2>

<p>Agency with £180,000 in net fee income and £8,000 in VAT-recoverable inputs, goods spend £500 per year (within LCT thresholds):</p>

<ul>
  <li>Standard VAT: output £36,000, inputs reclaimed £8,000, net payment £28,000.</li>
  <li>FRS (LCT rate 16.5 per cent): gross turnover £216,000, flat rate payment £35,640.</li>
  <li>Difference: FRS costs £7,640 more per year.</li>
  <li>Best scheme: Standard VAT.</li>
</ul>

<p>An agency with higher goods spend that brings it below the LCT threshold would use the standard FRS rate of 12.5 per cent. At that rate the comparison changes:</p>

<ul>
  <li>FRS (12.5 per cent) on £300,000 gross turnover: flat rate payment £45,000.</li>
  <li>Standard VAT on the same business with £10,000 recoverable inputs: net payment £50,000.</li>
  <li>Difference: FRS saves £5,000 per year.</li>
  <li>Best scheme: Flat Rate.</li>
</ul>

<h2 id="who-benefits">Who benefits from the FRS</h2>

<p>The FRS tends to benefit agencies where:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Goods spend is high enough to fall below the LCT threshold (above 2 per cent of gross turnover or above £1,000 per year).</li>
  <li>Input VAT on services (software, subscriptions) is low relative to fee income.</li>
  <li>Administrative simplicity is valuable: the FRS requires no input VAT tracking.</li>
</ul>

<p>It tends to work against agencies where:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Goods spend is low (triggering the 16.5 per cent LCT rate).</li>
  <li>Software and SaaS costs are high and carry recoverable input VAT.</li>
  <li>The agency makes significant capital purchases that carry reclaimable VAT.</li>
</ul>

<h2 id="joining-and-leaving">Joining and leaving the FRS</h2>

<p>An agency can apply to join the FRS if its expected taxable turnover in the next 12 months does not exceed £150,000 (excluding VAT). It must leave if turnover exceeds £230,000 (excluding VAT).</p>

<p>When joining the FRS, the agency can no longer reclaim input VAT on individual purchases (except in specific circumstances, such as a single capital purchase exceeding £2,000). This is an important consideration for an agency planning significant equipment or software investment in the near term.</p>

<h2 id="using-the-model">Using the Excel model</h2>

<p>The VAT scheme comparison model accepts three inputs: your annual net fee turnover, the estimated annual VAT-recoverable inputs under standard accounting, and your estimated annual goods spend. It applies the LCT test to determine whether the 12.5 per cent or 16.5 per cent flat rate applies, calculates the VAT payment under both schemes and identifies the better option and annual saving.</p>

<p>The model covers the standard single-principal scenario. It does not model group VAT registrations, partial exemption, the capital goods scheme or overseas supplies (which may be outside the scope of UK VAT). All formulas are compatible with LibreOffice Calc.</p>

<h2 id="limitations">Limitations and when to take advice</h2>

<p>The VAT position for digital agencies supplying international clients (particularly B2B services to EU-based businesses post-Brexit) is more complex than the domestic comparison covered here. The place of supply rules, the VAT treatment of digital services, and any obligations in jurisdictions where your clients are based may all affect the correct VAT accounting treatment.</p>

<p>If your agency has significant international revenue, supplies mixed taxable and exempt services, or is considering joining or leaving the FRS around a period of significant investment, take specialist advice before changing your VAT accounting method. An error in scheme selection can result in arrears or penalties.</p>
