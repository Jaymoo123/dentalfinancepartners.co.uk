<p>The salary-and-dividend combination is the default pay structure for owner-directors of UK limited companies. For agency founders it typically produces a lower combined tax burden than an equivalent salary because corporation tax rates are generally lower than the combined income tax and National Insurance (NI) that would apply to the same cash drawn as employment income.</p>

<p>This guide explains how the structure works for 2026/27, what has changed since prior years, and how to model the optimal split for your situation using the accompanying Excel tool.</p>

<h2 id="how-the-structure-works">How the structure works</h2>

<p>A UK owner-director pays themselves a modest salary (often at or just above the NI secondary threshold) and then takes any additional income as dividends from retained post-tax profit. The sequence is:</p>

<ol>
  <li>The agency earns revenue and incurs costs. The remaining profit is subject to corporation tax.</li>
  <li>After corporation tax, retained profits can be distributed as dividends.</li>
  <li>The director pays dividend tax on dividends received above the dividend allowance, at rates below the equivalent income tax rates on salary.</li>
</ol>

<p>The structural advantage is that dividends are not subject to National Insurance. For higher earners, this makes a meaningful difference to the effective rate on each additional pound extracted.</p>

<h2 id="2026-27-rates">2026/27 rates</h2>

<p>The following rates apply from 6 April 2026 under the Finance Act 2026:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Dividend allowance: £500 (tax-free)</li>
  <li>Dividend basic rate: 10.75 per cent (on dividends falling in the basic rate band)</li>
  <li>Dividend higher rate: 35.75 per cent (on dividends above £50,270)</li>
  <li>Dividend additional rate: 39.35 per cent (on dividends above £125,140)</li>
  <li>Personal allowance: £12,570</li>
  <li>Employer NIC: 15 per cent above the secondary threshold of £5,000 (from 6 April 2025)</li>
  <li>Employee NIC basic rate: 8 per cent between £12,570 and £50,270</li>
</ul>

<p>Note: dividend rates increased slightly from 2025/26 to 2026/27. If you have been using figures from a prior guide, refresh your model with the rates above.</p>

<h2 id="corporation-tax">Corporation tax in 2026/27</h2>

<p>Corporation tax uses a banded structure based on annual profit:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Small profits rate: 19 per cent on profits up to £50,000</li>
  <li>Marginal relief: a tapered rate applies between £50,000 and £250,000</li>
  <li>Main rate: 25 per cent on profits above £250,000</li>
</ul>

<p>For agency founders whose companies have profits in the marginal relief range, the effective corporation tax rate sits between 19 per cent and 25 per cent. The model calculates this correctly by applying the marginal relief fraction.</p>

<h2 id="optimal-salary">Setting the optimal salary</h2>

<p>The conventional starting point is to pay a salary at the NI secondary threshold (£5,000 in 2026/27), which keeps the salary below the point at which employer NIC is due. This preserves a qualifying year for State Pension without triggering employer NIC costs.</p>

<p>For many agency founders, a salary of £12,570 (the personal allowance) is better because it stays within the employee NIC primary threshold, so no employee NIC is due, and the corporation tax deduction on the salary reduces the company profit subject to corporation tax at a higher effective rate than the income tax saved.</p>

<p>If the company qualifies for the Employment Allowance (£10,500 in 2026/27), it may be worth taking a higher salary, up to £60,000 or more, because the Employment Allowance offsets what would otherwise be an employer NIC liability. Single-director companies do not qualify for the Employment Allowance unless a second director or employee is on the payroll.</p>

<h2 id="employment-allowance">Employment Allowance for agencies</h2>

<p>The Employment Allowance allows eligible employers to reduce their employer NIC bill by up to £10,500 per tax year. Most digital agencies with employees beyond the founder will qualify. However, companies where the sole employee is also the sole director are excluded.</p>

<p>If your agency has one or more staff in addition to the founder on PAYE, you can typically claim the Employment Allowance. This changes the optimal salary calculation significantly: the Excel model accepts a yes/no input on Employment Allowance to model both scenarios.</p>

<h2 id="dividend-tax">Dividend tax: worked example</h2>

<p>Assume an agency with £120,000 of pre-director profit, director salary set at £12,570 (no Employment Allowance):</p>

<ul>
  <li>Employer NIC on salary: approximately £1,136 (15 per cent on salary above £5,000)</li>
  <li>Profit after salary and NIC: approximately £106,294</li>
  <li>Corporation tax (banded): approximately £24,418</li>
  <li>Post-tax profit available for dividend: approximately £81,876</li>
  <li>Dividend tax (above the £500 allowance): approximately £19,667</li>
  <li>Net cash to the director: approximately £74,779</li>
</ul>

<h2 id="timing">Timing dividends across tax years</h2>

<p>Dividends are taxable in the tax year they are received. If your agency generates profit unevenly, it may be worth timing dividends to fall across two tax years to keep total dividend income within the basic rate band in each year, avoiding the higher 35.75 per cent rate.</p>

<p>This requires retained profit to accumulate in the company rather than being distributed immediately. The approach works best when the company has consistent profitability and the director has stable personal income needs.</p>
