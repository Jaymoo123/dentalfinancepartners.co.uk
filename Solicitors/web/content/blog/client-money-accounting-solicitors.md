<p>Client money accounting for solicitors is governed by strict SRA Accounts Rules that every law firm must follow. These rules protect client funds and ensure proper segregation between client money and practice funds. Getting this wrong can lead to serious regulatory consequences, including SRA intervention.</p>

<p>This guide covers the essential requirements for client money accounting, from basic segregation principles to detailed record-keeping obligations.</p>

<h2>What Constitutes Client Money</h2>

<p>Client money includes any funds held by your firm on behalf of clients or third parties. The SRA Accounts Rules define this broadly to capture various scenarios where you're holding money that doesn't belong to your practice.</p>

<p>Common examples include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Property purchase deposits</li>
<li>Settlement funds awaiting distribution</li>
<li>Court deposits and bail money</li>
<li>Money held pending completion of transactions</li>
<li>Damages received on behalf of clients</li>
<li>Unpaid professional disbursements funded by clients</li>
</ul>

<p>The key test is whether the money belongs to someone other than your firm. If so, it's likely client money requiring separate accounting treatment.</p>

<h2>SRA Accounts Rules: Core Requirements</h2>

<p>The SRA Accounts Rules establish fundamental principles that every solicitor must understand. These rules apply regardless of your practice structure, whether you're a sole practitioner or large LLP.</p>

<h3>Segregation Requirements</h3>

<p>Client money must be kept separate from your practice funds at all times. This means:</p>
<ul>
<li>Maintaining separate client account bank accounts</li>
<li>Never mixing client money with practice money</li>
<li>Ensuring client accounts are clearly designated as such</li>
<li>Using only approved banking institutions</li>
</ul>

<p>A typical 5-partner conveyancing firm might maintain three separate client accounts: one for general client money, one for property transactions, and one for litigation matters. This segregation helps track different types of client funds.</p>

<h3>Record Keeping Obligations</h3>

<p>Client money accounting solicitors must maintain detailed records showing all client money movements. The SRA requires:</p>
<ul>
<li>A client cash account for each client matter</li>
<li>A client ledger account for each client</li>
<li>A record of all receipts and payments</li>
<li>Reconciliations of client accounts at least every five weeks (Rule 8.3)</li>
<li>Annual reconciliations and reports</li>
</ul>

<p>These records must be kept for at least six years and be available for SRA inspection at any time.</p>

<h2>Trust Accounting Procedures</h2>

<p>Trust accounting forms the backbone of proper client money management. Your procedures should ensure complete transparency and accountability for all client funds.</p>

<h3>Daily Recording Requirements</h3>

<p>Every receipt and payment of client money must be recorded on the day it occurs. This includes:</p>
<ul>
<li>Date and amount of each transaction</li>
<li>Name of the client or matter</li>
<li>Purpose of the receipt or payment</li>
<li>Method of payment (BACS, cheque, etc.)</li>
</ul>

<p>For example, when receiving a £50,000 property deposit, you'd record the client name, property address, date received, paying bank, and the completion date it's being held for.</p>

<h3>The Five-Weekly Reconciliation Process</h3>

<p>SRA Accounts Rule 8.3 requires a reconciliation between your client ledger balances and actual bank balances at least every five weeks, signed off by the COFA or a manager. Many firms run it monthly out of habit, but five weeks is the regulatory backstop, and a monthly routine can drift past it. This process identifies discrepancies early and ensures accuracy.</p>

<p>The reconciliation should show:</p>
<ul>
<li>Total of all client ledger balances</li>
<li>Bank statement balance for each client account</li>
<li>Outstanding lodgements and unpresented cheques</li>
<li>Any unexplained differences requiring investigation</li>
</ul>

<h2>Common Compliance Challenges</h2>

<p>Many firms struggle with specific aspects of client money accounting. Understanding these common pitfalls helps prevent regulatory issues.</p>

<h3>Interest on Client Money</h3>

<p>Under SRA Accounts Rule 7.1 you must account to clients for a fair sum of interest on client money you hold. The 2019 rules removed the old prescriptive formula, so the current approach requires:</p>
<ul>
<li>A written interest policy that defines what your firm treats as fair, by amount and period held</li>
<li>Clear terms of business explaining that policy to clients (a written agreement can vary it)</li>
<li>Regular calculation and payment of accrued interest under the policy</li>
</ul>

<p>A litigation firm holding £25,000 settlement funds for 3 months would need to calculate and pay interest to the client, even if the amount seems small relative to their practice size.</p>

<h3>Minimum Cash Shortage Rules</h3>

<p>The SRA allows minor cash shortages without regulatory action, provided they don't exceed £50 or represent more than 5% of client money held. However, this isn't permission to be careless with client money accounting.</p>

<p>Best practice involves immediate investigation of any shortage, regardless of size, to understand the underlying cause and prevent recurrence.</p>

<h2>Technology and Client Money Management</h2>

<p>Modern practice management systems can streamline client money accounting for solicitors while improving compliance. Key features to consider include:</p>

<ul>
<li>Automated client ledger updates</li>
<li>Built-in reconciliation tools</li>
<li>Interest calculation functionality</li>
<li>SRA-compliant reporting formats</li>
<li>Audit trails for all transactions</li>
</ul>

<p>However, technology doesn't replace the need for proper procedures and regular oversight. A sole practitioner using basic accounting software still needs to understand the underlying rules and perform regular checks.</p>

<p>Start by reviewing your current client money accounting procedures against SRA requirements. Key actions include:</p>

<ul>
<li>Conducting a compliance audit of existing procedures</li>
<li>Training all staff handling client money</li>

<li>Establishing clear escalation procedures for discrepancies</li>
<li>Planning regular compliance reviews</li>
</ul>

<p>Remember that client money accounting isn't just about regulatory compliance—it's fundamental to maintaining client trust and professional integrity. Getting the basics right protects both your clients and your practice.</p>
