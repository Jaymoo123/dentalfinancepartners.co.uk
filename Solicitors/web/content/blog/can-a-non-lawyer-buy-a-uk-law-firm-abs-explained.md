<p><strong>Yes, non-lawyers can buy or own UK law firms via the Alternative Business Structure (ABS) regime.</strong> The Legal Services Act 2007 introduced ABS, opening UK legal services to non-solicitor ownership and investment. The SRA licence process takes 3-6 months, requires Approved Owner and Approved Manager status for those involved, and adds £10,000-£40,000+ in application costs on top of the firm purchase price.</p>

<p>This guide covers who can use the ABS route, how the licence application works, what tax structure makes sense, and the realistic timeline from "deciding to buy" to "completion of a regulated firm under non-lawyer ownership."</p>

<h2>What an Alternative Business Structure (ABS) actually is</h2>

<p>An ABS is an SRA-authorised legal services provider where one or more owners or managers are non-lawyers. Pre-2007, only solicitors (and certain other approved legal professionals) could own or manage UK law firms in England and Wales. The Legal Services Act 2007 created the ABS regime to allow:</p>

<ul>
<li>Non-lawyer investors (private equity, family offices, business angels)</li>
<li>Multi-disciplinary practices (law firms with accountants, financial planners, surveyors as owners)</li>
<li>Corporate ownership structures (insurance companies, banks, retailers owning law firms)</li>
<li>Employee ownership models</li>
<li>External executive management (a non-lawyer CEO running a solicitor-owned firm)</li>
</ul>

<p>The ABS is regulated by the SRA the same way a traditional Recognised Body is — same Accounts Rules, same PII obligations, same conduct standards. The difference is at the ownership and management level.</p>

<h2>Who can use the ABS route</h2>

<h3>Non-lawyer individual investors</h3>

<p>Anyone with the financial standing and clean regulatory record to pass the Approved Owner assessment. Most common scenarios: experienced professionals from adjacent industries (accountancy, financial services, insurance) buying into a law firm; entrepreneurs acquiring a law firm as a platform investment.</p>

<h3>Corporate buyers</h3>

<p>Insurance companies (Saga Personal Injury, BT Law, etc.), retailers (the Co-operative Group's legal services arm), private equity firms (multiple have invested in UK law firms since 2014), and other corporates can own ABSs. The corporate's directors and beneficial owners typically need Approved Owner status.</p>

<h3>Multi-disciplinary practice models</h3>

<p>Accountancy firms adding legal services. Financial planning firms adding wills and probate. Surveyors adding property law. These are common ABS models post-2015.</p>

<h3>Employee ownership structures</h3>

<p>Employees collectively own the firm, often via an Employee Ownership Trust. Increasingly common in the legal sector for succession planning at firms where there's no obvious internal partner buyer.</p>

<h2>What's restricted under ABS</h2>

<p>Not everything is allowed:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Convicted criminals</strong>: typically can't pass Approved Owner / Manager assessment. Spent convictions may be considered case by case</li>
<li><strong>Anyone subject to SRA enforcement</strong>: barred from holding regulatory status until resolved</li>
<li><strong>Anyone whose source of funds can't be evidenced</strong>: anti-money-laundering requirements apply rigorously to ABS applications</li>
<li><strong>Reserved legal activities by non-lawyers</strong>: even within an ABS, the actual practice of reserved legal activities (rights of audience in court, conduct of litigation, certain probate work, certain immigration work) must be done by appropriately qualified individuals</li>
</ul>

<p>An ABS can be owned by non-lawyers but the day-to-day legal work is still performed by solicitors (or other appropriate qualified individuals).</p>

<h2>The ABS licence application process</h2>

<h3>Months 1-2: Pre-application preparation</h3>

<ul>
<li>Engage an ABS specialist solicitor and a legal-sector-specialist accountant</li>
<li>Confirm the proposed ownership structure and identify Approved Owners (anyone holding 10%+ of shares or voting rights)</li>
<li>Identify Approved Managers (anyone exercising material management control)</li>
<li>Compile financial model, source-of-funds documentation, business plan</li>
<li>Prepare regulatory documents: proposed governance structure, conflicts policy, AML procedures, complaints handling</li>
</ul>

<h3>Months 2-3: Application submission</h3>

<ul>
<li>Submit ABS application to the SRA with supporting documentation</li>
<li>Approved Owner / Manager applications for each named individual (separate applications running in parallel)</li>
<li>SRA acknowledges application and assigns a case officer</li>
<li>Initial review questions issued by SRA, typically requiring 2-6 weeks to address</li>
</ul>

<h3>Months 3-5: SRA review</h3>

<ul>
<li>Detailed SRA due diligence on owners, managers, and the firm itself</li>
<li>Background checks (criminal record, regulatory history, financial standing)</li>
<li>Source of funds verification for any capital being invested</li>
<li>Conflict of interest review against other regulated activities of the proposed owners</li>
<li>Interviews with named Approved Manager candidates where the SRA wants face-to-face assessment</li>
<li>Possible site visit to the firm being acquired</li>
</ul>

<h3>Month 5-6: Decision and post-decision work</h3>

<ul>
<li>SRA decision: licence granted, licence granted with conditions, or licence refused</li>
<li>Where granted with conditions, work with SRA on the conditions before completion</li>
<li>Where refused, appeal route to the SDT (Solicitors Disciplinary Tribunal) — rarely used</li>
<li>Where granted clean, schedule deal completion and ABS authorisation simultaneously</li>
</ul>

<h2>Cost breakdown for an ABS application</h2>

<p>For a typical mid-market ABS acquisition (acquiring a £2-5m turnover firm):</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>SRA application fee</strong>: £2,000-£8,000 (scales with firm size)</li>
<li><strong>Approved Owner application fees</strong>: £150-£400 per individual</li>
<li><strong>Approved Manager application fees</strong>: £150-£400 per individual</li>
<li><strong>Legal fees (ABS specialist solicitor)</strong>: £8,000-£25,000 for application drafting, governance structure, conflicts policy</li>
<li><strong>Accountancy fees</strong>: £4,000-£10,000 for financial model, source-of-funds documentation, ongoing compliance setup</li>
<li><strong>Regulatory advisory (compliance consultants if needed)</strong>: £3,000-£8,000</li>
<li><strong>Background check costs</strong>: £200-£500 per individual</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Total realistic ABS application cost: £15,000-£50,000+</strong> depending on complexity. This is on top of the firm purchase price, deal due diligence costs, and ongoing regulatory compliance.</p>

<h2>Tax structure considerations</h2>

<p>The ABS regulatory licence doesn't dictate tax structure. The acquiring entity can be:</p>

<h3>Limited company ABS</h3>

<ul>
<li>Corporation tax at 19-25% on company profit</li>
<li>Dividend tax on distributions (8.75% / 33.75% / 39.35%)</li>
<li>Cleanest for non-lawyer corporate ownership</li>
<li>Goodwill amortisation: 6.5% per year tax relief for goodwill acquired post-1 April 2019</li>
<li>Suits long-term hold strategies and corporate ownership</li>
</ul>

<h3>LLP ABS</h3>

<ul>
<li>Tax-transparent: members on personal tax</li>
<li>Suits non-lawyer individual investors who want the LLP structure (separate legal personality, limited liability) but the LLP profit allocation flexibility</li>
<li>Less common than Ltd-co structure for ABS but does happen for hybrid ownership (some solicitor members, some non-solicitor members)</li>
</ul>

<h3>Partnership ABS</h3>

<ul>
<li>Possible but rare</li>
<li>Unlimited liability for all partners (including non-lawyer investors) makes this unattractive for most non-solicitor capital</li>
</ul>

<p>For most non-lawyer buyers of UK law firms, the Ltd-co ABS structure is the default — it gives liability protection, supports goodwill amortisation tax relief, and is the structure private equity and corporate investors are familiar with from other sectors.</p>

<h2>The deal flow: ABS acquisition timeline</h2>

<p>Working backwards from completion:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Month 0 (target completion)</strong>: deal completes, ABS licence active, regulatory transition</li>
<li><strong>Months -1 to -2</strong>: ABS licence granted, deal financing finalised, client matter novation prep</li>
<li><strong>Months -3 to -5</strong>: SRA ABS application under review, financial due diligence on target firm complete, heads of terms signed</li>
<li><strong>Months -5 to -8</strong>: ABS application submitted, target firm identified, exclusive due diligence period</li>
<li><strong>Months -8 to -12</strong>: ABS specialist solicitor engaged, governance structure agreed, capital structure decided, banking and financing relationships established</li>
</ul>

<p>Realistic deal cycle from "deciding to acquire" to "completed under ABS ownership": 12-18 months for a clean, well-resourced acquisition. Faster is possible but rare.</p>

<h2>Common reasons ABS applications fail or take longer</h2>

<ul>
<li><strong>Source of funds unclear</strong>. Capital from offshore structures or complex investment vehicles attracts extra SRA scrutiny. Document the source comprehensively up front.</li>
<li><strong>Conflicts of interest</strong>. Owner-side conflicts with the firm's client base require disclosure and management. Hidden conflicts surfacing late in the application kill credibility.</li>
<li><strong>Approved Manager candidates with thin legal-sector experience</strong>. SRA prefers managers who understand the regulated environment. A pure financial executive without legal-sector context faces a harder approval.</li>
<li><strong>Governance structure that concentrates control inappropriately</strong>. SRA scrutinises governance to ensure regulatory compliance isn't subordinated to commercial considerations.</li>
<li><strong>Inadequate compliance infrastructure</strong>. Plans for the firm's COFA / COLP roles, AML supervision, and PII arrangements must be credible.</li>
</ul>
