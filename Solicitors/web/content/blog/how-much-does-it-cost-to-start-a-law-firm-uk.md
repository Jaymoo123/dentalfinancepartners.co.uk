<p><strong>Starting a UK law firm in 2025/26 typically costs £15,000 to £35,000 in upfront setup plus 6 to 12 months of working capital.</strong> The realistic first-year cash requirement is £40,000 to £100,000 for a sole practitioner; £80,000 to £250,000 for a small partnership of two or three solicitors. Most of the variance comes from PII premium (driven by practice area), office costs (home-based vs serviced vs leased), and the time it takes to build a billable client base.</p>

<p>This guide breaks down the actual cost lines, what changes between sole-practitioner and partnership setups, and what realistic working capital looks like for the first 12 months.</p>

<h2>The upfront setup costs</h2>

<p>Day 1 to month 1 cash outflows to get the firm operational and SRA-authorised.</p>

<h3>SRA application and authorisation fees</h3>

<ul>
<li><strong>Recognised Body application fee</strong>: £1,540 (2025/26 fee, paid once at application)</li>
<li><strong>Practising certificate fees for each solicitor</strong>: £307 per individual annually, paid via the firm</li>
<li><strong>Compensation Fund contribution</strong>: £75 per solicitor annually</li>
<li><strong>SRA periodical fee</strong>: based on the firm's gross fee income, minimum £338, scaling with turnover</li>
</ul>

<p>For a sole practitioner: £2,260 in year-1 SRA fees. For a two-partner firm: £2,567. These are minor relative to the other start-up costs, but they have to be paid before the firm can take any client work.</p>

<h3>Professional Indemnity Insurance (PII)</h3>

<p>The largest single recurring cost for most new firms. SRA minimum cover is £2m per claim for unincorporated firms (sole practitioner, partnership, LLP) or £3m for incorporated firms, on the SRA Minimum Terms and Conditions from a qualifying insurer.</p>

<p>Indicative 2025/26 premiums for a brand new firm with no claims history:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Sole practitioner, private client / wills / probate focus</strong>: £4,000-£8,000</li>
<li><strong>Sole practitioner, mixed practice</strong>: £5,000-£10,000</li>
<li><strong>Sole practitioner, conveyancing focus</strong>: £8,000-£18,000</li>
<li><strong>Two-partner firm, mixed</strong>: £8,000-£15,000</li>
<li><strong>Two-partner firm, conveyancing-heavy</strong>: £12,000-£25,000</li>
</ul>

<p>Most insurers offer 6-12 month payment plans at modest interest (3-7% APR), spreading the cash flow impact. The October renewal cycle is the standard cadence; firms starting mid-year may need a short-term policy bridging to October.</p>

<h3>IT and practice management</h3>

<ul>
<li><strong>Cloud-based practice management system</strong> (Clio, Leap, MyCase, Actionstep): £50-£150 per user per month, typically £600-£1,800 year-1 for a sole practitioner. Includes time recording, billing, matter management, document management.</li>
<li><strong>Accounting software</strong> (Xero, QuickBooks): £15-£45 per month plus £400-£800 annual accountancy fees for SRA-compliant client account setup. Year-1 total: £600-£1,400.</li>
<li><strong>Computers and basic hardware</strong>: £1,500-£4,000 for laptop, monitor, scanner, printer, secure backup.</li>
<li><strong>Email, document storage, cybersecurity</strong>: Microsoft 365 Business £6-£20 per user per month. Cyber-essentials accreditation if you want to bid for work that requires it: £300-£500 one-off.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Office costs</h3>

<p>Three realistic options:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Home office with serviced-office meeting rooms</strong>: £0 base + £200-£400/month for ad-hoc meeting room hire (Regus, We Work, local serviced providers). Total year 1: £2,400-£4,800. Suits sole practitioners doing mostly digital work; client meetings are infrequent and can be hosted at the client's location or in hired space.</li>
<li><strong>Serviced office (full-time desk)</strong>: £250-£700 per month per person depending on location. Total year 1 for a sole practitioner: £3,000-£8,400. Includes meeting rooms, reception, printing.</li>
<li><strong>Conventional leased office</strong>: £8 to £35 per square foot depending on region (London top end, regional bottom). A 400 sq ft office at £15/sq ft is £6,000 annual rent plus business rates (typically 30-50% of rent), utilities (£1,500-£3,000), insurance, cleaning. Total year-1 cash burn £12,000-£18,000+. Worth it once the firm has 3+ people and meaningful client visit traffic.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Professional setup costs</h3>

<ul>
<li><strong>Accountancy setup</strong>: £500-£1,500 for initial registration, SRA-compliant client account setup, accounting system configuration, year-end planning.</li>
<li><strong>Solicitor (for the partnership / LLP agreement if 2+ partners)</strong>: £1,500-£4,000 for a properly drafted partnership or LLP agreement covering admission, exit, drawings, profit allocation, dispute resolution.</li>
<li><strong>Bank account setup</strong>: typically free, but client account requirements (separate accounts, SRA-compliant signatories) can take 4-8 weeks to set up at the major banks. Start the process at SRA application time.</li>
<li><strong>Website and brand</strong>: £1,500-£8,000 for a credible professional website plus basic brand identity. Cheaper options exist but a credible online presence is now table stakes for client acquisition.</li>
<li><strong>Professional memberships</strong>: Law Society £350 annually, plus any practice-area-specific memberships (Resolution for family, Society of Trust and Estate Practitioners for private client, etc.) typically £100-£400 each.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Working capital — the 6-12 month buffer</h2>

<p>The setup costs above are the easy part. The harder part is the 6-12 months of working capital you need before the firm becomes cash-flow positive.</p>

<h3>Personal living costs</h3>

<p>A sole practitioner solicitor needs to cover their own living costs while the practice ramps. Typical UK living costs for a 5-10 year qualified solicitor: £3,000-£6,000 per month including mortgage / rent, household, basic lifestyle. For 6 months that's £18,000-£36,000; for 12 months £36,000-£72,000.</p>

<p>The wider the gap between solicitor experience level (which sets the expected lifestyle baseline) and immediate fee-earning velocity, the more working capital is needed.</p>

<h3>Cash conversion cycle</h3>

<p>The lag between doing work and getting paid varies by practice area:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Residential conveyancing</strong>: 4-8 weeks per matter (instruction to completion to fee collection). Steady throughput once running.</li>
<li><strong>Wills and probate</strong>: 6-16 weeks for routine matters; longer (6-18 months) for complex estates. Probate fees on application; ongoing administration billed at intervals.</li>
<li><strong>Family law</strong>: 8-24 weeks per matter typical; ongoing instruction may stretch 12-18 months. Mix of private fees (billed monthly) and legal aid (paid in arrears).</li>
<li><strong>Commercial litigation</strong>: 6-24 months from instruction to billing; cash collection lags. Hardest practice area to start in from a cash-flow perspective without significant working capital.</li>
<li><strong>Personal injury (CFA)</strong>: 9-36 months from instruction to settlement / recovery. Cash conversion is slow but each matter has a defined endpoint with a payout. Many PI specialists rely on After The Event insurance and litigation funding to bridge the cash gap.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Operating costs during the ramp</h3>

<ul>
<li>PII monthly instalments (if on payment plan): £400-£1,500</li>
<li>Practice management software: £50-£150 per user</li>
<li>Office costs (if not home-based): £250-£700 per month</li>
<li>Marketing and client acquisition (Google Ads, local SEO, directory listings): £300-£1,500 per month depending on practice area competitiveness</li>
<li>Accountancy retainer: £100-£300 per month</li>
<li>Continuing professional development</li>
</ul>

<p>For a sole practitioner, typical monthly operating cost during the ramp is £1,500-£3,500 excluding personal living costs.</p>

<h2>Realistic total first-year cash requirements</h2>

<h3>Sole practitioner, home-based, private client focus</h3>

<ul>
<li>Upfront setup (SRA, PII year 1, IT, professional setup): £10,000-£18,000</li>
<li>Operating costs months 1-12: £20,000-£35,000</li>
<li>Personal living costs months 1-9 (assumed to be cash-flow positive by month 9): £27,000-£54,000</li>
<li><strong>Total realistic first-year cash requirement: £57,000-£107,000</strong></li>
</ul>

<h3>Sole practitioner, serviced office, conveyancing focus</h3>

<ul>
<li>Upfront setup (higher PII): £15,000-£28,000</li>
<li>Operating costs months 1-12: £25,000-£45,000</li>
<li>Personal living costs months 1-6 (faster cash conversion in conveyancing): £18,000-£36,000</li>
<li><strong>Total realistic first-year cash requirement: £58,000-£109,000</strong></li>
</ul>

<h3>Two-partner mixed firm, leased office</h3>

<ul>
<li>Upfront setup (LLP agreement, larger PII, office fit-out): £25,000-£50,000</li>
<li>Operating costs months 1-12 (rent, utilities, salaries for 1-2 support staff): £60,000-£120,000</li>
<li>Personal living costs months 1-6 across both partners: £36,000-£72,000</li>
<li><strong>Total realistic first-year cash requirement: £121,000-£242,000</strong></li>
</ul>

<h2>How to fund the launch</h2>

<ul>
<li><strong>Personal savings</strong>: most common funding route. Allows full ownership without external pressure but constrains scale.</li>
<li><strong>Family financing</strong>: short to medium-term loan from family, usually unsecured. Document properly to avoid HMRC characterising it as income.</li>
<li><strong>Bank business loan</strong>: harder than acquisition lending for greenfield start-ups but achievable for credible business plans, typically £25,000-£100,000 over 5-7 years. Mainstream banks (Lloyds, NatWest) plus specialist lenders (Allica Bank, Funding Circle) lend in this segment.</li>
<li><strong>Wesleyan / specialist legal-sector lenders</strong>: products designed specifically for solicitors starting practices, often with deferred repayment in the first 6-12 months.</li>
<li><strong>Practice management software financing</strong>: some PMS providers offer 0-2% APR financing on the first-year subscription, reducing upfront cash drag.</li>
</ul>
