<p>Merging two law firms is a significant strategic move. Whether you are combining two high-street conveyancing practices, a niche litigation boutique with a full-service firm, or forming a new LLP from several sole practitioners, the post-merger integration phase is where the deal succeeds or fails.</p>

<p>For solicitors and law firm partners, the financial and regulatory integration is often more complex than the commercial negotiation. The SRA Accounts Rules do not pause for a merger. Client money must remain protected. The COLP and COFA designations must be clear. And the combined firm's accounting systems must produce a single, accurate picture from day one.</p>

<p>This guide covers the practical steps for post-merger integration from a finance and SRA compliance perspective. It is written for equity partners, COFAs, and practice managers who need to know what to do after the signatures are dry.</p>

<h2>Why Post-Merger Integration Matters for Solicitors</h2>

<p>A law firm merger creates a single regulated entity. The SRA treats the merged firm as one practice from the date of completion. That means one set of accounts, one client account, one COLP and COFA, and one set of SRA reporting obligations.</p>

<p>If the two predecessor firms held client money in separate client accounts, those accounts must be consolidated or closed. If each firm had its own accounting software, the data must be migrated. If the firms used different chart of accounts or VAT schemes, those differences must be reconciled.</p>

<p>Failure to integrate properly creates regulatory risk. A COFA who cannot see all client money movements across the merged firm cannot sign off on the five-weekly reconciliation. A COLP who does not know the compliance history of the acquired firm's fee-earners cannot assess risk. And the SRA's annual accountant's report will require a single, auditable set of books.</p>

<p>We see firms where post-merger integration was rushed or delegated to junior staff. The result is often a qualified accountant's report, a referral to the SRA, or a costly remediation project. Getting it right from the start saves time, money, and reputational damage.</p>

<h2>Step 1: Appoint the COLP and COFA for the Merged Firm</h2>

<p>The merged firm must have one COLP and one COFA. These roles cannot be split between predecessor firms. The SRA requires a single individual to hold each role for the authorised body.</p>

<p>If both predecessor firms had their own COLP and COFA, you must decide who will take the roles in the merged entity. The outgoing COLP and COFA should resign their designations with the SRA as part of the merger notification process.</p>

<p>Practical considerations:</p>
<ul>
<li>The COFA must have access to all financial systems and client money records across the merged firm. If the COFA comes from one predecessor firm, they need training on the other firm's systems.</li>
<li>The COLP must understand the risk profile of all fee-earners from both firms. This includes reviewing SRA records, practising certificate histories, and any past regulatory findings.</li>
<li>Both officers should be involved in the integration planning from the start. They need to sign off on the client matter migration plan and the closure or consolidation of old client accounts.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Step 2: Consolidate Client Accounts and Client Matter Migration</h2>

<p>Client matter migration is the most technically demanding part of post-merger integration. Each open matter from both predecessor firms must be transferred to the merged firm's accounting system. The client ledger balances must match exactly. Any discrepancies must be investigated before the migration.</p>

<p>The SRA Accounts Rules require that client money is held in a designated client account. After the merger, the merged firm should open a new client account in its own name. The old client accounts of the predecessor firms should be closed as soon as practical, with all client money transferred to the new account.</p>

<p>Key steps for client matter migration:</p>
<ul>
<li>Run a full client ledger reconciliation for each predecessor firm. This must match the client account bank balance to the total of all client ledger balances.</li>
<li>Identify any residual balances, unallocated deposits, or old cheques that have not cleared. These must be resolved before migration.</li>
<li>Map the chart of accounts from each predecessor system to the merged firm's system. This includes matter types, cost codes, and VAT codes.</li>
<li>Test the migration on a sample of matters before doing a full transfer. Check that opening balances, billed amounts, and receipts are all carried across correctly.</li>
<li>After migration, run a full reconciliation of the new client account. The total of all client ledger balances in the new system must equal the bank balance of the new client account.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Step 3: Align Accounting Policies and VAT Treatment</h2>

<p>Two firms rarely use identical accounting policies. One may recognise work in progress (WIP) on a time-cost basis. The other may use a percentage-of-completion method. One may be VAT-registered on the cash accounting scheme. The other may use the standard accruals scheme.</p>

<p>After the merger, the combined firm must adopt a single set of accounting policies. This is required under FRS 102 (or FRS 105 for smaller firms) and is also necessary for the SRA accountant's report.</p>

<p>Common areas of difference:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>WIP valuation:</strong> Decide whether to use time cost, net realisable value, or a blended approach. The policy must be applied consistently across all matters.</li>
<li><strong>Revenue recognition:</strong> If one firm billed on completion and the other billed monthly, the merged firm needs a single billing policy.</li>
<li><strong>VAT scheme:</strong> If one firm used the cash accounting scheme and the other did not, you may need to leave the cash accounting scheme or apply for a new VAT registration for the merged entity. HMRC's permission is required to change schemes.</li>
<li><strong>Depreciation and fixed assets:</strong> Align useful lives for office equipment, IT, and leasehold improvements.</li>
</ul>

<p>We recommend preparing a post-merger accounting policies manual. This document should be approved by the partnership board and shared with the firm's accountants and auditors.</p>

<h2>Step 4: Merge the Nominal Ledger and Management Accounts</h2>

<p>The nominal ledger (or general ledger) is the backbone of the firm's financial reporting. After the merger, all transactions from both predecessor firms must feed into a single nominal ledger.</p>

<p>If the firms used different accounting software, you have two options:</p>
<ul>
<li>Migrate all data from one system into the other. This is usually the simpler option if one system is more modern or widely used.</li>
<li>Adopt a new system for the merged firm and migrate data from both old systems. This is more work initially but can be better if neither system is ideal.</li>
</ul>

<p>Whichever route you choose, the nominal ledger must include all opening balances from both firms. This includes:</p>
<ul>
<li>Client account balances (as a liability on the balance sheet).</li>
<li>Office account balances (cash, debtors, creditors, accruals).</li>
<li>Partner capital accounts and current accounts.</li>
<li>Fixed assets and depreciation.</li>
<li>Loans and borrowings.</li>
</ul>

<p>Management accounts for the merged firm should be produced monthly from the date of merger. The first set of post-merger management accounts should include a reconciliation showing that the combined balance sheet equals the sum of the two predecessor firms' balance sheets at the merger date.</p>

<h2>Step 5: Notify the SRA and Update Regulatory Records</h2>

<p>The SRA must be notified of the merger. This is done through the SRA's online portal. You will need to update the firm's authorised body details, including:</p>
<ul>
<li>The firm's name and address.</li>
<li>The COLP and COFA designations.</li>
<li>The practising certificate holders (all solicitors in the merged firm).</li>
<li>The firm's PII arrangements (see below).</li>
</ul>

<p>The SRA also requires notification of any material changes to the firm's structure. A merger is a material change. The notification should be made within 28 days of completion.</p>

<p>If the merger creates an ABS (Alternative Business Structure) because non-solicitor owners are involved, additional licensing requirements apply. Most law firm mergers between traditional partnerships or LLPs do not create an ABS, but it is worth checking if the merger brings in external investors.</p>

<h2>Step 6: Review Professional Indemnity Insurance</h2>

<p>PII is a critical consideration in any law firm merger. The merged firm must have PII that covers all the work of both predecessor firms. This typically means taking out a new policy for the merged entity from the date of merger.</p>

<p>The predecessor firms' policies may have run-off cover for work done before the merger. But the merged firm's policy must cover ongoing work and any claims that arise after the merger date.</p>

<p>Key points:</p>
<ul>
<li>Check that the merged firm's PII meets the SRA Minimum Terms and Conditions. The minimum cover is £2m per claim (or £3m for sole practitioners and partnerships, though a merged firm with multiple partners may need the higher limit depending on structure).</li>
<li>Notify the PII insurer of the merger. Some insurers require prior approval before the merger completes.</li>
<li>If the predecessor firms had different renewal dates, you may need to arrange a short-term policy to align the merged firm's renewal date.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Step 7: Integrate Partner Capital and Profit-Sharing Arrangements</h2>

<p>The merger will create a new partnership or LLP agreement. This agreement should specify how partner capital is structured. If the predecessor firms had different capital requirements, those differences must be resolved.</p>

<p>Common issues:</p>
<ul>
<li>One firm required £50,000 capital per equity partner. The other required £100,000. The merged firm needs a single capital policy.</li>
<li>One firm had fixed-share partners with no capital. The other required capital from all partners. The merged firm must decide whether fixed-share partners will contribute capital.</li>
<li>Profit-sharing ratios must be agreed and documented. This is often the most contentious part of the merger negotiation, but it must be finalised before the first post-merger profit allocation.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Step 8: Prepare for the First SRA Accountant's Report</h2>

<p>The merged firm's first full accounting period will end on the date chosen in the new partnership agreement. At that point, the firm must commission an SRA accountant's report from a qualified reporting accountant.</p>

<p>The accountant's report will cover the entire period from the merger date to the accounting year-end. The reporting accountant will need to see:</p>
<ul>
<li>The client account reconciliation at the merger date.</li>
<li>Evidence of the client matter migration and that all balances were transferred correctly.</li>
<li>The merged firm's client account reconciliations for the period.</li>
<li>The nominal ledger and management accounts.</li>
<li>The COFA's annual declaration.</li>
</ul>

<p>If the integration was done properly, the accountant's report should be unqualified. If there are gaps or errors, the report may be qualified, which triggers an SRA referral.</p>

<h2>Common Pitfalls in Post-Merger Integration</h2>

<p>Based on our work with merging law firms, these are the most common issues we see:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Rushing the client matter migration.</strong> Firms that try to migrate all matters in a weekend often find errors months later. Allow at least two weeks for a careful migration with testing.</li>
<li><strong>Ignoring residual balances.</strong> Old client account balances that are not reconciled before migration create a mess that is hard to clean up.</li>
<li><strong>Not updating the COLP and COFA designations promptly.</strong> The SRA expects the merged firm's COLP and COFA to be in place from day one.</li>
<li><strong>Using different accounting policies for different parts of the firm.</strong> This makes the management accounts unreliable and the accountant's report difficult.</li>
<li><strong>Forgetting about PII run-off.</strong> The predecessor firms' policies may need run-off cover for pre-merger work. Check with your broker.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Final Thoughts</h2>

<p>Post-merger integration is not glamorous, but it is essential. A well-integrated firm runs smoothly, meets its SRA obligations, and can focus on growth. A poorly integrated firm spends months or years fixing problems that should have been resolved in the first few weeks.</p>
