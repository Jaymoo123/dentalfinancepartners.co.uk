<p><strong>UK conveyancing solicitor fees in 2025/26 typically range from £600 for a sale to £2,000 for a complex purchase</strong> — plus disbursements of £300-£1,000 (searches, Land Registry, SDLT submissions) and the buyer's actual Stamp Duty Land Tax liability where applicable. The professional fee variance reflects transaction complexity: freehold vs leasehold, residential vs buy-to-let, mortgage vs cash, chain vs no chain.</p>

<p>This guide gives realistic fee ranges by transaction type, what the disbursements actually cover, and where buyers and sellers can expect to find variance.</p>

<h2>Conveyancing solicitor fees by transaction type — 2025/26</h2>

<h3>Residential sale (freehold)</h3>

<ul>
<li><strong>Property value under £250,000</strong>: £600-£900 professional fee + VAT</li>
<li><strong>£250,000-£500,000</strong>: £700-£1,200 professional fee + VAT</li>
<li><strong>£500,000-£1,000,000</strong>: £900-£1,600 professional fee + VAT</li>
<li><strong>£1m-£2m</strong>: £1,400-£2,500 professional fee + VAT</li>
<li><strong>Over £2m</strong>: bespoke quote, typically £1,800-£5,000+ professional fee + VAT</li>
</ul>

<h3>Residential purchase (freehold)</h3>

<ul>
<li><strong>Property value under £250,000</strong>: £700-£1,000 professional fee + VAT</li>
<li><strong>£250,000-£500,000</strong>: £800-£1,400 professional fee + VAT</li>
<li><strong>£500,000-£1,000,000</strong>: £1,000-£1,800 professional fee + VAT</li>
<li><strong>£1m-£2m</strong>: £1,500-£2,800 professional fee + VAT</li>
<li><strong>Over £2m</strong>: bespoke quote, typically £2,000-£6,000+ professional fee + VAT</li>
</ul>

<h3>Residential sale (leasehold)</h3>

<p>Typically £150-£400 higher than the equivalent freehold sale fee. Additional work includes management pack review, leaseholder enquiries, ground rent / service charge confirmation, and lease length review.</p>

<h3>Residential purchase (leasehold)</h3>

<p>Typically £200-£600 higher than the equivalent freehold purchase fee. Additional work includes detailed lease review, management pack review, leaseholder consent requirements, and explaining the leasehold position to the buyer.</p>

<h3>Buy-to-let purchase</h3>

<p>Typically £100-£300 higher than the equivalent owner-occupier purchase. Additional work includes higher-rate SDLT calculation (5% additional dwelling surcharge applies post-31 October 2024), buy-to-let mortgage processing, and any consent requirements on a leasehold property.</p>

<h3>Help to Buy / Shared Ownership</h3>

<p>Typically £200-£500 higher than the standard fee. Additional work includes interfacing with the housing association / shared ownership provider, drafting / reviewing shared ownership lease, and the bespoke completion logistics.</p>

<h3>Commercial conveyancing</h3>

<p>Always quoted bespoke based on transaction complexity. Indicative ranges:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Small commercial sale or purchase (under £500k)</strong>: £1,500-£4,000 professional fee</li>
<li><strong>Mid-market commercial (£500k-£5m)</strong>: £3,000-£12,000 professional fee</li>
<li><strong>Larger commercial transactions</strong>: bespoke, often £10,000-£50,000+ professional fee</li>
</ul>

<h2>Disbursement breakdown on a typical residential purchase</h2>

<p>Disbursements are third-party costs the solicitor pays on the client's behalf. For a £350,000 residential freehold purchase in 2025/26:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Land Registry official copies</strong>: £3-£12 (typically £6 for register + plan)</li>
<li><strong>Land Registry search (OS1)</strong>: £3</li>
<li><strong>Bankruptcy search (K15/K16)</strong>: £2-£6</li>
<li><strong>Local Authority search</strong>: £75-£200 (varies by council; some London boroughs at the high end)</li>
<li><strong>Drainage and water search</strong>: £40-£80</li>
<li><strong>Environmental search</strong>: £40-£100</li>
<li><strong>Chancel repair search</strong> (where required): £20-£40</li>
<li><strong>Anti-money-laundering electronic identity check</strong>: £10-£30 per individual</li>
<li><strong>Land Registry application fee for transfer</strong>: £100 (for £100k-£200k) up to £455 (for £1m+)</li>
<li><strong>SDLT submission fee</strong>: usually no separate charge; SDLT is the buyer's tax payment, not a disbursement</li>
</ul>

<p>Total disbursements for a £350,000 residential purchase typically £270-£550 excluding the SDLT payment itself.</p>

<h2>SDLT: the buyer's actual tax</h2>

<p>SDLT is not a solicitor fee — it's the buyer's stamp duty payment to HMRC, which the solicitor calculates and submits as part of the transaction. The amount depends on the property price and whether the buyer is a first-time buyer, has an additional property, or is a non-UK resident:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Standard SDLT rates (England + NI, residential)</strong>: 0% on the first £125,000, 2% on £125,001-£250,000, 5% on £250,001-£925,000, 10% on £925,001-£1.5m, 12% above £1.5m</li>
<li><strong>First-time buyer relief</strong>: 0% on first £300,000 if property value under £500,000</li>
<li><strong>Additional dwelling surcharge</strong>: +5% (raised from 3% on 31 October 2024) on each SDLT band where the buyer is acquiring an additional property</li>
<li><strong>Non-UK resident surcharge</strong>: +2% on each SDLT band for non-UK resident buyers</li>
<li><strong>Wales</strong>: Land Transaction Tax (LTT) with different rates and bands</li>
<li><strong>Scotland</strong>: Land and Buildings Transaction Tax (LBTT) with different rates and bands</li>
</ul>

<p>On a £350,000 owner-occupier purchase in England, SDLT is currently £5,000. On a £350,000 buy-to-let purchase, SDLT is £22,500 (5% additional dwelling surcharge applied across bands). Material amounts that have nothing to do with the solicitor's professional fee.</p>

<h2>What drives variance in conveyancing fees</h2>

<h3>Firm type</h3>

<ul>
<li><strong>National conveyancing factories</strong> (My Home Move, Homeward Legal, etc.): cheapest, often £400-£800 for routine sales. High volume, automated systems, minimal partner contact. Best for very straightforward transactions.</li>
<li><strong>Local high-street firms</strong>: middle of the range, typically £700-£1,500 for routine residential. More personal service, easier to escalate issues, partner is named on file.</li>
<li><strong>City firms doing residential conveyancing</strong>: top of the range, often £1,500-£3,500 for routine residential plus VAT. Premium service for higher-value transactions where the value of certainty justifies the cost.</li>
<li><strong>Specialist residential conveyancers</strong>: vary but typically positioned between high-street and City — focus on a single practice area allows efficient delivery.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Region</h3>

<ul>
<li>London and South East: top end of the range</li>
<li>Other English regions: middle</li>
<li>Wales, Northern Ireland: typically lower than English equivalents</li>
<li>Scotland: different system (LBTT, Scottish conveyancing process) and different fee structures</li>
</ul>

<h3>Transaction complexity</h3>

<ul>
<li>Cash transactions: simpler, slightly lower fees</li>
<li>Mortgage transactions: standard fees</li>
<li>Help to Buy / Shared Ownership: higher fees</li>
<li>Leasehold: meaningfully higher</li>
<li>New-build (off-plan or pre-completion): higher due to developer-specific requirements</li>
<li>Probate sales: higher due to additional executor work</li>
<li>Repossession sales: higher due to lender requirements</li>
<li>Buy-to-let purchases: slightly higher</li>
</ul>

<h2>How firms cost-set conveyancing fees</h2>

<p>From the firm's side, residential conveyancing is largely a volume business with thin margins. A typical conveyancing fee-earner handles 25-60 matters in progress at any time, billing £700-£1,200 per matter on average. Annual gross fees per fee-earner are typically £150,000-£400,000.</p>

<p>The firm's economics:</p>

<ul>
<li>Fee-earner salary (NQ to mid-level): £35,000-£65,000</li>
<li>Allocated overhead (PII, premises, support, technology): typically 40-60% of salary</li>
<li>Effective per-matter cost: £400-£700</li>
<li>Margin per matter after costs: £100-£400 typical</li>
</ul>

<p>This is why volume matters in conveyancing — single-matter losses are common, and profitability comes from converting at scale. It's also why conveyancing-heavy firms are most exposed to PII claims and SRA scrutiny: high volume of client money flowing through, tight margins per matter, and lower fee-earner experience on average.</p>
