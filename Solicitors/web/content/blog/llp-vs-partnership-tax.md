<p>When you weigh up <strong>LLP vs partnership tax</strong> for a law firm, the headline that surprises most partners is that the two structures are taxed the same way at member level. A traditional (general) partnership and a limited liability partnership (LLP) are both <strong>tax-transparent</strong>, so the differences people expect to find on the tax side are mostly not there. The genuine tax divergence is narrow and specific, and this guide isolates it.</p>

<p>This page focuses on the tax comparison between the two transparent structures. For the wider structural decision (liability, Companies House filing, regulatory authorisation) see our <a href="/blog/partnership-llp-structure/llp-vs-traditional-partnership-uk-law-firms">LLP vs traditional partnership structure comparison</a>. For a single-structure deep dive, see the <a href="/blog/partnership-llp-accounting/law-firm-partnership-tax-guide">law firm partnership tax guide</a> and the <a href="/blog/partnership-llp-accounting/llp-member-taxation-guide-uk-law-firms">LLP member taxation guide</a>.</p>

<h2>Both Structures Are Tax-Transparent</h2>

<p>The starting point, and the reason the two structures share a tax treatment, is transparency. Neither the partnership nor the LLP pays income tax or corporation tax on its trading profit. Instead the profit is allocated to the partners or members, and <strong>each individual is taxed personally on their allocated share</strong> as a self-employed person.</p>

<p>For an LLP this is set by <strong>ITTOIA 2005 section 863</strong>: all the LLP's activities are treated as carried on in partnership by its members, so the LLP is looked through and each member is taxed as a self-employed partner. A general partnership reaches the same place under the long-standing partnership rules. In both cases, profit is allocated under the profit-sharing arrangement (<strong>ITTOIA 2005 section 850</strong>), reported on the partnership return (SA800), and each partner or member carries their share onto the partnership pages of their own self-assessment return.</p>

<p>Because both structures are transparent, the firm-level tax bill is identical: zero on trading profit, with the charge falling on the individuals. The label on the door does not change that.</p>

<h2>You Are Taxed on Your Profit Share, Not Your Drawings</h2>

<p>This rule is the same for both structures and it is the single most common cash-flow trap for partners. You are taxed on your <strong>allocated profit share</strong>, not on the cash you draw. Drawings are simply advances against your share. A partner or member who draws less than their allocation in a year still pays tax on the <strong>full allocation</strong>, with the retained balance sitting in the firm as working capital.</p>

<p>So a partner who leaves profit in the firm to fund growth, or to build up capital, does not defer the tax. The tax follows the allocation, not the withdrawal. Budgeting for self-assessment on the full share, and timing capital decisions around that, matters equally in a traditional partnership and an LLP.</p>

<h2>Income Tax and National Insurance: Identical for Both</h2>

<p>A self-employed partner or member pays income tax on their profit share at the normal rates (2025/26: personal allowance 12,570 pounds, tapered above 100,000 pounds and lost at 125,140 pounds; basic 20% to 50,270 pounds; higher 40% to 125,140 pounds; additional 45% above). The National Insurance position is the same in both structures and is where the stale advice usually lurks.</p>

<p><strong>National Insurance on a partner's or member's profit share (2025/26):</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Class 4 NIC at 6%</strong> on profits between 12,570 pounds and 50,270 pounds, then <strong>2%</strong> above 50,270 pounds.</li>
<li><strong>Class 2 NIC liability was removed from 6 April 2024.</strong> A partner with profits at or above the Small Profits Threshold is treated as having paid and keeps state pension entitlement without paying anything. Those below the threshold can pay Class 2 voluntarily to protect their record.</li>
<li>Paid through self-assessment, with <strong>payments on account</strong> due 31 January and 31 July.</li>
<li><strong>No employer National Insurance</strong> on a genuine partner's or member's profit share in either structure.</li>
</ul>

<p>If you still see "Class 2 at a few pounds a week" quoted for partners, that guidance predates 6 April 2024 and is wrong. There is no weekly Class 2 charge to budget for any more.</p>

<h2>The One Real Tax Difference: Salaried Member Rules</h2>

<p>Here is the genuine divergence, and it runs one way only. The <strong>salaried member rules</strong> (Finance Act 2014, inserting <strong>ITTOIA 2005 sections 863A to 863G</strong>) apply <strong>only to LLP members</strong>. A traditional partnership has <strong>no salaried member equivalent</strong>: a general partner is always taxed as self-employed, full stop.</p>

<p>For an LLP, the rules can treat a member as an <strong>employee for tax purposes</strong> (PAYE on their reward plus employer NIC) if <strong>all three</strong> of the following conditions are met. Crucially, this is not a choice the member or the firm makes; <strong>status depends on the conditions</strong>. Fail any one and the member stays self-employed.</p>

<h3>The Three Conditions (all must be met)</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Condition A, disguised salary (section 863B):</strong> at least <strong>80%</strong> of the member's total reward for their services is "disguised salary" (fixed, or varying without reference to the LLP's overall profits or losses).</li>
<li><strong>Condition B, significant influence (section 863C):</strong> the member does <strong>not</strong> have significant influence over the affairs of the LLP.</li>
<li><strong>Condition C, capital contribution (section 863D):</strong> the member's capital contribution is <strong>less than 25%</strong> of their disguised salary.</li>
</ul>

<p>The rules apply year by year and to all members, but in practice they catch <strong>fixed-share and salaried partners</strong>, not full-equity partners whose reward genuinely varies with firm profits (those fail Condition A). The usual way to keep a member self-employed is to deliberately fail one condition: make more than 20% of reward genuinely profit-dependent (fails A), give the member real influence (fails B), or have them commit capital of at least 25% of disguised salary, genuinely at risk (fails C). Section 863G is the anti-avoidance backstop. For the detail, see our guide to the <a href="/blog/partnership-llp-structure/salaried-member-rules-uk-llps-explained">salaried member rules for UK LLPs</a>.</p>

<p>The practical effect on the comparison: a <strong>fixed-share or salaried partner</strong> can be taxed very differently depending on the structure. In an LLP they may be pulled into PAYE and trigger employer NIC; in a traditional partnership the same person stays self-employed. A <strong>full-equity partner</strong> is taxed identically in both, because the salaried member rules cannot bite on genuinely profit-dependent reward.</p>

<h2>Worked Comparison (Anonymised)</h2>

<p>Take a three-partner firm with 600,000 pounds of allocable profit, split equally so each partner is allocated 200,000 pounds. Whether the firm is a traditional partnership or an LLP, each full-equity partner is taxed the same way: income tax on the 200,000 pound share plus Class 4 NIC (6% on the band to 50,270 pounds, 2% above), through self-assessment, with no employer NIC and no Class 2 charge. The structure makes no difference to these three partners.</p>

<p>Now add a fixed-share partner on a largely fixed 90,000 pound reward, little firm influence and minimal capital. In a traditional partnership that person is still self-employed. In an LLP they are likely to meet all three salaried member conditions, so they fall into <strong>PAYE with employer NIC</strong> on their reward. That is the single point in a typical firm where the <strong>LLP vs partnership tax</strong> outcome actually diverges, and it is driven by the conditions, not by anyone's choice of title.</p>

<h2>What About a Corporate Member?</h2>

<p>Some firms (in either structure, but most often an LLP) admit a <strong>company</strong> as a member so that profit can be retained at the corporation-tax rate. This is a real planning route but a heavily policed one: the <strong>mixed-membership partnership rules</strong> (ITTOIA 2005 sections 850C to 850E) reallocate any profit above the company's genuine commercial return back to the individual partners and tax it on them. A corporate member is not a clean way to halve a partner's tax, and the same rules apply whether the firm is a partnership or an LLP. See our guide on the <a href="/blog/structure-incorporation/corporate-member-llp-law-firm-mixed-membership-rules">corporate member and the mixed-membership rules</a>.</p>

<h2>The 2026 Employer NI Question, Answered Plainly</h2>

<p>A persistent myth says that from 2026 LLPs will pay <strong>employer National Insurance</strong> on members' profit shares. There is <strong>no such general charge</strong>, and Finance Act 2026 did not introduce one. A genuine LLP member is self-employed for NIC and pays Class 4, exactly as a general partner does. The <strong>only</strong> route to an employer NIC cost on a member's reward is the salaried member rules above, where a member is reclassified as an employee.</p>

<p>What did change for 2026 sits on the incorporated side, not the transparent side: the <strong>dividend tax rates rose from 6 April 2026</strong> (ordinary to 10.75%, upper to 35.75%), which affects a firm that operates as a company and extracts profit by salary and dividend. It does not touch a partnership or an LLP taxed transparently. We set out the genuine position in detail in <a href="/blog/partnership-llp-accounting/llp-employer-ni-2026">will LLP members pay employer NI in 2026</a>.</p>

<h2>Administrative and Compliance Differences (Not Tax)</h2>

<p>Where the two structures genuinely diverge is administration and regulation, not member tax. The tax-return mechanics are largely shared, but the LLP carries additional filing.</p>

<h3>Traditional Partnership</h3>
<ul>
<li>Partnership tax return (SA800) plus each partner's self-assessment.</li>
<li>No Companies House accounts filing.</li>
<li>Unlimited joint and several liability among the partners.</li>
</ul>

<h3>LLP</h3>
<ul>
<li>Partnership tax return (SA800) plus each member's self-assessment, the same as a partnership.</li>
<li><strong>Companies House filing</strong> (annual accounts and confirmation statement): a separate legal personality brings public filing obligations.</li>
<li>Limited liability for members (subject to their own negligence and any personal guarantees).</li>
<li><strong>PAYE</strong> only where the salaried member rules catch a member.</li>
</ul>

<p>For most multi-partner law firms the limited-liability protection and separate legal personality are why the LLP is chosen, even though the member tax treatment is the same. Note too that <strong>LLPs and partnerships with a corporate partner are excluded from the cash basis</strong>, so most law firms compute profits on the <strong>accruals basis</strong>, recognising work in progress under FRS 102.</p>

<h2>Basis Period and MTD Apply to Both</h2>

<p>Two compliance changes land on both structures equally. <strong>Basis period reform</strong> moved unincorporated businesses to a <strong>tax-year basis</strong> (profits taxed for the actual tax year) from <strong>2024/25</strong>, with 2023/24 the transition year; firms with a non-March or non-April year end may carry transition profits spread over five tax years. <strong>Making Tax Digital for Income Tax</strong> applies from <strong>6 April 2026</strong> to individuals with qualifying income over 50,000 pounds (then 30,000 pounds from 2027, 20,000 pounds from 2028), so most full-time partners and members are in scope, in either structure.</p>

<h2>Which Structure Is More Tax-Efficient?</h2>

<p>For the member tax bill itself, neither structure is more efficient, because both are transparent and the income tax and Class 4 NIC treatment is identical. The structure choice is therefore driven by <strong>liability protection, regulatory standing and filing</strong>, not by a member-level tax saving.</p>

<p>The one place the structures genuinely diverge on tax is the <strong>fixed-share or salaried partner</strong>: an LLP can pull that person into PAYE and employer NIC through the salaried member rules, while a traditional partnership cannot. If your firm relies heavily on fixed-share roles, model the salaried member conditions carefully before settling the structure, and remember the outcome is decided by the three conditions, not by choice.</p>

<h2>Professional Advice and Planning</h2>

<p>Given that the real <strong>LLP vs partnership tax</strong> divergence is narrow but consequential, and that the salaried member and mixed-membership rules are condition-driven and anti-avoidance-policed, it is worth modelling your specific membership before committing. Consider specialist legal-sector accountants who understand both the tax position and your <a href="/blog/sra-compliance-trust-accounting/sra-accounts-rules-compliance-guide">SRA compliance</a> obligations.</p>

<p>Review the structure as the firm grows and the membership mix changes. What suits a small full-equity team may not suit a firm building a layer of fixed-share partners, precisely because that is where the salaried member rules engage.</p>
