<p>Solicitor client account reconciliation is a critical monthly process that ensures your firm protects client money and maintains SRA compliance. Every solicitor handling client funds must perform regular reconciliations to verify that client money balances match bank records and accounting systems.</p>

<p>This process goes beyond basic bookkeeping – it's your primary safeguard against client money shortfalls and regulatory breaches that can result in SRA intervention or disciplinary action.</p>

<h2>What is Solicitor Client Account Reconciliation?</h2>

<p>Client account reconciliation involves comparing three key records to ensure they align:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Bank statements</strong> for all client accounts</li>
<li><strong>Client ledger balances</strong> from your accounting system</li>
<li><strong>List of client liabilities</strong> (money owed to each client)</li>
</ul>

<p>The total of individual client balances must equal the total client money held in your client accounts. Any discrepancy indicates potential compliance issues or accounting errors that require immediate investigation.</p>

<h2>SRA Requirements for Client Account Reconciliation</h2>

<p>Under the SRA Accounts Rules 2019, firms must complete solicitor client account reconciliation at least every five weeks. However, most practices perform monthly reconciliations for better cash flow management and earlier error detection.</p>

<p>Key compliance requirements include:</p>

<ul>
<li>Reconciliations must be completed by a qualified person (typically a solicitor, COFA, or qualified accountant)</li>
<li>All discrepancies must be investigated and resolved promptly</li>
<li>Written records of each reconciliation must be maintained</li>
<li>Any client money shortfalls must be rectified immediately</li>
</ul>

<p>The <a href="/sra-compliance">SRA compliance framework</a> requires firms to demonstrate robust systems for protecting client money, making regular reconciliation essential.</p>

<h2>Step-by-Step Client Account Reconciliation Process</h2>

<h3>1. Gather Required Records</h3>

<p>Collect bank statements for all client accounts up to the reconciliation date. Include deposit accounts and any client money held in separate designated accounts for specific matters.</p>

<h3>2. Extract Client Ledger Balances</h3>

<p>Run a client balance report from your accounting system showing the balance for each client at the reconciliation date. This should include both office account and client account balances.</p>

<h3>3. Calculate Total Client Liabilities</h3>

<p>Sum all positive client ledger balances (money owed to clients). Negative balances typically represent unpaid bills or advances from office account funds.</p>

<h3>4. Reconcile Bank Balances</h3>

<p>Compare your accounting system's client account balance with the actual bank balance, adjusting for outstanding cheques and deposits in transit.</p>

<h3>5. Perform the Three-Way Match</h3>

<p>Verify that:</p>
<ul>
<li>Total client account bank balances (adjusted)</li>
<li>Total client account balance per accounting system</li>
<li>Total client liabilities</li>
</ul>

<p>All three figures must match. Any variance requires investigation.</p>

<h2>Common Client Account Reconciliation Issues</h2>

<h3>Unallocated Receipts</h3>

<p>Money received but not allocated to specific clients creates reconciliation problems. Common causes include unclear payment references or delays in posting receipts to client ledgers.</p>

<h3>Outstanding Cheques</h3>

<p>Cheques issued but not yet presented for payment affect bank balances but not ledger balances. Maintain a schedule of outstanding cheques and investigate any aged items.</p>

<h3>Bank Charges and Interest</h3>

<p>Bank charges on client accounts must be reimbursed from office account funds. Interest earned on client money belongs to clients unless properly disclaimed.</p>

<h3>Inter-Client Transfers</h3>

<p>Transfers between different client matters require careful recording to maintain accurate individual client balances while preserving overall client account totals.</p>

<h2>Technology and Client Account Management</h2>

<p>Modern legal practice management software automates much of the solicitor client account reconciliation process. Cloud-based systems can import bank feeds directly, reducing manual data entry and reconciliation time.</p>

<p>Key features to look for include:</p>

<ul>
<li>Automated bank feed integration</li>
<li>Three-way reconciliation reporting</li>
<li>Client balance aging reports</li>
<li>SRA-compliant audit trails</li>
</ul>

<p>However, technology doesn't replace the need for qualified oversight. Someone with appropriate knowledge must review and approve each reconciliation.</p>

<h2>Resolving Reconciliation Discrepancies</h2>

<p>When your solicitor client account reconciliation reveals discrepancies, investigate systematically:</p>

<h3>Check Recent Transactions</h3>

<p>Review transactions near the reconciliation date for timing differences or posting errors. Bank deposits made late on the last day may not appear on statements until the following period.</p>

<h3>Verify Transaction Coding</h3>

<p>Ensure transactions are correctly allocated between client and office accounts. Misposted items create artificial discrepancies that mask real issues.</p>

<h3>Review Client Transfers</h3>

<p>Inter-client transfers require equal and opposite entries. Missing or unmatched transfers commonly cause reconciliation problems.</p>

<h3>Investigate Client Money Shortfalls</h3>

<p>If total client liabilities exceed available client funds, you have a shortfall requiring immediate correction. This typically involves transferring money from office account to client account.</p>

<h2>Best Practices for Effective Reconciliation</h2>

<p>Implement these practices to streamline your reconciliation process:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Reconcile monthly</strong> rather than waiting for the five-week deadline</li>
<li><strong>Maintain clear procedures</strong> with documented steps for consistency</li>
<li><strong>Segregate duties</strong> between transaction processing and reconciliation approval</li>
<li><strong>Archive supporting documentation</strong> for each reconciliation</li>
<li><strong>Train backup staff</strong> to ensure continuity during holidays or staff changes</li>
</ul>

<p>Regular reconciliation helps identify issues early, reducing compliance risks and improving cash flow management. Many firms find monthly reconciliation actually saves time compared to less frequent, more complex exercises.</p>

<h2>Record Keeping and Documentation</h2>

<p>The SRA requires firms to maintain comprehensive records of all client account reconciliations. Essential documentation includes:</p>

<ul>
<li>Signed reconciliation worksheets</li>
<li>Supporting bank statements</li>
<li>Client balance reports</li>
<li>Investigation notes for any discrepancies</li>
<li>Correcting journal entries</li>
</ul>

<p>Retain these records for at least six years, as required for SRA inspections and compliance reviews.</p>
