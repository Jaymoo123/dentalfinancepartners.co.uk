<p>SRA accounts rules compliance forms the backbone of trust accounting for UK law firms. Every solicitor handling client money must understand and implement these regulations to maintain their practising certificate and protect both clients and the firm from regulatory action.</p>

<p>The SRA Accounts Rules 2019 replaced the previous 2011 rules, introducing significant changes to how firms manage client money and maintain accounting records. Understanding these requirements isn't optional—it's fundamental to legal practice in England and Wales.</p>

<h2>Understanding the SRA Accounts Rules Framework</h2>

<p>The SRA accounts rules compliance framework centres on protecting client money through strict segregation and accounting requirements. Client money includes advance payments for costs, money held as stakeholder, and settlement funds pending completion.</p>

<p>The rules apply to all authorised bodies, including sole practitioners, partnerships, and LLPs. Whether you handle £1,000 or £1 million in client money, the compliance requirements remain the same.</p>

<p>Key principles include maintaining client money in designated client accounts, keeping detailed accounting records, and ensuring prompt delivery of money to clients when due.</p>

<h2>Client Money Handling Requirements</h2>

<p>Proper client money handling sits at the heart of SRA accounts rules compliance. Client money must be paid into a client account at a bank or building society in England and Wales (or other approved jurisdiction).</p>

<p>You cannot use client money for office expenses, VAT payments, or business costs—even temporarily. Mixed payments require immediate allocation between client and business elements, typically within 14 days of receipt.</p>

<p>Common client money scenarios include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Completion funds held pending property transactions</li>
<li>Court damages received on behalf of clients</li>
<li>Advance payments for future legal costs</li>
<li>Money held as executor or trustee</li>
</ul>

<h2>Accounting Records and Documentation</h2>

<p>SRA accounts rules compliance demands meticulous record-keeping. You must maintain a cash book showing all client account transactions, with separate records for each client matter.</p>

<p>Client ledgers must show the balance held for each client, updated promptly after each transaction. Most firms use legal accounting software, but manual systems remain acceptable if they meet SRA requirements.</p>

<p>Bank reconciliations must be completed at least every 5 weeks, comparing your records against bank statements. Any discrepancies require immediate investigation and correction.</p>

<h3>Required Documentation</h3>

<p>Essential records for SRA accounts rules compliance include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Client cash book with running balance</li>
<li>Individual client matter ledgers</li>
<li>Bank statements for all client accounts</li>
<li>Copy bank reconciliation statements</li>
<li>Supporting documents for all transactions</li>
</ul>

<h2>Regular Reconciliation and Monitoring</h2>

<p>Effective monitoring prevents minor issues becoming major compliance breaches. The comparison date (typically month-end) reconciliation compares total client liabilities against client account balances.</p>

<p>This reconciliation often reveals timing differences, such as cheques issued but not yet cleared. However, any unexplained shortage in client accounts constitutes a serious breach requiring immediate rectification.</p>

<p>Many firms perform weekly reconciliations to identify problems early. This approach, while exceeding minimum requirements, significantly reduces compliance risks.</p>

<h2>Common Compliance Issues</h2>

<p>Several areas frequently cause SRA accounts rules compliance problems. Round sum withdrawals without proper authorisation remain a persistent issue, particularly in smaller firms without robust controls.</p>

<p>Improper use of client money represents another common breach. Using client funds to pay disbursements before receiving proper authority violates the rules, even if reimbursement follows quickly.</p>

<p>Poor record-keeping causes many compliance failures. Missing bank statements, inadequate transaction descriptions, or delayed posting of transactions can all trigger SRA intervention.</p>

<h3>High-Risk Areas</h3>

<p>Particular attention should focus on:</p>
<ul>
<li>Property transactions with tight completion deadlines</li>
<li>Personal injury settlements with complex payment structures</li>
<li>Probate matters involving multiple beneficiaries</li>
<li>Commercial transactions with staging payments</li>
</ul>

<h2>Technology and Compliance Systems</h2>

<p>Modern legal accounting software significantly simplifies SRA accounts rules compliance. These systems automatically maintain separate ledgers, prevent unauthorised transfers, and generate required reconciliation reports.</p>

<p>However, technology doesn't eliminate the need for understanding the underlying rules. Partners and COFAs must still review reconciliations, investigate discrepancies, and ensure proper client money handling.</p>

<p>Cloud-based systems offer particular advantages for remote working, provided they maintain appropriate security standards and backup procedures.</p>

<h2>COFA Responsibilities</h2>

<p>The Compliance Officer for Finance and Administration bears ultimate responsibility for SRA accounts rules compliance. COFAs must ensure systems operate effectively and identify compliance risks before they become breaches.</p>

<p>Regular compliance monitoring should include reviewing reconciliations, checking authorisation procedures, and ensuring staff understand client money handling requirements.</p>

<p>COFAs must report serious compliance issues to the SRA promptly. Attempting to hide problems typically results in more severe regulatory consequences than early disclosure and remediation.</p>

<h2>Remediation and Breach Management</h2>

<p>When compliance breaches occur, prompt action minimises regulatory consequences. Minor technical breaches often require simple system improvements, while serious shortages may trigger forensic investigation.</p>

<p>Document all remedial actions taken, including system changes, staff training, and enhanced monitoring procedures. The SRA expects firms to learn from mistakes and implement preventive measures.</p>

<p>For complex compliance issues, seek specialist advice immediately. <a href="/services">Professional guidance</a> can help navigate regulatory requirements and develop appropriate response strategies.</p>

<h2>Training and Staff Development</h2>

<p>Effective SRA accounts rules compliance requires ongoing staff training. All personnel handling client money must understand the rules, regardless of their seniority or experience.</p>

<p>Regular training sessions should cover rule changes, common mistakes, and proper procedures for different transaction types. Document all training provided, as the SRA may request evidence of competence development.</p>

<p>New staff require comprehensive induction training before handling any client money. Supervision arrangements should ensure experienced personnel review their work until competence is established.</p>
