<p>Cash flow is the lifeblood of any law firm. Yet many UK solicitors find themselves in a paradox: the firm is profitable on paper, but the bank balance says otherwise. This is especially common in firms handling litigation, conveyancing, or corporate work where bills are paid months after the work is done.</p>

<p>The good news is that you can improve cash flow without taking on expensive external debt. The strategies below focus on operational discipline, better use of the client account, and smarter profit extraction. They are designed for <strong>law firm</strong> partners, COLPs, and COFAs who want practical, regulator-safe solutions.</p>

<h2>Why Law Firms Face Cash Flow Problems</h2>

<p>Before fixing the problem, it helps to understand why it happens. Most <strong>solicitor</strong> practices operate on a time-lag model. You incur costs (salaries, rent, PII premiums, disbursements) today, but you invoice the client weeks or months later. In litigation, the gap can stretch to years.</p>

<p>Three structural factors create this pressure:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>WIP funding.</strong> Work in progress represents unbilled time and disbursements. Until you bill and collect, that value is locked in your practice.</li>
<li><strong>Client account restrictions.</strong> Money held on client account is not yours. You cannot use it to pay office account bills, no matter how tempting.</li>
<li><strong>Partner drawings.</strong> Partners often take drawings based on anticipated profit, not realised cash. This can drain the office account before bills are paid.</li>
</ul>

<p>Each of these can be managed. The key is to treat cash flow as a separate discipline from profit. A profitable firm can fail if cash is mismanaged.</p>

<h2>1. Use a Cash Flow Forecast to See the Gaps</h2>

<p>Many <strong>law firm</strong> partners run the business on gut feel. They know roughly when the big conveyancing completions fall due, but they do not model the timing of VAT, payroll, and PII premiums. A cash flow forecast changes that.</p>

<p>A good forecast covers at least 12 weeks ahead. It should include:</p>

<ul>
<li>Expected fee income (by matter type and expected billing date)</li>
<li>Fixed outgoings (rent, salaries, software subscriptions)</li>
<li>Variable costs (disbursements, counsel fees, search fees)</li>
<li>Tax payments (VAT, corporation tax, partner self-assessment)</li>
<li>Partner drawings and capital repayments</li>
</ul>

<p>You do not need complex software. A simple spreadsheet updated weekly works well for most firms. The discipline of forecasting forces you to think about timing, not just amounts. If you see a shortfall in week 8, you can accelerate a billing or delay a non-urgent payment.</p>

<h2>2. Improve WIP Funding Without Borrowing</h2>

<p>WIP funding is the single biggest cash drain for many firms. Every hour a fee-earner works but does not bill is an hour of cash tied up. The solution is not to stop doing the work, but to bill more frequently and more accurately.</p>

<p>Here are three practical steps:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Bill on account for disbursements.</strong> In litigation and conveyancing, you can ask the client to pay a fixed sum into the client account before you incur the cost. This is permitted under the SRA Accounts Rules, provided you have the client's written authority and the money is held for the specific purpose.</li>
<li><strong>Use interim bills.</strong> Do not wait for the end of a matter. Bill monthly or at key milestones. This is standard in commercial litigation and increasingly common in residential conveyancing. It reduces the average WIP lock-up period from months to weeks.</li>
<li><strong>Negotiate faster payment terms.</strong> If you bill corporate clients on 30-day terms, consider moving to 14-day or even 7-day terms for smaller matters. You can also offer a small prompt-payment discount (e.g. 2% if paid within 7 days) to incentivise early settlement.</li>
</ul>

<p>Some firms also use invoice discounting or factoring, but that is external debt. The methods above are cheaper and carry no interest cost. They simply require better <strong>client account discipline</strong> and billing habits.</p>

<h2>3. Tighten Client Account Discipline</h2>

<p>The SRA Accounts Rules are clear: client money must be held separately and used only for the client's matter. But poor client account discipline can still hurt cash flow in indirect ways.</p>

<p>Common mistakes include:</p>

<ul>
<li>Holding client money for longer than necessary before transferring it to the office account as earned fees</li>
<li>Failing to reconcile client account balances regularly, leading to delays in billing</li>
<li>Using the client account as a "parking spot" for funds that should be returned to the client</li>
</ul>

<p>Each of these creates a drag on your office account. If you hold client money for weeks after the work is done, you are effectively lending the client interest-free credit. The remedy is simple: bill promptly, transfer earned fees to the office account, and return any residual client money within a reasonable time.</p>

<h2>4. Align Partner Drawings with Cash Reality</h2>

<p>This is often the hardest conversation in a law firm. Partners want to take drawings monthly, but the firm's cash flow may not support it. The solution is to link drawings to realised cash, not budgeted profit.</p>

<p>Consider a two-tier system:</p>

<ul>
<li>A base monthly drawing equal to the partner's personal tax and living costs (say £8,000 per month)</li>
<li>A variable profit distribution paid quarterly, based on the firm's actual cash position</li>
</ul>

<p>This prevents the office account from being drained before VAT and payroll are due. It also forces partners to think about the firm's cash cycle, not just their own income.</p>

<p>If you are an LLP, remember that drawings are not the same as profit share. The partnership agreement should specify when profit is allocated and when it is paid. Many firms use a "lock-up" period of 3 to 6 months between the end of the accounting period and the distribution of profit. This builds a cash reserve.</p>

<h2>5. Review Your Billing and Credit Control Processes</h2>

<p>Cash flow improvement is not a one-off fix. It requires ongoing attention to billing and collection. Here are the key metrics to track:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>WIP days.</strong> How many days on average between starting work and billing? Aim for under 30 days for most matters.</li>
<li><strong>Debtor days.</strong> How many days between billing and payment? Aim for under 45 days for commercial clients, under 30 for private clients.</li>
<li><strong>Lock-up days.</strong> The sum of WIP days and debtor days. This is the total time your cash is tied up. A target of 60 to 75 days is reasonable for most firms.</li>
</ul>

<p>If your lock-up days exceed 90, you have a structural problem. You are effectively funding your clients' working capital. The solution is to bill faster, collect faster, or both.</p>

<p>Consider automating your credit control process. Most practice management systems can send automatic reminders at 7, 14, and 30 days overdue. A human follow-up call at 45 days is often the most effective step.</p>

<h2>When External Debt Makes Sense</h2>

<p>This article focuses on avoiding external debt, but there are times when borrowing is sensible. For example, a practice acquisition or a major IT investment may require a loan. The key is to use debt for growth, not to plug a recurring cash flow gap.</p>

<p>If you find yourself borrowing to pay salaries or VAT every quarter, the root cause is not a cash flow problem. It is a profitability or billing problem. Fix that first.</p>

<h2>Final Thoughts</h2>

<p>Cash flow management is not glamorous, but it is essential. The firms that survive economic downturns are not always the most profitable. They are the ones with the strongest cash reserves and the best billing discipline.</p>

<p>Start with a simple cash flow forecast. Review your WIP funding approach. Tighten your client account processes. Align partner drawings with cash reality. And measure your lock-up days every month.</p>
