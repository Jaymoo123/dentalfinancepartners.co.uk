<p><strong>Legal cashiering sits at the centre of every law firm that holds client money.</strong> Without a competent cashier, the SRA Accounts Rules 2019 cannot be met in practice, the five-weekly reconciliation slips, and the COFA carries risk they cannot manage. Yet the role is often underspecified in job descriptions, poorly understood by fee-earners, and structurally underinvested until something goes wrong.</p>

<p>This guide is the hub for legal cashiering on this site. It explains what the role is and what it is not, how it sits within the SRA compliance framework, how a cashier develops from junior to senior, what qualifications exist, when fractional or freelance cover is the right answer, and how to weigh the in-house versus outsourced decision. Two companion guides go deeper on specific topics: <a href="/blog/sra-compliance-trust-accounting/what-does-a-legal-cashier-do-sra-requirements">what a legal cashier does day to day and the specific SRA rule obligations</a>, and <a href="/blog/sra-compliance-trust-accounting/outsourced-legal-cashiering-guide-uk-law-firms">the full outsourced cashiering decision framework for UK law firms</a>.</p>

<h2>What a Legal Cashier Is (and Is Not)</h2>

<p>A legal cashier is the person responsible for the operational management of a law firm's client and office accounts. They are not an accountant in the statutory sense, they are not a solicitor, and they do not hold a practising certificate. There is no licence required to work as a legal cashier. What the role does carry is a close operational relationship with the firm's regulatory obligations under the SRA Accounts Rules 2019.</p>

<p>The distinction matters. A solicitor bears personal regulatory responsibility. The COFA (Compliance Officer for Finance and Administration) bears the firm-level compliance duty. The cashier executes the day-to-day tasks that make compliance possible: posting every receipt and payment on the day it occurs, allocating client money to the correct matter ledger, verifying that every withdrawal from client account has a Rule 5 ground, and producing the reconciliation data the COFA relies on.</p>

<p>The cashier is therefore an operational role with a regulatory spine. A firm that treats it as a pure bookkeeping post misunderstands what it is being paid to do.</p>

<h2>Why the Role Is Regulated in Practice</h2>

<p>The SRA does not regulate legal cashiers directly. It regulates the firm and the COFA. But because cashiering is the mechanism through which the firm meets its SRA Accounts Rules obligations, a cashier who makes errors, misses postings, or fails to escalate breaches creates the regulatory exposure the rules are designed to prevent.</p>

<p>Three obligations flow most directly through the cashier's work:</p>

<h3>Client money separation</h3>

<p>The SRA Accounts Rules 2019 require that client money (money held on behalf of clients) is kept separate from the firm's own money. That separation is maintained transaction by transaction in the cashier's posting work. When a mixed receipt arrives (part client, part firm) the cashier must allocate it promptly between client and office accounts under Rule 4.2. When the firm's costs are to be transferred from client account, the cashier must confirm a bill has been issued first (Rule 4.3). Errors in this daily work create the breaches that show up on accountants' reports.</p>

<h3>Rule 8.3: five-weekly bank reconciliations</h3>

<p>Every client account held or operated by the firm must be reconciled at least every five weeks. The reconciliation is a three-way check: the balance on the bank statement, the balance in the firm's cash book, and the total of client matter ledger balances must all agree. If they do not, the difference must be investigated and resolved before the reconciliation is presented for sign-off. The cashier prepares this reconciliation; the COFA or a manager of the firm signs it off, as SRA guidance expects. A reconciliation difference that is presented without resolution is not a complete reconciliation.</p>

<h3>Breach reporting and escalation</h3>

<p>The cashier is typically the first person to identify a potential breach: an unreconciled difference, a withdrawal without a clear Rule 5 ground, a residual client balance that should have been returned, or a transaction that looks like the firm's client account is being used as a banking facility (prohibited under Rule 3.3). The cashier's job is not to correct these unilaterally. It is to escalate immediately in writing to the COFA. The COFA then decides whether and how to report to the SRA. A cashier who tries to fix a breach quietly rather than escalating it compounds the problem.</p>

<h2>Junior vs Senior Cashier: How Responsibilities Split</h2>

<p>In most firms the cashiering function has at least an implicit junior-to-senior structure, even when one person covers the whole role. Understanding the split matters for job descriptions, appraisal frameworks, and deciding when a second hire is needed.</p>

<h3>Junior legal cashier</h3>

<p>A junior cashier typically handles:</p>

<ul>
<li>Daily posting of receipts and payments to client and office ledgers</li>
<li>Allocating receipts to the correct matter</li>
<li>Processing authorised payments under instruction and with supervision, checking Rule 5 grounds before processing</li>
<li>Maintaining petty cash and office account transactions</li>
<li>Preparing the underlying ledger data and bank statements for the senior cashier's reconciliation</li>
<li>Managing the residual balance register under direction</li>
<li>Handling basic supplier payment runs and disbursement postings</li>
</ul>

<p>The junior role is high-volume and transactional. Accuracy matters more than judgement at this stage. The risk is that a junior cashier who is unsupervised begins making allocation decisions above their competence, which is where posting errors and small compliance failures originate.</p>

<h3>Senior legal cashier</h3>

<p>A senior cashier owns the process end to end and carries material compliance responsibility:</p>

<ul>
<li>Preparing and delivering the five-weekly reconciliation to the COFA for sign-off</li>
<li>Investigating and resolving reconciliation differences before presentation</li>
<li>Managing banking relationships and client account bank mandates</li>
<li>Overseeing complex or high-value client-money movements</li>
<li>Liaising with the firm's accountant during the Rule 12 accountant's report cycle</li>
<li>Identifying and escalating potential breaches to the COFA in writing</li>
<li>Supervising and training junior staff</li>
<li>Maintaining the firm's cashiering procedures and keeping them current with SRA Accounts Rules developments</li>
<li>Producing management-information reports on client account balances, residuals and billing lag for firm leadership</li>
</ul>

<p>In a larger firm with a cashiering team, the most senior person may carry the title of Legal Cashier Manager or Head of Finance, taking on people-management and strategic responsibilities alongside the compliance ones.</p>

<h2>Qualifications and Training</h2>

<p>There is no statutory qualification required to work as a legal cashier. That said, the market has a recognised professional body and a clear progression pathway.</p>

<h3>The ILFM</h3>

<p>The Institute of Legal Finance and Management (ILFM) is the main professional body for legal cashiers and legal finance staff in England and Wales. It offers:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>ILFM Diploma (ILFM Dip):</strong> the entry qualification, made up of two certificates: Bookkeeping for Legal Finance Professionals and Legal Finance Compliance &amp; Accounts Rules. Suited to a cashier in their first or second year in the role.</li>
<li><strong>Associate (AILFM):</strong> the next level, open to Diploma holders, requiring further certificates covering accounting for legal finance professionals, financial management and legal practice management. AILFM after someone's name is the recognised signal of senior professional competence in the cashiering market.</li>
<li><strong>Fellowship (FILFM):</strong> the highest level, awarded on a thesis and presentation in a chosen legal finance subject.</li>
</ul>

<p>The ILFM also offers short courses and CPD modules on topics including SRA Accounts Rules updates, legal aid billing, and practice finance management. Many firms fund ILFM study as a condition of progression rather than initial appointment.</p>

<h3>Other routes</h3>

<p>Some cashiers come from AAT (Association of Accounting Technicians) or bookkeeping backgrounds and cross-train into legal cashiering. The AAT qualification covers general accounting principles competently but does not cover the SRA Accounts Rules or legal-specific compliance obligations, so legal-context training (formal or on-the-job) is still required. A cashier with an AAT background who then completes ILFM study combines the strongest foundation.</p>

<h2>Part-Time and Freelance Cashiering</h2>

<p>Not every law firm needs a full-time cashier. A small firm of two or three fee-earners with a manageable transaction volume may not generate enough work to justify a full-time post, and the five-weekly reconciliation cycle means the work is not evenly distributed across weeks. Part-time and freelance arrangements are common and well-established in the sector.</p>

<h3>When part-time cover works</h3>

<p>A part-time in-house cashier works well when the volume is low enough that one or two days per week covers postings, payment runs and reconciliation preparation, and when there is enough consistency in working days that the COFA can rely on timely escalation. The risk is cover: a part-time employee who is absent leaves a compliance gap. Many firms with a part-time cashier also carry a relationship with an outsourced provider for cover.</p>

<h3>Freelance and portfolio cashiers</h3>

<p>A freelance legal cashier works across multiple firms simultaneously, typically on a day-rate or retainer basis. The arrangement suits a small firm that needs a few hours of cashiering each week but does not want to take on an employment relationship. It also suits firms going through a transitional period: a recent merger, a period of rapid growth before headcount justifies a full-time post, or a gap between in-house cashiers.</p>

<p>The SRA Accounts Rules implications of freelance cashiering are the same as for any outsourced arrangement: the firm remains fully responsible for the accuracy of postings and the timeliness of reconciliations regardless of who is doing the work. The COFA must have sufficient visibility to sign off reconciliations and discharge their own obligations. A freelance cashier who works two days per month cannot provide the continuous monitoring a high-volume firm needs.</p>

<h2>In-House vs Outsourced: The Framework</h2>

<p>The decision between an in-house cashier and an outsourced provider is ultimately about volume, risk tolerance, management overhead and cost structure. Our <a href="/blog/sra-compliance-trust-accounting/outsourced-legal-cashiering-guide-uk-law-firms">full outsourced cashiering guide</a> covers the decision framework in detail, including a cost comparison template and a due-diligence checklist for evaluating providers. The summary version:</p>

<p><strong>In-house works best when</strong> the firm has sufficient transaction volume to keep a dedicated cashier productively occupied, the COFA wants close daily oversight, the firm's work is complex enough (legal aid billing, high-value commercial transactions, mixed civil and criminal) that institutional knowledge of the client base matters, or there are systems access or confidentiality reasons that make an external provider operationally harder to integrate.</p>

<p><strong>Outsourcing works best when</strong> the firm is small enough that a full-time post is not cost-justified, continuity cover is a material concern (outsourced providers do not get ill, leave, or resign without notice in the way individual employees do), the COFA wants a provider with SRA Accounts Rules specialism rather than a general bookkeeper who has been adapted into the role, or the firm is growing fast and needs a scalable arrangement before it is ready to hire.</p>

<p>The critical non-negotiable in an outsourced arrangement is COFA accountability. A provider can post, reconcile and report. The provider cannot substitute for the COFA's sign-off on reconciliations, cannot authorise withdrawals on the firm's behalf, and cannot make breach decisions. The firm must retain enough internal oversight to ensure the COFA can discharge their SRA obligations on the basis of the work the provider delivers.</p>

<h2>What a Legal Cashier Costs a Firm vs the Risk of Getting It Wrong</h2>

<p>Salary benchmarking in a guide like this dates quickly, and inventing figures does more harm than good. What we can say is that the market distinguishes clearly between junior cashiers (typically with one to three years of experience, no ILFM qualification yet), senior cashiers (three to seven years, ILFM Diploma or Associateship), and legal cashier managers (seven-plus years, team-lead experience). London rates are materially higher than the rest of England and Wales. Regional law firms in major cities sit between the two.</p>

<p>Beyond salary, the in-house cost picture includes employer NIC (15% from April 2025 on earnings above the secondary threshold), auto-enrolment pension contributions, holiday cover, recruitment fees, and training and ILFM study support. These costs are real and should be included in any comparison with an outsourced provider's quoted fee.</p>

<p>The risk-side calculation is harder to quantify but more important. An adverse accountant's report under Rule 12 must be delivered to the SRA. A material client-money breach can trigger an SRA investigation, a regulatory outcome, or in serious cases an intervention. The reputational and financial cost of a cashiering failure dwarfs the cost of getting the cashiering function right in the first place. A small firm that economises on cashiering cover to save a few thousand pounds per year and then suffers a reconciliation failure that prompts a Rule 12 qualification has not saved money.</p>

<h2>How Cashiering Interacts with the Accountant's Report and COFA Duties</h2>

<p>The legal cashier's work is the primary input into two of the firm's most important compliance outputs: the annual accountant's report and the COFA's ongoing compliance record.</p>

<h3>The accountant's report</h3>

<p>Under Rule 12 of the SRA Accounts Rules 2019, most firms that hold client money must obtain an annual accountant's report from a qualified firm of accountants. The accountant reviews a sample of client ledgers, reconciliations, bank statements and postings across the year. A clean (unqualified) report means the accountant found no material breaches in the sample. A qualified report (one that identifies material breaches) must be delivered to the SRA.</p>

<p>The cashier's records are what the accountant reviews. Clean, complete and timely posting records, fully reconciled accounts with no outstanding differences, and a complete reconciliation trail from the period are the building blocks of a clean accountant's report. A cashier who keeps poor records, delays reconciliations, or fails to document breach escalations creates the evidence base for a qualified report.</p>

<h3>The COFA's compliance duties</h3>

<p>The COFA's job is to ensure the firm's compliance with the SRA Accounts Rules and to report material breaches to the SRA. In practice, the COFA cannot do this without the cashier. The cashier delivers the reconciliations the COFA signs off, escalates the potential breaches the COFA decides how to handle, and maintains the records the COFA relies on for the accountant's report cycle. A COFA who does not have a reliable cashier beneath them is carrying compliance obligations they cannot discharge.</p>

<h2>Getting the Cashiering Function Right</h2>

<p>Legal cashiering is not a commodity function. It is a specialist, regulated discipline that requires sector-specific knowledge, procedural rigour, and a clear understanding of how the role connects to the firm's SRA compliance obligations. Whether the right answer for a firm is a full-time in-house cashier, a part-time employee, a freelance arrangement, or an outsourced provider depends on transaction volume, the COFA's appetite for oversight, and the firm's cost structure.</p>

<p>What does not vary is the standard. The five-weekly reconciliation must happen. The client money must stay separate. The breaches must be escalated. And the accountant who reviews the year must find records that support a clean report. The cashier is the person who makes that possible.</p>
