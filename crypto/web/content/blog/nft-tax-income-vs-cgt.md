<p>Whether you mint original work or buy and resell NFTs created by others changes everything about your UK tax position. This post maps out both routes, covers ongoing royalties, explains why paying for an NFT in ETH creates two taxable events, and flags the badges-of-trade risk that can push even a flipper into income tax territory.</p>

<h2>The short answer: creators are usually taxed on income, flippers under CGT, and you can be both</h2>

<p>If you mint an NFT from work you created and sell it, HMRC will generally treat the proceeds as income, taxed at your marginal income tax rate. If you buy an NFT someone else made and later resell it, that sale is a <a href="https://www.gov.uk/guidance/check-if-you-need-to-pay-tax-when-you-sell-cryptoassets">disposal for Capital Gains Tax</a>. The two roles carry different tax treatments, different rates and different reporting obligations. The same person can be both: income tax on NFTs they created, CGT on NFTs they flipped.</p>

<h2>Which are you? Creator, flipper, or both</h2>

<p>The dividing line is straightforward in most cases:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Creator:</strong> you made the underlying work (digital art, music, video, code, collectible design) and minted it as an NFT. When you sell, you are converting a product of your own labour into cash. That is income, not a capital return on an investment.</li>
  <li><strong>Flipper:</strong> you bought an NFT that someone else created. Your intention was to hold it as an asset and sell it at a gain. That is the investor route: each sale is a disposal subject to CGT.</li>
  <li><strong>Both:</strong> many people mint their own work and also hold NFTs by other creators speculatively. Each category is assessed on its own facts.</li>
</ul>

<p>The classification matters significantly because income tax rates run from 20% to 45% depending on your total income, while CGT on cryptoassets is <a href="https://www.gov.uk/capital-gains-tax/rates">18% within the remaining basic-rate band and 24% on gains above it</a> (higher and additional-rate taxpayers pay 24% on the whole gain).</p>

<h2>The creator route: income tax on sales of work you made</h2>

<p>When you sell an NFT you created, the proceeds are generally taxable as income. There is no NFT-specific HMRC manual paragraph locking this treatment: it follows the general income-tax principle that proceeds from disposing of a product of your own skill and effort are income, not a capital receipt. The position is analogous to a photographer selling prints or a musician selling downloads.</p>

<p>In practice this means:</p>

<ul>
  <li>The full sale price (in sterling at the time of the transaction) is receipts for income tax purposes.</li>
  <li>Allowable costs (gas fees, platform commissions, software subscriptions directly related to the work) reduce the taxable amount.</li>
  <li>Profits are added to your other income for the tax year and taxed at your marginal rate (20%, 40% or 45% for rUK; Scottish rates differ and will change the marginal outcome if your income straddles a Scottish band).</li>
  <li>If the scale, frequency and commercial organisation of your NFT creation amounts to a trade, Self Assessment under the trading-income rules applies. If it is smaller in scale and more occasional, it may fall under miscellaneous income rules instead.</li>
</ul>

<p>Because the exact classification depends on your facts and the activity level, the creator income route is one where professional advice is genuinely useful rather than optional. The principles are clear; the application to your specific situation requires judgment on the facts.</p>

<h2>Royalties: ongoing income each time a secondary sale pays you</h2>

<p>Many NFT platforms pay the original creator a royalty percentage whenever the NFT resells on the secondary market. Those royalties are generally taxable income when received. As with the initial sale proceeds, the treatment (trading income or miscellaneous income) depends on the scale and organisation of the overall activity, but in either case income tax applies at your marginal rate.</p>

<p>There is no NFT-specific HMRC guidance on royalties at the time of writing. The treatment follows general principles for royalties paid to the creator of intellectual property. If royalty streams are material to you, confirm the classification with an adviser.</p>

<p>One practical note: royalty payments are often made in the same token used on the NFT platform (ETH, SOL, and so on). The sterling value at the date you receive the payment is what matters for income tax. That same sterling value becomes your CGT base cost if you later dispose of the tokens received.</p>

<h2>The flipper route: CGT on each disposal</h2>

<p>If you buy an NFT as an investment and later sell it, <a href="https://www.gov.uk/guidance/check-if-you-need-to-pay-tax-when-you-sell-cryptoassets">that sale is a disposal for Capital Gains Tax</a>. The gain is the difference between the sale proceeds (in sterling) and the allowable acquisition cost (also in sterling, at the time you bought it).</p>

<p>The rates for 2026/27 are:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>18%</strong> on the part of the gain that falls within your remaining basic-rate income tax band (the band ceiling is £37,700 of taxable income).</li>
  <li><strong>24%</strong> on any gain above that boundary.</li>
  <li><strong>24% flat</strong> for higher and additional-rate taxpayers on the whole gain.</li>
</ul>

<p>The <a href="https://www.gov.uk/capital-gains-tax/allowances">CGT annual exempt amount is £3,000</a> for 2026/27. Once your total net gains for the year exceed that, the excess is taxable. For anyone active in the NFT market, the £3,000 exemption is easily exhausted across multiple disposals.</p>

<p>One critical point: the CGT annual exempt amount covers the net gain, not gross proceeds. Even if the tax is nil (because the gain is under £3,000), you may still need to report the disposals in Self Assessment if your total disposal proceeds exceed the reporting threshold.</p>

<h2>Crypto-to-crypto reminder: paying for an NFT in ETH is itself a disposal of the ETH</h2>

<p>Most NFT purchases are made with a token rather than with sterling. <a href="https://www.gov.uk/guidance/check-if-you-need-to-pay-tax-when-you-sell-cryptoassets">Spending crypto on goods or services is a disposal of that crypto</a> at its sterling market value at the moment of the transaction. When you buy an NFT with ETH, two events happen simultaneously:</p>

  <thead>
    <tr>

      <th>What is triggered</th>

    </tr>
  </tbody>

<p>This means a single NFT purchase generates a CGT calculation on the ETH you spent, even though you never converted to pounds. "I paid in ETH, not cash" is not a defence against CGT on the ETH disposal. This is the most frequently missed event in DIY NFT tax returns.</p>

<h2>When a flipper's activity looks like a trade: the badges-of-trade risk</h2>

<p>A flipper who is treated under CGT is almost always in a better position than one whose activity is reclassified as a trade. <a href="https://www.gov.uk/hmrc-internal-manuals/cryptoassets-manual/crypto20250">HMRC expects trading treatment only in exceptional circumstances</a> for individuals dealing in cryptoassets, and high frequency alone does not make a trade. The same principle applies to NFTs.</p>

<p>The <a href="https://www.gov.uk/hmrc-internal-manuals/business-income-manual/bim20205">badges of trade</a> are applied qualitatively, on all the facts. There is no numerical threshold (no rule that "more than X trades per month" or "more than £Y profit" makes you a trader). Relevant factors include:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Frequency and volume:</strong> buying and selling NFTs at very high frequency, with a large number of transactions in a short period, points toward trade.</li>
  <li><strong>Motive:</strong> the dominant purpose of making a quick profit from resale (rather than holding for long-term appreciation or personal enjoyment) points toward trade.</li>
  <li><strong>Organisation and commercial basis:</strong> running the activity with business-like systems, dedicated time, a profit plan, or leveraging external financing points toward trade.</li>
  <li><strong>Subject matter:</strong> assets that produce no yield and are held only for resale profit are more trade-like than income-producing assets held for appreciation.</li>
  <li><strong>Period of ownership:</strong> very short holding periods between purchase and resale point toward trade.</li>
</ul>

<p>If HMRC successfully argues that the flipping activity is a trade, the profits are taxed as income at up to 45%, plus <a href="https://www.gov.uk/self-employed-national-insurance-rates">Class 4 NIC at 6% on profits between £12,570 and £50,270 and 2% above</a>. That is a materially worse outcome than the 18%/24% CGT route for almost all individuals. The common assumption that "trader status" is somehow advantageous is incorrect for most people: it mainly matters for loss-relief edge cases.</p>

<p>The assessment is always on the facts of the specific activity. If you are concerned that your NFT flipping volume or approach could attract scrutiny, a formal review of your position before filing is the right move rather than self-assessing as investor without checking.</p>

<h2>Getting the classification right</h2>

<p>NFT tax is one of the areas where HMRC's general principles are reasonably clear but the application to your specific facts requires judgment. Key questions include: did you create the NFT or buy it from someone else? Are you earning royalties on secondary sales? Did you pay for NFTs with tokens (triggering disposal events you may not have tracked)? Does the scale and frequency of your flipping activity cross the line into trading?</p>

<p>Getting the wrong answer costs money: too much tax if you declare income when CGT applies, penalties and interest if you under-declare or miss events entirely.</p>
