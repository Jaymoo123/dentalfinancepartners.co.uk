<p>Many UK miners have heard that the first £1,000 of mining income is tax free. That is partly true. The £1,000 trading and miscellaneous income allowance can shelter small mining receipts from income tax. What those guides rarely explain is that a second tax applies when you eventually sell, swap or spend those coins. This post explains both legs and why getting them right matters.</p>

<h2>The short answer: mining rewards are taxable income when you receive them, and there is a second tax when you sell</h2>

<p>Mining rewards are taxable in the UK the moment they land in your wallet, based on their sterling value on that day. For most individual miners, <a href="https://www.gov.uk/hmrc-internal-manuals/cryptoassets-manual/crypto21150">HMRC classes this as miscellaneous income</a>. If your mining activity rises to the level of a trade, it becomes trading income instead. Either way, that receipt value then becomes your base cost for Capital Gains Tax when you later dispose of the coins. The £1,000 trading and miscellaneous income allowance can reduce or remove the income tax charge on small receipts, but it has no effect on the CGT that arises at disposal. Mining below £1,000 a year is not the same as mining tax free.</p>

<h2>Income at receipt: sterling value on the day the reward lands</h2>

<p>Under <a href="https://www.gov.uk/hmrc-internal-manuals/cryptoassets-manual/crypto21150">HMRC's Cryptoassets Manual at CRYPTO21150</a>, mining rewards are valued in sterling at the point of receipt. If you mine 0.01 BTC on a day when Bitcoin is worth £50,000, you have received £500 of income. That £500 is what goes on your Self Assessment return as miscellaneous income (or trading income if applicable). The value of those coins a year later, when you decide to sell, is irrelevant for this leg of the calculation.</p>

<p>This means you need a record of the sterling value of every reward on the day it arrived. Many miners track portfolio value in their chosen currency but not the per-reward sterling receipt value. That gap creates a records problem that compounds over time.</p>

<h2>Hobby mining vs a mining trade: the misc-vs-trading-income boundary and why it changes the tax</h2>

<p>HMRC does not publish a bright line separating hobby mining from a mining trade. Instead, the same badges-of-trade analysis that applies to share-dealing applies here: the scale of activity, the level of organisation, the commercial intent, whether profits are the primary motive, and whether there is a systematic approach to the operation.</p>

<p>Trading status is not a prize. The income tax rates are the same, and Class 4 National Insurance is added on top. The main advantage of trading status is the ability to offset genuine trading losses against other income, which is a narrow benefit for most miners. If you are unsure which category applies to your operation, speak to us before filing.</p>

<h2>The £1,000 trading and miscellaneous income allowance: what it does and what it does NOT do</h2>

<p>The <a href="https://www.gov.uk/guidance/tax-free-allowances-on-property-and-trading-income">trading and miscellaneous income allowance</a> gives every individual a £1,000 exemption against trading or miscellaneous income in a tax year. If your total mining receipts (plus any other miscellaneous or trading income) stay below £1,000, you have no income tax to report on them. If your receipts are above £1,000, you can either use the allowance to reduce taxable receipts by £1,000, or deduct actual allowable expenses instead (whichever is more beneficial).</p>

<p>What the allowance does NOT do:</p>

<ul>
  <li>It does not remove Capital Gains Tax on the later disposal of mined coins.</li>
  <li>It does not reduce your CGT base cost to zero. Your base cost is still the sterling value at receipt, regardless of whether income tax was charged.</li>
  <li>It does not exempt coins from being reportable assets for CGT purposes.</li>
</ul>

<p>See also <a href="https://www.gov.uk/guidance/check-if-you-need-to-pay-tax-when-you-receive-cryptoassets">HMRC's guidance on receiving cryptoassets</a> for confirmation that the two-step applies independently of the allowance.</p>

<h2>The second leg: the receipt value becomes your CGT base cost</h2>

<p>When you dispose of mined coins (by selling for fiat, swapping for another token, or spending them on goods or services), CGT applies to the gain above your base cost. Your base cost is the sterling value of the reward on the day you received it, which is the same figure that was (or would have been) your income tax starting point.</p>

<p>The <a href="https://www.gov.uk/capital-gains-tax/allowances">CGT annual exempt amount is £3,000</a> (frozen for 2026/27). You can apply this against total capital gains across all assets in the tax year. Gains above the exempt amount are charged at:</p>

<ul>
  <li><a href="https://www.gov.uk/capital-gains-tax/rates">18% on the part of the gain within your remaining basic-rate band</a> (the basic-rate band ceiling is £37,700 of taxable income for 2026/27)</li>
  <li>24% on any gain above that boundary</li>
  <li>24% on the whole gain if you are a higher or additional-rate taxpayer</li>
</ul>

<p>Do not rely on a flat 18% rate. The correct rate depends on how much of your basic-rate band is already used by your income.</p>

<h2>Why the £1,000 allowance does not make mining "tax free"</h2>

<p>The allowance eliminates the income tax charge on receipts below £1,000. It does not eliminate the CGT charge on the subsequent gain. Even if you had zero income tax to pay on a mining reward (because receipts were below £1,000), your base cost is still set at the sterling value of those coins on receipt. When you later sell at a higher price, CGT applies to the difference.</p>

<p>If you mined coins worth £800 (below the allowance, so no income tax) and later sold them for £5,000, the capital gain is approximately £4,200 (£5,000 minus £800 base cost). Only the first £3,000 of total gains across all assets is covered by the annual exempt amount. The balance is chargeable at 18% or 24% depending on your income.</p>

<p>The "mining is tax free under £1,000" claim is therefore accurate only for the income tax leg, and only in that tax year. The CGT obligation deferred to the disposal year remains.</p>

<h2>Keeping the records HMRC and CARF will expect</h2>

<p>HMRC expects you to keep a record of:</p>

<ul>
  <li>The date of each mining reward</li>
  <li>The sterling value of each reward on the day of receipt (this is your income figure and your CGT base cost)</li>
  <li>The number of coins received in each reward</li>
  <li>The date, proceeds, and disposal method when you sell or swap those coins</li>
</ul>

<p>From 1 January 2026, UK cryptoasset platforms are required to collect user and transaction data under the Cryptoasset Reporting Framework (CARF). The first report to HMRC covers the 2026 calendar year and is due between 1 January 2027 and 31 May 2027. See <a href="https://www.gov.uk/guidance/collecting-cryptoasset-user-and-transaction-data">HMRC's CARF collection guidance</a> and <a href="https://www.gov.uk/guidance/reporting-cryptoasset-user-and-transaction-data">reporting guidance</a>. Mining rewards that pass through an exchange will be visible to HMRC. Gaps in your records are likely to be noticed.</p>
