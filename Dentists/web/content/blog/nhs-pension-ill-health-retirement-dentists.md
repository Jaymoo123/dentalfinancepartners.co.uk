<h2>What Is Ill Health Retirement for NHS Pension Dentists?</h2>

<p>Ill health retirement (IHR) is a provision within the <strong>NHS Pension Scheme</strong> that allows a <strong>dentist</strong> to access their pension benefits before normal pension age if they become permanently medically unfit to work. Unlike early retirement on actuarially reduced terms, ill health retirement can provide enhanced benefits depending on the severity and permanence of your condition.</p>

<p>For a <strong>dentist</strong> in the NHS Pension Scheme, the key question is whether you meet the medical criteria set by NHS Pensions. The scheme does not simply require you to be unable to do your current job. It assesses your fitness for any form of regular employment, or at least for your specific dental role, depending on the tier applied.</p>

<p>This article explains the two tiers of <strong>ill health</strong> retirement, who qualifies, how the application works, and what the benefits look like in practice. It is general guidance only. Your individual circumstances, pension section, and medical evidence will determine the outcome.</p>

<h2>Who Qualifies for Ill Health Retirement in the NHS Pension Scheme?</h2>

<p>To qualify for an <strong>ill health pension</strong>, you must meet all of the following conditions:</p>

<ul>
<li>You are a member of the NHS Pension Scheme (1995 section, 2008 section, or 2015 CARE scheme).</li>
<li>You are permanently medically unfit to carry out your duties (or any regular employment, depending on the tier).</li>
<li>You have at least two years of qualifying membership in the scheme.</li>
<li>Your condition is expected to last until retirement age (or is likely to be permanent).</li>
</ul>

<p>NHS Pensions will ask for detailed medical evidence from your GP or consultant. They may also require an independent medical assessment. The decision is made by NHS Pensions, not by your practice or local NHS board.</p>

<p>It is important to note that temporary conditions, stress-related absence without permanent disability, or conditions that can be managed with reasonable adjustments are unlikely to qualify. The bar is set at permanent unfitness, not temporary incapacity.</p>

<h2>Tier 1 vs Tier 2 Ill Health Retirement: What Is the Difference?</h2>

<p>The NHS Pension Scheme operates a two-tier system for <strong>ill health</strong> retirement. The tier you are awarded determines the level of pension enhancement you receive.</p>

<h3>Tier 1: Permanently Unfit for Any Regular Employment</h3>

<p>Tier 1 is the higher tier. It applies when you are permanently medically unfit for any form of regular employment, not just dentistry. This is a strict test. If you could realistically work in another profession, even at a lower skill level or reduced hours, you will not qualify for Tier 1.</p>

<p>If awarded Tier 1, your pension benefits are calculated as follows:</p>

<ul>
<li>Your accrued pension to date is paid immediately, without actuarial reduction for early payment.</li>
<li>You receive an enhancement equal to the pension you would have built up if you had worked for two-thirds of your remaining potential service to normal pension age (typically age 60 for the 1995 section, or state pension age for the 2015 scheme).</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Example:</strong> Dr Patel, a 52-year-old associate dentist in the 1995 section, has 22 years of membership and a current pension of £18,000 per year. She is diagnosed with a progressive neurological condition that makes her permanently unfit for any work. Her normal pension age is 60. She has 8 years of potential service remaining. Her enhancement is 2/3 × 8 years = 5.33 years of additional pension. Her total ill health pension becomes approximately £18,000 + (5.33/22 × £18,000) = £22,360 per year, paid immediately.</p>

<h3>Tier 2: Permanently Unfit for Your Dental Role but Able to Do Other Work</h3>

<p>Tier 2 is the lower tier. It applies when you are permanently medically unfit to carry out the duties of a <strong>dentist</strong>, but you could realistically do some other form of regular employment. This might apply to a principal who can no longer perform clinical work but could manage a practice, or an associate who could work in a non-clinical role.</p>

<p>If awarded Tier 2, your benefits are:</p>

<ul>
<li>Your accrued pension to date is paid immediately, without actuarial reduction.</li>
<li>There is no enhancement. You receive only the pension you have already built up.</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Example:</strong> Dr Jones, a 48-year-old principal with 20 years of membership and a pension of £16,000 per year, develops a severe hand tremor that prevents him from carrying out clinical dentistry. He could work in practice management or dental education. He qualifies for Tier 2. His ill health pension is £16,000 per year paid immediately, with no enhancement. He can continue to work in a non-clinical role without affecting his pension.</p>

<h2>How Do You Apply for Ill Health Retirement as a Dentist?</h2>

<p>The application process for an <strong>ill health pension</strong> involves several steps. You should start as early as possible, because the process can take several months.</p>

<ol>
<li><strong>Speak to your GP or consultant.</strong> You need a clear medical diagnosis and a prognosis that your condition is permanent. Your doctor must be willing to support your application.</li>
<li><strong>Contact NHS Pensions.</strong> Request an ill health retirement application pack. This includes form AW33E (for the 1995/2008 sections) or the equivalent for the 2015 scheme.</li>
<li><strong>Complete the application.</strong> You fill in Part A. Your employer (or practice) fills in Part B, confirming your role and duties. Your GP or consultant fills in Part C, providing medical evidence.</li>
<li><strong>Submit to NHS Pensions.</strong> They will review the application and may refer it to the Scheme Medical Adviser (SMA) for an independent opinion.</li>
<li><strong>Decision.</strong> NHS Pensions writes to you with the outcome, including which tier (if any) you have been awarded and the pension amount.</li>
</ol>

<p>If your application is rejected, you have the right to request a review or appeal. You should seek advice from a dental-specialist accountant or a pensions adviser before appealing, because the grounds for appeal are limited to errors in the application of the rules or new medical evidence.</p>

<h2>What Happens to Your NHS Pension Benefits on Ill Health Retirement?</h2>

<p>When you retire on ill health grounds, your NHS pension benefits are calculated based on your membership to the date of retirement. The key differences from normal retirement are:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>No actuarial reduction.</strong> Your pension is not reduced for being paid early, unlike voluntary early retirement.</li>
<li><strong>Enhancement (Tier 1 only).</strong> Additional pension for two-thirds of your remaining potential service.</li>
<li><strong>Lump sum.</strong> You can commute part of your pension for a tax-free lump sum, subject to HMRC limits (typically 25% of the capital value of your benefits, capped at £268,275 for 2025/26).</li>
<li><strong>Dependants' benefits.</strong> Your spouse, civil partner, or eligible children may receive a pension if you die after retirement.</li>
</ul>

<p>If you are in the 2015 CARE scheme, your pension is calculated on a career-average basis. The enhancement for Tier 1 is based on your final year's pensionable earnings, not your career average. This can be a significant benefit for dentists whose earnings have risen over their career.</p>

<h2>Can You Work After Ill Health Retirement?</h2>

<p>This depends on the tier awarded and the type of work you do.</p>

<p><strong>Tier 1:</strong> You are considered permanently unfit for any regular employment. If you take up any paid work (including self-employment) after Tier 1 retirement, you must notify NHS Pensions. Your pension may be suspended or reduced if your earnings exceed certain thresholds. The rules are complex and depend on which section of the scheme you are in.</p>

<p><strong>Tier 2:</strong> You are unfit for your dental role but can do other work. You can take up other employment without affecting your pension, as long as it is not in the same dental role. Many Tier 2 retirees move into practice management, dental education, or non-clinical advisory roles.</p>

<p>If you return to NHS dental work after ill health retirement, your pension will almost certainly be affected. You should seek specialist advice before accepting any offer of work.</p>

<h2>Tax Implications of Ill Health Retirement for Dentists</h2>

<p>Your ill health pension is taxable as earned income in the year you receive it. The tax treatment is the same as any other NHS pension income. Key points for <strong>dentist</strong>s:</p>

<ul>
<li>Your pension is paid through PAYE, so tax is deducted at source.</li>
<li>If you take a tax-free lump sum, that portion is not taxable.</li>
<li>Your personal allowance (£12,570 for 2025/26) still applies. If your total income (pension plus any other earnings) exceeds £100,000, your personal allowance is tapered.</li>
<li>If you receive a Tier 1 enhancement, that additional pension is taxable in the same way as your base pension.</li>
</ul>

<p>If you are also receiving income from other sources (for example, rental income from a practice property you own, or dividends from a limited company), you need to factor that into your overall tax position. A <a href="/services/dental-accountants">dental accountant</a> can help you model the tax impact before you retire.</p>

<h2>Common Mistakes Dentists Make With Ill Health Retirement Applications</h2>

<p>Several pitfalls can delay or derail an <strong>ill health</strong> retirement application. Avoid these:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Applying too early.</strong> You need a permanent diagnosis, not a temporary condition. Applying before your condition is stable often leads to rejection.</li>
<li><strong>Incomplete medical evidence.</strong> NHS Pensions will not accept a vague letter from your GP. You need a detailed report confirming the diagnosis, prognosis, and why you are permanently unfit for work.</li>
<li><strong>Not understanding the tier test.</strong> Many dentists assume they qualify for Tier 1 when their condition only prevents clinical work. If you could do any other job, you are Tier 2 at best.</li>
<li><strong>Ignoring the two-year membership rule.</strong> If you have less than two years of NHS Pension Scheme membership, you cannot claim ill health retirement at all.</li>
<li><strong>Failing to notify NHS Pensions of any work after retirement.</strong> This can lead to overpayment recovery and penalties.</li>
</ul>
