<p>A dentist planning to leave the UK, whether for a fixed overseas contract, a permanent emigration or an open-ended adventure, usually starts from the same assumption: leave the country, stop paying UK tax. It is the most common misconception in this area and it is wrong. Your UK tax does not switch off when you board the plane. It switches off, if at all, only when you become <strong>non-resident</strong> under a precise legal test, and that test can keep you UK-resident long after you have physically gone.</p>

<p>This guide is the pre-departure checklist. We cover how you actually become non-resident under the <strong>Statutory Residence Test</strong>, why a single year abroad rarely does it, how <strong>split-year treatment</strong> protects your overseas earnings in the year you leave, and the <strong>temporary non-residence</strong> trap that can reach back and tax income or gains realised while you are away. Then the practical settling-up: the final return, payments on account, the NHS pension, GDC registration and UK rental income. The theme is simple. Plan the departure before you take it, because almost everything here is easy to arrange in advance and painful to fix from abroad.</p>

<h2>The fork that decides your UK tax: resident or non-resident</h2>

<p>Your UK tax exposure after leaving follows one fork. If you remain <strong>UK-resident</strong>, you are taxable in the UK on your <strong>worldwide income</strong>, including everything you earn abroad, with double-tax relief to stop the same income being taxed twice. If you become <strong>non-resident</strong>, your UK liability generally narrows to <strong>UK-source income</strong> only, such as UK rental profits. Everything in this guide is about which side of that fork you land on, and it is not a matter of intention. It is decided mechanically by the Statutory Residence Test, which we set out in full in our guide to <a href="https://www.dentalfinancepartners.co.uk/blog/associate-tax/dentist-working-abroad-uk-tax-residence-implications">working abroad as a UK dentist and tax residence</a>.</p>

<p>Because the test is mechanical, the way to get the result you want is to engineer the facts deliberately: the day count, the ties you keep, the working pattern. A dentist who emigrates cleanly, with no UK home, few return visits and full-time work abroad, will usually become non-resident. A dentist who keeps a UK home, returns often and works on and off in the UK may not, however firmly they intended to leave.</p>

<h2>How a dentist actually becomes non-resident</h2>

<p>There are two realistic routes to non-residence for a departing dentist:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>The full-time-work-overseas automatic test.</strong> This is the cleanest route for someone taking a genuine job abroad. It broadly requires sufficient working hours overseas across the tax year, <strong>fewer than 91 days</strong> in the UK, and <strong>no more than 30 UK workdays</strong>. Meet it and you are non-resident regardless of your ties.</li>
<li><strong>The sufficient ties test on a low day count.</strong> If you do not meet the full-time-work test, you fall to the ties test, where the more UK ties you keep (family, accommodation, work, the 90-day tie, and for leavers the country tie), the fewer UK days you can spend before becoming resident. A leaver with a UK home and UK family can become resident on as few as 46 days, so a clean break needs you to shed ties, not just days.</li>
</ul>

<p>This is why a <strong>single year abroad rarely achieves non-residence</strong> on its own. A dentist who keeps the family home, leaves children in UK schools and pops back for holidays is a leaver with multiple ties, and the ties test will often hold them UK-resident. Becoming non-resident generally needs a sustained, genuine break, planned around the day count and the ties from the outset.</p>

<h2>Split-year treatment in the year you leave</h2>

<p>Even when you become non-resident, the tax year in which you leave straddles the move. <strong>Split-year treatment</strong> solves this by dividing the departure year into a <strong>UK part</strong> (up to the point you leave) and an <strong>overseas part</strong> (after), so you are taxed as UK-resident only for the UK part and your overseas earnings after the move are not caught by UK residence taxation for that year.</p>

<p>For a leaver, split-year usually applies under one of the departure cases, most commonly <strong>starting full-time work overseas</strong> or <strong>ceasing to have a UK home</strong>. It is not automatic, you must meet the conditions for one of the recognised cases, and only one tax year can be split. Where more than one case could apply, a priority order decides which governs and from what date the year splits. Our guide to <a href="https://www.dentalfinancepartners.co.uk/blog/associate-tax/split-year-treatment-dentist-arriving-leaving-uk">split-year treatment for a dentist arriving in or leaving the UK</a> works through the departure cases in detail, with dentist scenarios.</p>

<h2>The temporary non-residence trap: the rule that follows you</h2>

<p>The least understood rule at departure is the one that can reach forward in time. The <strong>temporary non-residence</strong> anti-avoidance rule taxes certain income and gains realised <em>while you are abroad</em> in the <strong>year you return</strong>, if your absence turns out to be temporary. It applies where <strong>both</strong> of these hold:</p>

<ul>
<li>You were <strong>UK-resident in four or more of the seven tax years before the year you left</strong> (the residence-history condition, which a career UK dentist usually meets).</li>
<li>Your <strong>period of non-residence is five years or less</strong>.</li>
</ul>

<p>Where both apply, amounts you realised during the absence are charged in the year of return. The amounts caught are the investment-type receipts a short absence might otherwise shelter: <strong>capital gains</strong> on assets held before you left (section 10A of the Taxation of Chargeable Gains Act 1992), <strong>close-company dividends</strong> from your own UK company, certain <strong>pension lump sums and drawdown</strong>, and <strong>chargeable-event gains</strong> on life policies (under Part 4 of Schedule 45 to the Finance Act 2013). Ordinary salary for clinical work done abroad is <strong>not</strong> caught.</p>

<p>Why this matters at departure rather than return: it changes what you should do <em>before</em> you go. A dentist tempted to draw a big dividend from their UK company just before leaving, or to sell shares or a second property while abroad, on the assumption that two or three years of non-residence cleanses the UK tax, is walking into this rule if they come back inside five years. If returning within five years is even possible, do not treat those amounts as outside UK tax.</p>

<h2>Should you take a dividend or sell assets before you leave?</h2>

<p>Timing a distribution or a disposal around emigration is a legitimate planning lever, but only if it survives the temporary non-residence rule. There are two ways it works cleanly. First, if your absence will <strong>genuinely exceed five years</strong>, the period of non-residence is not temporary and the charge does not apply, so a gain realised abroad can fall outside UK tax. Second, even within five years, if the amount is <strong>taxed at a better rate in your new country</strong> with treaty relief, the comparison may favour realising it abroad despite the UK charge on return. The point is that the answer is a calculation, not a reflex. Our companion guide to <a href="https://www.dentalfinancepartners.co.uk/blog/associate-tax/dentist-working-abroad-double-tax-treaty-relief">double-tax-treaty relief for a UK dentist working abroad</a> explains how credit relief interacts with this, and it should be modelled before you act on any pre-departure distribution.</p>

<h2>Settling up with HMRC before you go</h2>

<p>The administrative side is the part most likely to follow you abroad if you leave it undone. Before you go:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>File outstanding Self Assessment returns and pay what is due.</strong> Penalties and interest keep accruing whether or not you are in the country.</li>
<li><strong>Tell HMRC you are leaving.</strong> This is how you formally signal the change in your circumstances and start the non-resident position on the record.</li>
<li><strong>Understand your payments-on-account schedule.</strong> If you are self-employed, you will usually have interim payments due on 31 January and 31 July. A reducing departure year can mean an over-stated payment on account you are entitled to reduce, or a final payment you must not forget. Get the schedule clear before you leave so you are not reconciling it from a different time zone.</li>
</ul>

<p>If you are self-employed, your departure year usually produces a <strong>final UK return</strong> covering the UK part of the year. Sorting it before you go, while your records and your accountant are within easy reach, is far simpler than assembling it from abroad against a deadline.</p>

<h2>The ties you keep decide whether the break is clean</h2>

<p>If you are not taking a clear full-time job abroad, your residence on departure is decided by the <strong>sufficient ties test</strong>, and the ties you keep are within your control in a way the day count partly is not. The five ties that bind a leaver to UK residence are worth naming, because shedding them is the practical work of a clean break:</p>

<ul>
<li>The <strong>family tie</strong>: a UK-resident spouse, civil partner or minor child. If your family moves with you, this falls away; if they stay, it holds.</li>
<li>The <strong>accommodation tie</strong>: available UK accommodation that you use. Keeping the family home available is the most common reason an intended clean break is not clean.</li>
<li>The <strong>work tie</strong>: 40 or more UK workdays of at least three hours. A dentist who keeps doing occasional UK sessions can trip this.</li>
<li>The <strong>90-day tie</strong>: 90 or more days in the UK in either of the previous two tax years, which lingers from your pre-departure life.</li>
<li>The <strong>country tie</strong>: for leavers, the UK being the country where you spend the most midnights.</li>
</ul>

<p>The more of these you keep, the fewer UK days you can spend before becoming resident again. A leaver with family, a home and a UK working pattern can become UK-resident on a low day count, which is why a dentist who emigrates but keeps the house, leaves the children in school here and flies back for the odd weekend of private work may find they never achieved non-residence at all. Plan to shed the ties you can, and count the days carefully on the ones you cannot.</p>

<h2>The day-count discipline</h2>

<p>Whichever route to non-residence you take, the <strong>day count</strong> is the thing HMRC can check most easily, so it has to be managed and recorded, not estimated after the fact. A day generally counts as a UK day if you are in the UK at <strong>midnight</strong>. For the full-time-work-overseas test you need fewer than 91 UK days and no more than 30 UK workdays across the year; for the ties test the permitted days depend on how many ties you keep. The practical discipline is to keep a <strong>contemporaneous record</strong> of every UK day from the moment you leave: travel dates, the reason, and whether you worked. A dentist who relies on memory at filing time, or who quietly overshoots the day limit on return visits, can convert an intended non-resident year into a resident one and undo the whole plan. Treat the day count as a budget you spend down deliberately across the year.</p>

<h2>Capital gains on the way out</h2>

<p>Leaving the UK is also a moment to think about <strong>capital gains</strong>, because non-residence changes what the UK can tax and the temporary non-residence rule changes it back if you return. While non-resident, you are generally outside UK capital gains tax on most assets, with the major exception of <strong>UK land and property</strong>, which stays within UK CGT for non-residents. So a dentist who wants to sell a portfolio of shares might be tempted to do it once abroad. But as covered above, if the absence is temporary, under five years with the four-of-seven history, the gain is pulled back into the year of return. And UK property gains are taxable for non-residents regardless. The upshot is that a disposal around emigration needs the same two-part check as a dividend: is the asset UK property (taxable anyway), and will the absence genuinely exceed five years (otherwise the temporary non-residence rule applies). Run that check before realising anything significant on the way out.</p>

<h2>NHS pension, GDC registration and UK property</h2>

<p>Three standing items need a decision rather than drift. The <strong>NHS pension</strong>: active accrual normally stops when you cease pensionable NHS employment, and your benefits to that point stay preserved, but the detail of a long-term move is a question for NHSBSA, so confirm it rather than assume. <strong>GDC registration</strong>: keeping it costs the annual retention fee and continuing professional development, but you need it to return to UK practice, so decide deliberately whether to maintain or lapse it. <strong>UK rental income</strong>: this stays UK-taxable even when you are non-resident, usually under the Non-resident Landlord Scheme, which can require tax to be withheld on your rent unless HMRC approves you to receive it gross. Non-residence narrows your UK exposure to UK-source income, but UK property income is precisely that, so it keeps generating a UK filing obligation while you are away.</p>

<h2>If you stay UK-resident: dual residence and treaty relief</h2>

<p>Not every departure achieves non-residence, and that is not necessarily a disaster, but it changes the tax. If the residence test keeps you <strong>UK-resident</strong> while you work abroad, you remain taxable in the UK on your <strong>worldwide income</strong>, including your overseas dental earnings. The country where you work will usually tax that income too, so you can be resident in both countries at once. This is where a <strong>double taxation agreement</strong> earns its keep: its residence tie-breaker can allocate treaty residence to one country, and credit relief offsets foreign tax against the UK bill, so the same income is not taxed twice. The net effect is broadly that you pay the higher of the two countries' rates, not the sum.</p>

<p>The practical point for a leaver is that becoming non-resident is not the only acceptable outcome. A dentist who remains UK-resident but works abroad is not facing a double tax bill, provided the treaty relief is claimed correctly. Our guide to <a href="https://www.dentalfinancepartners.co.uk/blog/associate-tax/dentist-working-abroad-double-tax-treaty-relief">double-tax-treaty relief for a UK dentist working abroad</a> explains the tie-breaker and credit mechanics in detail. So if a clean break is not achievable, for example because family stays in the UK, the fallback is to manage the dual-residence position through the treaty rather than to assume the move has failed.</p>

<h2>A realistic pre-departure timeline</h2>

<p>The administrative steps are easiest if spread across the months before you go rather than crammed into the final fortnight. A workable sequence looks like this. <strong>Several months out</strong>, get the residence analysis done for the departure year and the years abroad, so you know whether and how you become non-resident and can shape the day count and ties accordingly. <strong>A few months out</strong>, decide the standing items: the NHS pension position with NHSBSA, whether to keep or lapse GDC registration, and the arrangements for any UK property, including the Non-resident Landlord Scheme if you are letting. <strong>In the weeks before you leave</strong>, check any planned dividend or disposal against the temporary non-residence rule and fix the timing, and prepare to file the final return covering the UK part of the departure year. <strong>On or shortly after leaving</strong>, tell HMRC you have gone and confirm your payments-on-account position. Spread like this, none of it is heavy; left to the last minute, all of it competes with packing.</p>

<h2>A departure checklist, in order</h2>

<p>Pulling it together, the pre-departure sequence for a dentist leaving the UK looks like this:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Run the residence analysis</strong> for your departure year and the years abroad. Confirm whether and when you become non-resident, and engineer the day count and ties to get there if that is the goal.</li>
<li><strong>Confirm split-year treatment</strong> for the departure year and which case applies, so your post-move overseas earnings are protected.</li>
<li><strong>Check the temporary non-residence rule</strong> against any dividend or disposal you are considering around the move, and decide the timing accordingly.</li>
<li><strong>Settle Self Assessment</strong>: outstanding returns, the final return, payments on account, and tell HMRC you are leaving.</li>
<li><strong>Decide the standing items</strong>: NHS pension position with NHSBSA, GDC registration, and the UK rental and property arrangements.</li>
</ul>

<p>Do these in advance and the move is clean. Leave them and you spend your first months in a new country untangling UK paperwork, often after the moment to plan has passed.</p>

<h2>Plan the leaving, not just the arriving</h2>

<p>It is natural to pour energy into the destination, the new job, the visa, the place to live, and to treat the UK tax side as something to deal with later. Later is exactly when it becomes hard. The residence test, split-year treatment, the temporary non-residence rule and the timing of any distribution all turn on facts you can shape before you leave and cannot easily change once you have gone. A specialist dental accountant can model your residence position for the relevant years, confirm the split-year case, check any pre-departure distribution against the five-year rule, and get the final return and standing items settled, so the only thing you are carrying abroad is the move itself.</p>
