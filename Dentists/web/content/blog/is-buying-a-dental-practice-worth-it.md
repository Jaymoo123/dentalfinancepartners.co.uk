<p>Most of the content on this site covers the mechanics of buying a dental practice: how valuations work, what due diligence to run, how to structure financing, and how goodwill is taxed. This page sits upstream of all of that. Before you engage a solicitor or approach a lender, you need an honest answer to the prior question: is practice ownership worth it for you at all?</p>

<p>That is a harder question than it looks, because the answer is genuinely not always yes.</p>

<h2>The honest economics: associate vs principal</h2>

<p>Associates are often told that ownership is the obvious next step. The income comparison is usually the starting point, so let us state it plainly.</p>

<p>A mid-career NHS or mixed associate typically earns £50,000 to £70,000 gross. After tax and NIC as a self-employed individual, take-home on that range sits at roughly £40,000 to £52,000 (around £46,100 at £60,000 gross). A single-surgery principal who owns and operates their practice can net £80,000 to £120,000 before drawings, after paying staff, lab, materials, premises and other practice costs. That gap is real.</p>

<p>But it is not the whole picture. From that principal net figure you must subtract:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Debt service</strong> on the acquisition loan. A £500,000 practice purchase financed at current commercial rates can mean repayments of £40,000 to £60,000 per year in the early years, depending on term and structure.</li>
<li><strong>Personal guarantees</strong> on that debt, which follow you personally if the practice runs into difficulty.</li>
<li><strong>Management time</strong>. Running a practice means recruiting, supervising and retaining staff, handling CQC compliance, contract management and premises issues. This time is real and largely uncompensated in the year-one numbers.</li>
<li><strong>Risk premium</strong>. An associate's income is relatively predictable. A principal's income is not: it moves with patient volume, NHS contract performance, staff turnover and the local market.</li>
</ul>

<p>Adjusted for these factors, the genuine uplift in year one is often modest. The long-term case for ownership rests on something different: equity accumulation and the capital exit.</p>

<h2>What you gain as a practice owner</h2>

<p><strong>Equity growth.</strong> A well-run practice tends to appreciate in value over time, particularly if you expand capacity, improve private revenue or increase list size. The multiple you sell at may be higher than the multiple you bought at if the underlying EBITDA has grown. Your <a href="/blog/buying-a-practice/dental-practice-valuation-methods-uk">valuation</a> at exit reflects earnings, not hours worked.</p>

<p><strong>Tax-efficient exit via BADR.</strong> When you eventually sell, Business Asset Disposal Relief (BADR) applies to qualifying gains on goodwill and other chargeable assets, taxing them at 18% from 6 April 2026 rather than at income tax rates. On a substantial goodwill value, the difference between 18% CGT and 45% income tax is material. This is structurally unavailable to associates: they accumulate no business equity to sell.</p>

<p><strong>Control over clinical direction.</strong> As principal you set the associate split, the fee structure, the treatment mix, the lab contracts and the patient experience. Associates work within a structure set by someone else.</p>

<p><strong>Pension and retained profit flexibility.</strong> Practice profits can be routed through pension contributions and, for incorporated practices, through retained earnings in a company. These tools can significantly reduce the effective tax rate on profit that is not needed for immediate drawings.</p>

<h2>What you take on</h2>

<p><strong>NHS contract risk.</strong> If the practice holds an NHS contract, that contract comes with a UDA target. Shortfall triggers clawback at year end, which can crystallise a significant and unexpected liability. The contract is also subject to NHS England decisions on renewal and renegotiation. Associates experience this indirectly; principals own it directly. Our guide on <a href="/blog/buying-a-practice/dental-practice-due-diligence-buyers-checklist">practice due diligence</a> covers what to check on the contract before you sign.</p>

<p><strong>Staff responsibility.</strong> The moment you are the principal, all employment law, pension auto-enrolment, disciplinary and HR obligations land on you. Staff problems that were previously invisible to you as a chair-side associate become your problem to solve at 7pm on a Tuesday.</p>

<p><strong>CQC compliance.</strong> Registered providers carry the compliance burden for the whole premises. Inspection, remediation notices, and the cost of keeping systems current sit with you, not with your associates or nurses.</p>

<p><strong>Premises and plant.</strong> Equipment breakdowns, lease renewals, dilapidations clauses, and the decision about whether to <a href="/blog/buying-a-practice/dental-practice-lease-vs-buy-decision-framework">lease or buy the premises</a> all become your responsibility. Associates rarely think about these until they are a principal.</p>

<p><strong>Key-person risk on the vendor.</strong> If the selling dentist's personal reputation or list is the main source of patients, there is a real risk that goodwill evaporates post-sale. This is one of the most common causes of disappointment in practice acquisitions and one of the most important things to stress-test in <a href="/blog/buying-a-practice/practice-acquisition-financial-due-diligence">financial due diligence</a>.</p>

<h2>The break-even mindset: price paid vs maintainable EBITDA</h2>

<p>The question "is this practice worth buying?" is ultimately a question about price relative to earnings. A practice that generates strong EBITDA at a sensible acquisition multiple can absorb debt service and still leave the principal ahead. A practice bought at a high multiple with inflated EBITDA can leave you worse off than you were as an associate for years.</p>

<p>Understanding <a href="/blog/practice-finance/understanding-dental-practice-ebitda-normalisation">EBITDA normalisation</a> is essential before you form a view on price. And understanding how <a href="/blog/buying-a-practice/dental-practice-goodwill-buying-selling">goodwill is valued and taxed</a> prevents surprises at heads of terms.</p>

<h2>When buying is NOT worth it</h2>

<p>There are circumstances where the honest answer is: not yet, or not at all.</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Short career runway.</strong> If you are ten years or fewer from your intended exit, the capital growth thesis weakens significantly. Acquisition debt costs real money and the compounding equity argument does not have time to work.</li>
<li><strong>High-rate acquisition debt in a thin-margin practice.</strong> If the practice EBITDA barely covers debt service, you are taking on all the risk of ownership for income that is lower than your associate earnings. This is not a stepping stone; it is a trap.</li>
<li><strong>Thin associate pipeline.</strong> A practice that depends on the principal doing significant chair-side work to hit its UDA target or revenue budget is not a scalable business; it is a self-employed job with extra liabilities.</li>
<li><strong>Buying at peak multiples without upside.</strong> Acquisition multiples cycle with sentiment, lending conditions and the supply of available practices. Buying at a cycle peak in a mature, fully-utilised practice with no obvious growth lever means you are unlikely to sell at a higher multiple than you paid.</li>
<li><strong>You do not want to manage people.</strong> This is underrated. If the clinical work is what you love and management responsibility sounds like a burden rather than an opportunity, the income and equity upside may not compensate for the daily reality of being an employer and a registered provider.</li>
</ul>

<h2>When buying clearly IS worth it</h2>

<ul>
<li>The practice has an established list with genuine patient loyalty not tied solely to the vendor.</li>
<li>The price paid reflects a realistic and verifiable maintainable EBITDA, not an optimistic projection.</li>
<li>You have enough career runway (ideally fifteen or more years) to benefit from both the income differential and the capital growth.</li>
<li>You are genuinely willing to take on the management role and have a plan for doing so, either personally or by hiring a practice manager.</li>
<li>The acquisition can be financed at a rate and on terms where debt service does not wipe out the income advantage over associate earnings.</li>
<li>You have stress-tested the NHS contract position and the associate retention risk with proper due diligence.</li>
</ul>

<h2>The squat alternative</h2>

<p>For some dentists, particularly those who are predominantly private and want to build a list from scratch in their own image, a squat practice is a genuine alternative to acquisition. You pay no goodwill, you choose the location and equipment, and you build the culture from day one. The downside is the ramp period: income can be very low for twelve to twenty-four months while the list grows. Our dedicated post on <a href="/blog/buying-a-practice/squat-vs-existing-practice-purchase-uk-tax">squat vs existing practice purchase</a> compares both routes in detail, including the tax treatment differences.</p>

<h2>Decision checklist</h2>

<p>Before pursuing an acquisition, work through these questions honestly. A clear "no" to more than two or three of them is worth pausing on.</p>

<ol>
<li>Do I have at least fifteen years of intended clinical practice ahead of me?</li>
<li>Am I willing to take on a management role (staff, CQC, contracts) alongside clinical work?</li>
<li>Is the EBITDA independently verified and does it reflect the practice without the vendor chair-side?</li>
<li>Can I service the acquisition debt and still net more than my current associate earnings in year one or two?</li>
<li>Have I stress-tested the NHS contract position, including UDA target achievability and clawback risk?</li>
<li>Is patient loyalty demonstrably attached to the practice, not solely to the selling dentist?</li>
<li>Do I have adequate working capital for the first year beyond the purchase price itself?</li>
<li>Have I read and understood the <a href="/blog/buying-a-practice/heads-of-terms-dental-practice-purchase">heads of terms</a> and had them reviewed by a specialist solicitor?</li>
<li>Have I modelled the exit economics including BADR at 18% from April 2026 and the likely multiple at sale?</li>
<li>Am I buying this practice because it stacks up financially, or because I am tired of being an associate?</li>
</ol>

<p>The last question is the most important. Fatigue with associate life is a legitimate feeling, but it is not a business case. The practices that work out best for buyers are the ones where the numbers made sense before the emotion entered the room.</p>
