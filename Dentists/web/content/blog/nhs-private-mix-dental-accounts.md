<h2>Why blended practices need blended reporting</h2>
<p>When NHS contract income sits next to private treatment plans, a single "sales" line on a P&amp;L rarely tells you what is driving profit. Associates, hygienists, and labs can behave differently across each stream.</p>

<p>A practice generating £800,000 turnover with a 50/50 NHS-private split might assume even profitability. But when you separate the streams, NHS work might contribute 60% of revenue but only 35% of profit — while private treatments deliver higher margins but with greater variability month to month.</p>

<h2>Structuring management accounts for a mixed practice</h2>
<p>Effective management accounts for a blended dental practice need at least three layers of detail.</p>

<h3>Income segmentation</h3>
<p>Break revenue into NHS contract payments (UDAs), private fee-per-item treatments, hygiene income, and any other streams like facial aesthetics or orthodontics. Ideally, track income per clinician per stream — this reveals whether individual associates are generating adequate returns against their costs.</p>

<h3>Cost allocation</h3>
<p>Some costs are directly attributable to a stream: lab work for private crowns, specific materials for NHS treatments. Others are shared — rent, utilities, reception staff. A reasonable allocation method (often based on chair time or treatment hours) gives you a meaningful profit-per-stream figure without over-engineering the numbers.</p>

<h3>Key performance indicators</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Revenue per UDA</strong> — tracks whether your NHS contract delivers acceptable returns as costs rise.</li>
<li><strong>Private conversion rate</strong> — percentage of patients accepting private treatment plans.</li>
<li><strong>Lab costs as percentage of private revenue</strong> — typically 8-12% for well-managed practices.</li>
<li><strong>Associate cost ratio</strong> — what percentage of their production goes to their fees, split by stream.</li>
<li><strong>Hygienist productivity</strong> — revenue generated per hygienist hour against their total employment cost.</li>
</ul>

<h2>What good looks like in management accounts</h2>
<ul>
<li>Income split that mirrors how clinicians actually produce revenue.</li>
<li>Lab and materials tracked to chair time where possible.</li>
<li>Clear associate and hygienist costs with UDA/FP17 context where relevant.</li>
<li>Equipment and finance costs separated so tax relief is easy to support.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Using segment data for strategic decisions</h2>
<p>Once you have clean segment reporting, it drives better decisions across the practice.</p>

<p><strong>Chair time allocation:</strong> If private treatments generate £180 per hour versus £95 for NHS work, you can make informed decisions about how to allocate surgery time — without guessing.</p>

<p><strong>Associate recruitment:</strong> Understanding which stream needs capacity helps you recruit the right associate. An NHS-focused associate filling UDA targets requires different skills and expectations than one building a private restorative caseload.</p>

<p><strong>Investment planning:</strong> A CBCT scanner or CAD/CAM system primarily serves private patients. Segment data tells you whether private revenue justifies the capital outlay and what ROI timeline is realistic.</p>

<h2>Linking operational clarity to tax</h2>
<p>Year-end compliance is smoother when the underlying bookkeeping already answers HMRC's questions: who earned what, what was rebilled, and what was capital rather than revenue spend.</p>

<p>If you are preparing for a sale, refinance, or partnership change, clean segment reporting is not "nice to have" — it is part of due diligence. Buyers and lenders want to see where profit comes from, and how sustainable each stream is independently.</p>

<p>Capital allowances claims are stronger when you can demonstrate which equipment serves which income stream and provide supporting utilisation data. This matters particularly for high-value items like digital imaging equipment or specialist treatment chairs.</p>

<h2>Common mistakes in mixed practice accounting</h2>
<p>The most frequent errors we see in blended practices include:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Treating all income as one pool</strong> — hiding the true profitability (or loss) of individual streams.</li>
<li><strong>Ignoring associate economics per stream</strong> — an associate generating high NHS UDA numbers might actually cost more per unit of profit than one doing fewer private cases.</li>
<li><strong>Failing to reconcile NHS payments</strong> — contract adjustments, clawback, and mid-year changes can create significant discrepancies if not tracked monthly.</li>
<li><strong>Over-allocating overheads to private work</strong> — inflating private costs and underestimating the true burden of NHS work on practice resources.</li>
</ul>

<p>Getting these fundamentals right transforms your accounts from a compliance obligation into a tool that actively helps you run a more profitable practice.</p>
