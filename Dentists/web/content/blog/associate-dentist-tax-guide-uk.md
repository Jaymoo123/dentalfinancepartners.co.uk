<p>If you are an associate dentist in the UK, you are almost certainly running a small business, even when you only ever set foot in one practice. Your fee statements arrive without tax deducted, you carry your own indemnity, and at the end of the year it is you, not the practice, who has to account to HMRC. This guide is the overview: who counts as self-employed, how associate income is taxed for <strong>2026/27</strong>, the National Insurance you pay, the expenses you can claim, the Self Assessment and payment timetable, what Making Tax Digital changes from April 2026, and where your NHS pension fits. Each section links to a deeper guide so you can drill into the detail that matters to you.</p>

<h2>Are you actually self-employed? Status comes first</h2>

<p>Everything that follows depends on one threshold question: are you self-employed for tax? The answer turns on the <strong>substance of the working arrangement, not the label on the agreement</strong>. HMRC and the tribunals weigh a familiar set of factors: the degree of <strong>control</strong> over how, when and where you work; whether you must give <strong>personal service</strong> or could send a substitute; <strong>mutuality of obligation</strong> (is the practice obliged to offer work and you to accept it); your <strong>financial risk</strong>; and how far you are <strong>integrated</strong> into the practice. No single factor decides it. The overall picture governs.</p>

<p>The good news is that the typical dental model leans naturally towards self-employment: you exercise clinical judgement, supply your own loupes and indemnity, and are paid on a fee split rather than a salary. The <strong>BDA model associate agreement</strong> is drafted to reinforce that position and is strong evidence, but it is not a shield. A rostered, practice-supplied, no-autonomy arrangement can still carry genuine status risk whatever the paperwork says. If you work through your own limited company on NHS engagements, a separate set of rules applies: under the off-payroll (IR35) regime, where the client is medium or large (most NHS practices and dental groups are), the <strong>client decides your IR35 status and issues a Status Determination Statement</strong>, not you. We unpack the factors, the BDA contract and the IR35 position in the dedicated guide to <a href="/blog/associate-tax/dental-associate-vs-self-employed-tax-employment-status">associate versus self-employed status</a>.</p>

<h2>How associate income is taxed</h2>

<p>As a self-employed associate you are taxed on your <strong>profit</strong>, which is your income from all dental sources less your allowable business expenses, not on your gross fees. Income usually arrives from several streams that are all treated as self-employed earnings:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>NHS (UDA) work:</strong> income from completed Units of Dental Activity, shown on your monthly practice statements.</li>
<li><strong>Private work:</strong> private treatment fees, which may not always appear on a practice-generated statement, so keep your own record.</li>
<li><strong>Additional services:</strong> domiciliary visits, sedation sessions, teaching and similar work.</li>
</ul>

<p>Your profit is taxed at the standard income-tax rates. For 2026/27 the bands are unchanged from the prior year:</p>

<table>
<thead>
<tr><th>Band</th><th>Taxable income (2026/27)</th><th>Income-tax rate</th></tr>
</thead>
<tbody>
<tr><td>Personal allowance</td><td>Up to £12,570</td><td>0%</td></tr>
<tr><td>Basic rate</td><td>£12,571 to £50,270</td><td>20%</td></tr>
<tr><td>Higher rate</td><td>£50,271 to £125,140</td><td>40%</td></tr>
<tr><td>Additional rate</td><td>Over £125,140</td><td>45%</td></tr>
</tbody>
</table>

<p>The £12,570 personal allowance is tapered away once your adjusted net income passes <strong>£100,000</strong>, falling by £1 for every £2 above that figure and reaching zero at £125,140. That taper creates an effective marginal rate of around 60% on income between £100,000 and £125,140, which is exactly the band where pension contributions earn the most relief. Many higher-earning associates sit in or near it.</p>

<h2>National Insurance for associates</h2>

<p>On top of income tax, self-employed associates pay <strong>Class 4 National Insurance</strong> on their profits. The most common point of confusion here is Class 2, so it is worth being precise.</p>

<table>
<thead>
<tr><th>Class</th><th>2026/27 position</th></tr>
</thead>
<tbody>
<tr><td>Class 4 (main)</td><td>6% on profits from £12,570 to £50,270</td></tr>
<tr><td>Class 4 (upper)</td><td>2% on profits above £50,270</td></tr>
<tr><td>Class 2</td><td>No weekly charge. From 6 April 2024, profits at or above the small-profits threshold are treated as having paid it, protecting your state pension and NHS officer entitlements at no cost</td></tr>
<tr><td>Voluntary Class 2</td><td>£3.65 a week, only for those below the small-profits threshold who want to bank a qualifying year</td></tr>
</tbody>
</table>

<p>So if you are a working associate above the threshold, you do not pay a separate weekly Class 2 bill. Anyone still budgeting for one is using an out-of-date figure. Combining income tax and Class 4, a basic-rate associate faces an effective marginal rate of roughly 26% on extra profit, and a higher-rate associate roughly 42%.</p>

<h2>Allowable expenses: the overview</h2>

<p>Claiming legitimate business expenses is the most reliable way to reduce your tax, because you are only ever taxed on profit after genuine costs. The statutory test is that an expense must be incurred <strong>wholly and exclusively</strong> for the trade. The main categories an associate can claim are:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Professional indemnity</strong> (Dental Protection, MDU, MDDUS): fully allowable.</li>
<li><strong>GDC annual retention fee</strong> and specialist-register fees: allowable. Note the carve-outs: <strong>restoration fees and CPD-shortfall penalties are not</strong> deductible.</li>
<li><strong>Professional subscriptions</strong> on HMRC's approved List 3 (BDA, faculty and specialty bodies, dental journals).</li>
<li><strong>CPD</strong> that is relevant to your current practice. A course that simply maintains or updates existing skills is revenue. A course that gives you a genuinely <strong>new</strong> skill or qualification can be treated as capital and is not deductible as a normal expense.</li>
<li><strong>Loupes and clinical instruments:</strong> usually claimed through capital allowances (the Annual Investment Allowance gives 100% relief), not as a straight expense.</li>
<li><strong>Mileage between practices:</strong> claimable. Home to your first or usual practice is non-deductible commuting.</li>
<li><strong>Home-office and phone or internet</strong> on a fair business-use apportionment, plus <strong>accountancy fees</strong>, business bank charges and business-loan interest.</li>
</ul>

<p>Two areas catch associates out most often. The first is <strong>clothing</strong>: ordinary clothes are never allowable, and even scrubs are contested, so do not assume a blanket claim. The second is <strong>travel</strong>, which has its own rules worth getting right. For the full category-by-category breakdown, including what fails the test, see the guide to <a href="/blog/associate-tax/associate-dentist-allowable-expenses-uk-2025-26">allowable expenses for associate dentists</a>. For the mileage rules where you work across more than one site, read <a href="/blog/associate-tax/can-dental-associates-claim-mileage-multiple-practices">whether associates can claim mileage at multiple practices</a>, which works through the commuting trap and the simplified flat-rate-per-mile method.</p>

<h2>Self Assessment, deadlines and payments on account</h2>

<p>Once your self-employed income passes £1,000 in a tax year you must register for Self Assessment and file a return. The key dates are:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>31 January:</strong> online filing deadline, plus the balancing payment for the tax year that ended the previous 5 April and the first payment on account for the current year.</li>
<li><strong>31 July:</strong> second payment on account, where you have one.</li>
<li><strong>31 October:</strong> paper-return deadline, though almost everyone files online.</li>
</ul>

<p>The part that surprises most new associates is <strong>payments on account</strong>. Where your prior-year tax and Class 4 bill exceeded £1,000 (and most of it was not collected at source), HMRC asks you to pay towards the next year in advance, in two instalments each equal to 50% of the prior year's liability. In your first self-employed year this means a January bill that can be roughly 150% of a single year's tax: the year just ended, plus a half-year advance. It is not a penalty and it evens out over time, but it has to be planned for, ideally by setting aside tax as you earn rather than scrambling in January. We walk through the mechanics, the cash-flow timeline and how to reduce a payment on account when your income falls in the <a href="/blog/associate-tax/payment-on-account-uk-dentists-guide">payments-on-account guide for dentists</a>.</p>

<p>If you are filing for the first time and want a step-by-step run through registration, the SA103 self-employment pages and the actual submission, follow the <a href="/blog/associate-tax/dental-associate-self-assessment-step-by-step">dental associate Self Assessment guide</a>.</p>

<h2>Worked example: a higher-rate associate</h2>

<p>Take an associate with gross dental income of £85,000 for 2026/27 and £9,000 of allowable expenses (indemnity, GDC retention, List 3 subscriptions, CPD, business mileage and a share of phone and home-office costs). Their taxable profit is £76,000.</p>

<tr><td>Income tax: 0% on first £12,570</td><td>£0</td></tr>

<p>The figures are illustrative and ignore student loan repayments, pension relief and any other income, but they make the point. The £9,000 of expenses saved this associate tax and NIC at their 42% marginal rate, worth roughly £3,780. There is no separate Class 2 charge in the year. In their first self-employed year, the January bill on top of this would also include a payment on account of 50% of the liability towards next year.</p>

<h2>Making Tax Digital: what changes from April 2026</h2>

<p>Making Tax Digital for Income Tax (MTD for ITSA) is the biggest near-term change for associates. From <strong>6 April 2026</strong> it becomes mandatory for sole traders whose <strong>qualifying income is over £50,000</strong>, where qualifying income means gross trading plus property income, tested on the relevant prior-year Self Assessment return. The threshold then steps down to <strong>£30,000 from April 2027</strong> and <strong>£20,000 from April 2028</strong>. Because most full-time associates gross well above £50,000, the majority are <strong>caught from April 2026</strong>.</p>

<p>In scope, you must keep digital records and submit <strong>quarterly updates</strong> through MTD-compatible software, followed by a year-end finalisation in place of the old single return. If you still run on a shoebox of receipts and a spreadsheet built in January, now is the moment to move to software and a habit of recording as you go. The discipline that MTD forces is exactly the discipline that makes payments on account and expense claims painless.</p>

<h2>Your NHS pension in brief</h2>

<p>Most associates accrue in the <strong>NHS Pension Scheme</strong> as practitioners, and it remains one of the strongest features of the dental career. Two points matter at the tax level. First, the <strong>annual allowance</strong> caps tax-relieved pension growth at <strong>£60,000</strong> for 2026/27. For a defined-benefit scheme like the NHS one, what counts is the capitalised <strong>growth</strong> in your benefits (the pension input amount), not the contributions you pay, so a busy year or a pay step can push you over without you noticing. The allowance <strong>tapers</strong> only where threshold income exceeds £200,000 <strong>and</strong> adjusted income exceeds £260,000, which is the territory of senior principals rather than typical associates. Second, if you ever consider incorporating, remember that <strong>dividends are not pensionable</strong>: switching profit into a salary-plus-dividend mix can quietly shrink your NHS pension accrual, so the pension cost has to be weighed against any tax saving, never ignored.</p>

<h2>Common mistakes associates make</h2>

<ul>
<li><strong>Assuming the contract settles your status.</strong> Substance beats paperwork, so the way you actually work is what HMRC tests.</li>
<li><strong>Budgeting for a Class 2 weekly charge.</strong> It is gone from 6 April 2024 for anyone above the small-profits threshold.</li>
<li><strong>Forgetting the payment on account.</strong> The first-year January bill is bigger than one year's tax. Set money aside as you earn.</li>
<li><strong>Claiming home-to-practice travel.</strong> That is commuting and is not allowable.</li>
<li><strong>Leaving MTD until the deadline.</strong> If you cross £50,000 you need compatible software and digital records from April 2026.</li>
<li><strong>Looking at the incorporation tax saving alone.</strong> Always price in the NHS pension accrual you would give up.</li>
</ul>

<h2>When to get specialist help</h2>

<p>Plenty of associates handle a straightforward return themselves. It is worth bringing in a specialist dental accountant once your affairs get more involved: when you earn over £100,000 and hit the personal-allowance taper, when you work across several practices with different agreements, when you are weighing incorporation against your NHS pension, or when you are moving into a principal role. A dental-savvy adviser maps your <strong>fee statements</strong> to your return rather than just your bank balance, which matters because timing differences between production, collection and lab recharges are common in dentistry and the return should reflect what is actually taxable.</p>

<p>The aim throughout is simple: pay the right amount, on time, on profit after genuine costs, with records that stand up to scrutiny. That is not aggressive planning, it is what HMRC expects.
