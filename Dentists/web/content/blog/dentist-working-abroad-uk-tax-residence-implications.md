<p>A UK dentist offered a post abroad, a year in Australia, a stint in the Middle East, a commute to a clinic in Ireland, usually makes one assumption: leave the country, stop paying UK tax. It is the most common and most expensive misconception in this area. Whether you remain liable to UK tax does not turn on having a job overseas. It turns on your <strong>tax residence</strong>, and tax residence is decided by a precise legal test that can keep you <strong>UK-resident even after you have moved</strong>.</p>

<p>This guide walks through that test, the <strong>Statutory Residence Test</strong> in Finance Act 2013 Schedule 45, in the order it actually applies. We cover the automatic tests that can settle the question quickly, the sufficient ties test that catches most dentists who keep a foot in the UK, the day-count tables, split-year treatment for the year you move, and the crucial difference between being taxed on your <strong>worldwide income</strong> and only on your <strong>UK-source income</strong>. The aim is to stop you assuming non-residence, because the assumption is usually wrong, and a wrong assumption here produces an unwelcome UK tax bill on income you thought was safely overseas.</p>

<h2>The question that decides everything: are you still UK tax-resident?</h2>

<p>Your UK tax exposure on overseas work follows one fork in the road. If you remain <strong>UK-resident</strong>, you are taxable in the UK on your <strong>worldwide income</strong>, including everything you earn abroad, with relief to avoid double taxation. If you become <strong>non-resident</strong>, your UK liability generally narrows to <strong>UK-source income</strong> only. Everything else in this guide is about which side of that fork you land on, because it is worth thousands of pounds and is not a matter of choice or intention. It is determined mechanically by the Statutory Residence Test.</p>

<h2>The Statutory Residence Test, in order</h2>

<p>The test runs in a fixed sequence for each tax year, and you apply the steps in order until one gives an answer:</p>

<ol>
<li><strong>Automatic overseas tests.</strong> If you meet any one, you are <strong>non-resident</strong> for the year, and you stop.</li>
<li><strong>Automatic UK tests.</strong> If you have not been made non-resident above and you meet any one of these, you are <strong>resident</strong>.</li>
<li><strong>Sufficient ties test.</strong> If neither set of automatic tests is decisive, you count your UK ties and read them against your days in the UK.</li>
</ol>

<p>Throughout, a <strong>day</strong> generally counts if you are in the UK at <strong>midnight</strong>. That midnight rule matters, because it shapes how return trips are counted.</p>

<h2>Automatic overseas tests</h2>

<p>You are automatically <strong>non-resident</strong> if any one of these applies:</p>

<ul>
<li>You spend <strong>fewer than 16 days</strong> in the UK in the tax year, and you were UK-resident in one or more of the previous three years.</li>
<li>You spend <strong>fewer than 46 days</strong> in the UK in the tax year, and you were not UK-resident in any of the previous three years.</li>
<li>You work <strong>full-time overseas</strong> (broadly 35 hours a week on average) with <strong>fewer than 91 days</strong> in the UK and <strong>no more than 30 UK workdays</strong>.</li>
</ul>

<p>For a dentist genuinely relocating, the full-time-work-overseas test is the realistic route to non-residence, but note its conditions: enough hours, very few UK days, almost no UK working. A dentist who pops back regularly or works the odd UK locum shift can fail it.</p>

<h2>Automatic UK tests</h2>

<p>If no overseas test was met, you are automatically <strong>resident</strong> if any one of these applies:</p>

<ul>
<li>You spend <strong>183 days or more</strong> in the UK in the tax year.</li>
<li>Your <strong>only home</strong>, or all of your homes, is in the UK for a qualifying period.</li>
<li>You work <strong>full-time in the UK</strong> over a 365-day period.</li>
</ul>

<p>The 183-day test is the one most people know, but the only-home test catches dentists who keep their UK house and have nowhere abroad they could call a home. If you keep your UK home and have not properly established one overseas, you can be resident regardless of a fairly low day count.</p>

<h2>The sufficient ties test</h2>

<p>Most dentists who move abroad but keep links to the UK are decided here. You count how many of the five <strong>UK ties</strong> you have, then read across to your day count. The five ties are:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Family tie:</strong> a UK-resident spouse or civil partner, or a minor child.</li>
<li><strong>Accommodation tie:</strong> available UK accommodation that you use during the year.</li>
<li><strong>Work tie:</strong> 40 or more UK workdays of at least three hours.</li>
<li><strong>90-day tie:</strong> 90 or more days in the UK in either of the previous two tax years.</li>
<li><strong>Country tie:</strong> the UK is the country where you spend the most midnights. This one applies to <strong>leavers only</strong>.</li>
</ul>

<p>The more ties you keep, the fewer days you can spend in the UK before becoming resident. The tables run as follows.</p>

<h2>Arrivers versus leavers: the day-count tables</h2>

<p>The rules distinguish <strong>arrivers</strong> (not UK-resident in any of the previous three years) from <strong>leavers</strong> (UK-resident in one or more of them). Leavers face the tougher thresholds.</p>

<p><strong>Arrivers</strong> become UK-resident if:</p>

<ul>
<li>46 to 90 days in the UK and they have <strong>all 4</strong> ties;</li>
<li>91 to 120 days and they have <strong>3 or more</strong> ties;</li>
<li>over 120 days and they have <strong>2 or more</strong> ties.</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Leavers</strong> become UK-resident if:</p>

<ul>
<li>16 to 45 days in the UK and they have <strong>4 or more</strong> ties;</li>
<li>46 to 90 days and they have <strong>3 or more</strong> ties;</li>
<li>91 to 120 days and they have <strong>2 or more</strong> ties;</li>
<li>over 120 days and they have <strong>1 or more</strong> ties.</li>
</ul>

<p>A UK dentist moving abroad is almost always a <strong>leaver</strong> in the year of departure. So a dentist who keeps a UK home (accommodation tie) and has a spouse who stays in the UK (family tie) already has two ties, and could become resident on as few as <strong>46 days</strong>. That is barely six weekends. This is exactly why a clean break is harder than it looks, and why counting days alone is never the answer.</p>

<h2>Split-year treatment</h2>

<p>Residence is normally an all-or-nothing call for a whole tax year, which would be unfair in the year you actually move. <strong>Split-year treatment</strong> can divide the year of departure (or arrival) into a UK part and an overseas part, so you are taxed as resident only for the part before you left. It applies in specific recognised cases, such as leaving to work full-time abroad. Where it applies, your overseas earnings after the move can escape UK tax even though you were resident for the earlier part of the year. It is not automatic, and you have to meet the conditions for one of the cases, so it is a point to confirm, not assume.</p>

<h2>If you stay UK-resident: worldwide income and double-tax relief</h2>

<p>If the test keeps you UK-resident, your overseas dental income is within the UK net, taxed alongside any UK income. To stop that income being taxed twice, <strong>double taxation relief</strong> under the relevant treaty gives you a credit for the foreign tax you paid on the same earnings. The mechanics depend on the country and its treaty with the UK, but the broad effect is that you pay roughly the <strong>higher</strong> of the two countries' rates on that income, not the sum of both. The point to grasp is that staying resident does not mean double tax, but it does mean your overseas earnings are reported and assessed in the UK, which is precisely the outcome dentists assume they have escaped by moving.</p>

<h2>If you become non-resident: UK-source income only</h2>

<p>If you do achieve non-residence, your UK liability generally narrows to <strong>UK-source income</strong>, for example UK rental profits or certain UK earnings. Your overseas dental income then falls outside UK tax. This is the result most dentists are aiming for, and it is achievable, but it generally requires a <strong>genuine, sustained break</strong>: real full-time work abroad, few UK days, and care over the ties you keep. A single year away while keeping a UK home and family rarely gets you there.</p>

<h2>Counting days correctly: the detail that decides borderline cases</h2>

<p>Because the whole test pivots on day counts, it pays to know how a day is counted, since a careless tally can produce a wrong answer. The basic rule is the <strong>midnight test</strong>: a day counts as a UK day if you are in the UK at the end of it, at midnight. So a day-trip where you arrive in the morning and leave before midnight generally does not count, while a single overnight stay does. There are anti-avoidance refinements for people who spend a lot of time in the UK without staying overnight (the <strong>deeming rule</strong>, which can count days even without a midnight present, where you have several ties and a history of UK presence), and exceptions for <strong>exceptional circumstances</strong> beyond your control, such as serious illness, subject to a cap. The practical message is to keep a contemporaneous record of your travel, dates in and out, with evidence, because in a borderline case the burden is on you to show your day count, and a reconstructed guess years later is worth little.</p>

<p>Workdays matter too, separately from days of presence. The <strong>work tie</strong> turns on 40 or more UK workdays of at least three hours, and the full-time-work-overseas test caps UK workdays at 30. A dentist who returns to do the occasional UK locum shift can rack up workdays that quietly tip them into a tie or break an overseas test, even if their total days in the UK look modest. If non-residence matters to you, treat UK clinical work while you are abroad as something to count deliberately, not do casually.</p>

<h2>Residence is not the same as domicile</h2>

<p>One area to keep separate in your mind is <strong>domicile</strong>, which is a different concept from residence and answers a different question. Residence, through the Statutory Residence Test, decides whether the UK taxes you on your income for a given year. Domicile is about your long-term, permanent home and historically governed whether non-UK-domiciled residents could use the remittance basis for foreign income and gains. From <strong>6 April 2025</strong> the old non-dom remittance basis was replaced by a new <strong>foreign income and gains (FIG) regime</strong>, which offers relief on foreign income and gains for a limited number of years to people who become UK-resident after a long period abroad. For the typical UK-born, UK-domiciled dentist taking a job overseas this is usually not in point, but it can matter for a dentist arriving in the UK from a long spell abroad, or one with a genuinely foreign domicile. It is a specialist question, and it is flagged here only so you do not confuse it with the residence test, which is the one that decides your position when you leave.</p>

<h2>What this means for the tax you actually pay</h2>

<p>Bringing the pieces together, the realistic outcomes for a UK dentist working abroad fall into three broad shapes. If you remain <strong>UK-resident</strong>, you report your overseas dental income on your UK Self Assessment return, pay UK tax on it, and claim a credit for the foreign tax suffered, so you end up paying broadly the higher of the two rates. If you achieve <strong>non-residence for a full year</strong>, your overseas income falls outside UK tax for that year, and only UK-source income (such as UK rents) remains in the UK net. If you qualify for <strong>split-year treatment</strong> in the year you leave, the year is divided so your post-departure overseas earnings are sheltered even though you were resident for the earlier UK part. Which of these you land in is not a matter of preference; it follows mechanically from the test, the days you spend, and the ties you keep. Planning is about shaping those inputs deliberately, in advance, so the mechanical answer is the one you want.</p>

<p>Two professional points sit alongside the tax. Your <strong>NHS pension</strong> accrual normally pauses when you stop UK NHS employment, with your existing benefits preserved; whether any option exists to maintain or later rejoin is a question for NHSBSA, not a general rule, so confirm your position before leaving. Your <strong>GDC registration</strong>, which you need to return to UK practice, usually continues only if you keep paying the retention fee and meeting continuing professional development requirements. Plan both around the move rather than discovering the gaps on your return.</p>

<h2>Common dentist scenarios</h2>

<ul>
<li><strong>A year in Australia.</strong> To be non-resident you would need to meet the full-time-work-overseas test: enough hours, under 91 UK days, no more than 30 UK workdays. Keep a UK home and a UK-resident spouse and the ties test may pull you back to resident, especially if you return for visits. Split-year treatment may apply for the departure year if you qualify.</li>
<li><strong>A weekly commute to Ireland.</strong> Working abroad but sleeping in the UK at midnight on most nights keeps your UK day count high, so you are very likely to remain UK-resident and taxed on worldwide income, with treaty relief for Irish tax.</li>
<li><strong>Locum stints abroad between UK work.</strong> Short overseas spells interspersed with UK locum work almost never achieve non-residence, because the UK days and UK workdays accumulate. Expect to stay resident and report the overseas income.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Planning the move: the levers you actually control</h2>

<p>Because the test is mechanical, the way to influence the outcome is to shape its inputs deliberately, before you leave. The main levers a dentist controls are these. <strong>Days in the UK</strong>: keep a deliberate budget for the year and track it, because the difference between 45 and 46 days, or between 90 and 91, can change the answer. <strong>UK workdays</strong>: if non-residence matters, minimise UK clinical work, since both the work tie and the full-time-work-overseas cap turn on workdays. <strong>Your UK home</strong>: keeping a home available and used is an accommodation tie, and having your <em>only</em> home in the UK can trigger an automatic UK test, so what you do with your property matters. <strong>Where your family lives</strong>: a UK-resident spouse or minor child is a family tie. None of these should override your real life and family decisions, but each should be made with open eyes, because each feeds directly into the residence result.</p>

<p>The corollary is that <strong>order and timing</strong> matter. Establishing genuine full-time work abroad, properly reducing UK days, and qualifying for split-year treatment in the departure year are things you set up in advance, not after the fact. A dentist who leaves casually, keeps the UK house, returns most months and does the odd UK shift will usually find they never broke residence at all, and that the overseas income they assumed was tax-free is reportable in the UK. The same dentist, advised before departure, might structure the move to achieve a clean split-year and genuine non-residence thereafter. The facts that differ are small and controllable; the tax difference is large.</p>

<h2>Returning to the UK</h2>

<p>Finally, think about the return leg before you go, because the residence test applies to the year you come back as well. If you were non-resident and return to the UK, the year of return can itself be a <strong>split year</strong>, with UK tax resuming from the point you re-establish UK residence. If you were only ever a <strong>leaver</strong> who never broke residence, there is nothing to resume because you never left the UK tax net. And if you spent time genuinely non-resident and built up foreign income or gains, the rules on what the UK can tax on your return, including the temporary non-residence anti-avoidance rules that can claw certain income and gains back into UK charge if your absence was short, become relevant. The practical point is that a stint abroad has a UK tax beginning and a UK tax end, and both should be planned as a pair, not just the departure.</p>

<h2>Get advice before you assume non-residence</h2>

<p>The single most important message is this: <strong>do not assume you become non-resident just because you take a job abroad.</strong> The Statutory Residence Test is mechanical and fact-specific, the ties test commonly keeps a leaver UK-resident, and small choices, how many days you return, whether you keep a UK home, where your family lives, can flip the result. Split-year treatment and double-tax relief both carry conditions. A specialist accountant can model your residence position for each affected year, structure the move to reach the result you actually want, and make sure you are not surprised by a UK tax charge on overseas earnings. Plan it before you leave, because once the days are counted, they cannot be uncounted.</p>
