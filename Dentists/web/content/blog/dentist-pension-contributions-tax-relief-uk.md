<p>Pension tax relief is one of the few genuinely large levers a UK dentist still controls. Used well, it converts income that would otherwise be taxed at 40% or 45% into long-term savings at full relief. Used carelessly, it produces the opposite: an annual allowance charge that quietly hands back the relief you thought you had banked. For dentists the rules are unusually layered, because most of you carry a defined-benefit NHS pension alongside any private saving.</p>

<p>This guide explains how relief works at your marginal rate, how the £60,000 annual allowance is measured (which is not the same as the cash you pay in), why the NHS pension input amount trips up high earners, how the taper and Scheme Pays interact, and how carry-forward, AVCs, Added Pension and employer contributions fit together. It applies whether you are a self-employed associate or an incorporated practice owner, and points to deeper dives on each sub-topic.</p>

<h2>How pension tax relief works for dentists</h2>

<p>When you make a pension contribution you receive income tax relief at your <strong>marginal rate</strong>: 20% for a basic-rate payer, 40% for a higher-rate payer and 45% for an additional-rate payer. The relief is the whole point, because it lets you fund a pension with money that has not yet been taxed (or recovers the tax if it already has).</p>

<p>There are two delivery mechanisms, and dentists frequently use both at once:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Relief at source.</strong> Used by personal pensions and SIPPs. You pay in net, the provider reclaims 20% basic-rate relief and adds it to your pot, and any higher or additional-rate relief is claimed through your Self Assessment return. A higher-rate associate paying £8,000 net sees £10,000 land in the pension, then reclaims a further £2,000 through their tax return.</li>
<li><strong>Net pay arrangement.</strong> Used by the NHS Pension Scheme and many workplace schemes. Your contribution comes out of pay before income tax is calculated, so full relief is given immediately and there is nothing to reclaim.</li>
</ul>

<p>The table below shows the cash mechanics of a £10,000 gross personal contribution at each band.</p>

<tr><th>Marginal rate</th><th>Gross contribution</th><th>Tax relief</th><th>Net cost to you</th></tr>
</thead>

<p>Two limits sit on top of this. First, personal relief is capped at <strong>100% of your relevant UK earnings</strong> in the tax year (associate fees, salary and similar, not dividends or rental income), with a £3,600 gross floor for those with little or no earnings. Second, the total tax-relieved pension input across all your schemes is capped by the <strong>annual allowance</strong>, which we turn to next and which is where the dental complications begin.</p>

<h3>The personal allowance trap, and why relief is sometimes worth more than 40%</h3>

<p>One quirk routinely affects associates and owners with income in the £100,000 to £125,140 band. Your personal allowance is withdrawn by £1 for every £2 of income above £100,000, creating an effective marginal rate of around 60% across that band. A pension contribution that pulls your income back below £100,000 reclaims relief at that effective rate, not just 40%, so for a dentist hovering just above £100,000 it is often the single highest-value contribution of the year.</p>

<h2>The annual allowance, and what it actually measures</h2>

<p>The annual allowance is the maximum tax-relieved pension input you can have in a tax year across all your pensions combined. For <strong>2025/26 it is £60,000, and it is unchanged for 2026/27</strong>. Input above your available allowance triggers an <strong>annual allowance charge</strong> at your marginal rate, which is designed to remove the relief on the excess.</p>

<p>The critical point, and the one that catches dentists most often, is how input is measured for each pension type:</p>

<ul>
<li>For a <strong>defined-contribution pension</strong> (a SIPP, a personal pension, or NHS money-purchase AVCs), the input is simply the gross money paid in, by you and by any employer.</li>
<li>For a <strong>defined-benefit scheme</strong> like the NHS Pension Scheme, the input is the <strong>pension input amount</strong>: the capitalised growth in your accrued benefits over the year, not the contributions paid. This is a calculated figure, and it can be much larger than the cash that left your bank.</li>
</ul>

<p>That distinction is the heart of the dental annual-allowance problem, so it deserves its own section.</p>

<h3>The NHS pension input amount problem for high earners</h3>

<p>The 2015 CARE scheme adds <strong>1/54th of your pensionable earnings</strong> to your pension each year and revalues your active benefits by <strong>CPI plus 1.5%</strong>. The pension input amount roughly captures the year-on-year increase in the capital value of those benefits, after stripping out a CPI inflation adjustment. In a flat-earnings year the input amount is modest. In a year with a meaningful pay rise, a backdated seniority payment, or a jump in pensionable NHS-derived profit, the calculation multiplies the extra annual pension by a large factor and the input amount can spike well into five figures.</p>

<p>This is why a dentist who has paid no more than usual into the scheme can still breach a £60,000 allowance: it is the <em>growth</em> in promised benefits that is measured, not the cash. Your figure appears on the <strong>pension savings statement</strong> from NHS Pensions, requested through the NHS Business Services Authority portal. For how the scheme growth is valued and how to read the statement, see our guide on <a href="/blog/associate-tax/nhs-superannuation-pension-annual-allowance-uk-dentists">NHS superannuation and your pension annual allowance</a>, and for what feeds the calculation, our guide on <a href="/blog/nhs-pension/nhs-pension-pensionable-pay-dentists">what counts as NHS pensionable pay</a>.</p>

<h2>The tapered annual allowance for high earners</h2>

<p>High-earning dentists face a second restriction. The annual allowance <strong>tapers</strong> where you cross two income tests in the same year:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Threshold income</strong> exceeds <strong>£200,000</strong> (broadly your total taxable income less your own personal pension contributions), and</li>
<li><strong>Adjusted income</strong> exceeds <strong>£260,000</strong> (broadly your total taxable income plus the value of all pension input, including the NHS pension input amount and any employer contributions).</li>
</ul>

<p>If, and only if, both are exceeded, the allowance reduces by <strong>£1 for every £2 of adjusted income above £260,000</strong>, down to a <strong>£10,000 floor</strong>. The two-gate structure matters. Because a personal pension contribution reduces threshold income, it can sometimes keep you under the £200,000 first gate and switch the taper off entirely, even when adjusted income is high.</p>

<p>The taper assumes threshold income is also above £200,000 in each row. It bites hardest on practice owners with strong profits and high-earning associates with substantial private income on top of NHS work. The interaction with NHS pension growth is where it gets genuinely awkward, because the NHS input amount inflates adjusted income at the same time as it consumes the allowance. We work through the taper arithmetic in detail, including how to estimate threshold and adjusted income, in our dedicated guide on <a href="/blog/nhs-pension/annual-allowance-tapering-nhs-dentists-pension">how the annual allowance taper affects NHS dentists</a>.</p>

<h2>Scheme Pays: settling an annual allowance charge</h2>

<p>If you breach the allowance and the charge is large, you do not always have to find the cash yourself. <strong>Scheme Pays</strong> lets the NHS Pension Scheme settle the charge in exchange for a permanent reduction in your eventual benefits, applied through scheme factors.</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Mandatory Scheme Pays</strong> is available where the charge <strong>exceeds £2,000</strong> and your pension input amount <strong>exceeds the standard £60,000</strong> allowance. The scheme must accept the request.</li>
<li><strong>Voluntary Scheme Pays</strong> covers other cases, including a charge driven only by the taper where input is below £60,000. It is at the scheme's discretion.</li>
</ul>

<p>The election deadline for mandatory Scheme Pays is <strong>31 July</strong> in the year following the relevant tax year. For many high-earning dentists it is the cash-flow-sensible route, since settling a five-figure charge from taxed income in one go is painful. It is not free, though: you trade present cash for a smaller pension later, so it deserves a deliberate decision rather than a default.</p>

<h2>Carry-forward: smoothing a spike</h2>

<p>You are not limited to a single year's allowance. After using the <strong>current year's allowance first</strong>, you can <strong>carry forward unused allowance from the previous three tax years</strong>, provided you were a member of a registered pension scheme in each of those years. This is the standard tool for absorbing a one-off pension-input spike before a charge ever arises.</p>

<p>Worked example. Suppose a dentist's NHS pension input amount jumps to £75,000 in 2026/27 after a backdated pay award, against the £60,000 allowance, a £15,000 excess. Their input in the prior three years was lower, leaving unused allowance of, say, £8,000, £6,000 and £10,000. They first use the full £60,000 for 2026/27, then draw £15,000 of carry-forward from the oldest available years. The excess is fully covered and there is no charge at all. Without checking carry-forward, the same dentist might have assumed a £15,000 excess taxed at 45%, roughly £6,750, that simply did not exist.</p>

<p>Note that carry-forward lifts the annual allowance ceiling, but it does not lift the separate relief cap: a <strong>personal</strong> contribution still cannot exceed 100% of your relevant UK earnings in the year you pay it. Carry-forward is most powerful for a large <strong>employer</strong> contribution from a dental company, which is not constrained by your personal earnings.</p>

<h2>AVCs and Added Pension inside the NHS scheme</h2>

<p>NHS scheme members who want to save more in a tax-efficient way have two in-scheme routes, and both attract income tax relief within the annual allowance:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Money Purchase AVCs (MPAVC).</strong> A separate defined-contribution pot run by the scheme's AVC providers. Contributions are gross money in, so they count towards the allowance pound for pound. If you later access a money-purchase pot flexibly, be aware of the <strong>money purchase annual allowance of £10,000</strong>, which restricts future DC contributions once triggered.</li>
<li><strong>Added Pension.</strong> Buys extra defined-benefit accrual inside the 2015 scheme. Because it increases your DB benefits, it increases your pension input amount and so uses allowance through the input calculation rather than as a cash contribution.</li>
</ul>

<p>For a high earner already close to the allowance, Added Pension can be a trap that quietly pushes the input amount over the line, while for someone with plenty of headroom it is an efficient way to build guaranteed, inflation-linked income. The right answer depends on your input-amount position, not on the headline relief.</p>

<h2>Employer contributions for incorporated practice owners</h2>

<p>If you have incorporated your practice, the company opens a route that a sole trader or partner does not have: it can make <strong>employer pension contributions</strong> directly into your private pension (a SIPP or workplace personal pension). These are attractive for three reasons. They are deductible against corporation tax on a <strong>paid basis</strong> under <strong>section 196 of the Finance Act 2004</strong>, so the contribution must actually leave the company before the year-end to score relief in that period. They carry <strong>no National Insurance</strong>, unlike salary. And they are not a benefit in kind in your hands.</p>

<p>That said, the same two constraints apply: employer contributions count towards your <strong>£60,000 annual allowance</strong> alongside any NHS pension input, and they count towards <strong>adjusted income</strong> for the taper, so a large company contribution can itself deepen the taper for a high-earning principal. The mechanics, the dividend comparison and the year-end timing are covered in full in our deep dive on <a href="/blog/practice-finance/employer-pension-contributions-dental-company-profit-extraction">employer pension contributions as a profit-extraction route for dental company owners</a>.</p>

<h3>The incorporation pension trap</h3>

<p>There is a cost on the other side of incorporation that is easy to miss. For an incorporated principal taking a PAYE salary plus dividends, only the <strong>salary is pensionable</strong> in the NHS scheme and <strong>dividends are not pensionable</strong>. A dentist who converts pensionable NHS-derived profit into a low salary and a large dividend cuts their NHS pensionable pay to the salary level, losing CARE accrual that can total tens of thousands of pounds over a run to retirement. (One narrow exception exists for an incorporated GDS or PDS contract-holding provider, who can pension drawn income up to a net pensionable earnings ceiling; an incorporated associate cannot pension dividends.) An employer contribution into a private SIPP replaces some of that lost saving, but it builds a defined-contribution pot, not guaranteed NHS benefits, so the two should always be weighed together rather than the corporation-tax saving alone.</p>

<h2>A combined worked example</h2>

<p>Consider an incorporated practice owner in 2026/27 with adjusted income of £280,000, comfortably over both income gates. Their tapered allowance is £50,000 (the £20,000 excess over £260,000, halved, taken off £60,000). Their NHS pension input amount for the year is £22,000. That leaves £28,000 of headroom before a charge bites.</p>

<p>A £28,000 employer contribution exactly fills that headroom: deductible against corporation tax, no NIC, and within the tapered allowance. A £40,000 contribution would exceed the allowance by £12,000, but £12,000 or more of unused allowance carried forward from the previous three years would absorb it with no charge. Pushing beyond even that triggers an annual allowance charge at their marginal rate, which the private DC pot cannot settle through NHS Scheme Pays. The lesson is that the NHS input amount, the taper and any company contribution must be modelled in one calculation, because each one moves the others.</p>

<h2>Common mistakes dentists make</h2>

<ul>
<li><strong>Assuming the allowance measures cash.</strong> For the NHS scheme it measures benefit growth (the pension input amount), which can spike in a pay-rise year even if contributions look normal.</li>
<li><strong>Ignoring carry-forward.</strong> A breach is frequently covered in full by unused allowance from the previous three years, turning an expected charge into nothing.</li>
<li><strong>Forgetting the two taper gates.</strong> Both threshold income (£200,000) and adjusted income (£260,000) must be exceeded; a personal contribution that cuts threshold income can switch the taper off.</li>
<li><strong>Overlooking the personal-allowance band.</strong> A contribution that drops income below £100,000 reclaims relief at an effective rate near 60%.</li>
<li><strong>Missing the Scheme Pays deadline.</strong> Mandatory Scheme Pays must be elected by 31 July following the tax year.</li>
<li><strong>Pricing incorporation on tax alone.</strong> The dividend route saves tax but reduces NHS pensionable pay; both effects belong in the same decision.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Practical planning steps</h2>

<ul>
<li>Request your NHS pension savings statement each year and read the pension input amount, not just your contributions.</li>
<li>Calculate threshold and adjusted income to confirm whether the taper applies before deciding any contribution.</li>
<li>Check the previous three years for unused allowance before assuming a charge is due.</li>
<li>Time contributions towards higher-income years to maximise the marginal-rate relief, and watch the £100,000 personal-allowance band.</li>
<li>For incorporated owners, coordinate employer contributions with salary, dividends and NHS pension growth in a single profit-extraction plan, not in isolation.</li>
<li>Keep records of every contribution and each year's input amount, because carry-forward depends on an accurate three-year history.</li>
</ul>

<p>The pension rules reward dentists who plan a year ahead and punish those who learn their input amount after the fact. Because the NHS pension input, the taper, Scheme Pays and any company contribution all interact, a dental-specialist accountant who models the whole position together is usually the difference between using your allowance fully and stumbling into an avoidable charge.</p>
