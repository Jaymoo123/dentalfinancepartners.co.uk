<h2>What Is a Dental Practice Valuation and Why Does It Matter?</h2>

<p>A dental practice valuation is an estimate of what a practice is worth on the open market. For a UK dentist, it is the single most important number when buying, selling, or restructuring a practice. The valuation determines the <strong>practice price</strong> you pay or receive, the <strong>goodwill</strong> figure in the accounts, and the tax consequences of the transaction.</p>

<p>Valuation is not a single formula. It depends on the practice's NHS/private mix, location, patient list stability, earnings history, and the buyer's appetite. Two identical practices in different regions can have very different values. This guide explains the three main methods used by dental accountants and practice brokers, and how to apply them to your situation.</p>

<p>If you are a principal planning a sale or a buyer evaluating a target, understanding valuation is essential before you instruct a specialist dental accountant or broker. For a full checklist of what to examine before buying, see our <a href="/dental-guides/practice-purchase-financial-due-diligence">financial due diligence guide for practice buyers</a>.</p>

<h2>The Three Main Valuation Methods for UK Dental Practices</h2>

<p>No single method is universally correct. Most professional valuations use at least two methods and cross-check the result. The three most common are:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>EBITDA multiple method</strong>, the most widely used for established practices</li>
<li><strong>Goodwill as a percentage of fee income</strong>, a rule-of-thumb approach</li>
<li><strong>Asset-based valuation</strong>, used for smaller or less profitable practices</li>
</ul>

<p>Each method has strengths and weaknesses. The right approach depends on the practice's size, profitability, and the purpose of the valuation (sale, partnership buy-in, or divorce).</p>

<h3>EBITDA Multiple Method</h3>

<p>EBITDA stands for Earnings Before Interest, Tax, Depreciation, and Amortisation. It measures the practice's underlying trading profit, excluding one-off costs and non-cash charges. The valuer applies a multiple to this figure to arrive at the enterprise value (the total value of the practice's business, excluding property).</p>

<p>For UK dental practices, EBITDA multiples typically range from <strong>0.6 to 1.4 times</strong> adjusted EBITDA. The exact multiple depends on:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>NHS vs private mix</strong>: Private-heavy practices (70%+ private) often command higher multiples (1.0-1.4x) because private fees are less constrained by UDA contract values and can be increased more easily. NHS-heavy practices (80%+ NHS) typically attract lower multiples (0.6-0.9x) due to UDA rate caps and contract risk.</li>
<li><strong>Location</strong>: Practices in affluent areas with high private demand (London, South East, commuter belts) tend to achieve multiples at the upper end. Rural or less competitive areas may see lower multiples.</li>
<li><strong>Patient list stability and recall rates</strong>: A practice with 95%+ recall and a full book is worth more than one with high churn.</li>
<li><strong>Property ownership</strong>: If the practice owns the freehold, the property value is separate from the business valuation. If the practice leases, the lease terms (length, rent review provisions) affect the multiple.</li>
<li><strong>Staff retention and associate dependency</strong>: Practices reliant on one principal dentist are riskier than those with stable associates and hygienists.</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Worked example</strong>: A mixed practice in the South East has adjusted EBITDA of £180,000. It is 60% private, 40% NHS, with a stable patient list and a 10-year lease. A valuer applies a multiple of 1.1x. The enterprise value is £198,000. If the practice owns the freehold valued at £400,000, the total practice price would be approximately £598,000 (business + property).</p>

<p>The EBITDA multiple method is the most reliable for established practices with consistent earnings. However, it requires careful adjustment of the EBITDA figure. One-off costs (legal fees for a past dispute, non-recurring equipment repairs) must be added back. Owner-remuneration above market rate must be normalised. For a detailed breakdown of how to adjust EBITDA, see our <a href="/services/practice-valuation">practice valuation service page</a>.</p>

<h3>Goodwill as a Percentage of Fee Income</h3>

<p>This is a simpler rule-of-thumb method often used by brokers for initial price indications. The valuer takes the practice's gross fee income (turnover) and applies a percentage to estimate goodwill value. Typical ranges are:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Private practices</strong>: 40-60% of gross fee income</li>
<li><strong>Mixed practices (50/50 NHS/private)</strong>: 30-50% of gross fee income</li>
<li><strong>NHS-heavy practices</strong>: 20-35% of gross fee income</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Worked example</strong>: A private practice in central London has gross fee income of £500,000. Using 50% goodwill, the goodwill value is £250,000. Adding equipment (£80,000) and leasehold improvements (£30,000) gives a total practice price of £360,000 (assuming no freehold).</p>

<p>This method is quick but imprecise. It does not account for profitability, cost structure, or earnings growth potential. Two practices with identical fee income can have very different EBITDA margins. A practice with £500,000 fee income but £350,000 costs (30% margin) is worth less than one with £500,000 fee income and £250,000 costs (50% margin). The EBITDA multiple method captures this; the percentage-of-fee method does not.</p>

<p>Use this method only as a rough sanity check. For a formal valuation, always use the EBITDA multiple method or instruct a specialist valuer.</p>

<h3>Asset-Based Valuation</h3>

<p>This method values the practice by adding up its net assets: equipment, leasehold improvements, stock, and debtors, minus liabilities. It is most relevant for:</p>

<ul>
<li>Very small practices with low profitability</li>
<li>Practices where goodwill is minimal (e.g., a struggling NHS practice with high UDA clawback risk)</li>
<li>Partnership buy-ins where the incoming partner is buying a share of the assets</li>
</ul>

<p>Asset-based valuation typically produces a lower figure than the EBITDA multiple method because it ignores the practice's earning power. It is rarely used alone for a going-concern sale. However, it is important for tax purposes: the split between goodwill and equipment affects capital gains tax (CGT) and capital allowances claims. For more on this, read our <a href="/dental-guides/goodwill-valuation-and-sale-playbook">goodwill valuation and sale playbook</a>.</p>

<h2>What Drives Practice Price in 2026?</h2>

<p>Several factors are shaping dental practice valuations in 2025/26:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>NHS contract uncertainty</strong>: Ongoing reforms and UDA rate negotiations mean NHS-heavy practices carry more risk. Buyers discount for this, lowering multiples.</li>
<li><strong>Private demand growth</strong>: Post-pandemic, private patient demand has risen. Practices with strong private books and waiting lists command premiums.</li>
<li><strong>Interest rates and borrowing costs</strong>: Higher interest rates reduce what buyers can borrow, which can suppress practice prices. However, the market has adjusted, and well-priced practices still sell.</li>
<li><strong>Property values</strong>: Freehold practice premises have held value well. The property element of a practice price is driven by local commercial property markets, not dental earnings.</li>
<li><strong>Regulatory and compliance costs</strong>: CPD, indemnity, GDC fees, and CQC/HTM 01-05 compliance add overhead. Practices with efficient systems and low compliance risk are more attractive.</li>
</ul>

<p>If you are a principal considering a sale, now is a good time to get a professional valuation. The market remains active for well-run practices. For buyers, understanding the drivers of practice price helps you negotiate effectively and avoid overpaying.</p>

<h2>Common Mistakes in Dental Practice Valuation</h2>

<p>Even experienced dentists make errors when valuing a practice. Here are the most common:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Using the wrong multiple</strong>: Applying a private-practice multiple to an NHS-heavy practice, or vice versa, produces a misleading figure. Always adjust the multiple for the practice's specific mix.</li>
<li><strong>Ignoring property value</strong>: If the practice owns the freehold, the property value must be separated from the business value. A combined figure can confuse tax planning and CGT calculations.</li>
<li><strong>Failing to adjust EBITDA</strong>: Using the raw profit figure without adding back one-off costs, above-market owner salary, or non-recurring items understates the true earnings.</li>
<li><strong>Overlooking goodwill amortisation</strong>: For a company buyer, acquired goodwill is amortised at 6.5% per year (for qualifying goodwill post-1 April 2019). This affects post-tax cash flow and should be factored into the price.</li>
<li><strong>Not getting a specialist valuation</strong>: General practice accountants or online calculators cannot capture the nuances of dental practice valuation. Use a specialist dental accountant or a practice broker with a track record.</li>
</ul>

<h2>How to Get a Professional Dental Practice Valuation</h2>

<p>A professional valuation typically costs between £1,500 and £5,000, depending on the practice's size and complexity. The valuer will:</p>

<ul>
<li>Review the last 3 years of accounts and management accounts</li>
<li>Interview the principal about operations, patient mix, and staff</li>
<li>Adjust EBITDA for one-off items and normalise owner remuneration</li>
<li>Apply an appropriate EBITDA multiple based on comparable sales data</li>
<li>Separate business value from property value (if freehold)</li>
<li>Provide a written report with a valuation range and supporting rationale</li>
</ul>

<p>If you are buying, always commission your own valuation rather than relying on the seller's figure. If you are selling, a professional valuation helps you set a realistic asking price and supports your negotiation position.</p>

<h2>Tax Implications of Practice Price and Goodwill</h2>

<p>The way a practice price is allocated between goodwill, equipment, and property has direct tax consequences:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Goodwill</strong>: For a company buyer, goodwill amortisation gives tax relief at 6.5% per year (post-1 April 2019 acquisitions). For a sole trader or partnership buyer, goodwill is not amortisable for tax purposes, but the cost is deducted on eventual sale.</li>
<li><strong>Equipment</strong>: The buyer can claim capital allowances (AIA at 100% up to £1m) on qualifying fixtures and equipment. This reduces taxable profit in the first year.</li>
<li><strong>Property</strong>: The buyer can claim Structures and Buildings Allowance (3% per year) on qualifying construction or acquisition costs of post-29 October 2018 premises.</li>
<li><strong>CGT on sale</strong>: The seller pays CGT on the gain (sale price minus base cost). Business Asset Disposal Relief (BADR) at 14% in 2025/26 (rising to 18% from April 2026) applies if conditions are met, including a £1m lifetime limit.</li>
</ul>

<p>Getting the allocation wrong can cost thousands in extra tax. Always involve a dental-specialist accountant before finalising the sale or purchase agreement. The percentage-of-fee method is a useful cross-check but should never be used alone. Asset-based valuation is relevant only for specific situations.</p>
