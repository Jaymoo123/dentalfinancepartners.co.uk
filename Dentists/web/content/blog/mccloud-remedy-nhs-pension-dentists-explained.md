<p>The McCloud remedy is one of the most significant changes to the NHS Pension Scheme in recent years, and it directly affects thousands of UK dentists. If you are a dentist who has been a member of the 1995 or 2008 sections of the NHS Pension Scheme at any point between 1 April 2015 and 31 March 2022, you need to understand what this remedy means for your retirement benefits.</p>

<p>In short, the McCloud remedy gives you a choice at retirement. For the remedy period (1 April 2015 to 31 March 2022), you can elect to have your pension benefits calculated under the rules of either your original legacy scheme (1995 or 2008) or the 2015 CARE scheme. This choice is personal and can have a material impact on the value of your NHS pension, your tax position, and your wider retirement planning.</p>

<p>This article explains the McCloud remedy in plain English, focusing on what it means for dentists. We cover who is affected, how the choice works, the tax implications, and what you should do next. As with all pension and tax matters, the details depend on your individual circumstances, so you should take professional advice from a dental-specialist accountant or an independent financial adviser with NHS pension knowledge.</p>

<h2>What Is the McCloud Remedy and Why Does It Matter for Dentists?</h2>

<p>The McCloud remedy stems from a legal challenge. In 2018, the Court of Appeal found that the transitional protections introduced when the 2015 CARE scheme was launched amounted to unlawful age discrimination. Those protections allowed older members (those within 10 years of normal pension age in 2012) to remain in their legacy 1995 or 2008 sections. Younger members were moved to the 2015 scheme. The court ruled this was discriminatory.</p>

<p>The remedy, named after the lead claimant (Lord McCloud), applies to all members of public sector pension schemes, including the NHS Pension Scheme. For dentists, the practical effect is that anyone who had benefits in the 1995 or 2008 sections between 1 April 2015 and 31 March 2022 now has a choice. At retirement, you can decide which set of scheme rules applies to your pension benefits for that remedy period.</p>

<p>This is not a one-size-fits-all fix. The choice you make will affect your annual pension, your lump sum, and your tax position. For many dentists, particularly those with high earnings or who are approaching retirement, the McCloud remedy creates both opportunities and complications.</p>

<h2>Who Is Affected by the McCloud Remedy?</h2>

<p>You are affected if you were a member of the NHS Pension Scheme and you had benefits in the 1995 or 2008 sections at any time between 1 April 2015 and 31 March 2022. This includes:</p>

<ul>
<li>Dentists who remained in the 1995 or 2008 section throughout the remedy period because they were within 10 years of normal pension age in 2012.</li>
<li>Dentists who were moved from the 1995 or 2008 section to the 2015 CARE scheme during the remedy period (typically younger members).</li>
<li>Dentists who left the NHS Pension Scheme during the remedy period and may have taken a refund or transferred benefits.</li>
<li>Dentists who have already retired and taken benefits during the remedy period.</li>
</ul>

<p>If you joined the NHS Pension Scheme for the first time on or after 1 April 2015, you are in the 2015 CARE scheme only and the McCloud remedy does not apply to you. Similarly, if you never held benefits in the 1995 or 2008 sections, you are not affected.</p>

<h2>How Does the McCloud Remedy Choice Work?</h2>

<p>At the point you retire and take your NHS pension benefits, you will be given a choice. For the remedy period (1 April 2015 to 31 March 2022), you can elect to have your benefits calculated under either:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Option A:</strong> The rules of your original legacy scheme (1995 or 2008 section).</li>
<li><strong>Option B:</strong> The rules of the 2015 CARE scheme.</li>
</ul>

<p>This choice is known as the "deferred choice underpin" or DCU. You do not have to make the decision now. You make it when you retire. However, the NHS Business Services Authority (NHS BSA) is currently in the process of remedying benefits for members who have already retired or who will retire before the full remedy is implemented. If you retired between 1 April 2015 and 31 March 2022, you should have received (or will receive) a remedy notice asking you to make your choice.</p>

<p>The key point is that the choice is retrospective. It applies to a period that has already passed. You are effectively deciding which set of pension rules should have applied to your pensionable earnings during those seven years.</p>

<h2>What Are the Practical Differences Between the 1995, 2008, and 2015 Schemes?</h2>

<p>To understand your choice, you need to know how the schemes differ. Here is a summary of the key features relevant to dentists:</p>

<h3>1995 Section</h3>
<ul>
<li>Normal pension age: 60.</li>
<li>Pension accrual: 1/80th of final pensionable pay for each year of service, plus an automatic lump sum of 3/80ths per year.</li>
<li>Benefits are based on final pensionable pay (typically the best of the last three years).</li>
<li>No automatic lump sum commutation (you can commute pension for additional lump sum, subject to HMRC limits).</li>
<li>Pension increases in payment are linked to the Consumer Prices Index (CPI).</li>
</ul>

<h3>2008 Section</h3>
<ul>
<li>Normal pension age: 65.</li>
<li>Pension accrual: 1/60th of final pensionable pay for each year of service.</li>
<li>No automatic lump sum. You can commute part of your pension for a lump sum at retirement (typically up to 25% of the capital value).</li>
<li>Benefits are based on final pensionable pay.</li>
<li>Pension increases in payment are CPI-linked.</li>
</ul>

<h3>2015 CARE Scheme</h3>
<ul>
<li>Normal pension age: State Pension Age (currently 67 for most, but rising).</li>
<li>Pension accrual: 1/54th of pensionable earnings in each scheme year, revalued annually in line with CPI plus 1.5%.</li>
<li>No automatic lump sum. You can commute pension for a lump sum at retirement.</li>
<li>Benefits are based on career-average earnings, not final salary. This can be better for dentists whose earnings fluctuate or decline later in their career.</li>
<li>Pension increases in payment are CPI-linked.</li>
</ul>

<p>The choice between these schemes is not straightforward. For a dentist with a high final salary, the 1995 or 2008 section may produce a larger pension because it is based on final pay. For a dentist whose earnings peaked earlier or who had significant career breaks, the 2015 CARE scheme may be more favourable because it revalues each year's earnings and does not penalise a drop in income near retirement.</p>

<h2>Tax Implications of the McCloud Remedy for Dentists</h2>

<p>The McCloud remedy can create tax complications, particularly around the annual allowance and the lifetime allowance (which was abolished from 6 April 2024 but still relevant for historical periods).</p>

<h3>Annual Allowance</h3>
<p>The annual allowance is the amount of pension growth you can have in a tax year without incurring a tax charge. For the 2025/26 tax year, the standard annual allowance is £60,000. However, the tapered annual allowance applies to individuals with adjusted income above £260,000 and threshold income above £200,000. For dentists with high NHS pension growth, particularly those in the 1995 section with final salary benefits, the annual allowance can be a significant issue.</p>

<p>The McCloud remedy means that your pension growth for the remedy period (2015/16 to 2021/22) may need to be recalculated. If you choose the legacy scheme option, your pension growth for those years will be based on final salary, which could be higher than the CARE revaluation. This could trigger or increase an annual allowance tax charge for those years.</p>

<p>The good news is that HMRC has introduced a "McCloud easement" that allows you to carry forward unused annual allowance from the remedy period to offset any additional charges. However, the calculations are complex and require careful modelling. If you are a high-earning dentist, you should have your pension growth projections run by a specialist accountant or actuary before making your choice.</p>

<h3>Lifetime Allowance (Historical)</h3>
<p>The lifetime allowance was the total amount of pension benefits you could build up without incurring a tax charge. It was abolished from 6 April 2024, but it still applies to benefit crystallisation events (BCEs) that occurred before that date. If you retired during the remedy period (2015-2022) and your benefits exceeded the lifetime allowance at that time, the McCloud remedy could affect your lifetime allowance position. This is a niche but important point for dentists with very large NHS pensions.</p>

<h2>What Should Dentists Do Now?</h2>

<p>If you are affected by the McCloud remedy, here is a practical checklist:</p>

<ol>
<li><strong>Check your NHS Pension records.</strong> Log in to your NHS BSA online account and review your pensionable service history. Confirm which sections you were in during the remedy period.</li>
<li><strong>Understand your choice.</strong> Read the NHS BSA's guidance on the McCloud remedy. The key document is the "McCloud remedy: member guide" available on the NHS BSA website.</li>
<li><strong>Model the outcomes.</strong> If you are approaching retirement, ask your accountant or financial adviser to model the pension benefits under both options. This should include the impact on your annual allowance position and any tax charges.</li>
<li><strong>Consider the timing.</strong> If you are already retired and have received a remedy notice, you have a limited time to respond. Do not ignore it. If you are still working, you do not need to decide now, but you should plan ahead.</li>
<li><strong>Take professional advice.</strong> The McCloud remedy is not a simple "pick the higher pension" decision. The tax implications, particularly around the annual allowance, can be significant. A dental-specialist accountant or an independent financial adviser with NHS pension expertise can help you make an informed choice.</li>
</ol>

<h2>Common Questions About the McCloud Remedy for Dentists</h2>

<h3>Do I have to make the McCloud choice now?</h3>
<p>No. If you are still working and have not yet retired, you do not need to make the choice until you take your pension benefits. However, you should be aware of the implications now so you can plan your retirement and tax position accordingly.</p>

<h3>What if I have already retired?</h3>
<p>If you retired between 1 April 2015 and 31 March 2022, the NHS BSA is in the process of contacting affected members. You will receive a remedy notice asking you to make your choice. You must respond within the timeframe specified in the notice. If you do not respond, your benefits will default to the 2015 CARE scheme for the remedy period.</p>

<h3>Can the McCloud remedy affect my tax return?</h3>
<p>Yes. If the remedy increases your pension growth for a past tax year, you may have an additional annual allowance tax charge to report. You may need to amend previous Self Assessment returns. This is a complex area and you should seek advice from a <a href="/services/dental-accountants">dental-specialist accountant</a>.</p>

<h3>Does the McCloud remedy affect my spouse's or dependant's benefits?</h3>
<p>Yes. The choice you make for your own pension benefits also affects the benefits payable to your spouse, civil partner, or dependants after your death. This is an important factor to consider, particularly if you have a younger spouse who would benefit from a higher ongoing pension.</p>

<h2>Final Thoughts</h2>

<p>The McCloud remedy is a positive development for dentists who were affected by the discriminatory transitional arrangements. It gives you a choice that can increase your pension benefits. But it also introduces complexity, particularly around tax. The choice between the 1995, 2008, or 2015 scheme rules for the remedy period is not obvious. It depends on your earnings history, your retirement age, your health, your family circumstances, and your tax position.</p>

<p>If you are a dentist with NHS pension benefits, do not ignore the McCloud remedy. Start planning now. Review your pension records, understand the options, and take professional advice. A small difference in your choice can mean tens of thousands of pounds in pension benefits and tax charges over your retirement.</p>
