<h2>Introduction: Locum Dentist Expenses for 2025/26</h2>

<p>If you work as a locum dentist in the UK, you are almost certainly self-employed for tax purposes. That means you pay tax and National Insurance on your profits, not your gross income. The difference between gross fees and taxable profit is your allowable expenses.</p>

<p>HMRC allows you to deduct expenses that are "wholly and exclusively" for the purposes of your trade as a locum dentist. This is not a grey area. The rules are clear, but the application depends on your specific working pattern. This guide covers the main categories of locum dentist expenses for the 2025/26 tax year, with practical examples and figures.</p>

<p>We cover locum mileage, locum CPD, locum indemnity, equipment, home office costs, and professional subscriptions. We also highlight common mistakes that trigger HMRC enquiries. For a full review of your specific situation, speak to a <a href="/services/locum-dentist-tax">dental-specialist accountant who works with locum dentists</a>.</p>

<h2>Travel and Locum Mileage: The Most Common Claim</h2>

<p>Travel is the largest expense category for most locum dentists. You can claim the cost of travelling between your home and each practice where you work as a locum. This is not a commute in the employment sense. Each practice is a temporary workplace, and your home is your base of operations.</p>

<p>HMRC accepts that a locum dentist has no permanent workplace. Each assignment is a separate temporary workplace. The key test is that you do not work at the same practice for more than 24 months. If you do, that practice becomes a permanent workplace and travel to it is no longer allowable.</p>

<h3>Mileage Rates for 2025/26</h3>

<p>You have two options for claiming vehicle costs:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>HMRC approved mileage rates (simplified)</strong>: 45p per mile for the first 10,000 business miles in the tax year, then 25p per mile thereafter. This covers all vehicle costs including fuel, insurance, servicing, and depreciation.</li>
<li><strong>Actual costs method</strong>: Keep records of all vehicle expenses (fuel, insurance, repairs, MOT, road tax, finance costs) and claim the business proportion based on your mileage split. You also claim capital allowances on the vehicle cost.</li>
</ul>

<p>For most locum dentists, the simplified mileage rate is easier and often more beneficial. You do not need to keep fuel receipts. You do need a mileage log showing date, destination, purpose, and miles for each journey.</p>

<p><strong>Worked example</strong>: Sarah works as a locum dentist across three practices in the Midlands. In 2025/26 she drives 12,000 business miles. Her claim is: 10,000 miles at 45p = £4,500, plus 2,000 miles at 25p = £500. Total mileage claim = £5,000. She keeps a simple spreadsheet log of each journey.</p>

<h3>What About Travel Between Practices on the Same Day?</h3>

<p>If you work at Practice A in the morning and Practice B in the afternoon, the journey between them is a business journey. You can claim the mileage for that leg. The journey from home to the first practice and from the last practice back home is also business mileage (not commuting).</p>

<p>If you travel from home to a practice, then to another practice, then home, all three legs are claimable. Keep a log of each leg separately.</p>

<h3>Public Transport and Accommodation</h3>

<p>If you use trains, buses, or taxis, keep the receipts. Overnight accommodation is allowable if you are working away from your home area and need to stay overnight. The cost of a hotel room, meals, and incidental expenses are all deductible. HMRC does not apply a fixed scale rate for meals when you are a locum (unlike some employed roles), so keep actual receipts.</p>

<h2>Locum Indemnity: A Compulsory Deduction</h2>

<p>Professional indemnity insurance is mandatory for all GDC-registered dentists. For locum dentists, the cost of your indemnity cover (with MDU, Dental Protection, MDDUS, or another provider) is an allowable expense in full.</p>

<p>If you pay your indemnity annually, claim the full amount in the tax year you pay it. If you pay monthly, claim each payment as it falls due. The cost is typically between £2,000 and £6,000 per year for a locum dentist, depending on your mix of NHS and private work and your claims history.</p>

<p><strong>Important</strong>: If you work through a limited company and the company pays your indemnity, the company claims the expense. If you are self-employed as a sole trader, you claim it on your Self Assessment tax return.</p>

<h2>Locum CPD: Claiming Your Continuing Professional Development</h2>

<p>The GDC requires all dentists to complete 100 hours of verifiable CPD over a five-year cycle. The costs of meeting this requirement are allowable expenses. This includes:</p>

<ul>
<li>Course fees for verifiable CPD events and conferences</li>
<li>Travel and accommodation to attend CPD courses (if the course is not at your usual practice)</li>
<li>Online course subscriptions and e-learning modules</li>
<li>Books, journals, and dental publications directly relevant to your professional development</li>
<li>GDC registration fees (these are a separate allowable expense)</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Worked example</strong>: Tom, a locum dentist, attends a two-day restorative dentistry conference in Manchester. Costs: conference fee £450, train fare £120, hotel for one night £95, meals £40. Total claim = £705. He keeps the conference programme, receipts, and a note of the CPD hours earned.</p>

<p>You cannot claim CPD costs that are not directly related to your work as a dentist. A general management course for a non-dental business would not qualify. But most dental-specific CPD is clearly allowable.</p>

<h2>Equipment and Instruments</h2>

<p>Locum dentists often need to provide their own handpieces, instruments, loupes, or other dental equipment. These are capital items. You can claim capital allowances on the cost.</p>

<p>For 2025/26, the Annual Investment Allowance (AIA) gives 100% tax relief on most plant and machinery up to £1,000,000 per year. This means you can deduct the full cost of equipment in the year you buy it, rather than spreading the relief over several years.</p>

<p>Items that typically qualify include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Handpieces and turbines</li>
<li>Curing lights and ultrasonic scalers</li>
<li>Loupes and headlights</li>
<li>Computers, laptops, and tablets used for practice management or CPD</li>
<li>Dental software and subscriptions</li>
<li>Protective eyewear and PPE</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Important</strong>: If you buy a single item that costs less than £100, you can treat it as a revenue expense (consumable) rather than a capital item. This simplifies your records. For items over £100, claim capital allowances via your tax return.</p>

<p>If you work through a limited company, the company buys the equipment and claims the AIA. If you are a sole trader, you claim it on your Self Assessment.</p>

<h2>Home Office Expenses</h2>

<p>Many locum dentists do some administrative work from home: booking appointments, completing patient notes, managing invoices, arranging CPD, and handling correspondence. If you use part of your home for these purposes, you can claim a proportion of your household costs.</p>

<p>HMRC accepts two methods:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Simplified flat rate</strong>: £10 per month for 25-50 hours of home working per month, £20 per month for 51-100 hours, £26 per month for 101+ hours. This is simple but often underclaims.</li>
<li><strong>Actual cost method</strong>: Work out the proportion of your home used for business (by floor area or number of rooms) and the proportion of time it is used for business. Apply that fraction to costs like mortgage interest (not capital repayments), rent, council tax, utilities, insurance, and broadband.</li>
</ul>

<p>For most locum dentists, the actual cost method yields a higher claim. You need a clear basis of apportionment and records of the costs. A typical claim might be £300-£800 per year for a locum dentist using a spare room as an office for a few hours per week.</p>

<p><strong>Warning</strong>: If you claim home office expenses, HMRC may query whether you have a genuine business use of the home. Keep a log of the hours you work from home and what you do. Do not claim if you only check emails occasionally on your phone.</p>

<h2>Professional Subscriptions and Other Costs</h2>

<p>The following are all allowable expenses for a locum dentist:</p>

<ul>
<li>GDC annual retention fee (currently £978 for dentists in 2025)</li>
<li>BDA membership fees</li>
<li>Dental defence organisation subscriptions (indemnity, covered above)</li>
<li>Practice insurance (if you pay for your own public liability or equipment insurance)</li>
<li>Bank charges on a business bank account</li>
<li>Accountancy fees for preparing your Self Assessment and tax advice</li>
<li>Stationery, postage, and printing</li>
<li>Telephone and internet costs (business proportion only)</li>
<li>Uniform and protective clothing (scrubs, tunics, safety glasses)</li>
</ul>

<p>You cannot claim the cost of clothing that is not protective or uniform. A standard suit or smart dress for work is not allowable, even if you only wear it for locum work.</p>

<h2>Common HMRC Traps for Locum Dentists</h2>

<p>HMRC has a specific interest in locum dentists because the expense profile is often high relative to income. Common triggers for an enquiry include:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Claiming 100% of home costs</strong>: You must apportion. HMRC will ask for your calculation basis.</li>
<li><strong>No mileage log</strong>: Without a contemporaneous log, HMRC may disallow the entire mileage claim. A spreadsheet updated weekly is acceptable. A retrospective estimate is not.</li>
<li><strong>Claiming mileage to the same practice for over 24 months</strong>: After 24 months at one practice, it becomes a permanent workplace. Travel to it is no longer allowable.</li>
<li><strong>Mixing business and personal use of a vehicle</strong>: If you use the same car for personal and business journeys, you must apportion. HMRC will look at your MOT mileage and compare it to your claimed business mileage.</li>
<li><strong>Claiming capital allowances on a car used for business</strong>: Cars do not qualify for AIA. You claim writing-down allowances at 18% or 6% per year depending on CO2 emissions. The simplified mileage rate avoids this complexity.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Should You Be a Sole Trader or Use a Limited Company?</h2>

<p>Most locum dentists are sole traders. It is simpler, cheaper to administer, and avoids the IR35 complications that affect locum dentists working through a limited company. Since 6 April 2021, if the practice engaging you is a medium or large client, it must determine your IR35 status. If it decides you are inside IR35, your limited company must operate PAYE on your fees, removing most of the tax advantage.</p>

<p>For a detailed comparison of the two structures, see our guide on <a href="/dental-guides/associate-tax-survival-guide">associate and locum tax structures</a>. For most locum dentists, sole trader status is the right choice. If you are already trading through a limited company, you should review your IR35 position with a <a href="/services/locum-dentist-tax">specialist locum dentist accountant</a>.</p>

<h2>How to Record and Claim Your Expenses</h2>

<p>You need to keep records for at least 22 months after the end of the tax year (until 31 January 2028 for 2025/26). HMRC can ask to see them. A simple system works:</p>

<ul>
<li>Use a spreadsheet or accounting app to log mileage each week</li>
<li>Keep digital copies of receipts (scan or photograph them)</li>
<li>Separate business and personal bank accounts</li>
<li>Record your expenses in categories (travel, equipment, CPD, subscriptions, etc.)</li>
<li>Give your totals to your accountant by 31 May following the tax year end</li>
</ul>

<p>If you use accounting software like FreeAgent, Xero, or QuickBooks, it will categorise expenses and calculate your totals automatically. Many dental-specialist accountants offer a package that includes software and monthly bookkeeping.</p>

<h2>Final Thoughts</h2>

<p>Locum dentistry offers flexibility and often higher hourly rates than associate roles. But the tax position is different. You are responsible for your own tax and NI, and you must claim the expenses you are entitled to. Missing a claim costs you money. Overclaiming risks an HMRC enquiry.</p>

<p>The key is good record keeping and understanding the rules. If you are unsure about any category of expense, ask a dental-specialist accountant before filing your return. The cost of professional advice is itself an allowable expense.</p>
