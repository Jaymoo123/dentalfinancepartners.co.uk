<p>NHS contract payments are the foundation of many UK dental practices, yet the payment system often feels like a black box. Whether you're an associate trying to understand your NHS earnings or a practice owner reconciling BSA payments, getting to grips with how NHS contract payments work is essential for proper financial management.</p>

<p>This guide breaks down the NHS payment system, explains how to account for contract income, and covers the reconciliation processes that keep your books accurate.</p>

<h2>Understanding NHS Contract Payments</h2>

<p>NHS dental contracts operate on an annual contract value (ACV) system. Your practice agrees to deliver a specific number of Units of Dental Activity (UDAs) over 12 months in return for monthly advance payments from NHS BSA.</p>

<p>These advance payments typically represent one-twelfth of your annual contract value, paid monthly regardless of how many UDAs you actually deliver. At year-end, BSA reconciles what you've been paid against what you've delivered.</p>

<p>For example, if your practice has a £240,000 NHS contract, you'll receive £20,000 per month in advance payments. If you deliver fewer UDAs than contracted, you may face clawback. If you over-deliver significantly, you might receive additional payments.</p>

<h2>Types of NHS Payments</h2>

<h3>Monthly Advance Payments</h3>
<p>These form the bulk of NHS contract income. BSA calculates your monthly payment by dividing your annual contract value by 12. Payments typically arrive around the 15th of each month, though this can vary.</p>

<p>The key accounting point is that these payments don't directly relate to the work you do each month. They're advances against your annual contract commitment.</p>

<h3>Additional Payments</h3>
<p>Beyond monthly advances, you might receive payments for:</p>
<ul>
<li>Domiciliary visits</li>
<li>Sedation services</li>
<li>Out-of-hours work</li>
<li>Band 1 treatments above your UDA allocation</li>
<li>Orthodontic work (if contracted separately)</li>
</ul>

<p>These payments typically follow a month or two behind when the work was completed, as BSA processes the claims.</p>

<h3>Year-End Reconciliation</h3>
<p>At contract year-end (usually 31st March), BSA reconciles your payments against delivered UDAs. This can result in additional payments if you've over-delivered, or clawback demands if you've under-delivered beyond tolerance levels.</p>

<h2>Accounting for NHS Contract Income</h2>

<p>The monthly advance nature of NHS payments creates specific accounting challenges. You need to match income recognition with work actually performed, not just cash received.</p>

<h3>Accruals Accounting</h3>
<p>Most dental practices use accruals accounting, which means recognising income when earned rather than when cash is received. This creates a mismatch with NHS payments.</p>

<p>For example, if you receive £20,000 in April but only complete £18,000 worth of NHS work that month, you shouldn't recognise the full £20,000 as April income. The £2,000 difference represents a creditor to NHS BSA.</p>

<h3>Monthly Reconciliation Process</h3>
<p>Each month, compare your NHS contract payments received against the value of NHS work completed:</p>

<ul>
<li>Calculate the value of NHS treatments completed (UDAs delivered × UDA value)</li>
<li>Add any additional payments earned (domiciliary visits, sedation, etc.)</li>
<li>Compare this to cash received from BSA</li>
<li>Post the difference as either a debtor (if you've earned more than received) or creditor (if received more than earned)</li>
</ul>

<p>This process ensures your profit and loss account reflects actual work performed rather than BSA's payment timing.</p>

<h2>BSA Payment Reconciliation</h2>

<p>Regular reconciliation with BSA statements is crucial for accurate accounting. BSA provides monthly payment summaries showing:</p>

<ul>
<li>Monthly advance payment</li>
<li>Additional payments for specific treatments</li>
<li>Any adjustments or clawbacks</li>
<li>Running totals for the contract year</li>
</ul>

<p>Match these statements against your bank receipts and internal records. Discrepancies are common and usually relate to timing differences or claim processing delays.</p>

<h3>Common Reconciliation Issues</h3>
<p>Typical problems include:</p>

<ul>
<li>Domiciliary visit claims processed in the following month</li>
<li>Sedation payments delayed due to claim verification</li>
<li>UDA adjustments from previous months</li>
<li>Bank holiday payment timing differences</li>
</ul>

<p>Keep detailed records of all NHS work performed to support your reconciliations and any queries from BSA.</p>

<h2>Year-End Contract Reconciliation</h2>

<p>The annual reconciliation is when BSA squares up your contract performance. This typically happens 3-4 months after contract year-end, once all claims are processed.</p>

<p>BSA calculates your final position by comparing:</p>
<ul>
<li>Total payments made during the year</li>
<li>UDAs actually delivered × UDA value</li>
<li>Additional payments earned</li>
</ul>

<p>If you've under-delivered UDAs beyond the 4% tolerance, BSA will demand clawback. If you've significantly over-delivered, you might receive additional payments, though these are often limited.</p>

<h3>Accounting for Year-End Adjustments</h3>
<p>Year-end reconciliation adjustments need careful accounting treatment. If you're due a clawback, this reduces your NHS income for the year. Additional payments increase it.</p>

<p>Many practices provision for expected clawbacks during the year based on UDA performance tracking. This smooths the impact rather than taking a large hit when BSA finalises the reconciliation.</p>

<h2>VAT Considerations</h2>

<p>NHS dental services are VAT-exempt, so you don't charge VAT on NHS contract income. However, this creates mixed supplies if you also provide private treatments, which complicates VAT accounting.</p>

<p>Keep NHS and private income clearly separated in your records. Most practice management systems can provide this split automatically.</p>

<h2>Cash Flow Management</h2>

<p>The advance payment system provides steady cash flow, but creates challenges when contracts reduce or if significant clawbacks arise. Some practices set aside a portion of monthly advances to cover potential year-end adjustments.</p>

<p>Consider the cash flow impact when planning practice investments or making financial commitments. NHS contract income appears stable month-to-month but can fluctuate significantly at year-end reconciliation.</p>

<h2>Record Keeping Requirements</h2>

<p>HMRC requires detailed records supporting your NHS contract income. Essential records include:</p>

<ul>
<li>BSA payment statements</li>
<li>Bank statements showing payments received</li>
<li>Practice management system reports showing treatments completed</li>
<li>UDA tracking spreadsheets</li>
<li>Year-end reconciliation correspondence with BSA</li>
</ul>

<p>These records should be retained for at least six years after the relevant tax year.</p>

<p>Proper NHS contract accounting forms the foundation of accurate practice financial management. Get this right, and you'll have clarity on your true profitability and cash position. Get it wrong, and you risk nasty surprises when BSA reconciliations arrive.</p>
