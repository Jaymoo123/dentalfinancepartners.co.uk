<p>Whether to run your practice through a limited company is one of the questions owners ask most often. It is rarely answered well in a single conversation, because it depends on profit level, how you take money out, what you plan to invest in, and how you work with associates. Below is a practical framing — and where profit extraction fits once the structure is clear.</p>

<h2>Start with clarity on true profit</h2>
<p>Extraction planning should sit on top of <strong>reliable management accounts</strong>. In dental practices, EBITDA can move with associate pay mixes, lab costs, and equipment cycles — if you only look at the bank, you can over-draw or miss pension opportunities.</p>

<p>Before deciding how to extract profit, know what sustainable profit actually looks like for your practice. A good year boosted by one-off private cases shouldn't set the template for ongoing drawings. Work from an average of the last 2-3 years, adjusted for known changes.</p>

<h2>Salary and dividends: the standard approach</h2>
<p>Most dental practice owners who operate through a limited company take a combination of salary and dividends. The typical approach involves:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Salary up to the NI threshold</strong> — currently around £12,570, preserving state pension entitlement without triggering employer or employee NI contributions.</li>
<li><strong>Dividends from remaining profits</strong> — taxed at lower rates than salary (8.75% basic rate, 33.75% higher rate), though only available from post-tax company profits.</li>
</ul>

<p>The optimal split depends on your personal tax position, other income sources, and whether you have a spouse who could be a shareholder in the company.</p>

<h2>Pension contributions as extraction</h2>
<p>Employer pension contributions from the company are one of the most tax-efficient extraction methods for dental practice owners. The company gets corporation tax relief, and the contributions don't attract NI or income tax (within annual allowance limits).</p>

<p>For practice owners already in the NHS pension scheme, careful coordination is needed to avoid breaching the annual allowance — which has caught many dentists with unexpected tax charges. Your accountant should model combined NHS and private pension contributions to find the optimal level.</p>

<h2>Common extraction building blocks</h2>
<p>Most limited company owners consider a blend of salary and dividends, alongside pension contributions where appropriate. The balance shifts with dividend allowances, corporation tax rates, and personal cash needs.</p>

<p>Specialist dental accountants will also stress-test decisions against <strong>regulatory and NHS contract</strong> realities — not because tax drives clinical decisions, but because cash timing and investment plans are intertwined.</p>

<h2>Director loan accounts: proceed with caution</h2>
<p>Director loan accounts allow practice owners to take money from the company as a loan rather than income. While flexible, they carry significant risks:</p>

<ul>
<li>Loans over £10,000 outstanding at year-end trigger a Section 455 tax charge (33.75%).</li>
<li>The charge is repayable when the loan is repaid, but creates cash flow problems for the company.</li>
<li>HMRC scrutinises director loan accounts closely — inconsistent or large balances raise red flags.</li>
</ul>

<p>Use director loans as a short-term cash management tool, not a permanent extraction strategy. Clear them before year-end wherever possible.</p>

<h2>Timing extraction around investment</h2>
<p>Dental practices often face lumpy capital expenditure — a new chair, digital imaging equipment, or a practice refurbishment. Timing profit extraction around these investments can improve tax efficiency.</p>

<p>Retaining profits in the company before a major investment means corporation tax has already been paid at 19-25%. If you extract and then reinvest personally, you pay income tax first and lose the benefit of using pre-tax company funds.</p>

<h2>Compliance and calm governance</h2>
<p>HMRC risk rises when transactions between the practice and directors are informal. Clean documentation, board minutes where relevant, and a clear policy for expenses protect both the practice and the individuals behind it.</p>

<p>If you are weighing a significant investment — a squat practice, a merger, or a large equipment package — model the next three years before fixing extraction.</p>

<h2>When to review your extraction strategy</h2>
<p>Review annually at minimum, but also trigger a review when:</p>

<ul>
<li>Practice profits change significantly (up or down).</li>
<li>Tax rates or thresholds change — the 2026/27 tax year brings further adjustments.</li>
<li>Your personal circumstances change — marriage, children, approaching retirement.</li>
<li>You are planning a major investment or acquisition.</li>
<li>You are considering bringing in a partner or selling the practice.</li>
</ul>

<p>A proactive accountant should initiate these conversations rather than waiting for you to ask.</p>
