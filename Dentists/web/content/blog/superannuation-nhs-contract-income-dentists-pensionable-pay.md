<p>Most dentists assume their NHS pension is built on their NHS income. It is not. Pensionable pay on an NHS dental contract is calculated from the <strong>contract value</strong>, and it is <strong>capped</strong>, at a net pensionable earnings ceiling that covers every dentist on the contract combined. The number that governs the whole calculation is <strong>43.9% of the total contract value</strong>, and a dentist who does not understand it will badly misjudge how much pension their NHS work is actually building.</p>

<p>This guide explains the calculation in detail: how the <strong>NPE ceiling</strong> works, how a provider <strong>allocates</strong> the capped pool to performers and associates, the difference between NPE and NPEE, the role of the <strong>Annual Reconciliation Report</strong>, and why <strong>incorporation</strong> can quietly lose pensionable pay you assumed was safe. It complements our broader overview of <a href="https://www.dentalfinancepartners.co.uk/blog/nhs-pension/nhs-pension-pensionable-pay-dentists">pensionable pay and the practitioner versus officer distinction</a> by going deep on the one thing that overview leaves general: exactly how the figure is calculated from a contract.</p>

<h2>What "superannuation" means here</h2>

<p>Superannuation is simply the older word for pension contributions, and in NHS dentistry it refers to the part of your income that builds NHS Pension Scheme benefits. The crucial point is that not all of your NHS income is superannuable. Only a defined, capped slice of the contract value counts as <strong>net pensionable earnings</strong> and feeds your pension. Everything else, the part of the contract that pays for staff, premises, materials and your profit, sits outside the pension calculation. So when a dentist talks about "their superannuation", they mean their share of that capped pensionable slice, not their whole NHS income.</p>

<p>Getting this right matters because the NHS pension is, for most dentists, one of the most valuable financial assets they will ever hold: a guaranteed, inflation-linked, defined-benefit income for life, backed by the scheme rather than by investment markets. A figure that valuable deserves to be understood precisely rather than assumed, and the precision starts with knowing that pensionable pay is calculated from the contract, capped at the ceiling, allocated among the dentists on it, and reconciled each year. The sections below work through each of those steps in turn, so that by the end you can look at your own contract and understand, at least in outline, how much of it is building your pension and how much is not.</p>

<h2>Practitioner versus officer: two ways to be pensioned</h2>

<p>Before the calculation itself, it helps to know which of two pensionable routes you are on, because it shapes everything that follows. A <strong>practitioner</strong>, a sole-trader or partnership dentist working in NHS general dental services, is pensioned on their <strong>NHS-derived earnings</strong> under the scheme-specific rules, with the net pensionable earnings figure drawn from the contract pool described in this guide. An <strong>officer</strong>, by contrast, is pensioned on a <strong>salary</strong>: a foundation dentist during training, a salaried community or hospital dentist, or an incorporated dentist taking PAYE pay, is treated as an officer, and only that salary is pensionable.</p>

<p>The distinction matters because the two routes measure pensionable pay completely differently. The practitioner route ties pensionable pay to the contract value through the NPE mechanism; the officer route ties it to a salary figure. Most associates and principals delivering NHS work as self-employed practitioners are on the practitioner route and so are governed by the NPE ceiling. The moment a dentist incorporates and starts taking a salary, the officer rules can apply to that salary, which is the root of the incorporation pension trap discussed later. Knowing which route you are on is the first step to understanding your own pensionable pay. Our broader guide to the <a href="https://www.dentalfinancepartners.co.uk/blog/nhs-pension/nhs-pension-pensionable-pay-dentists">practitioner versus officer distinction</a> sets out the two routes side by side.</p>

<h2>The contract value is the base</h2>

<p>The calculation starts from the <strong>total contract value (TCV)</strong>, the annual value of the GDS or PDS contract. This is the same figure that drives your UDA rate (contract value divided by contracted UDAs) and your delivery target. Pensionable pay is a <strong>function of that contract value</strong>, not of your personal drawings or your private income. This is why two dentists with similar take-home pay can have very different pensionable pay: it depends on the contract behind the income, not the income itself. Anchoring everything to the contract value is the first step in the calculation.</p>

<h2>The 43.9% NPE ceiling explained</h2>

<p>Here is the central rule. The <strong>maximum net pensionable earnings that can be declared for a single GDS or PDS contract is 43.9% of the total contract value</strong>. Take 43.9% of your contract value and you have the <strong>pensionable-earnings ceiling</strong>: the most that all the dentists working on that contract can <em>collectively</em> pension. The remaining 56.1% of the contract value, broadly the part that funds the practice's costs and profit, is <strong>not pensionable</strong>.</p>

<p>This single figure reshapes how a dentist should think about their NHS pension. A principal on a £400,000 contract does not pension £400,000 of income; the entire contract can build pension on at most £175,600 (43.9% of £400,000), and that pool is shared with every associate on the contract. Understanding the ceiling stops the common overestimate of NHS pension accrual and grounds retirement planning in reality.</p>

<h2>How the provider allocates NPE to performers</h2>

<p>The <strong>provider</strong>, the dentist or entity holding the contract, has a duty to <strong>allocate</strong> the pensionable earnings within the 43.9% ceiling among the active performers. Each performer's net pensionable earnings figure is <strong>deducted from the pool</strong>, so the more one dentist pensions, the less is left for the others. How the provider determines each performer's figure is, within the rules, a matter of practice discretion, based on what each dentist earned from the contract. In a <strong>partnership</strong>, any balance remaining after the performers are allocated is split between the partners under the partnership agreement. A firm rule applies throughout: <strong>a dentist cannot claim a colleague's pensionable income</strong>, even if that colleague is not in the scheme. The pool is finite, and allocation is a real decision with real consequences for each dentist's pension.</p>

<h2>Why the ceiling exists and what it means for planning</h2>

<p>The 43.9% ceiling is not arbitrary; it reflects the principle that a dental contract pays for far more than the dentists' own labour. A large share of the contract value funds <strong>nurses, receptionists, premises, materials, laboratory work and equipment</strong>, none of which is the dentist's pensionable pay. The ceiling is, in effect, the system's view of how much of a contract's value represents the dentists' own earnings rather than the cost of running the practice around them. Whether or not that 43.9% matches any individual practice's true labour share, it is the cap that applies, and planning has to work within it.</p>

<p>For retirement planning, the implication is significant and often sobering. A dentist building a financial plan on the assumption that their NHS pension reflects their whole NHS income will <strong>overestimate their pension</strong> substantially, because at most 43.9% of the contract value can ever have been pensionable, and their personal share of that is smaller still. Realistic planning takes the actual allocated net pensionable earnings figure, not the headline income, as the basis for projecting NHS benefits, and treats any gap between desired retirement income and projected NHS pension as something to fill with additional provision. The ceiling is the reason a high-earning NHS dentist can still have a more modest pension than they expect, and recognising that early is what lets them act on it.</p>

<h2>NPE versus NPEE: provider and performer</h2>

<p>Two acronyms appear in this area. <strong>NPE</strong> (net pensionable earnings) is broadly the pensionable figure for a <strong>performer</strong> paid from the contract. <strong>NPEE</strong> (net pensionable earnings equivalent) is used for a <strong>provider</strong>, the contract holder, to reflect their pensionable position. Both are drawn from the same 43.9% pool of the total contract value. The distinction reflects the slightly different mechanics for a provider versus a performer, but the principle is identical for both: pensionable pay is a <strong>capped share of contract value</strong>, allocated and reconciled, not a free percentage of personal earnings.</p>

<h2>Tiered contributions on your pensionable pay</h2>

<p>Once your net pensionable earnings figure is set, you pay <strong>employee contributions</strong> on it at a tiered rate, where the contribution percentage rises with the level of pensionable pay. This is worth understanding because it affects the net value of pensioning more income. A dentist deciding how much to pension, or a provider deciding allocation, is not just dividing a benefit; they are also dividing a contribution cost, and the tiered structure means the marginal cost of pensioning an extra slice can be meaningful. The contributions attract <strong>tax relief</strong>, which softens the cost, but the cash outflow is real and lands through the year. For most dentists the calculus still favours pensioning to the extent the ceiling allows, because the guaranteed, inflation-linked benefit is valuable, but the tiered contribution is part of the picture and should not be ignored when modelling the net benefit of NHS accrual.</p>

<h2>The Annual Reconciliation Report</h2>

<p>The figures are confirmed each year through the <strong>Annual Reconciliation Report (ARR)</strong>, which is how providers and performers <strong>check and confirm that the net pensionable earnings paid from 1 April to 31 March are correct</strong>. The ARR reconciles each dentist's pensionable figure for the year so that their pension record and contributions reflect what they actually pensioned. It is an annual obligation, and accuracy matters because the figures feed directly into your pension record and your annual allowance position. A mis-stated ARR can understate your pension or, at the other end, contribute to an unexpected annual allowance charge, so it deserves a proper check rather than a rubber stamp.</p>

<h2>Common misunderstandings about NHS pensionable pay</h2>

<p>Several persistent misunderstandings cause dentists to misjudge their NHS pension, and naming them is the quickest way to avoid them. The first is assuming <strong>all NHS income is pensionable</strong>; at most 43.9% of the contract value is, shared across the dentists on the contract. The second is believing <strong>private income builds NHS pension</strong>; it does not, only NHS-contract-derived earnings within the ceiling do. The third is thinking that <strong>incorporating is pension-neutral</strong>; for an officer-route incorporated dentist, dividends are not pensionable, so it can sharply cut accrual. The fourth is assuming an unused share of a colleague's pensionable pool <strong>passes to you</strong>; it does not, a dentist cannot claim a colleague's pensionable income. And the fifth is treating the <strong>headline income figure</strong>, rather than the allocated NPE, as the basis for projecting the pension; only the allocated, reconciled figure builds benefits. Each of these errors leads a dentist to overestimate their NHS pension, which is the most expensive direction to be wrong in, because the shortfall only becomes visible near retirement when it is hardest to fix. Understanding the capped, shared, allocated nature of pensionable pay corrects all five at once.</p>

<h2>A worked allocation</h2>

<p>Take a contract with a <strong>total contract value of £400,000</strong>, held by a provider with two associates.</p>

<ul>
<li>The <strong>NPE ceiling</strong> is 43.9% of £400,000, which is <strong>£175,600</strong>. That is the most all three dentists can pension between them.</li>
<li>Suppose <strong>Associate A</strong> is allocated net pensionable earnings of £55,000 and <strong>Associate B</strong> £45,000, based on their share of contract-derived income. Together they use £100,000 of the pool.</li>
<li>That leaves <strong>£75,600</strong> of the ceiling available to the <strong>provider</strong> as their NPEE, assuming their drawn position supports it.</li>
</ul>

<p>The example shows three things at once: the ceiling is a shared pool, allocation to associates reduces what is left for the provider, and the whole pensionable picture is far smaller than the £400,000 contract value suggests. Change the allocation and you change each dentist's pension, which is why this is a decision to make deliberately and reconcile carefully.</p>

<h2>Why allocation is a decision with consequences</h2>

<p>Because the pensionable pool is finite and shared, the way a provider <strong>allocates</strong> net pensionable earnings among the dentists on a contract is a genuine decision with lasting effects on each individual's pension, not a mere formality. Allocate more to one associate and there is less ceiling left for the others and the provider. Over a career, differences in allocation compound, through the 2015 CARE scheme's annual accrual, into materially different guaranteed pensions. This makes the allocation a matter dentists should understand and, where they are performers on a contract, take an interest in rather than leave entirely to the practice.</p>

<p>A few principles keep allocation sound. It should reflect <strong>what each dentist actually earned</strong> from the contract, be applied <strong>consistently</strong> year to year, and be <strong>documented</strong> so it can be reconciled through the ARR and stand up to scrutiny. An associate who suspects their allocated figure does not reflect their contribution is entitled to query it, because it directly affects their pension record. And a provider has to remember the firm rule that a dentist cannot claim a colleague's pensionable income: an unused share left by a colleague who is not in the scheme does not simply transfer. Treating allocation as the consequential decision it is, rather than a box to tick, protects every dentist on the contract.</p>

<h2>Incorporation: the trap and the provider exception</h2>

<p>How you are structured changes what of this you can actually pension. For an <strong>incorporated dentist on the officer route</strong>, only the <strong>PAYE salary is pensionable and dividends are not</strong>. So an incorporated associate who takes a small salary and large dividends can cut their pensionable pay dramatically, losing CARE accrual, the well-known <strong>incorporation pension trap</strong>. For an <strong>incorporated provider</strong> who holds the contract, the better-evidenced position is more favourable: they can pension <strong>salary and dividends actually drawn, up to the NPE ceiling</strong>, with <strong>retained profit and anything above the ceiling not pensionable</strong>. The distinction between an incorporated associate (trap) and an incorporated provider (drawn income up to the ceiling) is nuanced and genuinely case-specific, so confirm your exact position with NHSBSA. Our guide to the <a href="https://www.dentalfinancepartners.co.uk/blog/nhs-pension/incorporated-dental-principal-nhs-pension-pensionability">incorporated principal's NHS pension position</a> works through this in detail.</p>

<h2>Why dividends are not pensionable</h2>

<p>It is worth stating the principle plainly because it drives so many decisions. The NHS scheme pensions <strong>employment-type earnings</strong>, salary for an officer, contract-derived income for a practitioner, not investment returns. A <strong>dividend</strong> is a return on shareholding, not pay for work, so for an incorporated dentist treated as an officer it does not count as pensionable pay. This is why pairing the incorporation tax saving with the pension loss is essential: a dentist who incorporates and extracts dividends may save tax but quietly stops building one of the most valuable assets they have, a guaranteed, inflation-linked NHS pension. Never weigh the tax saving without the pension cost beside it.</p>

<h2>Added Pension and AVCs alongside the contract pension</h2>

<p>Because the NPE ceiling caps how much of a contract can build pension, dentists who want more retirement provision than the ceiling allows have routes to add to it, and these matter most for exactly the higher earners the ceiling constrains. Within the NHS scheme, <strong>Added Pension</strong> lets a member buy extra defined-benefit accrual, and <strong>Money Purchase Additional Voluntary Contributions (MPAVCs)</strong> build a separate defined-contribution pot alongside the main scheme. Both attract income-tax relief, subject to the annual allowance. Outside the scheme, a dentist can use a <strong>personal pension or SIPP</strong> to top up further, again within the annual allowance and the relevant-earnings cap.</p>

<p>The planning logic is straightforward: the NPE ceiling sets how much your NHS contract can pension, and these top-up routes fill the gap between that and the retirement income you actually want. For a dentist on a large contract, the gap can be substantial, because 43.9% of the contract value shared across the dentists may build a smaller pension than the headline income suggests. Identifying that gap and filling it deliberately, rather than assuming the NHS scheme alone will suffice, is the practical upshot of understanding how pensionable pay is capped.</p>

<h2>How clawback and carry-forward feed into it</h2>

<p>Because pensionable pay derives from contract value and what was actually earned, the year-end <strong>reconciliation</strong> matters here too. A <strong>clawback</strong> that reduces what you effectively earned from the contract can flow through to your pensionable figure, and the ARR needs to reconcile to the true post-reconciliation position. A <strong>carry-forward</strong> shifts delivery, not cash, but it still bears on how the contract performs and is reported. The practical point is that your pensionable pay, your delivery and your contract reconciliation are connected, so they should be looked at together rather than in separate silos. <h2>Checking your figure and the annual allowance</h2>

<p>Finally, the pensionable pay figure does not stand alone; it feeds your <strong>annual allowance</strong> position. Your NHS pension growth, measured as the pension input amount, counts towards the <strong>£60,000</strong> annual allowance with any other pension inputs. The 43.9% ceiling usually keeps pensionable pay well within the allowance, but a dentist with significant historic service or large inputs can approach or breach it and face a charge, as our guide to <a href="https://www.dentalfinancepartners.co.uk/blog/nhs-pension/annual-allowance-tapering-nhs-dentists-pension">annual allowance and tapering</a> explains. The sensible discipline is a periodic check, ideally with a specialist dental accountant, that your allocated net pensionable earnings are correct and within the ceiling, that the provider's allocation across performers is fair, that the ARR reconciles, and that the figure sits comfortably against your annual allowance. Pensionable pay errors compound over years into real pension differences, so the check is worth making, especially around incorporation, a change of contract, or a year with a clawback or carry-forward.</p>
