<h2>What Is the AIA Allowance for Dental Equipment?</h2>

<p>The Annual Investment Allowance (AIA) is a capital allowance that gives UK dentists 100% tax relief on qualifying plant and machinery purchases in the year you buy them. For the 2025/26 tax year, the AIA limit is £1 million <sup><a href="#ref-1" id="cite-1">[1]</a></sup>. This means if you buy a new dental chair for £15,000, you can deduct the full £15,000 from your taxable profits in that accounting period, rather than spreading the relief over several years through writing down allowances.</p>

<p>The AIA applies to most businesses, including sole traders, partnerships, and limited companies. However, partnerships where one of the partners is a company are excluded <sup><a href="#ref-2" id="cite-2">[2]</a></sup>. For dental practice owners, this is one of the most valuable tax reliefs available when investing in new equipment.</p>

<p>The £1 million limit has been in place since 1 January 2019 and is currently confirmed until 31 March 2026 <sup><a href="#ref-2" id="cite-2">[2]</a></sup>. After that date, the government may reduce the limit, so timing your equipment purchases can have a significant tax impact.</p>

<h2>What Dental Equipment Qualifies for the AIA?</h2>

<p>Most plant and machinery used in a dental practice qualifies for the AIA. This includes:</p>

<ul>
<li>Dental chairs and delivery units</li>
<li>X-ray machines, including OPG and CBCT scanners</li>
<li>Compressors and suction units</li>
<li>Autoclaves and sterilisation equipment</li>
<li>Computer hardware and practice management software</li>
<li>Dental laboratory equipment</li>
<li>Air conditioning and ventilation systems</li>
<li>Lighting and electrical installations</li>
</ul>

<p>There are important exclusions. Cars do not qualify for the AIA, nor do assets used for leasing, or assets with a useful life of more than 100 years <sup><a href="#ref-2" id="cite-2">[2]</a></sup>. Land and buildings are also excluded, though fixtures within a building (like dental chairs bolted to the floor) can qualify if they meet the definition of plant and machinery.</p>

<p>If you buy a dental practice as a going concern, you need to agree a capital allowances valuation with the seller. This is done through a Section 198 election under the Capital Allowances Act 2001, which allocates part of the purchase price to qualifying fixtures. Without this election, you may lose the right to claim AIA on those assets.</p>

<h2>How the AIA Works in Practice</h2>

<p>The AIA is claimed in the accounting period when you buy the item <sup><a href="#ref-1" id="cite-1">[1]</a></sup>. You cannot carry the allowance forward to a later period. If your accounting period is shorter than 12 months, the £1 million limit is reduced proportionally. For example, if your accounting period is 9 months, the AIA limit is 9/12 x £1,000,000 = £750,000 <sup><a href="#ref-1" id="cite-1">[1]</a></sup>.</p>

<p>If you buy equipment under a hire purchase contract, you can claim AIA on the full purchase price when you start using the item, even though you have not yet paid the full amount <sup><a href="#ref-1" id="cite-1">[1]</a></sup>. This is a significant cash flow advantage for practices financing new equipment.</p>

<p>For expenditure above the £1 million AIA limit, the excess enters the main pool or special rate pool and attracts writing down allowances at 18% or 6% per year respectively <sup><a href="#ref-2" id="cite-2">[2]</a></sup>.</p>

<h3>Example: Single-Handed Principal Buying New Equipment</h3>

<p>Dr Patel, a single-handed principal, buys a new dental chair (£18,000), an OPG machine (£35,000), and a compressor (£6,000) in the 2025/26 tax year. Total qualifying expenditure: £59,000. She claims the full £59,000 as AIA, reducing her taxable profit by that amount. At her 40% tax rate, this saves her £23,600 in income tax.</p>

<h3>Example: Multi-Site Group Practice</h3>

<p>BrightSmile Dental Ltd, a limited company with three practices, spends £250,000 on new equipment across all sites in 2025/26. The company claims the full £250,000 as AIA, reducing its corporation tax liability by £47,500 (at 19% small profits rate). If the company had spent £1.2 million, only the first £1 million would qualify for AIA; the remaining £200,000 would attract writing down allowances.</p>

<h2>Interaction with Other Capital Allowances</h2>

<p>The AIA is not the only capital allowance available. From 1 April 2023, full expensing and a 50% first-year allowance can be claimed on qualifying plant and machinery investments <sup><a href="#ref-3" id="cite-3">[3]</a></sup>. Full expensing gives 100% relief on main pool assets, similar to the AIA, but without a £1 million cap. However, full expensing is only available to limited companies, not sole traders or partnerships.</p>

<p>If an item qualifies for more than one capital allowance, you can choose which one to use <sup><a href="#ref-3" id="cite-3">[3]</a></sup>. For most dental practices, the AIA is the simplest and most generous option, especially given the £1 million limit covers the vast majority of equipment purchases.</p>

<p>For assets bought between 1 April 2021 and 31 March 2023, the super-deduction (130% for main pool assets) and 50% special rate first-year allowance were available <sup><a href="#ref-3" id="cite-3">[3]</a></sup>. These have now ended, so the AIA is the primary relief for current purchases.</p>

<p>From 1 January 2026, a 40% first-year allowance will be available for qualifying plant and machinery <sup><a href="#ref-3" id="cite-3">[3]</a></sup>. This is less generous than the AIA's 100% relief, so practices should consider accelerating purchases before the AIA limit potentially changes after 31 March 2026.</p>

<h2>Common Pitfalls and How to Avoid Them</h2>

<h3>1. Forgetting to Claim the AIA</h3>
<p>The AIA is not automatic. You must include it in your tax return or company accounts. Many dentists miss this relief, especially if they use a generalist accountant who does not specialise in dental practices. A <a href="/services/dental-accountants">dental-specialist accountant</a> will ensure you claim the full AIA on all qualifying equipment.</p>

<h3>2. Incorrectly Classifying Assets</h3>
<p>Some assets that look like plant and machinery may not qualify. For example, decorative items or assets with a life over 100 years are excluded. If you are unsure, check HMRC's Capital Allowances Manual or ask your accountant.</p>

<h3>3. Missing the Section 198 Election on Practice Purchase</h3>
<p>When buying a dental practice, the seller may have claimed capital allowances on fixtures. Without a Section 198 election, you cannot claim AIA on those fixtures. This is a common issue in practice acquisitions. Our <a href="/dental-guides/practice-purchase-financial-due-diligence">practice purchase due diligence guide</a> covers this in detail.</p>

<h3>4. Exceeding the AIA Limit in a Short Period</h3>
<p>If your accounting period is less than 12 months, the £1 million limit is reduced. Plan your purchases to fall within a full 12-month period where possible.</p>

<h3>5. Group Company Rules</h3>
<p>If two or more limited companies are controlled by the same person, they only get one AIA between them <sup><a href="#ref-1" id="cite-1">[1]</a></sup>. This catches out multi-site groups that try to claim separate AIAs for each company. The same rule applies to companies under common control, even if they operate different practices.</p>

<h2>AIA and Practice Purchase: A Special Case</h2>

<p>When you buy a dental practice, the purchase price typically includes goodwill, property, and equipment. Only the equipment element qualifies for capital allowances. The goodwill element does not qualify for AIA, though it may attract amortisation relief at 6.5% per year if acquired after 1 April 2019.</p>

<p>You need a capital allowances valuation to split the purchase price between qualifying and non-qualifying assets. This valuation should be agreed with the seller via a Section 198 election. Without it, HMRC may challenge your AIA claim, and you could lose relief on fixtures that the seller has already claimed.</p>

<p>For more on this, see our <a href="/dental-guides/goodwill-valuation-and-sale-playbook">goodwill valuation and sale playbook</a>.</p>

<h2>Planning Your Equipment Purchases</h2>

<p>The AIA's £1 million limit is generous enough to cover most dental equipment purchases. However, the limit is only confirmed until 31 March 2026 <sup><a href="#ref-2" id="cite-2">[2]</a></sup>. After that, the government may reduce it to a lower level, as it has done in the past (the limit was £200,000 in 2016/17 <sup><a href="#ref-4" id="cite-4">[4]</a></sup>).</p>

<p>If you are planning significant capital expenditure in the next few years, consider accelerating purchases into the current AIA window. This is particularly relevant for practices considering CBCT scanners, digital impression systems, or full surgery refurbishments.</p>

<p>Business investment in the UK fell by more than 20% during the Covid pandemic <sup><a href="#ref-5" id="cite-5">[5]</a></sup>. The government has maintained the £1 million AIA limit to encourage investment and support economic recovery. Taking advantage of this relief now can reduce your tax bill and improve your practice's equipment and efficiency.</p>

<h2>How to Claim the AIA</h2>

<p>Claiming the AIA is straightforward if you have the right records. Here is the process:</p>

<ol>
<li>Identify all qualifying plant and machinery purchases in the accounting period.</li>
<li>Calculate the total qualifying expenditure. If it exceeds £1 million, only the first £1 million qualifies for AIA; the rest goes into the main pool or special rate pool.</li>
<li>Include the AIA claim in your tax return (for sole traders and partnerships) or company tax return (for limited companies).</li>
<li>Keep invoices, delivery notes, and evidence of when the equipment was brought into use.</li>
</ol>
