<p>For a long time, a dental practice could get away with a relaxed approach to bookkeeping: keep the receipts, bank the income, and hand a year's worth of paperwork to the accountant a few months after the year end to turn into a Self Assessment return. <strong>Making Tax Digital for Income Tax</strong> ends that. From April 2026 most unincorporated dentists must keep <strong>digital records</strong> and send <strong>quarterly updates</strong> to HMRC, and the once-a-year reconciliation gives way to a continuous, digital rhythm.</p>

<p>This guide explains what that means in practice and how to be ready for it. We cover the <strong>thresholds and timeline</strong> that decide when you are caught, what <strong>digital record-keeping</strong> and <strong>quarterly updates</strong> actually require, the records a dental practice specifically needs, and how to build bookkeeping systems that make MTD a continuation of good habits rather than a quarterly scramble. The reassuring headline is that MTD does not change how your tax is calculated; it changes how you report, and a practice with clean digital systems will find the new rhythm easier than the old annual cram.</p>

<h2>What Making Tax Digital actually is</h2>

<p><strong>Making Tax Digital for Income Tax Self Assessment</strong>, MTD for ITSA, requires sole traders and landlords above an income threshold to keep <strong>digital records</strong> and send <strong>quarterly updates</strong> to HMRC through compatible software, replacing the single annual Self Assessment return with more frequent digital reporting. It applies to <strong>income tax</strong>, so it catches <strong>unincorporated dentists</strong>, sole-trader associates and principals, and partnerships in due course, but <strong>not limited companies</strong>, which report corporation tax under a separate regime. We cover the framework in our wider guide to <a href="https://www.dentalfinancepartners.co.uk/blog/vat-and-compliance/making-tax-digital-dental-practices-mtd-guide">Making Tax Digital for dental practices</a>; this page focuses on the bookkeeping systems that make you ready for it.</p>

<p>The change is one of <strong>rhythm and form</strong>, not of substance. Your taxable profit and the reliefs you claim are worked out under the same rules. What is new is that the records must be digital and the reporting happens four times a year plus a final declaration, rather than once. That sounds like more work, and at the margin it is, but for a practice that keeps clean records it mostly formalises what good bookkeeping already does.</p>

<h2>The thresholds and timeline</h2>

<p>MTD for Income Tax is phased in by <strong>qualifying income</strong>, which is your gross trading income plus any property income, tested on the relevant prior year's Self Assessment return:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Over £50,000: from 6 April 2026.</strong></li>
<li><strong>Over £30,000: from 6 April 2027.</strong></li>
<li><strong>Over £20,000: from 6 April 2028.</strong></li>
</ul>

<p>For dentists, the practical reality is that <strong>most full-time associates and unincorporated principals earn well over £50,000</strong>, so they are in scope from the <strong>first phase, 6 April 2026</strong>. That makes MTD-readiness an immediate priority for the typical unincorporated dentist, not a distant concern. Because the threshold is tested on <strong>gross income</strong>, not profit, even a dentist with high expenses and modest net profit is likely caught, since the gross figure is what counts. Date-tag these thresholds, as the timeline is set in regulations that can be amended, but plan on the April 2026 start for a full-time dentist.</p>

<h2>What digital record-keeping requires</h2>

<p>The heart of MTD is <strong>digital record-keeping</strong>. It means recording your income and expenses in a <strong>digital form</strong>, in compatible accounting software or in a spreadsheet linked to HMRC by compatible bridging software, rather than on paper or in a way that has to be manually retyped to file. Each transaction is captured digitally, with its date, amount and category, and the data flows through to the quarterly updates and the final declaration <strong>without being rekeyed</strong>. The principle is a <strong>digital link</strong> from the original record all the way to the HMRC submission.</p>

<p>The consequence is that the old method, a shoebox of receipts reconciled by hand at year end, no longer meets the standard. You need the records to exist digitally and to be kept reasonably current, because the quarterly updates draw on them every three months. This is the single biggest behavioural change MTD asks of a dental practice: not the reporting itself, but keeping the books up to date through the year rather than catching up annually.</p>

<h2>What quarterly updates involve</h2>

<p>Under MTD you send a <strong>summary of your business income and expenses to HMRC every quarter</strong> through compatible software, <strong>four updates a year</strong>, followed by a <strong>final declaration</strong> after the year end that finalises the figures and brings in adjustments, reliefs and any other income. Two points reassure practice owners worried about the workload. First, the quarterly updates are <strong>cumulative summaries</strong>, not four full tax returns, so they are lighter than they sound. Second, they do <strong>not each trigger a tax payment</strong>: your tax-payment dates and payments on account are unchanged, so the quarterly updates are reporting, not extra bills.</p>

<p>What the quarterly cycle does require is that your records are <strong>current every quarter</strong>. A practice that keeps its books live finds each update a quick confirmation; one that lets the records drift faces a mini year-end four times a year. The whole case for good systems is that they turn the quarterly update into a formality.</p>

<h2>The records a dental practice needs</h2>

<p>Dental practices have a distinctive income and expense profile, and MTD-ready bookkeeping should capture it properly. On the <strong>income</strong> side, record NHS contract payments, private fees, and any cosmetic or retail income separately, because their tax and VAT treatment differs and clean separation matters for the <a href="https://www.dentalfinancepartners.co.uk/blog/practice-accounting/reading-dental-practice-profit-loss-account-uk">profit and loss account</a> and for VAT. On the <strong>expense</strong> side, capture all allowable costs:</p>

<ul>
<li>Professional indemnity, the GDC retention fee and professional subscriptions.</li>
<li>Relevant CPD, staff costs and laboratory fees.</li>
<li>Materials, premises costs, and equipment via capital allowances.</li>
<li>Motor between practices, home-office apportionment, phone and internet, and accountancy fees.</li>
</ul>

<p>Keeping these <strong>digitally and categorised correctly through the year</strong> is what makes the quarterly updates straightforward and, just as importantly, ensures you <strong>claim every deduction</strong> you are entitled to. A practice that records costs as they arise rarely misses a deduction; one that reconstructs them at year end usually does. The detail of what is allowable sits in the wider expenses rules that govern a dental practice's deductions.</p>

<h2>Building MTD-ready systems</h2>

<p>Getting ready is a sequence of practical steps:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Adopt compatible software.</strong> If you are not already on accounting software, move to it. Dedicated software categorises transactions, links the bank feed and submits to HMRC directly, which is simpler and more robust than a spreadsheet for a practice with multiple income streams and staff costs.</li>
<li><strong>Set up dental-appropriate categories.</strong> Match your income and expense categories to how a dental practice actually earns and spends, so the data is meaningful, not generic.</li>
<li><strong>Link the business bank account.</strong> A bank feed brings transactions in automatically, cutting manual entry and keeping the records current.</li>
<li><strong>Keep it live.</strong> Get into the habit of recording as you go, so the books are always reasonably up to date.</li>
<li><strong>Agree the split with your accountant.</strong> Decide who does what each quarter, so nothing falls between you.</li>
</ul>

<p>The goal is that by your MTD start date the system already runs in a <strong>quarterly rhythm</strong>, so the regime is a continuation of good habits rather than a shock. The move to MTD is, for many practices, the prompt to finally put proper systems in place, and the side benefit, better visibility of the numbers, is worth having in its own right. Good systems also feed your <a href="https://www.dentalfinancepartners.co.uk/blog/practice-accounting/dental-practice-management-accounts-metrics-tracking">management accounts and metrics</a>, turning compliance data into genuine business insight.</p>

<h2>The shift from annual cram to quarterly rhythm</h2>

<p>The deepest change MTD brings is cultural rather than technical: the move from an <strong>annual cram</strong> to a <strong>quarterly rhythm</strong>. Under the old system, a dental practice could let bookkeeping slide through the year and reconstruct it in a burst before the January deadline. That worked, after a fashion, but it had real costs: deductions missed because receipts were lost, a tax bill that arrived as a surprise because the numbers were not visible until the last minute, and a stressful annual scramble.</p>

<p>MTD forces, and rewards, a different habit. Because the records must be current for each quarterly update, the practice that keeps its books live through the year finds the updates trivial and the year-end final declaration straightforward. The benefit is not just compliance; it is <strong>visibility</strong>. A practice with current digital records always knows roughly where its profit, and therefore its tax, stands, which makes everything from drawings decisions to investment planning better informed. Owners who have made this shift often say the discipline improved how they run the practice, not just how they file. So while MTD is imposed rather than chosen, the quarterly rhythm it enforces is genuinely good business hygiene, and the practices that embrace it rather than resent it get the most from it.</p>

<h2>Who is exempt or deferred</h2>

<p>Not every dentist is caught immediately, and it is worth knowing the edges. Those with <strong>qualifying income below the relevant threshold</strong> are outside MTD for ITSA until their income or the phased threshold brings them in: a part-time associate or a dentist with modest income under £20,000 may remain outside for some time. <strong>Limited companies</strong> are outside the income-tax regime entirely, as covered above, though they may already be within Making Tax Digital for VAT if they are VAT-registered above the threshold for taxable cosmetic income. There are also limited <strong>exemption</strong> categories, for example for those who genuinely cannot use digital tools for reasons such as disability, age, location or religious grounds, which have to be applied for and are not a general opt-out.</p>

<p>For the typical full-time dental associate or unincorporated principal, though, none of these applies: gross income comfortably exceeds £50,000, so they are in from the first phase. The exemptions are real but narrow, and a dentist should not assume one applies without confirming it. The safe planning assumption for a busy unincorporated dentist is that <strong>MTD applies from April 2026</strong>, and to prepare accordingly rather than hope to fall outside it.</p>

<h2>How good systems reduce your tax</h2>

<p>One under-appreciated benefit of MTD-ready bookkeeping is that it tends to <strong>reduce the tax you actually pay</strong>, not because the rules change but because better records capture <strong>more deductions</strong>. A dental practice has a long list of allowable costs, and the ones most often missed are the small, scattered ones: a professional subscription paid by personal card, mileage between practices, a proportion of the home office and phone, a CPD course, a piece of small equipment. In an annual scramble these get forgotten; in a live digital system, captured as they arise, they are all there at the final declaration.</p>

<p>Over a year, the cumulative effect of capturing every legitimate expense can be a meaningful reduction in taxable profit, and therefore in income tax and Class 4 National Insurance. The same records also ensure capital allowances are claimed correctly on equipment, and that the treatment of mixed NHS and private income, with its different VAT and pension implications, is clean. So the investment in good systems pays back partly in compliance, partly in lower stress, and partly in a lower tax bill from claiming everything you are entitled to. That last point reframes MTD from a cost into an opportunity: the practice that records diligently is the practice that pays no more tax than it must.</p>

<h2>Can you keep using a spreadsheet?</h2>

<p>A common question is whether a long-standing spreadsheet can survive MTD. The answer is <strong>potentially, but only if it connects to HMRC properly</strong>: a spreadsheet can be part of MTD-compliant record-keeping if it is linked to HMRC through <strong>compatible bridging software</strong> that submits the quarterly updates, with digital links rather than manual retyping. In practice, dedicated accounting software is usually <strong>simpler and more reliable</strong> for a dental practice, because it handles the categorisation, the bank feed and the submissions in one place. A spreadsheet plus bridging software can work for a simple set of affairs, but for most practices with staff, mixed income and capital allowances, proper software is the cleaner route, and MTD is the natural moment to adopt it.</p>

<h2>MTD does not change your tax, only your reporting</h2>

<p>It bears repeating, because it calms a lot of anxiety: <strong>MTD changes how and when you report, not how your tax is calculated</strong>. Your taxable profit, your allowable expenses, your income tax and Class 4 National Insurance, and your payments on account all follow the same rules. The regime is a <strong>compliance and systems change</strong>, not a tax rise. If anything, better records tend to <em>reduce</em> the tax you pay, because a practice that captures every expense digitally through the year claims deductions it would otherwise miss in an annual scramble. Framing MTD as a discipline that improves your numbers, rather than a burden imposed on them, is the right way to approach it.</p>

<h2>The final declaration and year-end adjustments</h2>

<p>The four quarterly updates are not the end of the year's reporting. After the tax year ends, MTD requires a <strong>final declaration</strong> that finalises the figures, brings in any <strong>adjustments and reliefs</strong> not captured in the quarterly summaries, and incorporates other income and the personal allowances to arrive at the actual tax position. For a dental practice this is where the year-end accounting judgments live: the <strong>capital allowances</strong> claim on equipment, accruals and prepayments, the correct treatment of <strong>mixed NHS and private income</strong>, the private-use adjustments on motor and home costs, and any reliefs. So the quarterly updates are a running summary, and the final declaration is where the return is genuinely finalised, much as the annual Self Assessment is today.</p>

<p>This matters for how you plan the year. The quarterly updates can be relatively rough running totals; the precision and the professional input concentrate at the <strong>final declaration</strong>. A practice that keeps clean digital records through the year makes that final step quick and accurate, because the data is all there to adjust. A practice with poor records faces the same reconstruction problem at the final declaration that it used to face at the annual return, just with the added obligation of having filed quarterly updates as well. So good systems pay off most at the year-end finalisation, which is where the real tax outcome is settled.</p>

<h2>Penalties under the new points-based system</h2>

<p>MTD comes with a <strong>points-based penalty system</strong> for late submissions, designed to be more proportionate than flat fines but still real. Broadly, late quarterly updates and the final declaration accrue penalty <strong>points</strong>, and once points reach a threshold a financial penalty follows, with separate penalties for late <strong>payment</strong> of tax. The detail of the regime is set in regulations and is worth confirming for the current year, but the principle for a practice owner is clear: <strong>habitual lateness now carries a cost</strong> even on the interim quarterly submissions, not just the final figure. This is another reason the quarterly rhythm has to be built into the practice's systems rather than treated as optional. A practice that files on time every quarter never troubles the points system; one that drifts accumulates points and then penalties. The lesson is the same as for payroll and auto-enrolment: under MTD, deadlines are recurring, so the systems have to be recurring too.</p>

<h2>Get ready before the start date, not on it</h2>

<p>Making Tax Digital is a real change of rhythm for unincorporated dentists, and the practices that handle it well are the ones that build the systems <strong>before</strong> their start date rather than reacting on it. Adopt compatible software, set up dental-appropriate categories, link the bank, keep the records live, and agree the quarterly split with your accountant. For a full-time associate or unincorporated principal caught from April 2026, that preparation is needed now. A specialist dental accountant can set up the software, structure the categories around how a dental practice really works, and run or review the quarterly cycle, so MTD becomes a tidy, continuous process and the January cliff edge disappears for good.</p>

<p>The dentists who will look back on MTD as a non-event are simply the ones who prepared a little ahead of time. The regime asks for digital records and quarterly updates, and a practice that already keeps clean digital books in compatible software has, in effect, already met it; the quarterly submission is just a button. The dentists who struggle will be those who left paper records and an annual habit in place until the last moment and then had to change everything under deadline pressure. Given that most full-time unincorporated dentists are caught from April 2026, the sensible course is to treat the move to MTD-ready systems as a project to complete in good time, capturing the side benefits of better visibility and fuller expense claims along the way, so that when the start date arrives the practice is simply carrying on as it already works.</p>
