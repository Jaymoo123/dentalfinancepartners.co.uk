<p>Ask a room of NHS dentists what a good per-UDA rate is and you will get a dozen different numbers, all delivered with confidence and most of them missing the point. The uncomfortable truth is that <strong>there is no national UDA value</strong>, so a great deal of UDA benchmarking compares figures that were never meant to be compared. Your rate was set individually, years ago, by your own practice's history, and whether it is "good" depends far more on what it costs you to deliver a unit than on where it sits against someone else's number.</p>

<p>This guide does UDA benchmarking properly. We explain how your rate was actually set, the realistic range across England and where most contracts cluster, what genuinely drives a high or low rate, and the crucial point that a <strong>high rate is not automatically a good one</strong> once you count the workload and cost behind it. Then we set out how to benchmark your own value in a way that tells you something useful, which means comparing your rate to your cost per unit, not to a mythical national average.</p>

<h2>Why there is no national UDA rate</h2>

<p>The single most important fact in this whole subject is that the UDA system has <strong>no national tariff</strong>. Unlike a fee scale where every dentist is paid the same for the same treatment, each NHS dental contract carries its own per-UDA value, and those values vary enormously from practice to practice. A dentist who hears that the rate "should" be a particular figure has usually been told a half-truth: there is a typical range, but no correct number, and certainly no rate you are entitled to simply because others have it.</p>

<p>This matters because so much anxiety and so many poor decisions come from comparing your rate to a headline figure as though it were a standard you are falling short of. It is not a standard. It is the output of a historic calculation specific to your contract, and understanding that calculation is the first step to benchmarking sensibly.</p>

<h2>How your rate was set: the 2006 baseline</h2>

<p>When the current UDA contract was introduced in 2006, each practice's per-UDA value was set by a backward-looking calculation: broadly, the practice's <strong>historic test-year NHS fee income divided by the number of units of dental activity</strong> it was contracted to deliver. That produced a starting rate unique to each contract, and it has been <strong>uplifted annually</strong> ever since. So a practice that happened to earn well in the test year, relative to its contracted activity, started with a high rate and has kept it; a practice that earned less started low and has stayed low.</p>

<p>The consequence is that two practices on the same street can have materially different rates for reasons that have nothing to do with their current quality, efficiency or value, and everything to do with their pre-2006 history. Once you see this, the futility of asking "what should my rate be?" becomes clear. The rate is what your history made it, and the live question is whether that rate works for the practice you run today.</p>

<h2>The realistic range and where rates cluster</h2>

<p>Across England, per-UDA values typically span roughly <strong>£15 to £45 or more</strong>, with the bulk of contracts <strong>clustering between £25 and £35</strong>. There is a tail of low-rate contracts below £20 and a thinner band of high-rate contracts above £45. The distribution is wide and uneven, which is exactly why an average is a poor benchmark: it hides a skew and tells you nothing about whether your particular rate covers your particular costs.</p>

<p>Use the range as orientation, not as a target. If your rate sits in the £25 to £35 cluster you are in the broad middle; below it you are in the lower tail and should look hard at your cost of delivery; above it you have a relatively comfortable rate, though even a high rate can be undone by a demanding contract or an expensive cost base. Our guide to <a href="https://www.dentalfinancepartners.co.uk/blog/nhs-contracts/how-uda-rates-vary-by-region-uk">how UDA rates vary by region</a> looks at the geographic spread in more detail.</p>

<h2>What actually drives a high or low rate</h2>

<p>Because the rate descends from a 2006 baseline, the main driver of whether yours is high or low is simply <strong>historic</strong>: what the practice earned relative to its contracted activity before the system began. Layered on top are a few real-world factors that correlate with rate level:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Pre-2006 fee income.</strong> Practices that delivered high-value work, or simply earned well, in the test year started higher.</li>
<li><strong>Contracted activity.</strong> The rate is income divided by UDAs, so a practice with a high baseline income but modest contracted activity can show a high rate.</li>
<li><strong>Subsequent variations.</strong> Contract changes, flexible commissioning and occasional renegotiations have nudged some rates over the years.</li>
</ul>

<p>What does <strong>not</strong> reliably drive the rate is your current performance. A superbly run, efficient practice can sit on a low historic rate, and a struggling one can sit on a high rate it inherited. This is why the rate alone is such a weak measure of how good a contract is.</p>

<h2>Why a high rate is not automatically good</h2>

<p>This is the point most benchmarking misses. A high per-UDA rate is welcome, but it has to be read against <strong>what it costs you to earn it</strong>. Consider two practices. One has a £35 rate in a high-cost city, with expensive premises, high staff wages, a complex patient base that needs more chair time per UDA, and a heavy Band 2 and Band 3 caseload. The other has a £27 rate in a low-cost town, with cheaper overheads, straightforward patients and an efficient skill mix. The lower-rate practice can easily be the <strong>more profitable</strong> of the two, because its cost per UDA is so much lower.</p>

<p>The rate is one input to profitability, not profitability itself. A genuinely useful benchmark therefore compares your rate to your <strong>cost per UDA</strong>: what it actually costs in staff, materials and overhead to deliver one unit. A £30 rate against a £20 cost per UDA is healthy; a £35 rate against a £33 cost per UDA is precarious. Always pair the two.</p>

<h2>Benchmarking your rate properly</h2>

<p>Doing this well is straightforward once you stop chasing a national figure. Work through four steps:</p>

<ol>
<li><strong>Find your rate.</strong> Divide your annual contract value by your contracted UDAs. Both figures are in your contract and monthly payment schedule.</li>
<li><strong>Place it in the range.</strong> See where it sits against the £25 to £35 cluster, as orientation only.</li>
<li><strong>Calculate your cost per UDA.</strong> Take your total cost of delivering NHS work, staff, materials, lab, a fair share of overhead, and divide by the UDAs you actually deliver. This is the number that matters.</li>
<li><strong>Compare rate to cost.</strong> The gap between your rate and your cost per UDA is your real margin per unit, and that, not the headline rate, tells you whether your contract is healthy.</li>
</ol>

<p>Remember the patient-charges point throughout: <strong>patient charges go towards your contract value, not on top of it</strong>, so do not double-count them as extra income when you model. Our guide to <a href="https://www.dentalfinancepartners.co.uk/blog/nhs-contracts/uda-value-explained-for-uk-dentists">how UDA value is explained for dentists</a> and to <a href="https://www.dentalfinancepartners.co.uk/blog/nhs-contracts/band-1-band-2-band-3-uda-treatment-explained">the Band 1, 2 and 3 treatment bands</a> set out the mechanics that feed this calculation.</p>

<h2>The cost-per-UDA calculation in detail</h2>

<p>Because cost per UDA is the number that turns a rate into a verdict, it is worth doing carefully rather than roughly. The aim is to capture the full cost of delivering your NHS activity, then divide by the UDAs you actually deliver. Start with the costs that clearly belong to NHS delivery: the <strong>associate or performer fees</strong> paid to deliver the work, the <strong>nursing and support time</strong> on NHS sessions, the <strong>materials and laboratory costs</strong> consumed on NHS treatment, and a fair <strong>share of premises and overhead</strong>. In a mixed practice you have to apportion shared costs between NHS and private work on a consistent basis, usually by chair time or by income share, so that NHS work carries its proper slice and no more.</p>

<p>Two refinements make the figure honest. First, include the <strong>owner's own clinical time</strong> at a realistic notional cost if the owner delivers NHS work, otherwise a single-handed practice will flatter its cost per UDA by treating the owner's labour as free. Second, be careful with the <strong>band mix</strong>: a Band 3 course of treatment counts as 12 UDAs but consumes far more chair time and laboratory cost than twelve Band 1 examinations, so a practice with a heavy complex caseload has a genuinely higher cost per UDA even at the same headline rate. The result of this exercise is a single, defensible cost per UDA that you can set against your rate, and the gap between the two is the only benchmark that actually predicts whether your contract makes money.</p>

<h2>Why two practices on the same rate can have opposite fortunes</h2>

<p>It is worth dwelling on how much can sit behind the same headline rate, because it explains why rate-only benchmarking is so misleading. Take two practices both on exactly £30 per UDA. The first is in an affluent, well-staffed area with stable, low-needs patients who mostly attend for examinations and simple care, so its band mix is light, its chair time per UDA is low, and it recruits associates easily at sensible fee shares. The second is in a high-needs area with an unstable list, a heavy Band 2 and Band 3 caseload, recruitment difficulty that forces up locum and associate costs, and patients who need more time per unit. Same rate, completely different economics. The first practice keeps a healthy margin on every UDA; the second may be delivering at close to cost or below.</p>

<p>This is why a dentist comparing their £30 rate to a friend's £30 rate, and concluding they are equally well-off, can be badly wrong. The rate is identical; the cost of earning it is not. The only way to know which of two same-rate practices is the stronger is to compare their cost per UDA, their band mix and their delivery security, none of which the rate reveals.</p>

<h2>What benchmarking the rate alone misses</h2>

<p>Beyond cost, several other factors decide whether a contract is genuinely good, and the rate captures none of them. <strong>Delivery security</strong>: a high rate on a contract you cannot reliably deliver, because of recruitment or patient-flow problems, carries clawback risk that can wipe out the apparent advantage. <strong>Contract size</strong>: a small contract at a high rate may matter less to your finances than a large contract at a modest rate. <strong>Patient mix and stability</strong>: a loyal, attending patient base makes any rate easier to deliver against than a transient, high-needs one. <strong>Future direction</strong>: with the contract reforms phasing in, the way activity is measured and paid is shifting, so a rate that looks good today should be read against where the contract is heading. A rate, in short, is a snapshot of one input. A good contract is a whole system, and benchmarking only the rate is like judging a car by its top speed.</p>

<h2>A worked comparison</h2>

<p>Take two practices, each contracted for <strong>6,000 UDAs</strong>.</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Practice A:</strong> £35 per UDA, so a contract value of £210,000. But its cost per UDA is £30, leaving a margin of £5 per UDA, or £30,000 across the contract.</li>
<li><strong>Practice B:</strong> £27 per UDA, so a contract value of £162,000. Its cost per UDA is £19, leaving a margin of £8 per UDA, or £48,000 across the contract.</li>
</ul>

<p>On the headline rate, Practice A looks far better. On the only measure that pays the bills, margin per UDA, Practice B is comfortably ahead. This is the entire argument for benchmarking cost alongside rate, in one example.</p>

<h2>UDA value and what your practice is worth</h2>

<p>Your rate does feed your practice's value, because valuations rest on normalised profit and the rate is a major input to NHS income. A higher rate, all else equal, supports a higher profit and a higher price. But buyers do not buy a rate; they buy a contract with a delivery history, a clawback and carry-forward position, a patient mix and a cost base. A strong rate helps a sale, but it will not rescue a contract that is expensive to deliver or carries delivery risk. If a sale is on your horizon, benchmark the whole economics, not just the rate. <h2>Benchmarking before a purchase</h2>

<p>Nowhere does rate benchmarking matter more than when <strong>buying a practice</strong>, and nowhere is the rate-only mistake more expensive. A buyer who sees a high per-UDA rate and pays a premium for it, without checking the cost of delivery and the delivery history, can buy a contract that is high-rate but low-margin, or high-rate but hard to deliver and therefore exposed to clawback. The benchmarking a buyer needs is the full economics: the per-UDA rate, the <strong>cost per UDA</strong> the practice actually runs at, the <strong>delivery history</strong> and any carry-forward or clawback position, the <strong>patient mix</strong> and stability, and the <strong>staffing model</strong> that delivers the activity. A rate is a single data point in that picture, and on its own it tells a buyer almost nothing about whether the contract will be profitable in their hands, with their cost base and their ability to recruit. A specialist dental accountant modelling the contract's real profitability is the proper safeguard, turning a headline rate into a verdict on whether the price is justified. <h2>Red flags in a low rate, and what to do</h2>

<p>A rate well below the cluster is not automatically a problem, plenty of low-rate contracts work fine in low-cost areas, but it is a prompt to investigate. The questions to ask are whether your <strong>cost per UDA</strong> leaves a workable margin at that rate, whether your <strong>delivery</strong> is comfortable or stretched, and, if you are buying, whether the contract is sustainable for you specifically. If your rate is low and your cost per UDA is close to it, the levers you control are the cost base and the mix of work, not the rate itself. Reducing the cost of delivering each unit, through skill mix, efficiency and materials, is usually more achievable than changing a rate set by history.</p>

<p>If you are weighing whether the NHS contract is worth keeping at all, that is a larger strategic question we cover separately, and the carry-forward and clawback position is central to it. Our companion guide to <a href="https://www.dentalfinancepartners.co.uk/blog/nhs-contracts/nhs-dental-uda-carry-forward-and-credit-rules-tax">UDA carry-forward and credit rules</a> explains how under and over-delivery are reconciled, which is the other half of understanding your contract's real economics.</p>

<p>The levers you control deserve emphasis, because a low rate can feel like a fixed sentence when it is not. You cannot easily change the historic rate, but you can change the <strong>cost</strong> of delivering against it and the <strong>mix</strong> of work behind it. Improving the <strong>skill mix</strong>, so that activity is delivered by the most appropriate, cost-effective team member, lowers cost per UDA. Tightening <strong>materials and laboratory</strong> spend, reviewing <strong>recall intervals</strong> in line with guidance, and improving <strong>appointment efficiency</strong> all reduce the cost of each unit. None of these touches the rate, but every one widens the gap between rate and cost, which is the margin that actually matters. A practice on a modest rate that relentlessly manages its cost per UDA can out-earn a practice on a higher rate that does not, which is the most encouraging conclusion of the whole benchmarking exercise: the number you inherited matters less than the cost base you control.</p>

<h2>How the contract reforms change the benchmarking picture</h2>

<p>The UDA system is not static, and the quality and payment reforms phasing in from April 2026 change some of what feeds your rate and your delivery. New <strong>UDA credits</strong>, such as those for Band 1 fluoride varnish and Band 1 urgent care, count towards delivery, which affects how easily you reach your target and therefore how your rate translates into reconciled income. Requirements around <strong>unscheduled care</strong> and prevention reshape the activity behind the contract value. None of this rewrites the core principle, that your rate is historic and your margin depends on cost, but it does mean a benchmarking exercise should be dated and revisited rather than treated as a one-off. A rate that supported a comfortable margin under the old activity rules may behave differently once the measurement of activity shifts. Keep your benchmarking current, and read your rate against the contract as it is configured for the year in question, not as it was five years ago. <h2>The benchmark that matters</h2>

<p>Stop asking what a good UDA rate is in the abstract, because the question has no answer. Your rate is a historic figure set by your own contract, and its quality is defined entirely by what it costs you to deliver against it. Benchmark your rate to the £25 to £35 cluster for orientation, then benchmark it against your own cost per UDA for the truth. A practice that knows its margin per unit, rather than just its headline rate, knows whether its contract is an asset or a liability, and can make every decision, from staffing to mix to a possible sale, on solid ground rather than on a number that was never a standard in the first place.</p>
