<h2>What Is AIA in Tax? The Short Answer for Dentists</h2>

<p>The Annual Investment Allowance (AIA) is a capital allowance that gives you 100% tax relief on qualifying plant and machinery costs up to £1 million per year <sup><a href="#ref-1" id="cite-1">[1]</a></sup>. For a UK dentist, this means you can deduct the full cost of most dental equipment from your taxable profits in the year you buy it, rather than spreading the deduction over several years.</p>

<p>If you are a practice owner replacing a dental chair, buying a new OPG machine, or fitting out a surgery, the AIA is likely your most valuable tax relief. It applies to sole traders, partnerships, and limited companies alike <sup><a href="#ref-1" id="cite-1">[1]</a></sup>. The £1 million limit is available to all businesses regardless of size until 31 March 2026 <sup><a href="#ref-2" id="cite-2">[2]</a></sup>.</p>

<p>This guide explains what qualifies, how to claim, and what traps to watch for. It is written for UK dentists, not general business owners, so the examples use dental equipment and practice scenarios.</p>

<h2>How the Annual Investment Allowance Works</h2>

<p>The AIA is effectively a 100% first-year allowance for business expenditure on qualifying plant or machinery <sup><a href="#ref-3" id="cite-3">[3]</a></sup>. You claim it in the accounting period when you incur the expenditure, not when you pay for it <sup><a href="#ref-1" id="cite-1">[1]</a></sup>. For a cash-basis sole trader, the timing rules differ slightly, but the principle is the same.</p>

<p>Here is a worked example. Dr Patel, a single-handed principal, buys a new dental chair for £18,000 and a compressor for £4,000 in the 2025/26 tax year. Her total qualifying expenditure is £22,000. She claims AIA on the full amount. Her taxable profit reduces by £22,000, saving her income tax at her marginal rate. If she is a higher-rate taxpayer (40%), the saving is £8,800. If she is an additional-rate taxpayer (45%), the saving is £9,900.</p>

<p>The AIA is not a separate tax return form. You include it in your capital allowances computation within your Self Assessment tax return (for sole traders and partnerships) or your company tax return (for limited companies).</p>

<h3>What Qualifies for AIA in a Dental Practice</h3>

<p>Most tangible assets used in a dental practice qualify as plant and machinery. The following items typically qualify for AIA:</p>

<ul>
<li>Dental chairs and delivery systems</li>
<li>X-ray units, including OPG and CBCT machines</li>
<li>Compressors and suction units</li>
<li>Autoclaves and sterilisation equipment</li>
<li>Computers, practice management software (if purchased as a capital asset, not a subscription)</li>
<li>Furniture and fittings (reception desks, waiting room chairs)</li>
<li>Air conditioning and ventilation systems</li>
<li>Lighting (if fixed and part of the building's infrastructure, it may qualify as plant)</li>
</ul>

<p>Items that do NOT qualify include cars (unless used wholly for business, which is rare for dentists), assets used for leasing, and assets gifted to connected parties <sup><a href="#ref-2" id="cite-2">[2]</a></sup>. Land and buildings themselves do not qualify for AIA, though fixtures within them may.</p>

<h3>What Does Not Qualify</h3>

<p>The AIA is not available for cars <sup><a href="#ref-2" id="cite-2">[2]</a></sup>. If you buy a car for the practice, you must claim capital allowances through the general pool at 18% per year (or 6% for cars with CO2 emissions above 50g/km). Dental vans used solely for business may qualify, but check the specific rules.</p>

<p>Assets used for leasing also do not qualify <sup><a href="#ref-2" id="cite-2">[2]</a></sup>. If you buy equipment and lease it to another practice, you cannot claim AIA on that equipment. Similarly, assets gifted to connected parties (for example, giving a used chair to a family member's practice) do not qualify.</p>

<h2>AIA for Different Business Structures</h2>

<h3>Sole Traders and Partnerships</h3>

<p>If you are a self-employed associate or a principal trading as a sole trader, you claim AIA on your Self Assessment tax return. The £1 million limit applies to your business as a whole <sup><a href="#ref-1" id="cite-1">[1]</a></sup>. If you use the cash basis of accounting, you can only claim capital allowances on business cars <sup><a href="#ref-4" id="cite-4">[4]</a></sup>. Most dentists use the accruals basis, so this restriction is unlikely to affect you.</p>

<p>For a partnership, the AIA limit applies to the partnership as a whole, not to each partner individually. If the partnership spends £200,000 on qualifying equipment, the partnership claims AIA on that amount, and the relief is shared between partners according to their profit-sharing ratio.</p>

<h3>Limited Companies</h3>

<p>Limited company dentists claim AIA through the company tax return. The £1 million limit applies to the company. If two or more limited companies are controlled by the same person, they only get one AIA between them <sup><a href="#ref-1" id="cite-1">[1]</a></sup>. This is a common trap for dentists who own multiple practice companies through a group structure.</p>

<p>For example, Dr Khan owns two limited companies: Khan Dental Ltd and Khan Ortho Ltd. Both are controlled by him. If Khan Dental Ltd spends £600,000 on equipment and Khan Ortho Ltd spends £500,000, the total group expenditure is £1.1 million. The group can only claim AIA on £1 million. The remaining £100,000 goes into the general pool at 18% writing-down allowance.</p>

<h3>Accounting Periods Shorter or Longer Than 12 Months</h3>

<p>If your accounting period is shorter than 12 months, the AIA limit is proportionally reduced. For an accounting period of 9 months, the AIA will be 9/12 x £1,000,000 = £750,000 <sup><a href="#ref-1" id="cite-1">[1]</a></sup>. If your accounting period is longer than 12 months, you get the full £1 million for each 12-month period within it, but the calculation can be complex. Speak to a dental-specialist accountant if your period straddles the 31 March 2026 expiry date.</p>

<h2>Full Expensing and the Super-Deduction: Alternatives to AIA</h2>

<p>From 1 April 2023, full expensing replaced the super-deduction for limited companies <sup><a href="#ref-4" id="cite-4">[4]</a></sup>. Full expensing gives 100% relief on qualifying main-rate plant and machinery (the same assets that qualify for AIA) with no cap. It is available only to limited companies, not to sole traders or partnerships.</p>

<p>For most dental limited companies, full expensing is more generous than AIA because there is no £1 million cap. However, full expensing does not apply to special-rate assets (integral features, long-life assets) which get a 50% first-year allowance instead <sup><a href="#ref-4" id="cite-4">[4]</a></sup>.</p>

<p>Sole traders and partnerships cannot use full expensing. They must rely on AIA (up to £1 million) and the general pool (18% or 6% writing-down allowance) for expenditure above the cap.</p>

<p>From 1 January 2026, a 40% first-year allowance will be available for qualifying plant and machinery purchased after that date <sup><a href="#ref-4" id="cite-4">[4]</a></sup>. This is a separate relief, not a replacement for AIA.</p>

<h2>Common Pitfalls for Dentists Claiming AIA</h2>

<h3>Pitfall 1: Buying from a Connected Party</h3>

<p>If you buy equipment from a connected party (a spouse, a company you control, a family trust), the AIA may be restricted. HMRC looks closely at transactions between connected parties. The relief is limited to the lower of the purchase price and the original cost to the connected party. Always get a professional valuation and keep records.</p>

<h3>Pitfall 2: Not Making a Section 198 Election When Buying a Practice</h3>

<p>When you buy a dental practice as a going concern, the seller has likely claimed capital allowances on the fixtures (chairs, lights, compressors). To claim AIA on those fixtures yourself, you and the seller must jointly make an election under CAA 2001 section 198. This election fixes the value of the fixtures for capital allowances purposes. Without it, HMRC may challenge your claim.</p>

<p>For more detail on practice purchase tax planning, see our <a href="/dental-guides/practice-purchase-financial-due-diligence">practice purchase financial due diligence guide</a>.</p>

<h3>Pitfall 3: Claiming AIA on Assets You Already Own</h3>

<p>You can only claim AIA in the period you bought the item <sup><a href="#ref-1" id="cite-1">[1]</a></sup>. You cannot go back and claim AIA on equipment you bought in previous years. If you missed a claim, you can amend your tax return within the statutory time limits (usually 12 months after the filing deadline), but you cannot use AIA for past purchases.</p>

<h3>Pitfall 4: Ignoring the Group Company Rule</h3>

<p>As noted above, if you control two or more limited companies, they share one AIA limit. This catches many dentists who incorporate each practice as a separate company. Plan your capital expenditure across the group to stay within the £1 million cap, or use full expensing if available.</p>

<h2>How to Claim AIA on Your Tax Return</h2>

<p>For sole traders and partnerships, you claim AIA in the capital allowances section of your Self Assessment tax return (SA103 or SA104). You list the qualifying expenditure and the amount of AIA you are claiming. The remaining expenditure (if any) goes into the main pool or special rate pool.</p>

<p>For limited companies, you claim AIA in the company tax return (CT600) and the accompanying capital allowances computation. Most accounting software will generate this automatically if you enter the asset details correctly.</p>

<p>HMRC has warned that companies should review their AIA claims to ensure they are correct and supported by appropriate records <sup><a href="#ref-2" id="cite-2">[2]</a></sup>. Keep invoices, delivery notes, and evidence of payment for every asset on which you claim AIA.</p>

<h2>Planning Your Equipment Purchases to Maximise AIA</h2>

<p>The £1 million AIA limit is temporary. It is set to expire on 31 March 2026 <sup><a href="#ref-2" id="cite-2">[2]</a></sup>. After that date, the limit will revert to £200,000 (the previous permanent level) unless the government extends it again. If you are planning significant capital expenditure, consider accelerating purchases into the current tax year to lock in the higher limit.</p>

<p>For example, Dr Singh plans to buy two new dental chairs (£36,000), an OPG machine (£25,000), and a CBCT scanner (£60,000) over the next 18 months. Total spend: £121,000. If he buys them all before 5 April 2026, he claims AIA on the full amount. If he delays, the limit may be lower, and he may need to spread the relief over several years.</p>

<p>For associates considering buying their own equipment, see our <a href="/for-associates">associate tax resources</a> for guidance on claiming capital allowances as a self-employed dentist.</p>

<h2>Record-Keeping Requirements</h2>

<p>HMRC expects you to keep records of all capital expenditure for at least six years after the end of the tax year to which the claim relates. For AIA claims, you should retain:</p>

<ul>
<li>Invoices or receipts showing the date, supplier, and amount</li>
<li>Proof of payment (bank statement, credit card statement)</li>
<li>A description of the asset and its location in the practice</li>
<li>Any valuation reports for second-hand assets</li>
<li>The section 198 election (if buying a practice with fixtures)</li>
</ul>

<p>If HMRC enquires into your tax return, they will ask for these records. Incomplete records can lead to the claim being disallowed, with interest and penalties.</p>

<h2>When to Speak to a Specialist</h2>

<p>The AIA rules are straightforward for most single-purchase scenarios, but complications arise with group companies, connected-party transactions, practice purchases, and assets straddling the 31 March 2026 expiry date. A dental-specialist accountant can help you structure your capital expenditure to maximise relief and avoid common pitfalls.</p>
