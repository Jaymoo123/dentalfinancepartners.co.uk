<p>Making Tax Digital for Income Tax (MTD ITSA) is the biggest change to how landlords report their rental income in decades. It does not change how much tax you pay; it changes how, and how often, you tell HMRC about it. From April 2026, instead of one Self Assessment return a year, affected landlords will keep digital records and file four quarterly updates plus a final declaration. This guide explains exactly who is in scope and from when, how the all-important gross qualifying-income test works, what the quarterly cycle actually involves, the software you will need, how to get ready, and the penalties for getting it wrong. It is general information, not advice for your specific situation: the last section explains when to get your own position checked.</p>

<h2>What MTD for Income Tax is, in one paragraph</h2>

<p>MTD for Income Tax replaces the annual Self Assessment tax return, for those in scope, with a quarterly digital reporting cycle. You must keep your income and expense records digitally in HMRC-recognised software (or a spreadsheet linked to recognised bridging software), send HMRC a cumulative summary of your figures every quarter, and then submit a final declaration after the tax year that confirms the year's figures and any other income. It applies to individuals (sole traders and landlords), not companies, and it is being phased in by income level rather than switched on for everyone at once.</p>

<h2>Who is in scope, and from when</h2>

<p>The mandate is phased in by <strong>qualifying income</strong>, with the threshold dropping in three steps:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>From 6 April 2026:</strong> mandatory for individuals whose qualifying income is <strong>over £50,000</strong>. The threshold is tested against your 2024/25 Self Assessment return.</li>
<li><strong>From 6 April 2027:</strong> the threshold drops to <strong>over £30,000</strong>, tested against your 2025/26 return.</li>
<li><strong>From 6 April 2028:</strong> the threshold drops to <strong>over £20,000</strong>, tested against your 2026/27 return.</li>
</ul>

<p>Note the word "over". The thresholds are strictly greater-than figures, so qualifying income of exactly £50,000 is not in the first cohort (it is over £30,000, so it joins from April 2027). HMRC writes to taxpayers who appear to be in scope, but the obligation is yours whether or not the letter arrives, so do not wait to be told.</p>

<h2>The qualifying-income test (this is the part that catches people out)</h2>

<p>Qualifying income is the figure the whole thing turns on, and it is widely misunderstood. It is:</p>

<ul>
<li>Your <strong>gross property rental income</strong> (total rents, <em>before</em> any expenses), <strong>plus</strong></li>
<li>Your <strong>gross sole-trade or self-employment turnover</strong> (again before deductions),</li>
<li><strong>added together.</strong></li>
</ul>

<p>Three things follow from this, and each one trips landlords up:</p>

<ol>
<li><strong>It is gross, not profit.</strong> A landlord with £52,000 of rent and £40,000 of allowable costs has a net profit of only £12,000, but is still <strong>in scope from April 2026</strong>, because the test is on the £52,000 of gross rent. High-rent, low-profit landlords (heavily geared, or with high running costs) are exactly the population HMRC's outreach targets.</li>
<li><strong>The two streams aggregate.</strong> £30,000 of trade plus £25,000 of rent is £55,000 of qualifying income, so that person is in from April 2026 even though neither stream alone clears £50,000.</li>
<li><strong>A lot of income does not count.</strong> Employment income (PAYE), pensions, dividends, savings interest and partnership profit shares are <strong>excluded</strong> from the qualifying-income test. A landlord with a £60,000 salary and £20,000 of rent has qualifying income of just £20,000 for this purpose, not £80,000.</li>
</ol>

<p>Foreign property income counts as property income for MTD where it is reported on your UK return.</p>

<h2>Who is not affected</h2>

<p>MTD for Income Tax does <strong>not</strong> apply to:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Limited companies.</strong> Companies are outside MTD ITSA entirely and continue to file an annual Company Tax return (CT600). This is one reason some landlords look at incorporation, though that decision has far bigger tax consequences than the reporting method and should never be driven by MTD alone.</li>
<li><strong>General partnerships and LLPs.</strong> Partnerships were originally pencilled in for April 2027 but have been <strong>deferred to a date to be confirmed</strong>. As things stand there is no confirmed start date. Note, though, that a partner with separate sole-trade or rental income outside the partnership can still be brought into MTD ITSA through that other income.</li>
<li><strong>Trustees.</strong> Trust property income reported on the SA900 trust return continues unchanged.</li>
</ul>

<h2>How joint ownership works</h2>

<p>If you own a property jointly, you test the threshold against <strong>your share</strong> of the gross rent, not the property's total. A couple owning a property jointly that produces £100,000 of gross rent each test £50,000 (on the default 50/50 split, absent a Form 17 election). A Form 17 election that splits the income 75/25 can bring the 75% owner into scope earlier than their partner. A common error is reporting "net of the letting agent's fees" as your gross income: that understates your qualifying income and can lead you to wrongly conclude you are below the mandate threshold, so always work from the <em>gross</em> rent collected on your behalf.</p>

<h2>What actually changes: the quarterly cycle</h2>

<p>Once you are in scope, your reporting year looks like this:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Keep digital records.</strong> Every item of income and expense recorded digitally, as you go, in compatible software (or a spreadsheet with a digital link to bridging software). The annual shoebox-of-receipts approach no longer works.</li>
<li><strong>Four quarterly updates.</strong> A cumulative summary of your income and expenses for each quarter, sent to HMRC through your software. On the standard UK-tax-year quarters the periods end on 5 July, 5 October, 5 January and 5 April, and each update is due roughly one month later (by 7 August, 7 November, 7 February and 7 May respectively). A calendar-quarter election (period ends 30 June, 30 September, 31 December and 31 March) is available from 6 April 2026 if you prefer.</li>
<li><strong>A final declaration.</strong> After the tax year ends, a final declaration replaces the old Self Assessment return. It confirms the year's figures, brings in any other income (employment, dividends, savings and so on) and finalises your tax. It is due by <strong>31 January following the tax year</strong>, the same deadline you are used to.</li>
</ul>

<p>The quarterly updates are summaries, not full tax computations: you are not calculating and paying tax four times a year. The tax position is finalised once, at the final declaration. Your payment dates (the 31 January and 31 July payments on account) are unchanged by MTD.</p>

<h2>Worked example: a landlord who is in from April 2026</h2>

<p>Priya lets two flats. The gross rents are £34,000 and £21,000, so £55,000 in total. Her allowable costs (mortgage interest, agent fees, repairs, insurance) come to £33,000, leaving a real profit of £22,000.</p>

<p>Her qualifying income is the <strong>gross £55,000</strong>, not the £22,000 profit. Because that is over £50,000, Priya is mandated into MTD for Income Tax <strong>from 6 April 2026</strong>. From that date she must keep digital records, file four quarterly updates and a final declaration each year. The fact that her profit is well under £50,000 is irrelevant to the test.</p>

<h2>Worked example: aggregation brings someone in</h2>

<p>Tom has a small consultancy with £28,000 of turnover and lets one property for £24,000 of gross rent. Neither figure alone reaches £50,000, but his qualifying income is the aggregate, <strong>£52,000</strong>. Tom is therefore in scope <strong>from 6 April 2026</strong>, a point he would miss if he looked at each activity in isolation.</p>

<h2>Worked example: a salary that does not count</h2>

<p>Amara earns £70,000 in employment (PAYE) and has £18,000 of gross rent from one flat. Her salary is excluded from the qualifying-income test, so her qualifying income is just £18,000. That is below every published threshold, so she is <strong>not yet mandated</strong> (though she could join voluntarily, and would fall in scope if her rent rose over £20,000 once the April 2028 threshold takes effect).</p>

<h2>The software you will need</h2>

<p>MTD requires <strong>HMRC-recognised compatible software</strong>. HMRC maintains a "find software that's compatible with Making Tax Digital for Income Tax" list on gov.uk, which is updated regularly, so check the live list rather than relying on any fixed set of product names. Whatever you choose must support digital record-keeping, the quarterly update submissions, and the final declaration.</p>

<p>You do not necessarily have to abandon spreadsheets. A spreadsheet plus <strong>bridging software</strong> is acceptable, provided the bridging software is on HMRC's recognised list, and provided the data moves between your spreadsheet and the bridging software via a <strong>digital link</strong>. A digital link is a transfer that does not involve manual transcription: cell references and formulae, linked tables, an API extract or a scripted CSV import all qualify. Copy-paste, manual re-keying and reading a number off one screen to type into another do <strong>not</strong> qualify. If you go the spreadsheet route, discipline your columns into the SA105 property categories (gross rent, agent fees, repairs, insurance, council tax/utilities, finance costs, other) from day one.</p>

<p>Some free or low-cost options exist (for example, FreeAgent is bundled for certain NatWest and Royal Bank of Scotland business customers), but the recognised list is dominated by paid products. Cost should not be the only factor: pick something you will actually keep up to date every quarter.</p>

<h2>If you use an accountant</h2>

<p>For MTD ITSA, your accountant connects to your account through an <strong>Agent Services Account (ASA)</strong>, and you must authorise them specifically for MTD. The old 64-8 paper authorisations do <strong>not</strong> carry across, and authorisations do not transfer automatically if you change accountant, so a new accountant will need to request fresh authorisation. If you own property jointly, each owner authorises the agent separately; there is no "one spouse covers both" rule.</p>

<h2>How to prepare (a sensible runway)</h2>

<p>The single biggest mistake is leaving it until the start date. A practical order of preparation:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Confirm whether and when you are in.</strong> Work out your gross qualifying income (rent plus any trade, before expenses) and find the earliest threshold you clear. The checker on this page does exactly that.</li>
<li><strong>Choose and set up software early.</strong> Get it in place a tax year ahead if you can, so you go live already comfortable with it rather than learning it under a deadline.</li>
<li><strong>Get your bookkeeping current and digital.</strong> Move to recording income and expenses as they happen, categorised into the SA105 categories, rather than reconstructing the year each January.</li>
<li><strong>Handle the joint-ownership and agent-statement detail.</strong> Make sure you are capturing your <em>share</em> of joint rents and the <em>gross</em> rent on agent-managed properties, not the net figure paid into your account.</li>
<li><strong>Sort out agent authorisations.</strong> If an accountant will file for you, get the ASA authorisation in place before your first quarter, not in the week it is due.</li>
</ul>

<h2>The penalty regime</h2>

<p>From 6 April 2026, MTD ITSA brings a new, points-based late-submission regime and an accelerated late-payment schedule:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Late submissions are points-based.</strong> You receive one point for each missed quarterly update. A <strong>£200 fixed penalty</strong> is charged when you reach the points threshold (four points, within a rolling 24-month window), and again for each further default at that threshold. So a single late update does not, by itself, trigger an immediate £200 charge; it is the accumulation of points that does.</li>
<li><strong>Late payment is charged on an accelerated schedule.</strong> For MTD ITSA from 6 April 2026 (following the Spring Statement 2025 changes), the late-payment penalties are <strong>3% of the unpaid tax from day 15, a further 3% from day 30, and then 10% per annum from day 31</strong>. Do not apply the older 2%/2%/4% schedule (the 31/46/91-day triggers): that legacy regime still applies to non-MTD income tax and VAT, but not to MTD ITSA.</li>
</ul>

<p>Interest also runs on late-paid tax in the usual way, on top of the penalties.</p>

<h2>Can you leave MTD once you are in?</h2>

<p>Yes, but not quickly. You can <strong>exit</strong> if your qualifying income falls below the threshold for <strong>three consecutive tax years</strong>; you notify HMRC and they confirm removal from the MTD obligations. You then return to the ordinary Self Assessment cycle, and you would re-enter MTD if your income rose back above the threshold. You can also <strong>opt in voluntarily</strong> if you are below the threshold and would rather move to digital quarterly reporting now; voluntary participants are bound by the same quarterly cycle and penalties as mandated ones.</p>

<h2>Frequently asked questions</h2>

<h3>Is the threshold based on my rental profit or my rents?</h3>
<p>Your gross rents, before any expenses, added to any gross sole-trade turnover. It is not based on profit. This is why landlords with modest profits but high rents are frequently in scope.</p>

<h3>I earn a salary and have a small rental. Am I in?</h3>
<p>Employment income does not count towards qualifying income. Only your gross rent (plus any gross self-employment) is tested. If that figure is below the relevant threshold, you are not mandated, regardless of how high your salary is.</p>

<h3>What was the £10,000 threshold I read about?</h3>
<p>The original 2018 design used a £10,000 threshold, but that was abandoned in late 2022 and never took effect. The phased £50,000 / £30,000 / £20,000 schedule replaced it. Ignore any older page still quoting £10,000.</p>

<h3>Do I pay tax four times a year now?</h3>
<p>No. The quarterly updates are summaries of your income and expenses, not tax payments. Your tax is finalised once, at the final declaration, and your payment dates (31 January and 31 July) are unchanged.</p>

<h3>Does MTD apply to my limited company's rental income?</h3>
<p>No. Companies are outside MTD for Income Tax entirely and continue to file a Company Tax return. MTD ITSA is for individuals.</p>

<h3>I own a property 50/50 with my spouse. Whose income is tested?</h3>
<p>Each of you tests your own share of the gross rent. On a default 50/50 split, a property producing £100,000 of gross rent gives each owner £50,000 of qualifying income from that property.</p>
