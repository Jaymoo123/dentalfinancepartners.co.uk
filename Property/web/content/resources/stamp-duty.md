<p>Stamp Duty Land Tax (SDLT) is usually the single largest tax an investor pays on the way into a property, and for buy-to-let it is loaded with surcharges that can double or triple the bill a homeowner would face on the same purchase. This guide explains exactly how the bands work, what the additional-dwelling and non-resident surcharges add, how first-time-buyer relief works (and when it disappears), the edge cases that genuinely move the figure, and where Scotland and Wales part company from the rest of the UK. It is general information for England and Northern Ireland, not advice for your specific purchase: the last section explains when to get your own numbers confirmed.</p>

<h2>What SDLT is, and where it applies</h2>

<p>SDLT is a tax on the purchase of land and property in <strong>England and Northern Ireland</strong>. It is charged on the "chargeable consideration" — broadly the price you pay, including certain things given in addition to cash. It is your responsibility (in practice, your conveyancer's) to file an SDLT return and pay within <strong>14 days</strong> of completion, even where no tax is due because a relief applies.</p>

<p>Crucially, SDLT is an <strong>England and NI</strong> tax only. Scotland has its own Land and Buildings Transaction Tax (LBTT) and Wales has its own Land Transaction Tax (LTT), each with different bands, different thresholds and different surcharges. The figures in this guide do not apply north of the border or in Wales; there is a dedicated section on both below.</p>

<h2>The standard residential bands</h2>

<p>SDLT is a <em>slice</em> (marginal) tax: each rate applies only to the part of the price that falls within its band, not to the whole price. From <strong>1 April 2025</strong>, the standard residential bands (England and NI) are:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>0%</strong> on the first £125,000</li>
<li><strong>2%</strong> on the slice from £125,001 to £250,000</li>
<li><strong>5%</strong> on the slice from £250,001 to £925,000</li>
<li><strong>10%</strong> on the slice from £925,001 to £1,500,000</li>
<li><strong>12%</strong> on everything above £1,500,000</li>
</ul>

<p>Because the rates are sliced, your <em>effective</em> rate is always lower than the top band you reach. A £350,000 purchase, for example, pays nothing on the first £125,000, 2% on the next £125,000 (£2,500) and 5% on the final £100,000 (£5,000) — £7,500 in total, an effective rate of around 2.1%, even though the buyer has "reached" the 5% band.</p>

<h2>The additional-dwelling surcharge: the buy-to-let cost</h2>

<p>This is the part that matters most to investors. If the property you are buying is an <strong>additional dwelling</strong> — a buy-to-let, a second home, a holiday home, or <em>any</em> residential purchase made by a company — a flat <strong>5% surcharge</strong> is added <em>on top of</em> the standard rates, applied to the <strong>whole price</strong>, not just a slice of it.</p>

<p>The surcharge was raised from 3% to <strong>5%</strong> for transactions on or after <strong>31 October 2024</strong>. Anyone quoting 3% is working from out-of-date figures. The "additional" test broadly catches you if, at the end of the day of the purchase, you own (or part-own) two or more residential properties anywhere in the world and you are not replacing your only or main residence.</p>

<p>Because the 5% is flat and applies from the first pound, it dwarfs the standard bands at lower prices. On that same £350,000 property, the surcharge adds 5% × £350,000 = <strong>£17,500</strong>, taking the total from £7,500 to £25,000 — an effective rate of about 7.1%. The surcharge is, in most investor purchases, the largest single component of the bill.</p>

<h3>The replacement-main-residence exception</h3>

<p>If you are buying a new main home and selling your old one, you are not buying an "additional" dwelling and the surcharge should not apply — even if you own buy-to-lets. If the timing means you complete on the new home before you have sold the old one, you pay the surcharge up front and can <strong>reclaim</strong> it if you sell your previous main residence within <strong>36 months</strong>. The reclaim has its own time limit and must be made through HMRC; do not assume your conveyancer has flagged it.</p>

<h2>The non-resident surcharge</h2>

<p>A separate <strong>2% surcharge</strong> applies where the purchaser is a <strong>non-UK resident</strong> buying residential property in England or NI. Residence for this test has its own definition (broadly, present in the UK for fewer than 183 days in the 12 months around the transaction) and is not the same as your income-tax residence status.</p>

<p>The 2% sits <em>on top of</em> everything else. A non-UK resident buying a buy-to-let therefore pays the standard bands <strong>plus the 5% additional-dwelling surcharge plus the 2% non-resident surcharge</strong>. On the £350,000 example, that is £7,500 + £17,500 + £7,000 = <strong>£32,000</strong>, an effective rate of around 9.1%. As with the replacement exception, a non-resident who later becomes UK-resident within a set period may be able to reclaim the 2%.</p>

<h2>First-time-buyer relief</h2>

<p>First-time-buyer relief runs the other way, reducing the bill for buyers who have never owned a residential property anywhere in the world. The relief gives:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>0%</strong> on the first £300,000</li>
<li><strong>5%</strong> on the slice from £300,000 to £500,000</li>
</ul>

<p>The relief is <strong>fully withdrawn</strong> once the price exceeds <strong>£500,000</strong> — there is no taper, the buyer simply pays standard rates on the whole purchase. And it never applies to an additional property: a first-time buyer is, by definition, not buying an additional dwelling. For an investor this section is mostly relevant as a contrast — it shows how far the surcharges push an investor's bill above what a first-time owner-occupier would pay on the identical property.</p>

<h2>Worked example: a £300,000 flat, four ways</h2>

<p>Take a single £300,000 flat and run it through the four buyer types in our calculator and Excel model:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Standard (main home):</strong> 0% on £125,000, 2% on £125,000, 5% on £50,000 = £0 + £2,500 + £2,500 = <strong>£5,000</strong>.</li>
<li><strong>Additional property (+5%):</strong> £5,000 + (5% × £300,000 = £15,000) = <strong>£20,000</strong>. The surcharge alone is three times the standard bill.</li>
<li><strong>Non-resident buy-to-let (+2% and +5%):</strong> £20,000 + (2% × £300,000 = £6,000) = <strong>£26,000</strong>.</li>
<li><strong>First-time buyer:</strong> 0% on the whole £300,000 = <strong>£0</strong>. The relief covers the full price because it is at the cap.</li>
</ul>

<p>The same flat costs a first-time owner-occupier nothing, a UK landlord £20,000, and a non-resident landlord £26,000. That £20,000 gap between the homeowner and the UK landlord is the entire story of how SDLT treats investors.</p>

<h2>Worked example: a £600,000 house</h2>

<p>Now a £600,000 house, where the higher bands and the FTB cliff-edge come into play:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Standard:</strong> £0 + £2,500 (2% band) + £17,500 (5% on £250,001–£600,000) = <strong>£20,000</strong>.</li>
<li><strong>Additional property (+5%):</strong> £20,000 + £30,000 = <strong>£50,000</strong>.</li>
<li><strong>Non-resident buy-to-let (+2% and +5%):</strong> £50,000 + £12,000 = <strong>£62,000</strong>.</li>
<li><strong>First-time buyer:</strong> the price is over £500,000, so the relief is withdrawn entirely and the buyer pays the <strong>standard £20,000</strong> — exactly the same as any other owner-occupier, with no relief at all.</li>
</ul>

<p>This is the trap at £500,000: a first-time buyer at £500,000 pays £10,000 (0% to £300k, 5% on £200k); a first-time buyer at £500,001 pays the standard rate, around £15,000, because the relief vanishes. The pound that crosses the line costs roughly £5,000.</p>

<h2>Edge cases that genuinely change the figure</h2>

<p>The four headline scenarios cover most purchases, but several special rules can move the number materially. None of these are modelled in the standard calculator, and each is a reason to get the figure confirmed before exchange.</p>

<h3>Mixed-use property</h3>

<p>Where a transaction includes both residential and non-residential elements — the classic case is a shop with a flat above, or a house bought with a genuine paddock or commercial land — the whole purchase is charged at the <strong>non-residential</strong> rates (0% to £150,000, 2% on £150,000–£250,000, 5% above), and <strong>no additional-dwelling surcharge applies</strong>. The non-residential rates are often much lower, so this can be a large saving. HMRC scrutinises mixed-use claims hard, and a garden or a field used as part of the home does not make a property mixed-use; there must be a genuine non-residential element. Get this assessed properly — a wrong claim invites an enquiry and penalties.</p>

<h3>Six or more dwellings in one deal</h3>

<p>Where six or more separate dwellings are bought in a single transaction, they are <strong>automatically treated as non-residential</strong> (section 116(7) FA 2003): non-residential rates apply and there is no 5% surcharge. This is a statutory deeming, not an election, and it survived the abolition of Multiple Dwellings Relief. It is the principal SDLT-efficient route for genuine bulk portfolio acquisitions.</p>

<h3>Multiple Dwellings Relief is gone</h3>

<p>Multiple Dwellings Relief (MDR), which used to reduce SDLT on a purchase of two or more dwellings, was <strong>abolished</strong> for transactions with an effective date on or after <strong>1 June 2024</strong>. Anti-forestalling rules block late claims dressed up through sub-sales or options. If a competitor page, an old calculator, or an over-eager adviser tells you MDR can cut your SDLT on a portfolio purchase, it is wrong — the relief no longer exists.</p>

<h3>Uninhabitable property</h3>

<p>A building so derelict that it is not "suitable for use as a dwelling" at the effective date can fall outside residential treatment (the <em>Bewley</em> principle), meaning non-residential rates and no surcharge. The bar is high — a property needing modernisation is still a dwelling; this is about genuine structural dereliction or contamination — and HMRC routinely challenges these claims. Contemporaneous surveyor evidence is essential.</p>

<h3>The 15% corporate rate</h3>

<p>A "non-natural person" (broadly a company) buying a single dwelling worth more than £500,000 can face a flat <strong>15% rate</strong> on the whole price under Schedule 4A FA 2003, unless a relief applies. A genuine property-rental business is relieved from the 15% rate and instead pays the standard rates plus the 5% surcharge — which is what our model assumes — but the 15% trap is real for purchases that are not genuine lettings (for example, a property held for an occupant connected with the company).</p>

<h3>Linked transactions and leases</h3>

<p>Separate purchases between the same parties can be "linked", aggregating the prices for rate purposes. And on the grant of a <strong>lease</strong>, SDLT is charged not only on any premium but also on the net present value of the rent over the term, which is a separate calculation entirely. Neither is in the standard model.</p>

<h2>Scotland (LBTT) and Wales (LTT)</h2>

<p>If your property is in Scotland or Wales, none of the SDLT figures above apply.</p>

<p><strong>Scotland — Land and Buildings Transaction Tax (LBTT),</strong> collected by Revenue Scotland, has its own bands and thresholds. Its equivalent of the additional-dwelling surcharge is the <strong>Additional Dwelling Supplement (ADS)</strong>, which is <strong>8%</strong> (raised from 6% on 5 December 2024) — considerably higher than the English 5%. There is no LBTT non-resident surcharge.</p>

<p><strong>Wales — Land Transaction Tax (LTT),</strong> collected by the Welsh Revenue Authority, also has its own bands and its own higher rates for additional properties, and notably <strong>no first-time-buyer relief</strong> (the nil-rate threshold for main homes is set higher instead). There is no LTT non-resident surcharge.</p>

<p>Always use the correct devolved figures for Scottish and Welsh property; applying SDLT numbers to an LBTT or LTT purchase will give the wrong answer, usually understating the bill in Scotland.</p>

<h2>Mitigation and planning (balanced, not advice)</h2>

<p>SDLT is hard to avoid legitimately, and the surcharges are the policy point of the tax, not a loophole to be engineered away. The genuine routes are narrow and fact-specific:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Get the classification right.</strong> The biggest legitimate savings come from correctly identifying mixed-use, six-or-more-dwellings, or genuinely uninhabitable property — not from a clever structure. These are evidence-based factual questions, and worth proper advice when the facts support them.</li>
<li><strong>Claim what you are owed.</strong> The replacement-main-residence reclaim (sell the old home within 36 months) and the non-resident reclaim are commonly missed. If you paid a surcharge you should not have, you can reclaim it within the time limit.</li>
<li><strong>Plan the timing of ownership.</strong> For couples and joint buyers, who is on the title and whether anyone already owns a dwelling affects the additional-property test. This needs to be decided before exchange, not after.</li>
<li><strong>Company purchases are not an SDLT saving.</strong> Buying through a company does not avoid the 5% surcharge — a company always pays it (and risks the 15% rate above £500,000). The case for a company is about income tax and Corporation Tax, not SDLT; the SDLT is a cost of that decision, not a benefit. Model it before incorporating.</li>
</ul>

<p>What does <em>not</em> work: artificial sub-sales, "SDLT mitigation schemes" sold as products, and retrofit "partnership" structures slapped together just before completion to access reliefs. HMRC has a general anti-avoidance rule (s.75A) aimed squarely at these, and the schemes routinely fail. Treat anything that promises to make the surcharge disappear with deep suspicion.</p>

<h2>Frequently asked questions</h2>

<h3>Do I pay the 5% surcharge if I am buying my first buy-to-let but already own my home?</h3>
<p>Yes. The surcharge applies because, at the end of the day of the purchase, you own two residential properties (your home and the buy-to-let) and you are not replacing your main residence. Owning your own home plus one investment property is the most common surcharge case.</p>

<h3>Can I avoid the surcharge by buying through a limited company?</h3>
<p>No. A company always pays the 5% additional-dwelling surcharge on residential property, and a company buying a single dwelling over £500,000 can face the 15% rate unless it is a genuine letting business. Incorporation is an income-tax and Corporation-Tax decision; the SDLT is a cost of it.</p>

<h3>Does first-time-buyer relief apply to a buy-to-let?</h3>
<p>No. The relief is only for someone who has never owned a residential property and is buying their first home to live in. A buy-to-let is an additional/investment property, so the relief cannot apply, and the surcharge will.</p>

<h3>I am buying a new home before selling my old one — do I pay the surcharge?</h3>
<p>You pay it up front because, on completion, you briefly own two homes. You can reclaim it if you sell your previous main residence within 36 months. The reclaim is not automatic — it must be made to HMRC within the time limit.</p>

<h3>Is Multiple Dwellings Relief still available?</h3>
<p>No. MDR was abolished for transactions on or after 1 June 2024. Any tool or adviser still applying it is out of date.</p>

<h3>My property is in Scotland — can I use these figures?</h3>
<p>No. Scotland charges LBTT with an 8% Additional Dwelling Supplement, and Wales charges LTT. Both have different bands. Use the correct devolved figures; SDLT numbers will be wrong.</p>
