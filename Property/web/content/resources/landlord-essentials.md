<p>If you let a residential property in the UK, you are running a small business in the eyes of HMRC, and there is a set of rules you need to get right from the start. This guide is the plain-English version of those rules: what counts as taxable rental income, which costs you can and cannot deduct, the £1,000 property allowance, how Section 24 changes the treatment of your mortgage, how and when to register for Self Assessment, the deadlines that carry penalties, what records to keep and for how long, and the mistakes we see landlords make again and again. It is general information for England, Wales and Northern Ireland, not advice for your specific situation: the last section explains when to get your own numbers run.</p>

<h2>What you actually pay tax on: rental profit, not rent</h2>

<p>You do not pay income tax on the rent you collect. You pay it on your rental <strong>profit</strong>, which is your rental income for the tax year less your allowable expenses. The rental business is taxed as a whole, so if you let more than one property you pool the income and the costs across the portfolio (UK and overseas property are two separate businesses and are pooled separately). The profit is then added to your other income, salary, pension, self-employment, and taxed at your marginal rate.</p>

<p>That "added to your other income" point matters more than landlords expect. A profit that would be basic-rate on its own can be taxed at 40% once it sits on top of a salary, and once your total income passes £100,000 you begin to lose your personal allowance. The right way to think about your rental tax is always "what band does this profit land in, given everything else I earn", which is exactly what the accompanying calculator and Excel model do.</p>

<h2>What income to declare</h2>

<p>Rental income is more than the headline rent. You must declare:</p>

<ul>
<li>The <strong>rent</strong> your tenants pay, gross, before any letting-agent fees are taken off. If your agent pays you the rent net of commission, you still declare the gross figure and deduct the commission as an expense, not the net amount as income.</li>
<li><strong>Non-refundable deposits</strong> and any part of a deposit you keep at the end of a tenancy to cover unpaid rent or damage (the part you keep is income; a deposit held in a protection scheme and returned is not).</li>
<li>Payments for <strong>services and use of furniture</strong>, ground rent and service charges you recharge to tenants, and money from grants or insurance that replaces lost rent.</li>
<li>Income from a <strong>lodger</strong> in your own home, though the Rent-a-Room scheme can exempt up to £7,500 of this (£3,750 if you share the income), which is a separate relief from the property allowance below.</li>
</ul>

<p>You report property income on the <strong>SA105</strong> pages of your Self Assessment tax return (or, once you are inside Making Tax Digital for Income Tax, via quarterly digital updates and a final declaration, see below).</p>

<h2>Allowable expenses versus capital: the distinction that trips people up</h2>

<p>To be deductible, a cost must be <strong>revenue</strong> in nature (a running cost of the letting) and incurred <strong>wholly and exclusively</strong> for the rental business. Costs that improve the property or that are part of buying or selling it are <strong>capital</strong>, and they are not deductible against your rental income, though they usually reduce a future Capital Gains Tax bill instead. Getting an item on the wrong side of this line is one of the most common and most expensive errors.</p>

<h3>Typically allowable (revenue) running costs</h3>

<ul>
<li>Repairs and maintenance that restore the property to its previous condition: redecorating, fixing a roof, replacing a broken boiler with a like-for-like one, mending guttering.</li>
<li>Letting-agent and management fees, advertising for tenants, and the cost of drawing up tenancy agreements.</li>
<li>Landlord insurance (buildings, contents, rent guarantee, public liability).</li>
<li>Ground rent, service charges and any council tax or utility bills you pay during void periods.</li>
<li>Accountancy fees for preparing the rental accounts, professional fees for rent collection and legal costs of renewing a short lease.</li>
<li>The cost of services you provide, such as a cleaner or gardener for the let property.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Typically NOT allowable against income (capital or private)</h3>

<ul>
<li><strong>Improvements:</strong> an extension, a new conservatory, upgrading from a basic kitchen to a high-spec one, or converting a property. These add to the property's value and are capital. The "repair versus improvement" line is genuinely difficult, and the modern materials rule means a like-for-like replacement using the nearest current equivalent (a single-glazed window replaced with double glazing because that is now standard) is usually still a repair, not an improvement.</li>
<li>The cost of <strong>buying or selling</strong> the property, including SDLT, legal fees on purchase and estate-agent fees on sale (these are CGT items).</li>
<li><strong>Mortgage interest</strong>, which is no longer deducted as an expense at all for residential lets, see Section 24 below.</li>
<li>Your own time and labour, and any private-use portion of a cost.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Replacing furnishings</h3>

<p>For furnished or part-furnished lets you cannot deduct the cost of the original furniture, but the <strong>replacement of domestic items relief</strong> lets you deduct the cost of replacing a like-for-like item (sofa, bed, white goods, carpets, curtains, crockery) once it wears out, less any proceeds from selling the old one and less the cost of any upgrade element. The first purchase is not relievable; subsequent replacements are.</p>

<h2>The £1,000 property allowance</h2>

<p>Every individual gets a <strong>£1,000 property allowance</strong> each tax year. It works in one of two ways, and you take whichever helps you most:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Full relief.</strong> If your <em>gross</em> property income for the year is £1,000 or less, it is completely tax-free and you generally do not need to declare it or register for Self Assessment for it. You cannot create a loss with the allowance, and there are anti-avoidance rules where the income comes from a connected party (such as your own employer or a company you control).</li>
<li><strong>Partial relief.</strong> If your gross property income is more than £1,000, you can choose to deduct the £1,000 allowance <em>instead of</em> your actual expenses. This is worth doing only when your real expenses are below £1,000, for example a low-cost let with little maintenance in the year. You cannot claim the allowance and also deduct expenses, it is one or the other.</li>
</ul>

<p>For most landlords with a mortgaged property and normal running costs, actual expenses exceed £1,000 and the allowance is irrelevant, so claim your real costs. The accompanying calculator applies whichever is better for each property automatically.</p>

<h2>Section 24 in brief: your mortgage is not a normal expense</h2>

<p>Since April 2020, an individual residential landlord can no longer deduct mortgage interest (or other finance costs such as arrangement fees) from rental profit. Instead, your full profit is taxed and you receive a separate <strong>basic-rate tax credit</strong> on the finance costs: 20% for 2026/27, rising to 22% from 2027/28. For a basic-rate taxpayer the credit broadly cancels the extra tax. For a higher or additional-rate taxpayer it does not, because the credit is given at 20%/22% while the income is taxed at 40%/45% (42%/47% from 2027/28), and that gap is the real cost of Section 24.</p>

<p>The credit is capped at the lowest of three figures: the credit rate times your finance costs; the credit rate times your rental profit; and the credit rate times your income above the personal allowance. Where the cap restricts the credit, the un-relieved finance costs carry forward to future years. Companies are outside Section 24 entirely, which is why incorporation is on every geared higher-rate landlord's mind, but moving a portfolio into a company is a major decision with its own CGT and SDLT costs. We cover that in depth in our dedicated Section 24 guide and calculator.</p>

<h2>Worked example: a single higher-rate landlord (and why the band split matters)</h2>

<p>Take a landlord with a £45,000 salary and one let property:</p>

<ul>
<li>Rental income: £18,000</li>
<li>Allowable running costs: £3,000</li>
<li>Mortgage interest: £6,000</li>
</ul>

<p>Taxable rental profit is £18,000 − £3,000 = £15,000 (interest is not deducted). This is the point most quick calculators get wrong: the profit does <em>not</em> all fall in the 40% band. The £45,000 salary uses £32,430 of the £37,700 basic-rate band (after the £12,570 personal allowance), leaving £5,270 of basic-rate room. So £5,270 of the rental profit is taxed at 20% (£1,054) and the remaining £9,730 at 40% (£3,892), giving £4,946 before relief. The Section 24 credit is 20% × £6,000 = £1,200, so income tax on the rentals is £4,946 − £1,200 = £3,746. The cash kept is £18,000 − £3,000 − £6,000 − £3,746 = £5,254, an effective rate of 25% on the £15,000 profit. Layer a bigger salary on top and more of the profit is dragged into the 40% band, which is exactly why the calculator and Excel model ask for your other income rather than just a tax band.</p>

<h2>Worked example: a basic-rate landlord with two properties</h2>

<p>Now a landlord with a £20,000 pension and two modest lets:</p>

<ul>
<li>Property A: rent £9,000, costs £1,800, interest £2,000</li>
<li>Property B: rent £8,000, costs £1,500, interest £1,500</li>
</ul>

<p>Combined taxable profit is (£9,000 − £1,800) + (£8,000 − £1,500) = £7,200 + £6,500 = £13,700. Added to the £20,000 pension, total income is £33,700, comfortably within the basic-rate band, so the profit is taxed at 20% = £2,740. The Section 24 credit is 20% × (£2,000 + £1,500) = £700, capped checks pass. Income tax is £2,740 − £700 = £2,040. For a genuine basic-rate landlord, Section 24 is broadly neutral, the danger is only if the rents tip total income into the 40% band.</p>

<h2>Registering for Self Assessment</h2>

<p>If you have rental profit to declare (and you are not within the £1,000 full-relief allowance), you must tell HMRC. The deadline is <strong>5 October following the end of the tax year</strong> in which the income first arose. So if you started letting in the 2026/27 tax year (6 April 2026 to 5 April 2027), you must register for Self Assessment by <strong>5 October 2027</strong>. You register online at gov.uk and HMRC issues you a Unique Taxpayer Reference (UTR).</p>

<p>Failing to notify HMRC of a new source of taxable income on time is a "failure to notify" and carries a penalty based on the tax you owe and your behaviour. If you have several years of undeclared rental income, the <strong>Let Property Campaign</strong> is HMRC's disclosure route for residential landlords and gives the lowest penalties for coming forward voluntarily before HMRC contacts you. Do not ignore historic letting income, HMRC receives data from letting agents, deposit schemes and the Land Registry, and unprompted disclosure is always cheaper than being found.</p>

<h2>The deadlines that carry penalties</h2>

<p>For the traditional Self Assessment cycle (before you move into Making Tax Digital):</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>5 October</strong> after the tax year ends: register for Self Assessment if you are new to it.</li>
<li><strong>31 October</strong> after the tax year ends: deadline for a <em>paper</em> return.</li>
<li><strong>31 January</strong> after the tax year ends: deadline for the <em>online</em> return AND for paying the tax due. A 2026/27 return and its tax are due by 31 January 2028.</li>
<li><strong>31 January and 31 July:</strong> payments on account, advance instalments toward the next year's bill, each broadly half of the previous year's liability, due if your last bill was over £1,000 and less than 80% was collected at source.</li>
</ul>

<p>Miss the filing deadline and you face an immediate <strong>£100 penalty</strong>, even if no tax is due, with daily penalties of £10 (up to £900) once you are three months late, and further 5% tax-geared penalties at six and twelve months. Late payment attracts its own interest and penalties. The single cheapest thing a landlord can do is file on time.</p>

<h2>Making Tax Digital for Income Tax is coming</h2>

<p>Making Tax Digital for Income Tax (MTD for ITSA) replaces the annual return with quarterly digital updates plus an end-of-year final declaration, using HMRC-recognised software. It is being phased in by income level, tested against your gross self-employment plus gross property income (the two are added together):</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>From 6 April 2026:</strong> qualifying income over £50,000.</li>
<li><strong>From 6 April 2027:</strong> qualifying income over £30,000.</li>
<li><strong>From 6 April 2028:</strong> qualifying income over £20,000.</li>
</ul>

<p>"Qualifying income" is <strong>gross</strong>, before expenses, so a landlord with £52,000 of rents and £40,000 of costs (net profit £12,000) is in scope from April 2026. Joint owners test their own share of the gross, not the property's total. If MTD applies to you, you will keep digital records, file four quarterly updates and a final declaration, and a separate points-based penalty regime applies to late submissions. The watchword is early preparation: clean digital records from the start of the relevant tax year make the transition painless.</p>

<h2>Record-keeping: what to keep and for how long</h2>

<p>You must keep records of all the income and expenses of your rental business, together with the documents that support them. For an individual landlord the statutory minimum is to keep your records for <strong>at least five years after the 31 January submission deadline</strong> for the relevant tax year, so records for 2026/27 (return due 31 January 2028) must be kept until <strong>31 January 2033</strong>. If you let through a limited company, the company's accounting-record obligation is a separate six-year rule. As a practical buffer against late-discovered errors and enquiries, we recommend landlords keep records for <strong>seven years</strong>.</p>

<p>Keep, at minimum: rent statements and bank records of rent received; invoices and receipts for every expense; mortgage statements showing the interest; tenancy agreements and deposit-protection certificates; and your annual rental accounts. Records can be kept digitally, and once you are in MTD they must be. A simple categorised spreadsheet (rent, agent fees, repairs, insurance, council tax, finance costs, other), kept up to date through the year, is worth far more than a shoebox of receipts in January.</p>

<h2>Common mistakes that cost landlords money</h2>

<ul>
<li><strong>Declaring rent net of agent fees.</strong> Always declare the gross rent and deduct the commission separately; netting it off understates your income for the MTD threshold test and looks wrong to HMRC.</li>
<li><strong>Trying to deduct mortgage interest as an expense.</strong> Under Section 24 it is a tax credit, not a deduction. Put it in the finance-costs box, not the expenses box.</li>
<li><strong>Treating an improvement as a repair.</strong> A new extension or a kitchen upgrade is capital. Claiming it against income invites an enquiry, and you lose the CGT relief you should have had.</li>
<li><strong>Forgetting the rental profit pushes you up a band.</strong> Adding gross rents to your salary can tip you into the 40% band or start the £100,000 personal-allowance taper. Model the band, do not assume.</li>
<li><strong>Missing the 5 October registration deadline</strong> on a first let, or ignoring years of historic income rather than using the Let Property Campaign.</li>
<li><strong>Not splitting jointly owned income correctly.</strong> Married couples and civil partners are taxed 50/50 by default regardless of the actual ownership split; a Form 17 election (backed by a declaration of trust) is needed to depart from that, and it must reflect real beneficial ownership.</li>
<li><strong>Throwing away records too early.</strong> Five years from the 31 January deadline, not five years from the tax year.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Frequently asked questions</h2>

<h3>Do I have to declare rental income if I make a loss?</h3>
<p>Yes, if your gross rents exceed £1,000 you should report the rental business even in a loss-making year. Declaring the loss is what lets you carry it forward to set against future rental profits, so it is in your interest to report it.</p>

<h3>Can I deduct my mortgage payments?</h3>
<p>Only the interest element counts, and even then not as a deduction. Under Section 24 the interest gives you a basic-rate tax credit (20%, rising to 22% from 2027/28) rather than reducing your profit. The capital repayment part of a mortgage payment is never deductible.</p>

<h3>What is the difference between the £1,000 property allowance and Rent-a-Room?</h3>
<p>The property allowance (£1,000) applies to ordinary rental income from property you do not live in. Rent-a-Room (£7,500, or £3,750 if shared) applies specifically to letting furnished accommodation in your own main home. They are separate reliefs for different situations.</p>

<h3>I have a salary and one rental. Do I still need a tax return?</h3>
<p>If your rental profit is more than the £1,000 allowance, yes, you need to register for Self Assessment and file a return, even though your salary is taxed through PAYE. The return brings the two together and works out the extra tax on the rentals.</p>

<h3>When does Making Tax Digital affect me?</h3>
<p>From April 2026 if your gross self-employment plus property income exceeds £50,000, April 2027 above £30,000, and April 2028 above £20,000. Remember the test is on gross income, before expenses, and is added across your trading and property income.</p>

<h3>How long do I keep my records?</h3>
<p>At least five years after the 31 January filing deadline for that tax year (so 2026/27 records until 31 January 2033). We recommend seven years as a safe practical buffer. Companies keep accounting records for six years.</p>
