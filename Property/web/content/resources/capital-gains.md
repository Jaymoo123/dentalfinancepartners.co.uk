<p>Capital Gains Tax (CGT) is the tax on the profit you make when you sell, give away or otherwise dispose of a residential property that is not your main home. It catches landlords selling buy-to-lets, people selling a second home or an inherited property, and anyone gifting property to family. The rules look simple on the surface, a rate applied to a gain, but the detail is where money is won and lost: what counts as an allowable cost, whether any Private Residence Relief applies, how a spouse can halve the bill, and the strict 60-day clock for reporting and paying. This guide walks through all of it with worked examples. It is general information, not advice for your specific situation; the final section explains when to get your own numbers run.</p>

<h2>What CGT applies to, in one paragraph</h2>

<p>You pay CGT on the <strong>gain</strong>, not the sale price. The gain is broadly what you sold the property for, less what you paid for it, less the costs of buying and selling and the cost of any capital improvements. A "disposal" is not only a sale: gifting a property, transferring it to someone other than your spouse, or selling at undervalue all count as disposals at <strong>market value</strong>. Your own home is normally exempt under Private Residence Relief, so for most people CGT only bites on a property they did not live in throughout, or only lived in for part of the time.</p>

<h2>The rates and the annual allowance</h2>

<p>For disposals in 2026/27, residential property gains made by individuals are taxed at:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>18%</strong> on the part of the gain that falls within your unused basic-rate income tax band; and</li>
<li><strong>24%</strong> on the part above it.</li>
</ul>

<p>These rates have applied since 30 October 2024 (Autumn Budget 2024). Before that the higher residential rate was 28%, so be wary of older guidance and calculators that still show 28%; that figure is wrong for current disposals.</p>

<p>Every individual has an <strong>annual exempt amount</strong> of <strong>£3,000</strong> for 2026/27. This is the slice of gains you can make each year tax-free. It has fallen sharply in recent years, from £12,300 before April 2023 and £6,000 in 2023/24, so far more disposals now produce a tax bill than used to. The allowance cannot be carried forward: use it or lose it. Trustees generally get half the individual amount (£1,500), and trustees and personal representatives pay 24% throughout with no basic-rate slice.</p>

<p>Because the rate split depends on your income, your <strong>other taxable income for the year matters</strong>. The gain effectively sits on top of your income: the more of your basic-rate band is already used by salary and rental profit, the more of the gain is pushed into the 24% rate.</p>

<h2>Working out the gain: what you can deduct</h2>

<p>The gain is the proceeds less your <strong>allowable costs</strong>. Getting the allowable costs right is where landlords most often overpay, by forgetting deductions they are entitled to. You can deduct:</p>

<ul>
<li>The <strong>original purchase price</strong> (or the probate / market value if you inherited or were gifted the property).</li>
<li><strong>Acquisition costs</strong>: the Stamp Duty Land Tax you paid when you bought, legal and conveyancing fees, and survey fees.</li>
<li><strong>Selling costs</strong>: estate agent fees and legal fees on the sale.</li>
<li><strong>Capital improvements</strong>: money spent enhancing the property beyond its original state, such as an extension, a loft conversion, or adding a bathroom or central heating where there was none. The improvement must still be reflected in the property at the date of sale.</li>
</ul>

<p>You <strong>cannot</strong> deduct against the gain:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Repairs and maintenance</strong>: redecorating, replacing a worn-out boiler or kitchen like-for-like, fixing the roof. These are revenue costs you set against your <em>rental income</em> each year instead, so deducting them again here would be double-counting.</li>
<li><strong>Mortgage interest or capital repayments.</strong> These are not a cost of the asset for CGT.</li>
<li>Costs you have already claimed against income tax.</li>
</ul>

<p>The line between an "improvement" (capital, deductible against the gain) and a "repair" (revenue, deductible against rent) is a frequent grey area. A new kitchen that simply replaces the old one is usually a repair; building a kitchen extension is an improvement. Keep invoices: HMRC can ask for evidence, and good records often recover thousands in deductions.</p>

<h2>Worked example: a straightforward buy-to-let</h2>

<p>A higher-rate landlord sells a rental flat:</p>

<ul>
<li>Sale price: £320,000</li>
<li>Original purchase price: £200,000</li>
<li>Buying and selling costs (SDLT, legal, agent): £9,000</li>
<li>Capital improvements (an extension): £8,000</li>
<li>Other taxable income this year: £50,000</li>
</ul>

<p>Total allowable costs are £200,000 + £9,000 + £8,000 = £217,000. The gain is £320,000 − £217,000 = £103,000. Deduct the £3,000 annual exempt amount, leaving a taxable gain of £100,000. With £50,000 of income, the basic-rate band is fully used, so the whole £100,000 is taxed at 24%. CGT is <strong>£24,000</strong>, an effective rate of about 23.3% of the gain. The landlord must report and pay this within 60 days of completion.</p>

<h2>Worked example: halving the bill with a spouse</h2>

<p>Take the same property and figures, but the landlord transfers a half-share to their spouse before the sale. Transfers between spouses and civil partners are on a <strong>no-gain/no-loss</strong> basis (s.58 TCGA 1992), so the transfer itself triggers no CGT and the spouse simply inherits half the base cost.</p>

<p>The £103,000 gain is now split £51,500 each. Each spouse deducts their own £3,000 annual exemption, leaving £48,500 each. If the first spouse still has £50,000 of income (band used, all at 24%) but the second spouse has only £20,000 of income, the second spouse has roughly £18,270 of unused basic-rate band, so £18,270 of their gain is taxed at 18% and the rest at 24%. The household CGT falls from £24,000 to roughly <strong>£22,584</strong>, a saving of around £1,400 from the extra allowance and band, with no tax cost on the transfer itself. The saving is larger where the gain is smaller relative to the bands, or where the second spouse has little or no income. This is one of the most-used legitimate CGT planning levers for couples.</p>

<p>Two cautions. The transfer must be a <strong>genuine</strong> change of beneficial ownership, completed <strong>before</strong> you exchange contracts, ideally documented with a declaration of trust. And on a jointly held, mortgaged property, transferring a share can itself trigger SDLT if the spouse takes on a share of the mortgage debt above the SDLT threshold.</p>

<h2>Private Residence Relief: the former-home case</h2>

<p>If the property was <strong>ever your only or main home</strong>, Private Residence Relief (PRR, sometimes PPR) exempts the part of the gain relating to the period you lived there. This is the relief that makes most people's homes CGT-free, but it also helps landlords who once lived in a property they later let out.</p>

<p>PRR is given on a <strong>straight-line, time-apportioned</strong> basis: the exempt fraction of the gain is the qualifying period of ownership divided by the total period of ownership. The qualifying period is the time you actually occupied it as your main home, <strong>plus the final 9 months</strong> of ownership, which always count whether or not you lived there at the end (s.223(2) TCGA 1992). The final-period rule means you do not lose relief just because the property was empty or let while you were trying to sell.</p>

<p><strong>Worked example.</strong> You owned a flat for 15 years (180 months). You lived in it as your main home for the first 5 years (60 months), then let it out for 10 years. The gain on sale is £103,000. Qualifying months are 60 (lived in) + 9 (final period) = 69. The PRR fraction is 69 ÷ 180 = 38.3%, so PRR is about £39,483. The chargeable gain falls to roughly £63,517 before the annual exemption. PRR has removed more than a third of the gain because of the years you lived there plus the final 9 months.</p>

<p>There are additional <strong>deemed-occupation</strong> periods that can also count as qualifying (for example certain periods working abroad, or working away from home, or up to 3 years for any reason, provided you occupied the property as your home before and after). These are fact-specific and beyond a calculator; if you ever lived in the property, they are worth checking.</p>

<h2>Lettings Relief: much narrower than it used to be</h2>

<p>Lettings Relief used to give an extra slice of relief, up to £40,000, to landlords who let out a property that had at some point been their main home. <strong>Since 6 April 2020 it is only available where you shared occupation of the property with your tenant</strong>, for example a live-in landlord letting a spare room while living there. For the vast majority of landlords who moved out and then let the whole property to tenants, Lettings Relief no longer applies. Older articles still describe the pre-2020 version; do not rely on them. Because of this restriction, our calculator and model do not assume any Lettings Relief; if you genuinely shared occupation, raise it with your adviser.</p>

<h2>Offsetting losses</h2>

<p>If you sell one property (or any chargeable asset) at a loss, that <strong>capital loss</strong> can be set against capital gains:</p>

<ul>
<li>Losses in the <strong>same tax year</strong> are set against gains in that year before the annual exemption, which can be inefficient if it wastes the £3,000 allowance, but the rules require it.</li>
<li><strong>Unused losses carry forward indefinitely</strong> and are used in later years only to the extent needed to bring gains down to the annual exempt amount, so the allowance is not wasted in carry-forward years.</li>
<li>To use a loss, you generally have to <strong>claim it</strong>, within 4 years of the end of the tax year in which it arose.</li>
</ul>

<p>For a landlord with several properties, timing disposals so a gain and a loss fall in a way that uses allowances well can make a real difference. Losses cannot be transferred between spouses directly, but a no-gain/no-loss transfer of an asset before sale can put a future gain in the hands of the spouse who has the losses.</p>

<h2>The 60-day report-and-pay deadline</h2>

<p>Residential property has its own, much tighter, reporting rule. Where CGT is due, <strong>UK residents must report the disposal and pay the tax within 60 days of completion</strong>, using HMRC's "Capital Gains Tax on UK property" online service. This is <strong>separate from, and earlier than</strong>, your Self Assessment return; you still report the gain again on your tax return, with the 60-day payment treated as on account.</p>

<p>Key points:</p>

<ul>
<li>The 60-day clock runs from the <strong>completion</strong> date, not exchange.</li>
<li>If the gain is fully covered by PRR, losses or the annual exemption so that <strong>no CGT is due</strong>, UK residents do not need to file the 60-day return.</li>
<li><strong>Non-UK residents</strong> must report <em>every</em> UK land disposal within 60 days, even where no tax is due.</li>
<li>Late filing brings a £100 fixed penalty, then daily penalties from day 91, plus further penalties at 6 and 12 months and interest on late-paid tax.</li>
</ul>

<p>The 60-day window is short and the penalties are real. If you are selling, line up the figures and your CGT account <em>before</em> completion, not after.</p>

<h2>Selling an inherited property</h2>

<p>When you inherit a property, your base cost for CGT is its <strong>market value at the date of death</strong> (the probate value), not what the deceased originally paid. Any Inheritance Tax is a separate matter dealt with by the estate. If you then sell the property for more than the probate value, the gain is the increase since death, less selling costs and the annual exemption. This is why getting a sensible probate valuation matters: too low a figure saves a little IHT but can create a larger CGT bill later.</p>

<h2>Incorporation and CGT</h2>

<p>Moving an existing portfolio into a limited company is a disposal at market value and can trigger a large CGT charge. <strong>Section 162 incorporation relief</strong> can defer that gain where a genuine property <em>business</em> (not merely passive investment) is transferred as a going concern wholly or partly in exchange for shares. Since Finance Act 2026 this relief is <strong>no longer automatic for transfers on or after 6 April 2026: it must be claimed</strong>, and HMRC looks for evidence of a real business, usually a portfolio under active management. Incorporation also carries an SDLT cost on the company's deemed purchase. It is a consequential decision that should be modelled property by property; our Section 24 and incorporation tools cover that comparison.</p>

<h2>Frequently asked questions</h2>

<h3>What is the CGT rate on a buy-to-let in 2026/27?</h3>
<p>Residential property gains are taxed at 18% to the extent they fall within your unused basic-rate income tax band, and 24% above it. The old 28% higher residential rate was abolished from 30 October 2024.</p>

<h3>What is the CGT allowance for 2026/27?</h3>
<p>£3,000 per individual. A couple who jointly own a property have £3,000 each, so £6,000 between them. It cannot be carried forward.</p>

<h3>Can I deduct mortgage interest from my capital gain?</h3>
<p>No. Mortgage interest and capital repayments are not allowable costs for CGT. Interest is relieved (in restricted form) against your rental income under the Section 24 rules instead, not against the gain.</p>

<h3>Do I pay CGT if the property was once my home?</h3>
<p>Often less, sometimes none. Private Residence Relief exempts the proportion of the gain relating to the time you lived there as your main home, plus the final 9 months of ownership, on a straight-line basis. The longer you lived there relative to the total ownership, the more relief you get.</p>

<h3>How can a couple reduce the CGT?</h3>
<p>Holding the property jointly means two annual exemptions and two basic-rate bands, which usually lowers the total bill. A no-gain/no-loss transfer of a share to a spouse or civil partner before sale (s.58 TCGA 1992) achieves this without any CGT on the transfer, but it must be a genuine transfer completed before exchange, and SDLT can arise if the spouse takes on mortgage debt.</p>

<h3>When do I have to report and pay?</h3>
<p>Where CGT is due, within 60 days of completion through HMRC's CGT on UK property service, separately from your Self Assessment return. Non-UK residents must report every UK land disposal within 60 days even if no tax is due.</p>

<h3>Can I use a loss on another property?</h3>
<p>Yes. Capital losses are set against gains in the same year, and any unused loss carries forward indefinitely (used only down to the annual exemption in later years). You generally need to claim a loss within 4 years of the end of the tax year it arose.</p>
