<p>Section 24 is the single biggest change to landlord taxation in a generation. It is also one of the most misunderstood. This guide explains exactly how the finance-cost restriction works, who it affects, what it actually costs you at each tax band, the change coming in April 2027, and the legitimate ways landlords reduce its impact, with worked examples throughout. It is general information, not advice for your specific situation: the last section explains when to get your own numbers run.</p>

<h2>What Section 24 is, in one paragraph</h2>

<p>Before Section 24, an individual landlord deducted mortgage interest from rental income as a normal business expense, the same way a company does. Section 24 of the Finance (No. 2) Act 2015 stopped that for residential property. Since 6 April 2020, fully phased in, an individual landlord can no longer deduct mortgage interest or other finance costs from rental profit. Instead, the full rent (less non-finance costs) is taxed at your marginal rate, and you receive a separate <strong>basic-rate tax credit</strong> on the finance costs. For a basic-rate taxpayer the credit roughly cancels out the extra tax. For a higher or additional-rate taxpayer it does not, and that gap is the cost of Section 24.</p>

<h2>Why it exists</h2>

<p>The policy aim, set out by the Treasury when it was announced in the 2015 Summer Budget, was to "level the playing field" between landlords and owner-occupiers (who get no relief on their own mortgage interest) and to reduce what the government saw as a tax advantage fuelling buy-to-let demand. Whether it achieved that is debated; what is not debated is the mechanics, which are now settled law and have survived a judicial review challenge (<em>Cherie Blair and others v HMRC</em>, permission refused 2016).</p>

<h2>Exactly how the restriction and the tax credit work</h2>

<p>The mechanics are precise, and getting them right matters because the credit is capped. For an individual residential landlord:</p>

<ul>
<li>Mortgage interest and other allowable finance costs (arrangement fees, interest on loans to buy or improve the property) are <strong>not deducted</strong> from rental profit for income tax.</li>
<li>Your taxable rental profit is therefore rent minus the <em>non-finance</em> costs only (repairs, letting agent fees, insurance, ground rent, and so on).</li>
<li>That profit is added to your other income and taxed at your marginal rate.</li>
<li>You then get a <strong>finance-cost tax reducer</strong> (a credit) set against your overall income tax bill.</li>
</ul>

<p>The credit rate is <strong>20% for 2026/27</strong> and earlier. It is <strong>not</strong> a deduction from income; it is a reduction in tax. And it is capped at the lowest of three figures:</p>

<ol>
<li>20% of the finance costs for the year;</li>
<li>20% of the residential rental profit (before any finance-cost deduction);</li>
<li>20% of your total income that exceeds your personal allowance (your "adjusted total income").</li>
</ol>

<p>Where the credit is restricted by the cap, the un-credited finance costs <strong>carry forward indefinitely</strong> to future years. This matters for landlords whose rental business runs at a loss or near break-even, and for highly geared portfolios where the interest exceeds the rental profit.</p>

<h2>Who is affected, and who is not</h2>

<p>Section 24 <strong>applies</strong> to:</p>

<ul>
<li>Individual landlords letting residential property, including jointly owned property (each owner is restricted on their share of finance costs).</li>
<li>Partnerships of individuals.</li>
<li>Trusts and the income beneficiaries of interest-in-possession trusts.</li>
</ul>

<p>Section 24 <strong>does not apply</strong> to:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Limited companies.</strong> A company deducts mortgage interest in full before Corporation Tax. This single fact is the reason incorporation is on every geared higher-rate landlord's mind.</li>
<li><strong>Commercial property</strong> let by individuals (the restriction is residential only).</li>
<li>Loans taken out for purposes other than the residential letting business.</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Furnished holiday lets (FHL):</strong> while FHL was a separate tax regime, Section 24 did not apply. The FHL regime was <strong>abolished from 6 April 2025</strong>. Former FHL properties are now taxed as standard residential lets, so Section 24 now applies to them. If you ran an FHL, this is a material change you should not overlook.</p>

<h2>The real-world impact, beyond the headline tax</h2>

<p>The extra income tax is only part of the story. Because Section 24 taxes your gross rents rather than your real profit, it inflates your <em>taxable income</em>, and several things in the tax system key off that figure:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Being pushed into the higher-rate band.</strong> A landlord who was comfortably basic-rate on real profit can be tipped into the 40% band on paper, because the mortgage interest is no longer netted off. Once there, the credit no longer matches your rate.</li>
<li><strong>The £100,000 personal allowance taper.</strong> Above £100,000 of adjusted net income you lose £1 of personal allowance for every £2 of income, fully gone at £125,140. That is an effective marginal rate of around 60% on that band. Section 24 can push you into it. The tax credit does not undo the taper, because the taper bites on income <em>before</em> the credit is applied.</li>
<li><strong>The High Income Child Benefit Charge.</strong> This is now assessed on adjusted net income, with the charge tapering between £60,000 and £80,000 (from 6 April 2024). Inflated rental income can trigger or increase it.</li>
<li><strong>Tax-free childcare and the 30 hours free childcare</strong> have income limits that bite on the same inflated figure.</li>
</ul>

<p>These second-order effects can cost more than the headline Section 24 tax itself, and they are exactly the kind of thing a generic online calculator will miss.</p>

<h2>Worked example: basic-rate landlord</h2>

<p>Take a landlord whose only other income keeps them within the basic-rate band, with:</p>

<ul>
<li>Rental income: £18,000</li>
<li>Mortgage interest: £7,000</li>
<li>Other costs: £3,000</li>
</ul>

<p>Taxable rental profit is £18,000 − £3,000 = £15,000 (interest not deducted). Tax at 20% is £3,000. The finance-cost credit is 20% × £7,000 = £1,400. Tax payable is £3,000 − £1,400 = £1,600. Under the old, fully deductible system the taxable profit would have been £15,000 − £7,000 = £8,000, taxed at 20% = £1,600. <strong>Identical.</strong> For a pure basic-rate taxpayer Section 24 is broadly neutral, provided the rental profit does not itself push them over the higher-rate threshold.</p>

<h2>Worked example: higher-rate landlord</h2>

<p>Now the same property profile, but the landlord is a higher-rate (40%) taxpayer:</p>

<ul>
<li>Rental income: £18,000</li>
<li>Mortgage interest: £7,000</li>
<li>Other costs: £3,000</li>
</ul>

<p>Taxable rental profit is again £15,000. Tax at 40% is £6,000. The credit is still only 20% × £7,000 = £1,400. Tax payable is £6,000 − £1,400 = £4,600. Under the old system the taxable profit would have been £8,000, taxed at 40% = £3,200. Section 24 costs this landlord <strong>£1,400 a year extra</strong> on this one property, which is the 20-percentage-point wedge (40% rate minus 20% credit) applied to the £7,000 of interest. Scale that across a geared portfolio and the numbers become serious.</p>

<h2>The April 2027 change: the credit rises to 22%</h2>

<p>From <strong>6 April 2027</strong>, separate property income tax rates take effect (enacted by Finance Act 2026, Royal Assent 18 March 2026). Property income in <strong>England, Wales and Northern Ireland</strong> will be taxed at <strong>22% basic, 42% higher and 47% additional</strong> (Scotland is carved out and keeps its own rates). Critically, the Section 24 finance-cost reducer is given at the <strong>new property basic rate of 22%</strong>, not frozen at 20%.</p>

<p>What this means in practice:</p>

<ul>
<li>For a <strong>basic-rate</strong> landlord, the 22% credit matches the 22% rate on property income, so <strong>no new wedge opens</strong> from this change.</li>
<li>For a <strong>higher or additional-rate</strong> landlord, the credit improves from 20% to 22% (a 2-percentage-point gain), but the rate on the income also rises 40%→42% / 45%→47%. The finance-cost wedge is therefore 42% − 22% = 20pp, or 47% − 22% = 25pp, the <strong>same wedge</strong> as today. The restriction does not get worse, but it does not go away either.</li>
</ul>

<p>Do not believe anyone who tells you the credit "stays at 20%" in 2027/28, or that a "new basic-rate wedge" opens. Neither is true. The change is real but, for the finance-cost wedge specifically, broadly neutral.</p>

<h2>Mitigation options (balanced, not advice)</h2>

<p>There is no magic bullet, and every option below has trade-offs. The right answer depends on your marginal rate, gearing, plans for the portfolio, and your wider tax position. Here is the honest landscape:</p>

<h3>Incorporation: hold property through a limited company</h3>

<p>A company is outside Section 24 and deducts interest in full before Corporation Tax (19% on profits up to £50,000, 25% from £250,000, with marginal relief in between). For a heavily geared, higher-rate landlord building a portfolio, this is often the most powerful option. But the costs are real:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Capital Gains Tax</strong> on transferring existing property into the company (a disposal at market value). Incorporation relief under s.162 TCGA 1992 can defer this where the activity is a genuine <em>business</em> and all assets are transferred for shares, but since Finance Act 2026 the relief must be <strong>claimed</strong> for transfers on or after 6 April 2026; it is no longer automatic, and HMRC scrutinises whether residential letting amounts to a business (usually needing a portfolio under active management).</li>
<li><strong>Stamp Duty Land Tax</strong> on the company's "purchase", at standard rates plus the 5% additional-dwelling surcharge, on the whole value. This is often the single largest one-off cost and can wipe out years of saving.</li>
<li><strong>Mortgage costs.</strong> Company buy-to-let mortgages typically carry higher rates and fees.</li>
<li><strong>Extraction.</strong> Money in the company is not your money. Taking it out as salary or dividends is taxed again personally. Retaining and reinvesting profit is where the company structure shines; drawing it all out each year erodes the benefit.</li>
<li><strong>Ongoing costs and admin:</strong> annual accounts, Corporation Tax returns, Companies House filing.</li>
</ul>

<p>Incorporation suits portfolio landlords who reinvest, not a single-property landlord who needs the income to live on. Model it property by property before committing.</p>

<h3>Spousal transfers and ownership splits</h3>

<p>Where one spouse is a basic-rate taxpayer and the other higher-rate, shifting beneficial ownership of the property (and the associated income) toward the lower-rate spouse can reduce the household's exposure to the wedge. For married couples and civil partners, transfers are on a no-gain/no-loss basis for CGT (s.58 TCGA 1992), which keeps the CGT cost down, though SDLT can still arise if a mortgage is taken on. A Form 17 election is needed to depart from the default 50/50 income split on jointly held property, and it must reflect actual beneficial ownership. This is a genuine, much-used planning route, but it has to be done properly with a declaration of trust where appropriate.</p>

<h3>Reducing gearing</h3>

<p>Because the wedge applies to finance costs, less debt means less exposure. Some landlords use spare cash to reduce mortgage balances, or restructure borrowing away from the residential portfolio. This is a financing decision, not just a tax one, and tying up liquidity has its own risks.</p>

<h3>Reclassifying or repurposing property</h3>

<p>Commercial property and genuinely commercial activity sit outside Section 24. So, while FHL no longer does, some landlords look at the mix of their portfolio. This is rarely a "do it for the tax" decision and should follow the commercial logic of the property, not the other way around.</p>

<h3>What does not work</h3>

<p>Section 24 is in force and is not being repealed. It applies to higher-rate taxpayers (that is the whole point of it). Routing personal mortgage interest through artificial structures that lack commercial substance invites challenge. Treat anything that sounds too clever with suspicion.</p>

<h2>Frequently asked questions</h2>

<h3>Does Section 24 apply if I am a basic-rate taxpayer?</h3>
<p>The credit broadly cancels the extra tax for a pure basic-rate taxpayer. The danger is that adding your gross rents (rather than your net profit) to your income can tip you into the higher-rate band, at which point the wedge appears. Always check the band you actually land in after the rents are added.</p>

<h3>Can I still deduct repairs, letting fees and insurance?</h3>
<p>Yes. Section 24 only restricts <em>finance</em> costs (mortgage interest and similar). All your other allowable running costs are deducted from rental income as normal.</p>

<h3>Is the mortgage interest relief gone completely?</h3>
<p>No. You still get relief, but as a 20% (rising to 22% from April 2027) tax credit rather than a deduction at your marginal rate. For basic-rate taxpayers that is the same outcome; for higher-rate taxpayers it is worth less than the old deduction.</p>

<h3>Will incorporating save me money?</h3>
<p>It can, especially for geared higher-rate landlords who reinvest profits. But the CGT and SDLT on transferring existing property in, plus higher company mortgage costs and the tax on extracting money later, mean it is not automatically worthwhile. It must be modelled for your specific portfolio.</p>

<h3>What changes in April 2027?</h3>
<p>Property income in England, Wales and NI is taxed at 22/42/47% and the Section 24 credit rises to 22%. For basic-rate landlords this is neutral; for higher and additional-rate landlords the credit improves slightly but the finance-cost wedge stays the same size.</p>
