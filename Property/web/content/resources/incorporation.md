<p>Incorporation is the question on every geared, higher-rate landlord's mind, and it is the one most often got wrong. Done for the right portfolio, moving property into a limited company can save real money every year. Done for the wrong one, the upfront Capital Gains Tax and Stamp Duty wipe out a decade of saving, and you are left with higher mortgage costs and a pile of company admin for no benefit. This guide explains exactly when incorporation is worth it, what it actually costs to get property into a company, the reliefs that can defer that cost, the financing and ATED traps, and the ongoing obligations, with worked examples throughout. It is general information, not advice for your specific situation: the last section explains when to get your own numbers run.</p>

<h2>Why incorporation is even a question</h2>

<p>The driver is almost always Section 24. An individual landlord can no longer deduct mortgage interest from rental profit; instead the full rents (less non-finance costs) are taxed at the marginal rate, with only a 20% basic-rate tax credit (rising to 22% from April 2027) on the finance costs. For a higher-rate (40%) or additional-rate (45%) landlord, that credit is well below their tax rate, so the mortgage interest is effectively taxed. A heavily geared higher-rate landlord can end up paying tax on money the bank takes as interest.</p>

<p>A limited company is <strong>outside Section 24</strong>. It deducts mortgage interest in full before Corporation Tax, exactly as any other business deducts its finance costs. Corporation Tax is 19% on profits up to £50,000, 25% on profits of £250,000 or more, with marginal relief in between. For a geared higher-rate landlord building a portfolio, that difference, full interest relief plus a lower headline rate, is the prize. The rest of this guide is about whether the prize is worth the price of entry.</p>

<h2>The two halves of the decision: upfront cost vs annual saving</h2>

<p>Every incorporation decision comes down to two numbers and the relationship between them:</p>

<ul>
<li>The <strong>upfront cost</strong> of moving your existing property into the company: Capital Gains Tax on the gain, plus Stamp Duty Land Tax on the company's purchase. This is a one-off.</li>
<li>The <strong>annual saving</strong> the company structure gives going forward: the difference between the income tax you pay personally under Section 24 and the Corporation Tax (plus any dividend tax on extracting profit) the company pays.</li>
</ul>

<p>Divide the upfront cost by the annual saving and you get the <strong>break-even point</strong>: the number of years of saving it takes to recover the cost of getting in. If you intend to hold the portfolio well beyond break-even and reinvest the profits, incorporation can be compelling. If break-even is twenty years away, or you need to draw the income out to live on, it usually is not. The calculator on this page and the accompanying Excel model both compute exactly these three figures.</p>

<h2>The upfront cost in detail</h2>

<h3>Capital Gains Tax on the transfer</h3>

<p>Transferring a property into your own company is a <strong>disposal at market value</strong>. You and the company are connected persons (TCGA 1992 s.17 and s.18), so HMRC ignores the fact that no real money changes hands and treats the disposal as if it happened at full market value. If the property has risen in value since you bought it, that gain is chargeable to Capital Gains Tax in the year of transfer, even though you have not received any cash.</p>

<p>Residential property CGT rates (from 30 October 2024) are <strong>18%</strong> on the gain that falls within your unused basic-rate band and <strong>24%</strong> above it, after deducting the <strong>£3,000 annual exempt amount</strong>. Because the gain stacks on top of your other income, a sizeable gain on a property held for many years will mostly be taxed at 24%. This is frequently the figure that surprises landlords: a property bought for £200,000 and now worth £350,000 carries a £150,000 gain, and the CGT on that alone can run to tens of thousands of pounds, payable (and reportable) within <strong>60 days</strong> of completion.</p>

<h3>Stamp Duty Land Tax on the company's purchase</h3>

<p>When the company acquires the property, it pays SDLT just as any buyer would, and because the company already counts as owning (or is buying an additional) dwelling, it pays the <strong>5% additional-dwelling surcharge on the whole price</strong> on top of the standard bands. Standard SDLT (England and Northern Ireland) is 0% to £125,000, 2% to £250,000, 5% to £925,000, 10% to £1.5m and 12% above; the 5% surcharge is added to all of it. A genuine property-letting company is relieved from the punitive 15% Schedule 4A rate, so it pays standard rates plus the surcharge, but that surcharge alone, 5% of the entire value, is often the single largest cost of incorporating and the one that most often makes the numbers not work.</p>

<p><strong>This is not negotiable through Multiple Dwellings Relief.</strong> MDR was abolished for transactions completing on or after 1 June 2024. Anyone telling you MDR will cut the SDLT on a portfolio incorporation is working from old rules. There are narrow partnership routes (below) that can reduce the chargeable consideration, but they require a genuine, pre-existing partnership and are heavily scrutinised.</p>

<h2>s.162 incorporation relief: deferring the CGT</h2>

<p>The CGT on transfer is the cost most often deferred, through <strong>incorporation relief under s.162 TCGA 1992</strong>. Where it applies, the gain is not taxed now; instead it is rolled into the base cost of the shares you receive in the company, and only crystallises if and when you sell those shares. For a landlord who intends to hold long-term, this can effectively park the CGT indefinitely.</p>

<p>But s.162 is not a free pass, and the conditions are strict:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>It must be a genuine business, not mere investment.</strong> This is the crux. Passively holding one or two let properties is investment, and does not qualify. HMRC accepts a portfolio under <em>active management</em> as a business; the leading case, <em>Ramsay v HMRC [2013] UKUT 226</em>, accepted around 20 hours a week of genuine management activity. The more properties, the more hands-on the management, the stronger the case. A husband-and-wife couple with two buy-to-lets and a letting agent doing the work will struggle.</li>
<li><strong>All of the business's assets (other than cash) must be transferred</strong> to the company, wholly or partly in exchange for shares. You cannot cherry-pick.</li>
<li><strong>The relief must now be claimed.</strong> Since Finance Act 2026, for transfers on or after 6 April 2026, s.162 relief is <strong>no longer automatic</strong>: you must claim it, and the previous s.162A election to disapply the relief has been repealed. Do not rely on any guidance that says incorporation relief "happens automatically" or that "you don't need to claim it", that is no longer correct.</li>
</ul>

<p>Crucially, <strong>s.162 relief defers CGT only. It does nothing for SDLT.</strong> The Stamp Duty surcharge is still payable in full unless a separate partnership route applies. So even with textbook s.162 relief, a £400,000 property still carries roughly £30,000 of SDLT to get it into the company.</p>

<h3>The partnership route for SDLT</h3>

<p>Where a portfolio is genuinely held in a pre-existing <strong>partnership</strong>, FA 2003 Schedule 15 para 10 can reduce the chargeable consideration on transfer into a connected company, potentially to nil, through the "sum of the lower proportions" calculation. This is the mechanism some advisers use to incorporate without an SDLT charge. It is real, but it is not a quick fix: HMRC's standard attack is that the "partnership" is a paper contrivance entered into just before incorporation, and it will invoke the s.75A general anti-avoidance rule to ignore it. The working safe harbour is at least two years of genuine partnership operation with HMRC-filed SA800 partnership returns, joint borrowing, and real partnership accounting before the transfer. It is not available to a typical joint-ownership husband-and-wife portfolio that has never operated as a partnership.</p>

<h2>The annual saving in detail</h2>

<p>Going forward, the company position is better on the lettings themselves but worse when you take the money out. The honest comparison has three layers:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Personally:</strong> income tax under Section 24 on the rents, less the 20%/22% finance-cost credit, at your marginal rate.</li>
<li><strong>In the company, layer one:</strong> Corporation Tax on the rental profit (interest deducted in full). For a typical small portfolio this is 19%.</li>
<li><strong>In the company, layer two:</strong> the tax on getting the money out. The company's money is not your money. Drawing it as dividends is taxed again in your hands at 8.75% (basic), 33.75% (higher) or 39.35% (additional), after the £500 dividend allowance.</li>
</ul>

<p>This second layer is where many "incorporate to save tax" pitches quietly fall apart. If you need to extract all the profit each year to live on, the combined Corporation Tax plus dividend tax can be similar to, or even more than, the personal Section 24 position. The company structure shines when you <strong>retain and reinvest</strong> profit, growing the portfolio inside the company where only Corporation Tax has been paid, and defer extraction (or extract efficiently later through salary, pension contributions, or in years when you are a basic-rate taxpayer). The calculator lets you toggle between "draw it all out" and "retain and reinvest" precisely because the answer is completely different in each case.</p>

<h2>Worked example: when it works</h2>

<p>Take a higher-rate landlord with a single, well-geared property:</p>

<ul>
<li>Current value £300,000; bought for £200,000 (a £100,000 gain).</li>
<li>Rent £24,000; mortgage interest £9,000; other costs £3,000.</li>
<li>Higher-rate (40%) taxpayer, planning to reinvest profits, not draw them out.</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Upfront cost.</strong> CGT on the £100,000 gain, after the £3,000 exemption and mostly at 24%, is roughly £21,000. SDLT at standard rates plus the 5% surcharge on £300,000 is £20,000. Total upfront cost around <strong>£41,000</strong>.</p>

<p><strong>Annual saving.</strong> Personally, under Section 24, the income tax is about £6,600 a year. In the company, the rental profit of £12,000 attracts Corporation Tax of about £2,280, and because the profit is retained there is no dividend tax. Annual saving around <strong>£4,300</strong>.</p>

<p><strong>Break-even.</strong> £41,000 ÷ £4,300 is roughly <strong>9.5 years</strong>. For a landlord intending to hold and reinvest for the long term, this is a credible case, and the saving compounds as the portfolio grows inside the company. For a landlord planning to sell in five years, it is not.</p>

<h2>Worked example: when it does not work</h2>

<p>Now the same property, but the landlord needs the income and is a basic-rate taxpayer on real profit:</p>

<ul>
<li>Same £300,000 value and £100,000 gain.</li>
<li>Basic-rate taxpayer; intends to draw all profit out as dividends each year.</li>
</ul>

<p>The Section 24 wedge for a basic-rate taxpayer is broadly neutral, so there is little income tax to save in the first place. Layer the £41,000 upfront cost on top, add Corporation Tax <em>and</em> dividend tax on full extraction, and higher company mortgage rates, and the annual saving can be close to zero or negative. Break-even is "never". Incorporating here would cost £41,000 to make the landlord no better off, or worse. This is the single most common mistake: incorporating because it is fashionable, not because the figures support it.</p>

<h2>Mortgage and refinance considerations</h2>

<p>The financing side is frequently underweighted, and it can swing the whole decision:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Company buy-to-let mortgages usually cost more.</strong> Rates are typically higher than personal buy-to-let, and there are often higher arrangement fees. A higher interest rate inside the company eats into the very saving you incorporated to capture.</li>
<li><strong>You cannot just move the mortgage across.</strong> The existing personal mortgage is redeemed and the company takes out a new one. That means a fresh application, valuation, legal work, possible early-repayment charges on the old loan, and broker fees, all real cash costs that belong in the upfront total.</li>
<li><strong>Personal guarantees.</strong> Lenders almost always require directors to give personal guarantees, so the "limited liability" of the company is partly illusory for the borrowing.</li>
<li><strong>Director's loan account.</strong> Where the property is transferred for a mix of shares and a loan left outstanding to you, that director's loan can be drawn down tax-free later, a genuine planning advantage, but it interacts with s.162 (which requires consideration wholly or partly in shares) and needs careful structuring.</li>
</ul>

<h2>ATED: the charge that catches higher-value properties</h2>

<p>The <strong>Annual Tax on Enveloped Dwellings (ATED)</strong> applies to companies (and other "non-natural persons") holding a single UK residential dwelling worth more than <strong>£500,000</strong>. It is an annual charge, banded by value, starting at several thousand pounds a year for a £500k-£1m property and rising steeply above that.</p>

<p>The good news for most landlords: <strong>a property let to an unconnected tenant on commercial terms qualifies for relief</strong>, so the actual ATED payable is usually nil. The trap is that the relief is <strong>not automatic</strong>: you must file an ATED return claiming it, by <strong>30 April</strong> each year, even though no tax is due. Miss the return and there are escalating late-filing penalties (£100 / £200 / £300 plus tax-geared penalties) for a return that should have shown nothing to pay. If you incorporate a property worth over £500,000, the annual ATED return becomes a permanent fixture of your compliance, factor in your accountant's fee for it.</p>

<h2>Ongoing company admin and costs</h2>

<p>A company is a separate legal person with its own obligations, and they do not stop:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Annual accounts</strong> prepared and filed at Companies House.</li>
<li><strong>A Corporation Tax return (CT600)</strong> filed with HMRC each year, with the tax paid nine months and one day after the year end.</li>
<li><strong>A confirmation statement</strong> filed annually at Companies House.</li>
<li><strong>Director and PSC identity verification</strong> under the Economic Crime and Corporate Transparency Act 2023, now being rolled out.</li>
<li><strong>The ATED return</strong> where any property is worth over £500,000 (above).</li>
<li><strong>Accountancy fees</strong> for all of the above, typically more than a personal Self Assessment return.</li>
</ul>

<p>None of this is onerous for a portfolio of any size, but it is a real annual cost and a real time commitment that a single-property landlord may not want. It also means the company structure rarely makes sense below a certain scale: the admin overhead is broadly fixed, so it is easier to justify across a growing portfolio than a single flat.</p>

<h2>The trade-offs, honestly</h2>

<p>Incorporation is a long-term, hard-to-reverse decision. Weigh it like one:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>It suits</strong> portfolio landlords who are higher or additional-rate, geared, building rather than winding down, and willing to retain and reinvest profit inside the company.</li>
<li><strong>It rarely suits</strong> basic-rate landlords, single-property landlords, those who need the rental income to live on, those planning to sell in the medium term, or anyone whose gain (and therefore CGT) and property value (and therefore SDLT) make break-even a decade or more away without s.162 relief and a partnership SDLT route.</li>
<li><strong>The exit matters too.</strong> Getting property out of a company later, or selling it, can trigger Corporation Tax on the company's gain and then further personal tax on extracting the proceeds, a potential double layer. Companies do not get the personal CGT annual exemption or the 18%/24% residential rates. Plan the exit before you plan the entry.</li>
<li><strong>Inheritance and succession.</strong> Shares in a property company can be easier to pass on gradually than the properties themselves, and a family investment company can be a genuine succession tool, but standard buy-to-let is investment, not trading, so it does not qualify for Business Property Relief (<em>Pawson v HMRC</em>). Do not assume the company shelters the value from IHT.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Frequently asked questions</h2>

<h3>Will I pay Capital Gains Tax just to move my own property into my own company?</h3>
<p>Yes, unless a relief applies. The transfer is a disposal at market value between connected persons, so CGT is charged on the gain even though no cash changes hands. s.162 incorporation relief can defer it where your lettings are a genuine business and all the assets transfer for shares, but it must now be claimed (for transfers on or after 6 April 2026) and HMRC scrutinises whether you really run a business.</p>

<h3>Does incorporation relief also remove the Stamp Duty?</h3>
<p>No. s.162 relief defers CGT only. SDLT, including the 5% additional-dwelling surcharge on the whole value, is still payable unless a separate partnership route (FA 2003 Sch 15) applies, and that requires a genuine, pre-existing, properly-run partnership, not one set up just before incorporating.</p>

<h3>Can I use Multiple Dwellings Relief to cut the SDLT on a portfolio transfer?</h3>
<p>No. MDR was abolished for transactions completing on or after 1 June 2024. Any advice relying on MDR for a portfolio incorporation is out of date.</p>

<h3>Is 19% Corporation Tax always better than my income tax rate?</h3>
<p>On the lettings themselves, usually yes for a higher-rate landlord, because the company also deducts mortgage interest in full. But that is only the first layer. Taking the money out as dividends is taxed again personally, so the total tax on extracted profit can be similar to or more than the personal position. The company structure works best when you retain and reinvest.</p>

<h3>What about ATED, do I have to pay it?</h3>
<p>Only if a property is worth over £500,000 and no relief applies. A property let commercially to an unconnected tenant is relieved, so the charge is usually nil, but you must still file an ATED return each year by 30 April to claim the relief, or face penalties.</p>

<h3>How many properties do I need before incorporating makes sense?</h3>
<p>There is no statutory number. The question is whether the annual saving justifies the upfront CGT and SDLT and the ongoing admin, and whether your lettings amount to a genuine business for s.162. In practice it is a portfolio decision, not a single-property one; the more geared, higher-rate and growth-focused the portfolio, the stronger the case.</p>
