<p>If you're a non-resident landlord selling UK property, you'll face different capital gains tax rules compared to UK residents. <strong>Non resident CGT UK property</strong> rules include specific tax rates, mandatory 60-day reporting, and potential complications around withholding tax that can catch overseas investors off guard.</p>

<p>The key difference is timing and compliance. While UK residents can wait until their annual tax return to report capital gains, non-residents must notify HMRC within 60 days of completion and may need to pay tax on account immediately.</p>

<h2>Who Counts as Non-Resident for UK CGT?</h2>

<p>HMRC's residence test determines your status for the tax year of disposal. You're typically non-resident if you spend fewer than 16 days in the UK during that tax year, or fewer than 46 days if you haven't been UK resident in any of the previous three tax years.</p>

<p>The test can be complex if you're splitting time between countries. Factors include:</p>

<ul>
<li>Days spent in the UK (the primary test)</li>
<li>UK ties (family, accommodation, work, country ties)</li>
<li>Previous residence history</li>
<li>The specific tax year of the property sale</li>
</ul>

<p>For example, a landlord who moved to Spain in 2023 but returns to the UK for 30 days each year would typically be non-resident. However, someone spending 50 days annually in the UK with strong UK ties might still be considered resident.</p>

<h2>CGT Rates for Non-Resident Property Sales</h2>

<p>Non-residents pay the same <strong>overseas landlord CGT</strong> rates as UK residents on property disposals:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>18% basic rate:</strong> If your total taxable income plus gains don't exceed the higher rate threshold (£50,270 for 2026/27)</li>
<li><strong>24% higher rate:</strong> On gains above the higher rate threshold</li>
</ul>

<p>The annual exempt amount for 2026/27 is £3,000 for individuals. However, unlike UK residents, non-residents cannot use losses from other asset disposals to offset property gains.</p>

<p>Consider a non-resident who bought a Manchester buy-to-let for £200,000 in 2018 and sells for £280,000 in 2026. After deducting purchase costs, improvement costs, and selling expenses, the taxable gain is £70,000. If they have no other UK income, the first £50,270 would be taxed at 18% and the remainder at 24%.</p>

<h2>The 60-Day Reporting Rule</h2>

<p>Non-residents must report UK property disposals to HMRC within 60 days of completion. This isn't just a notification—you must also calculate and pay any CGT due.</p>

<p>The process involves:</p>

<ul>
<li>Completing a non-resident CGT return online</li>
<li>Calculating the exact gain and tax due</li>
<li>Paying the CGT by the 60-day deadline</li>
<li>Including the disposal on your annual Self Assessment (if required to file one)</li>
</ul>

<p>Failure to meet the 60-day deadline triggers automatic penalties, starting at £100 and escalating based on how late the return is filed. HMRC doesn't typically accept "I didn't know about the rule" as a reasonable excuse.</p>

<h2>Calculating Non-Resident Capital Gains</h2>

<p>The calculation follows standard CGT principles but with some non-resident specific considerations:</p>

<h3>Allowable Costs</h3>
<p>You can deduct:</p>
<ul>
<li>Original purchase price and associated costs (stamp duty, legal fees, survey costs)</li>
<li>Capital improvements (not repairs and maintenance)</li>
<li>Sale costs (estate agent fees, legal fees, removal costs)</li>
<li>Valuation costs for establishing the gain</li>
</ul>

<h3>Rebasing Relief</h3>
<p>Non-residents who owned UK property on 5 April 2015 can elect to use the April 2015 market value as their base cost, potentially reducing the taxable gain significantly.</p>

<p>For example, a property bought in 2010 for £150,000 but worth £220,000 in April 2015, and sold in 2026 for £300,000, could use rebasing to reduce the taxable gain from £150,000 to £80,000.</p>

<h2>Non-Resident Company Disposals</h2>

<p>UK companies owned by non-residents face different rules. The company pays corporation tax on property disposals at 19% (for gains up to £250,000) or 25% (above £250,000), regardless of the shareholders' residence.</p>

<p>However, <strong>non resident capital gains tax</strong> complications arise if the company is deemed to be controlled by non-residents and holds UK residential property worth over £500,000. Such companies may need to pay the Annual Tax on Enveloped Dwellings (ATED) and face higher CGT rates.</p>

<h2>Professional Valuations and Record Keeping</h2>

<p>Non-residents often need professional valuations, particularly for rebasing elections or where purchase records are incomplete. HMRC may challenge self-valuations more rigorously for non-residents.</p>

<p>Essential records include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Purchase documentation (contracts, completion statements)</li>
<li>Improvement receipts and invoices</li>
<li>Sale documentation</li>
<li>Professional valuations (especially for April 2015 rebasing)</li>
<li>Currency conversion records if transactions were in foreign currency</li>
</ul>

<p>Currency fluctuations can significantly affect gains for non-residents. The gain must be calculated in Sterling, but if you funded the purchase in foreign currency, exchange rate movements become part of the calculation.</p>

<h2>Withholding Tax and Conveyancer Obligations</h2>

<p>When a non-resident sells UK property, the conveyancer or solicitor may need to withhold CGT from the sale proceeds and pay it directly to HMRC. This happens unless the seller obtains clearance or provides sufficient security.</p>

<p>The withholding rate is typically the higher rate of CGT (24% for residential property). If the actual tax due is lower, you can reclaim the excess through the normal tax return process.</p>

<p>To avoid withholding, you can apply for a certificate of non-liability if no tax is due, or provide adequate security for the tax liability.</p>

<h2>Double Taxation Relief</h2>

<p>Non-residents may face CGT in both the UK and their country of residence. The UK has double taxation agreements with many countries that can provide relief.</p>

<p>Typically, the UK has the primary right to tax gains on UK property, and your home country should provide credit for UK tax paid. However, the specific relief depends on your country's domestic rules and the relevant tax treaty.</p>

<p>For example, a US resident selling UK property would pay UK CGT first, then potentially face US federal and state capital gains tax on the same gain, but with credit for the UK tax paid.</p>

<h2>Joint Ownership Complications</h2>

<p>When property is jointly owned between residents and non-residents, each owner's disposal is treated according to their individual residence status. This can create timing mismatches and compliance complications.</p>

<p>Consider a property owned jointly by a UK resident and their non-resident spouse. The non-resident must file within 60 days, while the resident can wait until their annual tax return. Both need separate calculations for their share of the gain.</p>

<h2>Common Pitfalls for Non-Resident Sellers</h2>

<p>The most frequent mistakes include:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Missing the 60-day deadline:</strong> Automatic penalties apply with no discretion</li>
<li><strong>Underestimating the gain:</strong> Forgetting to include currency gains or using wrong base costs</li>
<li><strong>Not claiming rebasing relief:</strong> Missing out on significant tax savings for pre-2015 purchases</li>
<li><strong>Poor record keeping:</strong> Inability to prove allowable costs increases the tax bill</li>
<li><strong>Assuming no tax is due:</strong> Even small gains can trigger reporting requirements</li>
</ul>

<h2>Planning Opportunities</h2>

<p>Non-residents have some planning options, though many are time-sensitive:</p>

<h3>Timing of Disposal</h3>
<p>Consider your residence status in the year of sale. Becoming UK resident before disposal could provide access to annual exempt amount usage against other gains and potentially better timing for payment.</p>

<h3>Spousal Transfers</h3>
<p>Transfers between spouses who are both non-resident are generally tax-neutral and can help optimise the use of annual exempt amounts.</p>

<h3>Company Structures</h3>
<p>For high-value properties, <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/buy-to-let-limited-company-complete-guide-uk">holding property through UK companies</a> might provide different tax outcomes, though this requires careful analysis of the overall tax position.</p>

<h2>When to Seek Professional Help</h2>

<p>Non-resident CGT involves multiple layers of complexity that typically require specialist advice. Consider professional help if:</p>

<ul>
<li>Your residence status is unclear or changes during the tax year</li>
<li>The property was owned before April 2015 (rebasing elections)</li>
<li>You face potential double taxation issues</li>
<li>The property is held through complex ownership structures</li>
<li>Significant currency movements affect your gain calculation</li>
</ul>

<p>A <a href="/blog/property-accountant-services/what-does-a-property-accountant-do">specialist property accountant</a> can help navigate the compliance requirements and identify opportunities to minimise your tax liability legally.</p>

<h2>Looking Ahead: Potential Changes</h2>

<p>The non-resident CGT regime has been relatively stable since its introduction in 2015, but ongoing political discussions about property taxation could affect future rules. The current government has indicated interest in reviewing various aspects of property taxation, though no specific proposals have been announced for non-resident CGT.</p>

<p>What remains clear is that compliance requirements are strictly enforced, and the penalties for late filing continue to apply automatically. Non-residents selling UK property should prioritise understanding these obligations well before any planned disposal.</p>
