<p>One of the biggest concerns for landlords considering <a href="/incorporation">incorporation</a> is whether they'll face a double stamp duty charge. The short answer is: it depends on your circumstances, but there are ways to minimise or avoid additional SDLT <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/incorporation-cost-calculator-cgt-sdlt-implications">costs</a>.</p>

<h2>When SDLT on Incorporation Applies</h2>

<p>SDLT on incorporation becomes payable when you <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/how-to-transfer-property-into-limited-company-uk">transfer properties</a> from your personal name to a company structure. HMRC treats this as a disposal and acquisition, potentially triggering stamp duty based on the property's current market value — not what you originally paid.</p>

<p>For example, if you bought a BTL property for £200,000 in 2020 and it's now worth £280,000, the SDLT calculation would be based on the £280,000 current value. At current rates, this could mean £8,400 in stamp duty (3% on the first £250,000, then 5% on the remaining £30,000).</p>

<p>This feels like paying twice because you already paid stamp duty when you first purchased the property. However, there are legitimate ways to reduce or eliminate this charge.</p>

<h2>SDLT Relief Options for Property Incorporation</h2>

<h3>Reconstruction Relief</h3>

<p>Reconstruction relief can eliminate SDLT on incorporation entirely if you meet specific conditions. The key requirements are:</p>

<ul>
<li>You must hold at least 75% of the shares in the new company</li>
<li>The transfer must be for genuine commercial reasons</li>
<li>Tax avoidance cannot be the main purpose</li>
<li>You must make a formal claim within two years</li>
</ul>

<p>Most landlords with straightforward property portfolios can qualify for this relief, particularly when incorporating to manage Section 24 mortgage interest restrictions or prepare for future growth.</p>

<h3>Multiple Dwellings Relief (MDR)</h3>

<p>If you're transferring multiple properties simultaneously, multiple dwellings relief can significantly reduce the SDLT charge. This relief calculates stamp duty on the average property value rather than applying higher rates to the total consideration.</p>

<p>For instance, transferring four properties worth £200,000 each (£800,000 total) could attract SDLT of £39,000 without MDR, but only £24,000 with the relief applied — a saving of £15,000.</p>

<h2>Planning Your Incorporation Timing</h2>

<p>The timing of your incorporation can impact SDLT costs significantly. Consider these factors:</p>

<p><strong>Market conditions:</strong> If property values have fallen since purchase, incorporation might actually reduce your overall stamp duty burden compared to selling and repurchasing.</p>

<p><strong>Portfolio expansion:</strong> If you're planning to purchase additional properties anyway, incorporating before new acquisitions means paying the higher company SDLT rates (minimum 3%) only once.</p>

<p><strong>Mortgage arrangements:</strong> Some lenders require property transfers to be completed within specific timeframes to maintain existing mortgage terms, which can affect your SDLT planning window.</p>

<h2>SDLT Rates for Company Purchases</h2>

<p>Once incorporated, your company will pay higher SDLT rates on future acquisitions:</p>

<ul>
<li>3% on the first £250,000</li>
<li>8% on £250,001 to £925,000</li>
<li>13% on £925,001 to £1.5 million</li>
<li>15% above £1.5 million</li>
</ul>

<p>This means a company purchasing a £300,000 BTL property pays £11,500 in SDLT, compared to £6,500 for an individual (assuming they already own property and pay the additional 3% surcharge).</p>

<h2>Practical Steps to Minimise SDLT</h2>

<p>To minimise SDLT on incorporation, consider these strategies:</p>

<p><strong>Professional valuation:</strong> Obtain an independent RICS valuation to ensure you're not overpaying SDLT based on an inflated property value.</p>

<p><strong>Debt assignment:</strong> If properties have mortgages, consider whether assigning the debt to the company (with lender consent) affects the SDLT calculation.</p>

<p><strong>Phased incorporation:</strong> For large portfolios, transferring properties in phases across different tax years might help manage cash flow and utilise annual reliefs more effectively.</p>

<p><strong>Mixed-use properties:</strong> If any properties have commercial elements, ensure these are correctly classified as mixed-use to benefit from lower SDLT rates.</p>

<h2>Documentation and Compliance</h2>

<p>Proper documentation is crucial for SDLT relief claims. You'll need:</p>

<ul>
<li>Independent property valuations</li>
<li>Evidence of commercial reasons for incorporation</li>
<li>Company formation documents</li>
<li>Transfer documentation showing consideration paid</li>
<li>SDLT return submitted within 14 days of completion</li>
</ul>

<p>The relief claim itself must be made within two years of the transfer date, but the SDLT return and any payment must be submitted within the standard 14-day deadline.</p>

<h2>When Professional Advice Is Essential</h2>

<p>SDLT on incorporation involves complex interactions between property law, company law, and tax legislation. The potential savings from proper planning often far exceed professional fees, particularly for substantial portfolios.</p>

<p>Consider seeking specialist advice if:</p>

<ul>
<li>Your portfolio is worth more than £500,000</li>
<li>You have unusual property types (commercial, mixed-use, overseas elements)</li>
<li>You're planning complex restructuring involving multiple entities</li>
<li>You're unsure whether reconstruction relief applies to your situation</li>
</ul>

<p>The interaction between SDLT relief and other taxes (particularly capital gains tax and corporation tax) requires careful consideration to ensure your incorporation delivers the intended benefits.</p>

<p>For many landlords, the SDLT cost of incorporation is a one-off expense that unlocks significant long-term tax advantages. With proper planning and the right relief claims, you can often avoid paying stamp duty twice while positioning your property business for future growth and tax efficiency.</p>
