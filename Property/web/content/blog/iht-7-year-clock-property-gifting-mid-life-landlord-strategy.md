  - question: "Why is mid-life (forties to mid-fifties) the planning window for direct property gifts?"
    answer: "Two reasons converge in that window. First, the seven-year clock works against age: by 70, the actuarial probability of clearing seven years drops sharply, and by 75 the dry-CGT cost paid today plus the survival-weighted IHT saving stops being worth the friction. Second, mid-life landlords typically have enough other income that the CGT bill on the gift can be absorbed without forced disinvestment, and enough discretionary capital that the property being gifted is not their planned retirement income. Pre-mid-life (under 40) the planning is often premature because the donor's own circumstances change too much over the 30-year window; post-mid-life (over 60) the survival arithmetic moves the right route toward Family Investment Company structures, life cover in trust, or deferring to a deed of variation after first death. The narrow corridor from roughly age 45 to 58 is where the direct-gift route, with its CGT cost today and its full-survival upside, is most likely to pay."
  - question: "What CGT do I pay on gifting a BTL to my children?"
    answer: "On a gift between connected persons (s.286 TCGA 1992), the disposal is deemed to take place at open-market value at the date of transfer regardless of any cash consideration. Section 17 TCGA 1992 substitutes market value for the consideration shown in the deed of gift. The donor's chargeable gain is the market value at gift date less the donor's base cost (purchase price plus enhancement expenditure plus incidental costs of acquisition and disposal). The gain is taxed at 18% in the donor's basic-rate band and 24% in the higher and additional-rate bands following the rate reform that took effect from 30 October 2024 for residential property. The CGT is due via the 60-day residential-property return to HMRC, with payment within the same 60 days. The donee's base cost is reset to the gift-date market value, so any subsequent gain accrues from that level on the donee's eventual sale."
  - question: "Can I use section 165 holdover relief to defer the CGT on a BTL gift?"
    answer: "No, not for a pure buy-to-let. Section 165 TCGA 1992 allows the donor and donee to jointly elect to roll the gain into the donee's base cost where the asset gifted is a business asset (broadly, an interest in a trading business, shares in a personal trading company, or agricultural property qualifying for APR). A BTL portfolio is on the investment side of the same line that closes BPR for buy-to-let (Pawson v HMRC, see our BPR pillar). Investment land is not a section 165 business asset, so the holdover claim is unavailable and the gain crystallises on the gift. Furnished holiday lets historically qualified for s.165 but lost that status with the FHL regime's abolition from 6 April 2025; gifts of former-FHL property made on or after that date are CGT-immediate at market value."
  - question: "What about section 260 holdover for a gift into trust?"
    answer: "Section 260 TCGA 1992 is the holdover relief for chargeable lifetime transfers, available when a gift triggers an immediate IHT charge. A gift of a BTL into a discretionary trust is a CLT for IHT (20% on the excess over the donor's available nil-rate band), and that IHT trigger unlocks the s.260 holdover for CGT. The donor and trustees jointly elect to roll the CGT gain into the trust's base cost; no immediate CGT is payable by the donor. Two important conditions: the trust must not be settlor-interested (sections 169B to 169G TCGA 1992 exclude trusts where the settlor or settlor's spouse can benefit), and the s.260 holdover is on top of the lifetime IHT cost, not instead of it. The trade-off becomes a 20% IHT now versus the CGT bill the donor would otherwise have paid (deferred into the trust). For a low-base-cost BTL with a large latent gain, the s.260 route can be cheaper than an outright PET; for a high-base-cost BTL with a modest gain, the outright PET to an individual is usually cheaper because the £325,000 nil-rate band shelters most of the early-CLT IHT."
  - question: "How do I calculate the CGT-now vs IHT-saved trade-off?"
    answer: "Five inputs. One: the donor's CGT cost today (market value minus base cost, taxed at 18% or 24% on residential property net of any unused annual exempt amount). Two: the donee's onward base cost going forward (gift-date market value), which sets the donee's future CGT bill on any onward sale. Three: the property's expected death-date value (current value compounded at the donor's view of property growth over their expected remaining life). Four: the IHT saving from a successful PET (40% of death-date value less any unused nil-rate band and RNRB available). Five: the donor's survival probability over the seven-year window (ONS life tables by age and sex are the starting point, adjusted for individual health). The net present value of the trade-off is the survival-weighted IHT saving minus the CGT cost today, discounted to present value. For a fit 52-year-old donor with normal life expectancy, the survival probability over seven years is in the high nineties as a percentage; for an 80-year-old donor, it is roughly half that. The arithmetic flips the decision at the boundary."
  - question: "What if the BTL has a mortgage on it at the gift date?"
    answer: "The CGT calculation does not change (the donor's gain is computed by reference to market value, not equity, under s.17 TCGA 1992). The SDLT position does change. Where the donee assumes the outstanding mortgage as part of the gift transaction, that assumption is treated as chargeable consideration by HMRC for SDLT purposes. The SDLT charge falls on the amount of debt assumed, not the value of the equity. For a property worth £400,000 with a £180,000 mortgage assumed by the donee, the SDLT chargeable consideration is £180,000 and SDLT is calculated on that figure. The 3% second-homes surcharge applies where the donee already owns another dwelling. The IHT and CGT analyses do not double-count: the IHT PET value is the equity element (market value minus debt assumed by the donee), the CGT gain is market value less the donor's base cost. The cleanest position is usually to repay or refinance the mortgage out of the donor's name before the gift so the donee takes a clear title with no debt assumption."
  - question: "Is the gift of a mortgaged property an SDLT event in any other circumstance?"
    answer: "Where there is no chargeable consideration (no debt assumed by the donee, no cash payment, no other valuable thing given in return), the gift is outside SDLT regardless of property value. A pure gift of an unmortgaged BTL with a clean deed of transfer is therefore SDLT-free even on a £1 million property. The SDLT trap is debt assumption, which is the most common chargeable-consideration trigger on family property transfers. A secondary trap: where the donor gifts to the donee subject to an existing tenancy, the tenancy itself can in rare cases be analysed as consideration if commercial terms are not maintained. The standard market gift on commercial-equivalent terms with no debt assumption is reliably outside SDLT."
  - question: "Should I gift one property at a time or the whole portfolio?"
    answer: "For most mid-life portfolios, gifting one property at a time over a planned window of three to seven years produces a better risk-adjusted outcome than a single bulk gift. The reasoning. One, CGT-band stacking: spreading gains across multiple tax years uses multiple annual exempt amounts (£3,000 per year on residential, after the 2023 reduction) and keeps the donor below the higher-rate threshold for as long as possible. Two, mortality-band hedging: each gift starts its own seven-year clock, so a portfolio gifted in years 0, 1, 2, 3 has its earliest gift clear of cumulation by year 7 (when the donor is age 59), allowing further gifts without restarting the cumulation. Three, cash-flow management: the CGT bill on each gift falls due in the 60 days after gift, and a phased schedule produces affordable instalments rather than a single large bill. Four, planning reversibility: a phased schedule allows the donor to stop or accelerate based on intervening health, market, or family circumstances. The trade-off against phased gifting is the donor's continued IHT exposure to the un-gifted portion of the portfolio during the phasing period."
  - question: "What happens if I die in year 6 of the seven-year clock?"
    answer: "The gift comes back into the estate as a failed PET, valued at the date of the gift (not at the date of death). The chargeable element is the gift value less the donor's available NRB at the gift date (cumulating against any earlier gifts in the seven years before the gift). The IHT rate is 40% reduced by taper relief under s.7(4) IHTA 1984: between years 5 and 6 the tax payable is 40% of the full rate, between years 6 and 7 it is 20%. The taper applies to the tax, not the gift value (the most common misconception). The CGT paid at the gift date is not refunded by HMRC on the donor's death within seven years. There is a small double-charge relief in the IHT computation where the cumulative gifts plus the death estate take the rate above the donor's NRB; the relief is mechanical and applied by the donor's personal representatives on the IHT400. Practical hedge: term life cover written in trust for the donee, covering the IHT exposure during years 1 to 7, can absorb the mortality risk at a modest annual premium for a fit donor in their fifties."
  - question: "Can I hedge the mortality risk with life cover during the seven-year clock?"
    answer: "Yes, and for the mid-life donor this is usually the cleanest and cheapest mortality hedge. A 7-year decreasing term assurance policy written in trust for the donee, with sum assured tracking the failed-PET IHT exposure each year (highest in year 1, tapering toward zero by year 7 as the taper schedule reduces the underlying tax), removes the IHT mortality risk for a known annual premium. The trust wrapper keeps the policy proceeds outside the donor's estate so they are not themselves an IHT event. For a healthy 50-year-old non-smoker, a £200,000 sum-assured 7-year decreasing term policy in trust typically prices in the order of £150 to £350 per year. The premium is paid from the donor's normal-expenditure-out-of-income exemption (s.21 IHTA 1984), which itself sits outside the donor's IHT estate provided the regular-pattern conditions are met. The combined route (gift the property, take CGT cost now, write life cover for the seven-year window, pay premiums from income) is the standard mid-life mitigation pattern in landlord planning."
  - question: "How does the mid-life direct gift compare to FIC growth-share gifting?"
    answer: "Two structurally different routes to a similar IHT endpoint, with different CGT and operational profiles. The direct gift transfers a specific asset, crystallises the donor's CGT immediately under s.17 (no s.165 holdover for investment BTL), and lets the donor walk away from the gifted asset. The Family Investment Company route transfers the property portfolio into a corporate wrapper at incorporation (incorporation relief under s.162 TCGA 1992 may apply where the unincorporated business meets the trading-business-by-substance condition, but for most BTL portfolios it does not and incorporation is itself a chargeable disposal). The donor retains preference shares with a fixed coupon (frozen value) and gifts growth shares to the next generation as PETs. The growth-share gift is the seven-year-clock event, not the FIC formation. The CGT on the growth-share gift is computed by reference to the share value (typically minority-discounted, often 25% to 40% below the corresponding underlying property value share), so the CGT bill can be materially lower than the equivalent direct-property gift on a similar percentage. The trade-off: FIC requires ongoing corporate governance, the underlying corporate-tax wrapper does not get BPR (Pawson reads through), and the operational discipline is real. For a sizeable portfolio the FIC route often wins on CGT; for one or two properties, the direct gift is usually simpler. For the detailed mechanics, see our forthcoming FIC value-freeze page (Wave 4 C7)."
  - question: "Is the deed-of-variation route after first death better than lifetime gifting?"
    answer: "Sometimes, on a couple's estate, where the first-to-die's portion can be redirected within 2 years of death to skip a generation, qualify for the charitable 36% reduced rate, or equalise NRB use across spouses. Section 142 IHTA 1984 lets a beneficiary vary their inheritance with read-back to the deceased for IHT and (with election under TCGA 1992 s.62(6)) for CGT. The advantage over lifetime gifting: the donor of the variation (the beneficiary) does not need to survive seven years, and there is no dry CGT cost on the variation because s.62(6) treats the redirection as a disposal by the deceased rather than by the beneficiary. The disadvantage: the first death must happen before the planning is activated, so the timing is not in the planner's hands. The route is therefore complementary rather than alternative: the mid-life lifetime gift starts the clock now on the planned-disposal portion of the portfolio; the deed of variation after first death adjusts the rest. The DoV mechanics deepen on our forthcoming page (Wave 4 C5). For the planning-lens companion, see the IHT decision framework."
  - question: "What records should I keep at the gift date?"
    answer: "Six items, all dated at the gift date, all retained for at least the life of the donor plus seven years (executors will need them at probate). One, an RICS valuation report of the property at the gift date supporting the s.17 market value figure used in the CGT and IHT computations. Two, the donor's CGT computation showing base cost (acquisition price, SDLT paid on acquisition, legal fees, enhancement expenditure with invoices), market value at gift, gain, annual exempt amount applied if available, rate applied (18% or 24%), tax due. Three, the 60-day residential-property return filed with HMRC and proof of CGT payment. Four, the deed of gift or transfer, properly drafted, registered at HMLR. Five, where the property had a mortgage that was repaid or refinanced before the gift, the lender's redemption statement and any new mortgage arrangement evidence. Six, a contemporaneous note of the donor's gift schedule (which property, what value, intended recipient, intended pattern across the planning window) so executors can reconstruct the cumulation at the time of death. The combination supports a clean IHT403 (gifts schedule) at probate and survives any HMRC enquiry on the CGT side."

<p>The seven-year clock is the IHT mitigation that almost every landlord with an exposure problem reaches for. The mechanics are not where this page lives; our Wave 2 walkthrough at <a href="/blog/landlord-tax-essentials/iht-lifetime-gifts-7-year-rule-property-taper">The IHT 7-Year Rule on Property Gifts</a> sets out the s.7(4) IHTA 1984 taper schedule, the PET / CLT distinction, the £3,000 annual exemption and the widely misunderstood point that taper relief reduces the tax payable, not the value of the gift. The question this page answers is different and harder: should the mid-life portfolio landlord actually use the seven-year clock as a planning tool, and if yes, when.</p>

<p>The answer turns on a trade-off most mechanic-led explainers underplay. A direct gift of a buy-to-let between connected persons is a deemed disposal at open-market value under s.17 TCGA 1992. There is no Section 165 holdover relief for non-business BTL (the Pawson investment-line that closes Business Property Relief, set out in our pillar at <a href="/blog/landlord-tax-essentials/business-property-relief-rental-property-iht">Business Property Relief on Rental Property</a>, also closes s.165). The CGT bill on the latent gain falls due now, via the 60-day residential property return. The IHT saving is contingent on the donor surviving seven years. The trade-off is dry CGT today against survival-weighted IHT saved.</p>

<p>Below: why the mid-life window is where the trade-off pays, the CGT overlay in concrete detail, two worked examples on the same £400,000 BTL with a £160,000 latent gain (one gifted at 52, one held to death at 84), the mortgaged-property SDLT complication, the decision-tree against the FIC growth-share alternative and the deed-of-variation-after-first-death route, the mortality hedge through life cover in trust, and the records discipline that survives an HMRC enquiry on the CGT side.</p>

<h2>The mid-life window: why 45 to 58 is the corridor</h2>

<p>The seven-year clock is a fixed-duration instrument applied to a donor with a variable expected lifespan. The instrument is most useful where the donor has enough remaining life expectancy to clear the clock with a high probability, and a low enough current CGT cost to make the trade-off pay against the unconditional IHT bill on the death-date value of the same property.</p>

<p>That is rarely true at 75. The actuarial survival probability over seven years for a non-smoking female aged 75 in good health is in the order of 70% to 75%; for a male of the same age in the same health, lower. Discounting the IHT saving by the failure probability, then subtracting the dry CGT cost the donor pays whether they survive or not, often produces a small or negative net benefit at that age. The right route for the 75-year-old is usually whole-of-life cover in trust to fund the IHT bill rather than a lifetime gift.</p>

<p>It is often true at 52. The same survival calculation for a fit 52-year-old produces a probability in the high nineties as a percentage. The dry CGT cost is the same either way, but it is being weighed against a near-certain IHT saving rather than a coin-flip. The CGT bill itself is also more affordable at mid-life: the donor is typically still earning, has other liquid assets to settle the 60-day CGT charge without forced disinvestment, and is not relying on the gifted property for retirement income.</p>

<p>Pre-mid-life (under 40) the planning is usually premature; the donor's own life will change too much over the 30-year window for a fixed gift to make sense. Post-60 the survival arithmetic moves the right route toward Family Investment Companies, life cover in trust, or deferring to a deed of variation after first death. The narrow corridor from roughly 45 to 58 is where the direct-gift route is most likely to be the cheapest single tool in the planning toolkit.</p>

<h2>The CGT overlay: the binding constraint</h2>

<p>A gift between connected persons (s.286 TCGA 1992 names spouses, civil partners, lineal ancestors, lineal descendants, siblings, and the spouses of any of these) is treated as a disposal at open-market value under s.17 TCGA 1992 regardless of the consideration shown in the deed. The donor's chargeable gain is computed against the donor's base cost (acquisition price plus SDLT plus legal fees on acquisition plus enhancement expenditure on works of a capital nature plus incidental costs of disposal). On a long-held BTL acquired in the early 2000s for £140,000 and now worth £400,000, the chargeable gain is £260,000 less any unused annual exempt amount (£3,000 for residential property in 2025-26 and 2026-27 after the 2023 reduction from £12,300).</p>

<p>The rate. Following the 30 October 2024 reform, residential-property gains are taxed at 18% in the donor's unused basic-rate band and 24% above. The 60-day residential property return delivers the report and payment to HMRC; both the return and the tax are due within the same 60-day window after the date of completion of the transfer. Penalties for late filing follow the standard schedule (£100 immediate, £300 or 5% of tax at 3 months, daily after 6 months).</p>

<p>The three holdover routes that might in principle defer the gain:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Section 165 TCGA 1992 (business-asset holdover):</strong> not available for non-business BTL. The section requires the asset to be an interest in a trading business, shares in a personal trading company, or agricultural property qualifying for APR. Investment land is on the other side of the same Pawson line that closes BPR. Donors and donees cannot jointly elect to defer a BTL gain into the donee's base cost.</li>
<li><strong>Section 260 TCGA 1992 (chargeable-lifetime-transfer holdover):</strong> available only where the gift triggers an immediate IHT charge, which means a gift into a trust that is a CLT for IHT. A gift to an individual is a PET, not a CLT, and s.260 is therefore unavailable. The gift-into-trust route, with its 20% IHT entry charge on the excess over NRB, is the only direct route to s.260 holdover for property; it is the right route for some estates and the wrong route for others, and is addressed in our forthcoming page on CLT into discretionary trust (Wave 4 C10).</li>
<li><strong>Section 162 TCGA 1992 (incorporation relief):</strong> applies on incorporation of an unincorporated business into a company, but the Ramsay test for whether a BTL portfolio meets the "business" condition for s.162 is genuinely difficult. Most casual or part-time BTL portfolios fail, and HMRC's view (set out in CG65700+ and recent tribunal cases) is that significant operational substance is required. Where s.162 is available, the gain is rolled into the share-cost basis at incorporation; the subsequent share-gift is then itself a CGT event but with the rolled-in base cost. The FIC growth-share alternative (Wave 4 C7) covers this route.</li>
</ul>

<p>The practical position for the mid-life direct-gift donor is that the CGT bill is real, payable now, and not deferrable. The trade-off worth running is the CGT cost today against the IHT saving on full survival.</p>

<h2>Worked example one: the gift at 52, full survival</h2>

<p>The Hollis-estate persona. Mr Hollis is 52, a portfolio landlord with four BTL flats in Bristol and a fifth he wants to start gifting. The candidate flat: acquired in 2007 for £140,000 (with £4,200 SDLT and £1,800 legal fees, base cost £146,000), enhancement expenditure on a new kitchen and bathroom in 2014 of £14,000 with invoices, current market value £400,000 as supported by an RICS valuation. The latent gain on a sale is £400,000 minus £160,000 (base cost plus enhancement) = £240,000.</p>

<p>He gifts the flat to his daughter at age 52. The CGT computation: chargeable gain £240,000, less the £3,000 annual exempt amount = £237,000. He is in the higher-rate band already, so the residential-property rate is 24%. CGT on the gift: £56,880, paid via the 60-day return within the window. The IHT analysis: gift value £400,000, a PET against his £325,000 NRB; the chargeable element is £75,000. The seven-year clock starts. He survives to age 70, dies in good health 18 years after the gift. The PET is fully outside his estate. The £400,000 (and its 18 years of growth, now £700,000 at death-date value) is in his daughter's hands free of IHT.</p>

<p>The IHT saving on the value that would otherwise have been in his death estate: 40% of (£700,000 less remaining NRB) is in the range of £250,000 to £280,000 depending on the rest of his estate. Against the £56,880 CGT cost paid in 2026, the net saving (undiscounted) is in the order of £200,000. Even with present-value discounting at 3% over 18 years and the donor's daughter's onward base-cost cost (her future CGT bill on eventual sale is computed against the £400,000 gift-date value, not Mr Hollis's £160,000 base cost), the trade-off pays comfortably.</p>

<aside>
<p>The mid-life trade-off looks expensive on the CGT line and pays on the IHT line.</p>
<p>The decision should be modelled on the donor's actual base cost, current value, expected death-date value, and survival probabilities, not assumed from general principles. We work alongside specialist estate-planning advisers who can run the model and identify the right phasing.</p>
</aside>

<h2>Worked example two: the same property held to death at 84</h2>

<p>Same Mr Hollis, same Bristol flat. The counterfactual: he does not gift in 2026 and instead holds the flat as part of his portfolio to death at age 84. Death-date market value, on the same 18-year growth assumption as above, £700,000 (a 3% annualised real growth). The flat falls into his estate, against the available NRB and RNRB (RNRB may be tapered out for portfolio estates above the £2 million threshold, see our taper page). For a portfolio estate above the taper threshold, the marginal IHT rate on the flat's value is 40% with no RNRB.</p>

<p>IHT on the flat's death-date value: 40% of £700,000 = £280,000. The CGT bill on the £240,000 latent gain at the 2026 gift date is avoided (death uplift under s.62 TCGA 1992 resets the donee's base cost to death-date value, wiping out the donor's gain for CGT purposes), so the daughter inherits with a £700,000 base cost and zero historic gain. On a future sale by the daughter at, say, £750,000 in year 23, her CGT bill is on a £50,000 gain, much smaller than her counterfactual £350,000-ish gain on the gift route.</p>

<p>The full comparison. Gift at 52, survive: CGT £56,880 now, IHT zero on the flat, daughter's future CGT on her own gains from £400,000 base. Hold to death at 84: CGT zero now, IHT £280,000 at death, daughter's future CGT on her own gains from £700,000 base. The IHT saving outweighs the CGT cost by a wide margin (around £223,000 in cash terms), and even after the daughter's higher future CGT exposure on the gift route (she pays CGT on her own onward gains from a lower base), the gift route is the dominant strategy at 52. At 70, the same arithmetic still favours the gift but by a narrower margin; at 80, the calculus flips and the death uplift wins.</p>

<h2>The mortgaged-property SDLT complication</h2>

<p>SDLT is the third tax in the mid-life property-gift decision and the one that catches the unwary. A pure gift with no chargeable consideration is outside SDLT. Where the donee assumes the outstanding mortgage as part of the transfer, the debt assumption is chargeable consideration in HMRC's view, and SDLT is charged on the assumed-debt figure. For a £400,000 BTL with a £180,000 outstanding mortgage assumed by the donee, the SDLT chargeable consideration is £180,000. With the 3% second-homes surcharge (assuming the donee already owns a home), the SDLT is approximately £8,400.</p>

<p>The cleanest mid-life gift structure is: refinance or repay the mortgage out of the donor's name in the months before the gift, then transfer a clear title. The CGT bill is the same either way (computed on market value, not equity), the IHT PET value is the equity element (market value less any debt assumed by the donee), and the SDLT trap is closed by the pre-gift refinance. Where the donee cannot qualify on their own income to take on the property without continued donor involvement, the refinance is not always possible and the SDLT charge has to be priced into the trade-off. Our companion page on the let-property GROB variant at <a href="/blog/landlord-tax-essentials/iht-gift-with-reservation-letting-children-paying-rent-mechanics">GROB on Gifted Let Property</a> covers the related s.102 risk where the donor remains a named borrower or guarantor for affordability reasons.</p>

<h2>The decision tree: direct gift vs FIC vs deed of variation</h2>

<p>For the mid-life portfolio landlord, three routes are seriously in scope. The decision between them is rarely a clean either-or; most portfolios end up using two routes in combination over a planning window of five to fifteen years.</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Route one: direct property gift (this page).</strong> Best for one or two individual properties from a portfolio, where the donor has the CGT capacity to absorb the bill today and the survival probability to clear the clock. Simple, clean, no ongoing structure. Trade-off: dry CGT now, no holdover, mortality risk hedged separately.</li>
<li><strong>Route two: FIC growth-share gift (Wave 4 C7, forthcoming).</strong> Best for larger portfolios where the donor wants to keep operational control, freeze the value at FIC formation, and gift the future growth to the next generation through growth-share PETs. The CGT bill on the share gift is computed on minority-discounted share value and can be materially lower than the equivalent direct-property gift. Trade-off: corporate governance overhead, no BPR on the underlying property (Pawson reads through), and the CT-on-rental-income wrapper has its own profile.</li>
<li><strong>Route three: deed of variation after first death (Wave 4 C5, forthcoming).</strong> Best for couples' estates where the first-to-die's portion can be redirected within 2 years of death to skip a generation, equalise NRB use, or qualify for the charitable 36% rate. Section 142 IHTA 1984 reads back to the deceased for IHT and (with election) for CGT under s.62(6). No dry CGT cost on the variation. Trade-off: the route is only available after the first death, so timing is not in the planner's hands; the route is complementary to lifetime gifting rather than alternative.</li>
</ul>

<p>The decision often runs: phase a small number of direct gifts during the mid-life corridor (route one) on the properties with the largest latent gains and the lowest current rental yields; consider FIC restructuring for the larger remaining portfolio (route two) once the donor crosses 55 to 58; reserve deed-of-variation flexibility for the couple's estate planning (route three) so the surviving spouse retains optionality on the first-death portion.</p>

<aside>
<p>The right combination of routes depends on the portfolio's CGT base-cost profile, the donor's age and health, the family's expected receipt profile, and the donor's appetite for ongoing corporate governance.</p>

<h2>Mortality hedge: life cover during the seven-year window</h2>

<p>The mid-life direct-gift route works best with a parallel life-cover arrangement that hedges the mortality risk during years 1 to 7 of the clock. The standard structure: a 7-year decreasing term assurance policy written in trust for the donee, with sum assured tracking the failed-PET IHT exposure year by year. The exposure is highest in year 1 (full 40% IHT on the chargeable element of the gift) and tapers each year as the s.7(4) IHTA 1984 schedule reduces the rate (80% of full rate at year 3 to 4, 60% at 4 to 5, 40% at 5 to 6, 20% at 6 to 7, zero after 7). A decreasing-term policy mirrors the exposure schedule and is materially cheaper than a level-term policy over the same window.</p>

<p>For a healthy non-smoking 52-year-old, a 7-year decreasing term with a £200,000 starting sum assured typically costs £150 to £350 a year, depending on insurer and medical underwriting. The trust wrapper keeps the policy proceeds outside the donor's estate at any time; without the trust, the proceeds are in the estate and themselves create an IHT charge. The premiums are paid by the donor and qualify for the normal-expenditure-out-of-income exemption under s.21 IHTA 1984 provided the donor maintains the regular-pattern conditions (premium paid out of income, leaves the donor with sufficient income to maintain their normal standard of living, regular and habitual pattern of payment). The exemption keeps the premium payments themselves outside the donor's IHT estate.</p>

<p>The combined position. Direct gift in year 0 (CGT cost now, mortgage refinanced out, deed of gift, 60-day return filed). Decreasing-term life cover in trust for the donee taken out the same week. Annual premium paid from income. Year 1 to 7 mortality risk hedged by the policy; year 8 onward the gift is fully outside the estate and the policy lapses. The total cost is the CGT bill at year 0 plus the cumulative premiums over years 1 to 7 (typically £1,500 to £3,500 in total for the example sums). Set against a successful PET's IHT saving in the £200,000+ range, the trade-off is comfortably favourable.</p>

<h2>Records discipline at the gift date</h2>

<p>The CGT computation, the IHT PET schedule, and the SDLT position all depend on contemporaneous documentation. Six items, all dated at or near the gift date, retained for the donor's lifetime plus seven years:</p>

<ol>
<li><strong>RICS valuation of the property at the gift date.</strong> The s.17 TCGA 1992 deemed disposal value, the IHT PET value, and any SDLT-on-debt-assumed calculation all turn on the market value. A formal RICS report removes the largest single area of HMRC enquiry exposure.</li>
<li><strong>CGT computation with all base-cost evidence.</strong> Acquisition documentation (purchase price, SDLT paid, legal fees with invoices), enhancement expenditure invoices (capital works only, not repairs), incidental costs of disposal. The donor's CGT return must be supported by the underlying evidence.</li>
<li><strong>60-day residential property return filed with HMRC, with proof of CGT payment.</strong> Filed via the donor's Government Gateway under the UK Property Account.</li>
<li><strong>Deed of gift or transfer.</strong> Counsel-drafted for any property above £500,000 in value, registered at HMLR.</li>
<li><strong>Lender documentation if the property had a mortgage.</strong> Either a redemption statement (where the mortgage was repaid pre-gift) or a transfer-of-equity arrangement with the new lender (where the donee assumed). The SDLT position depends on which.</li>
<li><strong>Contemporaneous gift schedule.</strong> A written note recording the donor's intended phasing of further gifts, retained for use by personal representatives in computing cumulation at the donor's eventual death. The cumulation rule requires gifts to be stacked in date order; the schedule supports the IHT403 schedule of gifts at probate.</li>
</ol>
