faqs:
  - question: "How do I calculate SDLT under first-time buyer relief for a £250,000 purchase?"
    answer: "A sole first-time buyer purchasing at £250,000 with no surcharge interaction pays £0 of SDLT. The full £250,000 falls within the £300,000 nil band under FA 2003 Schedule 6ZA paragraph 4 Table A. The standard residential rate table on the same purchase would produce £2,500 of SDLT (£0 on the first £125,000, plus 2% on the £125,000 between £125,000 and £250,000). The relief saving is therefore £2,500. The saving on lower-priced purchases scales with the price up to the £300,000 nil band: a £150,000 purchase saves £500; a £200,000 purchase saves £1,500; a £250,000 purchase saves £2,500; a £300,000 purchase saves £5,000 (because the standard rate table reaches into the 5% band at £250,000, while the relief still gives the full nil band)."
  - question: "How do I calculate SDLT under first-time buyer relief for a £400,000 purchase?"
    answer: "A sole first-time buyer at £400,000 with no surcharge pays £5,000 of SDLT under the relief. The calculation: 0% on the first £300,000 (£0) plus 5% on the £100,000 between £300,000 and £400,000 (£5,000). The standard residential rate table on the same purchase would produce £10,000 (£0 on the first £125,000; £2,500 on the £125,000 between £125,000 and £250,000; £7,500 on the £150,000 between £250,000 and £400,000). The relief saving is £5,000. The same £5,000 saving applies across the £300,000 to £500,000 range: £350,000 purchase saves £5,000 (relief £2,500 versus standard £7,500); £450,000 purchase saves £5,000 (relief £7,500 versus standard £12,500); £500,000 purchase saves £5,000 (relief £10,000 versus standard £15,000)."
  - question: "How does the £500,000 cliff edge work?"
    answer: "The cliff is binary. A purchase at exactly £500,000 by a qualifying sole first-time buyer attracts £10,000 of SDLT under the relief (5% on the £200,000 above the £300,000 nil band). A purchase at £500,001 falls outside the relief entirely under Schedule 6ZA paragraph 1(3) and attracts £15,000.05 of SDLT on the standard residential rate table (£0 on the first £125,000; £2,500 on the £125,000 between £125,000 and £250,000; £12,500 on the £250,000 between £250,000 and £500,000; £0.05 on the £1 above £500,000 at 5%). The £2 increase in consideration produces a £5,000.10 jump in SDLT. Buyers and sellers negotiating prices in the £490,000 to £510,000 range need to model the cliff at offer stage. A vendor pricing at £505,000 for a typical first-time buyer is asking the buyer to find an additional £5,005 in SDLT on top of the £5,000 price difference compared with a £499,999 deal."
  - question: "What is the SDLT on a £450,000 joint purchase by two qualifying first-time buyers?"
    answer: "Two qualifying first-time buyers joint-purchasing at £450,000 with no surcharge pay £7,500 of SDLT under the relief. The calculation is per-transaction, not per-buyer: 0% on the first £300,000 (£0) plus 5% on the £150,000 between £300,000 and £450,000 (£7,500). The standard residential rate table on the same purchase would produce £12,500. The relief saving is £5,000. The joint structure does not double the relief or change the band thresholds; the relief operates at transaction level."
  - question: "What is the SDLT on a £450,000 joint purchase by a first-time buyer and a partner who owns an existing buy-to-let property?"
    answer: "The same £450,000 joint purchase with one non-FTB partner who already owns residential property attracts £35,000 of SDLT. Two statutory effects combine. Schedule 6ZA paragraph 1(4) requires every joint buyer to be a first-time buyer; the non-FTB partner disqualifies the whole transaction from the relief. Schedule 4ZA paragraph 2 aggregates joint purchasers for the additional dwellings surcharge; the partner's existing dwelling triggers the 5% surcharge on the whole transaction. SDLT calculation at standard residential rates plus 5% surcharge: 0% + 5% on the first £125,000 = £6,250; 2% + 5% = 7% on the £125,000 between £125,000 and £250,000 = £8,750; 5% + 5% = 10% on the £200,000 between £250,000 and £450,000 = £20,000; total £35,000. Compared with the £7,500 the same property would cost if both buyers were FTBs and no surcharge applied, the joint structure with the BTL-owning partner costs £27,500 of additional SDLT."
  - question: "What is the SDLT on a £250,000 initial-share purchase of a shared-ownership property with a £500,000 full market value?"
    answer: "Two routes under Schedule 6ZA paragraphs 6A to 6D. Initial-share basis: SDLT is computed on the £250,000 share initially acquired plus the net present value of the rental element on the unsold 50%. The £250,000 share falls within the £300,000 nil band under the relief and produces £0 of SDLT on the share consideration; the NPV of the rental on the unsold 50% is typically modest and may also fall within the nil band. Total upfront SDLT is at or close to £0. Future SDLT arises if the buyer staircases above 80% ownership (staircasing between 0% and 80% is SDLT-free under the standard shared-ownership rules). Market-value election: SDLT is computed on the full £500,000 open market value at FTB relief rates = £10,000. No further SDLT arises on subsequent staircasing. The initial-share basis defers SDLT and may eliminate it entirely if the buyer never staircases above 80%; the market-value election locks in £10,000 now and provides certainty. The choice depends on the buyer's staircasing plans and cash flow position."
  - question: "What is the maximum cash saving I can claim?"
    answer: "Approximately £5,000, available across the £300,000 to £500,000 purchase price range for a sole first-time buyer with no surcharge interaction. The saving is the gap between the relief SDLT and the standard residential rate table SDLT at the same purchase price. The saving plateaus at £5,000 because the standard rate table's 5% band starts at £250,000 (so the relief's 0% nil band gives £5,000 of saving on the £100,000 between £250,000 and £350,000, and the saving does not grow as the price increases beyond that point because both the relief and the standard table charge 5% on the additional consideration). Above £500,000 the relief is unavailable and the saving drops to £0. The headline figure of £6,250 sometimes quoted refers to the SDLT-under-relief on a £425,000 purchase, not the saving relative to the standard table."
  - question: "Do these calculations apply in Wales or Scotland?"
    answer: "No. Wales uses LTT (Land Transaction Tax) administered by the Welsh Revenue Authority with no separate first-time buyer relief; the £225,000 nil band on Welsh LTT serves the function for starter purchases. Scotland uses LBTT (Land and Buildings Transaction Tax) administered by Revenue Scotland with its own first-time buyer relief at LBTT(S)A 2013 Schedule 4A (a £175,000 nil-band uplift with no upper property-value ceiling). The calculations on this page apply only to SDLT (England and Northern Ireland). Cross-border buyers (an English first-time buyer purchasing a Scottish property, for example) use the local jurisdiction's calculator, not the SDLT calculator."
  - question: "What about the 1 April 2025 reversion: do I use £300,000 and £500,000, or £425,000 and £625,000?"
    answer: "£300,000 and £500,000 for any transaction with an effective date on or after 1 April 2025. The temporary thresholds of £425,000 (nil band) and £625,000 (maximum chargeable consideration cap) were in force from 23 September 2022 to 31 March 2025 and reverted to the permanent figures on 1 April 2025. A large amount of competitor calculator content online still uses the temporary figures, which produce incorrect results for any purchase in 2025/26 or later. The effective date for the threshold determination is per FA 2003 section 119: the earlier of completion and substantial performance. Pre-1-April-2025 effective-date transactions used the temporary thresholds; post-1-April-2025 transactions use the permanent thresholds."
  - question: "What documentation does my conveyancer need to support the relief claim?"
    answer: "The conveyancer prepares a first-time buyer declaration as part of the SDLT-return readiness pack and enters relief code 32 on the return form (relief codes are subject to change; the conveyancer uses the current code at the time of filing). Supporting documentation worth retaining for at least six years after completion: confirmation of the buyer's prior property history (particularly relevant for buyers with cross-border family circumstances); evidence of intention to occupy as main residence (mortgage application stating residential occupation, council tax registration, utilities transfer); for shared-ownership purchases, copies of the SDLT-return election made (initial-share basis or market-value election) and the consideration breakdown. The conveyancer is the primary point of contact for the technical claim; the buyer's role is to provide accurate and complete information about prior property ownership and intended occupation."
  - question: "What if I am buying with a guarantor parent who owns property?"
    answer: "Guarantor status alone does not put the parent on the SDLT return. Where the parent is a guarantor for the mortgage only (no legal title interest, no beneficial interest in the property), the parent is not a purchaser for SDLT purposes and the buyer retains first-time buyer status. The relief is available subject to the other eligibility tests. Where the parent is a joint legal owner or joint beneficial owner (typically because the lender requires the parent on the title to satisfy affordability tests), the parent is a joint purchaser and the joint-purchaser rule applies: the parent's existing property ownership disqualifies the relief on the whole transaction and the 5% additional dwellings surcharge applies. The pre-completion structuring decision matters; engagement with the conveyancer and the lender at offer stage is the right time to clarify the legal title arrangement."

<h2>Scenario 1: sole first-time buyer at £250,000</h2>

<p>Profile: a sole first-time buyer purchasing their main residence for £250,000, no additional dwellings, no surcharge interaction.</p>

<p>Calculation under the relief (FA 2003 Schedule 6ZA paragraph 4 Table A):</p>

<ul>
<li>0% on the first £300,000 (the full price falls within the nil band).</li>
<li><strong>SDLT total: £0.</strong></li>
</ul>

<p>Calculation under the standard residential rate table for comparison (FA 2003 section 55 Table A, post-1-April-2025):</p>

<ul>
<li>0% on the first £125,000 = £0.</li>
<li>2% on the £125,000 between £125,000 and £250,000 = £2,500.</li>
<li><strong>Standard SDLT total: £2,500.</strong></li>
</ul>

<p>Relief saving: £2,500.</p>

<h2>Scenario 2: sole first-time buyer at £400,000</h2>

<p>Profile: a sole first-time buyer purchasing their main residence for £400,000, no surcharge interaction.</p>

<p>Calculation under the relief:</p>

<ul>
<li>0% on the first £300,000 = £0.</li>
<li>5% on the £100,000 between £300,000 and £400,000 = £5,000.</li>
<li><strong>SDLT total: £5,000.</strong></li>
</ul>

<p>Calculation under the standard residential rate table:</p>

<ul>
<li>0% on the first £125,000 = £0.</li>
<li>2% on the £125,000 between £125,000 and £250,000 = £2,500.</li>
<li>5% on the £150,000 between £250,000 and £400,000 = £7,500.</li>
<li><strong>Standard SDLT total: £10,000.</strong></li>
</ul>

<p>Relief saving: £5,000.</p>

<h2>Scenario 3: the £500,000 cliff edge (£499,999 versus £500,001)</h2>

<p>Profile: a sole first-time buyer at the cliff boundary. The same buyer, the same profile, two purchase prices £2 apart.</p>

<p>Purchase at £499,999 with the relief:</p>

<ul>
<li>0% on the first £300,000 = £0.</li>
<li>5% on the £199,999 between £300,000 and £499,999 = £9,999.95.</li>
<li><strong>SDLT total: £9,999.95.</strong></li>
</ul>

<p>Purchase at £500,001 (relief unavailable under Schedule 6ZA paragraph 1(3) because the consideration exceeds £500,000):</p>

<ul>
<li>0% on the first £125,000 = £0.</li>
<li>2% on the £125,000 between £125,000 and £250,000 = £2,500.</li>
<li>5% on the £250,000 between £250,000 and £500,000 = £12,500.</li>
<li>5% on the £1 above £500,000 = £0.05.</li>
<li><strong>Standard SDLT total: £15,000.05.</strong></li>
</ul>

<p>The £2 increase in consideration produces a £5,000.10 jump in SDLT. The cliff is sharp. Buyers and sellers negotiating prices in the £490,000 to £510,000 range need to model the cliff at offer stage. A vendor pricing at £505,000 for a typical first-time buyer purchaser is asking the buyer to find an additional £5,005 of SDLT on top of the £5,000 price difference compared with a £499,999 deal, so the effective cost of moving up by £5,001 in price is £10,005. Experienced conveyancers and buyers' agents in the relevant price band are typically alive to the cliff; first-time buyers without specialist input often miss it.</p>

<h2>Scenario 4: joint purchase by two qualifying first-time buyers at £450,000</h2>

<p>Profile: two first-time buyers (a couple, both never having owned residential property anywhere in the world) joint-purchasing their first home for £450,000, no surcharge interaction.</p>

<p>Calculation under the relief:</p>

<ul>
<li>0% on the first £300,000 = £0.</li>
<li>5% on the £150,000 between £300,000 and £450,000 = £7,500.</li>
<li><strong>SDLT total: £7,500.</strong></li>
</ul>

<p>Calculation under the standard residential rate table:</p>

<ul>
<li>0% on the first £125,000 = £0.</li>
<li>2% on the £125,000 between £125,000 and £250,000 = £2,500.</li>
<li>5% on the £200,000 between £250,000 and £450,000 = £10,000.</li>
<li><strong>Standard SDLT total: £12,500.</strong></li>
</ul>

<p>Relief saving: £5,000. The joint structure does not double the relief or change the band thresholds; the relief operates at transaction level. Two qualifying first-time buyers benefit from the relief on the same terms as one qualifying first-time buyer.</p>

<h2>Scenario 5: joint purchase with a non-FTB partner who owns an existing buy-to-let property at £450,000</h2>

<p>Profile: a first-time buyer joint-purchasing their first home with a partner who owns a buy-to-let property elsewhere. Same £450,000 purchase price as Scenario 4. The combined statutory effect is the relief-loss-plus-surcharge trap.</p>

<p>Schedule 6ZA paragraph 1(4) requires every joint buyer to be a first-time buyer; the partner's prior ownership disqualifies the relief on the whole transaction. Schedule 4ZA paragraph 2 aggregates joint purchasers for the surcharge; the partner's existing dwelling triggers the 5% additional dwellings surcharge on the whole transaction.</p>

<p>Calculation at standard residential rates plus 5% surcharge across all bands:</p>

<ul>
<li>0% + 5% surcharge = 5% on the first £125,000 = £6,250.</li>
<li>2% + 5% surcharge = 7% on the £125,000 between £125,000 and £250,000 = £8,750.</li>
<li>5% + 5% surcharge = 10% on the £200,000 between £250,000 and £450,000 = £20,000.</li>
<li><strong>SDLT total: £35,000.</strong></li>
</ul>

<p>Compared with the £7,500 the same property would cost if both buyers were qualifying first-time buyers and no surcharge applied (Scenario 4), the joint structure with the BTL-owning partner costs £27,500 of additional SDLT. The trap is sustained: the qualifying first-time buyer also loses their FTB status after the joint purchase, so any subsequent solo purchase is unlikely to re-engage the relief either.</p>

<h2>Scenario 6: shared-ownership initial-share purchase, £250,000 share of a £500,000 market-value property</h2>

<p>Profile: a sole first-time buyer acquiring a 50% initial share of a shared-ownership property with a full open-market value of £500,000. Annual rent on the unsold 50% is £6,000 (illustrative; actual rent depends on the housing-association valuation and the affordability rent percentage). The buyer elects the initial-share-only basis under Schedule 6ZA paragraphs 6A to 6D.</p>

<p>SDLT on the share consideration (£250,000):</p>

<ul>
<li>0% on the first £300,000 of the share = £0 (the share falls entirely within the nil band).</li>
<li><strong>Share SDLT total: £0.</strong></li>
</ul>

<p>SDLT on the net present value of the rental element on the unsold 50% (computed under FA 2003 Schedule 5, the NPV-of-rent mechanics for residential leases; typically modest for a shared-ownership lease at the £6,000-per-year level over the standard lease term). For most shared-ownership initial-share fact patterns at this price point, the rental NPV either falls within the nil band or produces a small SDLT figure (often under £500).</p>

<p>Upfront SDLT total: at or close to £0. Future SDLT arises if the buyer staircases above 80% ownership (staircasing between 0% and 80% is SDLT-free under the standard shared-ownership rules). The initial-share basis defers SDLT and may eliminate it entirely if the buyer never staircases above 80%.</p>

<h2>Scenario 7: shared-ownership market-value election, £500,000 full market value</h2>

<p>Profile: same buyer and same shared-ownership purchase as Scenario 6, but the buyer elects the market-value election under Schedule 6ZA paragraphs 6A to 6D. SDLT is computed on the full £500,000 open market value at FTB relief rates.</p>

<p>SDLT under the market-value election:</p>

<ul>
<li>0% on the first £300,000 = £0.</li>
<li>5% on the £200,000 between £300,000 and £500,000 = £10,000.</li>
<li><strong>SDLT total: £10,000.</strong></li>
</ul>

<p>The market-value election locks in £10,000 of SDLT now. No further SDLT arises on subsequent staircasing. The election is irrevocable for the transaction.</p>

<p>The choice between Scenario 6 (initial-share basis) and Scenario 7 (market-value election) is fact-specific. The initial-share basis saves upfront SDLT but defers a potentially-larger SDLT bill into the future if the buyer staircases above 80%. The market-value election locks in the £10,000 cost now and provides certainty. Indicative decision points:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Buyer plans to staircase rapidly to 100%:</strong> market-value election is typically preferable (£10,000 now versus potentially more in fragmented future returns).</li>
<li><strong>Buyer plans to remain at a low share for a long period:</strong> initial-share basis is typically preferable (defers and may eliminate the upfront cost).</li>
<li><strong>Open market value exceeds £500,000:</strong> initial-share basis is the only route that preserves the relief (the market-value election would lose the relief entirely under the Schedule 6ZA paragraph 1(3) cap).</li>
<li><strong>Buyer's cash flow at completion is tight:</strong> initial-share basis preserves cash now.</li>
</ul>

<h2>The 1 April 2025 reversion: thresholds in force</h2>

<p>For any transaction with an effective date on or after 1 April 2025, use the permanent thresholds: £300,000 nil band, £500,000 maximum chargeable consideration cap. The temporary thresholds of £425,000 and £625,000 that had been in force from 23 September 2022 sunset on 31 March 2025 and reverted to the permanent figures from 1 April 2025. A large amount of competitor calculator content online still uses the temporary figures, which produce incorrect results for any purchase in 2025/26 or later.</p>

<p>The effective date for the threshold determination is per FA 2003 section 119: the earlier of completion and substantial performance. For a typical residential purchase, the effective date is completion. For an off-plan or instalment purchase, substantial performance may bring the effective date forward; check with the conveyancer if the contractual structure is non-standard.</p>
