<p>Section 24 limits mortgage interest relief to a basic rate tax credit, but many landlords struggle with the calculation. This guide shows you exactly how to <strong>calculate your Section 24 tax credit</strong> using a clear step-by-step method with real examples.</p>

<p>The Section 24 tax credit replaces the old system where you could deduct mortgage interest as an expense. Instead, you now pay tax on your full rental profit, then claim a 20% tax credit on your finance costs.</p>

<h2>Understanding the Section 24 Calculation Method</h2>

<p>The Section 24 tax credit calculation follows this basic structure:</p>

<ul>
<li>Calculate your total rental income</li>
<li>Deduct all allowable expenses (except finance costs)</li>
<li>Add back any finance costs to get your taxable rental profit</li>
<li>Apply income tax rates to this profit</li>
<li>Calculate 20% tax credit on your finance costs</li>
<li>Subtract the tax credit from your income tax bill</li>
</ul>

<p>This method ensures finance costs are only relieved at the basic rate of 20%, regardless of your actual tax rate.</p>

<h2>Step-by-Step Section 24 Calculation Example</h2>

<p>Let's work through a detailed <strong>section 24 calculation example</strong> using a higher-rate taxpayer with a single BTL property:</p>

<h3>Property Details</h3>
<ul>
<li>Annual rental income: £18,000</li>
<li>Mortgage interest: £8,000</li>
<li>Other property expenses: £3,000</li>
<li>Other income: £60,000 (making them a higher-rate taxpayer)</li>
</ul>

<h3>Step 1: Calculate Taxable Rental Profit</h3>
<p>Under Section 24, you cannot deduct mortgage interest as an expense:</p>
<ul>
<li>Rental income: £18,000</li>
<li>Less: Other expenses: £3,000</li>
<li><strong>Taxable rental profit: £15,000</strong></li>
</ul>

<h3>Step 2: Calculate Income Tax on Rental Profit</h3>
<p>The £15,000 rental profit is added to their £60,000 other income, making their total income £75,000. This falls into the higher-rate tax band:</p>
<ul>
<li>Income tax on rental profit: £15,000 × 40% = <strong>£6,000</strong></li>
</ul>

<h3>Step 3: Calculate Section 24 Tax Credit</h3>
<p>The tax credit is calculated on the mortgage interest at 20%:</p>
<ul>
<li>Section 24 tax credit: £8,000 × 20% = <strong>£1,600</strong></li>
</ul>

<h3>Step 4: Calculate Net Tax Due</h3>
<p>The final tax liability is the income tax minus the tax credit:</p>
<ul>
<li>Income tax on rental profit: £6,000</li>
<li>Less: Section 24 tax credit: £1,600</li>
<li><strong>Net tax due: £4,400</strong></li>
</ul>

<h2>Comparing Pre and Post Section 24 Tax</h2>

<p>To understand the Section 24 impact, let's compare this with the old system using the same example:</p>

<h3>Pre-Section 24 Calculation</h3>
<ul>
<li>Rental income: £18,000</li>
<li>Less: All expenses (including £8,000 interest): £11,000</li>
<li>Taxable profit: £7,000</li>
<li>Tax at 40%: £2,800</li>
</ul>

<h3>Post-Section 24 Result</h3>
<ul>
<li>Net tax due: £4,400 (as calculated above)</li>
<li><strong>Additional tax cost: £1,600 per year</strong></li>
</ul>

<p>This shows how Section 24 increases the tax burden for higher and additional rate taxpayers, as they lose the benefit of mortgage interest relief at their marginal tax rate.</p>

<h2>Section 24 Calculation for Basic Rate Taxpayers</h2>

<p>Basic rate taxpayers are typically unaffected by Section 24. Here's why:</p>

<p>Using a landlord with £30,000 other income and the same property:</p>

<h3>Income Tax Calculation</h3>
<ul>
<li>Total income: £30,000 + £15,000 = £45,000</li>
<li>Income tax on rental profit: £15,000 × 20% = £3,000</li>
<li>Section 24 tax credit: £8,000 × 20% = £1,600</li>
<li>Net tax due: £3,000 - £1,600 = £1,400</li>
</ul>

<h3>Pre-Section 24 Comparison</h3>
<ul>
<li>Taxable profit: £7,000</li>
<li>Tax at 20%: £1,400</li>
</ul>

<p>The result is identical because both systems provide 20% relief on the mortgage interest.</p>

<h2>Complex Scenarios and Adjustments</h2>

<h3>Multiple Properties</h3>
<p>When you have multiple properties, you calculate the Section 24 impact across your entire portfolio:</p>

<ul>
<li>Combine all rental income</li>
<li>Combine all allowable expenses (excluding finance costs)</li>
<li>Combine all mortgage interest and finance costs</li>
<li>Apply the Section 24 calculation to the total figures</li>
</ul>

<h3>Losses and Finance Cost Restriction</h3>
<p>If your rental losses exceed your finance costs, the tax credit is limited. You can only claim relief up to the amount of finance costs, not the full loss amount.</p>

<p>For example, if you have:</p>
<ul>
<li>Rental profit before finance costs: -£2,000 (a loss)</li>
<li>Finance costs: £5,000</li>
</ul>

<p>You can only claim a tax credit on £2,000 of finance costs, not the full £5,000, as this is the amount needed to eliminate the loss.</p>

<h2>When Section 24 Doesn't Apply</h2>

<p>Section 24 only affects individual landlords and partnerships. It does not apply to:</p>

<ul>
<li>Limited companies owning property</li>
<li>Commercial property investments</li>
<li>Property trading businesses</li>
<li>Furnished holiday lettings (though this regime was abolished from April 2025)</li>
</ul>

<p>This is why many landlords consider <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/buy-to-let-limited-company-complete-guide-uk">incorporating their property business</a> to avoid Section 24 restrictions.</p>

<h2>How to Work Out Section 24 Impact on Your Portfolio</h2>

<p>To properly <strong>work out the Section 24 impact</strong> on your specific situation:</p>

<ol>
<li>Calculate your current tax position using the Section 24 method</li>
<li>Compare this with the pre-Section 24 calculation</li>
<li>Consider the impact of rising property income tax rates from April 2027</li>
<li>Factor in your long-term property investment strategy</li>
</ol>

<p>From April 2027, property income will be taxed at separate rates (22% basic, 42% higher, 47% additional rate), which will further increase the Section 24 burden for many landlords.</p>

<p>Understanding your Section 24 calculation helps with tax planning decisions:</p>

<h3>Timing of Property Purchases</h3>
<p>The Section 24 impact varies depending on your total income. Consider timing major purchases when your income might be lower.</p>

<h3>Incorporation Analysis</h3>
<p>Knowing your exact Section 24 cost helps you evaluate whether <a href="/incorporation">incorporating your property business</a> would be beneficial.</p>

<h3>Pension Contributions</h3>
<p>Increasing pension contributions can reduce your overall tax rate, potentially reducing the Section 24 impact if it brings you down from higher to basic rate.</p>
