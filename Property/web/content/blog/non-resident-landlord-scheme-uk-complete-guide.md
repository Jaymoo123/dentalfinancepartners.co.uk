<p>The <strong>non-resident landlord scheme</strong> is the rule that decides whether your UK rent reaches you in full or arrives with 20% already taken off. If you live outside the UK and let out UK property, this scheme governs how HMRC collects income tax on that rent: usually your <strong>letting agent</strong> deducts basic-rate tax at source and pays it to HMRC, unless you hold approval to receive the rent gross.</p>

<p>This guide is the broad overview of how the whole scheme works, who it catches, how to apply on form <strong>NRL1</strong> to be paid gross, where the form goes, when a tenant rather than an agent has to withhold, and how the scheme sits alongside Self Assessment, Making Tax Digital and capital gains tax. Where a single topic deserves a deep dive, we point you to the specialist page that covers it in full.</p>

<h2>At a glance: who withholds, who applies, which form</h2>

<p>The forms split by entity type and by role. <strong>NRL1</strong> is the gross-payment application for an individual, <strong>NRL2</strong> for a company and <strong>NRL3</strong> for a trustee. On the agent side, <strong>NRLQ</strong> is the quarterly return, <strong>NRLY</strong> the annual information return, and <strong>NRL6</strong> the annual certificate of tax deducted that an agent or tenant gives the landlord. Getting these labels right matters, because the scheme stops working cleanly the moment a form is used for the wrong purpose. A landlord never files an NRLQ, and an agent never files an NRL1 on the landlord's behalf.</p>

<h2>What the non-resident landlord scheme is, and what it is not</h2>

<p>The scheme is a UK statutory withholding mechanism. Its legal home is section 971 of the Income Tax Act 2007, which gives HMRC the power to make regulations collecting income tax from the UK representatives of non-resident landlords, and SI 1995/2902, which contains the detailed operating rules. The point of the scheme is purely collection: because the landlord is abroad and harder for HMRC to chase, tax is taken at source by whoever in the UK is paying the rent.</p>

<p>It helps to be clear about three things the scheme is not. It is not a separate tax. The 20% is an advance payment of the ordinary income tax on your rental profit, credited against your final bill. It is not optional for the agent. A UK letting agent who pays rent to a non-resident landlord must operate the scheme unless HMRC has authorised gross payment. And it is not a tax treaty mechanism. Being treaty-resident somewhere else does not switch the scheme off. A non-resident landlord who is treaty-resident abroad still has to apply for gross-payment approval if they want to be paid in full, and still owes UK tax on the UK rent. The treaty governs how double taxation is relieved, not whether the scheme applies. For that allocation-of-taxing-rights layer, see our <a href="/blog/non-resident-landlord-tax/tax-treaties-property-investors-treaty-framework-guide">UK tax treaties for property investors framework guide</a>.</p>

<h2>Who is a non-resident landlord</h2>

<p>For the scheme, the trigger is your <strong>usual place of abode</strong> being outside the UK, which is the test the legislation and HMRC guidance use for whether a payer has to operate the scheme. As a rough working rule HMRC treats a landlord as having a usual place of abode abroad if they are away for six months or more, but it is not identical to being non-UK resident under the Statutory Residence Test (Finance Act 2013, Schedule 45). You can be UK resident in a tax year and still have your usual place of abode outside the UK for part of it, which is why the scheme uses its own wording. If you are unsure where you stand under the wider residence rules, the Statutory Residence Test is the place to start.</p>

<p>The scheme catches more than just individuals. It applies to non-resident <strong>individuals, companies and trustees</strong> alike, each with their own gross-payment form. A company is generally non-resident for these purposes where it is neither incorporated in the UK nor centrally managed and controlled here, which is why a Luxembourg or other offshore holding entity letting UK property typically falls within the scheme. Trustees of a non-resident trust letting UK property are caught too, as are offshore pension and investment vehicles that own and let UK property. The mechanics of withholding are the same across all of them. What differs is how the underlying profit is then taxed: individuals and trustees on income tax, companies on corporation tax since 2020.</p>

<aside>
<p><strong>Not sure whether the scheme applies to you yet?</strong> The trigger is your usual place of abode, not a single day count, and it can change mid-year when you move. If you have just left the UK or are about to, a short conversation now can stop the 20% deduction starting on rent it should not, and can get an NRL1 in before your first payment is due.</p>
</aside>

<h2>How withholding works: agent, tenant, and the £100-a-week trigger</h2>

<p>Where a UK letting agent collects your rent, the agent is the person who operates the scheme. Each quarter the agent works out the tax on the rent received, deducts 20%, pays it to HMRC and reports it. Importantly, the agent does not withhold on the full rent. Under regulation 9(4) of SI 1995/2902 the agent can first take off the expenses they are <strong>reasonably satisfied are deductible</strong> under the Tax Acts, such as agency fees, and apply the 20% to what is left. The agent then gives you an NRL6 certificate each year showing the tax deducted, and accounts to HMRC on the NRLQ each quarter and the NRLY annually. We cover the agent machinery in full in our guide to <a href="/blog/non-resident-landlord-tax/nrl-scheme-letting-agents-quarterly-returns-mechanics">NRL letting-agent quarterly returns</a>, and the deduction mechanics in our guide to <a href="/blog/non-resident-landlord-tax/nrl-withholding-tax-20-percent-basic-rate-deduction">the 20% basic-rate withholding</a>.</p>

<p>One point trips up agents and landlords alike: what makes someone a <strong>letting agent</strong> for the scheme is acting in the business of managing and receiving rent for the property, not where the money lands. Rent being paid into a <strong>UK bank account</strong> does not, by itself, pull anyone into the scheme. A person who merely passes money on without being in the property-management business is not the agent, and the bank certainly is not. So the right question is never "is the account in the UK" but "is there a UK person acting as letting agent receiving this rent". If there is, that person operates the scheme; if there is not, you fall back to the tenant test below.</p>

<p>Where there is <strong>no UK letting agent</strong> and the tenant pays you directly, the position shifts to the tenant, but only above a threshold. The tenant has to deduct and account for tax only where the rent is more than <strong>£100 a week</strong>, which is about £5,200 a year. The calculation for a direct-paying tenant sits in regulation 8 of SI 1995/2902. Where the rent is £100 a week or less and no agent is involved, the tenant has no withholding duty at all, unless HMRC specifically directs otherwise. That does not make the income tax-free. You still have to declare the rent and pay any UK tax due through Self Assessment. It simply means nothing is taken at source.</p>

<h3>A worked example: why the 20% is rarely the final figure</h3>

<p>Suppose your UK flat is let through a UK agent for £1,500 a month, £18,000 a year, and you have not yet applied for gross payment. The agent first takes off the costs it is reasonably satisfied are deductible, say its own management fee of around £1,800, leaving £16,200, and withholds 20% of that, about £3,240, paying it to HMRC across the four quarterly NRLQ returns.</p>

<p>But that is not your real tax. When you file Self Assessment you claim everything the agent could not: mortgage interest of, say, £7,000 (relieved as the Section 24 basic-rate tax reducer), buildings and contents insurance, ground rent and service charge, repairs and the relief for replacing domestic items. Your actual taxable profit collapses to a few thousand pounds, and the income tax on it, after the personal allowance if you are entitled to one, may be well under £1,000. The £3,240 already withheld is credited against that, and the excess is repaid. The lesson is consistent across portfolios of every size: the 20% is a holding figure, and the real money is in filing the return correctly and reclaiming the difference.</p>

<h2>Applying to receive rent gross: NRL1, NRL2 and NRL3</h2>

<p>If you would rather receive your rent in full and handle the tax yourself, you apply for <strong>gross-payment approval</strong>. Individuals use form <strong>NRL1</strong>, companies use <strong>NRL2</strong> and trustees use <strong>NRL3</strong>. The test HMRC applies is essentially one of trust: your UK tax affairs must be up to date, with any required returns filed and any tax paid, and HMRC must expect you to keep meeting your UK obligations. A landlord with a clean record and a commitment to file is the standard candidate for approval.</p>

<p>On the form you give HMRC the basics it needs to authorise your agents: your details, your UK tax reference if you have one, the properties concerned and the letting agents involved. You submit the NRL1 online or on paper to HMRC's Personal Tax International team, which administers the scheme; confirm the current address or online route on the gov.uk apply page before you send it, because HMRC changes these from time to time. HMRC checks the application and, if satisfied, writes directly to your agents telling them to pay you gross. From that point the agent stops deducting.</p>

<p>As to timing, treat several weeks as a sensible planning assumption rather than a fixed promise, and apply early. Approval does not undo tax already withheld before it took effect; you reclaim that through Self Assessment. It also does not expire on a set date, but HMRC can withdraw it if you stop meeting your obligations, after which the 20% deduction resumes. The crucial point to hold onto is that approval changes <strong>when</strong> you pay, not <strong>how much</strong>. The tax due is identical either way. For the step-by-step on completing and submitting the application, see our guide to <a href="/blog/non-resident-landlord-tax/nrl-approval-receive-rent-gross-hmrc-guide">NRL gross-payment approval</a>.</p>

<h3>Keeping your scheme record current</h3>

<p>Because HMRC authorises your <strong>specific</strong> agents to pay gross, the approval is only as good as the details behind it. If you change letting agents and do not tell HMRC, a new agent can start deducting the 20% even though you hold approval, while an old agent might keep paying gross after it should have stopped. So tell the non-resident landlord team in writing, quoting your scheme reference, whenever your agent, your correspondence address, or (for a company) your registered details change. For a non-resident <strong>company</strong>, updating the scheme record is separate from updating Companies House and the Register of Overseas Entities; all three need to be kept in step.</p>

<h2>Refunds and over-withholding: you reclaim through Self Assessment</h2>

<p>Over-withholding is common, and it is built into how the scheme works. The 20% is deducted from rent after only the limited expenses the agent is reasonably satisfied are deductible. It is not deducted from your actual net profit after every allowable cost. So when you finally compute the real position, you almost always find too much tax has been taken.</p>

<p>The route to get it back is your <strong>UK Self Assessment return</strong>. On the return you declare the rent and claim everything you are entitled to: mortgage and loan interest through the Section 24 basic-rate tax reducer, letting-agent and management fees, repairs and maintenance, insurance, ground rent and service charges, accountancy, and the relief for replacing domestic items. The tax on your real profit is then compared with the 20% already withheld, and the excess is repaid. Small-scale landlords should also keep the £1,000 property allowance in mind (Income Tax (Trading and Other Income) Act 2005, section 783B series), which can remove the need to compute expenses at all where gross rents are very low.</p>

<p>Two myths to bury. There is no special <strong>£10,000</strong> income threshold that decides whether you reclaim through Self Assessment, and there is no separate small-amount refund form sitting alongside it. The reclaim route is Self Assessment. For how to complete the return as a non-resident, including the SA100, the property pages and the residence pages, see our guide to <a href="/blog/non-resident-landlord-tax/non-resident-landlord-self-assessment-filing-requirements">non-resident landlord Self Assessment filing</a>.</p>

<h2>Ongoing UK tax: Self Assessment and Making Tax Digital</h2>

<p>Gross-payment approval is not an exemption. A non-resident landlord with a UK property business generally has to notify HMRC and file a Self Assessment return each year, declaring the rental profit and paying the income tax due. This is true whether you receive rent gross or have it withheld. The withholding, where it happens, is just an advance payment credited against the same bill. A late NRL1 does not erase past years either: if you have let UK property without declaring it, those years still have to be brought up to date and the tax paid, with interest and any penalties, separately from the gross-payment application.</p>

<p>On top of Self Assessment, <a href="/blog/making-tax-digital-mtd/making-tax-digital-landlords-april-2026-deadline">Making Tax Digital for Income Tax</a> now applies, phased in by income level. It is mandatory from <strong>6 April 2026</strong> where qualifying income is over <strong>£50,000</strong>, from <strong>6 April 2027</strong> where it is over <strong>£30,000</strong>, and from <strong>6 April 2028</strong> where it is over <strong>£20,000</strong>. Qualifying income is gross rental and any self-employment income, before expenses. Because the test is income-based rather than residence-based, non-resident landlords are caught on the same thresholds. If you are in, you keep digital records and send quarterly updates through compatible software, then finalise the year. The non-resident landlord scheme runs <strong>alongside</strong> MTD: you can be subject to both 20% withholding and quarterly digital reporting at the same time.</p>

<h2>April 2027: separate property income tax rates (enacted)</h2>

<p>From <strong>6 April 2027</strong>, property income is taxed at its own dedicated rates rather than the main income tax rates. The rates are <strong>22% basic, 42% higher and 47% additional</strong>. This is not a proposal. It was announced at the Autumn Budget 2025 and enacted by the <strong>Finance Act 2026</strong> (2026 c.11), which received Royal Assent on <strong>18 March 2026</strong>. The framework sits in section 6 and the numeric rates in section 7.</p>

<p>Two points matter for landlords who own property across the UK. First, the separate rates apply to <strong>England, Wales and Northern Ireland</strong>. Scotland sets its own income tax rates for Scottish-resident taxpayers; a non-resident landlord is taxed at the default UK rates, so 22/42/47 apply to their UK property income wherever in the UK it sits. Second, the Section 24 finance-cost tax reducer rises to <strong>22%</strong> in step from 2027/28 (Finance Act 2026, Schedule 1, amending the relief in the Income Tax (Trading and Other Income) Act 2005). Because the reducer moves up with the basic rate to 22%, a basic-rate landlord gets no new mortgage-interest wedge. Higher and additional-rate landlords get the reducer at the same 22%, so their wedge stays at roughly 20 points (42% less 22%) and 25 points (47% less 22%), much as before.</p>

<h2>Non-resident companies: the 2020 corporation tax move</h2>

<p>If you let UK property through a non-resident <strong>company</strong>, the withholding scheme still applies and you apply for gross payment on form NRL2. What changed is the tax that sits underneath. From <strong>6 April 2020</strong>, non-resident corporate landlords moved from income tax to <strong>corporation tax</strong> on their UK property profits, under Schedule 5 to the Finance Act 2019. Any 20% withheld is now credited against the company's corporation tax rather than its income tax.</p>

<p>The move brought the corporation tax machinery with it. Non-resident company landlords now have to consider the corporate interest restriction, which can cap how much finance cost is deductible, along with the loss and anti-avoidance rules that come with the corporation tax regime. For the detail of the transition and the company tax stack, see our guide to the <a href="/blog/non-resident-landlord-tax/changes-nrl-companies">changes for non-resident company landlords</a>.</p>

<h2>Capital gains: the non-resident CGT 60-day return</h2>

<p>The non-resident landlord scheme is about <strong>income</strong> from letting. When you <strong>sell</strong>, a separate regime takes over. Non-residents pay UK capital gains tax on disposals of UK land and property, and the reporting is strict: you file a <strong>non-resident capital gains tax return and pay any tax due within 60 days of completion</strong>, and the return is required for <strong>every</strong> disposal of UK property, even where no tax is due. Residential gains are taxed at <strong>18% or 24%</strong> depending on where the gain sits against your income, with a <strong>£3,000</strong> annual exempt amount.</p>

<p>This 60-day deadline catches people out, because it runs from completion and is independent of the annual Self Assessment cycle. You can easily owe both a 60-day return and an annual return in the same tax year. For how the non-resident CGT regime works on a sale, see our guide to <a href="/blog/non-resident-landlord-tax/non-resident-cgt-uk-property-rates-reporting">non-resident CGT on UK property rates and reporting</a>, and for the wider picture our <a href="/blog/capital-gains-tax/capital-gains-tax-property-complete-guide-uk">complete guide to capital gains tax on property</a>.</p>

<h2>Double taxation: the treaty layer</h2>

<p>A common hope is that a tax treaty removes UK tax on UK rent. It does not. The UK keeps the right to tax income from UK land at source, and the property article in the standard treaty (modelled on the OECD article 6) confirms that the country where the property sits can tax the rental income. What the treaty does is prevent the same income being taxed twice, usually by your home country giving <strong>credit</strong> for the UK tax you have paid.</p>

<p>The relief is <strong>claimed, not automatic</strong>. You have to report the UK rent correctly in the UK, pay the UK tax, and then claim credit for it in your home country (or the other way round, depending on the treaty and your residence). Getting the order and the paperwork right is what avoids real double taxation. The interaction of the statutory scheme and the treaty is exactly the kind of point where specialist input pays for itself. Our <a href="/blog/non-resident-landlord-tax/tax-treaties-property-investors-treaty-framework-guide">treaty framework guide</a> sets out how the articles fit together.</p>

<h2>Non-resident companies and the Register of Overseas Entities</h2>

<p>A non-resident <strong>company</strong> that owns UK property has a separate, non-tax compliance duty. It must register on the <strong>Register of Overseas Entities</strong> at Companies House, identify its beneficial owners, and file an <strong>annual update statement</strong> to keep the entry current. Failing to register can block the company from buying, selling or charging the property and carries penalties. This sits on top of the tax obligations, not instead of them. For what the register requires and how the annual update works, see our guide to the <a href="/blog/non-resident-landlord-tax/register-of-overseas-entities-roe-annual-update-statement-non-resident-landlords">Register of Overseas Entities for non-resident landlords</a>.</p>

<h2>Common mistakes, and how a specialist helps</h2>

<p>Most problems we see are timing and assumption errors rather than deliberate non-compliance. The frequent ones are: assuming gross-payment approval is permanent (HMRC can withdraw it if you stop meeting your obligations, and the 20% deduction then resumes); not telling HMRC when you change letting agents, so an agent keeps deducting after you have approval, or starts late; treating gross-payment approval as if it ended the duty to file (it does not); forgetting the 60-day CGT return on a sale; and missing the £100-a-week tenant trigger where there is no agent. Each is straightforward to avoid once you know it is there.</p>

<p>To make the common situation concrete: an overseas landlord with a single London flat let through a UK agent, who applied for gross payment on an NRL1 and then filed Self Assessment claiming full expenses, will typically find the tax actually due is materially below the 20% the agent would otherwise have withheld, with the difference recovered through the return. The pattern repeats across portfolios of every size. The value of getting it right is in the timing and the reclaim, not in any single clever trick.</p>
