- question: What are AIA capital allowances and can a landlord use them?
  answer: The Annual Investment Allowance is a form of capital allowance that gives a 100% deduction for qualifying plant and machinery in the year you buy it, up to a permanent £1,000,000 limit (CAA 2001 s.51A(5), made permanent by Finance (No. 2) Act 2023 s.8). You can use AIA only on property held for a qualifying activity where the plant is not inside a dwelling. So a commercial let or the common parts of a block can support a claim, but plant inside a standard residential dwelling is barred by CAA 2001 s.35. The allowance is per business, not per property.
- question: Can a landlord claim AIA on a rental property?
  answer: It depends on the property. You can claim AIA on plant and machinery in commercial space, in the common parts of a block of flats or HMO, and on the commercial portion of a mixed-use building. You cannot claim AIA on plant inside a standard residential dwelling, because CAA 2001 s.35 removes that expenditure from qualifying expenditure. On a residential let you use replacement of domestic items relief and revenue repairs instead.
- question: Can landlords claim capital allowances on residential property?
  answer: Not on plant inside the dwelling itself. CAA 2001 s.35 bars plant and machinery allowances for plant for use in a dwelling-house within a property business, so fitted kitchens, boilers, bathroom suites and white goods inside a let flat or house do not qualify. The narrow exception is the common parts of a multi-unit building (a communal boiler, lift or shared-area lighting), which are not in a dwelling-house and can qualify. For the cost of items inside the dwelling, you rely on replacement of domestic items relief.
- question: Can you claim capital allowances on commercial property and which items qualify?
  answer: Yes. A UK property business holding commercial space is a qualifying activity (CAA 2001 s.15(1)(b)), and the s.35 dwelling-house bar does not bite on non-dwelling space. Qualifying plant typically includes heating and ventilation, air conditioning, fire and security systems, sanitary fittings, kitchen equipment and certain electrical plant. Integral features (electrical and cold-water systems, space heating, lifts, external solar shading) qualify at the special rate (CAA 2001 s.33A). The bare building shell, land and structural works do not qualify.
- question: Can you claim capital allowances on leasehold property improvements?
  answer: A commercial tenant or leaseholder who fits out leased space can claim on the plant and integral features they install, because they incur the expenditure on plant for their qualifying activity. The building shell and structural alterations are excluded under CAA 2001 s.21 (List A) and s.22 (List B). New non-residential construction may instead attract the Structures and Buildings Allowance at 3% a year. Improvements inside a residential dwelling remain barred by s.35.
- question: How does mixed-use property apportionment work for capital allowances?
  answer: You claim on the commercial part and not on the dwelling part. Where an asset serves both, such as a boiler heating a shop and the flat above it, you apportion the cost on a just and reasonable basis (CAA 2001 s.35(3)), usually by floor area or heat output, and claim allowances only on the commercial proportion. Keep the basis of apportionment documented in case HMRC asks.
- question: Can I claim capital allowances on a furnished holiday let after April 2025?
  answer: No new furnished holiday let claims. The FHL regime was abolished for income tax from 6 April 2025 and for corporation tax accounting periods beginning on or after 1 April 2025 (Finance Act 2025 Sch 5). A former FHL is now an ordinary residential property business, so the s.35 dwelling-house bar applies and no new plant inside the dwelling qualifies. Plant pools built up while the property was an FHL are grandfathered and continue to be written down in the ordinary property business, and a later sale can still trigger a balancing charge on those pools.
- question: Do HMOs and serviced accommodation qualify for capital allowances?
  answer: Usually only on the common parts. Most HMOs and serviced lets are ordinary property businesses after FHL abolition, so the s.35 bar applies to plant inside the let rooms while communal plant in shared areas can qualify. If the level of service is genuinely substantial (managed catering, daily cleaning, concierge), the activity may amount to a trade with a broader claim base, but that is a fact-specific question. Get advice on trade status before assuming a wider claim.
- question: What property improvements count as plant for capital allowances?
  answer: Plant and machinery is equipment with a function in the business, not the fabric of the building. In commercial or common-parts areas this covers items such as heating and ventilation, air conditioning, lifts, fire and security systems and certain electrical and water systems (the last group as integral features under s.33A). The walls, floors, ceilings, doors and windows that make up the building are excluded under s.21 List A. Replacing a worn-out boiler may be a revenue repair rather than capital, so the capital-versus-revenue line is the first test.
- question: How much is the AIA limit and is it permanent?
  answer: The AIA limit is £1,000,000 and it is permanent. CAA 2001 s.51A(5) sets the maximum at £1,000,000, made permanent from 1 April 2023 by Finance (No. 2) Act 2023 s.8. There is no scheduled reversion to £200,000. The limit applies per business across all qualifying expenditure in the chargeable period, not per property, and related companies under common control share a single allowance.
- question: What writing-down allowance rates apply from April 2026?
  answer: The main pool writing-down allowance is 14% on a reducing-balance basis from April 2026, cut from 18% by Finance Act 2026 s.28 (which substitutes 14% into CAA 2001 s.56(1)). The special-rate pool, which holds integral features, stays at 6% (s.104D). A chargeable period straddling the start date uses a time-apportioned hybrid rate between 18% and 14%. Expenditure above the AIA limit drops into these pools.
- question: Is there a new first-year allowance landlords can use?
  answer: Yes. Finance Act 2026 s.29 introduced a 40% first-year allowance (inserted as CAA 2001 s.45U) on new and unused main-rate plant and machinery, for expenditure on or after 1 January 2026, excluding cars and second-hand assets. It is not restricted to companies or to unincorporated businesses. In practice it is the route for unincorporated landlords with qualifying commercial plant, because a company buying qualifying new main-rate plant will normally claim 100% full expensing (s.45S) instead.
- question: What happens to capital allowances when you sell the property?
  answer: A sale that includes fixtures triggers disposal values on the plant pools (CAA 2001 s.61 and the s.196 fixtures Table), which can create a balancing charge that claws back allowances claimed. For a commercial buyer to claim on the fixtures, the seller must have pooled the expenditure and the parties must fix its value, usually by a joint s.198 election made within two years of completion (CAA 2001 s.201). Without that, the buyer's expenditure is treated as nil (s.187A(3)) and the allowances are lost for good.

<p>Before you work out how much you can claim in capital allowances, work out whether you can claim at all. For landlords, that answer turns almost entirely on what type of property you let and where the plant sits inside it. A commercial unit and a standard buy-to-let flat are taxed very differently here, and the most expensive mistake is assuming a residential portfolio carries the same claim base as an office or a shop.</p>

<p>One question sits upstream of capital allowances entirely: is the spend even capital, or is it a revenue repair you deduct in full this year? Our guide to <a href="/blog/landlord-tax-essentials/capital-vs-revenue-expenditure-landlord-uk">capital versus revenue expenditure for landlords</a> draws that line. If the cost is genuinely capital, eligibility is the next question, and that turns on the property type.</p>

<h2>Can landlords claim capital allowances? The short answer by property type</h2>

<p>Capital allowances are not a reward for owning a building. They are available where the property is held for a <strong>qualifying activity</strong>, and a UK property business is one (<a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2001/2/section/15">CAA 2001 s.15(1)(b)</a>). The catch for landlords is <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2001/2/section/35">CAA 2001 s.35</a>: expenditure on plant or machinery for use in a <em>dwelling-house</em> within a property business is not qualifying expenditure. That single rule is why capital allowances on investment property in the UK behave so differently between residential and commercial stock.</p>

<p>So rental capital allowances exist, but the dwelling-house bar gates them. Here is the quick verdict by property type.</p>

<h2>Standard residential buy-to-let: why the answer is usually no</h2>

<p>For a standard residential let, you cannot claim plant and machinery allowances on anything inside the dwelling. The boiler, the fitted kitchen, the bathroom suite, the carpets and the white goods are all plant for use in a dwelling-house, and s.35 takes them straight out of qualifying expenditure. This is a statutory rule in CAA 2001, not an informal HMRC view, and it is not a date-stamped concession that came in for a particular tax year. The dwelling-house bar applies to the whole UK property business.</p>

<p>That does not leave you with nothing. Two routes do the work that capital allowances would in a commercial setting. The first is <a href="/blog/section-24-and-tax-relief/replacement-domestic-items-relief-uk-landlords-guide">replacement of domestic items relief</a>, which gives a deduction when you replace (not initially provide) free-standing furniture, white goods, soft furnishings and similar items in a let dwelling. The second is the ordinary revenue-repairs deduction for fixing what is already there. Keep those two clearly separate from capital allowances, and keep both separate again from <a href="/blog/section-24-and-tax-relief/landlord-tax-deductions-uk-2026-complete-list">finance-cost relief under Section 24</a>, which restricts mortgage interest to a basic-rate tax reducer and is a different regime entirely.</p>

<h2>Common parts of a block or HMO: the carve-out that does qualify</h2>

<p>The dwelling-house bar is narrower than it first looks. It removes plant for use <em>in a dwelling-house</em>, and the communal areas of a multi-unit building are not a dwelling-house. A communal boiler serving the whole block, a shared lift, stairwell and corridor lighting, a communal fire-alarm and entry system: these sit outside the dwellings, so they can qualify for AIA or writing-down allowances, and any integral features among them (a communal electrical system, for example) go into the special-rate pool under <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2001/2/section/33A">CAA 2001 s.33A</a>.</p>

<p>If you own a block of flats or a larger HMO, this is your practical claim base. The line to hold is precise: plant inside a let room or flat is barred; plant in genuinely communal space is not. For how to identify and apportion that communal plant, see the <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/hmo-common-parts-capital-allowances-s35-claim-mechanics-multi-tenant-property">s.35 common-parts claim mechanics for multi-tenant property</a>.</p>

<h2>Commercial property: the full claim base</h2>

<p>Commercial property is where capital allowances do real work. A UK property business holding offices, shops, warehouses or industrial units is carrying on a qualifying activity under s.15(1)(b), and because the space is not a dwelling, s.35 does not apply. You do not need the letting to be a trade for this to work, which corrects a common misconception: the property business itself is enough.</p>

<p>What qualifies in commercial space includes heating and ventilation, air conditioning and cooling, fire-alarm and security systems, sanitary fittings, kitchen equipment and certain electrical plant. The electrical and cold-water systems, space and water heating, lifts, escalators and external solar shading are <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/integral-features-capital-allowances">integral features</a> and sit in the special-rate pool at 6%. The bare shell, the structural works and the land never qualify as plant.</p>

<p>On the allowances themselves, the AIA gives a 100% deduction for qualifying plant up to a permanent £1,000,000 limit (<a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2001/2/section/51A">CAA 2001 s.51A(5)</a>, made permanent by Finance (No. 2) Act 2023 s.8). There is also a 40% first-year allowance on new and unused main-rate plant for expenditure on or after 1 January 2026 (Finance Act 2026 s.29, inserted as CAA 2001 s.45U), and companies have 100% full expensing on new main-rate plant under s.45S. To work out the amount once you know the space qualifies, see <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/capital-allowances-on-property">capital allowances on property</a>, the detail on the <a href="/blog/section-24-and-tax-relief/annual-investment-allowance-uk">Annual Investment Allowance</a>, and the commercial-specific list of <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/capital-allowances-commercial-property-what-can-claim">what you can claim on commercial property</a>.</p>

<p><strong>Worked example: a small retail unit.</strong> Say you let a small retail unit through an unincorporated property business and spend £40,000 fitting out qualifying plant and integral features. Because this is commercial, non-dwelling space, the spend is qualifying expenditure. The £40,000 sits comfortably within the £1,000,000 AIA limit, so you can claim the whole £40,000 as a 100% deduction in the year of spend, and the relief reduces your taxable profit at your marginal income tax rate. If part of the spend were better matched to the 40% first-year allowance or, for a company, to full expensing, the choice between them is a planning point worth taking advice on before you file the return.</p>

<aside>Not sure how much of your commercial fit-out qualifies? Speak to a property tax specialist before you file. Identifying integral features and allocating the AIA correctly is where most claims are won or lost.</aside>

<h2>Mixed-use property: how to apportion</h2>

<p>Mixed-use buildings need the cleanest record-keeping of all. You claim on the commercial part and not on the dwelling part, and where a single asset serves both, you apportion. The classic pattern is a shop on the ground floor with a let flat above. The shop is non-dwelling space, so plant there qualifies; the flat is a dwelling, so plant there is barred by s.35.</p>

<p>For assets that genuinely serve both, s.35(3) requires a just and reasonable apportionment, and HMRC expects a defensible basis: floor area is the usual starting point, heat output or metered consumption can be more accurate for a heating system. Document the basis at the time, not in hindsight.</p>

<p><strong>Worked example: a boiler serving both floors.</strong> Say you own a building with a shop on the ground floor and a let flat above, and you install a £12,000 boiler that heats both. If floor area splits the building 60% commercial and 40% residential, you claim allowances on £7,200 (the commercial proportion) and the £4,800 attributable to the flat is barred by s.35. The £7,200 can be covered by AIA in the year of spend.</p>

<h2>HMOs and serviced accommodation: the grey area after FHL abolition</h2>

<p>HMOs and serviced lets attract more confusion than any other category, partly because the furnished holiday let regime that some of them relied on no longer exists. Most HMOs and serviced lets are now ordinary property businesses, which means the s.35 bar applies to plant inside the let rooms while the common-parts carve-out still rescues communal plant.</p>

<p>The one route to a wider claim base is trade status. Where the level of service genuinely tips the activity into a trade (think a managed operation with daily cleaning, catering and concierge rather than a furnished let with a cleaner between bookings), the dwelling-house bar can fall away and the full plant base reopens. That is a fact-specific judgement, and the fact patterns are closely argued. Do not assume trade status because the property is marketed as serviced accommodation; take advice on the activity before claiming anything inside the lets.</p>

<h2>Former furnished holiday lets: what changed in April 2025</h2>

<p>The furnished holiday let regime was abolished by <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2025/8/schedule/5">Finance Act 2025 Schedule 5</a>, taking effect for income tax from 6 April 2025 and for corporation tax for accounting periods beginning on or after 1 April 2025. The standalone FHL qualifying activity in s.15 was removed, so a former FHL is now an ordinary residential property business and the s.35 dwelling-house bar applies in full. No new plant inside the dwelling qualifies.</p>

<p>What survives is the grandfathered position. Plant pools built up while the property was an FHL are carried into the ordinary property business and continue to be written down, so historic claims are not clawed back simply because the regime ended. Two consequences follow: you keep getting writing-down allowances on those existing pools, but new spend inside the dwelling is barred; and a later sale can still trigger a balancing charge on the grandfathered pools. If you ran an FHL up to abolition, the transitional treatment is worth reviewing rather than assuming the allowances simply disappeared.</p>

<h2>Leasehold and property improvements: does fitting out qualify?</h2>

<p>A commercial tenant or leaseholder who fits out leased space can claim capital allowances on leasehold property improvements, because the lessee incurs the expenditure on plant for its own qualifying activity. The plant and integral features the tenant installs qualify; the building shell and structural alterations do not, because s.21 (List A) and <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2001/2/section/22">s.22 (List B)</a> keep the fabric of the building out of plant. Where the work is new non-residential construction rather than plant, the Structures and Buildings Allowance can give 3% a year instead, on a separate basis.</p>

<p>The dwelling line holds here too. Improvements inside a residential dwelling, whether by landlord or tenant, remain barred by s.35. So a tenant fitting out a ground-floor shop has a claim; a landlord upgrading a let flat does not, and falls back to the replacement and repairs routes.</p>

<h2>Selling the property: balancing charges and the s.198 election</h2>

<p>Capital allowances do not end neatly at sale. A disposal that includes fixtures triggers disposal values on the plant pools under <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2001/2/section/61">CAA 2001 s.61</a> and the s.196 fixtures Table, which can produce a balancing charge clawing back allowances already claimed. For a commercial buyer to claim on the same fixtures, two gates must be passed: the seller must have pooled the expenditure, and the parties must fix its value, usually by a joint s.198 election made within two years of completion (the time limit is in CAA 2001 s.201). Miss those, and the buyer's expenditure is treated as nil under s.187A(3), stranding the allowances for the property's remaining life.</p>

<p>For the buyer-side and seller-side detail, including how to embed the election in the sale agreement, see our guide to the <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/commercial-property-fixtures-claim-s198-election-purchase-mechanics">s.198 fixtures election on purchase</a>.</p>
