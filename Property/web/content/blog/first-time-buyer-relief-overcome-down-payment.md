<p><strong>First-time buyer SDLT relief is a cost reduction at completion, not a deposit grant.</strong> The maximum cash saving is around £5,000 for a sole qualifying first-time buyer purchasing between £300,000 and £500,000. In isolation that is modest. In combination with a Lifetime ISA (25% government bonus on contributions up to £4,000 per year, useful for first homes up to £450,000) and a Help to Buy ISA (existing holders only; 25% bonus up to £3,000), the SDLT saving and the deposit-side schemes compound into a meaningful affordability lift. The trap is the £500,000 cliff: a purchase £2 above the cap loses £5,000 of saving. Honest expectation-setting beats over-promise.</p>

<p>When you are mid-affordability-calculation on your first home, the question that matters is blunt: how much cash does the SDLT first-time buyer relief actually put back in your pocket, and does it move the deposit you need. The honest answer is that it helps, but as a cost reduction at completion rather than a deposit grant, and only inside a tight set of conditions that can flip it against you if you trip the price cap or buy jointly with a partner who has owned before. Here is exactly how the relief translates into deposit availability, what it combines with, and where it stops. For the rate table and the 1 April 2025 reversion, see our applicable SDLT rates for first-time buyers page; for the full eligibility tests, our first-time buyer relief benefits and eligibility requirements page; for seven worked scenarios, our first-time buyer relief calculator.</p>

<h2>How the relief actually appears in your transaction</h2>

<p>SDLT is paid at completion via your conveyancer, who files the SDLT return on your behalf and pays the tax to HMRC out of the completion-monies. The first-time buyer relief reduces the SDLT figure on the return, which reduces the amount the conveyancer needs in the completion-monies, which reduces the cash you have to send before completion day.</p>

<p>For a £400,000 purchase by a sole qualifying first-time buyer, the SDLT figure on the return drops from £10,000 (standard residential rate table) to £5,000 (first-time buyer relief). The £5,000 difference stays in your account. If you were assembling £40,000 of deposit-plus-completion-costs from a single savings pot, you now need £35,000 instead of £40,000. The cash benefit is real but it is not a separate payment: it is a smaller line on the conveyancer's completion statement.</p>

<p>If you are running marginally short of your target (the usual pattern: deposit money is mostly in place, but SDLT, conveyancing fees and other completion costs are stretching it), the relief closes the gap. If you are materially short and the deposit itself is not yet assembled, the relief is one component of a wider affordability picture and needs to combine with deposit-side schemes and mortgage-product choice.</p>

<h2>The cash saving by purchase price</h2>

<p>The first-time buyer relief saving across the £0 to £500,000 band:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Below £125,000:</strong> SDLT is £0 under both the relief and the standard rate table; relief saves £0.</li>
<li><strong>£125,000 to £300,000:</strong> increasing saving as the standard table charges 2% on the portion above £125,000 and 5% on any portion above £250,000, while the relief gives the full £300,000 nil band. A £200,000 purchase saves £1,500; a £250,000 purchase saves £2,500; a £300,000 purchase saves £5,000.</li>
<li><strong>£300,000 to £500,000:</strong> saving plateaus at £5,000. Both the relief and the standard table charge 5% on the portion above £300,000, so additional consideration in this band produces equal SDLT under both regimes.</li>
<li><strong>£500,001 and above:</strong> relief unavailable (Schedule 6ZA paragraph 1(3) cap exceeded); saving drops to £0.</li>
</ul>

<p>The peak £5,000 figure is the realistic ceiling for the relief in isolation. The often-cited "up to £6,250" headline refers to the SDLT-under-relief on a £425,000 purchase (5% on £125,000), not the saving relative to the standard table. The saving figure is more honestly framed as £5,000 across the £300,000 to £500,000 band, scaling downward below £300,000.</p>

<h2>Combining the relief with a Lifetime ISA</h2>

<p>The Lifetime ISA (LISA) is a government-supported savings scheme designed for first home purchase or retirement. The headline parameters (verified at <a href="https://www.gov.uk/lifetime-isa">gov.uk/lifetime-isa</a>):</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>25% government bonus</strong> on annual contributions up to £4,000 (maximum £1,000 of bonus per year).</li>
<li><strong>Age conditions:</strong> you must open the account before you turn 40; you can keep contributing until you turn 50.</li>
<li><strong>First-home use:</strong> the property must be your first home and must cost £450,000 or less. The bonus is withheld (and a withdrawal charge applied) if the property exceeds £450,000.</li>
</ul>

<p>The LISA and the SDLT first-time buyer relief operate on different cash flows. The LISA bonus increases your deposit pot at completion (the bonus is paid via the conveyancer at completion). The SDLT relief reduces the SDLT cost at completion. Both work together without conflict, provided you satisfy both schemes' eligibility tests independently.</p>

<p>Worked illustration. You contribute £4,000 to a LISA in each of three years (£12,000 of contributions) and receive £3,000 of government bonus (£1,000 per year). At completion, the LISA pot is £15,000 plus any investment growth or interest. On a £400,000 purchase, the SDLT relief saves a further £5,000. Total cash benefit from the government schemes at completion: £8,000 (the LISA bonus plus the SDLT saving) on top of the £12,000 you saved. You have effectively turned £12,000 of saving into £20,000 of cash at completion through the two schemes combined.</p>

<h2>The Help to Buy ISA: existing holders only</h2>

<p>The Help to Buy ISA is the precursor scheme to the Lifetime ISA. It closed to new accounts on 30 November 2019. If you already hold one, you can keep saving (up to £200 per month) until November 2029 and claim the 25% government bonus on a first home purchase until November 2030. The headline parameters (verified at <a href="https://www.gov.uk/help-to-buy-isa">gov.uk/help-to-buy-isa</a>):</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>25% bonus</strong> on the savings balance, capped at £3,000 (so the maximum useful contribution is £12,000).</li>
<li><strong>Property-value caps:</strong> £250,000 outside London; £450,000 in London (lower than the Lifetime ISA's £450,000 universal cap; substantially lower than the SDLT relief's £500,000 cap).</li>
<li><strong>Bonus claim deadline:</strong> November 2030.</li>
</ul>

<p>If you hold a Help to Buy ISA, check the account balance and the deadlines before completion is finalised. The bonus is paid via the conveyancer at completion and needs co-ordination on timing. If you have both a Help to Buy ISA and a Lifetime ISA, you can only claim the government bonus from one of them at the first home purchase; the choice typically favours the Lifetime ISA where the property exceeds the Help to Buy ISA's lower caps, or where the Lifetime ISA balance is materially larger.</p>

<h2>The £500,000 cliff edge: deposit impact</h2>

<p>The Schedule 6ZA paragraph 1(3) £500,000 maximum chargeable consideration cap is a binary cliff. A purchase at exactly £500,000 attracts £10,000 of SDLT under the relief. A purchase at £500,001 attracts £15,000.05 of SDLT on the standard residential rate table; the relief is unavailable in full. The £2 increase in consideration produces a £5,000.10 jump in SDLT, all of which comes off your deposit or completion-cash position.</p>

<p>If you are stretching toward the cap, the cliff matters. A £505,000 purchase costs the same SDLT as the £500,001 purchase (around £15,250 versus £15,000.05) because the relief is unavailable in both cases. The cliff is not "the SDLT goes up by 1% as you cross £500,000"; it is "the SDLT jumps by £5,000 as you cross £500,000". Negotiating the purchase price down by £1 to £499,999 saves £5,000 of SDLT; negotiating it down by £6,000 to £494,000 saves only the standard-rate differential on those £6,000 plus the same £5,000 of relief-restoration.</p>

<p>Vendors and estate agents are not always alive to the cliff. If you are approaching the cap, model the SDLT figure at offer stage and treat it as part of the price negotiation, not as a separate completion expense. The cliff is also a useful check on borrowing capacity: a lender willing to fund a £505,000 purchase needs to be willing to fund the additional £5,000 of completion costs that come with it.</p>

<h2>The joint-purchase trap: when the relief turns negative</h2>

<p>The most common way the relief fails is the joint purchase with a partner who has previously owned residential property. The Schedule 6ZA paragraph 1(4) joint-purchaser rule requires every joint buyer to be a first-time buyer; your partner's prior ownership disqualifies the relief on the whole transaction. The position is worse where your partner still owns the prior property: the 5% additional dwellings surcharge under Schedule 4ZA applies on the whole joint purchase, worsening the SDLT cost.</p>

<p>On a £400,000 joint purchase where you qualify as a first-time buyer but your partner owns a buy-to-let property:</p>

<ul>
<li>0% + 5% surcharge on the first £125,000 = £6,250</li>
<li>2% + 5% surcharge on the £125,000 between £125,000 and £250,000 = £8,750</li>
<li>5% + 5% surcharge on the £150,000 between £250,000 and £400,000 = £15,000</li>
<li><strong>Total: £30,000</strong></li>
</ul>

<p>Compared with the £5,000 the same property would cost as a sole-qualifying-FTB purchase, the joint structure costs £25,000 of additional SDLT. The question moves from "how does the SDLT saving help my deposit?" to "how do I find an additional £25,000 of completion cash?" Where one of you holds existing property, this usually resolves one of three ways: the one who owns sells before the joint purchase; the first-time buyer purchases alone (subject to mortgage lender willingness, which is the usual blocker); or you accept the cost and proceed.</p>

<h2>The worldwide-ownership disqualification</h2>

<p>The Schedule 6ZA paragraph 6 first-time buyer test is a worldwide test. Any prior ownership of a major interest in a dwelling anywhere in the world disqualifies. If you inherited or owned property abroad before moving to the UK, or you still hold an overseas family-home interest, you are not a first-time buyer for SDLT purposes. The disqualification applies even where the prior interest was small and the consideration paid was nominal, and even where you have never owned UK property.</p>

<p>If you have a cross-border family or asset history, the disclosure needs to happen at conveyancing stage rather than at SDLT-return stage. An FTB claim that turns out to be ineligible on overseas-ownership grounds attracts the underpaid SDLT plus interest plus potentially penalties under FA 2007 Schedule 24 (0% to 30% for careless inaccuracies; up to 100% for deliberate and concealed inaccuracies). Written confirmation from any relevant overseas family members or advisers about your prior property history is sensible documentary support.</p>

<h2>The bottom-line deposit-availability picture</h2>

<p>The SDLT first-time buyer relief is one of several available cash levers. In isolation it produces a modest £0 to £5,000 saving. In combination with deposit-side government schemes (Lifetime ISA, Help to Buy ISA for existing holders), family help (cash gift, deposit loan), and mortgage-product choice (higher-LTV mortgages reducing required deposit; mortgage-guarantee-scheme products at 95% LTV), the SDLT saving compounds into a materially more useful affordability outcome.</p>

<p>A typical assembled position for a £400,000 purchase, where you are a sole first-time buyer aged 32:</p>

<ul>
<li>Three years of full LISA contributions = £12,000 saved plus £3,000 bonus = £15,000.</li>
<li>Ordinary savings over the same period = £20,000.</li>
<li>Family deposit gift = £15,000.</li>
<li>SDLT first-time buyer relief saving = £5,000.</li>
<li>Total available at completion = £55,000 (deposit, fees, SDLT cash).</li>
<li>Of which deposit = £50,000 (12.5% LTV, qualifying for 87.5%-LTV mortgage products).</li>
<li>SDLT under the relief = £5,000.</li>
<li>Other completion fees (conveyancing, searches, valuation) = roughly £2,000 to £3,000.</li>
</ul>

<p>The SDLT relief here represents around 9% of the total cash you assemble and around 50% of your total SDLT cost. Not transformative, but material.</p>

<h2>What to do next</h2>

<p>If you are planning your deposit and modelling the SDLT position, work through this:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Verify your FTB eligibility on the worldwide test.</strong> Any prior ownership of a major interest in a dwelling anywhere in the world disqualifies. Check with family members about jointly-held or inherited property abroad.</li>
<li><strong>Check the joint-purchase position with your partner.</strong> If your partner has previously owned property, the relief is unavailable on the joint purchase and the 5% surcharge may apply. Model the cost difference between a joint purchase and the partner-sells-first or you-buy-alone alternatives.</li>
<li><strong>Check your Lifetime ISA balance and the £450,000 property cap.</strong> Confirm the bonus claim mechanics with your LISA provider.</li>
<li><strong>Check any Help to Buy ISA balance and the £250,000 / £450,000 caps and the November 2030 bonus deadline.</strong> Existing holders only.</li>
<li><strong>Get an Agreement-in-Principle from a mortgage lender.</strong> Understand your borrowing capacity and the LTV bands available; this constrains the purchase price range you are realistically working within.</li>
<li><strong>Instruct a conveyancer who will handle the FTB-relief claim on the SDLT return.</strong> Relief code 32 on the SDLT1 (subject to confirmation by the conveyancer at the time of filing). The conveyancer also co-ordinates LISA / Help to Buy ISA bonus payment with completion timing.</li>
<li><strong>Model the SDLT figure at offer stage.</strong> Particularly if you are negotiating near the £500,000 cap, where a £1 cliff produces a £5,000 SDLT jump, or buying jointly with a non-FTB partner, where the £25,000+ surcharge-plus-no-relief cost can change the affordability picture.</li>
</ul>
