<p>For investors moving entire portfolios in a single deal, the most consequential SDLT lever left in the legislation is the six-dwellings rule in paragraph 7 of Schedule 6B FA 2003. Where six or more residential dwellings are acquired in a single transaction, the buyer can elect to be taxed at non-residential SDLT rates, and all three residential surcharges (the 5% additional dwellings surcharge, the 2% non-resident surcharge, and the 15% rate for non-natural persons over £500,000) fall away. The saving on a £3 million bulk acquisition can run to six figures.</p>

<p>The mechanics are simple. The traps are not. This page sets out the statutory framework, runs a worked saving against the residential alternative, and walks through the single-transaction tests that most often defeat the election in practice. For wider context on portfolio incorporation routes, see our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/sdlt-transfer-property-company-cost">SDLT on transferring property to a company</a> page.</p>

<h2>The legal mechanic: what paragraph 7 of Schedule 6B actually does</h2>

<p>The six-dwellings election sits in paragraph 7 of Schedule 6B to <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2003/14/contents">Finance Act 2003</a>. It gives a buyer who is acquiring six or more separate residential dwellings in a single transaction the right to elect that the dwellings are treated as non-residential for SDLT purposes. The election is made on the SDLT return for the transaction.</p>

<p>Two consequences follow from the non-residential characterisation:</p>

<ul>
<li>The non-residential SDLT bands apply (0% to £150,000; 2% from £150,001 to £250,000; 5% above £250,000) instead of the residential bands.</li>
<li>None of the residential surcharges apply. The 5% additional dwellings surcharge raised on 31 October 2024 under <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2024/12/contents">Finance (No. 2) Act 2024</a> does not bite. The 2% non-resident surcharge under Schedule 9A FA 2003 does not bite. The 15% rate for non-natural persons over £500,000 under Schedule 4A FA 2003 does not bite.</li>
</ul>

<p>Multiple Dwellings Relief, which used to be the standard portfolio-friendly route, was abolished for transactions with an effective date on or after 1 June 2024 by the same Finance (No. 2) Act 2024. The six-dwellings election survived that reform. It is now the principal mitigation route for buyers acquiring genuine bulk packages.</p>

<h2>The single-transaction test: where the rule most often fails</h2>

<p>The election requires a single transaction. The legislation, and HMRC's <a href="https://www.gov.uk/hmrc-internal-manuals/stamp-duty-land-tax-manual">SDLT Manual</a>, treat "single transaction" narrowly. The buyer's acquisition must take place under one contract (or one arrangement amounting to a single contract) and complete on a single effective date.</p>

<p>Two distinctions matter. The first is between a single transaction and a series of linked transactions. The second is between substantial performance and completion.</p>

<h3>Single transaction versus linked transactions</h3>

<p>Linked transactions, defined in section 108 FA 2003, are multiple transactions between the same vendor and purchaser (or persons connected with them) that form part of a single scheme, arrangement, or series. Linked transactions are aggregated for the purpose of working out SDLT rates: a portfolio sold as six separate transactions between the same parties is treated as if it were a single transaction at the combined consideration for rate-band purposes.</p>

<p>That aggregation does <strong>not</strong>, by itself, satisfy the six-dwellings rule. Six separately contracted sales of one dwelling each, completing on six different days under one over-arching deal, are linked transactions but are not a single transaction for the six-dwellings rule. The buyer pays residential SDLT including the 5% surcharge on each one, with the rates pushed up by linkage but no access to the non-residential election.</p>

<p>To qualify, the six (or more) dwellings must come together on the same effective date under one contract or scheme that resolves to a single completion event. In practice this means a single share-purchase-style sale and purchase agreement covering all properties, a single completion statement, and Land Registry transfers all dated identically.</p>

<h3>Substantial performance and off-plan acquisitions</h3>

<p>The effective date of a land transaction is the date of completion unless the contract is substantially performed earlier, in which case substantial performance triggers the effective date under section 44 FA 2003. Substantial performance occurs when the buyer takes possession of most of the subject matter, or pays substantially all the price, or (in lease cases) any rent is paid.</p>

<p>This matters for off-plan bulk acquisitions. A forward-purchase contract for nine flats in a development under construction can be substantially performed when 90% of the consideration is paid and possession is taken of, say, three completed flats. The effective date is that date, and the SDLT is computed on the full £X million consideration at that point, with the six-dwellings election available. Where staged payments are structured so that substantial performance cannot be identified on a single date, the position is less clean and HMRC may argue separate completions defeat the election.</p>

<h2>Worked saving: a £3 million acquisition of nine flats</h2>

<p>To pin down the financial significance, consider a UK property investor company acquiring a block of nine self-contained flats from a developer for £3 million on a single sale and purchase agreement, completing on 14 September 2026. Each flat sits in its own self-contained unit with its own kitchen and bathroom. The buyer is UK-resident.</p>

<p>The two SDLT possibilities sit at very different price points.</p>

<p>The election saves £284,250, or roughly 9.5% of the purchase price. The saving scales with consideration: at £6m the residential alternative pushes nearly the entire price through the top 17% effective slab, while the non-residential ceiling at 5% does not. For limited-company buyers the saving widens further because companies pay the 5% surcharge on every residential purchase, regardless of whether they own other property.</p>

<h2>What counts as a dwelling: the boundary cases</h2>

<p>A separate dwelling for SDLT is a unit suitable for use as a single dwelling, with self-contained facilities for sleeping, washing and food preparation, not shared with another household. The same definition that drives the rates in section 116 also drives the unit count for the six-dwellings election.</p>

<p>The boundary cases that recur in practice:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Granny annexes</strong>: a self-contained annexe with its own entrance, kitchen and bathroom qualifies as a separate dwelling. A shared-kitchen lodger annexe does not.</li>
<li><strong>Houses in multiple occupation (HMOs)</strong>: an HMO is typically a single dwelling for SDLT purposes, even though it houses multiple tenants. The presence of shared kitchen, bathroom or living facilities means the rooms are not self-contained units. A block of six HMOs (each itself a single dwelling) does qualify; six rooms in one HMO does not.</li>
<li><strong>Studio blocks</strong>: a purpose-built block of self-contained studios, each with its own facilities and lockable entrance, qualifies as multiple dwellings.</li>
<li><strong>Mixed-use packages</strong>: a transaction comprising residential dwellings and non-residential property (commercial premises, agricultural land) is mixed-use and taxed at non-residential rates automatically. The six-dwellings election is irrelevant because the transaction is already non-residential.</li>
<li><strong>Partly constructed dwellings</strong>: a unit under construction at the effective date can still count where construction has substantially started and the unit is identifiable, but a paper allocation across an undefined site will not.</li>
</ul>

<h2>The traps that most often defeat the election</h2>

<p>From the bulk acquisitions we sense-check before contracts are signed, the same handful of issues defeat the election repeatedly.</p>

<p><strong>Trap 1: split into two transactions.</strong> A buyer wants flexibility to drop out of part of the package, so the deal is structured as two contracts, one for four flats and one for five. Each contract on its own falls below six. The election is unavailable on either contract. The single-transaction discipline must hold from start to finish.</p>

<p><strong>Trap 2: separate completion dates.</strong> Six dwellings are sold under one over-arching agreement, but each individual property completes on a different day to accommodate tenant turnover or mortgage drawdown. There is no single effective date for the package. HMRC's view is the six-dwellings rule does not apply. Substantial performance can sometimes rescue this where a single substantial-performance event covers the whole package, but the position is fact-sensitive.</p>

<p><strong>Trap 3: one of the units fails the dwelling test.</strong> The package is six properties, but one is an unfit shell with no kitchen or bathroom at the effective date. HMRC treats the shell as non-residential land at the effective date and the package as five dwellings. The election is unavailable.</p>

<p><strong>Trap 4: same vendor, different purchasers.</strong> A husband and wife each buy three properties from the same developer at the same time, structured as separate transactions for each spouse. Neither spouse has acquired six. The election applies to the purchaser, not to the vendor. If the spouses had bought all six together as joint purchasers under one contract, the election would have been available.</p>

<p><strong>Trap 5: SDLT return filed without the election.</strong> The buyer's conveyancer files the SDLT return on the assumption of residential treatment and pays the surcharge. The election is not made. Amendment is available within 12 months of the filing date; overpayment relief under paragraph 34 Schedule 10 FA 2003 is available within four years of the effective date but is harder to land.</p>

<p><strong>Trap 6: artificial unit creation.</strong> A six-pack assembled by splitting a single large dwelling into six notional units, or by adding three low-value studios to a three-house deal to clear the threshold, is at risk of challenge under SDLT anti-avoidance rules. HMRC's published view is that the election is for genuine commercial bulk acquisitions; engineered packages will be unwound.</p>

<h2>How the election is made on the SDLT return</h2>

<p>The SDLT return for the transaction (form SDLT1, filed by the buyer or their conveyancer within 14 days of the effective date) records the property type as non-residential under the six-dwellings rule. The election is reflected by the rate code applied, not by a separate tick-box election. HMRC's <a href="https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/stamp-duty-land-tax-online-and-paper-returns">SDLT return guidance</a> sets out the codes; the practical effect is that the return is filed at non-residential bands with no surcharge.</p>

<p>Late-running amendments to elect, where the original return was filed at residential rates, follow the standard SDLT amendment route within 12 months of the original filing date. Refunds where the original return overpaid the surcharge are processed through the same routes as any other SDLT refund. Beyond 12 months, overpayment relief under paragraph 34 Schedule 10 FA 2003 may be available within four years of the effective date but is subject to closer HMRC scrutiny.</p>

<h2>Choosing between the election and other portfolio routes</h2>

<p>The election is one route. For an existing portfolio being incorporated into a limited company, partnership incorporation under paragraph 10 Schedule 15 FA 2003 typically delivers a better answer because chargeable consideration is reduced to nil where the partnership is genuine and pre-existing. For a new acquisition where the buyer happens to take six or more, the election is usually the cleaner answer.</p>

<thead>
<tr><th>Scenario</th><th>Likely best route</th><th>Why</th></tr>
</thead>
<tbody>
<tr><td>New bulk acquisition from a developer or third party (6+ units, single deal)</td><td>Six-dwellings election</td><td>Simple, no partnership history needed, clean filing</td></tr>
<tr><td>Existing personally-held portfolio moving to limited company</td><td>Partnership incorporation under Sch 15 para 10 FA 2003</td><td>Reduces chargeable consideration to nil; election would still cost full non-residential SDLT</td></tr>
<tr><td>Pre-completion onward sale (buyer assigns contract before completion)</td><td>Sub-sale relief under s.45 FA 2003</td><td>SDLT lands only on the ultimate buyer's transaction</td></tr>
<tr><td>Mixed-use package (residential + commercial in one deal)</td><td>Mixed-use rates automatically</td><td>Non-residential rates apply without needing the election</td></tr>
<tr><td>Five or fewer residential dwellings</td><td>Residential rates + 5% surcharge</td><td>Election unavailable; consider whether timing or scoping the deal upwards makes commercial sense</td></tr>

<p>The decision is not just about the SDLT bill on day one. Partnership incorporation has CGT and IHT consequences that the six-dwellings election does not. The election leaves the buyer holding residential properties for ATED, CGT and income tax purposes, despite being taxed at non-residential rates on entry.
