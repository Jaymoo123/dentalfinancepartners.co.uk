    answer: "There is no statutory minimum for a private limited company under the Companies Act 2006; the company's articles set the cadence.   - question: "Which decisions must be minuted contemporaneously to defend the tax position?"
    answer: "Five categories of decision are tax-load-bearing and must be minuted at the time they are made. First, dividend declarations: dated and signed BEFORE the cash moves to the shareholder, with the declaration referencing the distributable-reserves test under section 830 of the Companies Act 2006. Second, share issues and allotments: board minutes documenting the section 551 allotment authority and any section 561 statutory-pre-emption disapplication. Third, property acquisitions and disposals: board approval, with reference to any reserved-matters list in the articles. Fourth, directors' loan account write-offs or restructuring: minuted at the date of decision with the section 455 timing implications considered. Fifth, preference share redemption: minuted at the date of decision with the redemption source (distributable reserves under section 687, or proceeds of a fresh share issue) identified. Post-dating any of these minutes destroys their evidential value at an HMRC enquiry."
  - question: "Does a sole-director property FIC need to hold formal board meetings?"
    answer: "Yes. The Companies Act 2006 treats a company with a single director as still requiring board decisions to be taken in compliance with the articles, and section 248 requires every meeting of directors (including a sole director acting alone) to be minuted, with the minutes retained for at least 10 years. The sole-director FIC's board meeting is a meeting of one. The minute documents: the date, the decisions taken, any declarations of interest under section 177, and the signature of the director. The minute carries the same evidential weight as a multi-director board minute. The discipline matters precisely because sole-director FICs are the most exposed to the substance-over-form challenge: with no second director to provide procedural rhythm, the only protection against the founder operating the FIC as personal property is the contemporaneous minute record."
  - question: "What is the difference between a board resolution and a shareholders' resolution?"
    answer: "A board resolution is taken by the directors and concerns the company's day-to-day operation: dividend declarations, share issues within an existing allotment authority, property acquisitions within the directors' powers, contractor appointments, banking arrangements. A shareholders' resolution is taken by the shareholders in general meeting and concerns matters reserved to the shareholders by the Companies Act or by the articles: articles amendments (special resolution, 75% under section 21), allotment authorities granted to directors under section 551, statutory pre-emption disapplication under section 567, removal of directors under section 168, and winding-up under the Insolvency Act 1986. A property FIC will typically run more board resolutions than shareholder resolutions; the shareholder resolutions cluster around the annual confirmation cycle and around growth-share issuance moments."
  - question: "When can a property FIC use written resolutions instead of meetings?"
    answer: "A private limited company may use written resolutions of shareholders under sections 288 to 300 of the Companies Act 2006. The mechanism requires the resolution to be circulated to every eligible member with the proposed wording; ordinary resolutions need a simple majority of total voting rights to pass (not just of those replying), and special resolutions need 75% of total voting rights. The 28-day deemed-acceptance window applies. Written resolutions are useful for routine shareholder approvals in a multi-shareholder property FIC where physical meetings are impractical, and are the standard mechanism for the annual confirmation-statement-period decisions. Note: written resolutions cannot remove a director under section 168 or remove an auditor under section 510; those require a physical or fairly-noticed meeting.   - question: "What goes in the statutory registers and how often must they be updated?"
    answer: "Sections 113 to 128 of the Companies Act 2006 require every private limited company to maintain a register of members, a register of directors and a register of directors' residential addresses, and a register of secretaries (where one is appointed). Section 790M and following require a register of people with significant control (PSCs). Section 859A requires a register of charges where the company has granted security over its assets. The registers must be kept at the registered office (or a single alternative inspection location notified to Companies House) and updated within a reasonable time of any change.   - question: "Why does HMRC scrutinise the dividend declaration timing?"
    answer: "HMRC's Company Taxation Manual at CTM15205 onwards is explicit that a dividend is only validly paid when it has been properly declared, and that the declaration must precede the payment. Where the company pays cash to a shareholder before the directors have declared the dividend, the cash transfer is potentially a directors' loan account credit (if the recipient is a director) or an unlawful distribution (if not). A directors' loan account credit can then be subject to section 455 CTA 2010 if it is later treated as a loan and not repaid within 9 months of year-end; an unlawful distribution can be recovered by the company from the recipient under section 847 CA 2006 and gives rise to a benefit-in-kind charge or an income tax assessment depending on the facts. The discipline is to declare the dividend in a board minute (dated, signed) before the cash moves. The minute references the distributable reserves available at the date of declaration (typically by reference to the most recent management accounts), specifies the class, the rate per share, and the total."
  - question: "What declarations of interest does the founder of a property FIC need to make?"
    answer: "Section 177 of the Companies Act 2006 requires a director to declare to the other directors (or to the company in writing if the director is the only director) the nature and extent of any direct or indirect interest in a proposed transaction or arrangement with the company. For a property FIC the founder typically has multiple recurring interests: any rent paid by the FIC to the founder for property the founder owns personally, any rent paid by the founder to the FIC for property the founder occupies that is owned by the FIC, any loan between the founder and the FIC, any sale of property between the founder's personal portfolio and the FIC, and any contract awarded to a family-controlled service provider. The declaration can be a standing notice under section 185 (declared once, then refreshed annually) or a case-by-case notice. The records discipline: the declaration sits in the minute of the board meeting where the relevant transaction is approved, and the company's register of directors' interests (where maintained) records the standing notices."
  - question: "What happens if HMRC challenges the FIC on substance-over-form grounds?"
    answer: "The challenge typically opens as a Schedule 36 information notice requesting the company's board minutes, statutory registers, bank records, and the founder's personal bank records for the period under enquiry. The Revenue is testing whether the operational record matches the structural form. Where the records show contemporaneous discipline (board minutes dated before cash movements, declarations of interest minuted at each related-party transaction, dividend declarations referencing the distributable-reserves test), the structural defence holds. Where the records show drift (post-dated minutes, founder personal expenditure paid from the FIC account, rent receipts not matched by declared dividends, board decisions taken without minutes), the Revenue applies the substance-over-form analysis: distributions re-characterised as personal income, settlements legislation attribution applied to spouse and adult-child dividend income, and potentially the General Anti-Abuse Rule under Finance Act 2013 Schedule 43 if the structure as operated looks like a sham. The remedies on an adverse finding can run to tens or hundreds of thousands across multiple tax years, with penalties under Schedule 24 FA 2007 for inaccuracies."
  - question: "How should the property FIC's banking arrangements be set up?"
    answer: "A separate company bank account in the FIC's name, with no comingling of founder personal funds or expenditure. This is the single most operationally load-bearing discipline. The bank account receives rental income directly (with the tenancy agreement naming the FIC as landlord), pays property expenses (mortgage interest, insurance, maintenance, agent fees), and pays dividends to shareholders by separate transfers from the FIC account to each shareholder's personal account. The founder's personal account receives only declared dividends, declared salary if applicable, and any directors' loan account repayments. The bank statements are the second-most-important evidential record after the minute book; HMRC's first request at any FIC enquiry is the bank statements for the period under enquiry. Any personal expenditure paid from the FIC account creates either a benefit-in-kind charge (if the founder is a director) or an overdrawn directors' loan account (with section 455 CTA 2010 implications). The discipline is absolute: FIC funds pay FIC expenses; founder funds pay founder expenses; the routes between them are minuted dividends, minuted DLA movements, and minuted salary."

<p>A well-articled FIC that is badly governed is a paper structure. The articles set out the founder's control mechanism, the share-class economic split, the reserved-matters list, the pre-emption cascade, and the entrenched provisions. None of that survives a substance-over-form analysis if the operational record shows the founder running the FIC as personal property: rent receipts paid into a personal account, expenditure paid from the company account without a minuted decision, dividend declarations dated after the cash has moved, and board meetings either not held or minuted retrospectively. HMRC's substance-over-form analysis lets the Revenue re-characterise distributions as personal income, collapse the settlements-legislation defences for the spouse and adult-child dividend stream, undermine any Business Property Relief argument on death, and apply the General Anti-Abuse Rule where the structure as operated looks like a sham.</p>

<p>This page is the operational discipline that holds the structure up. It walks the board-meeting cadence, identifies the five categories of decision that must be minuted contemporaneously, sets out the written-resolution mechanism under sections 288 to 300 of the Companies Act 2006, identifies the statutory registers that need to be maintained, walks the declarations-of-interest discipline under section 177, and lays out an annual operating rhythm for a property FIC. Out of scope: the articles drafting layer (see our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/fic-articles-of-association-property-control-mechanics">FIC articles of association page</a>), the share-class economic mechanics (see our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/fic-growth-shares-and-freezer-shares-design">growth-share design page</a>), and the income-drawdown question (covered separately in the FIC retirement income page).</p>

<h2>Substance over form: the failure mode you are defending against</h2>

<p>The substance-over-form analysis is older than the modern FIC. The leading authorities run back to Ramsay v IRC [1981] STC 174 and onward through W.T. Ramsay v IRC, Furniss v Dawson, and most recently Eclipse Film Partners (HMRC v Eclipse Film Partners No 35 LLP [2016] STC 1385). The principle: where the legal form of an arrangement does not match its economic substance, HMRC and the courts can disregard the form and apply tax on the substance. For a property FIC the live risk is what we call the company-within-a-person pattern. The founder owns 100% of the voting class, holds the preference class, controls the board through being sole director or chair-with-casting-vote, and treats the company's rental income, expenditure decisions, and property transactions as personal. The substance-over-form challenge is then that the FIC is the founder's personal property in corporate clothing.</p>

<p>The consequences on a successful HMRC challenge are layered. First, distributions can be re-characterised as personal income, taxed at marginal rates rather than dividend rates, with no dividend-allowance benefit. Second, the settlements legislation under <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2005/5/section/624">ITTOIA 2005 section 624</a> can attribute spouse and adult-child dividends back to the founder, collapsing the income-splitting benefit that the alphabet-share design was meant to deliver. Third, any Business Property Relief argument on the founder's death is undermined; the BPR investment-line test from Pawson v HMRC [2013] UKUT 050 (TCC) is already hostile to property-investment FICs (see our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/fic-iht-treatment-bpr-myth">FIC IHT and BPR myth page</a> for the substantive position), and a poorly-governed operational record makes the position worse. Fourth, the General Anti-Abuse Rule under Finance Act 2013 Schedule 43 sits as a backstop where the structure is technically valid but operationally a sham.</p>

<p>The defence is not the articles. The articles can be perfect and the company can still fail the substance-over-form analysis if it is not operated as a real company. The defence is the contemporaneous operational record: board minutes, declarations of interest, statutory registers, and the bank-account discipline that keeps founder funds and company funds separately accounted for.</p>

<h2>The directors' duty foundation: Companies Act 2006 sections 171 to 177</h2>

<p>Every director of a UK limited company is subject to the codified directors' duties in sections 171 to 177 of the Companies Act 2006. For a founder who is sole director of a property FIC, these are not formalities; they are the basis on which the operational record is judged.</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong><a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2006/46/section/171">Section 171 (act within powers).</a></strong> The director must act in accordance with the company's constitution and exercise powers only for proper purposes. For a property FIC, this means decisions taken inside the powers given by the articles, with any reserved-matters list properly respected.</li>
  <li><strong><a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2006/46/section/172">Section 172 (promote success of company).</a></strong> Decisions must be taken in the way the director considers, in good faith, will be most likely to promote the success of the company for the benefit of its members as a whole. A founder treating the FIC as a personal vehicle for cashflow optimisation fails this duty.</li>
  <li><strong>Section 173 (independent judgement).</strong> The director must exercise independent judgement.</li>
  <li><strong>Section 174 (reasonable care, skill, and diligence).</strong> The objective standard plus the director's actual knowledge, skill, and experience.</li>
  <li><strong>Section 175 (avoid conflicts of interest).</strong> Conflicts must be avoided or properly authorised by the other directors or by shareholder resolution.</li>
  <li><strong>Section 176 (no third-party benefits).</strong> The director must not accept benefits from third parties for acting as a director.</li>
  <li><strong>Section 177 (declare interest in proposed transactions).</strong> The most operationally important duty for a property FIC; covered separately below.</li>
</ul>

<p>The duties bite even where the founder is the sole director and the sole shareholder. The Companies Act does not exempt single-director-and-shareholder companies from compliance; the test is whether the company has been operated as a company, not whether anyone other than the founder cares.</p>

<h2>Board meeting cadence: how often, and what each meeting covers</h2>

<p>The Companies Act sets no statutory minimum frequency for board meetings of a private limited company; the cadence is set by the articles. The Model Articles default mentions no specific frequency. For a property FIC, quarterly is the practical floor. The reasons are operational rather than legal: the company has cash movements (rent in, expenses out, dividends declared and paid) that need to be approved against the contemporaneous management accounts, and a year-end-only meeting cannot credibly cover a year of decisions.</p>

<p>A typical property FIC quarterly meeting covers:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Review of the quarter's rental receipts and a check that they are reflected in the company bank account and the management accounts.</li>
  <li>Approval of any property-related decisions taken in the quarter (acquisitions, disposals, financing changes, capital expenditure decisions).</li>
  <li>Approval of any related-party transactions, with the relevant director's section 177 declaration of interest minuted.</li>
  <li>Review of the distributable-reserves position and the dividend declaration for the quarter (if any).</li>
  <li>Statutory-registers refresh check.</li>
  <li>Any other business arising.</li>
</ul>

<p>A sole-director property FIC still holds and minutes these meetings; the meeting is a meeting of one. The minute records the date, the decisions, any declarations of interest, and is signed by the director. Section 248 of the Companies Act requires the minutes to be retained for at least 10 years.</p>

<h2>The five categories of decision that must be minuted contemporaneously</h2>

<p>The contemporaneous-record test is what HMRC applies at enquiry. A minute created after a decision was taken, or after a cash movement happened, has materially less evidential weight than a minute dated and signed at the time. Five categories of decision are tax-load-bearing for a property FIC and must be minuted at the time they are made.</p>

<p><strong>1. Dividend declarations.</strong> The minute is dated and signed before the cash moves. <a href="https://www.gov.uk/hmrc-internal-manuals/company-taxation-manual/ctm15205">HMRC's Company Taxation Manual at CTM15205</a> is explicit on the timing. The minute references the distributable-reserves test under <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2006/46/section/830">section 830 CA 2006</a> (typically by reference to the most recent management accounts), specifies the class of share, the rate per share, and the total amount. Where the FIC has alphabet-share classes (see our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/alphabet-shares-property-spv-dividend-splitting-spouse-children">alphabet shares page</a> for the settlements-legislation boundary analysis), the class-by-class allocation is minuted with the discretionary justification on its face.</p>

<p><strong>2. Share issues and allotments.</strong> The board minute documents the section 551 allotment authority being exercised, any section 561 statutory-pre-emption disapplication being relied on (via the articles disapplication or a section 567 shareholders' resolution), the class issued, the number of shares, the consideration, and the recipient. For a growth-share tranche issued to next-generation holders or to a trust, the minute also references the valuation supporting the issue price (typically a contemporaneous specialist valuation report).</p>

<p><strong>3. Property acquisitions and disposals.</strong> Board approval pre-completion. The minute references any reserved-matters threshold in the articles, identifies the seller or buyer (with any connected-party status declared under section 177), records the consideration, the financing arrangement, and the post-completion statutory-registers update (typically the register of charges if the property is mortgaged).</p>

<p><strong>4. Directors' loan account decisions.</strong> Movements on the DLA above an immateriality threshold should be minuted: loans from the founder to the company, loans from the company to the founder (with the section 455 CTA 2010 9-month deadline considered), write-offs, and any interest applied. Where the DLA is in credit balance (the founder's incorporation-transfer loan), the minute records the planned repayment schedule and the timing of each repayment. Where the DLA is in debit balance (the founder has overdrawn the loan), the minute records the date by which it must be repaid to avoid the section 455 charge.</p>

<p><strong>5. Preference share redemption.</strong> The minute records the date of decision, the source (distributable reserves under <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2006/46/section/687">section 687 CA 2006</a> or proceeds of a fresh share issue under section 688), the price (par plus any cumulative unpaid dividend), and the post-redemption capital position. The redemption notice to the holder and the company's statutory-registers update both reference the minute.</p>

<h2>Written resolutions: the section 288 mechanism</h2>

<p>Sections 288 to 300 of the Companies Act 2006 let a private limited company pass shareholder resolutions in writing. The mechanism is useful for routine approvals where a physical meeting is impractical. The procedure: the company circulates the proposed resolution to every eligible member with the wording specified; ordinary resolutions need a simple majority of total voting rights to pass (not just of those replying), and special resolutions need 75% of total voting rights. A 28-day deemed-acceptance window applies; a member who does not reply within the window is treated as not having signed, so the resolution fails if insufficient signatures are received in time.</p>

<p>Two categories of resolution cannot be passed in writing. <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2006/46/section/168">Section 168</a> removal of a director requires a meeting with special notice (so that the director can speak in their defence). Removal of an auditor under section 510 has parallel procedural protections. Beyond those, the standard FIC approvals (allotment authorities, articles amendments, share-class variations) can run as written resolutions where the shareholder count is small enough to make the mechanism practical.</p>

<p>Board resolutions can also be passed in writing under the company's articles if every director signs. The Model Articles include a written-resolution provision for directors; bespoke articles typically replicate or vary it. For a sole-director FIC, every board decision is in effect a written decision of one, but the minute record discipline still applies.</p>

<h2>Statutory registers: what to maintain and where</h2>

<p>Sections 113 to 128 of the Companies Act 2006 require every private limited company to maintain a set of statutory registers. For a property FIC the relevant set is:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Register of members</strong> (section 113): name, address, class of shares held, number of shares held, date of entry. Updates on every issue, transfer, and class variation.</li>
  <li><strong>Register of directors</strong> (section 162) and <strong>register of directors' residential addresses</strong> (section 165). Updates on appointment, resignation, or change of details.</li>
  <li><strong>Register of secretaries</strong> (section 275) where a company secretary is appointed (private companies are not required to appoint one).</li>
  <li><strong>Register of people with significant control (PSC)</strong> (section 790M). For most property FICs the founder is a PSC; the register also captures any other family member or trust holding 25% or more of voting rights or shares. Updates on every change in PSC status.</li>
  <li><strong>Register of charges</strong> (section 859A) where the FIC has granted security. For a mortgaged property FIC, the register of charges updates on every property acquisition with a mortgage and on any subsequent refinancing.</li>
</ul>

<p>The registers are kept at the registered office or at a single alternative inspection location notified to Companies House. Updates must be made within a reasonable time of the underlying change (the Act does not specify a fixed deadline, but practice is days, not weeks). The Companies House public record also maintains its own register, populated by the company's filings (confirmation statement Form CS01, person-with-significant-control filings, charge registrations); the company's own statutory registers and the Companies House public record need to align. A discrepancy between the two is a common HMRC enquiry trigger.</p>

<p>Statutory-registers drift is one of the most common operational failures we see at FIC review.</p>
<p>If your FIC's registers have not been reviewed in the past 12 months, a structured registers refresh against the Companies House public record is an inexpensive way to close gaps before they surface at enquiry.</p>

<h2>Declarations of interest: the section 177 discipline</h2>

<p>Section 177 of the Companies Act 2006 requires a director to declare to the other directors (or to the company in writing if the director is the only director) the nature and extent of any direct or indirect interest in a proposed transaction or arrangement with the company. For a property FIC the founder typically has multiple recurring interests:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Any rent paid by the FIC to the founder personally for property the founder owns and lets to the company.</li>
  <li>Any rent paid by the founder to the FIC for property the founder occupies that the FIC owns.</li>
  <li>Any loan between the founder and the FIC (in either direction).</li>
  <li>Any sale of property between the founder's personal portfolio and the FIC.</li>
  <li>Any contract awarded by the FIC to a family-controlled service provider (letting agency, maintenance contractor, property manager).</li>
  <li>Any dividend declared on the founder's share class.</li>
</ul>

<p>The declaration can be a standing notice under section 185, given once and refreshed annually, or a case-by-case notice given at the board meeting where the relevant transaction is approved. Most property FICs run a hybrid: a standing notice for the recurring categories (the founder's preference dividend, the founder's A-class dividend, any continuing rental relationship), refreshed annually at the post-year-end board meeting, plus case-by-case notices for one-off transactions (a specific property sale, a specific loan).</p>

<p>The minute of the board meeting where each declaration is given records the date, the nature and extent of the interest, and the resolution that the interested director's vote is or is not counted on the approving resolution. The company's register of directors' interests (where maintained) holds the standing notices. For a sole-director FIC, the declaration is given to the company in writing under section 177(4) and held in the minute book.</p>

<h2>Banking discipline: separation as the operational backbone</h2>

<p>A separate company bank account in the FIC's name, with no comingling of founder personal funds or expenditure, is the single most operationally load-bearing discipline. The account receives rental income directly (with the tenancy agreement naming the FIC as landlord), pays property expenses (mortgage interest, insurance, maintenance, agent fees), and pays dividends to shareholders by separate transfers to each shareholder's personal account. The founder's personal account receives only declared dividends, declared salary if applicable, and any directors' loan account repayments.</p>

<p>The bank statements are the second-most-important evidential record after the minute book. HMRC's first request at any FIC enquiry is the bank statements for the period under enquiry. The Revenue checks for:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Personal expenditure paid from the FIC account (benefit-in-kind charge under ITEPA 2003 or overdrawn DLA under section 455 CTA 2010).</li>
  <li>Rent receipts that are not matched by minuted bookkeeping (potential undisclosed income).</li>
  <li>Cash transfers to the founder's personal account that do not correspond to minuted dividend declarations or DLA movements (potential unlawful distribution under section 847 CA 2006).</li>
  <li>Family-member receipts that do not correspond to minuted dividend declarations on the relevant share class (potential settlements-legislation attribution).</li>
</ul>

<p>The discipline is absolute. FIC funds pay FIC expenses; founder funds pay founder expenses. The routes between them are minuted dividends, minuted DLA movements, minuted salary, and minuted preference share redemption. Personal mixing is the single fastest route to a substance-over-form re-characterisation.</p>

<h2>The annual rhythm: a property FIC's operating cycle</h2>

<p>A property FIC's governance calendar runs on a quarterly base cycle with trigger-event meetings additionally. The annual rhythm:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Q1 (post-year-end).</strong> Year-end accounts approval (board meeting; minute references the figures and the company's distributable-reserves position). Ratification of any interim dividends declared in the prior year. Statutory-registers refresh (members, PSC, charges all reviewed against the prior year's transactions). Planning of any current-year growth-share issues. Pre-9-month-after-year-end review of any debit DLA balances against the section 455 deadline.</li>
  <li><strong>Q2.</strong> Confirmation statement to Companies House (Form CS01) within 14 days of the confirmation date. Annual board strategy review (portfolio composition, financing position, family circumstances changes that may affect the share-class architecture). Declarations-of-interest annual refresh (standing notices reviewed and re-signed).</li>
  <li><strong>Q3.</strong> Mid-year property portfolio review (occupancy, maintenance schedule, lettings discipline check, any tenant issues). Interim dividend declaration if reserves permit. Mid-year management accounts review.</li>
  <li><strong>Q4.</strong> Pre-year-end dividend planning across share classes (the dividend mix that lands within each shareholder's intended marginal rate for the tax year). Share-issue planning if a new growth-share tranche is contemplated for year-end. Preference share redemption decisions if relevant. Pre-year-end review of the DLA positions and the CT-deductibility planning.</li>
</ul>

<p>Trigger-event meetings additionally: a property acquisition or disposal, a refinancing, a related-party transaction outside the standing-notice scope, a change in family circumstances that affects the share-class composition (a marriage, a divorce, a death, the birth of an additional child), or any matter on the reserved-matters list that requires founder consent.</p>

<aside>
<p>A quarterly governance cycle plus trigger-event meetings is the rhythm that holds a property FIC up across the structural horizon.</p>
<p>For families using a FIC for a UK property portfolio and looking for an external check on the operational record, we can scope an annual governance review at a fixed-fee basis.</p>
</aside>

<h2>What HMRC actually looks at on enquiry</h2>

<p>An HMRC FIC enquiry typically opens as a Schedule 36 information notice. The Revenue requests the company's minute book, statutory registers, bank statements, management accounts, dividend vouchers, and any contemporaneous correspondence on related-party transactions. The founder's personal bank statements are also requested for the period under enquiry, to check the alignment between the FIC's records and the founder's personal cash flows.</p>

<p>Where the records show contemporaneous discipline (board minutes dated before cash movements, declarations of interest minuted at each related-party transaction, dividend declarations referencing the distributable-reserves test, bank-account separation maintained throughout, statutory registers up to date), the structural defence holds. The substance-over-form analysis has nothing to bite on; the form and the substance match.</p>

<p>Where the records show drift (post-dated minutes, founder personal expenditure paid from the FIC account, rent receipts not matched by declared dividends, board decisions taken without minutes, statutory-registers out of step with the Companies House public record), the Revenue applies the substance-over-form analysis. Distributions are re-characterised, settlements-legislation attribution applied, and the General Anti-Abuse Rule potentially invoked. The remedies on an adverse finding can run to tens or hundreds of thousands across multiple tax years, with penalties under Schedule 24 FA 2007 for inaccuracies. The single best protection is the contemporaneous record built up across the years of the FIC's life.</p>
