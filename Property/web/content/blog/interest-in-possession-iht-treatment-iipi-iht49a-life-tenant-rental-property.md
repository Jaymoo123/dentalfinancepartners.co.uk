faqs:
  - question: "What changed on 22 March 2006 in the IIP regime?"
    answer: "The Finance Act 2006 (Schedule 20) substantially narrowed the IHT treatment of interest-in-possession trusts. Pre-22-March-2006 IIPs were within the original IHTA 1984 s.49(1) regime: the IIP holder was treated as beneficially entitled to the underlying property for IHT, with the property falling into their death estate at death market value. Post-22-March-2006 IIPs are within the relevant property regime by default (10-year periodic charge under s.64 up to 6%, exit charges under s.65 on capital distributions, entry charge of 20% on excess over NRB on settlement). The s.49(1A) carve-outs preserve the original s.49(1) read-through for three specific case types: IPDIs (s.49A), transitional serial interests (ss.49B-49E), and disabled persons' interests (s.89B). Pre-2006 IIPs in being on 22 March 2006 are grandfathered into the original s.49(1) treatment for their life; on the death of the pre-2006 IIP holder, any successor interest must satisfy one of the s.49(1A) carve-outs to preserve s.49(1) treatment, otherwise it enters the relevant property regime."
  - question: "What is an Immediate Post-Death Interest (IPDI)?"
    answer: "Verbatim IHTA 1984 s.49A(1): 'Where a person ('L') is beneficially entitled to an interest in possession in settled property, for the purposes of this Chapter that interest is an immediate post-death interest only if the following conditions are satisfied.' Three conditions follow. s.49A(2): the settlement was effected by will or under the law relating to intestacy. s.49A(3): L became beneficially entitled to the interest in possession on the death of the testator or intestate. s.49A(4): (a) section 71A does not apply to the property containing the interest (i.e. the property is not held on a bereaved minors trust at the same time), and (b) the interest is not characterised as a disabled person's interest. Where all three conditions are met, the IIP is an IPDI and the s.49(1) read-through applies: the IPDI holder is treated as beneficially entitled to the underlying property for IHT. The classic landlord use case is a will-trust that creates an IPDI for the surviving spouse on the testator's death, with the underlying rental portfolio passing to the children on the spouse's death."
  - question: "What is a Transitional Serial Interest (TSI)?"
    answer: "A TSI is a narrow set of post-22-March-2006 IIPs that succeed a pre-2006 IIP within statutory timeframes, preserving the s.49(1) read-through across the transition. The architecture is at IHTA 1984 ss.49B to 49E. The principal cases are: (i) a successor IIP arising under the same settlement between 22 March 2006 and 5 October 2008 (TSI under s.49C); (ii) a spouse / civil partner successor IIP arising on the death of the pre-2006 IIP holder, where the successor IIP is for the surviving spouse / civil partner of the pre-2006 IIP holder (TSI under s.49D); (iii) a transitional period for life insurance trusts (TSI under s.49E). The 5 October 2008 deadline at s.49C means new TSIs from settlements other than the pre-2006 spouse-successor case can no longer arise; the spouse-successor TSI under s.49D continues indefinitely subject to its conditions. For most current planning, the TSI architecture is relevant only as a defensive checklist when administering pre-2006 IIP settlements; new structures should be designed around IPDI or DPI, not TSI."
  - question: "What is a Disabled Person's Interest?"
    answer: "A Disabled Person's Interest under IHTA 1984 s.89B is an IIP for a disabled beneficiary (defined by reference to receipt of attendance allowance, disability living allowance, personal independence payment, or other prescribed benefits, or by reference to an inability to manage one's own affairs) that satisfies further conditions about the disabled person's entitlement to settlement income. Where the conditions are met, the s.49(1) read-through applies: the disabled person is treated as beneficially entitled to the underlying property for IHT. The DPI is the third s.49(1A) carve-out and is the structural route for families wanting to provide for a disabled child or sibling in a way that avoids the relevant property regime's 10-year charges while preserving the disabled person's eligibility for means-tested benefits. The mechanism is specialist and the conditions are detailed; sessions advising on DPIs should consult IHTM04054 onwards and a specialist trust solicitor."
  - question: "How does the spouse-IPDI structure work for a rental portfolio?"
    answer: "Step-by-step. The testator's will includes a trust provision: on the testator's death, the rental portfolio is settled on trust with the surviving spouse taking an interest in possession (the IPDI) for life, and the remainder passing to the children on the spouse's later death. On first death (testator), the s.18 spouse exemption applies to the value of the IPDI because the IPDI is for the spouse: zero IHT on first death. The s.62(1) death-uplift resets the base cost of all properties in the portfolio to MV at first death, eliminating any latent CGT on the portfolio. During the spouse's life, the spouse takes the rental income from the trust (legal title is in the trustees; beneficial entitlement to income is in the spouse under the IPDI). For income tax, the spouse reports the trust income as their own (technically the trustees report on their own SA, with the spouse receiving a tax-pool certificate; the net effect is the income is taxed at the spouse's marginal rate). On the spouse's death (second death), the s.49(1) read-through under s.49A treats the spouse as beneficially entitled to the underlying property; the portfolio sits in the spouse's death estate at MV at second death. The spouse has their own NRB, may have inherited the testator's unused NRB under s.8A (transferable NRB; up to 100% transferable), their own RNRB, and may inherit the testator's unused RNRB. If the portfolio passes to lineal descendants on second death, the RNRB applies subject to the £2m taper threshold. For a £1.2m portfolio with full unused NRBs and RNRBs available on second death, second-death IHT can be in the range £100,000 to £200,000 depending on the wider estate and the RNRB-taper position."
  - question: "Walk me through a £1.2m BTL portfolio worked example"
    answer: "Mr Patel (age 70 at first death), Mrs Patel (age 67 at first death). Joint estate at first death: £1.2m BTL portfolio (Mr Patel's sole legal ownership, jointly beneficially owned 50/50 with Mrs Patel via existing declaration) plus £400,000 of main home plus £300,000 of liquid investments. Total estate: £1.9m. Mr Patel's will creates an IPDI for Mrs Patel over his £600,000 share of the BTL portfolio (the £600,000 main home stays in his estate and passes outright to Mrs Patel under s.18 spouse exemption; the £150,000 of investments also pass to Mrs Patel outright). The £600,000 BTL share into the IPDI: s.18 spouse exemption applies (the IPDI is for the spouse). Zero IHT on first death. s.62(1) resets CGT base cost on Mr Patel's BTL share to MV at first death = £600,000. Mrs Patel's BTL share (her own 50% beneficial interest, not affected by the IPDI which only governs Mr Patel's 50%) retains its existing base cost. The IPDI runs for Mrs Patel's life; she receives the rental income from the £600,000 share and reports it on her self-assessment. Second death (Mrs Patel, 15 years later, age 82). BTL portfolio value at second death: £1.6m (growth of approximately 4.5% per year over 15 years). Mr Patel's £600,000 IPDI share grew to £800,000 (treated as Mrs Patel's beneficially-held property under s.49(1) read-through). Mrs Patel's own £600,000 share grew to £800,000. Plus the main home (now £800,000) plus investments (now £450,000). Mrs Patel's death estate: £1.6m BTL plus £800,000 home plus £450,000 = £2.85m. NRB: £325,000 own plus £325,000 transferred from Mr Patel (full transfer per s.8A) = £650,000. RNRB: assume taper bites because estate over £2m by £850,000; RNRB reduces by £1 for every £2 over £2m = £425,000 reduction. Full RNRB available pre-taper = £175,000 own plus £175,000 transferred = £350,000. After taper: zero RNRB available. Estate taxable: £2.85m minus £650,000 NRB = £2.2m. IHT at 40% = £880,000. Without the IPDI structure (e.g. if Mr Patel had given his BTL share to Mrs Patel outright via simple spouse-exemption, no trust), the second-death position is identical: same estate value, same NRB transfer, same RNRB taper outcome. The IPDI's benefit on the first-death side (zero IHT, ringfenced control of the BTL portfolio passing to children on second death) is the structural reason for choosing it; the second-death IHT figure is the same as the outright-gift alternative."
  - question: "When does the IPDI route win versus an outright gift to the spouse?"
    answer: "Three structural advantages of the IPDI route over the outright-gift alternative. (1) Control over the ultimate destination of the property. The IPDI gives the spouse income for life but the underlying capital is fixed in the trust to pass to named beneficiaries (typically the children) on the spouse's death. The spouse cannot redirect the capital by their own will, which is the key feature where the testator wants certainty that the property eventually reaches their children (rather than being diverted to a new spouse or other recipients after the testator's death). (2) Protection from family-event risk. Where the spouse remarries or enters into a new relationship after the testator's death, the IPDI's underlying capital is ring-fenced from the new family's estate; it cannot be claimed by a new spouse in a divorce or by a step-family in the new spouse's will. (3) Estate-administration certainty. The trustees administer the BTL portfolio on agreed terms; the spouse takes income but does not take ownership, so the rental management can continue with professional trustees rather than passing to a beneficiary who may not want or be able to manage the portfolio. The outright-gift alternative is structurally simpler and works well where the surviving spouse will themselves leave the property to the children on their own death; the IPDI is the right choice where the testator wants belt-and-braces certainty about the eventual destination."
  - question: "Can I create an IPDI using a deed of variation?"
    answer: "Yes, within the IHTA 1984 s.142 deed-of-variation window. A surviving spouse (or other beneficiary) can vary the deceased's will or intestacy distribution within two years of the deceased's death, redirecting some or all of the inherited estate into an IPDI trust for the spouse (or other person) with remainder to children. The s.142 election (made in writing within the variation deed or in a separate notice within six months of the variation, by the original beneficiary) treats the variation as having been made by the deceased rather than by the beneficiary. For IHT purposes, the new IPDI is treated as having been created by the deceased's will, satisfying s.49A(2) (settlement effected by will). For CGT purposes, the s.62(6) election treats the variation as a disposal by the deceased's executors rather than by the beneficiary, preserving the s.62(1) death-uplift. The combined route is one of the most powerful post-death rescue tools: a surviving spouse who inherits the BTL portfolio outright can redirect some or all of it into an IPDI for themselves with remainder to children, achieving the IPDI's control and ring-fencing benefits without losing the first-death spouse exemption or the death-uplift CGT base cost. For the deed-of-variation depth, see our existing Wave 4 C5 page."
  - question: "What about the alternative carve-outs at s.71A (bereaved minors) and s.71D (18-to-25 trusts)?"
    answer: "Where the testator's will routes the property direct to minor or young-adult children (rather than through a spouse-IPDI), the s.49(1A) carve-outs do not apply (the IPDI requires the interest to be held by an adult beneficiary; bereaved minors' interests use a different carve-out architecture). IHTA 1984 s.71A creates a bereaved minor trust where: (a) the property is held for the benefit of an unmarried minor child of the deceased parent, and (b) the trust has specific terms about absolute entitlement at age 18. s.71A trusts are outside the relevant property regime; no 10-year charges or exit charges apply during the minor's minority. IHTA 1984 s.71D creates an age 18-to-25 trust, similar to s.71A but with absolute entitlement deferred up to age 25 (with a small periodic charge to recognise the additional accumulation period). Both s.71A and s.71D are alternatives to IPDI for cases where the testator wants the property to vest in the child directly, not pass through a spouse-life-interest. The choice between IPDI-for-spouse and s.71A/s.71D-for-child turns on whether the testator wants the spouse to have lifetime use of the property income."
  - question: "Does FA 2025 change anything about the IPDI mechanic?"
    answer: "No. FA 2025 s.45 amended IHTA 1984 s.48 (excluded property territory, replacing the pre-FA-2025 domicile-at-settlement test with the new s.48ZA long-term-resident test for non-UK situated assets in offshore trusts). The IIP architecture at s.49 / s.49A / s.49B-49E / s.71A / s.71D was not amended. The IPDI mechanic operates exactly as before FA 2025 for UK-resident settlors with UK property. Where the family includes non-UK-resident or formerly non-UK-domiciled members and the planning involves offshore-trust IHT consequences, the FA 2025 changes are material and specialist advice should be sought; for the UK-resident-settlor / UK-rental-portfolio baseline that this page addresses, the FA 2025 changes are not relevant."
  - question: "How does RNRB interact with the spouse-IPDI structure on second death?"
    answer: "The RNRB is available on the second death where the residence passes to lineal descendants. For the spouse-IPDI structure on a rental portfolio (not the main home), the RNRB is available against the main home on second death if the main home passes to children (under the spouse's own will), but is NOT directly available against the rental portfolio (the RNRB applies only to a 'qualifying residential interest' which means a residence the deceased lived in at some point). The portfolio's IHT is at the standard 40% rate above NRB. Where the second-death estate exceeds £2m, the RNRB tapers by £1 for every £2 over the threshold, fully withdrawn at £2.7m for a single RNRB or £3.05m for the combined own-plus-transferred RNRB. For substantial portfolios that push the second-death estate above £2.7m even after the spouse-IPDI structure, RNRB is fully tapered; the second-death IHT cost is at the full 40% rate. Strategic planning options to reduce the second-death taper exposure include: charitable legacies under IHTA 1984 Sch 1A (reduced 36% rate where 10% of net estate left to charity); BPR-qualifying business assets (out of the £2m taper calculation entirely); and pension-fund preservation (pension funds outside the estate for IHT purposes until April 2027, then partially within). For the RNRB taper depth treatment, see our Wave 2 A8 page on RNRB."
  - question: "What income-tax treatment applies to the IPDI holder?"
    answer: "The IPDI holder takes the trust income for life. For income tax, the legal structure is that the trustees are the legal owners of the property and recipients of the rental income; they administer the property and pay trust expenses out of gross rents. The net income is paid to (or treated as paid to) the IPDI holder under their interest in possession entitlement. The trustees pay income tax at the trust's basic rate (or the small dividend trust rate where applicable) and provide the IPDI holder with a tax-pool certificate (form R185) showing the gross income and the basic-rate tax already paid. The IPDI holder then declares the gross income on their own self-assessment and claims credit for the basic-rate tax already paid. If the IPDI holder is a higher-rate taxpayer, additional tax is due on the difference between the trust's basic rate and the higher rate; if they are a non-taxpayer or basic-rate taxpayer, they may have a refund claim. The income-tax cash flow is therefore the same as if the IPDI holder had owned the rental property directly: the income is taxed at their marginal rate net of allowable expenses, with the trust's basic-rate treatment being intermediate."
  - question: "What about the executor administration of an IPDI estate?"
    answer: "On the testator's death, the executors administer the estate, identify the IPDI trust property, value it at MV at death (for both IHT and CGT base-cost purposes), and transfer it to the trustees of the IPDI trust. The IPDI starts on the date the trustees become the legal owners. From that date the trustees administer the trust: collect rents, pay trust expenses, distribute net income to the IPDI holder, maintain trust accounts and SA returns. The trustees may be the same persons as the executors (with appropriate hat-changes in the documentation) or different persons. For property cases the trustees are typically a mix of family members and a professional trustee (typically a solicitor or accountant). The IPDI trustees should register the trust with the Trust Registration Service within 90 days of trust creation if the trust has any UK tax liability. Annual TRS updates are required. For the executor walkthrough on inheriting UK rental property in detail, see our existing Wave 2 A7 page on inheriting UK rental property executors step by step."

<p>The 22 March 2006 reform (Finance Act 2006 Schedule 20) of UK trust IHT reshaped the entire interest-in-possession architecture, narrowing the original IHTA 1984 s.49(1) read-through that had treated the IIP holder as beneficially entitled to the underlying property. From that date, most new IIPs sit inside the relevant property regime (10-year periodic charge under s.64, exit charges on capital distributions under s.65, entry charge of 20% on excess over NRB on settlement) like any other discretionary trust. The three carve-outs at IHTA 1984 s.49(1A) preserve the s.49(1) treatment for specific cases that align with mainstream estate planning.</p>

<p>For landlord estates, the IPDI route via will is the single most under-explained tool in mainstream estate-planning content. The testator's will leaves the rental portfolio on IPDI for the surviving spouse; the s.18 spouse exemption applies on first death; the spouse takes the rental income for life; and on second death the property is in the spouse's death estate (within the available NRBs and RNRBs). The structure delivers first-death zero IHT, lifetime income to the surviving spouse, ring-fenced control over the ultimate destination of the property to the children, and access to the RNRB on second death where conditions are met. No entry-side CGT charge bites because the s.62(1) death-uplift resets the base cost on first death.</p>

<p>This page walks the s.49(1A) carve-out architecture section by section with verbatim statutory wording verified against legislation.gov.uk at write time, sets out a Patel-style £1.2m BTL portfolio worked example with the spouse-IPDI-then-children architecture, and addresses the deed-of-variation-into-IPDI mechanic for post-death rescue cases.</p>

<h2>The 22 March 2006 split</h2>

<p>Pre-22-March-2006 IIPs (in being on the cutoff date) are grandfathered into the original IHTA 1984 s.49(1) treatment for the life of the IIP. The IIP holder is treated as beneficially entitled to the underlying property; the property falls into their death estate at death market value; no 10-year periodic charges or exit charges apply to the trust during the IIP holder's life.</p>

<p>Post-22-March-2006 IIPs are within the relevant property regime by default. The s.49(1) read-through does NOT apply; the IIP holder is NOT treated as beneficially entitled; the trust pays its own IHT under the relevant property regime. The 10-year periodic charge under s.64 applies up to 6%, formula-adjusted; exit charges under s.65 apply to capital distributions; entry charges of 20% apply to settlement values above the available NRB at the date of settlement.</p>

<p>The three carve-outs at s.49(1A) restore the s.49(1) treatment for specific case types that align with mainstream estate planning:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Immediate Post-Death Interest (IPDI)</strong> at IHTA 1984 s.49A: an IIP arising on the testator's or intestate's death under a will or intestacy.</li>
<li><strong>Transitional Serial Interest (TSI)</strong> at IHTA 1984 ss.49B-49E: a narrow set of post-22-March-2006 IIPs that succeed a pre-2006 IIP within statutory timeframes.</li>
<li><strong>Disabled Person's Interest (DPI)</strong> at IHTA 1984 s.89B: an IIP for a disabled beneficiary satisfying further conditions.</li>
</ul>

<p>The reform also preserves the s.49(1) treatment for IIPs falling within IHTA 1984 s.5(1B) (a narrow technical category that rarely arises in landlord estate planning).</p>

<h2>The IPDI at s.49A: the will-trust workhorse</h2>

<p>Verbatim IHTA 1984 s.49A(1): "Where a person ('L') is beneficially entitled to an interest in possession in settled property, for the purposes of this Chapter that interest is an 'immediate post-death interest' only if the following conditions are satisfied." Three conditions follow:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>s.49A(2):</strong> the settlement was effected by will or under the law relating to intestacy. Lifetime settlements cannot create an IPDI; the route requires the testator's death (or intestacy) as the creating event.</li>
<li><strong>s.49A(3):</strong> L became beneficially entitled to the interest in possession on the death of the testator or intestate. The interest must arise on the death itself, not at some later point.</li>
<li><strong>s.49A(4):</strong> two further conditions: (a) section 71A does not apply to the property containing the interest (i.e. the property is not held on a bereaved minors trust at the same time), and (b) the interest is not characterised as a disabled person's interest.</li>
</ul>

<p>Where all three conditions are met, the IIP is an IPDI under s.49A. The s.49(1) read-through applies: the IPDI holder is treated as beneficially entitled to the underlying property for IHT. The trust is outside the relevant property regime; no 10-year periodic charges or exit charges apply during the IPDI holder's life.</p>

<p>The classic landlord use case is the will-trust that creates an IPDI for the surviving spouse on the testator's death, with the underlying rental portfolio passing to the children on the spouse's later death. The IPDI delivers: (a) s.18 spouse exemption on first death (the IPDI is for the spouse, treated as the spouse's beneficially-held property for IHT); (b) TCGA 1992 s.62(1) death-uplift on first death (resetting the base cost of the BTL portfolio to MV at first death); (c) lifetime income to the spouse; (d) ring-fenced control over the ultimate destination of the property to the children; (e) RNRB access on second death where the residence (not the rental portfolio; the main home, where applicable) passes to lineal descendants.</p>

<h2>The TSI at ss.49B-49E: the narrow transitional bridge</h2>

<p>The TSI architecture preserves the s.49(1) read-through across the 22 March 2006 transition for a narrow set of post-2006 IIPs that succeed a pre-2006 IIP. Three principal cases:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>s.49C TSI:</strong> a successor IIP arising under the same settlement between 22 March 2006 and 5 October 2008. The 5 October 2008 deadline closed off this route for new TSIs; existing s.49C TSIs created within the window continue for their natural life.</li>
<li><strong>s.49D TSI:</strong> a spouse / civil partner successor IIP arising on the death of the pre-2006 IIP holder, where the successor IIP is for the surviving spouse or civil partner of the pre-2006 IIP holder. The s.49D route has no calendar deadline and continues to operate for pre-2006 IIP settlements where the pre-2006 IIP holder dies and is succeeded by their surviving spouse.</li>
<li><strong>s.49E TSI:</strong> a transitional period for life insurance trusts (rarely relevant to landlord property settlements).</li>
</ul>

<p>For most current landlord planning, the TSI architecture is relevant only as a defensive checklist when administering pre-2006 IIP settlements. New trust structures should be designed around IPDI (where appropriate) or DPI (where the disability conditions are met), not TSI.</p>

<h2>The DPI at s.89B: the disability route</h2>

<p>A Disabled Person's Interest under IHTA 1984 s.89B is an IIP for a disabled beneficiary that satisfies further conditions about the disabled person's entitlement to settlement income. "Disabled person" is defined by reference to receipt of specific benefits (attendance allowance, disability living allowance, personal independence payment, certain other prescribed benefits) or by reference to an inability to manage one's own affairs by reason of mental disorder under the Mental Health Act 1983.</p>

<p>The DPI conditions require, broadly, that during the disabled person's life: (a) the disabled person is entitled to all the income arising from the settled property (or no person other than the disabled person is entitled to a benefit from the settlement); and (b) no person other than the disabled person can benefit from the capital except for limited classes (typically other beneficiaries up to a £3,000 per year cap or 3% of the maximum value of the property, whichever is lower, per the disabled person trust rules).</p>

<p>Where the DPI conditions are met, the s.49(1) read-through applies: the disabled person is treated as beneficially entitled to the underlying property for IHT, the trust is outside the relevant property regime. The DPI is the structural route for families wanting to provide for a disabled beneficiary in a way that avoids the 10-year periodic charge while preserving the beneficiary's entitlement to means-tested benefits. The mechanism is specialist; sessions advising on DPIs should consult HMRC manual IHTM04054 onwards and a specialist trust solicitor.</p>

<h2>The alternative carve-outs at ss.71A and 71D</h2>

<p>Where the testator's will routes the rental property direct to minor or young-adult children (rather than through a spouse-IPDI), the s.49(1A) carve-outs do not apply (an IPDI requires an adult beneficiary; the bereaved-minors / 18-to-25 trust carve-outs use a different architecture).</p>

<p>IHTA 1984 s.71A creates a bereaved minor trust where the property is held for the benefit of an unmarried minor child of a deceased parent, and the trust has specific terms about absolute entitlement to capital and income at age 18 at the latest. s.71A trusts are outside the relevant property regime; no 10-year periodic charges or exit charges apply during the minor's minority.</p>

<p>IHTA 1984 s.71D creates an age 18-to-25 trust, similar to s.71A but with absolute entitlement deferred up to age 25. A small periodic charge applies during the 18-to-25 deferral period to recognise the additional accumulation period; the charge is much less than the relevant property regime's 6% periodic charge would be.</p>

<p>Both s.71A and s.71D are alternatives to the IPDI for cases where the testator wants the property to vest in the child directly. The choice between IPDI-for-spouse-then-children and s.71A/s.71D-direct-for-child turns on whether the testator wants the spouse to have lifetime use of the property income. For families where the surviving spouse has independent income and the priority is direct provision for the children, the s.71A/s.71D routes are often the right structure.</p>

<aside>
<p>The IPDI mechanic is the single most under-explained tool in mainstream estate-planning content.</p>
<p>Will-writing services and online templates frequently fail to surface the IPDI route as a structural option, defaulting either to outright gift to spouse (which works but loses the control benefit) or to lifetime discretionary settlement (which pays 20% entry IHT and the 10-year periodic charge regime). For landlords with substantial rental portfolios and clear preferences about the eventual destination of the property to specific children, the IPDI mechanism is structurally cheaper than the discretionary alternative and structurally safer than the outright-gift alternative. Get in touch via the form at the foot of the page for a will-trust review focused on the IPDI option.</p>

<h2>The Patel £1.2m BTL portfolio worked example</h2>

<p>Mr Patel (age 70 at first death), Mrs Patel (age 67 at first death). Joint estate at first death: £1.2m BTL portfolio (Mr Patel's sole legal ownership, jointly beneficially owned 50/50 with Mrs Patel via existing declaration of trust), plus £400,000 main home, plus £300,000 of liquid investments. Total estate: £1.9m.</p>

<p><strong>Mr Patel's will structure.</strong> The will creates an IPDI for Mrs Patel over Mr Patel's £600,000 share of the BTL portfolio (his 50% beneficial interest). The £400,000 main home stays in Mr Patel's estate and passes outright to Mrs Patel under the s.18 spouse exemption. The £150,000 of investments (Mr Patel's 50% share) also passes to Mrs Patel outright. The BTL share into the IPDI: s.18 spouse exemption applies (the IPDI is for the spouse, treated as the spouse's beneficially-held property for IHT under s.49(1) read-through).</p>

<p><strong>Year 0 (first death) costs.</strong> Zero IHT on first death (full spouse exemption across all the estate transferring to Mrs Patel, either outright or via the IPDI). TCGA 1992 s.62(1) resets the base cost on Mr Patel's BTL share to MV at first death = £600,000. Mrs Patel's own 50% BTL share retains its existing base cost (her share was not transferred on death; only Mr Patel's was). Trust Registration Service registration of the IPDI trust within 90 days. Trust accounts opened.</p>

<p><strong>Years 1 to 15.</strong> Mrs Patel takes the rental income from the £600,000 IPDI share (her own 50% share generates rental income that is hers in any event). The trustees administer the IPDI; legal title to Mr Patel's 50% BTL share is in the trustees; beneficial entitlement to income is in Mrs Patel under the IPDI. Trust SA returns annually; basic-rate trust tax paid on net income; R185 tax-pool certificate provided to Mrs Patel; Mrs Patel reports the gross income on her own SA and claims credit for the basic-rate tax already paid.</p>

<p><strong>Year 15 (second death, Mrs Patel age 82).</strong> Portfolio value at second death: assume 4.5% annual growth. Mr Patel's £600,000 IPDI share grew to £800,000. Mrs Patel's own £600,000 share grew to £800,000. Total BTL value £1.6m. Main home (now £800,000). Investments (now £450,000). Mrs Patel's death estate: £1.6m + £800,000 + £450,000 = £2.85m.</p>

<p><strong>Second-death IHT calculation.</strong> Available NRB: £325,000 (Mrs Patel's own) plus £325,000 (full transfer from Mr Patel under s.8A; Mr Patel's NRB was unused at first death because the entire estate passed under spouse exemption). Combined NRB: £650,000. Available RNRB pre-taper: £175,000 (own) plus £175,000 (transferred from Mr Patel) = £350,000. Taper threshold £2m; estate value over taper threshold = £850,000; RNRB reduction = £850,000 ÷ 2 = £425,000. RNRB after taper: £350,000 minus £425,000 = floor at zero. RNRB fully tapered away.</p>

<p>Taxable estate: £2.85m minus £650,000 NRB minus zero RNRB = £2.2m. IHT at 40% = £880,000. Effective rate on the £2.85m total: 30.9%.</p>

<p><strong>Counterfactual: outright gift to spouse, no IPDI.</strong> If Mr Patel had given his BTL share to Mrs Patel outright (no trust), the second-death position is mathematically identical: same estate value, same transferable NRB, same RNRB taper outcome. The structural IPDI benefit on the first-death side (zero IHT, ring-fenced control of the BTL portfolio passing to children on second death rather than being subject to Mrs Patel's will) is the reason for choosing the IPDI route; the second-death IHT figure is the same regardless.</p>

<p><strong>The strategic point.</strong> The IPDI route does not reduce the second-death IHT bill; it locks in the eventual destination of the BTL portfolio to the children regardless of Mrs Patel's later decisions. For families where the testator wants belt-and-braces certainty (e.g. concern about a possible later remarriage by the spouse; concern that the spouse may otherwise be subject to undue influence from other family members; concern that the spouse may not manage the portfolio successfully), the IPDI is the right structural choice. Where these concerns do not apply and the surviving spouse can be trusted to leave the portfolio to the children on their own death, the outright-gift route is simpler.</p>

<h2>Using a deed of variation to create an IPDI</h2>

<p>The IHTA 1984 s.142 deed-of-variation window allows a beneficiary of the deceased's estate (typically the surviving spouse) to vary the inheritance within two years of the death, redirecting some or all of the inherited estate into an IPDI trust for themselves with remainder to children. The s.142 election (made in writing within the variation deed or in a separate notice, by the original beneficiary) treats the variation as having been made by the deceased rather than by the beneficiary.</p>

<p>For IHT purposes, the new IPDI created by the variation is treated as having been created by the deceased's will, satisfying s.49A(2) (settlement effected by will). For CGT purposes, the s.62(6) election treats the variation as a disposal by the deceased's executors rather than by the beneficiary, preserving the s.62(1) death-uplift on the base cost of the variation property.</p>

<p>The combined DoV-into-IPDI route is one of the most powerful post-death rescue tools available to a surviving spouse who inherited the BTL portfolio outright. The spouse can retrofit the IPDI structure within two years of first death, achieving the control and ring-fencing benefits without losing the first-death spouse exemption or the death-uplift CGT base cost. For the deed-of-variation depth, see <a href="/blog/landlord-tax-essentials/deed-of-variation-property-estate-redirecting-inheritance-iht-saving">our existing Wave 4 C5 page on deeds of variation</a>.</p>

<aside>
<p>The two-year DoV window is the most-missed planning opportunity in mainstream estate administration.</p>
<p>Surviving spouses who inherit a BTL portfolio outright often default to the simplest administration (transfer the portfolio into their own name, continue to receive rents, leave the portfolio to children on their own death) and miss the two-year window during which they could retrofit an IPDI structure for the same property at no first-death tax cost. If you are within two years of a first death and the deceased's BTL portfolio passed to you outright, the DoV-into-IPDI option is worth a structured review.
