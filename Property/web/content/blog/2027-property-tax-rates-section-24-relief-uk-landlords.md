<p>The separate property income tax rates of 22% basic, 42% higher and 47% additional from 6 April 2027 are now enacted in <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2026/11/section/7">Finance Act 2026 c.11 section 7</a> (Royal Assent on 18 March 2026). The Section 24 mortgage interest credit under <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2007/3/section/272A">ITA 2007 s.272A</a> remains at 20%. The gap between the rate at which property income is taxed and the rate at which mortgage interest is relieved widens by 2 percentage points for basic-rate landlords and by the same 2 percentage points across all bands. This page walks through the wedge by landlord profile, sets out the planning responses available before commencement, and locates where the change does and does not bite.</p>

<h2>The April 2027 property income rates: now enacted via Finance Act 2026</h2>

<p>Finance Act 2026 c.11 section 7 establishes three new rates that apply specifically to property income from 6 April 2027:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Property basic rate: 22%</strong> (compared to 20% general basic rate)</li>
<li><strong>Property higher rate: 42%</strong> (compared to 40% general higher rate)</li>
<li><strong>Property additional rate: 47%</strong> (compared to 45% general additional rate)</li>
</ul>

<p>The rates apply for the tax year 2027-28 and subsequent tax years (Finance Act 2026 s.6(8)). Property income for this purpose follows the existing definition in ITTOIA 2005 Part 3 (UK property business income) and Part 4 (overseas property business income where relevant). The band thresholds (£12,570 personal allowance, £50,270 basic-rate ceiling, £125,140 higher-rate ceiling) continue to operate as for general income tax in 2027-28; band allocation depends on total income from all sources, with the property income slice falling within the appropriate band for the rate that then applies.</p>

<p>For 2026/27 the standard 20% / 40% / 45% rates continue. The commencement is 6 April 2027, so the 2026/27 Self Assessment return (due 31 January 2028) still uses the pre-FA-2026 rates. The 2027/28 Self Assessment return (due 31 January 2029) will be the first return computed under the new property-specific rates.</p>

<h2>How Section 24 produces a wedge in 2027-28</h2>

<p>The Section 24 mechanism for individual residential landlords has been unchanged since the phased introduction completed on 6 April 2020. Mortgage interest and other allowable finance costs are not deducted from rental profit; instead a 20% basic-rate tax credit is applied against the income tax liability. The calculation under ITA 2007 s.272A produces:</p>

<ol>
<li>Property profit before finance cost deduction (rental income minus all expenses other than finance costs)</li>
<li>Income tax on property profit at the applicable property income rate (in 2026/27, general 20% / 40% / 45%; from 6 April 2027, property 22% / 42% / 47%)</li>
<li>Section 24 credit: 20% of allowable finance costs</li>
<li>Net property income tax: tax under step 2 minus the credit under step 3</li>
</ol>

<p>The credit is capped at the lower of 20% of finance costs, 20% of residential rental profit before finance costs, or 20% of total income above the personal allowance. Where the cap bites, un-credited finance cost carries forward indefinitely and is available against future years.</p>

<p>From 6 April 2027 the calculation continues to work in the same way, but the rate at step 2 (now 22% basic / 42% higher / 47% additional) no longer matches the credit rate at step 3 (still 20%). The mismatch is the wedge. For every £1 of allowable finance cost:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Basic-rate landlord:</strong> 22% tax on the equivalent £1 of property profit; 20% credit on the £1 of interest; net 2 pence of additional tax per £1 of interest, compared to a zero-wedge pre-2027 position.</li>
<li><strong>Higher-rate landlord:</strong> 42% tax; 20% credit; net 22 pence of additional tax per £1 of interest. The pre-2027 gap of 20 pence per £1 widens to 22 pence per £1.</li>
<li><strong>Additional-rate landlord:</strong> 47% tax; 20% credit; net 27 pence of additional tax per £1 of interest. The pre-2027 gap of 25 pence per £1 widens to 27 pence per £1.</li>
</ul>

<p>The headline conclusion is straightforward: every landlord pays more tax per £1 of mortgage interest from 6 April 2027 than before, with the absolute increase highest for the most heavily geared portfolios.</p>

<h2>Planning responses available before 6 April 2027</h2>

<p>Four practical responses are available to individual landlords ahead of the April 2027 commencement. None is decisive in isolation; the right combination depends on the specific portfolio.</p>

<h3>Incorporation</h3>

<p>Limited companies are outside both Section 24 and the April 2027 property income rate change. Companies deduct mortgage interest in full against rental profit and pay corporation tax at 19% (small profits rate, profits up to £50,000) and 25% (main rate, profits above £250,000) with marginal relief tapering between. The personal-vs-company differential widens by 2 percentage points from 6 April 2027 for basic-rate landlords (where the 22% property income rate now sits above the 19% small profits CT rate), and remains the same in absolute terms for higher and additional rate landlords (where the gap was already material).</p>

<p>Incorporation is not free. Transferring existing personally-owned property into a company triggers SDLT (including the 5% additional dwellings surcharge), CGT at market value (with incorporation relief under TCGA 1992 s.162 available where the conditions are met), and brings ongoing additional compliance cost (CT600, annual accounts, confirmation statement, potentially ATED where property is worth over £500,000). Limited-company BTL mortgage rates run materially higher than personal BTL rates. The decision is sensitive to leverage, marginal income tax rate, length of intended hold, and the cost of transfer. For new acquisitions the analysis is simpler (no SDLT or CGT on transfer in); for existing portfolios the breakeven point typically sits 3-5 years out.</p>

<h3>Capital expenditure timing</h3>

<p>Capital expenditure (extensions, conversions, new bathroom suites) does not reduce rental profit but enters the CGT base cost on eventual sale. Revenue repairs (replacing a broken boiler like-for-like, fixing leaks, repainting) are deductible in the year incurred. Bringing forward revenue repair work from 2027-28 into 2026-27 produces tax relief at the pre-FA-2026 rates (20% / 40% / 45%) rather than the post-FA-2026 rates (22% / 42% / 47%). The differential per £1 of repair spend is 2 pence for basic-rate, 2 pence for higher-rate, 2 pence for additional-rate; small in absolute terms but real on a significant repair budget.</p>

<h3>Financing review</h3>

<p>The reduced post-tax value of mortgage interest from 6 April 2027 marginally tilts the financing calculation away from leverage. Where alternative financing structures are available (commercial finance, family equity injection, partial pay-down from accumulated profits), the relative attractiveness improves at the margin. For most landlords the financing decision is dominated by access-to-capital and rate-of-return considerations rather than by tax, but the tax tilt becomes more material at higher leverage ratios.</p>

<h3>Income redistribution</h3>

<p>For couples where one spouse is in a lower income tax band than the other, transferring beneficial interest in a property (or a share of it) to the lower-band spouse moves rental profit into a lower property income tax band. A spouse transfer under TCGA 1992 s.58 is on a no-gain-no-loss basis for CGT, and a Form 17 election allows beneficial ownership for income tax to reflect the actual ownership split (rather than the default 50/50 for jointly held property). The mechanic is fact-sensitive but routinely used; the post-FA-2026 wedge widens the value of the planning by 2 percentage points per pound of rental profit shifted.</p>

<h2>Where the wedge does not bite</h2>

<p>Three positions are outside the April 2027 surcharge:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Limited-company landlords.</strong> Companies pay corporation tax on rental profit, not income tax. Finance Act 2026 s.7 amends the income tax rates only. Corporation tax rates and the Section 24-irrelevance of company ownership are unchanged.</li>
<li><strong>Unmortgaged landlords.</strong> The wedge that widens is specifically the gap between property income tax rate and Section 24 credit rate. Where there is no mortgage interest, there is no credit to be capped or eroded; the only impact is the 2-percentage-point band uplift on rental profit itself (per Profile 1 above).</li>
<li><strong>Cessation in the 2026-27 year.</strong> A landlord who disposes of the last property in their portfolio before 6 April 2027 has no 2027-28 rental income to which the new rates apply. The CGT calculation on disposal is unaffected (residential CGT remains 18% / 24%); the disposal itself does not bring the new rates into scope retrospectively.</li>
</ul>

<p>Limited-liability partnerships (LLPs) where one or more partners is a natural person sit in a more nuanced position because LLP members are typically taxed on their share of LLP profits as if they had received them directly, applying the rate appropriate to their individual circumstances. An LLP partner who is an individual receiving a share of LLP rental profit will face the new 22/42/47 rates on their share from 6 April 2027.</p>
