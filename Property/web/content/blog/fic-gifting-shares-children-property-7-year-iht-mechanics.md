  - question: "How is a FIC growth share valued at the moment of gift?"
    answer: "The gift is between connected persons (founder and child) so TCGA 1992 section 17 deems the disposal at market value regardless of any actual consideration. The market value of a growth share is determined on the standard share-valuation methodologies: discounted cash flow for any dividend stream the growth class is entitled to (typically nil during the founder's voting-control phase), option-pricing methodology for the growth participation above the hurdle, and minority-control plus lack-of-marketability discounts applied to the underlying pro-rata value. Where the hurdle is set at the company's value on the date the growth shares were issued, the growth shares typically value at 1% to 5% of the underlying company NAV. A contemporaneous specialist valuation report at the date of gift is the standard evidential approach; HMRC's PET 6 valuation guidance applies to the discount calculation."
  - question: "Why is section 165 holdover not available for FIC growth share gifts?"
    answer: "TCGA 1992 section 165 gift relief lets a donor hold over the CGT gain on a gift to the donee, deferring it until the donee disposes of the asset. The relief is restricted to qualifying business assets defined in TCGA 1992 Schedule 7, which for shares means shares in a trading company or in the holding company of a trading group. An investment FIC (typically a property-letting company) is not a trading company per the Pawson v HMRC [2013] UKUT 050 (TCC) investment-line test; section 165 holdover is therefore not available. The CGT crystallises at the moment of gift, payable by the founder on the difference between the market value at gift and the founder's base cost. Compare with section 260 holdover (available for gifts into discretionary trusts under TCGA 1992 section 260), which works regardless of trading status but triggers the trust regime's own IHT and CGT charges; see our companion <a href=\"/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/fic-vs-discretionary-trust-property-comparison\">FIC vs discretionary trust comparison</a> for the trade-off."
  - question: "What is the CGT cost on a FIC growth share gift?"
    answer: "The founder pays CGT on the gift, calculated as: (market value at gift) less (founder's base cost) less (annual exempt amount of £3,000 for 2026/27), taxed at the founder's CGT rate. The CGT rate is 18% basic-rate or 24% higher-rate for residential-property-backed shares from April 2024 onward under the post-2024 reforms. For a growth share at low valuation (1% to 5% of underlying value), the CGT cost is typically small in absolute terms. Example: a founder gifts growth shares valued at £25,000 with base cost of nil (newly-issued growth shares); CGT on £22,000 of gain at 24% higher rate is £5,280. The CGT is reported on the founder's self-assessment for the tax year of gift and paid by 31 January following the tax year end. Where the gift is a residential-property-backed share, the 60-day NRCGT-equivalent reporting may apply if the founder is non-resident; check the residence position before the gift."
  - question: "What happens if I die within 7 years of the FIC growth share gift?"
    answer: "The gift becomes a chargeable transfer under IHTA 1984 section 3A, with the IHT rate tapered by section 7(4) depending on how many years passed between gift and death. The taper: 0% reduction for deaths within 3 years (full 40% rate applies), then 20% reduction (32% effective rate) for deaths in year 3 to 4, 40% reduction (24%) in year 4 to 5, 60% reduction (16%) in year 5 to 6, 80% reduction (8%) in year 6 to 7, and full exemption (0%) after year 7. Note: the taper applies to the IHT rate, not to the gift's chargeable value. The nil-rate band is set against gifts in chronological order (with 7-year cumulation); a gift exhausting NRB has full taper on the IHT chargeable on its value. The PET cumulation also affects the IHT on the death estate (subsequent NRB available is reduced by failed PETs)."
  - question: "Does the 7-year clock start at FIC formation or at the date of share gift?"
    answer: "At the date of share gift. IHTA 1984 section 3A is explicit that the PET is the transfer (the gift), and section 7 measures from the chargeable transfer. FIC formation is not the chargeable transfer; only the gift of shares from the founder to the child is. A founder who incorporates a FIC at age 60 and gifts growth shares to children at age 65 starts the 7-year clock at 65; survival to age 72 produces full exemption. A founder who gifts shares at FIC formation starts the clock at 60. Most FICs gift shares progressively across multiple tranches, with each tranche starting its own 7-year clock. The cumulative-PET analysis is then per-tranche."
  - question: "What if the children are minors at the time of the FIC growth share gift?"
    answer: "ITTOIA 2005 section 624 catches arrangements where the settlor (typically the founder-parent) retains an interest in property that produces income for another person. Section 629 specifically attributes income paid to or for the benefit of an unmarried minor child of the settlor back to the settlor for income tax purposes. For a FIC growth share gift to a minor child, any dividends declared on the growth class are attributed back to the founder until the child reaches 18. The IHT 7-year PET mechanic still works (the gift itself is a PET regardless of section 624), so the value-out-of-estate benefit is preserved; what does not work is the income-splitting benefit during minority. A common workaround is a bare trust for the minor with the gift made into the trust, but the section 629 attribution still applies until the child turns 18. Gifts to adult children (18+) are not within section 624/629; the income-splitting benefit becomes available from the gift forward."
  - question: "Can my spouse make the FIC growth share gift instead?"
    answer: "Yes, if the spouse holds the relevant shares. IHTA 1984 section 18 transfers between UK-domiciled spouses are exempt from IHT, so the founder can gift shares to a spouse first (no IHT, no CGT under TCGA 1992 section 58), and the spouse can then gift onwards to the children. The spouse's 7-year clock then starts at the spouse-to-child gift. Two scenarios where this is useful: where the spouse has a shorter life expectancy than the founder (the spouse-to-child gift starts a 7-year clock from a later date) and where the spouse's CGT base cost is higher (reducing the CGT on the onward gift). The mechanism does not bypass settlements legislation: where the underlying source of the wealth is the founder, the section 624 analysis can still look through the spouse to the founder. The discipline is that the spouse's gift has to be a genuine independent gift, not a conduit transaction; a spouse who promptly gifts on shares received from the founder may be challenged."
  - question: "What documentation is needed at the moment of FIC growth share gift?"
    answer: "Eight documents typically. First, a contemporaneous specialist valuation report at the date of gift, applying recognised methodologies (option-pricing for the growth-share class, with minority and lack-of-marketability discounts). Second, a board minute documenting the gift, the share-class transferred, the number of shares, the recipient, and the valuation reference. Third, a stock transfer form (Form J30 for a transfer between individuals; the J30 is exempt from stamp duty on a gift of unquoted shares). Fourth, an updated register of members reflecting the new shareholding. Fifth, an updated PSC register if the recipient now holds 25% or more of voting rights or shares. Sixth, a confirmation statement to Companies House if any details change. Seventh, the founder's self-assessment CGT computation for the tax year of gift (reportable on the SA108 supplement). Eighth, if a section 431 ITEPA election is needed (where the recipient is an office-holder of the FIC), the election must be made within 14 days of the gift."
  - question: "How is the founder's base cost determined for the gifted growth shares?"
    answer: "Depends on how the growth shares were acquired by the founder originally. If the founder subscribed for new growth shares on issue (most common for FICs founded post-2020), the base cost is the subscription price (typically very low because the growth shares are issued at par with minimal upfront value). If the founder acquired the growth shares as part of a share-class reorganisation, the base cost is the relevant proportion of the prior holding's base cost (TCGA 1992 sections 126 to 130 share reorganisation rules apply). If the founder acquired the shares before April 1982, the rebasing-to-31-March-1982 election may be available. For most modern FICs, the growth-share base cost is in the £100 to £1,000 range per tranche issued, so the CGT on the gift turns largely on the market value at gift rather than on the base cost subtraction."
  - question: "Should I make the FIC growth share gift now or wait?"
    answer: "Depends on three factors: the founder's expected mortality horizon, the expected growth trajectory of the underlying property value, and the founder's current CGT rate against expected future CGT rate. The 7-year PET clock favours earlier gifting: a gift at age 60 has a higher probability of full exemption (survival to 67) than the same gift at age 70 (survival to 77). The growth-share architecture also favours earlier gifting: the growth shares' market value at gift is lower when the hurdle is closer to the company's current value, so the CGT cost at gift is smaller; future growth then accrues to the children's class outside the founder's estate. The CGT rate is the variable that may push for waiting: where the founder is currently in the higher-rate band but expects to be in the basic-rate band post-retirement (lower personal income), waiting can save 6 percentage points (24% to 18%) on the CGT bill. The typical answer is gift earlier rather than later; the typical exception is a founder still earning at higher rate where the CGT differential is material against a modest growth-share value."
  - question: "What happens to the gift if I survive 7 years but the children sell the shares within that window?"
    answer: "The 7-year PET clock continues to run on the founder regardless of what the children do with the shares. If the children sell the shares within the 7 years, the children pay CGT on the difference between their sale proceeds and their base cost (which is the market value at the gift, not the founder's base cost; the TCGA 1992 section 17 deemed-MV disposal sets the donee's acquisition cost). If the founder dies within the 7 years, the gift is still a chargeable PET on the founder's estate regardless of whether the children still hold the shares. The two events are independent: the founder's PET clock is about value out of the founder's estate; the children's CGT on subsequent disposal is about their own gain on their own holding. A children's sale within the 7-year window can therefore produce double tax (CGT for the children, then PET-IHT for the founder on death within 7), which is a feature of the structure rather than a planning failure."
  - question: "Can the founder maintain control of the FIC after gifting growth shares?"
    answer: "Yes, because the growth-share class typically has no voting rights and only economic participation above the hurdle. The founder's voting control sits in the A-class (held by the founder), the reserved-matters list in the articles (covered in our <a href=\"/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/fic-articles-of-association-property-control-mechanics\">FIC articles of association page</a>), and the preference-class coupon (held by the founder). Gifting growth shares transfers the economic upside above the hurdle without affecting the voting control. The HMRC scrutiny point is whether the structure is genuine: where the founder operates the FIC as personal property after gifting the growth shares, the substance-over-form analysis can still attribute returns back to the founder. The contemporaneous operational record (covered in our <a href=\"/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/fic-property-corporate-governance-board-meetings-resolutions-discipline\">FIC governance discipline page</a>) is what supports the genuine-gift position."
  - question: "How does this differ from gifting the property directly to children?"
    answer: "Three structural differences. First, valuation: a direct property gift is at full market value of the property under TCGA 1992 section 17; a growth-share gift is at the much lower market value of the share class (with hurdle and discounts). Second, CGT: direct property gift triggers CGT on the founder at 18% basic / 24% higher rate on the full property gain; growth-share gift triggers CGT only on the small share-value gain. Third, control: direct property gift transfers ownership of the property to the child outright (with all the management questions that follow); growth-share gift transfers the economic upside above the hurdle while the founder retains operational and voting control via the FIC structure. The IHT 7-year-PET mechanism is the same on both routes (PET, 7-year clock, taper relief). Our companion direct-property-gift page (wave 4 C4 in this same wave's IHT bucket) covers the direct-property mechanics; this page covers the FIC-share-gift route."
  - question: "Is BPR available on a failed PET if I die within 7 years?"
    answer: "Generally no, for an investment FIC. IHTA 1984 section 104 grants BPR on the death estate, and section 113A extends BPR to certain failed PETs where the donee still owns the asset and the asset still qualifies for BPR. For an investment-FIC growth share, BPR is not available because the underlying company is investment (not trading) per the Pawson investment-line test; see our <a href=\"/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/fic-iht-treatment-bpr-myth\">FIC IHT and BPR myth page</a> for the substantive position. The failed PET is therefore a full chargeable transfer subject to the section 7(4) taper, with no BPR softening. For active-trading FICs (rare; hotel businesses, property development with continuous building, intensive serviced accommodation), BPR is potentially available subject to the April 2026 £2.5 million combined APR/BPR allowance under IHTA 1984 s.124D as inserted by FA 2026 Sch 12 para 4 (the GOV.UK announcement-stage summary page still cites the stale £1m headline figure); even then the section 113A continuing-ownership requirement and the trading-status-at-death requirement need to hold for the failed PET to attract relief."

<p>A property founder transferring FIC growth shares to children faces four operational questions at the moment of gift. Each question has a specific tax-statute anchor, each question has a documentary discipline that has to be met at the time of the transfer rather than reconstructed afterwards, and each question can be lost on the operational record if the gift is made informally or without the right paperwork. This page walks the four questions in turn: valuation, CGT treatment, settlements legislation interaction, and the 7-year PET clock.</p>

<p>Out of scope on this page: the strategic IHT-side framing of why a property founder uses a FIC architecture at all (covered separately in our wave 4 FIC-as-IHT-tool page); the direct-property-gift 7-year-clock mechanic where the founder gifts the property outright rather than via FIC shares (covered separately in our wave 4 direct-property gift page); the in-life retirement-income mechanics from the FIC (see our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/fic-property-retirement-decumulation-mechanics-uk">FIC retirement income page</a>); and the blended-family use case (covered separately). The boundary between this page and those pages is that this page is about the operational mechanics at the moment of the share gift, not about the strategic case for using a FIC or about other gift routes.</p>

<h2>Question one: valuation at the moment of gift</h2>

<p>The gift is between connected persons (the founder and the child are connected under TCGA 1992 section 286), which triggers the deemed market-value disposal rule in <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12/section/17">TCGA 1992 section 17</a>. The disposal is treated as taking place at market value regardless of any actual consideration; market value at gift is therefore the gain-determining figure for the founder's CGT computation and the donee's acquisition cost.</p>

<p>Market value of a growth share is not a straightforward calculation. The growth-share class has no entitlement to value at or below the hurdle, full entitlement above the hurdle, no voting rights, no fixed dividend, and standard transfer restrictions (pre-emption cascade per the articles). Three valuation discounts apply: hurdle discount (only the optionality above the hurdle has value), minority discount (the holder cannot force a sale of the underlying property and has limited rights to information), and lack-of-marketability discount (no ready market exists for the shares, the pre-emption cascade restricts sale, and the typical hold period is generational).</p>

<p>The methodology that survives an HMRC enquiry is option-pricing applied to the growth participation: Black-Scholes or a binomial-tree valuation treating the growth-share class as a call option on the company's underlying value with the hurdle as the strike price. The output is typically in the 1% to 5% of pro-rata NAV range for a growth-share class issued shortly after the hurdle was set. A specialist share valuation report at the date of gift is the standard evidential approach; HMRC's Shares and Assets Valuation team applies similar methodologies and will engage on the technical detail rather than dismiss the discount. The valuation report is dated, signed by the valuer, and references the methodology and inputs.</p>

<h2>Question two: CGT at the gift and the section 165 holdover problem</h2>

<p>The founder's CGT computation at gift is: (market value at gift) less (founder's base cost) less (£3,000 annual exempt amount for 2026/27) equals the chargeable gain. The CGT rate is 18% in the basic-rate band or 24% in the higher-rate band for residential-property-backed shares from April 2024 under the post-2024 reform. For most property FICs, the growth-share market value at gift is small (1% to 5% of NAV) and the founder's base cost is also small (typical subscription price at issue is par, around £1 per share), so the chargeable gain is modest in absolute terms.</p>

<p>The route that would normally defer this CGT is the <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12/section/165">section 165 holdover relief</a>. Section 165 lets a donor hold over the gain on a gift to the donee, deferring the CGT until the donee makes a subsequent disposal. The relief is restricted to qualifying business assets defined in <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12/schedule/7">TCGA 1992 Schedule 7</a>; for shares, this means shares in a trading company or in the holding company of a trading group. An investment FIC (a property-letting company collecting rental income) is not a trading company per the investment-line test in Pawson v HMRC [2013] UKUT 050 (TCC); section 165 holdover is therefore not available.</p>

<p>The CGT crystallises at the gift, payable by the founder on the founder's self-assessment for the tax year of gift. The £3,000 annual exempt amount can absorb modest gains; for larger gifts, the CGT cost is a real out-of-pocket consequence that needs to be funded from the founder's other resources. An alternative route, gift into a discretionary trust under <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12/section/260">TCGA 1992 section 260</a>, gives holdover regardless of trading status but triggers the trust regime's own entry IHT charge (20% on excess over NRB) and ongoing 10-year-anniversary IHT charges (up to 6% on chargeable value); the comparison is covered in our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/fic-vs-discretionary-trust-property-comparison">FIC vs discretionary trust page</a>.</p>

<h2>Question three: settlements legislation and the minor-child attribution</h2>

<p>The settlements legislation under <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2005/5/section/624">ITTOIA 2005 section 624</a> catches arrangements where the settlor (the founder) retains an interest in property that produces income for another person. The specific minor-child rule in section 629 attributes income paid to or for the benefit of an unmarried minor child of the settlor back to the settlor for income tax purposes.</p>

<p>For a FIC growth-share gift to a minor child, two consequences follow. First, the gift itself is still a PET under IHTA 1984 section 3A; the IHT 7-year clock starts on the date of gift, and the value-out-of-estate benefit applies regardless of section 629. Second, any dividends declared on the growth share class during the child's minority are attributed back to the founder for income tax, so the income-splitting benefit (paying dividends to the child at the child's own marginal rate) does not arrive until the child turns 18.</p>

<p>The bare-trust workaround does not solve the section 629 attribution. Where a parent gifts shares to a bare trust for the minor child, the child is the beneficial owner of the shares for property-law purposes, but section 629 still attributes the income for income-tax purposes. The bare-trust route is therefore not a settlements-legislation-bypass; it is a property-law convenience for holding shares for a minor beneficial owner. The companion settlements-boundary analysis is in our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/alphabet-shares-property-spv-dividend-splitting-spouse-children">alphabet shares page</a>.</p>

<p>For gifts to adult children (aged 18 or over at the date of gift), section 629 does not apply directly. The general section 624 analysis still does: the founder must not retain an interest in the dividends or capital that would attract the income back. The discretionary class-by-class dividend mechanism in the articles satisfies this where the directors' discretion is genuine and exercised at the time of each declaration (covered in our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/fic-articles-of-association-property-control-mechanics">FIC articles drafting page</a> and the operational discipline in our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/fic-property-corporate-governance-board-meetings-resolutions-discipline">governance page</a>).</p>

<h2>Question four: the 7-year PET clock from the date of gift</h2>

<p><a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1984/51/section/3A">IHTA 1984 section 3A</a> defines a Potentially Exempt Transfer: a transfer of value made by an individual to another individual that becomes exempt from IHT seven years after the date of transfer. The 7-year clock starts at the date of the gift, not at the date the FIC was formed and not at the date the growth shares were issued. A founder who incorporates a FIC at age 60 and gifts growth shares to children at age 65 starts the 7-year clock at 65; survival to age 72 produces full exemption on the gift.</p>

<p>Where the founder dies within the 7 years, the gift becomes a chargeable transfer and the IHT charge is calculated by applying the section 7 rate (40% on the cumulative excess over the nil-rate band, with the standard nil-rate band allocated to gifts in chronological order). <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1984/51/section/7">Section 7(4)</a> grants taper relief by reducing the IHT charge on a per-year basis:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Death within 3 years of gift: no reduction (full 40% rate applies).</li>
  <li>Death in year 3 to 4: 20% reduction (effective rate 32%).</li>
  <li>Death in year 4 to 5: 40% reduction (effective 24%).</li>
  <li>Death in year 5 to 6: 60% reduction (effective 16%).</li>
  <li>Death in year 6 to 7: 80% reduction (effective 8%).</li>
  <li>Death after 7 years: full exemption (0%).</li>
</ul>

<p>An important nuance: the taper relief applies to the IHT charge, not to the chargeable value of the gift. A gift exhausting the nil-rate band (currently £325,000 to April 2028) attracts no IHT in any taper year because no IHT is chargeable; the taper has nothing to bite on. Taper only saves IHT where the gift is large enough to attract IHT after the NRB allocation. For most FIC growth-share gifts at moderate valuations, the gift sits within or near the NRB and the taper relief is academic; what matters is the survive-7-years exemption.</p>

<p>Multiple gifts produce a cumulation analysis. The 7-year cumulation rule means that any chargeable transfer made within 7 years of a subsequent transfer absorbs nil-rate band first. A founder making annual growth-share gift tranches across the decumulation horizon starts a separate 7-year clock for each tranche; HMRC's IHTM14000 onwards on the inheritance tax manual covers the cumulation mechanics.</p>

<h2>The documentation checklist at the moment of gift</h2>

<p>Eight documents typically support a FIC growth-share gift and need to be in place at the moment of transfer:</p>

<ol>
  <li>A contemporaneous specialist valuation report at the date of gift, applying option-pricing methodology with minority and lack-of-marketability discounts.</li>
  <li>A board minute documenting the gift: date, share class transferred, number of shares, identity of donor and donee, reference to the valuation report.</li>
  <li>A stock transfer form (Form J30 for a gift between individuals). The J30 is exempt from stamp duty on a gift of unquoted shares but must still be completed.</li>
  <li>An updated register of members reflecting the new shareholding, with the date of transfer recorded.</li>
  <li>An updated PSC register if the recipient now holds 25% or more of voting rights or shares; updated within reasonable time and reported to Companies House via PSC01 / PSC02 / PSC03 as appropriate.</li>
  <li>The founder's section 177 declaration of interest in the gift transaction (recorded in the board minute).</li>
  <li>The founder's self-assessment CGT computation for the tax year of gift, with the gain reported on the SA108 supplement and the CGT paid by 31 January following the tax year end.</li>
  <li>A section 431 ITEPA 2003 election within 14 days of the gift where the recipient is an office-holder of the FIC (typically applies where adult children are also directors). The election treats the shares as acquired at unrestricted market value and protects against later restricted-securities income-tax exposure.</li>
</ol>

<p>The contemporaneity of these documents matters. A valuation report dated months after the gift, board minutes reconstructed retroactively, or a J30 stock transfer form signed years late, all carry less evidential weight than the same documents prepared at the time. HMRC's first request at any FIC gift enquiry is the contemporaneous record set; gaps in the set make the enquiry materially harder to defend.</p>

<h2>Sequencing the gift against the founder's life</h2>

<p>The gift question for most property founders is not "should we do this" but "when". Three factors weigh on the timing decision: the founder's expected mortality horizon, the expected growth trajectory of the underlying property value, and the founder's current CGT rate against expected future CGT rate.</p>

<p>The 7-year PET clock favours earlier gifting. A gift at age 60 has a higher probability of full exemption (survival to 67) than the same gift at age 70 (survival to 77). The longer the founder's expected life, the more value gifting earlier captures.</p>

<p>The growth-share architecture also favours earlier gifting. The market value of growth shares at gift is lowest when the hurdle is closest to the company's current value, so the CGT cost at gift is smallest when the gift is closest to issue. Future growth then accrues to the donee's class outside the founder's estate from issue. A founder who waits to gift until the property value has materially exceeded the hurdle gifts a higher-value growth share with a higher CGT cost and less growth-out-of-estate runway.</p>

<p>The CGT rate is the variable that may push for waiting. Where the founder is currently in the higher-rate band (24% on residential-property-backed share gains) but expects to be in the basic-rate band post-retirement (18%), waiting saves 6 percentage points on the CGT bill. The saving has to be set against the loss of the 7-year-clock runway from waiting. For most founders the timing-PET-clock factor dominates and the CGT-rate factor is secondary; for founders whose current marginal rate is materially higher than expected future rate and whose growth-share value is large, the CGT saving may outweigh.</p>

<p>Multiple gift tranches across the decumulation horizon are often better than a single large gift. Each tranche starts its own 7-year clock, each tranche valued at the date of that tranche, and the cumulative analysis spreads the CGT cost across multiple tax years' annual exempt amounts. The downside is the documentary discipline (each tranche needs its own valuation, board minute, J30, register update); the upside is the structural flexibility to vary the pace as circumstances change.</p>

<h2>What this page does not cover (boundary patrol)</h2>

<p>The mechanics on this page are about the share-gift event. Three adjacent topics are explicitly out of scope on this page, with each carrying its own cross-link:</p>

<p><strong>Direct property gifts as an alternative.</strong> A founder can gift property directly to children rather than via a FIC. The mechanics are different (full property MV at section 17 disposal, no share-valuation discounts, full CGT exposure, but a cleaner ownership transfer). The wave 4 C4 direct-property gift page covers the comparison; the boundary between this page and that page is the share-vs-property gift route.</p>

<p><strong>The strategic IHT-side why-to-use-a-FIC framing.</strong> Why a founder uses a FIC architecture at all for IHT planning, including the value-freeze mechanic, the BPR position (which is hostile for investment FICs per Pawson; see our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/fic-iht-treatment-bpr-myth">FIC IHT and BPR myth page</a>), and the comparison with discretionary trust and direct-gift routes. This page is the mechanic at the moment of gift, not the strategic case for the structure.</p>

<p><strong>In-life retirement income from the founder's retained FIC strands.</strong> The DLA repayment runway, the preference share coupon, and the preference share redemption mechanics. Those produce income for the founder during life and are independent of the growth-share gift mechanic. Our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/fic-property-retirement-decumulation-mechanics-uk">FIC retirement income page</a> covers that strand.</p>

<p>The four-question framework on this page (valuation, CGT, settlements, 7-year PET) is the operational mechanic at the moment of the FIC growth-share gift. Getting each of the four questions right at the time of transfer, with the contemporaneous documentation in place, is what delivers the structural benefit the FIC architecture was designed for. The benefit can be lost retroactively only by the founder's premature death; it cannot be reconstructed after the fact by retrospective documentation.</p>
