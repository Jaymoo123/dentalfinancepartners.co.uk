<p>A property accountant handles the tax and accounting work that arises from owning, letting and eventually disposing of UK property. The work overlaps with general accountancy but the technical depth on property-specific rules (Section 24, SDLT including the 5% additional dwellings surcharge, the 60-day CGT regime, MTD for Income Tax, ATED, section 162 incorporation relief) is what distinguishes a specialist from a generalist.</p>

<p>If you are ready to engage one rather than read about the role, see our main <a href="/">property accountants</a> page for UK-wide Section 24, MTD, and incorporation advice.</p>

<p>This page sets out what the work actually involves, the deliverables in a typical engagement, when professional input adds real value, and how to evaluate options.</p>

<h2>The core deliverables</h2>

<p>For a typical individual landlord client, a property accountant provides:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Annual Self Assessment return.</strong> Preparation and filing of the SA100 with the SA105 property pages, plus any other relevant pages (SA108 for capital gains, SA106 for foreign income, SA109 for non-residents). Allowable expenses applied, Section 24 mechanics calculated, the £1,000 property allowance considered where applicable.</li>
<li><strong>Bookkeeping or oversight of bookkeeping.</strong> Either full bookkeeping (the accountant handles the entries) or oversight (the client uses cloud accounting software and the accountant reviews). Most modern engagements use cloud software (Xero, QuickBooks, FreeAgent, or property-specific products like Hammock or Landlord Studio).</li>
<li><strong>MTD for Income Tax quarterly updates.</strong> For sole-trader landlords above the prevailing threshold (£50,000 from April 2026, £30,000 from April 2027, £20,000 from April 2028), four quarterly digital submissions plus the annual final declaration.</li>
<li><strong>Capital gains tax computations and 60-day returns.</strong> When a property is sold, the accountant calculates the gain (base cost, PRR if applicable, capital improvement adjustments, AEA, rate split), files the 60-day CGT on UK property return and pays the tax, and reconciles through the SA108 capital gains pages at year-end.</li>
<li><strong>Capital allowances and fixtures.</strong> Identifying allowances on qualifying fixtures and fittings (boilers, bathroom suites, integral features in qualifying property, and plant in commercial and furnished lets) and drawing the revenue-vs-capital line correctly so repairs reduce tax now while improvements are held against the eventual capital gain. This is a discrete service line, not an afterthought to the SA return.</li>
<li><strong>Ad-hoc advisory work.</strong> Section 24 mitigation, incorporation modelling, spouse-split planning, disposal timing, SDLT structuring, inheritance tax and succession planning, ATED returns for company-held property, HMRC enquiry response.</li>
</ul>

<p>For company-held property, the deliverables shift to:</p>

<ul>
<li>Corporation tax returns (CT600) within 12 months of the accounting reference date</li>
<li>Statutory accounts filed with Companies House within nine months of the year-end</li>
<li>Annual confirmation statement at Companies House</li>
<li>ATED returns where any property exceeds £500,000 in value (by 30 April annually, normally nil with property letting relief)</li>
<li>Director's loan account management and dividend planning</li>
<li>PAYE if the company pays salaries</li>
</ul>

<h2>The four service categories: which do you actually need?</h2>

<p>It helps to think of property accountant services as four categories rather than a single undifferentiated package. Most landlords need all of the first category, a growing share of the second and third as the portfolio scales, and the fourth only when a specific property type or transaction calls for it.</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Compliance.</strong> Self Assessment with the SA105 property pages, 60-day CGT returns on disposal, corporation tax and statutory accounts for company-held property, and MTD for Income Tax quarterly updates where in scope. This is the non-negotiable base layer.</li>
<li><strong>Bookkeeping and record-keeping.</strong> Either the accountant runs the books or reviews cloud records you keep yourself. Categorising rental income and expenses, reconciling bank statements, separating capital expenditure from repairs, and keeping records ready for any HMRC enquiry.</li>
<li><strong>Tax planning and advisory.</strong> Section 24 impact modelling, incorporation feasibility, spouse-split and disposal-timing planning, and capital gains projections. This is where forward-looking work, rather than backward-looking compliance, sits.</li>
<li><strong>Specialist property services.</strong> The areas a generalist rarely covers: HMO and multi-unit accounting, commercial property, property development, capital allowances, and inheritance tax structuring.</li>
</ul>

<h2>How to choose a service level by portfolio size</h2>

<p>The services you need scale with the size and complexity of the portfolio, not with how much you are willing to spend. Matching the service level to the portfolio is the single most common decision a new client gets wrong, usually by under-buying when the portfolio has outgrown DIY or by over-buying advisory work the position does not yet justify.</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Single-property landlord.</strong> Usually the annual SA return plus basic compliance support, with occasional strategic advice when something changes (a remortgage, a disposal, a move abroad). Bookkeeping is light enough to keep in a spreadsheet or simple cloud software.</li>
<li><strong>Two to four properties.</strong> The administrative load and the Section 24 interaction start to justify regular bookkeeping and a once-a-year planning conversation. This is the band where most landlords first benefit from a specialist over careful DIY.</li>
<li><strong>Five or more properties.</strong> Monthly or quarterly bookkeeping, quarterly management reporting, proactive tax planning, and periodic incorporation reviews become genuinely worthwhile. At this scale the structural decisions (incorporation, refinancing, disposal sequencing) move enough tax to repay the planning work on their own.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Buy-to-let accountant: what landlords specifically need</h2>

<p>For a residential buy-to-let landlord, the work a <strong>buy to let accountant</strong> does is a focused subset of the above. The recurring questions a landlord brings to a BTL specialist are practical: how Section 24 affects this specific portfolio, which expenses are allowable, when (if ever) to incorporate, and how to handle the 60-day CGT return when a property sells. Good <strong>BTL accountant services</strong> answer those in the context of your actual numbers rather than in general terms.</p>

<p>What landlords most often look for from <strong>accountants for buy to let</strong> property, in roughly the order it comes up:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Section 24 handled correctly.</strong> The basic-rate finance-cost credit applied accurately, with the impact modelled rather than just calculated, so a higher-rate landlord can see what the restriction is actually costing.</li>
<li><strong>Allowable expenses captured in full.</strong> Repairs, void-period costs, pre-letting expenditure, replacement of domestic items relief, and the line between revenue and capital drawn correctly.</li>
<li><strong>Disposal and CGT support.</strong> The 60-day CGT on UK property return prepared on time, with any pre-sale spouse-split or disposal-timing planning done before exchange.</li>
<li><strong>An honest incorporation view.</strong> A modelled answer to the personal-vs-company question for the specific portfolio, not a generic pitch, which is exactly the depth a generalist finds hard to match.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Specialist services by property type</h2>

<p>Beyond the standard residential buy-to-let, several property types carry their own tax treatment, and this is where a specialist earns the distinction from a general accountant:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>HMOs and multi-unit property.</strong> Room-by-room income and expense allocation, the business-rates-versus-council-tax question, licensing costs, and fire-safety and compliance expenditure categorised correctly.</li>
<li><strong>Commercial property.</strong> Capital allowances on the building and integral features, the VAT option to tax decision, business rates and service-charge accounting, and the tax treatment of lease premiums. Commercial property sits outside the Section 24 restriction, which changes the planning picture entirely.</li>
<li><strong>Property development.</strong> The trading-versus-investment distinction (which determines whether profits are income or capital), Construction Industry Scheme (CIS) compliance, VAT on development projects, and the treatment of development finance costs.</li>
</ul>

<h2>When professional input becomes worth paying for</h2>

<p>The cost-benefit case is portfolio-specific, but the trigger points where most landlords benefit from professional input are:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>3 to 4 properties or more.</strong> The administrative load alone usually justifies the cost.</li>
<li><strong>Higher-rate tax position with mortgage interest.</strong> Section 24 mechanics make the calculation worth getting right.</li>
<li><strong>Imminent disposal.</strong> The 60-day reporting deadline, the PRR computation, and the spouse-split opportunity all benefit from specialist handling.</li>
<li><strong>Considering incorporation.</strong> The section 162 CGT and SDLT analysis is too high-stakes to DIY.</li>
<li><strong>Non-resident position.</strong> The NRL scheme, withholding mechanics, and 60-day reporting for any UK land disposal add complexity.</li>
<li><strong>Mixed-use property.</strong> Properties spanning residential and commercial use, with different tax treatments on different parts.</li>
<li><strong>HMRC enquiry.</strong> Specialist response usually shortens and improves outcome.</li>
<li><strong>Within MTD-ITSA scope.</strong> Above £50,000 from April 2026, £30,000 from April 2027, £20,000 from April 2028.</li>
</ul>

<p>Below those triggers, a competent general accountant or careful DIY is often fine, though many landlords still find the certainty and time-saving of a specialist worthwhile.</p>

<h2>What good engagement looks like</h2>

<p>Three characteristics of a well-functioning property accountant engagement:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Proactive contact.</strong> The accountant flags relevant changes (FHL abolition, Section 24 thresholds, 2027 rates, MTD threshold drops) and how they affect your specific position. Static engagements that only touch base at SA time are missing the point.</li>
<li><strong>Year-round availability.</strong> Quick questions on a sale, a mortgage, a structural change should get a same-week response. Crisis-only access is a red flag.</li>
<li><strong>Clean numbers.</strong> Modelling for structural decisions is shown with clear inputs and outputs, not hand-waving generalities. If you cannot follow the calculation, ask for it broken down.</li>
</ul>

<h2>How to evaluate options</h2>

<p>Beyond credentials (ACA / ACCA / CTA / ATT), four practical evaluation questions to ask any prospective property accountant:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>What proportion of your client base is property-focused?</strong> A genuine specialist will say 70%+; a generalist offering property as one of many sectors will say 10-30%. Both can work, but the depth differs.</li>
<li><strong>Walk me through the personal-vs-company decision for someone in my position.</strong> Listen for whether the answer is informed by the specific portfolio, leverage and marginal rate, or whether it's a generic pitch for incorporation.</li>
<li><strong>How are you set up for MTD-ITSA?</strong> The answer should be concrete: which software they recommend, how they handle quarterly submissions, what their fees look like for MTD-in-scope clients.</li>
<li><strong>What's your approach to HMRC enquiries?</strong> The right answer is matter-of-fact: enquiries happen, here's how we handle them, here's our enquiry insurance position.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Online vs traditional: the remote property accountant model</h2>

<p>Most property accountant work is now delivered remotely. An <strong>online property accountant</strong> provides the same compliance, bookkeeping and advisory services as a traditional high-street firm, but delivers them digitally: cloud accounting software (Xero, QuickBooks or FreeAgent), video calls for planning conversations, secure online portals for documents, and electronic signing through tools like DocuSign. HMRC submissions are filed digitally, and MTD-compatible software is becoming the default rather than the exception. For most landlord tax work, this is now the mainstream model.</p>

The point of specialist depth over geography is that the right technical expertise now matters more than the firm's postcode.</p>

<h2>What you don't need a property accountant for</h2>

<p>Honest counterpoint: there are positions where professional input adds limited value:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Single basic-rate landlord, no mortgage, single property.</strong> The SA return is usually straightforward, and inexpensive cloud bookkeeping software does the job.</li>
<li><strong>Rent-a-room landlord under £7,500.</strong> No SA notification required; no return needed.</li>
<li><strong>Stable portfolio, no planned changes, basic rate position.</strong> Once a year SA return only; the accountant's annual fee may exceed any tax savings unless something changes.</li>
</ul>

<p>The value of professional input scales with complexity. Where complexity is genuinely low, the cost of professional advice may exceed the benefit. The question to ask yourself is not "should I have an accountant" but "is the engagement going to repay its cost given my specific position".</p>

<h2>How this fits with the broader landlord tax framework</h2>

<p>The property accountant is one element of the wider landlord support team that typically also includes a mortgage broker (for BTL mortgage structuring), a solicitor (for conveyancing, lettings disputes), and a letting agent (for tenant management). The accountant's role is to handle the tax and accounting side and to flag when structural changes (incorporation, disposal, gifting) affect the wider plan. Good coordination between the accountant and the other advisers tends to produce better outcomes than treating each professional in isolation.</p>
