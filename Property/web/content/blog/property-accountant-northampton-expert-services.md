<p>Finding the right <strong>property accountant in Northampton</strong> can make a real difference to your rental profits and your peace of mind. Landlord tax has tightened considerably: the <strong>Section 24 (S24) finance cost restriction</strong> is now fully in force, <strong>Making Tax Digital for Income Tax (MTD for ITSA)</strong> is live, and West Northamptonshire Council runs its own HMO licensing regime across parts of the town. Local, property-specific expertise has rarely mattered more.</p>

<p>Whether you own a single buy-to-let or run a larger portfolio across Northamptonshire, working with a specialist who understands both UK property tax and the West Northants market helps you stay compliant and keep more of what you earn.</p>

<h2>Northampton's rental market in brief</h2>

<p>Northampton sits at the centre of West Northamptonshire (a unitary authority since April 2021) and benefits from strong logistics employment and fast rail links to London Euston, the Midlands, and beyond. That mix supports steady rental demand from professionals, families, and students. Common letting situations a local <strong>buy-to-let accountant in Northampton</strong> sees include:</p>

<ul>
<li>Town-centre flats let to working professionals and commuters</li>
<li>Family houses across the suburbs and surrounding villages</li>
<li>Student lets linked to the University of Northampton's Waterside campus</li>
<li>Houses in multiple occupation (HMOs), which carry their own licensing and tax considerations</li>
</ul>

<p>Each of these comes with specific tax treatment, and the right structure for a single flat is rarely the right structure for a multi-property HMO operation.</p>

<h2>The tax rules every Northampton landlord needs to get right</h2>

<h3>Section 24: the finance cost restriction is fully in force</h3>

<p>Section 24 is no longer being phased in. It applies in full. You can no longer deduct mortgage interest and other finance costs from your rental profit. Instead, you receive a <strong>basic-rate (20%) tax credit</strong> against your tax bill. The practical effect is that your full rental income is taxed first, and the credit is applied afterwards.</p>

<p>For a higher-rate or additional-rate taxpayer with a mortgaged Northampton property, this can increase the effective tax rate noticeably compared with the pre-2017 position, and it can even push some landlords into a higher band because the gross rent (not the net profit after interest) counts toward total income. A specialist can model your exact position and review mitigation options. For the full mechanics, see our guide to the <a href="/blog/section-24-and-tax-relief/section-24-mortgage-interest-restriction-uk-landlords">Section 24 mortgage interest restriction</a>, and the complete <a href="/blog/section-24-and-tax-relief/landlord-tax-deductions-uk-2026-complete-list">list of allowable landlord tax deductions</a> that still reduce your bill.</p>

<h3>Making Tax Digital for Income Tax is live</h3>

<p>MTD for ITSA is no longer a future deadline. It is in force. Mandation follows a phased income schedule:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>From 6 April 2026:</strong> individuals with qualifying income above <strong>£50,000</strong></li>
<li><strong>From 6 April 2027:</strong> those with qualifying income above <strong>£30,000</strong></li>
<li><strong>From 6 April 2028:</strong> those with qualifying income above <strong>£20,000</strong></li>
</ul>

<p>Qualifying income is broadly your gross self-employment and property income before expenses, so a fair number of Northampton landlords are in scope sooner than they expect. If you are caught, you keep digital records, use MTD-compatible software, send quarterly updates, and file a final declaration after the tax year ends. Limited companies are outside MTD for ITSA. For the income test that decides whether you are mandated, read our explainer on the <a href="/blog/making-tax-digital-mtd/mtd-itsa-qualifying-income-test-gross-vs-net">MTD qualifying income test (gross vs net)</a> and the practical <a href="/blog/making-tax-digital-mtd/making-tax-digital-landlords-april-2026-deadline">MTD for landlords guide</a>.</p>

<p>Landlords with property across several Northamptonshire postcodes benefit from a system that tracks income and expenses per property while meeting the digital-link rules, so your accountant should help with software selection, setup, and the move to quarterly reporting.</p>

<h3>Capital gains tax when you sell</h3>

<p>When you dispose of a residential rental property, the gain is taxed at <strong>18%</strong> within your basic-rate band and <strong>24%</strong> above it (the rates set by the Autumn Budget 2024). The annual exempt amount is <strong>£3,000</strong> per person. Where capital gains tax is due, you must report and pay through HMRC's UK property service within <strong>60 days of completion</strong>. Reliefs such as private residence relief, and the no-gain-no-loss transfer between spouses or civil partners, can change the outcome significantly, so the planning is best done before you market the property, not after you complete.</p>

<h2>HMO and student lets: West Northamptonshire's licensing regime</h2>

<p>HMOs are one of the most tax- and compliance-sensitive areas for Northampton landlords. Two layers of licensing can apply:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Mandatory HMO licensing</strong> applies UK-wide to larger shared houses (broadly five or more occupiers forming more than one household and sharing facilities).</li>
<li><strong>Additional HMO licensing</strong> run by West Northamptonshire Council covers smaller HMOs in designated parts of Northampton. The council has operated an additional licensing scheme since 2020 and has consulted on continuing and extending it, aligning the licensed area more closely with its <strong>Article 4 direction</strong> under the Town and Country Planning Act (which removes permitted development rights for converting family homes into smaller HMOs in named areas).</li>
</ul>

<p>Getting this wrong is expensive. Letting an unlicensed HMO where a licence is required can lead to a civil penalty of up to <strong>£30,000</strong>, prosecution with an unlimited fine, and a rent repayment order under the Housing and Planning Act 2016. On the tax side, licence fees and most ongoing compliance costs are allowable revenue expenses, while works that improve or extend the property are usually capital and added to your base cost for CGT. A specialist makes sure each cost lands in the right column. For how the tax treatment works, see our guide on whether <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/hmo-licensing-fees-tax-deductible-uk-landlords">HMO licensing fees are tax deductible</a>.</p>

<p>Student lets near the Waterside campus add further nuance: full-time students are generally exempt from council tax, void periods need careful cash-flow planning, and the tighter HMO standards for student houses feed directly into both your costs and your licensing obligations.</p>

<h2>Should you incorporate your Northampton portfolio?</h2>

<p>Because companies deduct finance costs in full, incorporation is the most common response to Section 24, especially for higher-rate landlords with significant mortgage interest. Corporation tax (currently 19% to 25% depending on profit levels) can compare favourably with personal rates, and a company can make reinvesting profit more efficient.</p>

<p>It is not a default answer. Moving existing property into a company can crystallise capital gains tax and trigger stamp duty land tax (including the additional-dwellings surcharge), and extracting profit as salary or dividends brings its own tax. Incorporation relief under section 162 can defer the CGT in genuine business cases, but it depends on the facts. The right call follows a proper review of your income, gearing, and plans. Our complete <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/buy-to-let-limited-company-complete-guide-uk">buy-to-let limited company guide</a> walks through the trade-offs in detail.</p>

<h2>Looking ahead: the April 2027 property income rates</h2>

<p>The Finance Act 2026 enacted a surcharge on UK property income that takes effect from <strong>6 April 2027</strong>, producing effective rates of <strong>22% basic, 42% higher and 47% additional</strong>. For the current 2026/27 tax year, rental income is still taxed at the standard 20%, 40% and 45% rates. A forward-looking accountant will factor the 2027/28 change into decisions you make now, including the timing of larger repairs, income smoothing where possible, and whether your current structure still fits.
