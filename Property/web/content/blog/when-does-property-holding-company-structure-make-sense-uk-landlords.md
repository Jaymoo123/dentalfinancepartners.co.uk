  - question: "What is a property holding company structure?"
    answer: "It is a group in which a parent company (the holding company) owns the shares in one or more subsidiary companies, and those subsidiaries hold the actual rental property. The holding company does not usually own property or trade directly. It receives dividends from the subsidiaries and holds the shares. Landlords use the structure to ring-fence risk between properties, to move profit around the group, and to sell a property by selling the company that owns it rather than the property itself."
  - question: "When does a holding company structure make sense rather than a single company?"
    answer: "When you have several properties or SPVs and a real reason to keep them in separate companies: ring-fencing a development or HMO from the rest of the portfolio, bringing different investors or family members into specific subsidiaries, or planning to sell individual properties as clean companies in future. For a one or two property landlord drawing the rent to live on, a single buy-to-let limited company is simpler, cheaper to run, and gives almost all of the corporate tax benefit without the group overhead."
  - question: "Is it better to have multiple SPVs or one company?"
    answer: "One company is cheaper to administer and avoids inter-company complexity, and it is the right answer for most landlords building a modest portfolio. Multiple SPVs make sense where you need to isolate risk (so a claim against one property cannot reach the others), where lenders require a single-property SPV, or where you want to sell or gift individual properties cleanly later. A holding company over several SPVs combines the ring-fencing of separate companies with group relief and a single point of control."
  - question: "What is the substantial shareholding exemption and does it apply to property companies?"
    answer: "The substantial shareholding exemption (SSE) can exempt a company from corporation tax on the gain when it sells shares in a trading company or trading group it has held for at least 12 months. The hard part for landlords is the trading test: a company that simply lets residential property is treated as an investment company, not a trading company, so the standard SSE rarely applies to a pure buy-to-let subsidiary. It is more relevant to genuine development or trading subsidiaries within a group. Do not assume SSE will shelter the sale of a let-property SPV without specific advice."
  - question: "How does group relief work between property companies?"
    answer: "Group relief lets a loss-making company in a 75% group surrender its current-year trading losses, excess interest and certain other amounts to a profitable company in the same group, reducing the group's overall corporation tax. For a property group this is most useful where one subsidiary runs at a loss (a development in progress, a heavily geared acquisition, a refurbishment year) while another is profitable. The companies must meet the 75% ownership tests and the relief is claimed on the corporation tax returns."
  - question: "Does transferring property into a company trigger capital gains tax?"
    answer: "Yes. Transferring property you own personally into your company is a disposal at market value to a connected person, so capital gains tax is due on the gain at 18% (basic rate) or 24% (higher rate) on residential property, after the £3,000 annual exempt amount. Section 162 incorporation relief can defer the gain where you transfer a genuine property business as a going concern in exchange for shares, but HMRC tests whether the lettings are a business rather than passive investment."
  - question: "Can I incorporate my property portfolio without paying capital gains tax?"
    answer: "Not without meeting strict conditions. The route to defer the CGT is section 162 TCGA 1992 incorporation relief, which rolls the gain into the base cost of the shares you receive, so no cash CGT is payable on the transfer itself. It only works if the lettings amount to a business, not investment, which usually means a portfolio under active, time-intensive management. Since Finance Act 2026 the relief must be claimed for transfers on or after 6 April 2026; it is no longer automatic. A single let or a lightly managed pair of flats will not qualify."
  - question: "What is holdover relief and when do property landlords use it?"
    answer: "Holdover relief under section 165 TCGA 1992 defers a capital gain when you gift a qualifying business asset, by passing the gain to the recipient through a reduced base cost. The catch for landlords is that section 165 holdover is limited to assets used in a trade, so it does not cover gifts of ordinary buy-to-let investment property or shares in an investment company. Landlords more commonly rely on section 162 incorporation relief on the way in, not section 165 holdover, unless there is a genuine trading element."
  - question: "Do I pay stamp duty when I move property into a holding company or SPV?"
    answer: "Usually yes. The company is treated as buying the property at market value, and as a company acquiring residential property it pays the 5% additional-dwellings SDLT surcharge on top of the standard bands. In Scotland it is LBTT plus the 8% Additional Dwelling Supplement; in Wales it is LTT at the higher residential rates. The main exception is a genuine partnership incorporation under FA 2003 Schedule 15, where the sum-of-lower-proportions rules can reduce the chargeable consideration, but only for a real pre-existing letting partnership."
  - question: "How do I get money out of a property holding company?"
    answer: "Profit usually flows up from the subsidiaries to the holding company as inter-company dividends, which are normally exempt from corporation tax, then out to you. Your options for the final step are salary, dividends taxed at 10.75% (basic), 35.75% (higher) or 39.35% (additional) above the £500 dividend allowance, or repayment of a director's loan. A director's loan credit created on a section 162 incorporation can often be drawn tax-free until it is exhausted, which is the most efficient early route."
  - question: "What is a director's loan account in a property company?"
    answer: "A director's loan account records money owed between you and the company. A credit balance means the company owes you, often because you transferred property or lent cash in, and you can usually draw it back tax-free. A debit balance means you owe the company; if it is still overdrawn nine months after the year end the company pays a section 455 charge of 35.75% (for loans made on or after 6 April 2026), refundable when you repay. Director's loans are a core extraction tool in property groups, but the overdrawn rules need watching."

A company still pays corporation tax of 19% to 25% on its rental profit, so the personal-versus-corporate gap on retained profit grows. The Section 24 finance-cost credit rises to 22% in step with the new basic rate, so no new wedge opens for basic-rate landlords."
  - question: "What is the minimum portfolio size for a holding company structure?"
    answer: "There is no legal minimum, but the group overhead (multiple sets of accounts, multiple corporation tax returns, multiple Companies House filings and inter-company administration) only pays for itself once the portfolio is large and diverse enough to need ring-fencing, group relief or staged disposals. Most landlords with a handful of standard buy-to-lets are better served by a single company. The decision should follow detailed modelling of your own portfolio, not a headline number of properties."

<p>A property holding company structure puts a parent company at the top of a group, with one or more subsidiary companies underneath that each hold the actual rental property. The parent (the holding company) typically owns nothing but the shares in its subsidiaries; the subsidiaries are the operating companies that own the buildings, collect the rent and carry the mortgages.</p>
<p>The structure is genuinely useful, but it is over-engineered for most landlords. If you own two flats and draw the rent to live on, a single <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/buy-to-let-limited-company-complete-guide-uk">buy-to-let limited company</a> gives you nearly all of the corporate tax advantage with none of the group overhead. The holding company earns its place when the portfolio is large enough, or diverse enough, that ring-fencing risk, moving losses around the group, and selling individual properties as clean companies start to matter more than the extra cost of running several entities.</p>

<h2>How a property holding company structure works</h2>
<p>You incorporate a parent company, then incorporate one or more subsidiaries below it, each owned by the parent. So "ABC Holdings Ltd" might own 100% of "ABC Property 1 Ltd" and "ABC Property 2 Ltd". Property 1 owns the HMO in one city; Property 2 owns three standard buy-to-lets in another. Each subsidiary is a self-contained SPV (special purpose vehicle): it has its own mortgage, its own tenants, its own accounts and its own corporation tax return.</p>
<p>Profit moves up the group as inter-company dividends. When a subsidiary pays a dividend to its parent, that dividend is normally exempt from corporation tax under the distribution exemption, so cash can pool at the holding company without a tax charge on the way up. The holding company then either reinvests across the group, lends down to a subsidiary that needs funds, or distributes to you as the ultimate shareholder. This is the same conduit logic explained in our guide to <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/multi-company-group-extraction-spv-holding-co-dividend-conduit-mechanics">multi-company group extraction</a>.</p>
<p>The shareholders sit above the holding company, not the individual SPVs. That is deliberate: it means you can bring an investor or a family member into the group at the top, or use alphabet share classes, without having to restructure each subsidiary. For the wider extraction and family-planning version of this, see our guide to <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/a-complete-guide-to-family-investment-companies-fics">family investment companies</a>.</p>

<h2>Single company, multiple SPVs, or a holding company group?</h2>
<p>The real decision is rarely "company or not". For a profitable, geared, higher-rate landlord the company usually wins; that case is set out in our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/2027-tax-rates-incorporation-decision-uk-landlords">2027 incorporation decision guide</a>. The harder question is how many companies and in what shape. The three common answers compare like this.</p>

<h2>The real advantages of a holding company group</h2>
<h3>Group relief moves losses to where the profit is</h3>
<p>Companies in a 75% group can surrender current-year losses and certain other amounts (excess interest, for example) to profitable companies in the same group. This is the strongest day-to-day reason to put SPVs under a holding company. A development or refurbishment subsidiary running at a loss, or a newly acquired heavily geared SPV, can surrender that loss to a mature, profitable letting subsidiary, cutting the group's overall corporation tax in the same year rather than carrying the loss forward. The detail of who qualifies sits in our guides to <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/eligible-groups-for-group-relief-under-uk-corporation-tax">eligible groups for group relief</a> and <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/property-company-group-relief-corporation-tax">property company group relief</a>.</p>
<p><strong>Worked example.</strong> Holdco owns two subsidiaries. Letting Co made a taxable profit of £80,000 this year. Development Co, mid-build, made a loss of £30,000. Without a group, Letting Co pays corporation tax on the full £80,000 and Development Co carries its £30,000 loss forward. Inside a 75% group, Development Co surrenders its £30,000 loss to Letting Co, which is taxed on £50,000 instead of £80,000. The group keeps the cash this year rather than waiting for Development Co to turn a profit.</p>

<h3>Selling a property by selling the company</h3>
<p>Holding property in separate SPVs means a future buyer can purchase the shares in the SPV rather than the building. That can be attractive to a buyer (they may avoid the SDLT they would pay on a direct purchase, though anti-avoidance and ATED-related rules need checking), and it lets you exit one property without disturbing the rest of the group.</p>
<p>What it does <em>not</em> automatically give you is a tax-free share sale. The substantial shareholding exemption (SSE) can exempt a company from corporation tax on a gain when it sells shares in a <em>trading</em> company it has held for at least 12 months. A company that simply lets residential property is an investment company, not a trading company, so the standard SSE rarely shelters the sale of a pure buy-to-let SPV. SSE is far more likely to be in point for a genuine development or trading subsidiary. Treat "sell the SPV and pay no tax" as a claim to verify, not a feature of the structure.</p>

<h3>Ring-fenced risk and flexible ownership</h3>
<p>Each subsidiary is a separate legal person with limited liability. A claim against the HMO subsidiary, or a default on its mortgage, does not reach the properties held in the other subsidiaries. For landlords running different risk profiles side by side (HMOs, a development, plain vanilla single lets, perhaps a commercial unit) that separation is worth real money in protected equity, and it is cleaner than holding everything in one company where a single problem contaminates the whole balance sheet.</p>
<p>Ownership flexibility follows from sitting the shareholders above the group. Different family members can hold different share classes at the holding company; growth shares can pass future value to the next generation; an external investor can come in at the top for a defined slice of the group. Note that buy-to-let investment companies do not qualify for business property relief for inheritance tax (the <em>Pawson</em> line of cases), so the group is an estate-planning tool through share design and lifetime gifts, not through an IHT exemption on the company itself.</p>

<h2>What it costs to build the structure</h2>
<h3>Capital gains tax on the way in</h3>
<p>Moving property you already own personally into a company (single or group) is a disposal to a connected person at market value, whatever cash actually changes hands. So <a href="/blog/capital-gains-tax/capital-gains-tax-property-complete-guide-uk">capital gains tax</a> is due on the gain at 18% for the basic-rate band and 24% above it on residential property, after the £3,000 annual exempt amount. On a portfolio with years of pent-up growth, that dry tax charge is often the single biggest obstacle to incorporating.</p>
<p>The route to defer it is section 162 TCGA 1992 incorporation relief, which rolls the gain into the base cost of the shares you receive so no cash CGT falls due on the transfer. It only works where the lettings amount to a <em>business</em> rather than passive investment, which in practice means a portfolio under active, time-intensive management; <em>Ramsay v HMRC</em> [2013] is the leading case. Since Finance Act 2026 the relief must be <em>claimed</em> for transfers on or after 6 April 2026; it is no longer automatic. Our guides to <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/incorporate-rental-property-without-cgt">incorporating without an immediate CGT charge</a> and to <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/incorporation-holdover-relief-property">holdover relief on incorporation</a> go deeper. Note that section 165 holdover relief is generally <em>not</em> available for gifts of investment property or investment-company shares, because it is limited to trading assets, so section 162 on the way in does most of the work.</p>

<h3>SDLT, and its devolved equivalents</h3>
<p>The company is treated as buying the property at market value, and as a company acquiring residential property it pays the 5% additional-dwellings SDLT surcharge on top of the standard bands. There is no general "incorporation relief" from SDLT. The main exception is a genuine partnership incorporation under FA 2003 Schedule 15, where the sum-of-lower-proportions rules can reduce the chargeable consideration, sometimes substantially, but only for a real pre-existing letting partnership.</p>
<p>The picture changes across the UK. In Scotland the charge is LBTT through Revenue Scotland, with an Additional Dwelling Supplement of 8%. In Wales it is LTT through the Welsh Revenue Authority at the higher residential rates. Where you are moving property <em>between</em> companies already in a 75% group, SDLT group relief can apply, subject to a clawback if the receiving company leaves the group within three years (the detail is in our note on <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/sdlt-group-relief-for-corporate-landlord-portfolios">SDLT group relief for corporate landlord portfolios</a>).</p>

<h2>Getting money out: dividends and director's loans</h2>
<p>Once profit has pooled at the holding company through exempt inter-company dividends, you still have to extract it to spend it, and that final step is taxed. Dividends to you carry the £500 dividend allowance and then 10.75% (basic), 35.75% (higher) or 39.35% (additional). Salary is deductible for the company but caught by income tax and National Insurance.</p>
<p>The most efficient early route is usually a director's loan. If you incorporated under section 162, the share-for-property exchange typically creates a credit balance on your director's loan account, and you can draw that credit back tax-free until it is exhausted; our guide to a <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/btl-spv-directors-loan-repayment-strategy-tax-efficient-extraction">director's loan repayment strategy</a> works through the sequencing. The discipline runs the other way too: if your loan account goes <em>overdrawn</em> (you owe the company) and is still overdrawn nine months after the year end, the company pays a section 455 charge of 35.75% on the outstanding balance for loans made on or after 6 April 2026, refundable when you repay. In a group, watch the credit balance running dry: a founder drawing rent receipts as loan repayments can exhaust an incorporation credit in a few years and be forced into higher-rate dividends sooner than planned.</p>

<h2>The compliance load, honestly stated</h2>
<p>Every company in the group files its own annual accounts, its own corporation tax return and its own confirmation statement, and late-filing penalties multiply across all of them. Each must keep a registered office (an "appropriate address", not a PO box) and a registered email for Companies House. Every director and every person with significant control must complete Companies House identity verification; the same individual is usually the PSC of all the SPVs, and one natural-person verification covers all of their directorships and PSC interests across the group, so this is per person, not per company.</p>
<p>Cash management across entities is the other real cost. Each subsidiary needs enough cash to meet its own mortgage, expenses and tax, inter-company loans need documenting, and dividend policy across the group needs reviewing rather than improvising. A dormant subsidiary still files. None of this is a reason to avoid the structure where it is justified, but it is the reason the structure is wrong for a small portfolio: the overhead is fixed and the benefits scale, so below a certain size the maths simply does not work.</p>

<h2>Alternatives worth weighing first</h2>
<p>A single buy-to-let company is the default for most landlords and delivers the corporation tax rate (19% on profits up to £50,000, 25% above £250,000, with a 26.5% marginal band between) and full mortgage interest deductibility that personal ownership lost to <a href="/blog/section-24-and-tax-relief/section-24-tax-relief-complete-guide">Section 24</a>. Note that a close investment-holding company is denied the small profits rate, but most let-property SPVs are not CIHCs because letting to unconnected tenants meets the qualifying-purpose carve-out; the detail is in our note on the <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/close-investment-holding-company-property">close investment-holding company rules</a>.</p>
<p>A limited liability partnership suits joint ventures and family partnerships where you want limited liability with tax transparency, since profits are taxed on the partners personally. And plenty of landlords are right to stay in personal ownership and manage tax through allowances, timing of disposals and pension contributions, particularly where the portfolio is small or the embedded CGT on incorporating is prohibitive. If you are weighing the threshold question, our guide on <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/incorporating-property-portfolio-uk-2026">incorporating a property portfolio</a> sets out the trade-offs.</p>

<h2>How the 2027 rates and MTD change the calculus</h2>
<p>From 6 April 2027 property income is charged at its own rates of 22% basic, 42% higher and 47% additional under Finance Act 2026, applying in England, Wales and Northern Ireland, with only Scotland outside the new structure. Companies are not affected; they pay corporation tax on rental profit as now. So the gap between personal and corporate taxation of <em>retained</em> profit widens for higher and additional-rate landlords, strengthening the corporate case for those who reinvest rather than draw the rent. The Section 24 finance-cost credit rises to 22% in step with the new basic rate, so no new basic-rate wedge opens; the structural problem (interest relieved at the basic rate while rent is taxed at your marginal rate) persists for higher-rate landlords held personally.</p>
<p><a href="/blog/making-tax-digital-mtd/making-tax-digital-landlords-april-2026-deadline">Making Tax Digital for Income Tax</a> is being phased in from 6 April 2026 for individuals with property income over £50,000, then 6 April 2027 (£30,000) and 6 April 2028 (£20,000). Property held inside a company is outside that quarterly cycle (companies file corporation tax returns), so a group can simplify your personal MTD position, though any property you still hold personally is tested against the thresholds in its own right.</p>

<h2>Making the decision</h2>
<p>A holding company over SPVs is a portfolio-scale tool, not a starter structure. It pays where you genuinely need to ring-fence risk, move losses around the group, fund subsidiaries centrally, bring in investors or family at the top, and sell properties as companies in future. It does not pay where a single company would do the same job for a fraction of the admin, and it is rarely worth triggering a large CGT and SDLT bill to build a group you do not yet need. Model the entry cost (CGT, SDLT or LBTT or LTT) against the ongoing benefit for your own numbers before you commit, and treat any claim that the structure is automatically tax-free, on the way in or on a future sale, as something to test rather than assume.</p>
