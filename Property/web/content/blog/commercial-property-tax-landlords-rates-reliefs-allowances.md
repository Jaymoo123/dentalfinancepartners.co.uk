<p><strong>Commercial property tax for landlords</strong> works differently from residential buy-to-let taxation. While residential landlords grapple with Section 24 restrictions, commercial landlords operate under more favourable rules — but face their own complexities around corporation tax, capital allowances, and business rates.</p>

<p>This guide covers everything commercial property investors need to know about tax rates, reliefs, and allowances in 2026.</p>

<h2>How Is Commercial Property Income Taxed?</h2>

<p>Commercial rental income is treated as property income for tax purposes, just like residential rentals. However, the key difference is that <strong>Section 24 restrictions do not apply to commercial property</strong>.</p>

<p>This means commercial landlords can still deduct mortgage interest and finance costs in full against their rental income, unlike residential landlords who are restricted to a 20% tax credit.</p>

<h3>Tax Rates on Commercial Property Income</h3>

<p>For individual landlords, commercial rental profits are added to other income and taxed at marginal rates:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>2026/27 rates:</strong> 20% basic rate (£12,571-£50,270), 40% higher rate (£50,271-£125,140), 45% additional rate (£125,140+)</li>
<li><strong>From April 2027:</strong> Separate property income tax rates apply — 22% basic, 42% higher, 47% additional rate on all property income</li>
</ul>

<p>Many commercial landlords opt for limited company ownership to benefit from corporation tax rates of 19% (up to £250k profits) or 25% (above £250k).</p>

<h2>Key Differences Between Commercial and Residential Property Tax</h2>

<p><strong>Commercial landlord tax</strong> benefits from several advantages over residential property investment:</p>

<h3>No Section 24 Restrictions</h3>

<p>Commercial property remains exempt from Section 24. A commercial landlord with £100k rental income and £40k mortgage interest can deduct the full £40k, leaving £60k taxable profit.</p>

<p>The same figures for a residential landlord would result in £80k taxable income (with a 20% tax credit on the £40k interest).</p>

<h3>Capital Allowances Available</h3>

<p>Commercial properties qualify for capital allowances on fixtures and fittings, plant and machinery. This can include:</p>

<ul>
<li>Heating and cooling systems</li>
<li>Electrical installations</li>
<li>Lifts and escalators</li>
<li>Fire safety systems</li>
<li>Fitted furniture in serviced offices</li>
</ul>

<p>The Annual Investment Allowance (AIA) provides 100% tax relief on qualifying expenditure up to £1 million per year.</p>

<h3>VAT Considerations</h3>

<p>Commercial property can be subject to VAT through the "option to tax" election. While this means charging VAT on rent, it also allows recovery of VAT on expenses and improvements — potentially beneficial for substantial refurbishments.</p>

<h2>Business Premises Tax and Business Rates</h2>

<p>Commercial properties are subject to business rates rather than council tax. The landlord typically pays business rates on vacant properties, while tenants pay for occupied premises.</p>

<h3>Empty Property Relief</h3>

<p>Business rates relief is available for empty commercial properties:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Industrial properties:</strong> 100% relief for first 6 months</li>
<li><strong>Other commercial properties:</strong> 100% relief for first 3 months</li>
<li><strong>Properties with rateable value under £2,900:</strong> Ongoing 100% relief while empty</li>
</ul>

<p>After the relief period, full business rates apply to empty properties.</p>

<h2>Allowable Expenses for Commercial Property Landlords</h2>

<p>Commercial property expenses follow similar principles to residential, but with broader scope:</p>

<h3>Fully Deductible Expenses</h3>

<ul>
<li>Mortgage interest and finance costs (no Section 24 restriction)</li>
<li>Property management fees</li>
<li>Insurance premiums</li>
<li>Business rates on empty properties</li>
<li>Professional fees (legal, accountancy, surveying)</li>
<li>Marketing and letting fees</li>
<li>Repairs and maintenance</li>
</ul>

<h3>Capital Expenditure and Allowances</h3>

<p>Unlike residential property, commercial properties can claim capital allowances on qualifying fixtures and fittings. A commercial landlord refurbishing offices might claim allowances on:</p>

<ul>
<li>Air conditioning systems</li>
<li>Lighting installations</li>
<li>Fitted kitchens in office spaces</li>
<li>Security systems</li>
</ul>

<p>These allowances provide immediate tax relief rather than waiting for disposal to claim capital gains relief.</p>

<h2>Corporation Tax for Commercial Property Companies</h2>

<p>Many commercial property investors use limited companies to benefit from lower corporation tax rates. A commercial property company with £200k annual profit pays 19% corporation tax (£38k), compared to a higher-rate individual paying 40% income tax (£80k).</p>

<p>However, extracting profits through dividends incurs additional tax. The combined corporation tax and dividend tax often approximates higher-rate income tax, though timing benefits exist.</p>

<h3>Close Company Benefits</h3>

<p>Commercial property companies can provide certain tax-efficient benefits to director-shareholders:</p>

<ul>
<li>Use of company-owned commercial premises</li>
<li>Business-related travel expenses</li>
<li>Professional development costs</li>
</ul>

<p>These benefits must have a genuine business purpose to avoid benefit-in-kind charges.</p>

<h2>Capital Gains Tax on Commercial Property</h2>

<p>Commercial property disposals are subject to capital gains tax at the same rates as residential property:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>18%</strong> for basic rate taxpayers</li>
<li><strong>24%</strong> for higher and additional rate taxpayers</li>
</ul>

<p>The annual exempt amount is £3,000 for 2026/27.</p>

<h3>Business Asset Disposal Relief</h3>

<p>Commercial property may qualify for Business Asset Disposal Relief (formerly Entrepreneurs' Relief) if it forms part of a trading business. This reduces CGT to 10% on gains up to £1 million lifetime limit.</p>

<p>Pure property investment rarely qualifies, but property businesses with substantial additional services might meet the criteria.</p>

<h2>Stamp Duty on Commercial Property</h2>

<p>Commercial property stamp duty rates differ from residential SDLT:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>0-£150k:</strong> 0%</li>
<li><strong>£150k-£250k:</strong> 2%</li>
<li><strong>Over £250k:</strong> 5%</li>
</ul>

<p>The 5% surcharge on additional residential properties does not apply to commercial property purchases.</p>

<h2>VAT on Commercial Property</h2>

<p>Most commercial property rental is exempt from VAT, but landlords can elect to charge VAT through the "option to tax".</p>

<h3>Benefits of VAT Election</h3>

<ul>
<li>Recover VAT on purchase costs and improvements</li>
<li>Deduct VAT on ongoing expenses</li>
<li>Particularly beneficial for substantial refurbishments</li>
</ul>

<h3>Drawbacks of VAT Election</h3>

<ul>
<li>Must charge VAT on rent (usually passed to tenant)</li>
<li>May make property less attractive to VAT-exempt tenants</li>
<li>Election typically irrevocable for 20 years</li>
</ul>

<h2>Record Keeping for Commercial Property Tax</h2>

<p>Commercial property requires detailed records for tax compliance:</p>

<ul>
<li>Rental income by property and tenant</li>
<li>All allowable expenses with supporting invoices</li>
<li>Capital expenditure and improvement costs</li>
<li>Business rates payments and empty property periods</li>
<li>VAT records if option to tax elected</li>
</ul>

<p><a href="/blog/making-tax-digital-mtd/making-tax-digital-landlords-april-2026-deadline">Making Tax Digital</a> applies to landlords with gross property income over £10,000 from April 2026, requiring quarterly digital reporting.</p>

<h2>Planning Considerations for Commercial Property Investors</h2>

<h3>Structure Decision</h3>

<p>The choice between individual ownership and <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/buy-to-let-limited-company-complete-guide-uk">limited company incorporation</a> is crucial for commercial property. Companies benefit from:</p>

<ul>
<li>Lower corporation tax rates on retained profits</li>
<li>More flexible extraction strategies</li>
<li>Enhanced capital allowances planning</li>
<li>Easier portfolio expansion</li>
</ul>

<h3>Portfolio Diversification</h3>

<p>Commercial property offers different risk-return profiles from residential. Consider the tax implications of mixed portfolios, particularly if owning both commercial and residential properties in the same company structure.</p>

<h3>Exit Planning</h3>

<p>Commercial property disposals often involve larger capital sums than residential sales. Consider CGT planning through:</p>

<ul>
<li>Annual exempt amount utilisation</li>
<li>Timing of disposals across tax years</li>
<li>Potential Business Asset Disposal Relief claims</li>
<li>Company vs individual ownership for CGT rates</li>
</ul>

<h2>Common Commercial Property Tax Mistakes</h2>

<p>Avoid these frequent errors that can prove costly:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Not claiming capital allowances:</strong> Missing out on immediate tax relief on qualifying fixtures</li>
<li><strong>Incorrect VAT treatment:</strong> Failing to consider option to tax benefits on major refurbishments</li>
<li><strong>Poor record keeping:</strong> Inadequate documentation for business rates, empty periods, and expense claims</li>
<li><strong>Ignoring business rates relief:</strong> Not claiming available empty property relief</li>
<li><strong>Mixing residential and commercial:</strong> Incorrect application of Section 24 rules across property types</li>
</ul>
