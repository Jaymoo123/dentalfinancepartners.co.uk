<h2>Leicester's BTL Market in 2026</h2>

<p>The Leicester rental market splits roughly into four segments:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Student HMOs (Highfields, Stoneygate, Clarendon Park, West End):</strong> high yield (7-9% gross via 4-6 bed conversions), high management intensity, mandatory HMO licensing required, often inside Article 4 zones where C3-to-C4 conversion needs full planning consent.</li>
<li><strong>Family BTL (Oadby, Wigston, Knighton, Glenfield):</strong> 5-6% gross yields, lower management overhead, stronger capital growth potential, standard BTL mortgages and tax treatment.</li>
<li><strong>City centre flats and young professional lets (Highcross area, Cathedral Quarter):</strong> 5-7% yields, growing demand from professionals commuting to Birmingham via the M1, often new-build with leasehold management considerations.</li>
<li><strong>Affordable single-let stock (Belgrave, Spinney Hills, Beaumont Leys, Saffron Lane):</strong> 6-8% yields, generally lower property prices, sometimes subject to selective licensing depending on ward.</li>
</ul>

<p>Each segment has different tax implications. Student HMOs have heavier compliance overhead but higher gross yield. Family BTLs are simpler operationally but face the same Section 24 mechanic at lower headline yield. The right portfolio mix and ownership structure depends on which segments you operate in and your tax band.</p>

<h2>The Tax Pressures Hitting Leicester Landlords in 2026</h2>

<h3>Section 24 mortgage interest restriction</h3>
<p>The Section 24 rules (Finance (No.2) Act 2015, now in <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2005/5" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">ITTOIA 2005</a>) replace full mortgage interest deduction with a 20% basic-rate tax credit for individual BTL landlords. For high-yield student HMO portfolios, the absolute interest cost relative to rent often pushes effective tax rates above 50% of pre-interest profit. HMRC's <a href="https://www.gov.uk/hmrc-internal-manuals/property-income-manual" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Property Income Manual</a> covers the mechanics.</p>

<h3>Making Tax Digital from April 2026</h3>
<p>MTD-for-ITSA became mandatory on 6 April 2026 for sole-trader landlords with combined gross property and self-employment income above £50,000. The threshold drops to £30,000 from April 2027 and £20,000 from April 2028. Quarterly digital submissions via approved software replace traditional self-assessment. HMRC's <a href="https://www.gov.uk/guidance/check-when-to-sign-up-for-making-tax-digital-for-income-tax" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">sign-up checker</a> confirms whether you are caught.</p>

<h3>Separate property income tax rates from April 2027</h3>
<p>From 6 April 2027, rental profit faces separate rates of 22% basic, 42% higher, and 47% additional. For a higher-rate Leicester landlord with £30,000 of rental profit, the rate change adds roughly £600 to the annual tax bill before any behavioural response. The change strengthens the case for incorporation for many higher-rate landlords.</p>

<h3>Leicester HMO licensing and Article 4 directions</h3>
<p>Leicester City Council operates national mandatory HMO licensing (for any HMO with 5+ occupants forming 2+ households), additional HMO licensing schemes for smaller HMOs in defined wards, and selective licensing in specific areas. Article 4 directions in parts of the city remove permitted development rights for converting a standard family home (C3 use class) to a small HMO (C4 use class) without planning permission. Always check the council's planning portal at <a href="https://www.leicester.gov.uk" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">leicester.gov.uk</a> before exchange on a property intended for HMO conversion.</p>

<h2>Worked Example: Section 24 Impact on a Leicester Student HMO</h2>

<p>A 5-bed student HMO in Stoneygate bought for £350,000 with a 75% LTV mortgage at 5.5% (£14,438 annual interest). Five-room let at £550/month per room (£33,000/year gross). Allowable non-finance expenses (management, licensing, utilities included, repairs, accountancy) £10,500/year.</p>

<p><strong>Personal ownership (higher-rate taxpayer):</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Rental profit before interest restriction: £33,000 − £10,500 = £22,500</li>
<li>Taxable rental profit (after Section 24 add-back): £22,500</li>
<li>Income tax at 40%: £9,000</li>
<li>Less Section 24 tax credit (20% × £14,438): £2,888</li>
<li>Net income tax: £6,112</li>
<li>Cash position: £33,000 − £10,500 − £14,438 − £6,112 = <strong>£1,950</strong></li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Limited company ownership (same property):</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Rental profit after all expenses including full interest: £33,000 − £10,500 − £14,438 = £8,062</li>
<li>Corporation tax at 19% (small profits rate): £1,532</li>
<li>Net retained profit in company: £6,530</li>
<li>Cash position: <strong>£6,530</strong></li>
</ul>

<p>The structural difference is roughly £4,580 per year on this single property, driven by Section 24. Across a 3-4 HMO portfolio at similar gearing, that becomes £15,000-£20,000 per year, which generally clears any reasonable incorporation cost analysis within 2-3 years. The limited company route has its own costs (higher mortgage rates, transfer SDLT and CGT, ongoing compliance), but the gap is usually decisive.</p>

<h2>Allowable Expenses for Leicester BTL Landlords</h2>

<p>The Section 24 restriction only applies to finance costs. Everything else on this list is fully deductible against rental income (subject to the wholly-and-exclusively rule).</p>

<ul>
<li>Letting agent fees and management charges (typically 10-15% of rent for family BTL, 12-15% for HMOs)</li>
<li>Mortgage interest (restricted to 20% tax credit for individuals; fully deductible for companies)</li>
<li>Repairs and maintenance (like-for-like replacement is repair; upgrade is capital)</li>
<li>Buildings and contents insurance including landlord liability cover</li>
<li>Utility bills where the landlord pays (common in student HMOs with bills-included rents)</li>
<li>Council tax during void periods (and during summer voids in student HMOs)</li>
<li>Ground rent and service charges on leasehold properties</li>
<li>Legal and professional fees for tenancy agreements and disputes</li>
<li>Accountancy fees attributable to the rental business</li>
<li>HMO licensing fees and Leicester selective/additional licensing fees</li>
<li>Gas safety certificates, EICR (every 5 years), EPC (every 10 years)</li>
<li>Replacement of domestic items relief under ITTOIA 2005 s.311A (high relevance for student lets)</li>
<li>Travel costs for property visits (HMRC mileage rate 45p per mile for first 10,000 miles)</li>
</ul>

<h2>When Your General Accountant Is Costing You Money</h2>

<ul>
<li><strong>Your Section 24 modelling has never been done.</strong> If your accountant has not shown you the gap between your current personal tax bill and the same portfolio inside a limited company, they are filing, not advising.</li>
<li><strong>Incorporation has been dismissed without numbers.</strong> "It's not worth it" is an opinion, not an analysis. The right answer depends on your marginal rate, gearing, age, exit timeline, and intentions for the portfolio.</li>
<li><strong>You have not been told about the April 2027 rate change.</strong> A proactive accountant raises this 12-18 months ahead.</li>
<li><strong>MTD preparation is not on the agenda.</strong> If MTD has not been discussed and you are above the £50,000 income threshold, your accountant is leaving you exposed to penalties.</li>
<li><strong>Replacement of Domestic Items Relief is being missed.</strong> Especially impactful for high-turnover student lets where furniture replacement is frequent.</li>
<li><strong>Article 4 implications on intended HMO purchases have not been flagged.</strong> Buying a property for HMO conversion in an Article 4 zone without flagging the planning consent requirement can cost five figures.</li>
</ul>
