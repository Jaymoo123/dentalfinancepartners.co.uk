<p>There's a common misconception among basic rate taxpayers that Section 24 — the mortgage interest restriction — doesn't affect them. The thinking goes: "I'm only paying 20% tax anyway, so the 20% tax credit balances out."</p>

<p>This assumption can be costly. Section 24 affects basic rate taxpayers in ways that aren't immediately obvious, potentially pushing you into higher tax brackets and reducing your actual take-home income from property.</p>

<h2>How Section 24 Works for Basic Rate Taxpayers</h2>

<p>Before Section 24, you could deduct mortgage interest as a business expense. Now, you add the interest back to your taxable income, then claim a 20% tax credit against your final tax bill.</p>

<p>For basic rate taxpayers, this creates several problems:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Income bracket creep:</strong> Adding back mortgage interest can push you over the higher rate threshold (£50,270 for 2025/26)</li>
<li><strong>Benefit restrictions:</strong> Higher reported income can affect child benefit and other means-tested support</li>
<li><strong>Cash flow impact:</strong> You pay more tax upfront and get the relief later</li>
<li><strong>Student loan implications:</strong> Higher income triggers larger student loan repayments</li>
</ul>

<h2>Real Example: Basic Rate Taxpayer Impact</h2>

<p>Consider Sarah, who earns £35,000 from her day job and has two BTL properties generating £18,000 rental income. Her annual mortgage interest is £12,000.</p>

<p><strong>Before Section 24:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Employment income: £35,000</li>
<li>Rental profit (£18,000 - £12,000): £6,000</li>
<li>Total taxable income: £41,000</li>
<li>Tax rate: 20% basic rate</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>After Section 24:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Employment income: £35,000</li>
<li>Rental income: £18,000 (no mortgage deduction)</li>
<li>Total taxable income: £53,000</li>
<li>Tax: 20% on £50,270 + 40% on £2,730</li>
<li>Less 20% credit on £12,000 mortgage interest</li>
</ul>

<p>Sarah now pays higher rate tax on £2,730 and loses child benefit if she has children. Her effective tax rate on the property income increases significantly.</p>

<h2>The Hidden Costs Beyond Tax Rates</h2>

<p>The impact goes beyond just tax calculations. Many basic rate taxpayers discover they're affected by:</p>

<p><strong>Child Benefit Clawback:</strong> If your adjusted net income exceeds £50,000, you start losing child benefit. The mortgage interest addition can trigger this unexpectedly.</p>

<p><strong>Student Loan Repayments:</strong> Plan 2 student loans require 9% repayments on income over £27,295. Adding back mortgage interest increases your repayable income.</p>

<p><strong>Personal Allowance Tapering:</strong> While this starts at £100,000, landlords with multiple properties can find themselves closer to this threshold than expected.</p>

<h2>When Basic Rate Taxpayers Are Most at Risk</h2>

<p>You're particularly vulnerable to Section 24 impact if:</p>

<ul>
<li>Your total income is between £40,000-£60,000</li>
<li>You have high mortgage interest relative to rental income</li>
<li>You receive child benefit or other income-tested benefits</li>
<li>You're still paying student loans</li>
<li>You have other income sources that fluctuate</li>
</ul>

<p>Even landlords with just one or two properties can find themselves caught out, especially in areas where property values (and therefore mortgage amounts) are high relative to rental yields.</p>

<h2>What You Can Do About It</h2>

<p>If you're a basic rate taxpayer affected by Section 24, you have several options:</p>

<p><strong>Consider Incorporation:</strong> Moving your properties into a limited company can restore full mortgage interest deductibility. However, this involves other costs and complications that need careful analysis.</p>

<p><strong>Reduce Other Income:</strong> If possible, consider timing bonuses, dividend payments, or pension contributions to manage your total income level.</p>

<p><strong>Review Your Portfolio:</strong> High-yield, low-mortgage properties are less affected by Section 24 than highly leveraged investments.</p>
