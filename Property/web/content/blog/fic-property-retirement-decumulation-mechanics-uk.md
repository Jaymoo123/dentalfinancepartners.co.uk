faqs:
  - question: "Can I use my property FIC as a retirement income vehicle?"
    answer: "Yes, and three FIC-side income strands are typically available. First, the directors' loan account credit balance from the section 162 incorporation transfer can be repaid to the founder tax-free until the balance reaches zero; for a £500,000 incorporation-credit DLA this can produce £25,000 to £50,000 a year of tax-free runway over 10 to 20 years. Second, the preference share coupon (typically a fixed 4% to 7% cumulative dividend on the paid-up preference value) produces a predictable dividend stream taxed in the founder's hands at dividend rates (8.75%, 33.75%, or 39.35% depending on marginal band). Third, preference share redemption returns par value to the founder as a capital event and any excess above par as a distribution. The three strands layer with state pension and any private pension drawdown to give the founder a four or five strand income mix to sequence."
  - question: "How does a FIC retirement income compare to a SIPP drawdown for a property founder?"
    answer: "The FIC and the SIPP are complementary, not substitutes, for most property founders. SIPP drawdown gives 25% tax-free cash up to the lump sum allowance (£268,275 for most savers from April 2024 onward) plus taxable income from the remaining 75% pension fund; the SIPP cannot directly hold residential property without incurring unauthorised payment charges. The FIC owns the property portfolio directly and produces income via the three FIC strands described above. For most property founders, the optimal sequencing layers SIPP tax-free cash with FIC DLA repayment in the early decumulation years (to keep both inside personal allowance + basic-rate band), shifts to SIPP taxable drawdown plus FIC preference coupon as the DLA exhausts, and finally relies on FIC preference coupon plus optional preference redemption for the late decumulation years. The exact split depends on the relative sizes of the SIPP pot and the FIC's distributable reserves."
  - question: "What happens to my DLA credit balance when I retire and stop working at the company?"
    answer: "Nothing automatic; the credit balance survives the founder's withdrawal from active management and continues to be repayable on the same tax-free basis. The DLA balance is a debt owed by the company to the director, not employment income, and its tax treatment is independent of whether the founder is still working. The practical sequencing question is whether to accelerate DLA repayment in the early decumulation years (using up the tax-free runway sooner, freeing later years to rely on dividend extraction) or to stretch the DLA repayment across the decumulation horizon (preserving tax-free capacity in case other income strands change). For most founders, the stretched approach matches the typical declining marginal rate through decumulation as employment income drops away."
  - question: "How much can I draw from a FIC preference share coupon each year?"
    answer: "Depends on the paid-up preference value and the coupon rate set in the articles. A typical property FIC issues preference shares at a paid-up value equal to the company's pre-growth-share value (often £500,000 to £2 million for a mid-size portfolio), with a 5% cumulative coupon. The annual coupon on a £1 million preference value at 5% is £50,000. The company must have sufficient distributable reserves under section 830 of the Companies Act 2006 to declare the dividend; for a property FIC with steady rental profits, this is rarely the constraint. The coupon is taxed as dividend income in the founder's hands, so the £50,000 sits within the basic-rate dividend band (above the £500 dividend allowance) and produces a dividend tax bill of around £4,250 for a founder with no other income, rising depending on other income strands."
  - question: "What is preference share redemption and how does it work as a retirement income strand?"
    answer: "Preference share redemption is the mechanism under sections 684 to 689 of the Companies Act 2006 by which the company buys back its own preference shares from the holder. For a property FIC the founder typically holds the preference class; on redemption, the company pays the founder the redemption price (typically par plus any cumulative unpaid dividend). The redemption is funded from distributable reserves under section 687 or from the proceeds of a fresh share issue under section 688. The tax treatment splits: under CTA 2010 section 1000 onwards, the proceeds up to the issue price of the share are treated as a return of share capital (a capital event for the holder, taxed under CGT rules with the £3,000 annual exempt amount available in 2026/27 and CGT rates of 18% or 24% for residential-property-backed shares), and any excess above the issue price is treated as a distribution (taxed as a dividend in the founder's hands). The articles set the redemption mechanic; the amortisation profile (typically a tranche each year or each five years from a defined age) lets the founder convert frozen preference capital into income across the decumulation horizon."
  - question: "Can I take dividends only from my own A-class without paying dividends on the children's growth class?"
    answer: "Yes, and this is exactly what the discretionary class-by-class dividend mechanic in the articles is designed to allow. The directors (typically including the founder during the founder's active years) declare a dividend on the A-class without declaring on the B-class or any other class. The discretion must be genuine: the decision is minuted at the time of declaration, the dividend is paid into the founder's separate personal account, and the company's distributable reserves under section 830 cover the dividend declared. The dividend on the A-class is taxed in the founder's hands at dividend rates; the B-class holders receive no dividend that year so no settlements-legislation question arises on them. The companion settlements-boundary analysis is in our <a href=\"/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/alphabet-shares-property-spv-dividend-splitting-spouse-children\">alphabet shares page</a>."
  - question: "What is the dividend cliff at £50,270 and how do I avoid it?"
    answer: "The basic-rate dividend rate (8.75%) applies up to total income of £50,270 in 2026/27; the higher-rate dividend rate (33.75%) applies from £50,271 upwards. A property founder with combined state pension, FIC dividend coupon, and any other taxable income approaching £50,270 faces a marginal effective dividend rate jumping from 8.75% to 33.75% on the next pound. The avoidance mechanics: time DLA repayments (tax-free) into higher-marginal-rate years; defer FIC preference dividend declarations to lower-income years; consider spouse-side dividend allocation under the alphabet-share mechanic if the spouse has unused basic-rate capacity; sequence private pension drawdown to fill basic-rate capacity ahead of FIC dividend. The 33.75 to 39.35% cliff at £125,140 has similar avoidance logic, with the personal allowance taper between £100,000 and £125,140 (£1 of allowance lost for every £2 of income above £100,000) producing an effective marginal rate around 60% on income in that band."
  - question: "Should I leave rental profits inside the FIC or extract them as dividend?"
    answer: "Depends on the founder's current marginal rate and the planned use of the cash. Retained profits remain in the company at corporation tax rates (19% small profits, 25% main, 26.5% marginal-relief band per CTA 2010 sections 18-19 in 2026/27) and accumulate as distributable reserves available for future dividend declarations. Extracted profits hit dividend tax at the founder's marginal rate (8.75%, 33.75%, or 39.35%). For a founder already in the higher-rate band who does not need the cash, retention can produce a better after-tax outcome than extraction. For a founder in the basic-rate band who would otherwise leave personal allowance unused, extraction up to the basic-rate threshold is typically the right answer. The cumulative-profit retention discipline can also create flexibility in later years when the founder may need a higher draw (medical care, gifting to grandchildren, downsizing assistance)."
  - question: "Does the FIC retirement income mechanic also save IHT?"
    answer: "Indirectly, but the IHT-side framing is a separate question from the income-side framing on this page. The FIC's value-freeze mechanic (founder holds preference + A-class, growth accrues to B-class held by next generation) does transfer wealth out of the founder's estate from issue, and the share-gift PET mechanic transfers more on each gift. Those mechanics live in our wave 4 FIC-as-IHT-tool page and our FIC growth-share gifting page. The retirement income strands on this page (DLA repayment, preference coupon, preference redemption) are about putting cash in the founder's hands during life; the cash that arrives in the founder's personal account from those strands is then part of the founder's estate on death like any other personal asset. The IHT planning question is what happens to the underlying FIC value, not to the income paid out from it."
  - question: "Can my spouse share in the FIC retirement income through their own share class?"
    answer: "Yes, where the articles include a spouse class (typically a C-class with discretionary dividend rights). Dividends declared on the spouse's class are taxed in the spouse's hands at their own marginal rate, with their own £500 dividend allowance and their own basic-rate threshold. For a founder with significant FIC dividend extraction approaching the higher-rate threshold, declaring a dividend on the spouse's class with unused basic-rate capacity can reduce the combined household dividend tax bill by 25 percentage points per pound (the 33.75 vs 8.75 differential). The settlements legislation under ITTOIA 2005 section 624 sits over this; the section 626 spouse-exception carve-out applies to outright gifts of ordinary shares to a spouse, and the dividend mechanic in the articles must support genuine board discretion."
  - question: "What if my FIC is a close investment-holding company?"
    answer: "A CIHC under CTA 2010 section 18N is denied the small profits corporation tax rate; profits are taxed at 25% main rate regardless of profit level. For a retired founder drawing income from the FIC, the CIHC status raises the cost of dividend extraction by the 6 percentage point differential between the small profits rate (19%) and the main rate (25%) on profits up to £50,000, and by 1.5 percentage points on profits in the marginal-relief band (26.5% effective for non-CIHC vs 25% for CIHC). The CIHC test under section 18N excludes investment in land let to unconnected parties (the qualifying-purpose carve-out); most property FICs let to third-party tenants are NOT CIHCs. A FIC that lets predominantly to family members, or that holds securities and cash rather than property, is more likely to fall within CIHC. The status is per-FIC and should be checked annually."
  - question: "How does my state pension interact with the FIC income strands?"
    answer: "The state pension is taxable income for income tax purposes, with no NI on it (NI applies only to earnings, not pension). The state pension sits against the personal allowance (£12,570 for most taxpayers in 2026/27) so a state-pension-only retiree typically pays no income tax. For a founder also drawing FIC dividends, the state pension uses the personal allowance first (covering it entirely), the dividend allowance £500 covers the next slice, and then the basic-rate dividend rate of 8.75% applies up to £50,270 of total taxable income. The state pension claim begins at state pension age (66 as of 2026/27, rising to 67 from April 2028 onwards); the founder can defer the claim to increase the weekly rate. The deferral mechanic is rarely worth using for a property founder with substantial FIC income because the FIC income absorbs the personal allowance value the deferred state pension would otherwise capture."

Age 70 to 75 (mid decumulation), DLA still being repaid but slowing; FIC preference coupon becomes the primary income strand; state pension layers in; SIPP taxable drawdown layers in at basic-rate capacity. Age 75 to 85 (late decumulation), DLA fully repaid or nearly so; FIC preference coupon plus selective preference redemption tranches; state pension at full level; SIPP balance preserved or drawn down based on the IHT-2027 cohort question (covered in our wave 4 pension-decumulation page). Age 85 onwards, FIC preference coupon remains; preference redemption decisions per the articles' redemption schedule; consideration of dividend shifts to spouse class for marginal rate planning. The exact figures depend on the FIC size, the DLA credit balance, the SIPP pot, and the founder's circumstances; the framework is the sequencing logic, not the figures."

<p>A property founder approaching retirement holds three FIC-side income strands and two outside the FIC. The directors' loan account credit balance from the section 162 incorporation transfer is the tax-free runway. The preference share coupon, declared by the board at a fixed cumulative rate, is the predictable dividend strand. Preference share redemption, run as an amortisation profile across defined years, returns capital up to the issue price and any excess as a distribution. State pension and any private pension drawdown layer on top with their own marginal-rate consequences. The after-tax outcome turns on how these five strands are sequenced through the decumulation horizon, not on any one strand in isolation.</p>

<p>This page walks the three FIC strands in turn, identifies how each is taxed in the founder's hands, lays out the dividend cliffs to keep clear of (the 8.75 to 33.75 percent jump at £50,270, the 33.75 to 39.35 percent jump at £125,140, the personal allowance taper between £100,000 and £125,140), and works a default sequencing across four founder ages (65, 70, 75, 85). Out of scope: the IHT value-freeze framing of the FIC architecture as a next-generation wealth-transfer tool (covered separately in our wave 4 FIC-as-IHT-tool page); the share-gift Potentially Exempt Transfer mechanic at the point of gift (covered separately in our wave 4 FIC growth-share gifting page); and the blended-family use case (covered separately). This page is income now, during the founder's life.</p>

<h2>Strand one: the directors' loan account credit-balance runway</h2>

<p>The DLA credit balance is the tax-free income runway from the founder's incorporation. Where a property portfolio was transferred into the FIC under <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12/section/162">section 162 TCGA 1992</a> incorporation relief, the founder typically received a mix of shares (the freezer/preference class plus often an initial growth-share allocation) and a loan owed to the founder by the company equal to the balancing figure. The loan can be repaid out of post-corporation-tax profits at any rate the company can support, and the repayment is tax-free in the founder's hands (it is repayment of a debt, not income).</p>

<p>The runway calculation: a £500,000 DLA credit balance can produce £25,000 to £50,000 a year of tax-free income for 10 to 20 years depending on the repayment cadence. The trade-off: the faster the DLA is drawn down, the sooner the founder loses the tax-free strand and shifts to dividend extraction (taxed at dividend rates) for further income. Most founders sequence the DLA repayment to span the decumulation horizon rather than front-loading or back-loading it.</p>

<p>The DLA can also carry interest. The company can pay the founder interest on the credit balance, deductible by the company against corporation tax (within the CTA 2010 section 453 close-company rules) and taxable income on the founder. The interest rate cannot exceed the <a href="https://www.gov.uk/hmrc-internal-manuals/employment-income-manual/eim26101">HMRC official rate of interest</a> (published quarterly, recent rates in the 2% to 3.75% range) without triggering benefit-in-kind treatment on the excess. Interest is taxable as savings income (with the £1,000 savings allowance for basic-rate taxpayers, £500 for higher-rate, nil for additional-rate), so it sits in a different tax box from the repayment-of-principal element of the DLA reduction.</p>

<h2>Strand two: the preference share coupon</h2>

<p>The preference class in a property FIC's articles typically carries a fixed cumulative coupon, expressed as a percentage of paid-up value (4% to 7% is the typical range, set at incorporation). For a £1 million paid-up preference value at a 5% coupon, the annual dividend is £50,000. The coupon accrues whether or not the dividend is declared in any given year (cumulative), so the founder can defer declaration in higher-marginal-rate years and catch up in lower-marginal-rate years.</p>

<p>The mechanics of declaration: the board declares the dividend on the preference class only, dated and signed before the cash moves, referencing the distributable-reserves test under <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2006/46/section/830">section 830 CA 2006</a>. The preference dividend does not require any dividend declaration on the other classes; the discretionary class-by-class mechanism (covered in our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/fic-articles-of-association-property-control-mechanics">FIC articles of association page</a>) is what enables the targeted declaration.</p>

<p>The tax treatment in the founder's hands: dividend income taxed at the founder's marginal dividend rate. After the £500 dividend allowance (2026/27), the first slice falls in the basic-rate dividend band at 8.75% up to total income of £50,270, the next slice at 33.75% up to £125,140, and the remainder at 39.35%. A founder with no other income drawing the £50,000 preference coupon described above pays approximately £3,260 in dividend tax (using personal allowance and dividend allowance against the lower slices). A founder also drawing state pension and a SIPP partial drawdown can shift parts of the coupon between basic-rate and higher-rate bands depending on the total income level.</p>

<h2>Strand three: preference share redemption and amortisation</h2>

<p>Preference share redemption under <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2006/46/section/684">sections 684 to 689 CA 2006</a> is the mechanism for converting frozen preference capital into income across the decumulation horizon. The articles specify the redemption mechanic (covered in our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/fic-articles-of-association-property-control-mechanics">articles drafting page</a>); a typical property FIC includes a company-side option to redeem from a defined founder age (65 or 70) and a holder-side put option from a slightly later age (75 or 80), with the company-side option used to manage the founder's marginal-rate position across the years.</p>

<p>The tax treatment splits in a way that often produces a better after-tax outcome than dividend extraction at the same gross amount. Under <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2010/4/section/1000">CTA 2010 section 1000</a> and following, the redemption proceeds are decomposed: the amount up to the issue price of the share is treated as a return of share capital (a capital event for the holder, taxed under CGT rules with the £3,000 annual exempt amount for 2026/27 and CGT rates of 18% basic or 24% higher rate on the gain element, which for shares issued at par is nil or low), and any excess above the issue price is treated as a distribution (taxed as a dividend in the founder's hands at dividend rates). For a property FIC where the preference shares were issued at par with no premium, the redemption is largely a capital event with minimal distribution element, making it CGT-preferable to a pure dividend equivalent.</p>

<p>The amortisation profile is the operational design. A founder holding £1 million of preference shares might design a 20-year amortisation: £50,000 redeemed each year from age 70 to age 90. The company's distributable reserves under section 687 fund the redemption; if reserves are insufficient in any year, a fresh share issue under section 688 (typically of new B-class shares to the next generation) can fund the redemption. The amortisation schedule is fixed in the redemption-mechanic article clause but can be varied by board resolution if the company has the flexibility built into the wording.</p>

<h2>The two non-FIC strands: state pension and private pension drawdown</h2>

<p>State pension is taxable income for income tax purposes, with no NI charged (NI applies only to earnings). For 2026/27 the full new state pension is £230.25 per week or approximately £11,973 per year (verify against the current gov.uk figure at write time). For most retired founders, the state pension uses substantially all of the personal allowance (£12,570 for 2026/27), leaving only £597 of allowance available against other income.</p>

<p>Private pension drawdown from a SIPP follows the post-April-2024 framework. Up to the lump sum allowance (£268,275 for most savers under the standard rules), 25% of each crystallisation event can be taken tax-free; the remaining 75% is taxable income at the founder's marginal income tax rate. The annual allowance for further contributions is £60,000 in 2026/27 (subject to taper for adjusted income above £260,000), with carry-forward from the previous three tax years available where the founder still has earned income; for a retired founder with no earnings, no further contribution is generally possible and the existing pot is the focus.</p>

<p>The layering question with the FIC strands: SIPP tax-free cash and DLA repayment are both tax-free, so they compete only for the timing slot in the founder's decumulation calendar. Taking both in the same year produces tax-free cash without using personal allowance or basic-rate band, leaving those bands available for the taxable FIC dividend coupon. SIPP taxable drawdown sits against the personal allowance and basic-rate band; FIC dividend coupon sits against the dividend allowance and the dividend tax bands. The two interact only via the total-income threshold at £50,270 and £125,140 where dividend rates step up.</p>

<h2>The dividend cliffs and how to keep clear of them</h2>

<p>Three thresholds shape the FIC dividend extraction decision in any given year.</p>

<p><strong>The £50,270 basic-to-higher-rate cliff.</strong> Below this total income level the dividend tax is 8.75%; above it, 33.75%. The 25 percentage point jump is the largest cliff in the decumulation calendar. Avoidance mechanics: bring more of the DLA repayment forward in years where total income would otherwise cross £50,270; defer the FIC preference coupon declaration to lower-income years; consider a spouse-class dividend allocation if the spouse has unused basic-rate capacity (subject to the settlements analysis); time SIPP drawdown to fill basic-rate capacity ahead of FIC dividend.</p>

<p><strong>The £100,000 personal allowance taper.</strong> Between £100,000 and £125,140 of adjusted income, the £12,570 personal allowance tapers at £1 of allowance lost for every £2 of income, producing an effective marginal rate around 60% on income in that band. For a property FIC founder, the avoidance mechanic is to keep total income (FIC dividend plus pension plus state pension plus other) either below £100,000 or above £125,140; the band itself is uneconomic to draw into.</p>

<p><strong>The £125,140 higher-to-additional-rate cliff.</strong> Above this total income level the dividend tax is 39.35%; the 5.6 percentage point jump is smaller than the basic-to-higher cliff but still material across a £25,000 or £50,000 incremental draw. For most property FIC founders the cliff is rarely hit because the combination of state pension, modest SIPP drawdown, and FIC preference coupon plus DLA repayment sits well below £125,140. For founders with substantial SIPP balances drawing down aggressively, the cliff can become a planning consideration.</p>

<h2>Sequencing across four founder ages</h2>

<p>A defensible default sequencing pattern, illustrative and to be adjusted to the founder's specific position:</p>

<p><strong>Age 65 to 70 (early decumulation).</strong> Heavy on tax-free DLA repayment (£20,000 to £40,000 per year depending on credit balance size and pacing); modest FIC dividend draw to use personal allowance and basic-rate capacity not absorbed by the DLA (£10,000 to £25,000); SIPP 25% tax-free cash front-loaded if not yet taken. State pension not yet drawn (state pension age is 66 for 2026/27; some founders defer to 67 or 68 for higher weekly rate). Total income target £40,000 to £60,000 mostly tax-free or in basic-rate band.</p>

<p><strong>Age 70 to 75 (mid decumulation).</strong> DLA repayment slowing (balance closer to exhausted); FIC preference coupon becomes the primary income strand (£20,000 to £50,000); state pension layered in (£12,000 at full new state pension); SIPP taxable drawdown if balance available, at basic-rate capacity (typically £10,000 to £20,000 per year). Total income target £50,000 to £80,000, mix of basic-rate income tax (state pension and SIPP) and basic-rate dividend (FIC preference coupon up to £50,270 threshold).</p>

<p><strong>Age 75 to 85 (late decumulation).</strong> DLA exhausted or nearly so; FIC preference coupon continues plus selective preference redemption tranches (£25,000 to £50,000 per year); state pension at full level; SIPP balance preserved or drawn down depending on the IHT-2027 cohort question (covered in our wave 4 pension-decumulation page). Total income target £60,000 to £90,000, with the redemption tranches producing CGT-favoured income against the £3,000 annual exempt amount.</p>

<p><strong>Age 85 onwards.</strong> FIC preference coupon remains the primary strand; preference redemption decisions per the articles' redemption schedule; possible shift of dividend allocation to spouse class for marginal rate planning if the spouse holds a C-class (with the settlements analysis in our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/alphabet-shares-property-spv-dividend-splitting-spouse-children">alphabet shares page</a> still in force). State pension continues. SIPP balance is now an IHT consideration as much as an income one. Total income target tends to drop as care costs may rise on a different track outside the FIC structure.</p>

<aside>
<p>The four-age sequencing here is illustrative and figures will not match any specific founder's position.</p>
<p>A bespoke sequencing plan needs the founder's specific DLA balance, preference value, SIPP balance, and other income sources to produce useful year-by-year figures.</p>
</aside>

<h2>What to do with the underlying rental cashflow that is not extracted</h2>

<p>A property FIC's rental income is taxed at corporation tax rates in the company (19% small profits up to £50,000, 26.5% marginal-relief band, 25% main rate above £250,000 in 2026/27). The post-tax profit either funds dividend extraction (taxed again at dividend rates in the founder's hands) or is retained as distributable reserves available for future declarations. For a retired founder already extracting the planned FIC strands and not needing more cash, retention produces a better after-tax outcome than extraction: the cash accumulates inside the company taxed only at corporation tax, and the retained reserves preserve the optionality to declare higher dividends in later years when needed (medical care, gifting to grandchildren, downsizing assistance).</p>

<p>The retained reserves also fund preference share redemption (under section 687) and any future growth-share issues. For a founder whose decumulation needs are well-met by the existing three FIC strands, leaving the surplus rental profit inside the company is generally the right call. The trade-off: cash inside the FIC is owned by the company, not by the founder personally, and is subject to the company's corporate tax and (in the founder's estate, via the founder's FIC shares) the IHT regime. The IHT framing is on the wave 4 FIC-as-IHT-tool page; the operational point for retirement-income planning is that retention is a valid third extraction policy alongside DLA repayment and dividend declaration. The governance discipline supporting these declarations does not relax with retirement (see our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/fic-property-corporate-governance-board-meetings-resolutions-discipline">FIC governance discipline page</a>); a retired founder is the highest substance-over-form risk profile across the FIC's life, and the contemporaneous-minute discipline remains load-bearing.</p>

<h2>Boundary: this is income now, not next-generation wealth transfer</h2>

<p>The three FIC income strands described on this page produce cash in the founder's personal account during the founder's life. That cash is taxed on the way out (or in the case of DLA repayment, is not taxed at all because it is debt repayment), and once it is in the founder's hands it forms part of the founder's personal estate on death like any other personal asset. Spending the cash on living costs reduces the estate; saving the cash in personal accounts adds to the estate. Neither of those mechanics is FIC-specific; they are the personal-finance consequences of any source of retirement income.</p>

<p>What is FIC-specific is the next-generation wealth-transfer mechanic, which works independently of the founder's income extraction. The growth-share class held by the next generation participates in the underlying property value growth above the hurdle from issue, regardless of whether the founder is extracting income from the preference and A classes. The founder's income extraction strategy does not directly affect the next-generation wealth-transfer position; the two are decoupled by the share-class architecture. The IHT-side framing of the FIC value-freeze mechanic, and the share-gift PET mechanic at the point of gift, are covered separately on the wave 4 FIC-as-IHT-tool page and FIC growth-share gifting page respectively. The boundary between this page and those pages is that this page is about income now; those pages are about value transferred for the next generation.</p>
