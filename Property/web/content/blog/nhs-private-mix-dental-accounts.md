<h2>Why blended practices need blended reporting</h2>
<p>When NHS contract income sits next to private treatment plans, a single “sales” line on a P&amp;L rarely tells you what is driving profit. Associates, hygienists, and labs can behave differently across each stream.</p>

<h2>What good looks like in management accounts</h2>
<ul>
<li>Income split that mirrors how clinicians actually produce revenue.</li>
<li>Lab and materials tracked to chair time where possible.</li>
<li>Clear associate and hygienist costs with UDA/FP17 context where relevant.</li>
<li>Equipment and finance costs separated so tax relief is easy to support.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Linking operational clarity to tax</h2>
<p>Year-end compliance is smoother when the underlying bookkeeping already answers HMRC’s questions: who earned what, what was rebilled, and what was capital rather than revenue spend.</p>

<p>If you are preparing for a sale, refinance, or partnership change, clean segment reporting is not “nice to have” — it is part of due diligence.</p>
