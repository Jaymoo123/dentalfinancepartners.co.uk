<p>From April 2027, UK landlords face a fundamental change to how their rental income is taxed. The government is introducing separate <strong>2027 property income tax rates for landlords</strong> of 22%, 42% and 47%, replacing the current system where property income is taxed at general income tax rates.</p>
<p>This represents one of the most significant changes to UK property taxation in decades. Every individual landlord with rental income will need to understand how these new rates work and what they mean for their tax liability.</p>

<h2>The New 2027 Property Income Tax Rates and Bands</h2>
<p>From 6 April 2027, individual landlords will pay the following rates on their rental income:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>22% basic rate</strong> - on property income up to £50,270 (after personal allowance)</li>
<li><strong>42% higher rate</strong> - on property income from £50,270 to £125,140</li>
<li><strong>47% additional rate</strong> - on property income above £125,140</li>
</ul>
<p>These rates apply specifically to property income and are 2% higher than the general income tax rates that will continue to apply to employment income, pensions, and other non-property income. The bands remain the same as general income tax bands.</p>
<p>This creates a two-tier structure. For example, if you earn £40,000 from employment plus £15,000 from rental income, your employment income is taxed at 20%, but your rental income is taxed at the <strong>22% basic rate property income</strong> tax. The personal allowance (£12,570 in 2026/27) still applies against your total income, with property income typically treated as the top slice.</p>
<p>Compared to the current system, this represents a tax increase at all levels:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Basic rate:</strong> increases from 20% to 22% (10% increase)</li>
<li><strong>Higher rate:</strong> increases from 40% to 42% (5% increase)</li>
<li><strong>Additional rate:</strong> increases from 45% to 47% (4.4% increase)</li>
</ul>

<h2>Which Landlords and Property Types Are Affected?</h2>
<p>The separate property income tax rates apply to:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Individual landlords</strong> - whether you own one property or a large portfolio</li>
<li><strong>Joint owners</strong> - each owner pays the separate rates on their share of rental income</li>
<li><strong>Partners in property partnerships</strong> - individual partners pay the higher rates on their share</li>
<li><strong>Non-resident landlords</strong> - subject to the same rates (though other rules may apply)</li>
</ul>
<p>The <strong>separate property tax bands 2027</strong> apply to most forms of residential rental income:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Standard buy-to-let rentals</strong> - fully subject to the new rates</li>
<li><strong>HMO income</strong> - treated as property income, so higher rates apply</li>
<li><strong>Serviced accommodation</strong> - likely subject to new rates (post-FHL abolition)</li>
<li><strong>Holiday lets</strong> - treated as property income from April 2025 onwards</li>
</ul>
<p>The rates do <strong>not</strong> apply to:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Limited companies</strong> - continue to pay corporation tax at 19%/25%</li>
<li><strong>Commercial property income</strong> - remains subject to general income tax rates</li>
<li><strong>Property trading income</strong> - developers and traders pay general rates</li>
</ul>

<h2>Interaction with Existing Tax Rules</h2>
<p>The higher property income tax rates work alongside existing landlord tax rules, potentially creating additional complications.</p>
<h3>Section 24 Mortgage Interest Restriction</h3>
<p>The <a href="/blog/section-24-and-tax-relief/section-24-tax-relief-complete-guide">Section 24 mortgage interest restriction</a> remains in place, limiting mortgage interest relief to basic rate (20%). However, your actual property income is now taxed at 22% minimum, creating a larger gap between the relief available and tax paid. For higher rate taxpayers, this gap becomes even more significant - you get 20% relief but pay 42% tax on the restricted income.</p>
<h3>Making Tax Digital Compliance</h3>
<p>The new rates coincide with <a href="/blog/making-tax-digital-mtd/making-tax-digital-landlords-april-2026-deadline">Making Tax Digital requirements</a> that begin in April 2026. Landlords will need to track their property income separately for the higher tax rates while maintaining digital records for HMRC compliance.</p>

<h2>Examples of the Tax Impact</h2>
<p>Here are practical examples showing the tax impact of the new rates.</p>
<h3>Basic Rate Landlord</h3>
<p>Sarah owns 2 buy-to-let properties generating £25,000 annual profit. She has no other income.</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>2026/27:</strong> Tax = (£25,000 - £12,570) × 20% = £2,486</li>
<li><strong>2027/28:</strong> Tax = (£25,000 - £12,570) × 22% = £2,734</li>
<li><strong>Extra cost:</strong> £248 per year</li>
</ul>
<h3>Higher Rate Landlord</h3>
<p>David earns £45,000 from employment plus £30,000 rental profit from his portfolio.</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Employment income:</strong> continues to be taxed at 20%/40%</li>
<li><strong>Rental income:</strong> taxed at 22%/42% from 2027</li>
<li><strong>Extra cost:</strong> approximately £600 per year on the rental income portion</li>
</ul>

<h2>Considering Incorporation</h2>
<p>The introduction of higher property income tax rates makes <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/buy-to-let-limited-company-complete-guide-uk">incorporation into a limited company</a> more attractive for many landlords. Companies continue to pay corporation tax at 19% (small profits rate) or 25% (main rate), both significantly lower than the new property income tax rates.</p>
<p>However, incorporation involves several considerations:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Additional administrative burden</strong> - company accounts, corporation tax returns</li>
<li><strong>Potential SDLT costs</strong> - transferring properties may trigger stamp duty</li>
<li><strong>Different finance arrangements</strong> - commercial mortgages typically have higher rates</li>
<li><strong>Extraction tax</strong> - getting money out of the company creates additional tax charges</li>
</ul>
<p>For portfolio landlords or those in higher tax brackets, the savings may justify these complications. The decision requires careful analysis of your specific circumstances.</p>

<h2>Planning and Strategy for the Changes</h2>
<p>With the changes taking effect from April 2027, landlords have time to plan. Consider both short-term and long-term strategies.</p>
<h3>Short-term Planning (2025-2027)</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Accelerate income</strong> - consider bringing forward any discretionary rental income</li>
<li><strong>Defer expenses</strong> - where possible, time large deductible expenses for after April 2027</li>
<li><strong>Review financing</strong> - consider whether refinancing makes sense before the higher rates apply</li>
</ul>
<h3>Long-term Structural Changes</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Incorporation analysis</strong> - model whether company ownership becomes worthwhile</li>
<li><strong>Portfolio review</strong> - consider whether to reduce property holdings</li>
<li><strong>Investment strategy</strong> - evaluate whether commercial property offers better tax efficiency</li>
</ul>

<h2>Record-Keeping and Professional Advice</h2>
<p>The separate property income tax rates don't change your <a href="/blog/section-24-and-tax-relief/landlord-tax-deductions-uk-2026-complete-list">allowable deductions</a>, but they do make accurate record-keeping more important. With higher tax rates applying to property income, ensuring you claim all available expenses becomes more valuable. You'll also need to clearly separate property income from other income sources on your tax return, as they're now subject to different rates.</p>
<p>Given the complexity of these changes and their interaction with existing rules like Section 24, most landlords benefit from professional guidance. A <a href="/blog/property-accountant-services/what-does-a-property-accountant-do">specialist property accountant</a> can help you:</p>
<ul>
<li>Calculate your expected tax liability under the new rates</li>
<li>Model the benefits of incorporation for your specific situation</li>
<li>Develop tax planning strategies ahead of April 2027</li>
<li>Ensure compliance with both the new rates and Making Tax Digital requirements</li>
</ul>

<h2>Key Takeaways for Landlords</h2>
<p>The introduction of separate <strong>2027 property income tax rates for landlords</strong> represents a significant tax increase for all individual property investors. The 22%, 42% and 47% rates apply from April 2027, creating a permanent additional cost for rental income.</p>
<p>While you can't avoid these rates as an individual landlord, understanding them early gives you time to plan. Whether through incorporation, portfolio restructuring, or simply budgeting for higher tax bills, preparation is key to managing this change effectively. The new rates make professional advice more valuable than ever, as the tax landscape for property investors becomes increasingly complex.</p>
