  - question: "What is the difference between an HHSRS Category 1 hazard and a Decent Homes failure?"
    answer: "HHSRS is the existing risk-based assessment under Part 1 of the Housing Act 2004. An assessor scores 29 categories of potential hazard (damp and mould growth, excess cold, falls on stairs, electrical hazards, fire, food safety, and so on) and a hazard scoring above a defined threshold is a Category 1 hazard. A Category 1 hazard triggers a mandatory duty on the local housing authority to take enforcement action under section 5 of the Housing Act 2004. The Decent Homes Standard is broader: a property fails the standard if it has any Category 1 hazard (the statutory minimum limb), or if it is not in a reasonable state of repair, or if it does not have reasonably modern facilities and services, or if it does not provide a reasonable degree of thermal comfort. A property can pass HHSRS (no Category 1 hazards) and still fail Decent Homes (for example, kitchen units over 30 years old, single-glazed windows in poor condition, no central heating). When the full PRS Decent Homes regulations land, that wider four-limb test will be the operative floor for the private sector."
  - question: "Can the council force me to do work right now under the partial commencement of section 100?"
    answer: "Not under the Decent Homes Standard itself, because the substantive standard is not yet in force. The council can absolutely force you to do work under the existing HHSRS regime that continues alongside. After an HHSRS inspection finding a Category 1 hazard, the council must take one of: an Improvement Notice under section 11 or 12 of the Housing Act 2004 (compelling specified works within a defined period), a Prohibition Order under section 20 or 21 (restricting or prohibiting use of all or part of the premises), an Emergency Remedial Action under section 40 (council does the work and recovers the cost), an Emergency Prohibition Order under section 43, a Demolition Order, or designation of a Clearance Area. For Category 2 hazards the council has a power (not a duty) to take similar action plus the Hazard Awareness Notice under section 28 or 29. The council can already issue a Civil Penalty up to £30,000 under section 249A of the Housing Act 2004 as an alternative to prosecution for breach of an Improvement Notice or licensing offence."
  - question: "What is a Type 1 versus Type 2 requirement under the amended Housing Act 2004?"
    answer: "The split is built into paragraphs 4 and 7 of Schedule 4 to the RRA 2025. The Secretary of State's Decent Homes regulations will divide the standard into two categories. Type 1 requirements are the serious breaches: the local housing authority has a mandatory duty to take enforcement action under the amended section 5 of the Housing Act 2004, parallel to the existing Category 1 hazard duty. Type 2 requirements are the lesser breaches: the council has a discretionary power to act under the amended section 7, parallel to the existing Category 2 hazard power. The structural parallel to HHSRS is deliberate: it means a single inspection can deal with both the hazard assessment and the Decent Homes assessment, and a single enforcement notice can address both types of failure. The actual contents of the Type 1 and Type 2 lists are not yet published; sessions monitoring this should watch for the substantive Decent Homes Regulations 2026 (or 2027) when the Department for Levelling Up, Housing and Communities (now MHCLG in successor form) consults."
  - question: "Will my EPC band determine whether I pass the thermal comfort limb?"
    answer: "EPC will inform the assessment, but it is not the only input and it is not (on current government policy) the sole pass-fail test. The thermal comfort limb in the 2006 social-housing version of the standard is met if the property has effective insulation and an efficient heating system. In practice, an EPC band C or above is a strong indicator of compliance; an EPC band E or below would fail without compensating evidence. The Government's long-trailed PRS minimum EPC C target for new tenancies (originally floated for 2025, repeatedly delayed) sits alongside but separately from the Decent Homes thermal comfort limb. A landlord preparing a property today for the eventual full standard should treat EPC C as a planning floor for thermal comfort rather than a guaranteed pass. The detailed PRS Decent Homes Regulations, once made, will set out the technical test."
  - question: "How modern do my kitchen and bathroom have to be to pass the reasonably modern facilities limb?"
    answer: "The 2006 standard uses 'reasonably modern' rather than 'new', and the pass test in the social-housing version looks at age and condition jointly. As a working rule based on the existing guidance, a kitchen over 30 years old in poor cosmetic and functional condition fails; a kitchen over 20 years old in poor functional condition fails; a kitchen of any age that lacks adequate space for preparation and cooking fails. Bathrooms over 30 years old in poor condition fail. The detailed PRS test will be set by the substantive Decent Homes Regulations and may differ in calibration; landlords should not assume the social-housing thresholds translate one-for-one. The practical reading is that kitchens and bathrooms that have not been refurbished since the early 2000s should be on the planning list for the next void-period refit, and the spend should be budgeted as a mix of capital improvement (new units, new tiling, new sanitaryware) and revenue maintenance (replacement of like-for-like worn components)."
  - question: "What is the maximum financial penalty under the new section 6A of the Housing Act 2004?"
    answer: "Up to £7,000 per breach. Paragraph 6 of Schedule 4 to the RRA 2025 inserts a new section 6A into the Housing Act 2004 establishing a financial penalty regime specific to breaches of a Type 1 requirement on qualifying residential premises (and parallel breaches of the Category 1 hazard enforcement framework on those premises). The detailed procedural rules are set out in a new Schedule A1 to the 2004 Act inserted by paragraph 35. The new section 6A penalty operates alongside, not instead of, the existing section 249A penalty (up to £30,000), the new RRA 2025 section 15 / Schedule 5 civil penalty regime for housing offences (up to £40,000), and the Rent Repayment Order route.     answer: "Once the substantive standard is in force, yes, on terms specified in the implementing regulations. The Rent Repayment Order route under sections 40 to 52 of the Housing and Planning Act 2016 was extended by the RRA 2025 to cover the new RRA offences (housing offences from 1 May 2026 under section 15 of the RRA 2025) and is anticipated to extend to Type 1 Decent Homes breaches once those regulations land. The order requires the landlord to repay up to 12 months of rent to the tenant (or to the local authority where rent was paid by housing benefit or Universal Credit), and the application is made to the First-Tier Tribunal Property Chamber within 12 months of the offence date. Sessions advising on RRO exposure should treat the Decent Homes route as a live but transitional risk: not yet operative for routine condition failures, but operative the day the substantive standard regulations commence."
  - question: "Are improvement-notice works tax deductible against my rental income?"
    answer: "Yes, where the works are revenue in character (repair and maintenance restoring the property to its previous condition). No, where the works are capital in character (improvement of the property beyond its previous condition, addition of a new component, replacement of an entire asset on a like-for-better basis). The statutory test is the capital-versus-revenue boundary in ITTOIA 2005 s.272 (which applies trading-profit principles to property businesses) and the case law on like-for-like replacement. Practically: replacement of a broken boiler with a modern equivalent is revenue (asset is the central heating system, you have repaired it). Installation of central heating in a property that previously had only electric storage heaters is capital (you have improved the property). Replacement of a single-glazed window with a like-for-like single-glazed window is revenue; replacement with double glazing is treated as revenue by HMRC concession under PIM2030 because double glazing is the modern equivalent. Replacement of a worn kitchen unit with a similar standard unit is revenue; full kitchen refit with upgraded specification is capital (or, more accurately, an apportionment between the two). Where a single Improvement Notice mandates a mix of revenue and capital works, apportion the cost and allocate accordingly; keep the works schedule and the invoices for the audit trail."
  - question: "Is the £7,000 financial penalty under section 6A tax deductible?"
    answer: "No. HMRC's long-standing position, expressed in BIM38500 and applied across the property and trading-business manuals, is that financial penalties for breach of regulatory obligations are not laid out wholly and exclusively for trade or business purposes within ITTOIA 2005 s.34 or s.272. The non-deductibility of the penalty itself does not stop the deduction of underlying remediation works that are revenue in character, and does not stop the deduction of professional fees (surveyor reports, legal advice on representations, ICAEW-qualified tax review of cap-rev apportionment) where those fees are themselves revenue. The same treatment applies to the section 249A penalty, the new RRA 2025 section 15 penalty, and the older Housing Act 2004 enforcement penalties. The contrast with HMO licensing fees (which are fully deductible under PIM2080 because they are statutory operating costs of the business, not punitive) is worth keeping in mind."
  - question: "Does the standard apply if I have a fixed-term lease of 7 years or more on the property?"
    answer: "No. The new section 2B of the Housing Act 2004 (inserted by section 100(1) and (5) of the RRA 2025, in force from 27 December 2025) defines 'qualifying residential premises' by reference to dwellings and Houses in Multiple Occupation let under relevant tenancies. The relevant-tenancy definition tracks the underlying assured tenancy regime under the Housing Act 1988 as amended by the RRA 2025. Pre-existing fixed-term leases of 7 years or more sit outside the assured tenancy regime (Schedule 1 paragraph 3 of the Housing Act 1988), as do most company-let and genuine business tenancies, ground rents on long leases, and a small number of other categories. Properties outside the qualifying-residential-premises perimeter are outside the Decent Homes Standard. They are not outside the underlying HHSRS framework, which continues to apply to any dwelling regardless of the tenancy form."
  - question: "What happens if I refuse a council inspection of my property?"
    answer: "Refusing an inspection is itself a route to enforcement. Under section 239 of the Housing Act 2004, an officer of the local housing authority can enter premises after giving at least 24 hours notice for the purpose of HHSRS inspection or related enforcement decision. Obstruction of an officer is a summary offence under section 240 (Level 4 fine on the standard scale). In practice, councils will typically pursue a warrant under section 240 to compel entry; the cost of the warrant application is often passed on to the landlord in the eventual enforcement notice. The Stage 2 procedural amendments in paragraphs 35 to 37 of Schedule 4 to the RRA 2025 extend the service-of-notice framework to cover the new Decent Homes enforcement notices, requiring service on both the landlord and any superior landlord where the property is sub-let. Refusing inspection is not a defence; co-operating with a polite scope of work agreement is the more durable approach."
  - question: "How does the standard interact with my Selective Licence or HMO Mandatory Licence?"
    answer: "The licensing regime and the Decent Homes Standard are separate but interlocking. A Mandatory HMO Licence under Part 2 of the Housing Act 2004 imposes specific condition standards (room sizes, fire safety, amenity ratios) that often go beyond the general Decent Homes test; satisfying the licence will generally also satisfy the Decent Homes minimum, but not always. A Selective Licence under Part 3 in the council's designated area may impose property-condition conditions that overlap with Decent Homes; the licence conditions are enforceable in addition to the Decent Homes regime. Failure to obtain a required licence is itself a Rent Repayment Order trigger under the existing 2016 Act schedule. Where you hold both an HMO licence and a property in scope of Decent Homes, the single property is subject to both regimes simultaneously; council inspectors will normally address both in a single visit. <p>Most landlords reading about the Renters' Rights Act 2025 spend their energy on the headline tenancy reforms (Section 21 going, periodic tenancies as default, the new Section 13 rent-rise procedure). The property-condition arm of the Act, Part 3 sections 100 and 101 extending the Decent Homes Standard to the private rented sector, gets less coverage and is moving more slowly. As of 22 May 2026 the framework provisions are live (in force from 27 December 2025 under <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2025/1354/contents/made" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">SI 2025/1354</a>); the substantive standard (the regulations the Secretary of State must make defining the Type 1 and Type 2 requirements) is not yet in force. This page maps what is operative today, what is scheduled, what the four limbs of the standard will require when the further commencement order lands, and how the remediation spend hits the rental P&amp;L.</p>

<h2>The Statutory Framework: What is in Force on 22 May 2026</h2>

<p>Part 3 of the Renters' Rights Act 2025 amends the Housing Act 2004 to introduce a Decent Homes Standard for qualifying residential premises in England. The amendments are structured as a partial overlay onto the existing HHSRS enforcement framework, not a replacement of it, which means the property-condition floor for private landlords has two operative regimes after commencement: HHSRS (in force since 2006 and continuing) plus Decent Homes (framework live now, substantive standard pending).</p>

<p><a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2025/1354/contents/made" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">SI 2025/1354 (the Commencement No. 1 Regulations 2025)</a> brought the following into force on 27 December 2025:</p>

<ul>
<li>Sections 63 (residential landlord and residential tenancy definitions), 99 (marketing definition), and 100(1), (5) and (6) of the RRA 2025, sufficient to insert the new section 2B definition of qualifying residential premises into the Housing Act 2004.</li>
<li>Paragraphs 1 and 9(1) of Schedule 4 to the RRA 2025 (linked-purpose preliminary amendments).</li>
<li>Paragraph 9(3) of Schedule 4, inserting subsection (1A) into Housing Act 2004 section 9 (excluding subsection (1A)(a)).</li>
</ul>

<p>What is not yet in force is the substantive Decent Homes Standard itself. The regulation-making power under the new sections empowers the Secretary of State to make regulations defining the Type 1 requirements (mandatory enforcement, parallel to Category 1 hazards) and Type 2 requirements (discretionary enforcement, parallel to Category 2 hazards). Those regulations are not yet made; government policy statements anticipate commencement before April 2027, but no statutory instrument appointing the day has been made as of 22 May 2026. Until those regulations are in force, the operative property-condition regime for the PRS continues to be HHSRS under Part 1 of the Housing Act 2004 (in force since 2006), supplemented by the financial-penalty extension in the new section 6A and the procedural amendments in paragraphs 35 to 37 of Schedule 4 to the RRA 2025.</p>

<h2>The Four Limbs of the Decent Homes Standard</h2>

<p>The standard, in its 2006 social-housing version that the PRS extension will broadly track, requires a property to pass four tests. The detailed pass-fail criteria for the PRS test will be set by the substantive regulations when made; the four-limb structure itself is durable and is the planning framework landlords should adopt now.</p>

<thead>
<tr><th>Limb</th><th>Test (working description)</th><th>Underlying authority</th></tr>
</thead>

<h3>Limb 1 in detail: HHSRS Category 1 hazard freedom</h3>

<p>The HHSRS regulations list 29 categories of potential hazard. The most commonly cited in PRS enforcement decisions are damp and mould growth, excess cold, falls on stairs and between levels, fire safety, electrical hazards, food safety, personal hygiene, and entry by intruders. The assessor scores each potential hazard using the operating guidance methodology; a score above the Category 1 threshold triggers a mandatory enforcement duty under section 5 of the Housing Act 2004. A property with even one Category 1 hazard fails the statutory minimum limb of Decent Homes by definition.</p>

<p>The practical compliance moves for limb 1 are well-trodden: address visible damp and mould (root-cause investigation, not just paint over), install or maintain working smoke and carbon monoxide alarms (mandatory since the Smoke and Carbon Monoxide Alarm (Amendment) Regulations 2022 in any case), keep gas appliances in date (annual Gas Safety Record under the Gas Safety (Installation and Use) Regulations 1998), keep electrical installations in date (Electrical Installation Condition Report every 5 years under the Electrical Safety Standards in the Private Rented Sector (England) Regulations 2020), and address fall hazards on stairs (handrails, guarding, lighting). These are not new obligations; they are the existing HHSRS floor that limb 1 of Decent Homes will codify into a single pass-fail test.</p>

<h3>Limb 2 in detail: reasonable state of repair</h3>

<p>The 2006 social-housing version of the standard fails a property on repair grounds if (a) one or more key building components (external walls, structure, roof, chimneys, windows and external doors, kitchen, bathroom, electrical system, heating system) are old and in poor condition, or (b) two or more other building components are old and in poor condition. 'Old' is defined by component-specific age thresholds; 'poor condition' is defined by visible defects requiring replacement or substantial repair.</p>

<p>The PRS test will be calibrated by the substantive regulations and may differ in detail; the operative planning move for landlords today is to maintain a component-by-component condition log for each property and budget the replacement cycle accordingly. A roof at year 45 of a 50-year design life is not yet in poor condition; a roof at year 55 with visible slipped or missing tiles and inadequate flashings is. The audit trail (date of last inspection, photographs, contractor reports) is what will support a 'reasonable state of repair' defence at a future inspection.</p>

<h3>Limb 3 in detail: reasonably modern facilities and services</h3>

<p>The 2006 standard fails a property on modern facilities grounds if it lacks three or more of: a kitchen of an adequate layout and size, a kitchen under 20 years old, an appropriately located bathroom and WC, a bathroom under 30 years old, adequate noise insulation, and adequate size and layout of common parts (for HMOs and flatted blocks). 'Adequate layout and size' for a kitchen and bathroom is defined by reference to working space, storage, and the relationship between sanitary fittings.</p>

<p>The compliance move for landlords with older properties is the void-period refit. Kitchens and bathrooms that have not been refurbished since the early 2000s should be on the planning list for the next vacancy; the spend can be staged to align with rent-period planning and capital-allowances strategy. The capital-versus-revenue split (covered below) matters here: a like-for-like replacement of worn components is revenue and deductible against this year's rental income; a full refit with upgraded specification is capital and adds to the base cost for CGT on eventual sale.</p>

<h3>Limb 4 in detail: reasonable degree of thermal comfort</h3>

<p>The 2006 standard tests effective insulation and an efficient heating system. An EPC band C or above is a strong indicator of compliance; an EPC band E or below would fail without compensating evidence. The Government's separate PRS minimum EPC C policy (originally floated for 2025, repeatedly delayed) sits alongside but separately from the Decent Homes thermal comfort limb; landlords should treat EPC C as a planning floor for thermal comfort, not a guaranteed pass on the eventual Decent Homes test.</p>

<p>Practical compliance moves for older properties: loft insulation (cheap, high-impact, revenue-deductible), cavity wall insulation where the wall construction allows, hot water cylinder jacket, condensing boiler upgrade (capital, adds to base cost, but reduces running costs and EPC rating), draught-proofing on doors and windows. For pre-1919 solid-wall properties, internal or external solid-wall insulation is a substantial capital project that may or may not be commercially worthwhile against the rent profile; the planning question is whether to retrofit before the substantive regulations bite or to dispose of the property.</p>

<h2>The Compliance Checklist (Working Version Pending the Substantive Regulations)</h2>

<p>The checklist below bridges today's HHSRS regime to the anticipated four-limb Decent Homes test. Items in the first half are already mandatory under existing legislation; items in the second half are anticipated based on the 2006 social-housing version and should be planned for but not yet treated as legally binding.</p>

<h3>Already mandatory under existing law</h3>

<ol>
<li>Annual Gas Safety Record by a Gas Safe registered engineer (Gas Safety (Installation and Use) Regulations 1998).</li>
<li>5-yearly Electrical Installation Condition Report by a competent electrician (ESS PRS Regulations 2020).</li>
<li>Valid Energy Performance Certificate, currently EPC band E or above for new tenancies and renewals (MEES Regulations 2015).</li>
<li>Smoke alarm on every floor and carbon monoxide alarm in every room with a fixed combustion appliance (SCMA Regulations 2022).</li>
<li>Free of HHSRS Category 1 hazards (Housing Act 2004 Part 1).</li>
<li>Deposit protected in an authorised scheme within 30 days of receipt (Housing Act 2004 Part 6).</li>
<li>Right to Rent checks completed on all adult occupants (Immigration Act 2014).</li>
<li>HMO licence in force where the property is a Mandatory HMO (5+ occupants forming 2+ households) or falls within an Additional or Selective Licensing scheme.</li>
<li>Section 21 not served on or after 1 May 2026 (abolished by RRA 2025); possession via reformed Section 8 only.</li>
<li>From 27 December 2025: property registered as 'qualifying residential premises' under the new Housing Act 2004 section 2B framework for forthcoming Decent Homes enforcement purposes.</li>
</ol>

<h3>Anticipated under the forthcoming Decent Homes Regulations</h3>

<ol start="11">
<li>Kitchen under 20 years old in good condition, or older with documented good condition and adequate layout.</li>
<li>Bathroom under 30 years old in good condition, or older with documented good condition.</li>
<li>Effective central heating system; an EPC band C as planning floor, with full thermal-comfort assessment by the Decent Homes inspection methodology.</li>
<li>Loft insulation to current Building Regulations minimum.</li>
<li>Cavity wall insulation where the construction allows.</li>
<li>Adequate noise insulation between adjoining properties (relevant for terraced and semi-detached).</li>
<li>No old and in poor condition status on any key building component (roof, walls, windows, external doors, heating system, kitchen, bathroom, electrical system).</li>
<li>For HMOs and flatted blocks, adequate size and condition of common parts.</li>
<li>Component-by-component condition log with date-stamped photographs and contractor reports for the audit trail.</li>
<li>Standing arrangement with a competent property surveyor for periodic Decent Homes pre-inspection (recommended every 5 years, more frequently for older stock).</li>
</ol>

<h2>What Happens When the Council Inspects: Enforcement Powers Today</h2>

<p>The local housing authority's enforcement options after a property condition inspection are set out in Part 1 of the Housing Act 2004. The 2004 Act options operate today and will continue to operate after the substantive Decent Homes regulations commence; the Decent Homes regulations add the new financial-penalty layer under section 6A and the new mandatory-versus-discretionary split under amended sections 5 and 7. The table below sets out the current toolkit.</p>

<p>The procedural safeguards for each enforcement action follow the same shape as the civil-penalty regime covered in our <a href="/blog/landlord-tax-essentials/rra-2025-landlord-enforcement-civil-penalties-banning-orders-defence">civil penalty defence guide</a>: notice of intent, 28-day representations window, final notice, 28-day appeal to the First-Tier Tribunal Property Chamber. Defence on procedural grounds (defective notice, inadequate reasons, breach of the authority's published policy) succeeds more reliably than substantive defence on the facts, which is why the representations stage is the moment to put the contemporaneous evidence on the file.</p>

<h2>The Rent Repayment Order Risk (Live from 1 May 2026 in Anticipated Scope)</h2>

<p>Rent Repayment Orders under sections 40 to 52 of the Housing and Planning Act 2016 require the landlord to repay up to 12 months of rent to the tenant (or to the local authority where rent was paid by housing benefit or Universal Credit), on application to the First-Tier Tribunal Property Chamber within 12 months of the offence. The RRO route was extended by the RRA 2025 to cover new housing offences from 1 May 2026, and is anticipated to extend to Type 1 Decent Homes breaches once the substantive standard regulations commence.</p>

<p>The pre-1-May-2026 government guidance at <a href="https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/rent-repayment-orders-under-the-housing-and-planning-act-2016" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">gov.uk Rent Repayment Orders</a> explicitly confines itself to offences committed before that date; the post-1-May regime is being administered by reference to the RRA 2025 statutory framework. The tax treatment of an RRO repayment is the reversal of the original rental receipt rather than a fresh expense: the landlord adjusts the original receipt in the year the order is made, which produces a net deductible position only where the repayment exceeds the rent received in the original year. An RRO can run in parallel with a Civil Penalty on the same facts; one does not extinguish the other.</p>

<h2>Tax Treatment of Decent Homes Compliance Spend</h2>

<p>Compliance spend on Decent Homes works splits between revenue and capital on the standard ITTOIA 2005 s.272 boundary (the trading-profit principles imported into the property business computation). The split matters because revenue spend reduces this year's rental tax bill at the landlord's marginal rate, while capital spend adds to the property's base cost for CGT and reduces the gain on eventual sale. For a higher-rate landlord disposing of the property in 10 years, the revenue route is worth 40 pence in the pound today versus a CGT saving of 24 pence in the pound on disposal; the cash-flow effect favours revenue treatment where the cap-rev boundary genuinely allows it.</p>

<p>HMRC's <a href="https://www.gov.uk/hmrc-internal-manuals/property-income-manual" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Property Income Manual</a> at PIM2030 sets out the cap-rev boundary for property businesses, with the like-for-like replacement test at the centre. The PIM concession on double glazing (replacement of single-glazed windows with double-glazed equivalent is treated as revenue because double glazing is the modern equivalent) is a useful benchmark: where the works restore the property to its previous functional state using current-standard equivalents of the original components, treat as revenue. Where the works add a new component or upgrade the property's specification, treat as capital. Where a single Improvement Notice mandates a mix, apportion and document.</p>

<p>The financial penalties themselves (the s.6A penalty, the s.249A penalty, the new RRA 2025 s.15 penalty) are not deductible against rental income: HMRC's BIM38500 position is that regulatory fines are not laid out wholly and exclusively for trade or business purposes. The underlying remediation works remain deductible to the extent they are revenue in character. Professional fees (surveyor reports, legal advice on representations, accountancy fees on the cap-rev apportionment, ICAEW-qualified tax review) are deductible where they are revenue, on the same basis covered in our <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/hmo-licensing-fees-tax-deductible-uk-landlords">HMO licensing fees deductibility guide</a>. For pre-letting Decent Homes works on a newly-acquired property, the pre-trading expense rules in ITTOIA 2005 s.57 apply: revenue costs incurred up to 7 years before commencement of letting are deductible in the first year of letting, on the basis explored in our <a href="/blog/landlord-tax-essentials/pre-letting-expenses-landlord-claim-before-first-tenant">pre-letting expenses guide</a>.</p>

<h2>Worked Example: 1920s Three-Bedroom Terrace, Mid-Tier Northern City</h2>

<p>An anonymised illustration based on a typical mid-portfolio fact pattern. A landlord owns a 1920s three-bedroom mid-terrace with the following current state: EPC band D, single-glazed sash windows (original, peeling paint), kitchen refurbished in 2003 with serviceable units and a 10-year-old combi boiler, bathroom refurbished in 2008, loft insulation 100mm (below current Building Regulations minimum of 270mm), no cavity wall insulation (solid brick construction).</p>

<p>HHSRS assessment today: no Category 1 hazards (no damp, no mould, no fall hazards, gas and electrical certificates current). Passes limb 1 of Decent Homes.</p>

<p>Anticipated Decent Homes assessment under the forthcoming regulations:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Limb 1 (HHSRS):</strong> pass.</li>
<li><strong>Limb 2 (repair):</strong> windows at year 100+ in 'old and poor condition'; one key component fails. Boiler at year 10 still within design life. Other components in reasonable condition. Marginal pass; depends on how the regulations score the windows.</li>
<li><strong>Limb 3 (modern facilities):</strong> kitchen at year 23 (over the 20-year threshold), bathroom at year 18 (under the 30-year threshold). Marginal; kitchen condition will matter. Likely conditional pass if kitchen is well-maintained, likely conditional fail if visibly worn.</li>
<li><strong>Limb 4 (thermal comfort):</strong> EPC D, no cavity wall insulation, sub-standard loft insulation. Likely fail until insulation upgrade.</li>
</ul>

<p>Planned works for void-period refit:</p>

<ul>
<li>Replace single-glazed sash windows with double-glazed sash replicas (in a conservation area, may require like-for-style replacement). Spend £18,000. Tax treatment: per PIM2030 double glazing concession, revenue. Full deduction against this year's rental income.</li>
<li>Top up loft insulation from 100mm to 270mm. Spend £600. Revenue. Full deduction.</li>
<li>Internal solid-wall insulation to two key external walls (lounge and main bedroom). Spend £5,500. Capital (new component, not replacement). Adds to base cost for CGT.</li>
<li>Replace kitchen units with similar mid-range specification (units, worktop, sink, tap, no layout change). Spend £8,000. Revenue (like-for-like replacement). Full deduction.</li>
<li>Boiler service, no replacement. Spend £120. Revenue. Full deduction.</li>
</ul>

<p>Total spend £32,220. Revenue deductible this year £26,720. Capital added to base cost £5,500. At higher-rate income tax (40%), the revenue deduction is worth £10,688 in immediate tax relief. The capital element will save 24% (residential CGT rate from April 2024) on the eventual gain, worth £1,320 on the disposal. Total tax relief lifecycle £12,008 against a £32,220 spend, with the property now likely to pass all four limbs of the anticipated Decent Homes test and EPC band C.</p>

<h2>Timeline: What to Do Now, What to Do When the Substantive Regulations Land</h2>

<p>Concrete planning moves for the 22 May 2026 to 31 March 2027 window (the most likely period before the substantive Decent Homes regulations commence):</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Now to 30 September 2026:</strong> commission a portfolio-level condition audit. Identify which properties already pass the anticipated four-limb test and which will need staged investment. Build a contractor pipeline; competent surveyors and refurbishment contractors will become scarce as the commencement date approaches.</li>
<li><strong>1 October 2026 to 31 March 2027:</strong> execute the first wave of refits, prioritising properties with the largest gap to the four-limb pass and properties scheduled for void periods. Apportion cap-rev properly for each project; keep the works schedule, photographs, and contractor invoices for the audit trail.</li>
<li><strong>On commencement of the substantive Decent Homes Regulations (anticipated, not before April 2027):</strong> re-verify the operative pass-fail criteria against the planning assumptions above; the regulations may differ from the 2006 social-housing version in calibration. Update the compliance checklist accordingly.</li>
<li><strong>On commencement of the PRS Database (anticipated before April 2027):</strong> register every property in the portfolio. Compliance status (gas safety, EICR, EPC, Decent Homes status once substantive standard live) will be visible to local authorities at portfolio level; non-registration carries a possession bar under section 90 of the RRA 2025.</li>
</ul>

<p>The structural point: the property-condition obligations on PRS landlords are increasing in calibration and visibility through a phased commencement cycle, and the financial-penalty regime is moving from a £30,000 ceiling under the 2016 Act framework to a £40,000 ceiling under the RRA 2025 framework, plus the £7,000 section 6A penalty layered on top for Type 1 requirement breaches. The compliance cost is real but the planning window is open; the landlords who use this transitional period to stage their investment will spend less and face less enforcement risk than the landlords who wait for the substantive regulations to bite.</p>
