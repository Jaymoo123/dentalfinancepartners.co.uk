<p>When you own two residential properties in the UK, HMRC allows you to elect which one should be treated as your main residence for <strong>CGT main residence election two properties</strong> purposes. This election determines which property can benefit from Principal Private Residence (PPR) relief, potentially saving thousands in capital gains tax when you sell.</p>
<p>The ability to <strong>nominate main residence CGT</strong> becomes particularly important for landlords who live in one property while renting out another, or those who split their time between multiple homes. Without making this election, HMRC will determine your main residence based on facts and circumstances, which may not align with your tax planning objectives.</p>

<h2>What Is a CGT Main Residence Election?</h2>
<p>A CGT main residence election is a formal notification to HMRC specifying which of your residential properties should be treated as your main residence for capital gains tax purposes. This election affects your eligibility for <a href="/blog/capital-gains-tax/principal-private-residence-relief-landlords">Principal Private Residence relief</a>, which can exempt the gain on your main home from CGT entirely.</p>

<h2>Eligibility, Timing and How to Make the Election</h2>
<p>You can make a <strong>two homes CGT election</strong> if you:</p>
<ul>
<li>Own two or more residential properties</li>
<li>Have a residence in each property (you must actually live in both at some point)</li>
<li>Use both properties as homes, not purely as investments</li>
<li>Are a UK resident for tax purposes</li>
</ul>
<p>This means pure buy-to-let properties that you've never lived in cannot be elected as your main residence, even if you own them alongside your actual home.</p>
<p>The timing of your main residence election is crucial. You have <strong>two years from the date you first have two residences</strong> to make your initial election. This deadline is strict, and HMRC rarely accepts late elections. For example, if you buy a second home on 15 March 2025, you have until 15 March 2027 to make your election.</p>
<p>To <strong>nominate main residence CGT</strong>, you need to notify HMRC in writing. There's no official form, but your notification must include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Your name and National Insurance number</li>
<li>Addresses of both properties</li>
<li>Clear statement of which property you're electing as your main residence</li>
<li>Date from which the election should take effect</li>
<li>Your signature and date</li>
</ul>
<p>Send the election to HMRC's Self Assessment helpline or include it with your Self Assessment return. Keep a copy for your records. A simple election letter might read: <em>"I wish to elect that my property at [full address] should be treated as my main residence for capital gains tax purposes under Section 222(5) TCGA 1992, with effect from [date]. My other residence is at [full address]."</em></p>

<h2>Changing Your Election</h2>
<p>You can change your election at any time by notifying HMRC, but the change only takes effect from the date of the new election. You cannot backdate elections to gain a retrospective tax advantage.</p>
<p>Common scenarios for changing elections include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Moving your primary residence between properties</li>
<li>Converting your main home to a rental property</li>
<li>Preparing to sell one of the properties</li>
<li>Changes in property values affecting the potential CGT liability</li>
</ul>

<h2>Tax Implications and Strategic Planning</h2>
<p>Your election determines which property can benefit from PPR relief when sold. The elected main residence is potentially exempt from CGT, while the non-elected property will be subject to capital gains tax at 18% (basic rate taxpayers) or 24% (higher rate taxpayers) on any gain.</p>
<p>Consider Sarah, who owns two properties:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Property A:</strong> Purchased for £200,000, now worth £400,000 (potential gain: £200,000)</li>
<li><strong>Property B:</strong> Purchased for £300,000, now worth £450,000 (potential gain: £150,000)</li>
</ul>
<p>If Sarah elects Property A as her main residence and later sells it, she could save £48,000 in CGT (£200,000 × 24%) compared to electing Property B, which would only save £36,000 (£150,000 × 24%).</p>

<h2>Partial Relief, Letting Relief and Deemed Occupation</h2>
<p>Even if you elect a property as your main residence, you might not get full PPR relief if you:</p>
<ul>
<li>Rent out part of the elected main residence</li>
<li>Use it purely for business purposes during ownership</li>
<li>Don't occupy it for the entire ownership period</li>
</ul>
<p>However, you may qualify for letting relief if you rent out your elected main residence. This can provide up to £40,000 of additional CGT relief, subject to certain conditions.</p>
<p>PPR relief can still apply during periods when you don't physically occupy your elected main residence, including:</p>
<ul>
<li>The last 9 months of ownership (regardless of occupation)</li>
<li>Periods of employment abroad</li>
<li>Up to 4 years of employment elsewhere in the UK</li>
<li>Periods when prevented from occupying due to work conditions</li>
</ul>

<h2>Common Mistakes to Avoid</h2>
<p>Many landlords make costly errors with their main residence elections:</p>
<h3>Missing the Two-Year Deadline</h3>
<p>This is the most expensive mistake. Without an election, HMRC determines your main residence based on factors like where you spend most nights, receive post, and are registered to vote. This may not align with the most tax-efficient choice.</p>
<h3>Electing Based on Current Value Only</h3>
<p>Don't just elect the property with the highest current value. Consider potential future gains, likelihood of sale, and your long-term plans for each property.</p>
<h3>Forgetting to Update Elections</h3>
<p>If your circumstances change significantly, review whether your election still makes sense. You might want to switch elections before selling a property to maximize PPR relief.</p>

<h2>Special Considerations for Landlords</h2>
<p>As a landlord with multiple properties, several factors should influence your election strategy:</p>
<h3>Development Potential</h3>
<p>If one property has significant development potential, electing it as your main residence could shelter substantial future gains from CGT. However, you must actually live in the property for this to be valid.</p>
<h3>Rental Income Impact</h3>
<p>Remember that electing a property as your main residence doesn't affect its rental income tax treatment. You'll still pay <a href="/blog/section-24-and-tax-relief/rental-income-tax-uk-complete-guide-landlords">rental income tax</a> on any letting income, and <a href="/blog/section-24-and-tax-relief/section-24-tax-relief-complete-guide">Section 24 restrictions</a> still apply to mortgage interest relief.</p>
<h3>Company Ownership</h3>
<p>If you own properties through a limited company, these elections don't apply. Companies cannot claim PPR relief, as they cannot have a "residence" in the personal sense. Consider this when deciding whether to use <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/buy-to-let-limited-company-complete-guide-uk">limited company structures</a> for property investment.</p>

<h2>Record Keeping Requirements</h2>
<p>Maintain detailed records to support your election, including:</p>
<ul>
<li>Copy of your election notification to HMRC</li>
<li>Evidence of residence in both properties (utility bills, council tax, etc.)</li>
<li>Property purchase and improvement costs</li>
<li>Any letting income from either property</li>
<li>Dates of occupation for each property</li>
</ul>
<p>These records become crucial when calculating your CGT liability upon disposal, particularly if you've changed elections or had periods of non-occupation.</p>

<ul>
<li>Calculate the potential CGT implications of different election strategies</li>
<li>Ensure you meet all deadlines and requirements</li>
<li>Integrate the election with your broader <a href="/blog/landlord-tax-essentials/property-investment-tax-uk-complete-guide-2026">property tax planning</a></li>
<li>Review elections when your circumstances change</li>
</ul>
<p>The cost of professional advice is often far outweighed by the potential tax savings from making the right election at the right time.</p>
