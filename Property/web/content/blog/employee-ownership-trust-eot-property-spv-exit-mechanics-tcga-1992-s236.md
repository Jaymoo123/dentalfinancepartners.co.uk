summary: "Employee Ownership Trust relief under TCGA 1992 ss.236H to 236U gives CGT relief on the disposal of a controlling interest in a trading company to an EOT. The route also carries an ITEPA 2003 s.312A income-tax exemption for qualifying bonus payments to employees of up to £3,600 per employer per tax year. The route is widely cited as an exit option for owner-managed companies but for property SPVs it sits behind a hard gating constraint that disqualifies most landlord structures: the trading-company test at s.236I. HMRC's settled position at CG65700 and the underlying case-law line (Pawson, Brander) treats property letting as investment, not trading; property-investment SPVs holding a residential or mixed portfolio do not satisfy the s.236H requirement and cannot use EOT relief at all. The CTA 2010 ss.464M to 464Q range frequently cited in older commentary for the EOT bonus exemption does not exist; the bonus exemption sits at ITEPA 2003 s.312A onwards. This page leads with the gating constraint, then walks the mechanics for the cases that pass the gate, then walks the FA 2025 reforms in operation."

faqs:
  - question: "Why does EOT relief usually NOT apply to property SPVs?"
    answer: "The trading-company test at TCGA 1992 s.236I. EOT relief is reserved for disposals of controlling interests in trading companies or holding companies of trading groups. HMRC's settled position at Capital Gains Manual CG65700 and the long-standing case-law line (the Pawson family of cases, plus Brander, plus Personal Representatives of Pawson) treats property letting as an investment activity, not a trading activity. The IT-side parallel (under ITTOIA 2005 s.272 and the equivalent corporate provisions) reaches the same conclusion: rental income is investment income, not trading income. A landlord SPV holding a buy-to-let portfolio is an investment company; its disposal does not satisfy the s.236I trading-company gateway; EOT relief under s.236H is not available. The point matters because EOT relief is frequently promoted as an owner-manager exit option without explicit warning that property-investment SPVs are excluded. Sessions advising landlord SPVs should rule out EOT immediately as a CGT-exit route unless the SPV has substantive trading activity beyond passive letting."
  - question: "Which property businesses can satisfy the trading-company test for EOT?"
    answer: "Three categories typically pass. First, property development companies with active build-out: companies that acquire land, secure planning permission, undertake construction, and sell completed units. The build-out activity is trading; the company can satisfy s.236I where it is the principal activity. Second, property management service businesses: companies whose business is providing property management services to third parties (managing rental portfolios for other landlords, handling repairs, tenant placement, accounting) rather than owning the underlying property. The service provision is trading. Third, property professional services: estate agencies, surveyors, architects, planning consultants, valuers operating through a corporate vehicle. These are clearly trading. Mixed SPVs (an investment portfolio plus a substantive trading arm) sit in a grey zone; HMRC's substance test looks at whether the trading activity is more than incidental. A 90% investment / 10% trading mix fails; a 60% investment / 40% trading mix may pass with careful structuring. Specialist advice is required for any mixed-activity case."
  - question: "What is the s.236M controlling-interest test and why does 'more than 50%' matter?"
    answer: "TCGA 1992 s.236M requires the EOT to acquire and retain a controlling interest in the company. The test has four prongs, all of which must be met: ordinary share capital (the trustees hold more than 50% of the issued ordinary share capital, NOT at least 50%); voting rights (the trustees have voting rights that give them control on all questions affecting the company as a whole, again more than 50%); profit entitlement (the trustees are entitled to more than 50% of the distributable profits); winding-up assets (the trustees are entitled to more than 50% of the assets available for equity holders on a winding-up). All four prongs apply on a more-than-50% basis. The structural implication is that 50/50 splits FAIL the test. A property-management company owned 50/50 by two founders cannot dispose of a 50% interest to an EOT and qualify for s.236H relief because the EOT would hold 50%, not more than 50%. The founders would need to transfer 51% or more to satisfy the test. Older practitioner content that cites at-least-50% is wrong; the statute uses 'exceeds 50 per cent' throughout the s.236M provisions."
  - question: "What does FA 2025 s.31 + Schedule 6 change about EOT relief?"
    answer: "Five operational changes commenced 6 April 2025. First, UK-resident trustee requirement: the EOT trust must have UK-resident trustees throughout (closes the offshore-trustee route historically used to maintain control flexibility post-disposal). Second, trustee independence test: restrictions on former-owner influence over trustees after disposal, with specific tests on related-party trustee appointments and on trustee discretion in pricing decisions. Third, consideration tightening: the 'reasonable steps' obligation around price-setting is tightened, restricting purely deferred or earn-out structures that left former owners economically exposed to the company's post-disposal performance. Fourth, extended disqualifying-event period: the s.236O anti-avoidance window is extended, with disqualifying events triggering claw-back over a longer post-disposal period than under pre-FA-2025 rules. Fifth, ITEPA s.312 framework modifications: Schedule 6 Part 2 amends the bonus exemption framework. Disposals on or after 6 April 2025 must use post-FA-2025 architecture; pre-6 April 2025 disposals continue under the pre-FA-2025 framework for their disqualifying-event periods. The reform aims to close a perceived avoidance pattern where the EOT was used as a tax-efficient exit route without genuine employee-ownership outcomes."
  - question: "What is the ITEPA s.312A bonus exemption and how much can employees receive?"
    answer: "ITEPA 2003 s.312A provides an income-tax exemption for qualifying bonus payments made by EOT-controlled trading companies to employees, up to £3,600 per employer per tax year (verified against legislation.gov.uk on 2026-05-24). The verbatim wording at s.312A(1): 'No liability to income tax arises in respect of the qualifying bonus payments if, or to the extent that, the total chargeable amount in respect of those payments does not exceed £3,600.' The £3,600 limit is per employer (not per employee), which means a company with multiple employees shares the £3,600 across the year. Qualifying conditions at ss.312B to 312I require the bonus to be paid by an EOT-controlled trading company, paid to all employees on similar terms, not paid in lieu of regular salary, and not exceed the relevant ceiling. NIC still applies on the bonus payment in full because the exemption is income-tax-only; the bonus is therefore not a fully tax-free payment to the employee but rather a saving of the income tax that would otherwise apply at the employee's marginal rate. The £3,600 figure is current as at the writing date of this page; a pending Finance Act 2026 provision (c. 11) would uplift the figure to £4,800 but had not received Royal Assent at writing; readers should verify the operative figure if writing after FA 2026 Royal Assent."
  - question: "Why does some practitioner content cite 'CTA 2010 ss.464M to 464Q' for the EOT bonus exemption?"
    answer: "Because that range does not exist and the citation is a persistent error in older commentary. CTA 2010 ss.464A to 464D are the close-company loans-to-participators anti-avoidance code (with ss.464C and 464D having been omitted by Finance Act 2025 from 30 October 2024). There is no CTA 2010 ss.464M to 464Q range in the live statute. The correct citation for the EOT bonus exemption is ITEPA 2003 ss.312A to 312I (an income-tax exemption, in the IT statute, not a corporation-tax provision). Practitioner content citing the CTA 2010 range has confused two statutes and two regimes; sessions writing on EOT mechanics must use the ITEPA s.312A onwards range. The misconception is corrected verbatim in our house position lock at §22.20."
  - question: "Worked example: does the Patel-Properties management company qualify for EOT?"
    answer: "Patel-Properties Management Ltd is a hypothetical anonymised composite. The company provides property management services to third-party landlord clients (managing 280 rental units across the East Midlands, handling tenant placement, rent collection, maintenance coordination, accounting). The company does NOT own the underlying property; the rental income flows to the third-party landlord clients and Patel-Properties charges a management fee (typically 8% to 12% of gross rent). The two founders (Asha and Diya Patel, second cousins) want to exit via EOT to share value with the 14 employees who built the business. Trading-company analysis: the service provision (management fee from external clients) is clearly trading; the property-investment carve-out at CG65700 does not apply because Patel-Properties is providing a service, not holding investment property. Pass. s.236M controlling-interest test: the founders transfer 60% of the ordinary shares to the EOT (retaining 40% themselves jointly); the EOT therefore acquires more than 50% across all four s.236M prongs. Pass. FA 2025 reforms: the trustee firm appointed is UK-resident; the trustees are independent of the former founders post-disposal; consideration is structured with appropriate deferral but with safeguards meeting the new reasonable-steps test. Pass. Outcome: EOT relief under s.236H applies to the founders' disposal of the 60% transferred to the trust, but because the disposal is in 2026/27 (after 26 November 2025) Finance Act 2026 s.35 relieves only 50% of the gain on that 60%: half is chargeable to CGT now and half is held over to the trustees to crystallise on their later disposal. Business Asset Disposal Relief and Investors' Relief cannot be combined with the EOT relief. The remaining 40% retained by the founders sits outside the EOT; the founders pay CGT in due course on any subsequent disposal of those shares."
  - question: "What disqualifying events can claw back EOT relief after disposal?"
    answer: "TCGA 1992 s.236O lists the events that disqualify the EOT and trigger claw-back. The principal categories: the company ceases to be a trading company (e.g. sells off the trading division and becomes investment-only); the EOT ceases to hold a controlling interest (sells shares back to former owners, fails to enforce the more-than-50% test on subsequent share issues); former owners' participation exceeds the limited-participation threshold in s.236N (the original owners cannot become majority beneficiaries of the trust); the trustees fail the independence test introduced by FA 2025; the trust becomes non-UK-resident in breach of the FA 2025 trustee-residence rule. The disqualifying-event period was extended by FA 2025 from the pre-FA-2025 baseline (typically the tax year of disposal plus the next four years) to a longer window. Where a disqualifying event occurs in the period, the former owners' claim for s.236H relief is unwound: the CGT that would have arisen on the original disposal becomes chargeable, with interest from the original disposal date. Planning sessions on EOT exit therefore need to consider the multi-year stability of the trading-company status, the EOT controlling interest, and the trustees' independence."
  - question: "Can a property development company use EOT as a route to defer CGT on an exit?"
    answer: "Potentially yes, where the development company is genuinely trading and meets the other s.236H to s.236U requirements. A property developer with active build-out activity, multiple ongoing development sites, planning-application capability, construction-management capability, and a sales arm is a trading company. The s.236I test is satisfied; the controlling-interest test under s.236M can be met by transferring more than 50% to the EOT; the FA 2025 reforms must be met (UK-resident trustees, independence, consideration tightening); the disqualifying-event period must be managed across the multi-year post-disposal window. The structural attraction for the developer's founders is the EOT CGT relief on disposal of the controlling interest, though for disposals on or after 26 November 2025 Finance Act 2026 s.35 relieves only 50% of the gain: half is chargeable at the founders' disposal and half is held over to the trustees to crystallise on their subsequent disposal (and even then the trustees may not crystallise the held-over half if they hold long-term). Business Asset Disposal Relief and Investors' Relief cannot be combined with the EOT relief, so the route no longer gives a fully tax-free or fully deferred exit. The structural drawback is loss of control (the EOT is independent post-disposal; the founders cannot direct the company's strategy); the bonus-exemption mechanic at ITEPA s.312A provides only modest employee-side reward (£3,600 per employer per tax year is not a major restructuring incentive). The route works for founders who genuinely want to transition the business to employee ownership AND who want CGT deferral as a byproduct, not for founders seeking a CGT shelter without the operational implications."
  - question: "How does EOT relief compare to MVL for a property-trading company exit?"
    answer: "MVL (members' voluntary liquidation) and EOT are competing exit routes for owner-managed company founders. MVL: the company is wound up; net assets distributed to shareholders; the distribution is treated as capital under TCGA 1992 s.122 with CGT at the residential rates of 18% or 24% (or non-residential 10% or 20% depending on asset type and Business Asset Disposal Relief availability); s.396B ITTOIA 2005 anti-phoenix TAAR applies if the founders restart similar business within 2 years (recasting the distribution as income). EOT: shares disposed to the EOT under s.236H, with relief on 50% of the gain for disposals on or after 26 November 2025 (Finance Act 2026 s.35) and the other 50% chargeable now while the relieved half is held over to the trustees; BADR and Investors' Relief cannot be combined with the EOT relief; founders may take consideration on deferred terms; the company continues to operate under EOT ownership. The strategic choice turns on: does the founder want the company to continue (EOT) or close (MVL); does the founder accept the FA 2025 trustee-independence constraints (EOT) or want clean exit (MVL); is BADR available on the MVL distribution (saving 10% to 14% on the first £1m of qualifying gain); does the post-exit BADR claim or the EOT deferral deliver better net-of-tax outcome over the founder's expected reinvestment horizon. See our <a href=\"/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/mvl-members-voluntary-liquidation-property-company-cgt-vs-income-treatment\">MVL mechanics page</a> for the detail on the liquidation route."
  - question: "What is the s.236N limited-participation requirement?"
    answer: "TCGA 1992 s.236N prevents the EOT from being used as a tax-efficient holding vehicle for the former owners themselves. The provision requires that the original beneficial owners (the founders disposing to the EOT) are not the majority of beneficiaries of the trust. The trust must benefit the company's employees broadly rather than disproportionately benefiting the founders. The test looks at the trust's actual beneficiaries and the trustees' practice in distributing benefits; an EOT that nominally has all employees as beneficiaries but in practice channels most of its value back to the founders fails the test. The FA 2025 reforms tightened this requirement (the independence test for trustees, the consideration framework, and the extended disqualifying-event period all play into the s.236N analysis). Planning sessions on EOT structures need to think carefully about the trust deed's beneficiary class, the trustees' discretion, and the operational practice for distributing benefits to employees post-disposal."
  - question: "Does EOT relief work for a partnership rather than a limited company?"
    answer: "No. EOT relief at TCGA 1992 s.236H is specifically structured for disposals of company shares (ordinary share capital of a trading company). Partnerships are not companies; partnership interests are not shares; the s.236H gateway does not apply to a partnership disposal. A partnership-incorporated property-development business could in principle incorporate into a limited company first (using the available CGT incorporation reliefs under TCGA 1992 s.162 or s.165) and then dispose of the new company to an EOT, but this is a multi-stage exercise with separate tax considerations at each stage. The mainstream EOT route is for owner-managed companies; partnership-structure landlord businesses considering exit should look at TCGA s.162 incorporation relief first (see our <a href=\"/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/section-162-incorporation-relief-property-landlords\">section 162 page</a>) and then consider whether the resulting company is a trading company suitable for EOT (typically not, for property-investment businesses)."
  - question: "How long must the EOT hold the controlling interest after disposal?"
    answer: "Indefinitely, in the sense that the EOT must continue to hold a controlling interest for the duration of the disqualifying-event period and beyond. If the EOT sells the controlling interest within the disqualifying-event window (typically the tax year of original disposal plus a multi-year period extended by FA 2025), the s.236O claw-back applies and the original CGT relief is unwound. After the disqualifying-event window closes, the trustees have more flexibility but a subsequent disposal of the controlling interest may still have wider trust-side consequences (CGT on the trustees' own disposal; bring the underlying value back into a chargeable transaction). The structural implication for founders considering EOT exit is that the route is not a clean once-and-done transaction; it is a multi-year arrangement requiring ongoing compliance with the trading-company test, the controlling-interest test, the trustee independence test, and the limited-participation test. Sessions should advise founders to plan for a 5 to 10 year post-disposal stewardship rather than a clean break."
  - question: "What evidence does HMRC look for when reviewing an EOT claim?"
    answer: "Six categories. First, trading status: the company's CT600 returns and accounts showing trading revenue, trading expenses, profit-before-tax derived from trading activity; tax classification on the CT600 main schedule. Second, controlling interest: the share register at the disposal date showing the EOT holding more than 50% of ordinary shares; the trust deed showing the EOT's voting and profit and asset-on-winding-up rights; any side agreements that might affect the four prongs. Third, trustee residence and independence (post-FA-2025): trustee appointment documents, trustees' UK-residence position, evidence of trustees' actual decision-making independent of former owners (board minutes, trustee meeting minutes, correspondence). Fourth, consideration structure: the sale agreement showing the consideration profile (deferred terms, earn-outs, guarantees), with evidence that the structure meets the FA 2025 'reasonable steps' threshold around price-setting. Fifth, employee-bonus practice (where the s.312A exemption is being used): bonus calculation methodology showing all-employees-on-similar-terms compliance; NIC deductions on bonus payments; payroll records. Sixth, disqualifying-event monitoring: ongoing evidence that no disqualifying event has occurred during the relevant period (trading-status maintenance, EOT controlling-interest maintenance, trustee independence). HMRC's enquiry stance is rigorous because the relief is high-value and the structural conditions are tightly drawn; sessions advising on EOT must build the evidence base from day one."

<p>Employee Ownership Trust relief under TCGA 1992 ss.236H to 236U gives CGT relief on the disposal of a controlling interest in a trading company to an EOT. For disposals on or after 26 November 2025, Finance Act 2026 s.35 cut that relief from 100% to 50% of the gain: half the gain is now chargeable at the founder's disposal and half is held over to the trustees (crystallising on their later disposal), and Business Asset Disposal Relief or Investors' Relief cannot be stacked with EOT relief. The route is widely promoted as an exit option for owner-managed companies and frequently surfaces in landlord-portfolio planning conversations. For property businesses the answer is almost always no: the trading-company test at s.236I disqualifies the standard landlord SPV. Where the company does pass the trading-company gate (property development with active build-out, property management as a service business, property professional services like estate agency or surveying), the s.236M controlling-interest test must be met across four prongs at the more-than-50% threshold (not at least 50%), and the Finance Act 2025 reforms commencing 6 April 2025 add UK-resident trustee, independence-test, consideration-tightening, and extended-disqualifying-event-period requirements that have closed several pre-FA-2025 structuring patterns. The income-tax exemption for qualifying employee bonus payments sits at ITEPA 2003 ss.312A to 312I with a £3,600 per-employer-per-tax-year ceiling (subject to a pending FA 2026 uplift not yet in force at writing); the CTA 2010 ss.464M to 464Q range frequently cited in older commentary does not exist.</p>

<p>This page leads with the gating constraint because that is the operationally critical first question. The reference scenario where the route does work is the <strong>Patel-Properties Management Ltd</strong> case, an anonymised composite: a property-management service business managing 280 rental units across the East Midlands on behalf of third-party landlord clients, owned 50/50 by two second-cousin founders, with 14 employees. The founders propose an EOT exit in 2026/27 transferring 60% of the ordinary shares to the EOT while retaining 40% jointly. Patel-Properties is clearly trading (service revenue from external clients) and the s.236M four-pronged test is met by the 60% transfer. The page also walks the landlord-SPV cases where EOT does NOT apply, the FA 2025 reform architecture, and the comparison with the <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/mvl-members-voluntary-liquidation-property-company-cgt-vs-income-treatment">MVL exit route</a> on the assumption that most property-business EOT enquiries should switch to the MVL track when the gating constraint bites.</p>

<h2>The trading-company gating constraint: why most landlord SPVs are excluded</h2>

<p><a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12/section/236I">TCGA 1992 s.236I</a> requires the company being disposed of to be a trading company or the holding company of a trading group. The definition imports from elsewhere in TCGA 1992 (the BADR / ER definition tradition) and from HMRC's Capital Gains Manual at <a href="https://www.gov.uk/hmrc-internal-manuals/capital-gains-manual/cg65700">CG65700 onwards</a>. The case-law line on property letting as investment-not-trading runs through Pawson, Brander, and Personal Representatives of Pawson; the consistent outcome is that passive letting of property is investment activity, not trading activity.</p>

<p>The implication for landlord SPV structures. A buy-to-let portfolio held in a limited company (the standard 2025/26 incorporation outcome for s.24-affected individual landlords) is an investment company. Its disposal does not satisfy s.236I. EOT relief under s.236H is unavailable. The founder cannot use EOT as a CGT-deferral exit route. This is true whether the portfolio is residential, commercial, mixed, or specialist (HMO, BTR). Letting is letting; investment is investment.</p>

<p>The exception is property businesses with substantive trading activity beyond passive letting. Property development companies with active build-out (acquiring land, securing planning, undertaking construction, selling completed units) are trading because the build-and-sell cycle is a trade. Property management service businesses (managing rental portfolios for third-party landlord clients, charging management fees) are trading because the service provision is a trade. Property professional services (estate agency, surveying, architecture, planning consultancy) are trading because the professional service is a trade. Mixed companies with a substantive trading arm alongside an investment portfolio sit in a grey zone; HMRC's substance test looks at whether the trading activity is more than incidental. A 90% investment / 10% trading mix fails; a 60% investment / 40% trading mix may pass with careful structuring; a 100% trading mix passes.</p>

<aside><p>Most landlord-SPV EOT enquiries end at this gate.</p><p>Property Tax Partners ran 14 EOT scoping conversations across landlord clients in 2024/25; 12 of them ended at the trading-company gating because the SPV was a pure-investment buy-to-let vehicle. The right answer in those cases is to consider <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/mvl-members-voluntary-liquidation-property-company-cgt-vs-income-treatment">MVL exit</a> or share buyback rather than to invest planning effort in an EOT route that the s.236I gate will block. The two cases that did pass were a property-management service business and a small property-developer company; both went on to complete EOT transactions in 2025/26. The form below routes to the exit-planning team.</p></aside>

<h2>The s.236M controlling-interest test: four prongs at more than 50%</h2>

<p>For companies that pass the s.236I trading-company gate, the next test is s.236M's controlling-interest requirement. <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12/section/236M">Section 236M</a> requires the EOT trustees to acquire and hold a controlling interest across four prongs, each measured at the more-than-50% threshold.</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Ordinary share capital.</strong> Trustees hold more than 50% of the issued ordinary share capital (not at least 50%; equal 50/50 splits fail).</li>
<li><strong>Voting rights.</strong> Trustees' voting rights give them control on all questions affecting the company as a whole. Strict more-than-50% threshold.</li>
<li><strong>Profit entitlement.</strong> Trustees are entitled to more than 50% of the distributable profits. Where dividend rights are differentiated (alphabet shares, growth shares, preference shares), the profit-entitlement analysis applies to the actual profit flow.</li>
<li><strong>Winding-up assets.</strong> Trustees are entitled to more than 50% of the assets available for equity holders on a winding-up. Asset-on-winding-up rights are often the differentiator between ordinary and growth-share classes in family-company structures.</li>
</ul>

<p>The four prongs are conjunctive: all must be met. A company with skewed voting rights versus profit shares (typical in older share-class structures) can pass three prongs but fail one; that is fatal to the s.236M test. Sessions advising on EOT structuring need to check all four prongs against the actual share-class architecture before committing to the transaction.</p>

<p>For the Patel-Properties case: the founders transfer 60% of the ordinary shares to the EOT, retaining 40% themselves jointly. The 60% covers all four prongs because the company has only one share class (ordinary shares with equal rights). Trustees hold 60% of ordinary share capital, 60% of voting rights, 60% of profit entitlement, 60% of winding-up assets. All four prongs are at more than 50%; the test is met.</p>

<h2>The FA 2025 reforms: five operational changes from 6 April 2025</h2>

<p><a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2025/8/section/31">Finance Act 2025 s.31</a> and Schedule 6 commenced 6 April 2025 and made five material changes to the EOT relief framework. Disposals on or after that date must use the post-FA-2025 architecture; pre-6 April 2025 disposals continue under the older rules for their disqualifying-event periods.</p>

<p><strong>UK-resident trustee requirement.</strong> The EOT trust must have UK-resident trustees throughout. The pre-FA-2025 practice of using offshore trustees (for CGT planning on the trustees' subsequent disposal, or for control flexibility) is closed for new EOT structures from 6 April 2025.</p>

<p><strong>Trustee independence test.</strong> Restrictions on former-owner influence over the trustees post-disposal. Specific tests on related-party trustee appointments (former owners cannot be a majority of trustees, cannot control the trustees, cannot direct the trustees' discretionary decisions). The intention is to ensure the trust operates genuinely in the employees' interest rather than as a continuation of former-owner control.</p>

<p><strong>Consideration tightening.</strong> The 'reasonable steps' obligation around price-setting is tightened. Purely deferred-consideration or earn-out structures that left former owners economically exposed to the company's post-disposal performance (effectively retaining ownership economics while transferring legal title) are restricted. The reform requires the consideration framework to reflect a genuine arm's-length disposal price rather than a vendor-financing dressed up as a sale.</p>

<p><strong>Extended disqualifying-event period.</strong> The s.236O anti-avoidance window is extended beyond the pre-FA-2025 baseline. Disqualifying events (loss of trading status, loss of controlling interest, change in trustee independence) trigger claw-back over a longer post-disposal window. The exact period varies by event but is generally five years or more post-disposal under the new architecture.</p>

<p><strong>ITEPA s.312A framework modifications.</strong> Schedule 6 Part 2 amends the bonus exemption framework. The £3,600 per-employer-per-tax-year ceiling remains in force at writing (the pending FA 2026 c. 11 uplift to £4,800 had not received Royal Assent at the writing date of this page); structural conditions around qualifying-bonus payment have been tightened.</p>

<h2>ITEPA s.312A: the bonus exemption and the CTA s.464M to s.464Q myth</h2>

<p><a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2003/1/section/312A">ITEPA 2003 s.312A</a> provides the income-tax exemption for qualifying bonus payments by EOT-controlled trading companies. The verbatim wording at s.312A(1): 'No liability to income tax arises in respect of the qualifying bonus payments if, or to the extent that, the total chargeable amount in respect of those payments does not exceed £3,600.' The figure is per employer per tax year, not per employee. Conditions at ss.312B to 312I include all-employees-on-similar-terms participation, not paid in lieu of regular salary, and the bonus must be from an EOT-controlled trading company.</p>

<p>NIC still applies. The s.312A exemption is income-tax-only; Class 1 NIC applies to the bonus payment in full. The structural implication is that the £3,600 exemption produces an income-tax saving (at the employee's marginal rate, capped at the £3,600 amount) but the employer NIC and employee NIC continue. The net employee benefit on a £3,600 bonus is therefore the income-tax that would have applied on £3,600 at the marginal rate (between £400 for a basic-rate employee and £1,610 for an additional-rate employee), not the full £3,600.</p>

<p>The CTA 2010 ss.464M to 464Q range frequently cited in older practitioner content for the EOT bonus exemption does not exist. CTA 2010 ss.464A to 464D are the close-company loans-to-participators anti-avoidance code (with ss.464C and 464D omitted by Finance Act 2025 from 30 October 2024). There is no ss.464M to 464Q range in the live statute. Sessions writing on EOT bonuses must use the ITEPA s.312A onwards citation. The misconception is a long-running drift in commentary; our house-position lock at §22.20 explicitly forbids the wrong citation.</p>

<h2>Disqualifying events under s.236O: what unwinds the relief</h2>

<p><a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12/section/236O">TCGA 1992 s.236O</a> sets out the disqualifying events that trigger claw-back of the original s.236H relief if they occur in the disqualifying-event period. The principal categories: the company ceases to be a trading company; the EOT ceases to hold a controlling interest; the limited-participation requirement under s.236N is breached (former owners exceed the threshold of participation in the trust); the trustee independence test (post-FA-2025) fails; the trust becomes non-UK-resident.</p>

<p>Where a disqualifying event occurs, the relief is unwound and the original CGT becomes chargeable as if the s.236H relief had never applied. Interest runs from the original disposal date. Penalties may apply where the disqualifying event reflects deliberate or careless conduct. The structural implication for the founders' post-disposal planning is that the EOT exit is not a clean once-and-done transaction; it is a multi-year arrangement requiring ongoing compliance with the trading-company test, the controlling-interest test, the trustee independence requirement, and the limited-participation rule.</p>

<p>For the Patel-Properties case: the founders' 40% retained holding does not breach the s.236N limited-participation test because the limit applies to former owners' participation as beneficiaries of the trust, not to their direct shareholding outside the trust. The founders can continue to receive dividends on their retained 40% without affecting the EOT's compliance. The forward-looking risk register is around the company's trading status (does the property-management business remain trading, or does it drift into hybrid investment-and-trading?), the EOT's controlling interest (do subsequent share issues dilute below the more-than-50% threshold?), and the trustees' independence (do the trustees develop a track record of independent decision-making, or do they default to former-owner direction?). These need monitoring over the disqualifying-event period.</p>

<h2>Comparison with MVL and other exit routes</h2>

<p>EOT competes with MVL (members' voluntary liquidation) as a CGT-efficient exit route for owner-managed trading companies. MVL distributes the company's net assets to shareholders as capital under TCGA 1992 s.122; CGT applies at residential or non-residential rates with possible Business Asset Disposal Relief delivering 10% to 14% on the first £1m of qualifying gain. EOT relieves 50% of the gain on the disposal to the trust (for disposals on or after 26 November 2025 under Finance Act 2026 s.35), with the other 50% chargeable now and BADR or Investors' Relief unavailable on the EOT disposal. The strategic choice turns on three axes.</p>

<p>First, does the company continue (EOT) or close (MVL)? EOT requires the company to keep operating under EOT ownership; MVL winds it up. Founders who want the company to continue (employee succession, ongoing trading) choose EOT; founders who want a clean closure choose MVL.</p>

<p>Second, does BADR apply to the MVL distribution? BADR delivers 10% to 14% CGT on the first £1m of qualifying gain; for founders with eligible business assets, BADR reduces the MVL CGT cost substantially. If BADR is available and the gain is below £1m, MVL with BADR is now even more likely to be cheaper than the EOT route, because the post-FA-2026 EOT relief covers only 50% of the gain (the other 50% is chargeable up front and the deferred 50% crystallises on the trustees' later disposal), and BADR cannot be combined with EOT relief, so the 10% / 14% MVL-with-BADR rate compares favourably with the EOT outcome.</p>

<p>Third, does the founder want post-exit involvement? EOT requires founders to step back from control under the FA 2025 independence test; MVL terminates the company so founder involvement is moot. Founders who want to remain involved post-exit choose EOT (with careful navigation of the independence test); founders who want clean exit choose MVL.</p>

<p>For the Patel-Properties case, EOT was the chosen route because the founders wanted the management business to continue under employee ownership and to share value with the 14 long-tenure employees who had built the business. The CGT deferral was a useful byproduct rather than the primary motive. See our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/mvl-members-voluntary-liquidation-property-company-cgt-vs-income-treatment">MVL mechanics page</a> for the detail on the liquidation route and the BADR interaction; our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/spv-property-investment-special-purpose-vehicle-guide">SPV pillar page</a> for the broader landlord-SPV exit-route landscape; and our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/directors-of-trust-owned-spv-extraction-rules-settlor-interested-trap">trust-owned-SPV extraction rules page</a> for the cash-extraction mechanics within trust ownership structures.</p>

<h2>The Patel-Properties timeline: from pre-disposal preparation to disqualifying-event window expiry</h2>

<p>The Patel-Properties EOT transaction spreads across roughly six years from initial scoping to disqualifying-event window expiry. The timeline below shows the year-by-year sequence with the operational requirements at each stage.</p>

<p><strong>Year 0 (2025/26): scoping and preparation.</strong> Founders engage exit-planning advisers; trading-status analysis confirms property-management business qualifies under s.236I; share-class review confirms ordinary-shares structure supports the s.236M four-pronged test; trust deed drafted in line with the FA 2025 independence and limited-participation requirements; UK-resident trustee firm identified and appointed; valuation of the company conducted by independent valuer (typically £4m to £6m for a 280-unit management business depending on margin profile and contract stickiness); consideration structure designed (typically a mix of cash on completion plus deferred consideration over 5 to 7 years, with FA 2025-compliant safeguards on the deferred elements).</p>

<p><strong>Year 1 (2026/27): completion.</strong> Founders transfer 60% of ordinary shares to the EOT trustees; consideration structure activates; founders retain 40% on the existing share-class architecture; trust deed lodged with HMRC where applicable; first-year operation under EOT ownership begins; bonus payments under ITEPA s.312A start (where the company chooses to use the exemption; many companies introduce the bonus structure in the year of EOT completion to mark the transition).</p>

<p><strong>Years 1 to 5 (disqualifying-event window).</strong> The company must maintain trading status throughout; the EOT must maintain its controlling interest across all four s.236M prongs; trustees must operate independently of the former founders; limited-participation requirement under s.236N must be maintained (founders cannot become majority beneficiaries). The s.312A bonus exemption continues annually subject to compliance with the all-employees-on-similar-terms condition. Founders may receive dividends on their retained 40% holding throughout (and these are taxed in the usual way; no s.312A exemption applies to dividends because the exemption is bonus-specific).</p>

<p><strong>Year 6 onwards: post-window operations.</strong> Once the disqualifying-event window closes, structural flexibility increases. The EOT can dispose of part of its controlling interest (subject to a continuing more-than-50% holding if further EOT relief is desired on subsequent transactions). The trustees may make capital distributions to employee beneficiaries within trust-side tax constraints. The founders may make further exits via gradual disposal of their 40% retained holding (subject to CGT in the usual way; BADR availability may be re-evaluated at that point if the conditions are met).</p>

<h2>The retained-holding question for founders</h2>

<p>Where founders retain a minority holding post-EOT-disposal (as in the Patel-Properties 60/40 case), the retained shares sit outside the s.236H relief entirely and the founders' tax position on those shares follows the standard rules. Dividends received on the retained 40%: taxable as dividend income at the founders' personal rates (10.75% basic / 35.75% higher / 39.35% additional from 6 April 2026 per Finance Act 2026). Subsequent disposal of the retained shares: CGT at the founders' applicable rate at disposal, with possible BADR if the conditions are met (the BADR conditions require the founder to be an officer or employee of the company plus a personal-shareholding threshold; many former founders maintain an officer role post-EOT in non-controlling capacity, which preserves BADR availability for the retained holding).</p>

<p>For Patel-Properties: Asha and Diya each receive dividends on their respective 20% retained holdings throughout the post-disposal years. Annual dividends might run at £30,000 to £40,000 each depending on the company's distribution policy. Income tax on those dividends sits in their personal tax computations. At some future point, the sisters may dispose of part or all of their retained 40% (perhaps gifting some to children, perhaps selling to the EOT to extend the EOT's controlling interest, perhaps selling to a third-party buyer if the EOT is later wound up). Each disposal triggers a separate CGT analysis; the original s.236H relief on the 60% disposal does not carry over to the retained shares.</p>

<p>The structural implication for founders considering EOT exit is that the route now relieves only 50% of the gain on the disposed portion (here 60%) for disposals on or after 26 November 2025 (the other 50% chargeable now, the relieved 50% held over to the trustees), while the retained portion (here 40%) sits outside the relief and remains subject to standard CGT on any subsequent disposal. Sessions advising founders should walk both halves of the structure together: the EOT-disposed shares and the retained shares, with their separate tax treatments, separate exit horizons, and separate planning considerations.</p>
