<p>When it comes to <strong>CGT property transfer to spouse</strong>, the rules are generally favourable for married couples and civil partners. Understanding these exemptions can save you thousands in capital gains tax and help with property portfolio planning.</p>

<p>The key rule is straightforward: transfers between spouses and civil partners who live together are typically exempt from capital gains tax. But there are important exceptions and considerations you need to know about.</p>

<h2>The No Gain, No Loss Rule</h2>

<p>Under UK tax law, when you transfer property to your spouse or civil partner, the transaction is treated as taking place at "no gain, no loss". This means:</p>

<ul>
<li>No capital gains tax is payable on the transfer</li>
<li>Your spouse acquires the property at your original base cost</li>
<li>They inherit your acquisition date for CGT purposes</li>
<li>Any future disposal will calculate gains from your original purchase price</li>
</ul>

<p>For example, if you bought a buy-to-let property for £200,000 in 2020 and transfer it to your spouse in 2025 when it's worth £280,000, there's no CGT to pay. Your spouse's base cost becomes £200,000, not the current market value.</p>

<h2>When CGT Property Transfer to Spouse Isn't Exempt</h2>

<p>The exemption doesn't apply in all situations. Key exceptions include:</p>

<h3>Separated Couples</h3>

<p>If you're separated and no longer living together, transfers are treated as disposals at market value. CGT becomes payable on any gain since your original purchase.</p>

<p>However, you have until the end of the tax year of separation to make exempt transfers. If you separated in January 2025, you have until 5 April 2025 to transfer property without CGT implications.</p>

<h3>Non-Resident Spouses</h3>

<p>Special rules apply when transferring property to a non-UK resident spouse. The transfer may trigger CGT if the property has gained value, particularly for UK residential property under the non-resident capital gains tax rules introduced in 2015.</p>

<h3>Business Property Reliefs</h3>

<p>If the property qualifies for business asset disposal relief or other CGT reliefs, transferring to a spouse might not always be the most tax-efficient option. Your spouse won't automatically inherit your relief entitlements.</p>

<h2>Practical Planning Considerations</h2>

<p>The <strong>CGT property transfer to spouse</strong> exemption offers several planning opportunities:</p>

<h3>Income Tax Planning</h3>

<p>Transferring rental properties can help balance rental income between spouses, potentially reducing the overall tax burden. This is particularly valuable given Section 24 mortgage interest restrictions.</p>

<p>For instance, if one spouse is a higher-rate taxpayer paying 40% on rental profits while the other has unused basic rate band, transferring properties can reduce the family's total tax bill.</p>

<h3>CGT Annual Exemptions</h3>

<p>Each spouse has their own CGT annual exempt amount (£3,000 for 2024/25). Transferring properties before disposal can effectively double your available exemptions.</p>

<p>A couple with a property portfolio worth £500,000 more than the original cost could potentially save £1,200 annually by using both CGT allowances when selling properties.</p>

<h2>How to Make the Transfer</h2>

<p>Property transfers between spouses require proper documentation:</p>

<ul>
<li>Transfer deed (TR1 form) signed by both parties</li>
<li>Land Registry application to change ownership</li>
<li>Mortgage lender consent if there's an outstanding loan</li>
<li>Update insurance policies and rental agreements</li>
</ul>

<p>While no CGT is payable, you still need to keep records of the original purchase price and transfer date for future calculations.</p>

<h2>What About Stamp Duty?</h2>

<p>Transfers between spouses are generally exempt from stamp duty land tax (SDLT), provided no money changes hands. The transfer must be for "no chargeable consideration" to qualify for this exemption.</p>

<p>If you're transferring property as part of a financial settlement where one spouse pays the other, SDLT may become payable on the consideration received.</p>

<h2>Record Keeping and Future Disposals</h2>

<p>Even though the <strong>CGT property transfer to spouse</strong> is exempt, maintain detailed records:</p>

<ul>
<li>Original purchase documentation and costs</li>
<li>Transfer documents and dates</li>
<li>Enhancement expenditure invoices</li>
<li>Professional fees related to the transfer</li>
</ul>

<p>When your spouse eventually sells the property, they'll need this information to calculate any CGT liability based on the original acquisition cost and date.</p>

<h2>Getting Professional Advice</h2>

<p>While the basic rules around <strong>CGT property transfer to spouse</strong> are straightforward, property portfolios can involve complex situations. Factors like mixed-use properties, overseas elements, or business structures need careful consideration.</p>

<p>If you're planning transfers as part of broader tax planning or have properties held through companies, speak to a specialist property tax advisor. They can help ensure you're maximizing available exemptions while avoiding unexpected tax charges.</p>
