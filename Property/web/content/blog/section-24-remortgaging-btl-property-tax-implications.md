<p>When you <strong>remortgage your buy-to-let property</strong>, Section 24 continues to apply to all mortgage interest payments, regardless of whether you're refinancing existing debt or borrowing additional funds. The tax restriction caps your mortgage interest relief at the basic rate of 20%, even if you're a higher or additional rate taxpayer.</p>

<p>This means that <strong>section 24 remortgaging</strong> decisions can significantly impact your tax position, particularly if you're increasing your borrowing or moving from a lower to higher interest rate. Understanding how the restriction applies to different remortgaging scenarios is crucial for effective tax planning.</p>

<h2>How Section 24 Works with Remortgaging</h2>

<p>Section 24 treats all mortgage interest on residential buy-to-let properties as a basic rate tax credit, not a deduction from rental income. This applies whether the mortgage is:</p>

<ul>
<li>Your original purchase mortgage</li>
<li>A remortgage with the same lender</li>
<li>A remortgaging with a new lender</li>
<li>Additional borrowing secured against the property</li>
</ul>

<p>The restriction applies to the total annual interest paid, regardless of how many times you've remortgaged or restructured your financing during the tax year.</p>

<h2>Tax and Cash Flow Impact of Remortgaging</h2>

<p>Remortgaging, particularly to increase borrowing or at higher interest rates, significantly impacts your tax liability and cash flow under Section 24. The restriction makes higher and additional rate taxpayers less resilient to rising costs.</p>

<p><strong>Example of Increased Borrowing:</strong> A higher rate taxpayer remortgages their BTL property, increasing their mortgage from £200,000 to £300,000 at 5% interest. Their annual interest increases from £10,000 to £15,000. Under Section 24:</p>
<ul>
<li>Total interest: £15,000</li>
<li>Tax credit available: £15,000 × 20% = £3,000</li>
<li>If they could deduct the full amount, the tax saving would be £15,000 × 40% = £6,000</li>
<li>Section 24 cost: £6,000 - £3,000 = £3,000 additional tax annually</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Example of Higher Interest Rates:</strong> Consider a portfolio landlord with £500,000 of BTL mortgages who remortgages from 3% to 6% interest rates:</p>
<ul>
<li>Previous interest: £15,000 annually</li>
<li>New interest: £30,000 annually</li>
<li>Section 24 tax credit: £30,000 × 20% = £6,000</li>
<li>Additional rate taxpayer (45%): loses £30,000 × 25% = £7,500 annually in tax relief</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Cash Flow Calculation Example:</strong> Higher rate taxpayer with £25,000 annual rental income and £20,000 mortgage interest after remortgaging:</p>
<ul>
<li>Taxable rental income: £25,000 (full amount)</li>
<li>Income tax: £25,000 × 40% = £10,000</li>
<li>Section 24 tax credit: £20,000 × 20% = £4,000</li>
<li>Net income tax: £10,000 - £4,000 = £6,000</li>
<li>After-tax income: £25,000 - £6,000 - £20,000 = -£1,000 (cash flow negative)</li>
</ul>

<h2>Remortgaging from Interest-Only to Repayment</h2>

<p>Section 24 only applies to the interest portion of mortgage payments. If you remortgage from interest-only to repayment, only the interest element receives the basic rate tax credit. The capital repayment portion receives no tax relief.</p>

<p>This can make interest-only mortgages more attractive from a tax perspective, though you must consider the overall financial implications including eventual capital repayment requirements.</p>

<h2>Cash-Out Refinancing and Use of Funds</h2>

<p>When you remortgage to release equity (cash-out refinancing), the tax treatment depends on how you use the additional funds. HMRC requires you to apportion interest between qualifying and non-qualifying purposes, which can create complex calculations and record-keeping requirements.</p>

<h3>Qualifying Use (Property Business)</h3>
<p>Interest on funds used for property business purposes typically qualifies for Section 24 tax credit relief:</p>
<ul>
<li>Purchasing additional rental properties</li>
<li>Improving existing rental properties</li>
<li>Covering property business expenses</li>
</ul>

<h3>Non-Qualifying Use</h3>
<p>Interest on funds used for non-property purposes may not qualify for any tax relief:</p>
<ul>
<li>Personal expenses</li>
<li>Non-property investments</li>
<li>Other business activities</li>
</ul>

<h2>Planning Considerations Before Remortgaging</h2>

<p>Before proceeding with a BTL remortgage, consider these tax planning aspects:</p>

<h3>Incorporation Assessment</h3>
<p>Section 24 doesn't apply to companies. If remortgaging makes your portfolio cash flow negative, <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/buy-to-let-limited-company-complete-guide-uk">incorporating your property business</a> might provide better tax efficiency, as companies can deduct mortgage interest in full.</p>

<h3>Portfolio Restructuring</h3>
<p>Consider whether selling some properties before remortgaging might be more tax-efficient than maintaining a highly leveraged portfolio under Section 24.</p>

<h3>Interest Rate Sensitivity</h3>
<p>Model different interest rate scenarios to understand how rising rates will impact your after-tax cash flow, particularly given that Section 24 makes you less resilient to rate increases.</p>

<h2>Record Keeping for Remortgaged Properties</h2>

<p>Maintain detailed records of all remortgaging activities:</p>
<ul>
<li>Original mortgage amounts and terms</li>
<li>New mortgage amounts and interest rates</li>
<li>Purpose of any additional borrowing</li>
<li>Legal and arrangement fees (may be deductible)</li>
<li>Interest payments throughout the tax year</li>
</ul>
<p>This documentation is essential for accurate tax calculations and potential HMRC enquiries.</p>

<h2>Future Changes to Consider</h2>

<p>From April 2027, property income will be subject to separate tax rates (22% basic, 42% higher, 47% additional rate). While Section 24 will still apply, the interaction between the restriction and these new rates may affect remortgaging decisions.</p>
<p>The <a href="/blog/section-24-and-tax-relief/section-24-tax-relief-complete-guide">Section 24 restriction</a> is likely to remain in place, making it important to factor these rules into any long-term financing strategies.</p>

<h2>Professional Advice on Remortgaging Decisions</h2>

<p>Given the complexity of Section 24 interactions with different remortgaging scenarios, consider consulting both a mortgage adviser familiar with BTL lending and a <a href="/blog/property-accountant-services/what-does-a-property-accountant-do">specialist property accountant</a> who can model the tax implications of your specific situation.</p>
<p>This dual approach ensures you understand both the financing options available and their tax consequences, helping you make informed decisions about your property portfolio's financial structure.</p>
