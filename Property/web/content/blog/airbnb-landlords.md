<h2>The Annual Accounting Cycle, Month by Month</h2>

<p>The rhythm of running an Airbnb let in 2026 is structured around three calendars: the tax year (6 April to 5 April); the calendar year (the platform-reporting basis under DAC7-equivalent regulations); and the council-tax / business-rates year (1 April to 31 March, England). The discipline of reconciling across the three is what separates the well-run host from the one who lands in an HMRC enquiry.</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>January:</strong> Self Assessment SA100 plus SA105 property pages for the prior tax year (April to April) filed online by 31 January. Airbnb DAC7 annual statement for the prior calendar year arrives at HMRC by 31 January and is available to the host typically a few weeks earlier.</li>
  <li><strong>April:</strong> new tax year starts 6 April. Mortgage interest certificate for the prior calendar year arrives from the lender. Capital expenditure log up to 5 April closed. New tax year's first quarterly MTD ITSA update window opens (for hosts at or above the £50,000 threshold from April 2026, £30,000 from April 2027, £20,000 from April 2028).</li>
  <li><strong>May to September:</strong> occupancy peak in most UK tourism markets. Daily booking-ledger entries; weekly cleaner invoices; monthly Airbnb statement reconciliation; running 12-month-rolling VAT-threshold check.</li>
  <li><strong>September (mid-year):</strong> formal mid-year review of receipts. If combined gross receipts on the 12-month rolling basis approaching or crossing £90,000, register for VAT under VATA 1994 s.3 within 30 days of expecting to cross.</li>
  <li><strong>October:</strong> HMRC notice to file SA may arrive. Self Assessment paper deadline 31 October (in practice most hosts file online by 31 January).</li>
  <li><strong>End of tax year (5 April following):</strong> close prior tax-year ledger; reconcile to platform statements; commit to cash basis or accruals election for the year just ended.</li>
</ul>

<h2>OTA-to-Self-Assessment Reconciliation: The DAC7-Era Discipline</h2>

<p>Since January 2025, Airbnb, Booking.com, Vrbo and similar OTA platforms report each host's annual gross receipts plus property identifiers plus listing data directly to HMRC under SI 2023/817 (The Platform Operators (Due Diligence and Reporting Requirements) Regulations 2023). HMRC ingests the data and matches it against Self Assessment returns by taxpayer reference.</p>

<p>Two structural issues complicate the reconciliation:</p>

<ol>
  <li><strong>Calendar versus tax year.</strong> The Airbnb statement covers calendar year (1 January to 31 December). The SA105 covers tax year (6 April to 5 April). A worked reconciliation table splits the platform figures by tax-year quarter and re-aggregates. Where the host operates on accruals basis, receipts-versus-receivable timing adds a further layer.</li>
  <li><strong>Gross versus net presentation.</strong> Airbnb's annual statement reports 'gross receipts' which may include guest-side service fees, may include cleaning fees collected on the host's behalf, and may or may not be net of host-side commission depending on the fee structure (split-fee, simplified-fee, host-only-fee). The host's SA105 box 5 must report gross-of-OTA-commission receipts, with the OTA commission deducted in the relevant operating-expense deduction box. Cleaning fees received from guests are gross income; cleaner cost is deductible. The flow nets out neutral on profit but inflates gross-receipts measurement.</li>
</ol>

<p>The single most common DAC7-era enquiry trigger is a host's SA105 box 5 sitting materially below the Airbnb DAC7-reported figure for the matching period. A box 5 below the reported figure must be explainable by either (a) the calendar-versus-tax-year boundary, (b) refunds and cancellations that reverse pre-paid bookings, or (c) the gross-versus-net presentation difference. A box 5 below the DAC7 figure that is not explainable by any of those three triggers an enquiry letter as a matter of routine HMRC data-matching.</p>

<h2>Cash Basis or Accruals?</h2>

<p>ITTOIA 2005 s.271A puts individual landlords with combined property and trading turnover under £150,000 on the cash basis by default. Most single-property Airbnb hosts stay on cash basis: receipts recognised on the payment-into-host-account date; expenses recognised on payment date; no debtor or creditor accounting. The simplicity is the point.</p>

<p>Larger hosts (multi-property operations crossing the £150,000 turnover line) move to accruals. The mechanical reason is usually that the cash-basis finance-cost deductibility constraints penalise mortgage-funded operations relative to standard accruals treatment. Limited-company hosts use accruals by default. Election between bases is made annually on the SA return; switching mid-period requires HMRC notification and is usually best timed to a tax-year boundary.</p>

<h2>Capital Allowances Post-FHL Abolition</h2>

<p>This is the single most-asked operational question for hosts who had been claiming under the FHL regime up to 5 April 2025. The position now:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>FHL pool grandfathered.</strong> The capital-allowances pool existing at 6 April 2025 (individuals) or 1 April 2025 (companies) is preserved under FA 2025 Sch 5 transitional. Writing-down allowances continue on the existing balance. No new additions in FHL form.</li>
  <li><strong>New capital expenditure on the trading side.</strong> Where the host's operation crosses the §28 trading-versus-investment line (typically requires ten or more properties, in-house staff, daily turnover, hotel-style services), new capital expenditure on plant and machinery falls into the ordinary plant-and-machinery pool with Annual Investment Allowance at £1 million per CAA 2001 s.51A. The £1 million cap was substituted by Finance (No. 2) Act 2023 effective 11 July 2023.</li>
  <li><strong>New capital expenditure on the investment side.</strong> Where the host is on the investment side of §28 (most one- or two-property hosts), no new capital-allowances pool applies. Furniture, white goods and soft-furnishings replacements fall under ITTOIA 2005 s.311A replacement-of-domestic-items relief for like-for-like replacements. Improvements (structural works, kitchen replacements, extensions) go to CGT base cost on eventual disposal rather than against rental income.</li>
</ul>

<h2>The Section 24 Reducer, Worked Through</h2>

<p>Mortgage interest on the Airbnb property is no longer a profit deduction. Since 6 April 2020, mortgage interest is converted to a 20 percent basic-rate tax reducer. The mechanic bites the same way on Airbnb hosts as it does on long-let landlords, with one caveat: a host on the trading side of §28 escapes the s.24 restriction entirely because trading-income rules permit full mortgage-interest deduction.</p>

<p>Worked example. Mrs Patel: higher-rate-band salary £62,000; one Airbnb property in Cornwall; gross receipts £24,000; non-interest allowable expenses £4,800; mortgage interest £8,400.</p>

<ul>
  <li>Pre-reducer rental profit: £24,000 minus £4,800 equals £19,200. Mortgage interest is NOT netted off this line.</li>
  <li>Marginal-rate tax on £19,200 at higher rate of 40 percent: £7,680.</li>
  <li>Section 24 reducer: 20 percent times £8,400 mortgage interest equals £1,680 credit against tax due.</li>
  <li>Net rental tax: £7,680 minus £1,680 equals £6,000.</li>
  <li>Actual cash margin: £24,000 minus £8,400 interest minus £4,800 expenses equals £10,800. (Council-tax premium not modelled here; would reduce the cash margin further.)</li>
  <li>Effective tax rate on cash margin: £6,000 on £10,800 equals approximately 55.5 percent. The reducer is materially binding.</li>
</ul>

<h2>VAT Registration Trigger and the TOMS Choice</h2>

<p>The VAT registration threshold is £90,000 from 1 April 2024 under FA 2024 s.27 (measured on a 12-month rolling window). A two-property Airbnb host averaging £4,000 per month gross receipts crosses £90,000 inside the year. Registration takes effect from the start of the month after the threshold-crossing month, with backdating in some circumstances.</p>

<p>On registration, the host chooses between two regimes:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Standard 20 percent VAT.</strong> VAT charged on the rental margin to guests directly. VATA 1994 Schedule 9 Group 1 Item 1(e) carves holiday accommodation out of the default land-exemption treatment and makes it standard-rated. Output tax is 20 percent of the rental fee; input tax is recoverable on operating costs.</li>
  <li><strong>Tour Operators' Margin Scheme.</strong> VATA 1994 s.53 permits the host to apply VAT only to the margin between bought-in component cost and sale price, where the host buys-in components (cleaning, utilities, breakfast) and resells the package. TOMS can be materially advantageous depending on the supply mix. The deep-dive at our <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/toms-vat-serviced-accommodation">TOMS VAT for serviced accommodation</a> page walks the application criteria and the calculation.</li>
</ul>

<p>The choice between the two schemes is normally made on the first VAT return after registration and is operationally durable. Switching schemes mid-year is awkward; the right discipline is to model both before registering.</p>

<h2>Council Tax versus Business Rates: The 140/70-Day Test</h2>

<p>England operates a 140/70-day FHL self-catering test for the council-tax-versus-business-rates routing. Property available for letting at least 140 days a year and actually let at least 70 days in the relevant period qualifies for business rates (eligible for small-business-rates relief, which typically yields a zero rates bill for rateable values under £12,000). Property failing the test stays under council tax and is exposed to the LURA 2023 s.80 second-home premium of up to 100 percent.</p>

<p>The 140/70 test was tightened from an earlier 14-day standard in 2022 specifically to remove low-utilisation FHL claimers from business-rates relief. The change shifted many marginal Airbnb units back into the council-tax regime, where they then became exposed to the LURA 2023 s.80 premium when local authorities adopted it from 1 April 2024 (the earliest possible date under the s.80 one-year-prior-notice mechanic; most tourism-area authorities first imposed the premium from 1 April 2025).</p>

<p>The LURA 2023 s.80 premium is a charge on the second-home owner rather than an expense of the property business in the strict sense. It is not directly deductible against rental income on the investment side of §28. It IS deductible against trading income on the trading side of §28. This is one of the single largest practical reasons for hosts at the boundary to seriously model the §28 line.</p>

<h2>OTA Fees and Cleaning Fees: The Accounting Flow</h2>

<p>Airbnb operates two principal fee structures in 2026: a split-fee (host pays approximately 3 percent commission, guest pays a service fee) and a simplified-fee (host pays approximately 14 to 16 percent, guest pays no service fee). Each platform presents the gross figure differently; the host must reconcile each platform to its own ledger.</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Host-side OTA commission:</strong> deductible operating expense under ITTOIA 2005 s.270 trade-equivalent deduction basis. Report in the relevant SA105 deduction box (not netted at top-line).</li>
  <li><strong>Guest-side service fee:</strong> not income to the host; not visible on the host's ledger; not a host-side deduction.</li>
  <li><strong>Cleaning fees charged to guests as a separate line item:</strong> gross taxable income to the host. The cleaner cost (paid by the host to the cleaner) is a deductible expense. The two-way flow nets out neutral on profit, but inflates gross receipts for VAT-threshold and cash-basis-line measurement.</li>
  <li><strong>Inclusive cleaning bundled into the nightly rate:</strong> the cleaning component is part of the rental fee for VAT and accounting purposes. The cleaner cost remains a deductible expense.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Records, Evidence and the Six-Year Floor (TMA 1970 s.12B)</h2>

<p>Six-year record retention applies to property-business records under TMA 1970 s.12B (the trader-and-property-business analogue). The required documentation set for an Airbnb operation:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Per-booking ledger entries with check-in and check-out dates plus gross-of-OTA receipt amounts.</li>
  <li>Monthly OTA statements (Airbnb, Booking.com, Vrbo, others).</li>
  <li>Cleaner and management invoices (dated, named, with VAT registration number where applicable).</li>
  <li>Utility bills and council-tax statements, including any documentation of the LURA 2023 s.80 premium application.</li>
  <li>Building and contents insurance schedules.</li>
  <li>Capital-expenditure invoices with date, supplier and asset description.</li>
  <li>Mortgage interest certificate annually from the lender.</li>
  <li>Land Registry or leasehold title documents for CGT base-cost evidence on eventual disposal.</li>
  <li>Where claiming partial business-rates relief, an availability-and-actual-let calendar evidencing the 140 days available and 70 days actually let.</li>
</ul>

<p>Insufficient records exposes the host to Schedule 24 FA 2007 inaccuracy penalty (up to 30 percent of potential lost revenue for non-deliberate, 70 percent for deliberate-but-not-concealed, 100 percent for deliberate-and-concealed). The single most common record-keeping failure on Airbnb operations is the absence of a monthly OTA-statement-to-ledger reconciliation file note. The note does not need to be elaborate; it does need to exist.</p>

<h2>Exit Options: The Four-Route Decision Tree</h2>

<p>For a host considering exit, the four routes:</p>

<ol>
  <li><strong>Continue as investment and transfer to family later.</strong> CGT on transfer under TCGA 1992 s.17 at connected-party market value. Holdover relief under s.165 not available because investment-side property does not qualify for business-asset holdover. IHT planning by gift-with-holdover unavailable. Business Property Relief on death not available for serviced accommodation following the Pawson trajectory; see our <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/serviced-accommodation-bpr-eligibility-pawson-test">serviced accommodation BPR eligibility (Pawson test)</a> coverage.</li>
  <li><strong>Convert to BTL.</strong> Lower receipts, lower management overhead, standard property-income regime aligned to other landlords. Most economic for one- or two-property hosts where the location supports both modes. The decision matrix sits at our <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/serviced-accommodation-vs-buy-to-let-tax-comparison-2026">serviced accommodation versus buy-to-let tax comparison (2026)</a> page.</li>
  <li><strong>Sell.</strong> CGT at 24 percent residential rate on the gain (18 percent within basic-rate band). 60-day in-year reporting under TCGA 1992 Sch 2 paras 6 to 12 (extended from 30 days by FA 2022 s.23). PPR not available unless the property was ever a main residence.</li>
  <li><strong>Scale into the trading side of §28.</strong> Add properties, services and staff. Cross into trading under CTA 2010 Part 8ZB and ITA 2007 Part 9A, supported by the badges-of-trade case-law (Marson v Morton, Salt v Chamberlain, West v Phillips). Recovers AIA on plant and machinery, opens trade-loss relief routes, restores pension-relevant earnings treatment, and treats the LURA s.80 premium as a deductible business cost. Most viable for ten-plus property professional operators with in-house staff.</li>
</ol>

<p>The exit decision is rarely urgent at the property level but is materially shaped by the host's wider tax position (other income, marginal rate, IHT exposure, succession plans). A properly modelled exit decision typically includes a sensitivity on the next 5 to 10 years of expected rental margin against the post-FHL tax architecture rather than only on the value at point of sale.
