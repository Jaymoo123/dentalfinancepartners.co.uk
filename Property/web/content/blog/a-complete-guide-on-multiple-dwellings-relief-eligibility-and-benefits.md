  - question: "Is Multiple Dwellings Relief still available?"
    answer: "Not for new transactions. Finance (No. 2) Act 2024 section 7 abolished MDR for SDLT land transactions with an effective date on or after 1 June 2024. The relief survives only for two transitional cohorts: contracts entered into and substantially performed before 1 June 2024 (protected by section 7(4)(a)), and contracts entered into on or before 6 March 2024 (Spring Budget 2024 day) that have not been defeated by the section 7(5) anti-forestalling rule.   - question: "When was MDR available historically?"
    answer: "MDR was introduced by Finance Act 2011 Schedule 22, which inserted section 58D and Schedule 6B into FA 2003. The relief operated for SDLT land transactions with effective dates from 19 July 2011 (FA 2011 Schedule 22 commencement) to 31 May 2024 inclusive. The operative pre-abolition text is the version of Schedule 6B as in force on 31 May 2024, captured at legislation.gov.uk under the 31-May-2024 timestamp."
  - question: "What were the Schedule 6B paragraph 2 eligibility conditions?"
    answer: "Schedule 6B paragraph 2 set out the gateway conditions for the relief. You had to acquire two or more dwellings in a single transaction, or one dwelling and additional dwellings under linked transactions that aggregated under FA 2003 section 108. You also had to claim the relief on the SDLT return; without a claim the standard residential SDLT rates applied to the full consideration. The relief was unavailable where the transaction qualified for certain other reliefs (in particular the FA 2003 section 71 charities relief on the same property) and was subject to the anti-avoidance provisions in section 75A. You made the claim in the SDLT return for the transaction (or by amendment within the 12-month statutory amendment window under FA 2003 Schedule 10 paragraph 6)."
  - question: "How was a 'dwelling' defined for MDR purposes under Schedule 6B paragraph 3?"
    answer: "Schedule 6B paragraph 3 read: 'A building or part of a building counts as a dwelling if it is used or suitable for use as a single dwelling, or is in the process of being constructed or adapted for such use.' Land that subsisted for the benefit of the building (gardens, grounds, ancillary structures) was treated as part of the dwelling. The phrase 'suitable for use as a single dwelling' was the most-litigated boundary. The leading case is Fiander and Brower v HMRC [2021] UKFTT 190 (TC08020), where the FTT set out a multi-factor test focused on physical configuration, the degree of physical and functional separation, the existence of separate access, the presence of facilities supporting independent occupation, and the practical capacity for the secondary accommodation to function as a standalone dwelling. The Upper Tribunal sequel ([2023] UKUT 22 (TCC)) refined the test further and constrained adviser arguments that mere physical separation without functional independence would suffice."
  - question: "What was the annexe-as-dwelling test in practice?"
    answer: "The annexe-as-dwelling question was the most contested area of pre-abolition MDR litigation. The factors the tribunals weighted most heavily were: whether the annexe had its own kitchen and bathroom facilities (not merely a kettle or microwave); whether it had separate access from the main dwelling, ideally a separate street-facing entrance; whether it had its own utility metering or at least separable supply; whether its physical configuration would support a long-term separate occupier without modification; and whether the local-authority planning treatment recognised it as a separate residential unit. Annexes that were physically attached to the main dwelling but met the multi-factor test could qualify; annexes that lacked one or more of the core elements typically did not. The Fiander reasoning was that the test was forward-looking ('suitable for use', not 'used') but not so broad as to admit any space that could conceivably be adapted; some inherent suitability had to be present at the effective date."
  - question: "How was MDR computed under Schedule 6B paragraphs 4 and 5?"
    answer: "The computation worked in three steps. Step 1: identify the total dwellings consideration (the consideration attributable to the dwellings in the transaction or linked transactions). Step 2: divide that figure by the number of dwellings to give the average per-dwelling consideration. Step 3: compute SDLT on that average using the standard residential rates (including the 5% additional-dwellings surcharge where applicable under Schedule 4ZA), and multiply by the number of dwellings to give the dwellings-element tax charge. The 1% minimum-rate floor in paragraph 5 then applied: if the Step 3 computation produced an effective rate below 1% of the total dwellings consideration, the relief was capped at 1% of that consideration. Where the transaction included non-dwelling consideration (mixed transactions), the non-dwelling element was charged separately at the non-residential rates."
  - question: "Can you give a worked example showing the historical MDR saving?"
    answer: "Take a £2 million acquisition of four buy-to-let dwellings (£500,000 average per dwelling) where you already owned a residence, so the 5% additional-dwellings surcharge applied. Without MDR (standard residential computation on £2 million plus the 5% surcharge across the whole consideration): SDLT in the region of £170,000 to £180,000. With MDR (Step 2 average of £500,000 per dwelling, Step 3 residential rates plus 5% surcharge on £500,000 then multiplied by four): approximately £30,000 per notional dwelling, totalling around £120,000. The saving was therefore in the £50,000 to £60,000 range on a four-dwelling £2 million transaction. The 1% minimum floor did not bite here because the per-dwelling computation exceeded 1%. For higher-value transactions with smaller numbers of dwellings the saving was proportionately smaller; for lower-value transactions with more dwellings it could be material."
  - question: "What did the Schedule 6B paragraph 5A later-event withdrawal rule do?"
    answer: "Paragraph 5A provided a withdrawal mechanism where, within three years of the MDR claim, the number of dwellings fell below the relief-eligibility threshold (two dwellings, or one plus linked-transaction dwellings). The mechanism caught post-completion amalgamations and conversions: claim MDR on a transaction acquiring two adjoining dwellings, then knock through within three years to create a single larger dwelling, and you lost the relief. The withdrawn relief was recovered as additional SDLT plus interest. Paragraph 5A was anti-forestalling at the post-completion stage, mirroring the anti-avoidance architecture in the original FA 2011 Schedule 22 commencement."
  - question: "How did linked transactions affect MDR eligibility?"
    answer: "FA 2003 section 108 sets out the linked-transactions test: transactions are linked where they form part of a single scheme, arrangement, or series of transactions between the same vendor and purchaser (or persons connected with them). Linked transactions were aggregated for MDR purposes. If you acquired three dwellings from one vendor in three separate transactions on different days, you could (subject to the section 108 substance test) aggregate them into a single MDR computation. Argue the other way, that two transactions were not linked in order to escape an unfavourable computation, and you faced the same substance test. HMRC's standard enquiry posture probed both directions: claimed-as-linked transactions that lacked substance, and claimed-as-unlinked transactions that on the facts were a single scheme."
  - question: "What were HMRC's standard enquiry attack vectors on pre-abolition MDR claims?"
    answer: "Four recurring attack vectors. First, dwelling-count inflation: HMRC challenges the claim that a secondary structure (annexe, outbuilding, holiday-let cottage) is a 'separate dwelling' under the paragraph 3 test, applying the Fiander multi-factor analysis to argue the structure does not meet the threshold. Second, partial-occupancy and shared-services arguments: HMRC tests whether the secondary structure has genuine functional separation from the main dwelling, with shared kitchens, shared utilities, or shared access defeating the claim. Third, mixed-use re-characterisation: where you originally claimed MDR on a portfolio including a property with potential mixed-use character (a flat above a shop, a residential property with paddock land), HMRC may probe whether the property was actually residential at the effective date and whether the MDR or mixed-use route was correctly chosen. Fourth, linked-transactions substance: HMRC tests both directions of the section 108 test, depending on which outcome favours the additional SDLT charge. Enquiry letters typically open with a request for the architectural and occupational evidence and escalate from there."
  - question: "What is the four-year enquiry window for pre-abolition MDR claims?"
    answer: "FA 2003 Schedule 10 paragraph 12 sets HMRC's enquiry timing. The standard enquiry window closes nine months after the filing date of the SDLT return. An extended window of four years from the filing date applies where the loss of SDLT was brought about carelessly by you or someone acting on your behalf; a six-year window applies for deliberate behaviour, and a 20-year window applies for deliberate behaviour with concealment. For MDR claims filed in 2022 to 2024 that HMRC views as outside the policy intent (annexe-on-single-residence claims, marginal-subdivision claims), the four-year careless-behaviour window may still be open in 2026 and beyond. The original Schedule 6B eligibility tests apply at enquiry; the subsequent abolition does not validate or invalidate any pre-abolition claim."
  - question: "I have a pre-6-March-2024 contract that completed in October 2025. Can I still claim MDR?"
    answer: "Probably yes, subject to two layers of checks. First check: was the contract entered into on or before 6 March 2024 (Spring Budget 2024 day)? If yes, the section 7(4)(b) transitional protection is potentially available. Second check: was the contract varied, assigned, the subject of an option or pre-emption exercise, or fed through a sub-sale or further-transaction arrangement created after 6 March 2024? If any of these section 7(5) anti-forestalling triggers occurred, the protection is defeated and MDR is unavailable on the October 2025 completion. If neither anti-forestalling trigger fired, the contract retains MDR and the October 2025 completion is computed under the full Schedule 6B architecture as in force on 31 May 2024. Documentation matters: the contract execution date evidence, the timeline of any subsequent variations, and the chain of any rights granted under the contract."
  - question: "What is the position for a contract dated 15 April 2024 that completed on 5 June 2024?"
    answer: "The transaction is outside the section 7(4)(b) protection because the contract was entered into after 6 March 2024. It can only retain MDR under section 7(4)(a) if substantial performance under FA 2003 section 44 also occurred before 1 June 2024. Substantial performance is reached when the buyer (or a connected person) takes possession of substantially the whole of the property, when a substantial amount of the consideration (in practice usually 90% or more) is paid, or when the first payment of rent under a lease is made. A 15 April 2024 exchange with 5 June 2024 completion and no early possession, no early substantial payment, and no licence to occupy ahead of completion falls outside both carve-outs. MDR is unavailable; the transaction is computed on the post-abolition basis (residential rates plus surcharge on the full consideration). If you took possession on 28 May 2024 to begin renovations, the substantial-performance trigger would have fired before 1 June 2024 and section 7(4)(a) would preserve MDR; the documentary timeline is decisive."

<p><strong>Important historical badge.</strong> Multiple Dwellings Relief was abolished by <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2024/12/section/7">Finance (No. 2) Act 2024 section 7</a> for SDLT land transactions with an effective date on or after 1 June 2024. This guide is the pre-abolition reference for two audiences: (a) anyone with a transitional-cohort claim still live (substantially-performed pre-1-June-2024 contracts, or pre-6-March-2024 contracts that have not been defeated by the section 7(5) anti-forestalling rule); and (b) anyone defending an HMRC enquiry on a pre-abolition MDR claim within the four-year careless-behaviour enquiry window under FA 2003 Schedule 10 paragraph 12. For post-abolition planning, see our <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/multiple-dwellings-relief-abolition-fa-2024-transitional-rules-landlords">dedicated F(No.2)A 2024 s.7 architecture page</a>.</p>
</aside>

<p>If you still have a live MDR claim, or HMRC has opened an enquiry into one, the figures are not small: on a four-dwelling £2 million purchase the relief was worth roughly <strong>£50,000 to £60,000</strong>, and that is exactly the amount HMRC is trying to claw back when it challenges a dwelling count. Multiple Dwellings Relief let you, as a buyer acquiring two or more dwellings in a single transaction (or in linked transactions), compute the SDLT charge on the average per-dwelling consideration rather than the total transaction consideration. Introduced by Finance Act 2011 Schedule 22, it operated for transactions with effective dates from 19 July 2011 to 31 May 2024 inclusive. The full architecture sat in FA 2003 Schedule 6B (inserted by FA 2011), and remained in force in that form (with amendments through the Stamp Duty Land Tax Act 2015 and Finance Act 2016) until abolition commenced on 1 June 2024.</p>

<p>Whether you are testing a transitional-cohort claim or defending one at enquiry, the same source text governs: Schedule 6B as it stood on 31 May 2024. What follows works through that operative version, then sets out how HMRC attacks pre-abolition claims and how to tell whether your pre-1-June-2024 contract still qualifies.</p>

<h2>Schedule 6B paragraph 2: the gateway claim mechanic</h2>

<p>Paragraph 2 was the gateway. You had to acquire two or more dwellings in a single transaction, or one dwelling and additional dwellings under linked transactions that aggregated under FA 2003 section 108. The relief operated by election, not automatically: you had to claim it in the SDLT return for the transaction. Without a claim, the standard residential SDLT rates applied to the full consideration (with the 5% additional-dwellings surcharge under Schedule 4ZA layered on where applicable). If the original return omitted the claim, you could still claim by amendment within the 12-month statutory amendment window under FA 2003 Schedule 10 paragraph 6, provided you filed inside that window.</p>

<p>Two exclusions sat alongside the gateway. Where the same property was the subject of a claim under FA 2003 section 71 charities relief, MDR was not available. Where the section 75A anti-avoidance rule applied to the transaction or scheme, MDR (like other reliefs) was potentially defeated. That section 75A risk was real for highly-structured acquisitions involving chains of intermediary entities or unconventional payment routes; for an ordinary buy-to-let portfolio acquisition it rarely bit.</p>

<h2>Schedule 6B paragraph 3: the dwelling definition</h2>

<p>Paragraph 3 was the most-litigated provision. The statutory text read: 'A building or part of a building counts as a dwelling if it is used or suitable for use as a single dwelling, or is in the process of being constructed or adapted for such use.' Land that subsisted for the benefit of the building (gardens, grounds, ancillary structures) was treated as part of the dwelling. The deceptively simple wording produced a decade of tribunal litigation over what 'suitable for use as a single dwelling' meant in practice, particularly for secondary structures attached or appurtenant to a main residence.</p>

<p>The leading dwelling-definition authority is Fiander and Brower v HMRC [2021] UKFTT 190 (TC08020), with the subsequent Upper Tribunal decision [2023] UKUT 22 (TCC) refining the test. The First-tier Tribunal in Fiander set out a multi-factor analysis focused on physical configuration, the degree of physical and functional separation from the main dwelling, the existence of separate access, the presence of facilities supporting independent occupation (a kitchen and bathroom in particular), and the practical capacity for the secondary accommodation to function as a standalone dwelling at the effective date. The Upper Tribunal sequel constrained adviser arguments that mere physical separation without functional independence would suffice; some inherent suitability for separate occupation had to be present, not merely a future capacity that could be created by modification.</p>

<p>In practice the tribunals weighted most heavily: whether the annexe had its own kitchen and bathroom facilities (not merely a kettle or microwave); whether it had separate access from the main dwelling, ideally a separate street-facing entrance; whether it had its own utility metering or at least separable supply; whether its physical configuration would support a long-term separate occupier without further modification; and whether the local-authority planning treatment recognised it as a separate residential unit. Annexes that satisfied four or more of these factors typically qualified; annexes that lacked one or more of the core elements typically did not.</p>

<h2>Schedule 6B paragraphs 4 and 5: the rate computation</h2>

<p>The rate computation worked in three steps. Step 1: identify the total dwellings consideration, being the consideration attributable to the dwellings in the transaction or linked transactions. For a transaction of dwellings only, this was the full consideration; for a mixed transaction including non-dwelling consideration, the dwelling element was identified separately. Step 2: divide the dwellings consideration by the number of dwellings to give the average per-dwelling consideration. Step 3: compute SDLT on that average using the standard residential rates (including the 5% additional-dwellings surcharge under Schedule 4ZA where the surcharge conditions were met), and multiply by the number of dwellings to give the dwellings-element tax charge.</p>

<p>Paragraph 5 imposed a 1% minimum-rate floor: if the Step 3 computation produced an effective rate below 1% of the total dwellings consideration, the relief was capped at 1% of that consideration. The floor protected the Exchequer against very-low-rate outcomes on transactions involving large numbers of low-value dwellings, where the per-dwelling average could fall below the 2% SDLT band entry point.</p>

<p>Where the transaction included non-dwelling consideration (a portfolio including some commercial or mixed-use property, or non-residential land such as agricultural fields ancillary to the residential properties), the non-dwelling element was charged separately at the non-residential rates under FA 2003 section 55 Table B. The MDR computation operated only on the dwellings element.</p>

<h3>Worked example: £2 million / four dwellings</h3>

<p>Take a transitional-cohort claim now under enquiry. The Patel-estate acquired four buy-to-let dwellings in a single transaction for £2 million (£500,000 average per dwelling). The Patel-estate trustees already held a UK residence so the 5% additional-dwellings surcharge applied. The effective date was 12 May 2024, comfortably pre-abolition.</p>

<p>Without MDR (the comparator the enquiry is implicitly threatening to recompute on): SDLT on £2 million at standard residential rates plus the 5% surcharge across the whole consideration produces a charge in the region of £170,000 to £180,000.</p>

<p>With MDR (the Step 2 average of £500,000 per dwelling, Step 3 residential rates plus the 5% surcharge on the £500,000 per-dwelling figure, multiplied by four dwellings): approximately £30,000 per notional dwelling, totalling around £120,000. The MDR saving was approximately £50,000 to £60,000. The 1% minimum-rate floor did not bite because the per-dwelling computation comfortably exceeded 1% of the £2 million dwellings consideration (1% of £2 million is £20,000, well below the £120,000 computed charge).</p>

<p>Where HMRC challenges the dwelling-count under the Fiander framework (alleging that one of the four dwellings is not separately a dwelling under paragraph 3), the recomputation may move to a three-dwelling MDR position (still computed on the £2 million / three average) or to the full non-MDR position depending on whether the challenged dwelling is removed from the count entirely or reclassified as part of an adjoining dwelling. The financial stakes on the dwelling-definition argument are substantial.</p>

<h2>Schedule 6B paragraph 5A: the later-event withdrawal rule</h2>

<p>Paragraph 5A was an anti-forestalling provision at the post-completion stage. Where, within three years of the MDR claim, the number of dwellings fell below the relief-eligibility threshold (two dwellings, or one plus the linked-transaction dwellings that supported the original aggregation), the relief was withdrawn. It caught post-completion amalgamations and conversions: claim MDR on a two-dwelling transaction, then knock through within three years to create a single larger dwelling, and you lost the relief and faced an additional SDLT charge plus interest.</p>

<p>The three-year window started on the effective date of the original transaction. Withdrawal was triggered by the physical change in the property (the amalgamation or conversion), not by any subsequent disposal or change in ownership. Operate the dwellings as separate units for two years then amalgamate in year three and you were caught; hold them as separate units for the full three years and amalgamate after the window expired and you were not. The compliance trap at enquiry is the same: HMRC may test whether any post-completion physical change within the three-year window triggered a paragraph 5A withdrawal that you should have notified at the time.</p>

<h2>FA 2003 section 108: the linked-transactions architecture</h2>

<p>FA 2003 section 108 governed the aggregation of linked transactions for SDLT generally, and was load-bearing for MDR eligibility specifically. Transactions were linked where they formed part of a single scheme, arrangement, or series of transactions between the same vendor and purchaser, or persons connected with them. Linked transactions aggregated for MDR purposes: if you acquired three dwellings from one vendor in three separate transactions on different days, you could (subject to the section 108 substance test) aggregate them into a single MDR computation.</p>

<p>The connected-person test under section 108 ran through CTA 2010 section 1122 (the standard tax-statute definition of 'connected'), with the partnership-specific extensions where partners were on either side of the transaction. HMRC probed linked-transactions claims in both directions: challenging claimed-as-linked transactions that lacked substance (where you had structured the aggregation specifically to access MDR or to push down the per-dwelling average), and challenging claimed-as-unlinked transactions that on the facts were a single scheme (where unlinking would have produced an MDR computation but you had not made the linked claim).</p>

<h2>The enquiry-defence architecture</h2>

<p>HMRC's standard enquiry attack vectors on pre-abolition MDR claims fall into four patterns. Each requires a different defence approach.</p>

<p><strong>Dwelling-count inflation.</strong> HMRC challenges the claim that a secondary structure (annexe, outbuilding, holiday-let cottage in the grounds) is a 'separate dwelling' under paragraph 3, applying the Fiander multi-factor analysis. Your defence is the architectural and occupational evidence: floorplans showing the physical configuration; photographs evidencing the kitchen and bathroom facilities; utility-supply records evidencing separable metering; local-authority planning correspondence treating the structure as a separate residential unit; occupational history showing the structure has been let or occupied independently. The earlier you assemble this evidence pack, the better; HMRC's opening letter typically requests it, and a slow or thin response materially weakens your position.</p>

<p><strong>Partial-occupancy and shared-services arguments.</strong> HMRC tests whether the secondary structure has genuine functional separation from the main dwelling, with shared kitchens, shared utilities, or shared access defeating the claim. Your defence is the same evidence pack as for dwelling-count inflation, with particular emphasis on the functional-independence evidence. Where the original layout was ambiguous (a converted barn with shared access but separate facilities, or an internal annex with a shared entrance hall but separate kitchen), the tribunal-level argument is fact-sensitive and how well you prepared before the enquiry often determines the outcome.</p>

<p><strong>Mixed-use re-characterisation.</strong> Where you originally claimed MDR on a portfolio that included a property with potential mixed-use character (a flat above a shop, a residential property with paddock land or commercial outbuildings), HMRC may probe whether the property was actually residential at the effective date and whether the MDR route or the mixed-use 1% non-residential route was the correct classification. The post-Bewley enforcement of the section 116(3) 'unsuitable for use as a dwelling' test and the post-Hyman narrowing of the mixed-use classification under section 116(1)(b) shape this defence; the adviser who acted on the original transaction may have made a route-choice decision that HMRC now contests.</p>

<p><strong>Linked-transactions substance.</strong> HMRC tests whether the linked-transactions claim has substance under section 108 (or whether unlinked transactions should have been linked). Your defence is the commercial-reality evidence: vendor and purchaser connection or otherwise; the negotiation timeline; the documentary trail establishing whether the transactions were part of a single scheme. Where the linked claim was made simply to access MDR's per-dwelling averaging on transactions that were not commercially linked, the substance defence is weak.</p>

<h2>The transitional-cohort decision tree</h2>

<p>If you have a pre-1-June-2024 contract and want to know whether it still qualifies for MDR, work through two branches.</p>

<p><strong>Branch one: the section 7(4)(a) substantially-performed route.</strong> Was the contract entered into between 7 March and 31 May 2024 inclusive, and did substantial performance under FA 2003 section 44 occur before 1 June 2024? Substantial performance is reached when the buyer (or a connected person) takes possession of substantially the whole of the property, when a substantial amount of the consideration (in practice usually 90% or more) is paid, or when the first payment of rent under a lease is made. If yes, the transaction is inside the section 7(4)(a) protection and MDR remains available subject to the standard Schedule 6B eligibility tests. Your documentary timeline (possession evidence, payment records, licence-to-occupy agreements) is the load-bearing proof.</p>

<p><strong>Branch two: the section 7(4)(b) pre-6-March-2024 contract route.</strong> Was the contract entered into on or before 6 March 2024 (Spring Budget 2024 day)? If yes, the section 7(4)(b) protection is potentially available. The next check is the section 7(5) anti-forestalling rule: was the contract varied after 6 March 2024 in a way that affected the consideration or the property; were the rights under it assigned or transferred after that date; was an option, right of pre-emption, or similar right granted under the contract exercised after that date; or was the transaction effected in pursuance of a sub-sale, novation, or further transaction created after that date? If any anti-forestalling trigger fired, the protection is defeated and MDR is unavailable. If no trigger fired, the contract retains MDR and the post-1-June-2024 completion is computed under the full Schedule 6B architecture as in force on 31 May 2024.</p>

<p>If you fall outside both branches, you are on the post-abolition side: the transaction is computed on residential rates plus the 5% surcharge on the full consideration. Three statutory routes survive: the <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/sdlt-six-dwellings-non-residential-election">FA 2003 s.116(7) six-or-more-dwellings deeming</a>, the FA 2003 Schedule 15 partnership sum-of-lower-proportions, and the FA 2003 section 45 sub-sale relief. How to use them is set out in our <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/multiple-dwellings-relief-abolition-fa-2024-transitional-rules-landlords">post-abolition architecture guide</a>. For a plain-language explanation of the abolition itself, see our guide to the <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/abolishment-of-multiple-dwelling-relief">abolishment of multiple dwelling relief</a>, and for why the change drew criticism, our piece on <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/abolition-of-multiple-dwellings-relief-mdr-raises-concerns">how the MDR abolition raises concerns</a>.</p>

<h2>Procedural mechanics for transitional-cohort SDLT returns</h2>

<p>A transitional-cohort transaction completing in 2025 or 2026 under section 7(4)(a) or section 7(4)(b) protection is still an SDLT transaction subject to the standard return filing and payment timing. You must file the SDLT return and pay the tax within 14 days of the effective date under FA 2003 Schedule 10 paragraph 2 and section 76. The MDR claim sits in the return itself; you tick the relief box and the computation reflects the Schedule 6B mechanic as in force on 31 May 2024. If you are uncertain whether the transitional protection applies, the cautious approach is to file on the non-MDR basis at the higher SDLT charge, pay the higher amount, and then amend within the 12-month FA 2003 Schedule 10 paragraph 6 amendment window once the analysis is settled. The amended return claims MDR and triggers a refund, which avoids interest exposure under FA 2009 section 101 if the protection ultimately fails. Keep your documentary timeline (contract execution date, evidence of any post-Budget activity, substantial-performance trigger date) accessible for at least four years from the filing date in case HMRC opens an enquiry under the careless-behaviour window.</p>

<h2>Cross-border position: Welsh and Scottish MDR equivalents survive</h2>

<p>The abolition is SDLT-specific. The Welsh LTT MDR equivalent survives at LTTA 2017 Schedule 13; for the mechanics, see our guide to <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/welsh-ltt-multiple-dwellings-relief-bulk-purchases-mechanics-survives-vs-sdlt-abolition">how Welsh LTT multiple dwellings relief survives</a>. The Scottish LBTT MDR survives at LBTT(S)A 2013 Schedule 5 and has not been the subject of any Scottish Parliament repeal at the time of writing. If you are buying property in Wales or Scotland after 1 June 2024, compute the bulk-acquisition relief under the relevant devolved framework rather than the historical SDLT architecture set out here.</p>
