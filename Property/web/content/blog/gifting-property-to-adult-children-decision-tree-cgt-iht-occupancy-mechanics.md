faqs:
  - question: "Why is the dry CGT charge such a big deal on a parent-to-adult-child property gift?"
    answer: "Because the gift is a deemed disposal at market value under TCGA 1992 s.17, the parent (who is connected to the adult child under s.286) is treated as having sold the property at full market value on the gift date and pays CGT on the gain, even though no money has changed hands. For a typical buy-to-let with a £180,000 latent gain on a £400,000 property held by a higher-rate parent, the 24% residential rate (from 30 October 2024 onwards) produces a CGT bill around £42,500 after the £3,000 annual exempt amount. The parent has to find that cash from somewhere other than the property itself, because the property has gone to the child. The 60-day reporting and payment window under TCGA 1992 Sch 2 means HMRC wants the money within two months of completion."
  - question: "Why is s.165 holdover relief not available on a buy-to-let gift?"
    answer: "Because s.165 is restricted to business assets, and buy-to-let property letting is investment, not trade, for both income-tax and CGT purposes. The Upper Tribunal in Pawson v HMRC [2013] UKUT 0050 (TCC) confirmed the long-standing position that an ordinary residential letting (even with active management of multiple properties) is investment, with the parallel reasoning at McCall v HMRC [2009] NICA 12 on the s.105 inheritance-tax-side. The same investment-versus-trade boundary closes s.165 holdover for the CGT side. The furnished-holiday-letting carve-out that historically gave FHL access to s.165 was repealed with effect from 6 April 2025 by FA 2025 Schedule 5 Part 5, closing the only remaining route for residential lettings into business-asset holdover."
  - question: "Is s.260 holdover available?"
    answer: "Only if the gift is a chargeable lifetime transfer. A direct gift to an adult child is a potentially exempt transfer (PET), not a CLT, so s.260 is not available. The s.260 route opens up if the gift goes via a discretionary trust (the transfer into a non-settlor-interested discretionary trust is a CLT, holdover available, but the parent then pays 20% entry IHT on the value above the NRB and the trust is in the relevant property regime from then on). The bare-trust route does not generate a CLT either (bare trust is transparent; the gift is treated as a direct PET to the named beneficiary), so s.260 is not available on the bare-trust route. Effectively, for a direct adult-child gift outside the discretionary-trust regime, holdover is closed."
  - question: "What is the GROB trap when the parent retains the rent?"
    answer: "Where the parent gifts the legal title to the property but continues to receive the rental income, FA 1986 s.102 treats the property as a gift with reservation. The IHT consequence: the property remains in the parent's death estate at the death market value, defeating the entire IHT-mitigation purpose of the gift. The income-tax consequence is more subtle: HMRC may treat the rent flowing back to the parent as the child's income (the child is the beneficial owner of the property) and then as a separate transfer back to the parent, with the parent taxable on the rent in either capacity. The seven-year PET clock does not save the gift; the GROB rules treat the property as never having left the estate for IHT purposes."
  - question: "What if the parent retains the rent but pays the adult child a small token for the right to do so?"
    answer: "Does not work. FA 1986 s.102(1)(b) requires that the property be enjoyed to the entire exclusion, or virtually to the entire exclusion, of the donor. A token payment that is not at full open-market rent fails the test. The donor's continuing receipt of the rent is the canonical example of failing the virtual-exclusion test. The only structural escape from the rent-retention trap is for the parent genuinely to stop receiving the rent (the child receives the rent in full from the date of the gift) and to live or work with the financial consequences of that."
  - question: "What if the parent gifts a holiday home or second home and continues to use it occasionally?"
    answer: "Where the property is residential and the parent continues any use of it (occasional weekends, week-long stays in summer), FA 1986 s.102A treats the donor as having retained a significant interest unless an exception applies. The exceptions are narrow: full open-market rent paid by the parent for the periods of use (with documentary record), occasional visits not amounting to occupation (a strict factual test), or use occurring more than seven years after the gift. The s.102B(4) shared-occupation carve-out only applies to undivided-share gifts where both donor and donee actually occupy as their joint home, which does not fit the holiday-home scenario. The practical answer for the holiday-home gift is either (a) parent pays full market rent for all use, or (b) parent does not return at all. Our separate page on the s.102B(4) carve-out walks the family-home mechanic in detail."
  - question: "Can the adult child rent the gifted property back to the parent?"
    answer: "Yes if the rent is at full open-market level, paid in cash, regularly, with documentary record. The arrangement then escapes GROB because the parent is no longer enjoying the property otherwise than for full consideration. The structural consequence: the child receives the rent as income at the child's marginal rate (subject to the s.272 fair-rental test on connected-person transactions) and is the income-tax taxpayer on the rent; the parent's only continuing role is as paying tenant. The rent flow has reversed: rather than the parent receiving rent (the GROB-triggering pattern), the parent is paying it. The seven-year PET clock on the gift then runs unaffected by the rent arrangement."
  - question: "What is the staged-gift route and when does it work?"
    answer: "The staged-gift route uses the parent's annual exempt amount and IHT annual exemption to gift slices of the property each tax year, typically over five to ten years. Mechanics: the parent gifts (say) 10% of the beneficial share each year via declaration of trust. CGT on each annual slice can be sheltered within the £3,000 annual exempt amount on small gains, and the IHT annual exemption of £3,000 covers the IHT-side gift element (with the seven-year PET clock running on the excess). Constraints: the parent has to live through the staging period for the IHT benefit, the property has to be jointly held under successive declarations of trust (which carry their own TRS registration burden if the property is UK land), and the receiving adult child has to be ready to take the corresponding share of rent each year from the date of the slice. The route fits parents in their early sixties with a long enough life-expectancy horizon to complete the schedule."
  - question: "How does the FIC route differ from direct gift?"
    answer: "The FIC route splits the transaction into two steps. Step 1: parent transfers the property into a Family Investment Company (FIC) at market value, paying CGT on the latent gain at that step (a dry charge unless the parent has cash to cover it from other sources). Step 2: parent gifts shares in the FIC to the adult child, as PETs running the seven-year clock. The structural advantage: the share gift to the child is a PET on the share value (which can be reduced by minority discount and lack-of-marketability discount) rather than on the property's gross value. The structural disadvantage: the parent has paid dry CGT on the property-into-FIC step, the FIC will pay corporation tax on the rental income going forward (with no deduction for personal mortgage interest), and the family is locked into a corporate vehicle for the foreseeable future. Our existing page on FIC share gifts walks the share-side IHT clock in detail."
  - question: "What is 'hold to death' and when is it the best route?"
    answer: "Hold to death means the parent retains the property until death and lets the IHT-and-CGT regime operate on the death event. The CGT consequence: TCGA 1992 s.62 applies a market-value uplift on death, resetting the base cost in the hands of the heirs to MV at death. The latent CGT gain is wiped out without ever being taxed. The IHT consequence: the property is in the parent's death estate; IHT at 40% on the excess over NRB (£325,000) plus RNRB (£175,000 where the conditions for residence-direct-descendant inheritance are met, with the £2,000,000 taper threshold). For a £500,000 property in an estate where NRB and RNRB are otherwise fully consumed by other assets, hold-to-death produces around £200,000 of IHT, but no CGT. Direct gift now produces around £42,500 of CGT immediately, plus a seven-year PET clock for IHT that may or may not save the IHT bill depending on survival. The crossover point depends on the latent gain, the parent's age and health, and the overall estate value relative to NRB plus RNRB. For a parent in failing health with a small estate already within NRB plus RNRB, hold-to-death almost always wins. For a parent in good health with a large estate and substantial latent gain, gift-now (and survive seven years) typically wins."
  - question: "Does the s.624 settlement reading apply where the parent continues to use the property?"
    answer: "Possibly. ITTOIA 2005 s.620 defines 'settlement' broadly to include 'any disposition, trust, covenant, agreement, arrangement or transfer of assets'. Where a parent gifts a property to an adult child but the arrangement (whether documented or informal) is that the parent continues to live there rent-free, HMRC has argued in published practice (TSEM4205 onwards) that the broader arrangement is a 'settlement' under s.620 and that the rental value foregone by the child is income arising under the settlement, attributable to the parent-settlor under s.624. The s.624 attribution does not bite on capital growth (which sits with the child) but does affect the income-tax treatment of the rent-free occupation. In practice the GROB rules under FA 1986 s.102A almost always catch the same arrangement on the IHT side, so the s.624 settlement reading is a secondary concern. The cleanest structural defence is either (a) parent pays full market rent, or (b) parent does not occupy."
  - question: "What about Principal Private Residence relief on a gifted family home?"
    answer: "PRR under TCGA 1992 ss.222-226 shelters the gain on the donor's main residence at the date of disposal. If the parent has lived in the property as their main residence throughout ownership, PRR shelters the gain entirely and the CGT calculation falls to nil. The gift of a property that was the parent's main residence throughout is therefore CGT-free, with only the IHT-side seven-year PET clock to think about. The complications arise where (a) the property was the parent's main residence for only part of the period of ownership (PRR is then apportioned, with the final-nine-months relief at the end), (b) the parent moves out before the gift (PRR runs to the move-out date, with the final-nine-months relief stretching the relief to within nine months of disposal), or (c) the parent and child both occupy the property together and the parent has elected another property as their main residence. For the gifted-buy-to-let scenario PRR is rarely available; PRR is the differentiator that makes family-home gifts much cheaper than BTL gifts on the CGT side."
  - question: "How does the receiving adult child's tax position change after the gift?"
    answer: "From the date of the gift the adult child is the beneficial owner of the property for all UK tax purposes. Rental income is theirs, at their marginal rate. CGT on subsequent disposal is theirs, at their rates, with the base cost equal to the parent's deemed-MV at the gift date (since the parent already paid CGT on the uplift to MV at the gift). Any latent gain that accrues post-gift is the child's gain. The child also picks up the property for their own IHT estate from the gift date; their own seven-year clock is irrelevant unless they themselves gift the property on. SDLT on the gift transaction is governed by FA 2003 Sch 4 para 8 (assumed mortgage debt is chargeable consideration; debt-free gifts have no SDLT consequence). If the child is on the property ladder (owns another dwelling), the gift is the receipt of an additional dwelling and may trigger the higher-rate SDLT surcharge if assumed debt is involved."
  - question: "Does gifting a property affect the parent's care-home means-test?"
    answer: "Yes, potentially. The local authority's deprivation-of-assets rules (under the Care Act 2014 in England and equivalent legislation in the devolved nations) allow the council to treat the parent as still owning the gifted asset if the gift was made with the purpose of avoiding care-home charges. There is no fixed look-back period (unlike the IHT seven-year clock; care-home means-testing is open-ended). The factual test is whether the parent should have reasonably foreseen the need for care at the time of the gift. A gift made by a 60-year-old parent in good health is unlikely to be caught; a gift made by an 85-year-old parent already showing signs of cognitive decline almost certainly is. The care-home risk is independent of the tax analysis and frequently the bigger concern for elderly-parent gifting decisions; tax advice on the gift route does not displace the need for separate advice on the local-authority charging side."

<p>The decision facing a parent who is thinking about handing a buy-to-let or a second home to an adult son or daughter is rarely "gift yes or no". The real question is which of five structurally distinct routes to use, what the year-0 tax cost is on each, and which occupancy trap will catch the family if the planning is not clean. Competitor content treats this as a single decision; in practice there are five.</p>

<p>The receiving adult child wakes up after the gift with a new tax footprint: a beneficially-owned property that will produce rental income at the child's marginal rate, a base cost set at the parent's deemed-MV on the gift date, and (if the parent dies within seven years) a partly or wholly transferred IHT exposure depending on the taper-relief schedule. The route the parent picks determines whether that footprint is layered on top of a dry CGT charge to the parent, a series of staged annual gifts, a bare-trust wrapper, a Family Investment Company structure, or whether the gift simply does not happen and the property passes on the parent's death instead. This page walks the five routes side by side, then surfaces the two occupancy traps that catch families regardless of route.</p>

<h2>The five routes at a glance</h2>

<p>Each route is a different combination of CGT exposure, IHT exposure, and occupancy structure. Year-0 cost comparison uses a worked baseline: a parent (higher-rate taxpayer, full unused NRB and RNRB) gifting a £400,000 BTL with £200,000 acquisition cost and £180,000 latent gain to an adult child (no mortgage assumed; PRR not available; child has no other dwelling).</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Route 1, direct gift now.</strong> CGT: dry charge at 24% residential on £177,000 (gain less £3,000 annual exempt amount), approximately £42,500, payable within 60 days. IHT: PET; seven-year clock runs; if parent survives seven years, the gift drops out of the estate entirely. Year-0 cost: £42,500.</li>
<li><strong>Route 2, staged gift over years.</strong> CGT: each annual slice (typically 10% of beneficial share) sheltered within the £3,000 AEA on small gains, with the IHT annual exemption of £3,000 covering the IHT-side gift element on each slice. Over a 10-year schedule the cumulative CGT can fall to near zero on a slow-appreciating asset, although faster appreciation produces residual CGT later in the schedule. Year-0 cost: typically £0-£2,000 in the first slice.</li>
<li><strong>Route 3, bare-trust wrapper.</strong> CGT: same as direct gift (bare trust is transparent under TCGA 1992 s.60). IHT: same as direct gift (transfer into bare trust is a PET running the seven-year clock; property is outside settlement for IHT). The route changes nothing tax-wise; the wrapper exists for legal-protection or anonymity reasons. Year-0 cost: £42,500.</li>
<li><strong>Route 4, FIC route.</strong> Step 1: parent transfers the property into a Family Investment Company at market value, paying CGT of £42,500. Step 2: parent gifts FIC shares to the adult child, with the share-value reduced by minority discount and lack-of-marketability discount, typically 25-40% in the FIC context. The share gift is a PET running the seven-year clock; subsequent rental income is taxed inside the FIC at corporation tax rates with no deduction for personal mortgage interest. Year-0 CGT: £42,500. Net IHT-clock starting value: around £240,000-£300,000 (post-discount).</li>
<li><strong>Route 5, hold to death.</strong> No lifetime CGT (s.62 death-uplift wipes the latent gain). Full IHT at 40% on the excess over NRB and RNRB. For our worked baseline (£400,000 BTL inside an otherwise NRB-RNRB-consumed estate), IHT on death is £160,000. No CGT ever paid on the £180,000 latent gain. Year-0 cost: £0. Death-event cost: £160,000 IHT minus any taper or other reliefs.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Route 1: the direct gift now</h2>

<p>The simplest route. The parent executes a transfer deed (TR1) to the adult child at the Land Registry; the title passes; the gift is complete on registration. The tax stack is straightforward but the dry CGT charge surprises many parents who have not run the numbers.</p>

<p><a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12/section/17">TCGA 1992 s.17</a> deems any disposal "otherwise than by way of a bargain made at arm's length" to be made for consideration equal to the asset's market value, with subsection (1)(a) explicitly catching gifts. <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12/section/286">TCGA 1992 s.286</a> treats parent and adult child as connected persons. The gift is therefore a deemed-MV disposal; the parent's gain is calculated as MV at gift date less original base cost plus enhancement expenditure and incidental costs of disposal; the rate is 18% basic-rate or 24% additional-rate residential (post-30 October 2024); the £3,000 annual exempt amount applies; the 60-day report-and-pay window under TCGA 1992 Schedule 2 paragraphs 6-7 runs from completion. Payment in cash is required; HMRC does not accept the gifted property as payment because the gifted property has gone to the child.</p>

<p>For a £400,000 BTL with £200,000 acquisition cost and £180,000 latent gain, a higher-rate parent pays approximately £42,500 of CGT within 60 days of the gift. The cash has to come from somewhere other than the property. Routes to fund the CGT include: (a) refinancing other property in the parent's portfolio to release equity, (b) liquidating personal investments, (c) using a personal credit facility, or (d) abandoning the gift and switching to one of the other routes.</p>

<p>s.165 holdover is not available. The relief is restricted to business assets (TCGA 1992 s.165(2) and Schedule 7), and a buy-to-let letting business is investment, not trade, for these purposes per Pawson v HMRC [2013] UKUT 0050 (TCC) on the parallel IHT-business-property-relief line and the long-standing CGT-side position. The furnished-holiday-letting carve-out that gave FHL access to s.165 was repealed by FA 2025 Schedule 5 Part 5 with effect from 6 April 2025; no remaining residential letting route into s.165 holdover. <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12/section/165">TCGA 1992 s.165</a> remains alive for genuine business assets (a parent's own trading-company shares, agricultural land in active use), but not for ordinary residential lettings.</p>

<p>s.260 holdover is also not available. The relief opens only on a chargeable lifetime transfer (typically a transfer into a non-settlor-interested discretionary trust); a direct gift to an individual is a potentially exempt transfer, not a CLT. The closure of both holdover routes is what produces the dry charge.</p>

<h2>Route 2: stage the gift over years</h2>

<p>Where the parent has a multi-year horizon and the property is jointly transferable in undivided shares (most residential property in England and Wales), the staged-gift route uses the annual exempt amount and the IHT annual exemption to peel slices off each tax year. Mechanics: in year 1, the parent executes a declaration of trust transferring (say) 10% of the beneficial share to the adult child. The CGT calculation runs on 10% of the latent gain (£18,000 in our baseline), against the parent's £3,000 AEA, with CGT due on £15,000 of gain at 24% = £3,600. The IHT side is a PET on the £40,000 of value transferred (10% of £400,000), with the seven-year clock starting on each slice independently.</p>

<p>Year 2 onwards: same mechanic, with the parent's AEA refreshing each tax year. If the property is appreciating during the staging window, the per-slice gain grows; if the parent is using up AEA on other disposals, the shelter narrows. The staged route requires (a) a written declaration of trust for each slice (with the corresponding documentary discipline; declarations of trust over land must satisfy LPA 1925 s.53(1)(b), see our existing page on <a href="/blog/landlord-tax-essentials/declaration-of-trust-property-beneficial-ownership-mechanics-evidence-form-17">declaration-of-trust mechanics</a> for the writing requirement), (b) the adult child to receive the corresponding share of rent from the slice date forward (otherwise the parent's continued retention of the rent on the gifted slices triggers the GROB rules on each slice), and (c) TRS registration where the cumulative share creates an express trust over UK land.</p>

<p>The route fits parents in their early sixties with a long enough remaining life expectancy to complete the schedule (typically five to ten years). It does not fit parents in failing health (where the IHT seven-year clock on later slices may not run to completion), nor parents who want a clean single-event transfer (the staging window is administratively heavy and produces multiple TR1 filings or declaration deeds across years).</p>

<h2>Route 3: the bare-trust wrapper</h2>

<p>Tax-wise, the bare-trust route is identical to direct gift. The CGT calculation runs at the beneficiary level under TCGA 1992 s.60 (the bare trustee is invisible for CGT). The IHT calculation treats the transfer into the bare trust as a PET. The seven-year clock runs from the same point as direct gift. The dry £42,500 CGT bite is the same on the baseline scenario.</p>

<p>What changes with the bare-trust wrapper is the legal-protection profile, the disclosure profile, and the operational mechanics. The adult child has the beneficial right to call for legal title under the rule in Saunders v Vautier (1841), but until they do so, the trustee (typically the parent or a corporate nominee) retains legal control over day-to-day decisions. For adult children at risk of matrimonial breakdown, the bare-trust wrapper is often used (incorrectly) as an asset-protection mechanism; in fact bare trusts do not protect the beneficiary's property from the beneficiary's creditors (the property is the child's at general law). Where asset protection is the real goal, the formal discretionary trust route is the correct answer, but it carries the 20% entry IHT under the relevant property regime and the s.260 holdover (which is available where the trust is non-settlor-interested). Our separate page on <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/bare-trust-vs-nominee-company-vs-formal-trust-decision-property-investors">bare trust vs nominee vs formal trust</a> walks the structural choice in detail.</p>

<p>The bare-trust route fits parents who want the documentary record of a trust (for family-governance or anonymity reasons) but are happy to absorb the tax cost of a direct gift. The route does not fit families looking for asset protection or for any deviation from the standard direct-gift tax profile.</p>

<h2>Route 4: the FIC route</h2>

<p>The Family Investment Company route restructures the transaction as two events: a property transfer into a corporate vehicle (with the parent paying CGT on the latent gain at that step) and a subsequent gift of shares in the corporate vehicle to the adult child (running a seven-year PET clock on the share value, not the gross property value).</p>

<p>Step 1, property into FIC: the parent transfers the BTL to a newly-formed (or existing) FIC at market value. CGT at 24% residential on the £177,000 gain (after AEA) = £42,500. SDLT on the company side at the higher-rate residential surcharge (5% post-30 October 2024 on the full £400,000) = £20,000. The FIC issues shares to the parent of corresponding value.</p>

<p>Step 2, FIC shares to adult child: the parent gifts the FIC shares to the adult child. The share gift is a PET. Share valuation is at the discounted level (minority discount typically 15-25%, lack-of-marketability discount typically 10-25%, cumulative 25-40%) producing a transferred value of around £240,000-£300,000 rather than the gross £400,000 property value. The seven-year clock runs on the discounted share value.</p>

<p>Income going forward: the FIC pays corporation tax on the rental income at the relevant rate (25% main rate for FY 2025/26 onwards on profits above £250,000, marginal relief between £50,000 and £250,000, 19% small-profits rate up to £50,000). The personal mortgage-interest restriction under ITTOIA 2005 s.272A does not apply to corporate borrowers; FIC mortgage interest is fully deductible as a CT expense. The trade-off: the FIC route saves IHT on the discounted-share-value clock but pays more CT than the parent would have paid as a personal landlord, and the income inside the FIC is locked behind the corporate veil until extracted by dividend or salary (each with their own tax cost).</p>

<p>Our existing page on <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/fic-gifting-shares-children-property-7-year-iht-mechanics">FIC share gifts for property</a> walks the share-side IHT seven-year clock in detail. The FIC route fits families with substantial portfolios where corporate-governance separation between control (founder as director) and beneficial ownership (child as shareholder) adds value beyond the tax arithmetic.</p>

<h2>Route 5: hold to death</h2>

<p>The route with the lowest Year 0 cost (zero) and the most variable death-event cost. <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12/section/62">TCGA 1992 s.62</a> resets the base cost of the property to MV at the parent's death; the latent gain is wiped out without ever being taxed. The IHT side runs at 40% on the excess of the estate (including the property) over the parent's available NRB and RNRB.</p>

<p>For our baseline (£400,000 BTL in an estate where NRB and RNRB are otherwise consumed by other assets): IHT on the property is £160,000. CGT is zero. Total death-event cost: £160,000.</p>

<p>Comparison against Route 1 (direct gift now, £42,500 CGT plus PET seven-year clock): if the parent survives seven years, Route 1 saves £160,000 of IHT relative to hold-to-death, net of the £42,500 dry CGT, so net saving £117,500. If the parent dies within seven years, the PET fails and the property comes back into the estate at the gift-date MV (£400,000) less any taper relief at years 3-7 (20% relief year 3-4, 40% year 4-5, 60% year 5-6, 80% year 6-7, per IHTA 1984 s.7(4) and Sch 1). Worst case (death within three years), Route 1 produces £42,500 of CGT plus £160,000 of IHT = £202,500. Best case under Route 1 (death after seven years), Route 1 produces only the £42,500 of CGT and saves the £160,000 IHT entirely.</p>

<p>The crossover analysis: at what life-expectancy does Route 1 win against Route 5? Roughly speaking, where the latent gain is small relative to the property value and the parent has a reasonable life-expectancy ahead, Route 1 wins. Where the latent gain is large (so the dry CGT bites hard) and the parent is in failing health (so the PET clock may not complete), Route 5 wins. The arithmetic is family-specific and benefits from running both scenarios on the actual numbers.</p>

<h2>The GROB trap: parent retains the rent</h2>

<p>The single biggest mistake families make on the adult-child gift route is the parent continuing to receive the rental income after the title has passed. The structure looks like a gift on the Land Registry but is a non-gift for IHT purposes under <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1986/41/section/102">FA 1986 s.102</a>.</p>

<p>Worked example: a parent gifts a £400,000 BTL to their adult child in 2026. The arrangement is "the child owns the property, the parent collects the rent for now to help with school fees and the mortgage on the parent's main residence". Land Registry shows the child as owner. The parent continues to receive £1,500 a month into the parent's bank account. The parent dies in 2034 (eight years after the gift).</p>

<p>IHT outcome: the property is treated as gift with reservation throughout the 2026-2034 period. The seven-year PET clock did not save the gift because the GROB rules treat the property as remaining in the donor's estate. On death in 2034, the property is in the parent's estate at the death MV (say £500,000 by then). IHT at 40% on the £500,000 in excess of NRB-and-RNRB = £200,000. The "gift" achieved nothing.</p>

<p>Income-tax outcome over the 2026-2034 period: the rental income is the child's income (the child is the beneficial owner of the property post-gift). The parent's collection of the rent is treated as the child gifting the rent to the parent; if cumulative it can be material for the child's IHT-side PET clock running back to the parent. The child has nonetheless paid income tax on the rent throughout (or should have done; the alternative is HMRC alleging tax evasion).</p>

<p>The fix is mechanical: from the gift date forward, the child receives the rent, the rent is paid into the child's bank account, the child reports it on the child's self-assessment, and the parent has no continuing financial relationship with the property. If the parent needs ongoing income from the property's revenue stream, the parent should not have made the gift. The structural alternative is the FIC route (Route 4), where the parent can retain director-level remuneration from the FIC's rental income without triggering GROB on the shares (because the shares are not the gift of "property" in the s.102 sense, per <em>Ingram v IRC</em> [1999] UKHL 47).</p>

<p>Our separate page on <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/grob-s102-family-home-shared-occupation-s102b-uk-mechanics">family-home GROB depth</a> walks the FA 1986 s.102 and s.102A and s.102B mechanics in detail for occupational scenarios; this section is the rent-retention variant that catches BTL gifts specifically.</p>

<h2>The occupancy trap: parent retains use of a second home or holiday property</h2>

<p>Where the gifted property is not a BTL but a holiday home or second home that the parent continues to use, <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1986/41/section/102A">FA 1986 s.102A</a> bites on the IHT side. The section was added in 1999 to plug the gap that the original s.102 rules left for land interests: even where the parent does not formally retain rent or beneficial title, continuing to use the property as a holiday destination counts as a "significant right" that pulls the gift back into the donor's estate.</p>

<p>The exceptions are narrow. Full open-market rent paid by the parent for all periods of use defeats the trap (s.102A(3) and s.102B(3) carve-outs). Occasional visits that do not amount to occupation (a strict factual test; HMRC manual IHTM14333 is the published guidance) defeat the trap. Use occurring more than seven years after the gift defeats the trap (s.102A(5)). The s.102B(4) shared-occupation carve-out applies only to undivided-share gifts where both donor and donee actually occupy as their joint home; a holiday-home gift where parents and adult child each use the house at different times does not fit the carve-out.</p>

<p>Practical answer for the holiday-home gift: the parent stops using it entirely, or pays open-market rent for any period of use, with documentary record. The "informal continuing use" pattern that frequently arises in family contexts does not survive an HMRC enquiry and produces the same fall-back-into-estate consequence as the rent-retention trap.</p>

<h2>The settlements legislation reading: where it applies and where it does not</h2>

<p>ITTOIA 2005 s.620 defines "settlement" broadly enough that some parent-to-adult-child gift arrangements can be characterised as settlements even though no formal trust deed exists. Where a parent gifts a property to an adult child on the understanding (documented or informal) that the parent will continue to live there rent-free, HMRC has argued in published practice (TSEM4205 onwards) that the broader arrangement is a settlement under s.620, with the rental value foregone treated as income arising under the settlement, attributable to the parent-settlor under s.624.</p>

<p>The s.624 attribution does not affect the capital position (the property and its capital growth sit with the child) but does affect the income-tax treatment of the foregone rental value. In practice the FA 1986 s.102A GROB rules catch the same arrangement on the IHT side, so the s.624 reading is a secondary concern. The s.620 settlement reading is more relevant where the adult child sub-lets the property and the parent has continuing call on the rental income; the s.624 attribution can then pick up the actual rental income, not just the foregone notional rent.</p>

<p>The s.624 reading does not apply to a clean gift where the parent has no continuing use, no rent reception, and no continuing financial relationship with the property. Our separate page on <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/settlements-legislation-s624-s629-property-income-attribution-mechanics">settlements legislation</a> walks the s.620 / s.624 mechanism in detail.</p>

<h2>Decision matrix by cohort</h2>

<p>Five typical cohorts and the route that fits each.</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Parent aged 60-65, good health, small BTL portfolio, latent gain modest, adult child stable in their thirties.</strong> Route 1 (direct gift now), or Route 2 (staged) if the parent has cash-flow constraints around the dry CGT. Seven-year survival highly likely; gift drops out of estate; IHT saving compounds over time.</li>
<li><strong>Parent aged 70-75, good health, single high-value BTL, latent gain substantial, adult child needs the income.</strong> Route 4 (FIC) if the family is comfortable with the corporate structure and the founder-director governance. The minority discount on the FIC share gift mitigates the IHT-clock value; corporation tax inside the FIC mitigates the high marginal rate on rental income; the founder can keep director-level remuneration without triggering GROB.</li>
<li><strong>Parent aged 80+, failing health, primary residence plus BTL, estate substantially above NRB-RNRB.</strong> Route 5 (hold to death). The seven-year PET clock is unlikely to complete; the dry CGT cost of a Route 1 gift is paid in cash that cannot be recovered if the PET fails; the s.62 death-uplift wipes the latent gain regardless. Estate planning shifts to the will-trust side (IPDI on first death; discretionary deed-of-variation flexibility within two years of first death) and to managing the death-estate IHT bill.</li>
<li><strong>Parent and adult child both wanting asset-protection wrapper for matrimonial-uncertainty reasons.</strong> Formal discretionary trust route (CLT, 20% entry IHT, s.260 holdover available where trust is non-settlor-interested, beneficiary's creditors cannot reach trust assets). This sits outside the five routes above but is the right answer where genuine asset protection is the goal. Our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/settlor-interested-trust-iht-s49-1a-cgt-s169b-property-attribution-rules">three-statute attribution stack page</a> walks the drafting discipline.</li>
<li><strong>Parent who actually wants to continue living in or rent out the property.</strong> Do not make a "gift" at all. Either rent the property to the adult child commercially (Land Registry stays with parent, child has tenancy at full market rent) or use a will-trust mechanism to pass the property on death with the s.62 base-cost uplift. Any attempt to gift while retaining occupation or rent triggers GROB; the structure achieves nothing tax-wise and creates administrative complexity for no benefit.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Five mistakes to avoid</h2>

<ol>
<li><strong>Gifting while retaining the rent.</strong> The single most common failure pattern. FA 1986 s.102 treats the property as remaining in the donor's estate at death MV; the seven-year clock does not save the gift. The fix is to stop receiving the rent from the gift date; the alternative is the FIC route where rent-equivalent extraction is structured through director remuneration on the founder's shares.</li>
<li><strong>Assuming s.165 holdover saves the dry CGT.</strong> It does not, for BTL. The Pawson investment-line closes the relief for residential letting; the FA 2025 Schedule 5 abolition of FHL closes the only remaining residential route. The dry CGT is real and has to be planned for at gift date.</li>
<li><strong>Ignoring the 60-day reporting window.</strong> TCGA 1992 Schedule 2 paragraphs 6-7 require report and pay within 60 days of completion. Parents who treat the gift as paperwork and discover the dry CGT bill three months later face penalties on top of the headline charge. The 60-day window is hard.</li>
<li><strong>Forgetting the care-home means-test.</strong> Local authority deprivation-of-assets rules under the Care Act 2014 have no fixed look-back period. Gifts made by parents in declining health, especially where care needs are reasonably foreseeable, can be unwound by the local authority for means-testing purposes regardless of the tax position. The tax advice on the gift does not displace the need for separate advice on the care-home side.</li>
<li><strong>Not coordinating the gift with the will.</strong> A lifetime gift plus a will that leaves the same property to the same adult child via a residuary clause produces a contingent double-counting problem. The will must be updated to reflect the lifetime gift, ideally specifying that the lifetime gift was an advance against the child's share (an "hotchpot" provision) or expressly excluding the gifted property from the residuary estate. Without that coordination, sibling disputes after the parent's death are common.</li>
</ol>

<h2>Closing the loop</h2>

<p>The receiving adult child wakes up the day after the gift with a new property in their beneficial estate, a new income stream at their marginal rate, and a new base cost set at the parent's deemed-MV. The route the parent used to get the property there shaped the tax cost on the parent's side, the IHT clock running on the parent's estate, and whether any future enquiry will reopen the gift under GROB. The five routes above each suit a different set of facts; mapping the routes against the family's actual circumstances is the substance of the structuring conversation. The cheapest direct-comparison route, the safest occupancy-trap-free route, and the route that best fits the parent's life-expectancy horizon are frequently three different answers; a serious gift decision picks one consciously rather than defaulting to whichever option the conveyancer suggests first.</p>
