faqs:
  - question: "Why does surplus cash in the SPV at completion matter to the buyer?"
    answer: "Because the buyer is paying for the SPV's shares, not just the property. Every pound of cash on the SPV's balance sheet at completion is a pound the buyer is funding through the share consideration, then has to extract personally post-completion with its own tax cost. From a buyer's perspective, £180,000 of cash inside a £1.4m SPV is functionally £180,000 of share price that delivers £180,000 of post-tax cash only if extracted without tax cost (rare). More typically the buyer extracts the cash through a post-completion dividend at 35.75% (assuming higher-rate), losing £64,350 in dividend tax. The buyer therefore either insists on a pre-sale cash strip by the seller or prices a discount equal to roughly the post-completion tax cost. The seller's choice is which route shifts the tax burden in the most cash-efficient direction."
  - question: "What are the three pre-sale strip routes?"
    answer: "First, pre-sale dividend: declare and pay a dividend equal to the surplus cash before completion. The dividend is taxable on the seller at dividend rates (10.75% / 35.75% / 39.35% from 6 April 2026) but it is straightforward and (with HMRC clearance) clean against the transactions-in-securities (TiS) rules. Second, pre-sale employer pension contribution: the SPV makes a final contribution to the seller's pension before completion. Annual allowance (£60,000 in 2026/27) and carry-forward of up to three prior years bind the size; wholly-and-exclusively (W&E) test under CTA 2009 s.54 applies sharply because the company will not benefit from the contribution post-completion. Third, cash-left-in as separate consideration: the buyer agrees to take the cash inside the company for an additional payment outside the share price. This converts what would have been pre-sale dividend (income) into a separate consideration item (potentially capital or income depending on structuring), but the TiS rules at ITA 2007 s.684 watch this aggressively."
  - question: "What is the transactions-in-securities (TiS) counteraction risk?"
    answer: "ITA 2007 Part 13 Chapter 1 (sections 682 to 713) is the income tax anti-avoidance regime for transactions in securities. ITA 2007 s.684 ('Person liable to counteraction of income tax advantage') is the substantive counteraction power: it applies where a person obtains an income tax advantage from a transaction in securities, with the relevant 'transactions in securities' defined in s.684(2) to include purchases, sales, repayments of share capital, AND 'a distribution in respect of securities in a winding up'. HMRC can serve a counteraction notice that recharacterises capital receipts as income, taxable at dividend rates instead of CGT rates. For a property SPV share sale that includes any pre-sale cash-extraction step, the TiS rules sit over the structure and HMRC has wide power to recharacterise."
  - question: "What is the s.685 close-company-receipt trigger?"
    answer: "ITA 2007 s.685 ('Receipt of consideration in connection with distribution by or assets of close company') is the operative trigger for TiS counteraction in close-company contexts. Condition A applies where a relevant person receives consideration linked to a distribution, transfer, or realisation of close-company assets, or to the application of close-company assets to discharge liabilities, AND the person does not pay income tax on that consideration outside this Chapter. Condition B applies where a relevant person receives consideration connected to transactions in securities involving two or more close companies. For our pre-sale-strip scenario, Condition A is the live test: the seller's receipt of share-sale consideration is connected to a pre-sale distribution of close-company assets (the cash strip); if no income tax is paid on the consideration as such (because the sale is treated as CGT), HMRC can argue the TiS counteraction recharacterises the consideration as income."
  - question: "What is the s.686 fundamental-change-of-ownership exclusion?"
    answer: "ITA 2007 s.686 ('Excluded circumstances: fundamental change of ownership') excludes a transaction from TiS counteraction where there is a genuine and complete change of ownership of the close company. The test is a triple 25% test: as a result of the transaction(s), the original shareholder(s) together with their associates must (a) hold no more than 25% of ordinary share capital, (b) hold shares carrying no more than 25% of distributable-profits entitlement, AND (c) hold shares carrying no more than 25% of voting rights. All three conditions must be satisfied simultaneously. For a clean third-party share sale where the seller exits 100% and has no continuing association with the buyer or the SPV, the s.686 exclusion is comfortably met and TiS does not apply. The exclusion fails where the seller retains shares (even a small holding) or has any continuing association (post-completion employment, deferred consideration tied to performance, earn-out structures)."
  - question: "How does the s.701 HMRC clearance route work?"
    answer: "ITA 2007 s.701 ('Application for clearance of transactions') allows a person to apply to HMRC in advance for confirmation that a planned transaction will not trigger a TiS counteraction notice. The application is in writing, sets out the full facts and the proposed transactions, and requests clearance. HMRC has 30 days from receipt of complete particulars to respond; HMRC can request further information within that window, in which case a fresh 30-day clock starts from receipt of the further information. A favourable response from HMRC means no counteraction notice will be served in respect of the transactions as described. An adverse response means HMRC reserves the right to counteract, but the application's contents have not prejudiced the position; the seller can adjust the structure or proceed without clearance and litigate if HMRC issues a counteraction notice. The clearance route is the standard discipline for any non-vanilla pre-sale-strip transaction; the £20,000 to £40,000 of adviser cost on a moderately complex clearance application is dwarfed by the income-tax cost of getting it wrong."
  - question: "Is a pre-sale dividend always safe against TiS?"
    answer: "Not always, but usually. A simple pre-sale dividend paid out of distributable reserves, taxed in the seller's hands at dividend rates, where the seller exits 100% on the same transaction, typically meets the s.686 fundamental-change-of-ownership exclusion and so escapes TiS counteraction by operation of the exclusion (the counteraction looks for an income tax ADVANTAGE; a fully-taxed dividend that pre-empts the share-sale price is not an advantage). The position sharpens where (a) the seller retains shares after the dividend, (b) the dividend is in specie (a property distributed instead of cash), or (c) the dividend is timed to take advantage of a year-end rate change. For complex cases the s.701 clearance route is the discipline; for clean cases the analysis is straightforward and clearance may not be strictly necessary."
  - question: "What is the cash-left-in negotiated-as-separate-consideration route?"
    answer: "It is a structure where the buyer agrees to pay an additional amount (outside the share price) for the cash inside the SPV at completion, instead of the seller stripping the cash pre-completion. Mechanically, the share-sale agreement records two consideration items: a primary share price for the operational SPV (the property portfolio) and a separate consideration for the cash on hand at completion. The seller receives both, and the question is whether the separate-consideration leg is taxed as CGT (part of the disposal of the shares) or as income (a distribution by another name). HMRC's view is hostile to structures that try to recharacterise income as capital; the TiS s.685 trigger is precisely designed to catch close-company structures that try this. Without s.701 clearance, the route carries material TiS counteraction risk; with clearance, the position is the position HMRC has agreed."
  - question: "How big can a pre-sale pension contribution be?"
    answer: "The annual allowance (AA) is £60,000 in 2026/27 with carry-forward of unused AA from the three prior tax years (potentially £180,000 total if the seller had full unused AA in each of 2023/24, 2024/25, 2025/26). The contribution must be a real employer contribution from the company before completion (not a personal contribution); the W&E test under CTA 2009 s.54 applies sharply because the company will not benefit from the contribution post-completion. HMRC BIM46035 is the active manual: an unconnected employee performing duties of similar value would not receive a £180,000 final-year contribution if the company is about to be sold. The defence requires either (a) a long-established pension contribution pattern at similar scale (annual £60,000 contributions for three years before the sale) or (b) clear evidence that the contribution is part of a genuine remuneration package that the buyer is taking on. See our depth page on the employer pension contributions W&E gateway for the full mechanics."
  - question: "What if the buyer is a UK trading company and SSE applies on its side?"
    answer: "Substantial Shareholding Exemption (TCGA 1992 Sch 7AC) is an exemption available to a corporate shareholder selling shares in a qualifying trading company. SSE does not change the seller's pre-sale-strip analysis at the individual-shareholder level (this page's focus), but it does change the buyer's negotiation posture. A corporate buyer whose own future onward-sale of the SPV would qualify for SSE is more relaxed about acquiring a clean SPV without surplus cash, because the corporate buyer can extract cash from the SPV post-completion via tax-free inter-company dividend under CTA 2009 s.931A. The buyer may therefore prefer the cash-left-in route at a fair pound-for-pound separate consideration, because the buyer faces no extraction friction. For the individual seller, the comparison shifts: the cash-left-in route can be roughly tax-neutral if structured as part of the CGT-treated share consideration with s.701 clearance. See our SSE for property companies page for the buyer-side framework."
  - question: "What if there is no buyer at all and the alternative is an MVL?"
    answer: "Then the comparison is share-sale-with-pre-sale-strip versus MVL-with-capital-distribution. MVL distributes the SPV's net assets to the shareholder at the end of the liquidation; the distribution is treated as a capital receipt under TCGA 1992 s.122 (subject to the s.396B targeted anti-avoidance rule for two years post-distribution where the founder restarts similar activity). MVL gives the shareholder capital treatment without the TiS counteraction risk of a pre-sale strip (because MVL is itself the disposal, not a step before a disposal). The MVL route also has procedural cost (liquidator fees of £8,000 to £20,000 typical for a property SPV) and a longer timeline (4 to 9 months). Where a real buyer exists at a price that materially exceeds the MVL net-asset distribution, the share-sale route wins; where no buyer exists or buyer price barely exceeds NAV, MVL wins. See our MVL depth page for the full mechanics."
  - question: "What documentation do I need before any pre-sale strip?"
    answer: "Four documents anchor the position. Fourth, a written remuneration history for any pre-sale pension contribution, supporting the W&E defence. Without these documents, the position is fragile at HMRC enquiry, which typically comes 12 to 24 months after the sale completes."

<p>From a buyer's perspective, surplus cash inside the SPV at completion is a problem either way. Pay for cash you do not want, or negotiate a discount that shifts the post-completion tax burden onto you. The seller's pre-sale cash strip is the cleanest answer if it survives the transactions-in-securities (TiS) counteraction gate at ITA 2007 Part 13 Chapter 1, but the gate is sharp and the structuring choices are not interchangeable. A clean dividend taxed in the seller's hands at dividend rates typically clears TiS by meeting the fundamental-change-of-ownership exclusion at s.686. A cash-left-in route negotiated as separate consideration looks attractive on the seller's spreadsheet because it might convert income into capital, but it sits squarely inside the s.685 close-company-consideration trigger and HMRC's posture is hostile without advance clearance.</p>

<p>This page walks the three strip routes, the TiS counteraction framework end to end, the s.701 HMRC advance clearance discipline, and two worked share sales with the maths spelled out. Persona: <strong>Sarah</strong>, sole shareholder of Holdings Ltd (a 6-property residential BTL SPV), agreed share sale to corporate buyer Acme Property Partners Ltd for £1.4m of share consideration at completion; SPV expected to hold £180,000 of cash at completion date out of accumulated retained earnings. The page sits within the <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/extracting-cash-from-property-spv-extraction-sequence-pillar-2026-27">multi-year extraction sequence pillar</a> as the pre-sale-event applied lever, alongside the <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/mvl-members-voluntary-liquidation-property-company-cgt-vs-income-treatment">MVL exit page</a> (no-buyer alternative) and the <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/substantial-shareholding-exemption-property-companies">SSE for property companies page</a> (corporate-shareholder buyer-side framework).</p>

<h2>The buyer's negotiation posture: why cash is the central question</h2>

<p>Acme's offer for Holdings Ltd is £1.4m for 100% of the shares at completion. That figure was negotiated against Acme's view of the SPV's enterprise value: the discounted future cash flows of the rent on the 6 properties, less the SPV's debt, less Acme's required risk-adjusted return. The £180,000 of cash on the balance sheet at completion is not enterprise value; it is, from Acme's perspective, money Sarah leaves behind on the way out.</p>

<p>If Acme funds the full £1.4m share price and inherits the £180,000 cash, Acme is effectively paying £180,000 of share consideration for £180,000 of cash inside the company. The two should net out exactly on Day 1 (Acme withdraws £180,000 as a post-completion dividend; nothing changes economically). In practice the post-completion withdrawal carries tax cost: Acme's individual shareholders extracting at higher-rate dividend pay 35.75% on the £180,000, losing £64,350 in dividend tax. Even if Acme is a corporate buyer (cleaner inter-company dividend route under CTA 2009 s.931A), the funding cost of carrying £180,000 of cash on the SPV's balance sheet that Acme will eventually extract is non-zero.</p>

<p>Acme's three negotiation options at this point are:</p>

<ol>
<li><strong>Insist on a pre-sale cash strip:</strong> Sarah extracts the £180,000 before completion; the SPV passes to Acme with zero cash at completion. The £1.4m share price stands.</li>
<li><strong>Apply a discount equal to the post-completion extraction cost:</strong> the £1.4m share price falls to roughly £1.4m less the post-completion tax leakage. For an individual-shareholder Acme, the discount is roughly £64,350 (the dividend-tax cost). For a corporate Acme using SSE on eventual onward sale, the discount is closer to the funding-cost differential, perhaps £5,000 to £15,000.</li>
<li><strong>Pay separately for the cash as a distinct consideration line:</strong> the share-sale agreement records two items, £1.4m for the operational SPV and £180,000 for the cash at completion. Sarah receives £1.58m total. The question is whether the £180,000 second leg is CGT-treated (part of the share disposal) or income-treated (a distribution by another name).</li>
</ol>

<p>Sarah's preference depends on the tax character of each option. Pre-sale dividend at higher-rate costs Sarah 35.75% × £180,000 = £64,350 of dividend tax. CGT-treated share consideration at higher-rate 24% on the full £1.4m costs Sarah roughly £288,000 of CGT (assuming low base cost), so each marginal pound of share consideration costs 24%. Income-treated separate consideration costs roughly 35.75% to 39.35%. The lowest-cost route for Sarah is the share-consideration-treated cash, if it holds against TiS.</p>

<h2>Route 1: pre-sale dividend</h2>

<p>The simplest and most defensible route. Sarah's company declares and pays a dividend of £180,000 to Sarah before completion, taxed in Sarah's hands at dividend rates (10.75% basic / 35.75% higher / 39.35% additional from 6 April 2026 per house position section 21.4). For Sarah at higher rate with no other dividend income that year, the computation is:</p>

<ul>
<li>Dividend allowance £500 at 0%: £0</li>
<li>Basic-rate band remaining (assuming £50,000 of other income already used): £270 at 10.75% = £29</li>
<li>Higher-rate band £49,770 at 35.75% = £17,793</li>
<li>Higher-rate band remaining to £125,140 cap £74,870 at 35.75% = £26,766</li>
<li>Additional-rate band balance £54,590 at 39.35% = £21,481</li>
<li><strong>Total dividend tax: roughly £66,069</strong></li>
</ul>

<p>The £66,069 is the cash cost of the clean dividend strip. The SPV then passes to Acme with zero cash; the £1.4m share consideration is taxed in Sarah's hands at CGT (18% / 24% residential rates do not apply to share disposals; standard 10% / 20% rates apply per TCGA 1992 s.4, or 14% BADR rate from 6 April 2025 rising to 18% from 6 April 2026 if BADR is available, which it typically is not for an investment SPV per Pawson).</p>

<p>TiS analysis: Sarah exits 100% of Holdings Ltd on the share sale to Acme. The s.686 fundamental-change-of-ownership exclusion is comfortably met: Sarah holds 0% of ordinary share capital after the sale, 0% of distributable entitlement, 0% of voting rights. Therefore TiS counteraction does not apply by virtue of the exclusion, and the dividend tax computation above stands as the position. An s.701 clearance application is not strictly necessary on these facts, but is cheap insurance for a transaction of this scale.</p>

<h2>Route 2: pre-sale employer pension contribution</h2>

<p>If Sarah has unused annual allowance from the three prior tax years (2023/24, 2024/25, 2025/26) plus the current year, the company can make an employer pension contribution before completion. Annual allowance is £60,000 in 2026/27; full carry-forward of three prior years of unused AA gives up to £240,000 of contribution headroom. The contribution is deductible for the company under CTA 2009 s.40 (subject to the W&E test in s.54), and not assessable on Sarah personally at the contribution date.</p>

<p>For Sarah with full AA carry-forward, a £180,000 employer pension contribution would absorb the surplus cash before completion. The contribution is fully deductible against the company's corporation tax in the contribution period (saving roughly £45,000 of CT at 25%), and Sarah's pension pot grows by £180,000 without any personal-side tax cost at the contribution moment.</p>

<p>The W&E gate sharpens because the company will not benefit from the contribution post-completion. HMRC BIM46035 frames the test: an unconnected employee performing duties of similar value would not receive a £180,000 final-year contribution if the company is about to be sold. The defence requires either (a) a long-established pattern of contributions at similar scale (annual £60,000 contributions for three years before the sale, contemporaneous board minutes), or (b) clear evidence that the contribution is part of a genuine remuneration package the buyer is taking on. See our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/property-spv-employer-pension-contributions-wholly-and-exclusively-test-mechanics">employer pension contributions W&E gateway page</a> for the full framework.</p>

<p>Practical limit: the route works at full scale only where the seller has a documented multi-year contribution history. Without it, the contribution is capped at a defensible run-rate (perhaps £30,000 to £60,000 in the final year), which leaves residual surplus cash to address through Route 1 or Route 3.</p>

<h2>Route 3: cash-left-in as separate consideration</h2>

<p>The structure: the share-sale agreement records two consideration items. £1.4m for the operational SPV (the property portfolio) and £180,000 as separate consideration for the cash on hand at completion. Sarah receives £1.58m gross. The question is whether the £180,000 second leg is taxed as CGT (part of the share disposal) or income (a distribution by another name).</p>

<p>Without TiS clearance, HMRC's posture is hostile. The s.685 close-company-receipt trigger applies because Sarah is receiving consideration in connection with the realisation of close-company assets (the cash on the SPV's balance sheet). Condition A of s.685: Sarah is a relevant person, the consideration is connected to the realisation of close-company assets, and (if Sarah treats the £180,000 as CGT) Sarah does not pay income tax on it through the normal income-tax channels. HMRC can serve an s.684 counteraction notice recharacterising the £180,000 as income, taxable at dividend rates.</p>

<p>With s.701 clearance, the position is the position HMRC has agreed. Application requires full disclosure of the share-sale agreement, the cash-handling mechanic, the parties, and the commercial rationale for the separate-consideration structure. HMRC has 30 days from receipt of complete particulars to respond (further 30 days if HMRC asks for additional information). A favourable response means no counteraction notice will be served; an adverse response leaves Sarah's adviser to restructure or proceed without clearance and litigate.</p>

<p>For Sarah's £180,000 separate-consideration leg, the optimistic scenario is HMRC agrees CGT treatment because the £180,000 is genuinely part of the share consideration (the buyer is paying for the company plus the cash inside it as an integrated package, not for a pre-sale distribution dressed as separate consideration). The £180,000 at CGT higher rate 24% costs £43,200 of tax (saving £22,869 vs Route 1's £66,069 dividend tax). The pessimistic scenario is HMRC refuses clearance, Sarah restructures to Route 1, and the saving evaporates. The £15,000 to £40,000 of clearance-application adviser cost is the price of certainty before completion.</p>

Application includes full particulars: planned dividend of £180,000 from Holdings Ltd to Sarah, on date specified, funded from distributable reserves of £400,000 (verified by management accounts); planned share sale to Acme on completion date X; consideration £1.4m for 100% of shares.</li>
<li><strong>Month -2:</strong> HMRC clearance received on day 27 of the 30-day window. HMRC confirms s.684 counteraction will not be served on the dividend or the share sale as described.</li>
<li><strong>Month -1:</strong> board resolution declaring the dividend; dividend voucher issued; payment made from SPV bank account to Sarah's personal account.</li>
<li><strong>Month 0 (completion):</strong> share-sale agreement completed; £1.4m wired to Sarah; share register updated to Acme. SPV bank balance £nil.</li>

CGT on share sale: £1.4m less base cost roughly £100 (subscription) less CGT annual exempt amount £3,000 = roughly £1,397,000 gain; at 24% (assuming no BADR) tax £335,280. </ol>

<h2>Worked share sale B: cash-left-in with separate consideration</h2>

<p>Same SPV, same buyer, but Sarah's adviser proposes Route 3:</p>

<ol>
<li><strong>Month -4:</strong> heads of terms signed at £1.58m total consideration (£1.4m for the SPV shares + £180,000 separate consideration for the cash on hand at completion). Agreement explicit on the structure.</li>

<li><strong>Month 0 (completion):</strong> share-sale agreement completed; £1.58m wired to Sarah.</li>

</ol>

The saving is small in percentage terms (1.9% of net cash) but real, and is what justifies the s.701 clearance application cost (£15,000 to £40,000) on a transaction of this scale. Below roughly £100,000 of surplus cash, the clearance cost can exceed the saving and Route A is the better answer.</p>

<h2>Interaction with the SSE route (corporate shareholders)</h2>

<p>Where the seller is a corporate shareholder (a HoldCo above Sarah, for example), TCGA 1992 Sch 7AC Substantial Shareholding Exemption potentially exempts the entire share-sale gain from corporation tax. SSE requires the SPV to be a qualifying trading company; pure-investment BTL SPVs typically fail the trading test per the Pawson investment line (see house position section 22.1). Where the SPV is a development trading company (a different business), SSE may be available and the entire £1.4m gain escapes CT.</p>

<p>For SSE-eligible structures, the buyer-side analysis softens because the corporate buyer can extract post-completion cash via inter-company dividend under CTA 2009 s.931A without leakage. The buyer is therefore neutral on the cash-strip mechanic; the seller's choice is unconstrained by buyer preference. See our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/substantial-shareholding-exemption-property-companies">SSE for property companies page</a> for the full SSE framework.</p>

<h2>Interaction with the MVL alternative (no buyer)</h2>

<p>Where no real buyer exists at a price above the SPV's net asset value, the comparison is share-sale-with-pre-sale-strip versus MVL-with-capital-distribution. MVL distributes the SPV's net assets to the shareholder at the end of the liquidation under TCGA 1992 s.122 as a capital receipt. MVL has its own anti-avoidance gate at ITTOIA 2005 s.396B (the 2-year-post-distribution TAAR for founders restarting similar activity), but it sidesteps the TiS s.684 framework entirely because MVL is itself the disposal, not a pre-sale step.</p>

<p>For Sarah's £1.4m sale to Acme, the comparison is roughly: share sale Route A nets £1,178,651 to Sarah; share sale Route B nets £1,201,520; MVL nets the net asset value (which is enterprise value plus cash but minus liquidator fees and the MVL administration cost), so for the same SPV with £1.58m total assets minus £20,000 liquidator fees minus capital-treatment CGT on £1,560,000 at 24% (£374,400), nets £1,185,600. The share-sale Route B wins by roughly £15,920; share-sale Route A and MVL are within £7,000 of each other. The choice between MVL and share sale typically turns on whether a real buyer exists at a price materially above NAV. See our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/mvl-members-voluntary-liquidation-property-company-cgt-vs-income-treatment">MVL page</a> for the full mechanics.</p>

<h2>Decision-tree quick reference</h2>

<p>The four-question decision tree for any pre-sale-strip planning conversation:</p>

<ol>
<li><strong>Does a real buyer exist at a price materially above NAV?</strong> Yes → share-sale route (continue to question 2). No → MVL route (defer to MVL page).</li>
<li><strong>Is the seller a corporate shareholder with SSE eligibility on the SPV?</strong> Yes → SSE wrapper (defer to SSE page); cash-strip mechanic is buyer-driven. No → individual-shareholder mechanics apply (continue to question 3).</li>
<li><strong>Is the surplus cash material relative to s.701 clearance cost?</strong> Yes (above roughly £100,000 of surplus cash) → consider Route 3 with clearance application (s.701). No → use Route 1 (pre-sale dividend), s.701 clearance optional cheap insurance.</li>
<li><strong>Does the seller have unused pension AA carry-forward and a documented contribution history?</strong> Yes → consider Route 2 (employer pension contribution) for partial strip alongside Route 1 or Route 3. No → skip Route 2.</li>
</ol>

<h2>Failure modes</h2>

<p>The recurring patterns when pre-sale strips are run without adviser input:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Cash-left-in negotiated without s.701 clearance.</strong> Acme pays £180,000 separate consideration; Sarah treats it as CGT; HMRC enquires 18 months post-completion and serves an s.684 counteraction notice recharacterising the £180,000 as income. Tax cost jumps from £43,200 (24% CGT) to roughly £64,350 (35.75% dividend), plus interest and penalties.</li>
<li><strong>Pre-sale dividend declared after share-sale exchange of contracts.</strong> Sarah's lawyer drafts a dividend resolution dated after the share-sale exchange. HMRC argues the dividend was made by the new shareholder (Acme), not by Sarah, and the £180,000 was funded by Acme's purchase price. Position collapses; the £180,000 is reabsorbed into Sarah's CGT computation and Acme has a leakage on a payment it has already made.</li>
<li><strong>Pre-sale pension contribution made in the final month with no historic pattern.</strong> £180,000 employer contribution disallowed for CT under CTA 2009 s.54 W&E test; SPV faces CT bill on £180,000 of additional taxable profit (£45,000 at 25%); contribution treated as benefit-in-kind on Sarah.</li>
<li><strong>Seller retains a small post-completion shareholding "for transition".</strong> 5% of shares retained means s.686 fundamental-change-of-ownership exclusion fails (over 25% test only protects the seller, but ANY retained holding combined with deferred consideration triggers HMRC scrutiny on the s.685 close-company-consideration trigger). Pre-sale dividend that would have been clean becomes TiS-vulnerable.</li>
<li><strong>Deferred consideration tied to post-completion performance.</strong> Earn-out structures keep the seller economically associated with the SPV's performance; HMRC may argue the original sale was not a fundamental change of ownership and the TiS exclusion does not apply.</li>
</ul>
