    answer: "In James and Charlotte Averdieck v HMRC [2022] TC 8623 the First-tier Tribunal dismissed the appeal and confirmed that the residential SDLT rate table applied on the purchase.   - question: "What was the fact pattern?"
    answer: "James and Charlotte Averdieck jointly purchased a substantial residential property and grounds for £3 million in August 2020. An SDLT return was filed in September 2020 on the residential basis and £258,000 of SDLT was paid. In December 2020 the return was amended to claim mixed-use treatment under the non-residential rate table on the basis that the property contained a public right of way (an access lane) that imposed statutory maintenance obligations on the appellants. On the strength of the amended return, HMRC refunded £119,000. HMRC subsequently opened an enquiry into the amended return, the matter went to the FTT, and the Tribunal dismissed the appeal. The £119,000 refund became repayable."
  - question: "What was the public right of way over?"
    answer: "The right of way was an access lane that ran across the appellants' property and also provided access to five other residential homes and a farm beyond. So the lane was both the appellants' own access route to their property and the public's access route to neighbouring properties further along. The fact that the appellants themselves used the lane to access their own property was load-bearing in the Tribunal's reasoning: the right of way was not a discrete piece of non-residential land severed from the appellants' residential use of the grounds, it was integral to the appellants' residential access."
  - question: "What is the statutory test under FA 2003 section 116?"
    answer: "FA 2003 section 116 defines 'residential property' for SDLT purposes. Section 116(1)(a) brings within the definition any building used or suitable for use as a dwelling. Section 116(1)(b) brings within the definition any land that is or forms part of the garden or grounds of such a building (including any building or structure on such land). Section 116(1)(c) brings within the definition any interest in or right over land that subsists for the benefit of a section 116(1)(a) building or section 116(1)(b) garden or grounds. The mixed-use argument in Averdieck-style cases is that a portion of the grounds is not 'garden or grounds' for the purposes of section 116(1)(b) (typically because it is used for some non-residential purpose), and that the transaction therefore includes non-residential land which puts the whole purchase onto the Table B non-residential rate table."
  - question: "Why did the FTT reject the maintenance-obligations argument?"
    answer: "The Tribunal's reasoning was that 'the existence of obligations in respect of land does not exclude that land from being residential property'. A landowner with a public right of way over their land has statutory obligations to keep the path passable, not to obstruct it, and (depending on the type of right of way and the location) to bear part of the cost of repair. These are real burdens on the land, but they do not change the underlying use or character of the land. The land remains the appellants' garden or grounds, and the path is an encumbrance on that land, not a transfer of the underlying use to a non-residential purpose. The Tribunal also noted that the burden of the maintenance obligations on these particular facts was not so substantial as to take the land outside its residential character."
  - question: "How does Averdieck fit into the wider mixed-use case-law line?"
    answer: "Averdieck sits squarely within the post-2021 narrowing trajectory on SDLT mixed-use claims. The leading authority is Hyman v HMRC [2019] UKFTT 469, upheld by the Upper Tribunal at [2021] UKUT 68 and by the Court of Appeal at [2022] EWCA Civ 185, which established the 'in principle suitable' test for the garden-or-grounds analysis and rejected mixed-use claims based on minor non-residential features. The line continued in Mudan v HMRC [2023] UKFTT 317 (unmodernised property remains residential) and in subsequent FTT decisions including MHB Ltd and Brown. Averdieck applies the same narrowing logic to the public-rights-of-way fact pattern: features of the grounds that do not transform the underlying use are encumbrances, not non-residential elements. The mixed-use claim survives only on fact patterns where the non-residential element is substantive, exclusive, and severable from residential use."
  - question: "Has any PROW-mixed-use claim ever succeeded?"
    answer: "PROW-only arguments very rarely succeed. Where a public right of way is the sole feature pointed to (no commercial usage, no severable non-residential portion of the grounds), the FTT has consistently followed the Hyman-line reasoning and dismissed the claim. Mixed-use claims do sometimes succeed where the non-residential element is substantive and severable: paddocks let to a third party under a formal grazing agreement for several years (commercial agricultural use); buildings within the grounds used as a commercial premises (an office or workshop generating third-party rental income); a portion of the property severed from the dwelling and used for a discrete commercial purpose. The Averdieck-style PROW claim sits at the weaker end of the spectrum and tends to lose."
  - question: "How does mixed-use treatment differ from residential treatment on a £3m purchase?"
    answer: "On a £3 million purchase, the residential SDLT rate table (post-1-April-2025 figures, with the 5% additional dwellings surcharge where applicable as a second home) produces SDLT of approximately £258,000 on the standard residential basis without the surcharge (using the table-band-by-table-band calculation) or substantially more with the surcharge. The non-residential / mixed-use rate table at FA 2003 s.55(1B) Table B (0% to £150,000, 2% £150,000 to £250,000, 5% above £250,000) produces SDLT of approximately £139,500 on a £3 million purchase. The difference of around £118,500 to £119,000 is the saving the mixed-use claim seeks to lock in. The Averdieck amended return locked in roughly £119,000 of refund; the FTT decision required that figure to be repaid. This is why mixed-use claims are particularly attractive to second-home buyers and additional-property buyers: the same reclassification saves both the residential / mixed-use rate differential AND the 5% surcharge. The double-incentive does not change the FTT's narrow reading of the section 116 boundary, but it does explain why mixed-use claims are pursued aggressively by SDLT-refund firms on additional-property fact patterns."

<aside>
<p><strong>In James and Charlotte Averdieck v HMRC [2022] TC 8623 the First-tier Tribunal dismissed an SDLT mixed-use claim built on the existence of a public right of way across the grounds of a £3m country property.</strong> The taxpayers had amended their original return to claim mixed-use rates, HMRC paid out a £119,000 refund, then opened an enquiry. The FTT sided with HMRC: a right of way that the owners use for their own residential access, and that imposes ordinary statutory maintenance obligations, does not strip the land of its residential character. The £119,000 refund was clawed back. Averdieck applies the post-Hyman narrowing trajectory to the public-rights-of-way fact pattern: features of the grounds that do not change the underlying use are encumbrances on the land, not non-residential elements that move the whole transaction onto the mixed-use rate table.</p>
</aside>

<p>If a refund firm has told you that the public footpath crossing your grounds turns a £3m purchase into a mixed-use one and unlocks a six-figure SDLT refund, Averdieck is the case you need to read first. It is now the leading FTT authority on public-rights-of-way mixed-use claims, and the practical takeaway is blunt: a PROW on its own loses, and it loses with consistent frequency. What follows is the statutory test in FA 2003 section 116, exactly why the Tribunal rejected the argument, what failure costs you, and the questions to put to any firm offering you a contingent-fee claim.</p>

<h2>The fact pattern</h2>

<p>James and Charlotte Averdieck jointly purchased a substantial residential property and grounds for £3 million in August 2020. They filed an SDLT return in September 2020 on the residential basis. The SDLT figure on the original return was £258,000.</p>

<p>In December 2020, they amended the SDLT return to claim mixed-use treatment under the non-residential rate table at FA 2003 s.55(1B) Table B. The basis for the amendment was that an access lane crossing the property was a public right of way, that the lane provided access to five other residential properties and a farm beyond, and that the appellants had statutory maintenance obligations in respect of the lane. The argument was that these features made a portion of the grounds non-residential land within FA 2003 s.116, which (because the residential / mixed-use rate question is a binary one applied to the whole transaction, not apportioned) moved the whole £3m purchase onto Table B non-residential rates.</p>

<p>HMRC processed the amendment and refunded £119,000. The refund mechanism on SDLT return amendments is largely automatic at the amendment stage; substantive HMRC review typically follows. HMRC subsequently opened an enquiry into the amended return, the amendment was challenged, and the matter went to the First-tier Tribunal. The FTT dismissed the appeal. The £119,000 became repayable.</p>

<h2>The statutory test: FA 2003 section 116</h2>

<p>The residential property definition for SDLT is in <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2003/14/section/116">FA 2003 section 116</a>. The relevant subsections are:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>116(1)(a):</strong> "a building that is used or suitable for use as a dwelling, or is in the process of being constructed or adapted for such use".</li>
<li><strong>116(1)(b):</strong> "land that is or forms part of the garden or grounds of a building within paragraph (a) (including any building or structure on such land)".</li>
<li><strong>116(1)(c):</strong> "an interest in or right over land that subsists for the benefit of a building within paragraph (a) or of land within paragraph (b)".</li>
</ul>

<p>The mixed-use argument seeks to take some portion of the property outside one of these three sub-paragraphs. In Averdieck, the argument was that a portion of the grounds (the lane subject to the public right of way, plus arguably the burden the right of way placed on the surrounding grounds) was not 'garden or grounds' within section 116(1)(b) because of the public-access character and the maintenance obligations. The argument therefore engages the section 116(1)(b) "garden or grounds" question, not the section 116(1)(a) building question (the dwelling itself was unambiguously residential).</p>

<p>The money at stake explains why these claims keep being made. On a £3 million purchase, residential SDLT is approximately <strong>£258,000</strong> on the standard rates without the additional dwellings surcharge, computed under the residential band-by-band table. Mixed-use SDLT at the Table B non-residential rates (0% to £150,000; 2% £150,000 to £250,000; 5% above £250,000) is approximately <strong>£139,500</strong> on a £3 million purchase. The difference is around £118,500 to £119,000, which closely matches the refund HMRC paid out on the Averdieck amended return. If you already own another residential property, the residential figure also carries the 5% additional dwellings surcharge, and a mixed-use reclassification disapplies that surcharge entirely (it applies only to residential property under Schedule 4ZA), so the prize a refund firm dangles in front of a second home is larger still.</p>

<h2>The FTT's reasoning</h2>

<p>The Tribunal dismissed the appeal on three grounds, each of which is general (not narrowly confined to Averdieck's specific facts):</p>

<ol>
<li><strong>The right of way served the appellants' own residential access.</strong> The lane was the appellants' route to their own property and was integral to their residential use of the grounds; it was also used by the public for access to neighbouring properties, but the appellants' own residential use was the primary character.</li>
<li><strong>The existence of obligations in respect of land does not exclude that land from being residential property.</strong> Landowners routinely bear statutory or contractual obligations on their land (planning conditions, easement obligations, wayleave duties, maintenance covenants under restrictive covenants). These are burdens, not redesignations of the use. The land's character is determined by what it is and how it is used, not by the obligations attached to it.</li>
<li><strong>The burden of the obligations was not sufficient on these facts.</strong> The Tribunal made a factual finding that the maintenance burden was not so substantial as to take the land outside its residential character. The implication is that an exceptionally heavy burden might in principle do so, but the routine statutory maintenance obligations on a public right of way are unlikely to clear that threshold.</li>
</ol>

<p>The three grounds combine into a robust reading of section 116(1)(b): an encumbrance over residential grounds (public footpath, bridleway, byway; private easement; wayleave) does not redesignate the underlying land. Mixed-use treatment requires a substantive change in the use of a severable portion of the property, not the presence of a burden on the existing use.</p>

<h2>Averdieck in the wider narrowing trajectory</h2>

<p>The case-law line on the residential / mixed-use boundary has narrowed materially since 2021. The leading authority is Hyman v HMRC, decided at the FTT at [2019] UKFTT 469, upheld at the Upper Tribunal at [2021] UKUT 68, and confirmed at the Court of Appeal at [2022] EWCA Civ 185. Hyman established the 'in principle suitable' test for the garden-or-grounds question and rejected mixed-use claims founded on minor or marginal grounds-features. The line continued in Mudan v HMRC [2023] UKFTT 317, which held that unmodernised condition alone does not bring a property within mixed-use, and in subsequent FTT decisions including MHB Ltd v HMRC and Brown v HMRC.</p>

<p>Averdieck applies the same narrowing logic to the public-rights-of-way sub-fact-pattern. The Tribunal did not treat the PROW question as raising any new principle: it applied the same general approach to encumbrances and to the section 116(1)(b) garden-or-grounds analysis that the Hyman line had already established. The cumulative effect is that the residual class of fact patterns where mixed-use claims succeed is now narrow: substantive commercial use of a severable portion of the grounds (paddocks let on a formal grazing agreement to a third-party farmer; outbuildings used as third-party commercial premises generating rental income; a discrete portion of the property severed from the dwelling and put to a non-residential purpose). PROW, easement, wayleave, and modernisation-need arguments now consistently fail.</p>

<h2>What does it cost you if the claim fails?</h2>

<p>The Averdieck amended return locked in a £119,000 refund, and the FTT decision required that figure to be repaid. The risk in a mixed-use claim is therefore roughly symmetrical with the prize: on a £3 million purchase a contemplated £118,500 saving comes with a £118,500 exposure if the claim is refused. On top of the clawback sit the secondary costs:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Enquiry costs.</strong> HMRC and the appellants' own adviser fees through the enquiry, FTT correspondence, hearing preparation, and the hearing itself. For a fully-contested FTT case these typically run into five-figure sums on each side.</li>
<li><strong>Penalty exposure under FA 2007 Schedule 24.</strong> Inaccurate SDLT returns attract penalties calibrated to the degree of culpability: 0% for an unprompted disclosure of a genuine mistake, up to 30% for a careless inaccuracy with prompted disclosure, up to 70% for a deliberate inaccuracy, and up to 100% for a deliberate and concealed inaccuracy. Whether a refused mixed-use claim is held to be careless or deliberate depends on the specific facts and the quality of the original advice. An aggressive claim on weak facts can readily fall into the careless bracket; a claim taken on bad-faith fact representations can fall into the deliberate bracket.</li>
<li><strong>Interest.</strong> HMRC charges interest on the underpaid SDLT from the original due date. For a multi-year enquiry-and-litigation cycle, the interest figure can be material on a six-figure underpayment.</li>
</ul>

<p>The contingent-fee model SDLT-refund firms use typically takes a percentage of the refund (often 25% to 35%) and shifts none of the downside back to the firm. You are the one on the hook for the clawback, the enquiry costs, the penalty exposure and the interest. Getting independent advice from a chartered accountant or specialist solicitor (not the refund firm) before you sign any contingent-fee instruction is the sensible step.</p>

<h2>What about wayleaves, easements, and other encumbrances?</h2>

<p>The Averdieck reasoning is general. An encumbrance over residential land (a public right of way, a private easement granting access to a neighbouring property, a wayleave for overhead electricity cables, a wayleave for telecoms infrastructure, a restrictive covenant requiring maintenance of a boundary feature) is a burden on the land, not a redesignation of its use. ICAEW Tax Faculty published guidance on the analogous wayleave question reaches the same conclusion: a wayleave does not change a property from residential to non-residential.</p>

<p>Mixed-use treatment requires more than an encumbrance. Indicative fact patterns where the analysis may shift include:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Severable commercial use:</strong> a portion of the grounds (paddocks, an outbuilding, a yard) used exclusively for a commercial purpose under a formal commercial arrangement with a third party.</li>
<li><strong>Third-party occupation:</strong> a separate dwelling or self-contained unit within the grounds occupied by a third-party tenant under a residential or commercial lease.</li>
<li><strong>Substantial commercial infrastructure:</strong> a working farm, a commercial nursery, a kennels, or similar where the grounds host a discrete operational business.</li>
</ul>

<p>The common feature is substantive non-residential use by a party other than the owner-occupier, severable from the dwelling and grounds, not merely an encumbrance or a routine obligation. Each fact pattern is sensitive to the precise documentation, occupation history, and consideration arrangements.</p>

<h2>What should you ask if a refund firm approaches you?</h2>

<p>Aggressive SDLT-refund firms have marketed mixed-use claims heavily over the past several years, PROW-based claims of the Averdieck type among them. After Averdieck the FTT line is settled against these claims on the typical fact pattern. If a firm approaches you, ask at least these four questions before you commit to anything:</p>

<ul>
<li>Which specific FTT decisions support the firm's analysis on your fact pattern? (If the firm cites Hyman in support, treat it as a red flag; Hyman is the leading authority against mixed-use claims, not for them.)</li>
<li>What is the firm's fee structure, and which costs (the clawback if the claim fails, the enquiry costs, the FA 2007 Schedule 24 penalty exposure) sit with you?</li>
<li>Has the firm obtained tax counsel's written opinion on your specific fact pattern, and can you see that opinion?</li>
<li>What happens if HMRC opens an enquiry and reverses the amendment, as it did in Averdieck?</li>
</ul>

<p>A reputable adviser gives you straight answers on each of these. The right pre-claim diligence step is independent advice on your specific fact pattern from a chartered accountant or solicitor, separately from the refund firm.
