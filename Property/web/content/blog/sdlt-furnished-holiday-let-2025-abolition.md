<h2>Understanding SDLT on Furnished Holiday Lettings After April 2025</h2>

<p>The short answer is: <strong>yes, stamp duty land tax (SDLT) still applies to furnished holiday lettings (FHLs) after the abolition of the FHL regime from April 2025</strong>. However, whether the 5% additional property surcharge applies depends on whether the property is treated as a residential or non-residential (commercial) property for SDLT purposes.</p>

<p>This distinction matters because the 5% surcharge only applies to <strong>additional residential properties</strong>. If your FHL is classified as commercial or mixed-use for SDLT, you may avoid the surcharge entirely. Let's break this down with a worked example for a £400,000 FHL purchase.</p>

<h2>What Changed in April 2025 for Furnished Holiday Lettings?</h2>

<p>The Furnished Holiday Lettings regime was abolished from 6 April 2025. This means:</p>

<ul>
<li>FHLs are no longer treated as a separate class of property for tax purposes</li>
<li>Capital allowances on FHL assets are no longer available</li>
<li>Business Asset Disposal Relief (BADR) via FHL status is no longer available</li>
<li>FHL income is now treated as standard property income under the property income rules</li>
</ul>

<p>However, <strong>the abolition does not automatically change how SDLT is calculated</strong>. SDLT classification depends on the nature of the property at the time of purchase, not the tax regime that applies to the income.</p>

<h2>How SDLT Applies to Furnished Holiday Lettings</h2>

<p>For SDLT purposes, a property is classified as either:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Residential</strong>, includes buy-to-let properties, second homes, and holiday lets that are used primarily as dwellings</li>
<li><strong>Non-residential or mixed-use</strong>, includes commercial properties, hotels, guest houses, and properties used for trade</li>
</ul>

<p>If your FHL is a standard holiday cottage or apartment that is let out for short-term stays but remains a dwelling, HMRC will typically treat it as <strong>residential property</strong> for SDLT purposes. This means:</p>

<ul>
<li>Standard SDLT rates apply (0% up to £250,000, 5% on £250,001, £925,000, etc.)</li>
<li>The <strong>5% additional property surcharge</strong> applies if you already own another residential property</li>
<li>First-time buyers may qualify for relief if they meet the conditions</li>
</ul>

<p>If your FHL is more commercial in nature, for example, a guest house with multiple letting units, a hotel, or a property that is used for trade, it may be classified as <strong>non-residential or mixed-use</strong>. In that case:</p>

<ul>
<li>Commercial SDLT rates apply (0% up to £150,000, 1% on £150,001, £250,000, 5% above £250,000)</li>
<li>The 5% additional property surcharge does NOT apply</li>
<li>This can result in significantly lower SDLT costs</li>
</ul>

<h2>Worked Example: £400,000 FHL Purchase</h2>

<p>Let's look at a realistic example. Sarah owns one buy-to-let property in Manchester and wants to buy a £400,000 holiday cottage in Cornwall to let out as a furnished holiday let. She already owns a residential property, so she is not a first-time buyer.</p>

<h3>Scenario A: FHL Treated as Residential Property</h3>

<p>If the cottage is a standard dwelling (e.g., a 3-bedroom house with a kitchen, bathroom, and living areas), HMRC will treat it as residential. Sarah already owns another residential property, so the 5% surcharge applies.</p>

<p><strong>SDLT calculation:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>0% on first £250,000 = £0</li>
<li>5% on next £150,000 (£250,001 to £400,000) = £7,500</li>
<li>5% surcharge on entire £400,000 = £20,000</li>
<li><strong>Total SDLT: £27,500</strong></li>
</ul>

<p>Note: The surcharge is calculated on the full purchase price, not just the portion above £250,000. This is standard for additional residential properties.</p>

<h3>Scenario B: FHL Treated as Non-Residential or Mixed-Use</h3>

<p>If the property is a guest house with separate letting units, or a property that includes commercial space (e.g., a shop below the holiday let), it may be classified as non-residential or mixed-use. In this case, the 5% surcharge does not apply.</p>

<p><strong>SDLT calculation:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>0% on first £150,000 = £0</li>
<li>1% on next £100,000 (£150,001 to £250,000) = £1,000</li>
<li>5% on remaining £150,000 (£250,001 to £400,000) = £7,500</li>
<li><strong>Total SDLT: £8,500</strong></li>
</ul>

<p>The difference is stark: <strong>£27,500 vs £8,500</strong>. That's a saving of £19,000 simply based on the SDLT classification.</p>

<h2>How to Determine Your FHL's SDLT Classification</h2>

<p>HMRC looks at the <strong>physical characteristics and use</strong> of the property at the time of purchase. Key factors include:</p>

<ul>
<li>Does the property have self-contained living facilities (kitchen, bathroom, bedrooms)?</li>
<li>Is it marketed as a holiday let or as a commercial guest house?</li>
<li>Does it have multiple letting units or is it a single dwelling?</li>
<li>Is there any commercial element (e.g., a shop, restaurant, or office)?</li>
</ul>

<p>If your FHL is a standard holiday cottage or apartment, it will almost certainly be treated as residential. If it is a larger property with multiple units or a mixed-use element, you may have grounds to argue for non-residential classification.</p>

<h2>What About the 5% Surcharge on Additional Properties?</h2>

<p>The 5% surcharge on additional residential properties applies to purchases of residential property where the buyer already owns one or more residential properties. This surcharge was increased from 3% to 5% on 31 October 2024.</p>

<p>For FHLs treated as residential, the surcharge applies in the same way as for any other buy-to-let property. There is no special exemption for holiday lets simply because they are let out on a short-term basis.</p>

<p>However, if you are buying an FHL as your <strong>only residential property</strong> (i.e., you do not own any other residential property), the surcharge does not apply. You would pay standard residential SDLT rates.</p>

<h2>Current Rules for Furnished Holiday Lettings Post-Abolition</h2>

<p>Understanding the <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax">current rules for furnished holiday lettings</a> is essential for any landlord considering this type of investment. Here are the key points:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Income tax:</strong> FHL income is now treated as standard property income. You cannot claim capital allowances on furniture or equipment. Instead, you claim the replacement of domestic items relief.</li>
<li><strong>Mortgage interest:</strong> Section 24 applies in full. Mortgage interest relief is restricted to a 20% tax credit, not deducted from rental income.</li>
<li><strong>Capital gains tax:</strong> When you sell, you pay CGT at 18% (basic rate) or 24% (higher rate) on residential property gains. The annual exempt amount is £3,000.</li>
<li><strong>SDLT:</strong> As discussed above, the classification depends on the property's nature, not the FHL regime.</li>
<li><strong>Making Tax Digital:</strong> From 6 April 2026, landlords with qualifying income over £50,000 (the MTD-for-ITSA threshold from 6 April 2026, falling to £30,000 from 6 April 2027 and £20,000 from 6 April 2028) must file quarterly digital updates. This includes FHL income. See our <a href="/blog/making-tax-digital-mtd/making-tax-digital-landlords-april-2026-deadline">MTD for landlords guide</a> for details.</li>
</ul>

<h2>How Much Tax to Pay on a Furnished Holiday Let</h2>

<p>The question of <a href="/blog/section-24-and-tax-relief/rental-income-tax-uk-complete-guide-landlords">how much tax to pay in a furnished holiday let</a> depends on your overall income and property portfolio. Here's a simplified example:</p>

<p>Let's say your FHL generates £25,000 in rental income per year. Your allowable expenses (cleaning, utilities, insurance, repairs, letting agent fees) total £8,000. Your net rental profit is £17,000.</p>

<p>If you are a basic rate taxpayer (20%), you would pay £3,400 in income tax on this profit. However, if you have other income that pushes you into the higher rate band (40% from £50,271 in 2026/27), the tax on the FHL profit would be £6,800.</p>

<p>Remember: from April 2027, separate property income tax rates apply, 22% basic, 42% higher, and 47% additional rate on property income. This is a major change that will affect all landlords, including FHL owners.</p>

<h2>Can You Avoid SDLT on a Furnished Holiday Let?</h2>

<p>There is no blanket exemption from SDLT for furnished holiday lettings. However, there are ways to potentially reduce or avoid the surcharge:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Purchase through a company:</strong> If you buy the FHL through a limited company, the SDLT rates are the same as for individuals. However, the company may benefit from corporation tax rates (19% or 25%) instead of income tax. See our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/buy-to-let-limited-company-complete-guide-uk">buy-to-let limited company guide</a> for more.</li>
<li><strong>Mixed-use classification:</strong> If the property has a commercial element, you may qualify for mixed-use SDLT rates, which avoid the surcharge.</li>
<li><strong>First-time buyer relief:</strong> If you are a first-time buyer and the FHL is your only residential property, you may qualify for relief on purchases up to £625,000.</li>
<li><strong>Multiple dwellings relief:</strong> If the FHL consists of multiple self-contained units, you may be able to claim multiple dwellings relief, which can reduce the effective SDLT rate.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Final Thoughts</h2>
