  - question: "What is the 'double-trap' on a property trust?"
    answer: "Two independent IHT add-back regimes catching the same arrangement simultaneously. Trap one is the settlor-interest mechanism: the trust is a chargeable lifetime transfer into the relevant property regime, with 20% entry IHT above the NRB, 10-year periodic charges, exit charges, TCGA 1992 s.169B blocking both s.260 and s.165(4) holdover (so a dry CGT charge at 24% residential rates bites on entry), and ITTOIA 2005 s.624 attributing all trust income back to the settlor. Trap two is the FA 1986 s.102 gifts-with-reservation mechanism: the property is treated as remaining in the settlor's death estate at death market value, because HMRC's published view at IHTM42254 is that mere membership in a discretionary beneficiary class is reservation of benefit. The two traps have different triggers, different consequences, and different unwinding mechanisms, and the SI 1987/1130 Double Charges Relief Regulations provide only partial relief, leaving meaningful IHT exposure unrelieved."
  - question: "Does HMRC really say that being in a discretionary beneficiary class is reservation of benefit?"
    answer: "Yes, explicitly. HMRC Inheritance Tax Manual IHTM42254 states: 'if a settlor transfers property to a discretionary trust of which they are a member of a class of potential beneficiaries, the settlor has reserved a benefit. This applies even if trustees might never actually distribute to the settlor.' The published practice is unequivocal: the test is whether the settlor could in principle benefit, not whether they actually do. The practical consequence is that a trust deed naming 'the settlor and the settlor's descendants' as the discretionary class is GROB-caught from the date of settlement, even if the trustees never make a distribution to the settlor in their lifetime. Removing the settlor from the class prospectively does not retrospectively undo the reservation; the reservation must be actually released for the s.102(4) deemed PET to start the seven-year clock."
  - question: "What does the SI 1987/1130 Double Charges Relief actually do?"
    answer: "The Inheritance Tax (Double Charges Relief) Regulations 1987 provide partial relief where the same property is potentially taxed twice on death. The two regulations most relevant to the settlor-interest plus GROB scenario are Regulation 5 (gifts with reservation followed by death) and Regulation 7 (chargeable lifetime transfers followed by property return). Regulation 5 prevents double-charging where property has been subject to GROB and is in the donor's death estate AND has separately been chargeable as a CLT or failed PET in the seven years before death. The relief applies the higher of two calculations and gives credit for tax already paid on the earlier transfer. The critical limitation per IHTM42254: the relief 'applies only to charges on the settlor as an individual and do not affect tax on TYA or proportionate charges' inside the trust. So the trust still pays its 10-year periodic charge and its exit charges on the same value that is being separately taxed in the settlor's estate; the relief only reduces the settlor's personal-estate side."
  - question: "Why does the relief not cover the trust's 10-year and exit charges?"
    answer: "Because the trust is a separate IHT-paying entity from the settlor, and the relief regulations are structured to prevent double-charging within a single chargeable entity, not across them. The trust's 10-year periodic charge under IHTA 1984 s.64 is computed on the trust's relevant property value at the anniversary date, regardless of whether the same value is also being separately taxed in the settlor's death estate. The trust's exit charges under s.65 on capital distributions follow the same architecture. The SI 1987/1130 relief looks only at the settlor's personal estate, computing the higher of (a) the IHT on the GROB add-back at death, or (b) the IHT on the original CLT, and giving credit for tax already paid. The result is that a settlor-interested-plus-GROB structure of, say, a £500,000 property held for 11 years and worth £700,000 at death pays the trust-side 10-year charge AND the trust-side exit charge on distribution AND the settlor-side GROB death-charge with partial credit. The total IHT exposure can comfortably exceed what would have been paid had the property simply stayed in the settlor's direct ownership."
  - question: "What is the Mitchell £500,000 family-home worked example?"
    answer: "Mr Mitchell, age 62, owns a family home worth £500,000 (no mortgage, unencumbered title). He has been advised to settle the home on a discretionary trust naming himself, his wife, his two adult children, and any future grandchildren as the discretionary beneficiary class. He intends to continue living in the home rent-free; his wife also lives there. Year 0. IHT (CLT): £500,000 settled, less £325,000 available NRB = £175,000 chargeable at the 20% lifetime rate = £35,000 IHT payable to HMRC within six months. CGT (s.169B Condition 1): the trust is settlor-interested because Mr Mitchell is in the beneficiary class; s.260 holdover is blocked; the £300,000 latent gain (assumed) on the home crystallises at the 24% residential rate, less the £3,000 AEA, generating CGT of approximately £71,280 payable within 60 days. Note: the home is not Mr Mitchell's PPR for s.222 TCGA 1992 purposes after the transfer because the trustees are connected persons under s.286 but the s.225 trust-PPR election would itself trip the settlor-interest test. CGT exposure on Year 0: ~£71,280. GROB also bites immediately under HMRC's IHTM42254 position; Mr Mitchell continues to occupy rent-free, so the FA 1986 s.102(1)(b) test fails (the property is not enjoyed to the entire exclusion of the donor). Year 0 total cost: £35,000 IHT + £71,280 CGT = £106,280. Years 1 to 10. The trust pays the standard relevant property charges; Mr Mitchell pays POAT or IHT-elects under FA 2004 Sch 15 para 21. Year 11. Mr Mitchell dies; home worth £700,000 at death. The £700,000 is added back to his death estate under s.102(3) at MV at death. IHT at 40% on the GROB add-back, less the £325,000 NRB if available, less SI 1987/1130 Reg 5 credit for the £35,000 already paid: net death charge in the order of £125,000 to £140,000. The trust separately pays its s.64 10-year charge on the £700,000 value at year 10. Combined IHT exposure: significantly higher than if Mr Mitchell had retained the home and used a deed of variation on first death (estimated counterfactual: £20,000 to £40,000 of IHT)."
  - question: "Is removing the settlor from the beneficiary class via deed of variation enough to fix the structure?"
    answer: "No, by itself it ends only the settlor-interest, not the GROB. The settlor-interest ends prospectively from the date of the variation: future trust income is no longer attributed to the settlor under s.624; future disposals by trustees are no longer caught by s.169B; future periodic and exit charges still apply but without the settlor's personal liability exposure. The GROB however persists for as long as the settlor enjoys the actual benefit (continued occupation, rent receipt, any benefit deriving from the property). FA 1986 s.102(1)(b) is a fact-based test on actual enjoyment, not a class-membership test. To end the GROB the settlor must actually release the reservation: cease occupying, or begin paying full market rent to the trustees (with rent reviews and bank evidence), or use the FA 1986 Sch 20 para 6 co-ownership carve-out where applicable. The release of reservation then triggers a deemed PET under s.102(4); a fresh seven-year clock starts on the date of release."
  - question: "What does s.102(4) say about releasing the reservation?"
    answer: "Verbatim s.102(4): 'If, at a time before the end of the relevant period, any property ceases to be property subject to a reservation, the donor shall be treated for the purposes of the 1984 Act as having at that time made a disposition of the property by a disposition which is a potentially exempt transfer.' Three operational consequences. First, the release is a deemed gift at the date of release, not at the date of original gift. Second, the deemed PET runs the standard seven-year clock from the release date; the donor must survive seven years from release for the property to fall fully out of the death estate. Third, the deemed PET is at the market value on the release date (not the original gift date), so a property that has appreciated during the reservation period is now potentially in the seven-year cumulation at the higher value. The release strategy is therefore subject to the donor's surviving the fresh seven-year clock; for an elderly donor with poor health prospects, the release may not deliver any meaningful IHT saving and the alternative routes (POAT election, structural redesign) may dominate."
  - question: "What is the three-part unwinding playbook?"
    answer: "Step one: diagnose. Test the existing arrangement against (a) settlor-interest under ITTOIA 2005 s.624 / s.625 and TCGA 1992 s.169B / s.169F / s.169E (per our Wave 6 B4 page) and (b) GROB under FA 1986 s.102, s.102A, s.102B (per our Wave 2 A2 base page and Wave 6 B5 family-home depth page). The two diagnoses may have different answers; a trust may be settlor-interested without GROB (where the settlor is in the class but never receives any actual benefit), GROB without settlor-interest (where the settlor was excluded from the class but continues to occupy with the donee's permission), or both. Step two: release the settlor-interest. Where the deed permits, an exclusion deed expressly and irrevocably removes the settlor and settlor's spouse from the beneficiary class; the variation takes effect prospectively. The variation may itself trigger an exit charge under s.65 if it is a 'distribution' from relevant property in a material sense. Step three: release the GROB. Independent of step two. The settlor either ceases occupation entirely, or begins paying full open-market rent to the trustees on a documented commercial basis, or restructures into a Sch 20 para 6 co-ownership scenario. The release triggers the s.102(4) deemed PET; a fresh seven-year clock starts. Step three must be completed; step two alone does not fix GROB."
  - question: "Can I use the FIC structure to avoid the double-trap entirely?"
    answer: "Often yes, on the entry side. The Family Investment Company route is a corporate vehicle, not a trust; the property is transferred into the FIC at market value (triggering CGT under TCGA 1992 s.17, with no holdover available because there is no CLT to engage s.260), the founder's share of the FIC is then gifted as PETs to the next generation, and the gift is of shares (not of the property) so FA 1986 s.102 GROB does not bite on the share gift. The founder may continue to direct the company through their director role and may receive a director's salary, dividends, or rent (where the FIC leases the property back, with full market terms and the FIC paying CT on the rent). The s.624 income-tax attribution does not bite because there is no settlement (Arctic Systems share-class structures may have their own s.624 risk on dividend allocations, but that is a separate analysis). The trade-off: no s.260 CGT deferral, so the entry-side CGT bill is paid up front (often higher than the trust-route's £15,000 IHT entry cost, depending on the latent gain). For founders with substantial latent gains and an existing structural preference for trustee governance, the trust route with a clean (non-settlor-interested) drafting is still typically the dominant option. For founders with mid-life property and clear corporate-governance preferences, the FIC route is the structural escape from the double-trap. See our existing FIC vs Discretionary Trust pillar for the four-axis decision frame."
  - question: "What is POAT and when does it bite on top of the double-trap?"
    answer: "Pre-Owned Assets Tax under FA 2004 Sch 15 is the income-tax-side backstop where the GROB rules do not bite but the donor continues to enjoy a former asset. The classic POAT case: the donor makes a cash gift to children, the children buy the property, the donor lives in the property rent-free. No reservation under FA 1986 s.102 (the gift was cash, not property), but the donor enjoys benefit. POAT charges the donor an annual income-tax amount based on the deemed market rent of the property. For a settlor-interested-plus-GROB structure, POAT does NOT typically add a third charge; the FA 2004 Sch 15 para 21 election allows the donor to elect for GROB treatment instead of POAT, and the GROB death-charge has already been triggered by the s.102 mechanism. POAT becomes relevant where the structure has been re-engineered to release the GROB (so the s.102 charge no longer bites) but the donor continues to enjoy the property; in that case POAT picks up the IT-side charge that GROB had been providing the IHT-side charge for. The PaO interaction with the unwinding playbook is therefore: if step three (GROB release) is achieved, POAT becomes the active charge while the s.102(4) deemed-PET seven-year clock runs."
  - question: "Does the double-trap apply to let property (no occupation by settlor)?"
    answer: "Yes on the settlor-interest side, no on the standard GROB side. Where the settlor settles a let property on a discretionary trust naming themselves as a beneficiary, s.624 attribution and s.169B holdover block both apply on the settlor-interest analysis. The GROB analysis depends on actual benefit. If the trustees take the rents and accumulate or distribute them to other beneficiaries, the settlor enjoys no benefit from the property and s.102 does not bite (the standard let-property arms-length-gift outcome covered on our existing Wave 4 C3 page on let-property GROB). If the trustees pay rents to the settlor (whether as a beneficiary, as a loan repayment, as a salary for property management, or via any indirect mechanism), the rents are a benefit derived from the property and s.102 bites; the let-property variant of the double-trap then engages. Our Wave 4 C3 page on iht-gift-with-reservation-letting-children-paying-rent-mechanics walks the let-property GROB analysis in detail; B7 is the trust-overlay on that analysis."
  - question: "How does the double-trap compare to FA 2004 Sch 15 POAT and IHTA 1984 s.49(1A)?"
    answer: "Three different anti-avoidance regimes targeting overlapping but distinct arrangements. FA 1986 s.102 GROB targets the donor's continued benefit on a gift, with IHT-add-back at death market value as the consequence. FA 2004 Sch 15 POAT targets continued benefit on structures that escape GROB, with an annual income-tax charge as the consequence. IHTA 1984 s.49(1A) targets specific post-22-March-2006 IIPs (IPDIs, disabled persons' interests, transitional serial interests) by reading them through to the underlying property for IHT. The three regimes can each independently apply to the same arrangement, and a single structure can be caught by all three in adverse cases. The double-trap on B7 is specifically the s.102 GROB plus settlor-interest combination; the addition of s.49(1A) (if the trust is structured as an IPDI for the surviving spouse) creates a triple-trap that is rare in practice but worth modelling on edge-case advisory work. See our Wave 6 B4 page on the three-statute attribution stack for the s.49(1A) lens."
  - question: "When is unwinding worth it versus accepting the imperfect structure?"
    answer: "Four factors drive the decision. (1) Donor age and health. An elderly donor in poor health gets little benefit from a fresh s.102(4) seven-year clock; the unwinding tax cost may exceed the IHT saved. A 55-year-old donor in good health has substantial benefit from running a fresh clock. (2) Asset appreciation expected during the seven-year survival window. A high-appreciation property in a buoyant market makes the unwinding more valuable; a stable-value property in a flat market makes it less so. (3) Cost of the unwinding event itself. Releasing the GROB requires ceasing occupation (often emotionally and practically difficult for a family home) or paying full market rent (a real annual cash cost the family must fund). Ending the settlor-interest may trigger s.65 exit charges or s.71 trustee-deemed-disposal events. (4) Alternative-structure cost. If the family is willing to restructure entirely (FIC value-freeze, IHT-efficient direct gift with no continued occupation, no estate planning at all and acceptance of full IHT at death), the unwinding is compared against the alternative-structure's full cost. For some families the answer is to leave the trust in place, accept the imperfection, and route the family's IHT planning through other assets (pensions, ISAs outside the seven-year clock, charitable legacy under Sch 1A for the reduced-rate 36% band)."

<p>The single highest-frequency advisor error in UK property estate planning runs like this. Parent, age 60-something, owns the family home outright. They want to remove it from the estate for IHT purposes. They are advised by a generalist solicitor to settle the home on a discretionary trust naming themselves, their spouse, their children, and their future grandchildren as the discretionary beneficiary class. The advisor explains the deed gives the family maximum flexibility for future planning. The parent continues to live in the home rent-free, with their spouse, exactly as before. The advisor's view is that the home is now outside the estate.</p>

<p>The advisor's view is wrong, in two independent ways that bite the same arrangement simultaneously. The first is settlor-interest under the relevant property regime: the trust is a chargeable lifetime transfer, the entry-side CGT bites because TCGA 1992 s.169B blocks both s.260 and s.165(4) holdover where the trust is settlor-interested (covered in depth on our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/settlor-interested-trust-iht-s49-1a-cgt-s169b-property-attribution-rules">Wave 6 B4 page on the three-statute attribution stack</a>). The second is gifts-with-reservation under FA 1986 s.102: HMRC's published view at IHTM42254 is unequivocal that mere membership in a discretionary beneficiary class is reservation of benefit, even where the trustees never make a distribution to the settlor, and continued occupation of the property by the settlor is reservation of benefit independently of the class-membership analysis (covered in depth on our <a href="/blog/landlord-tax-essentials/iht-gifts-with-reservation-of-benefit-property">Wave 2 A2 page on the s.102 mechanic</a>).</p>

<p>This page is the interaction page. It takes both traps as read (the depth treatment is on the linked sibling pages) and walks the four practical points that determine the structure's tax outcome: how the two traps interact arithmetically, how the SI 1987/1130 Double Charges Relief regulations partially but only partially fix the double-counting on death, the three-part unwinding playbook for trusts already in place, and the FIC structural-redesign route that avoids both traps on the entry side.</p>

<h2>The two traps in 30 seconds</h2>

<p>Both regimes target the same factual pattern (donor benefits from gifted property) but via different statutory hooks and with different consequences. The amateur-planner mistake is to assume that addressing one regime fixes the other. It does not. The two regimes have independent triggers, independent operations, and independent unwinding mechanisms.</p>

<p><strong>Trap one: settlor-interest under the relevant property regime.</strong> The trust is a CLT into a discretionary trust under IHTA 1984 ss.43-44. Entry charge of 20% on the value above the available NRB (s.7(2) Table). 10-year periodic charge up to 6% under s.64 at each anniversary. Exit charge under s.65 on capital distributions. TCGA 1992 s.169B(1) blocks BOTH s.260 CLT holdover AND s.165(4) business-asset holdover where the trust is settlor-interested per s.169F, so the entry-side CGT is a dry market-value charge under s.17 at residential rates of 18% or 24%. ITTOIA 2005 s.624 attributes all trust income back to the settlor for income tax purposes. None of these is released by the trust failing to make distributions to the settlor; the test is whether the settlor could in principle benefit, not whether they do.</p>

<p><strong>Trap two: GROB under FA 1986 s.102.</strong> The property is treated as remaining in the settlor's death estate at death market value if at the time of death it is "property subject to a reservation". A reservation exists if at any time during the relevant period (typically the seven years before death, or the period gift-to-death if shorter), the property "is not enjoyed to the entire exclusion, or virtually to the entire exclusion, of the donor and of any benefit to him by contract or otherwise" (s.102(1)(b) verbatim). Continued occupation of the gifted home rent-free is the textbook reservation; HMRC's IHTM42254 adds that mere membership in a discretionary class is independently reservation of benefit even where actual occupation is not in issue. Either route is enough to engage s.102.</p>

<h2>The IHTM42254 sentence that drives the whole page</h2>

<p>HMRC Inheritance Tax Manual IHTM42254 (verified at gov.uk on 2026-05-23) states the published practice on the settlor-interest plus GROB intersection in one sentence: "if a settlor transfers property to a discretionary trust of which they are a member of a class of potential beneficiaries, the settlor has reserved a benefit. This applies even if trustees might never actually distribute to the settlor."</p>

<p>The sentence is load-bearing because it closes off the most-common advisor argument that "the settlor is only a backup beneficiary, will never actually receive anything from the trust, and the GROB risk is therefore theoretical". HMRC's view is the opposite: the reservation is the contingent power to benefit, not the actual benefit received. Trustees may go to their grave never distributing to the settlor; the reservation existed for the entire trust life and the s.102 charge bites at death regardless.</p>

<p>The IHTM42254 sentence also closes off the variant argument that the parent's continued occupation is "with the trustees' permission as one beneficiary among others" rather than as a reservation. HMRC's position is that the continued benefit IS the reservation; the trustees' permission does not convert it into something else. For the family home settled on a discretionary trust where the parent stays in occupation, the analysis is fully closed.</p>

<h2>SI 1987/1130 Double Charges Relief: what it does and does not fix</h2>

<p>The Inheritance Tax (Double Charges Relief) Regulations 1987 (Statutory Instrument 1987/1130) were enacted alongside the FA 1986 GROB rules to address the obvious double-counting risk where the same property is potentially in IHT scope twice: once as the original chargeable lifetime transfer and once as the GROB add-back at death. The relief is partial; sessions on this cluster must surface what the relief does and does not do.</p>

<h3>Reg 5: GROB plus death scenarios</h3>

<p>Regulation 5 applies where: (a) an individual makes a transfer of value by way of gift of property, (b) the transfer is or becomes a chargeable transfer (a CLT, or a PET that fails because the donor dies within seven years), and (c) the property of the gift is property subject to a reservation at the donor's death. The mechanism computes the IHT on both bases (the original chargeable transfer plus the GROB add-back at death) and applies the higher of the two, with credit given for tax already paid on the earlier transfer.</p>

<p>For a £500,000 home settled on a settlor-interested discretionary trust where the settlor dies 11 years after the settlement, the calculation runs roughly: (i) lifetime IHT of £35,000 was paid on the original CLT (above the NRB); (ii) at death, the property is in the death estate at MV at death (say £700,000) under s.102(3); (iii) the death-side IHT on the GROB add-back at 40% above the death-side NRB is, say, £160,000; (iv) Reg 5 applies the higher of the two, with credit for the £35,000 already paid: net death-side IHT of approximately £125,000 to £140,000. The relief prevents the obvious double-counting of the original CLT and the death-side GROB on the settlor's personal-estate side.</p>

<h3>Reg 7: CLT plus property return</h3>

<p>Regulation 7 addresses the lifetime-transfer plus property-return scenario where, broadly, property is given by way of CLT and returns to the donor (or is repaid to them) within the seven-year clock. Reg 7 is less commonly engaged on standard property-trust structures but is occasionally relevant where the trust assets are partially distributed back to the settlor under a settlor-interested arrangement before death.</p>

<h3>The relief that is NOT given</h3>

<p>IHTM42254 is explicit: the GROB double-charges relief "applies only to charges on the settlor as an individual and do not affect tax on TYA or proportionate charges" inside the trust. This is the load-bearing limitation. The trust is a separate IHT-paying entity from the settlor. The trust's 10-year periodic charge at the trust's 10-year anniversary is computed on the trust's relevant property value at that date, regardless of whether the same value is also being separately taxed in the settlor's death estate. The trust's exit charges on capital distributions follow the same architecture.</p>

<p>Practical consequence: for the £500,000 home settled at year 0, growing to £700,000 by year 10 and worth £700,000 at the settlor's death in year 11, the trust pays a year-10 s.64 periodic charge in the order of £15,000 to £25,000 (formula-adjusted) AND the settlor's personal-estate pays the GROB death-charge of £125,000 to £140,000 (with Reg 5 credit). The two charges are on the same underlying value; SI 1987/1130 does not relieve the trust-side charge. Combined IHT exposure: in the order of £140,000 to £165,000 in addition to the original £35,000 CLT entry charge and the £71,280 dry CGT entry charge, totalling £245,000 to £275,000 of tax across the life of the structure.</p>

<h2>The Mitchell £500,000 family-home worked example</h2>

<p>Mr Mitchell, settlor, age 62, owns the family home worth £500,000 (unencumbered, no mortgage). The home has been the principal private residence since 2000, acquired for £180,000; assumed base cost including improvements £200,000; latent gain £300,000. Mrs Mitchell, age 60, also lives there. Two adult children, neither living at home. The Mitchells settle the home on a discretionary trust naming "Mr Mitchell, Mrs Mitchell, the children of the marriage, and the grandchildren of the marriage" as the discretionary beneficiary class. The Mitchells continue to live in the home rent-free.</p>

<p><strong>Year 0 entry costs.</strong></p>

<ul>
<li><strong>IHT (CLT):</strong> £500,000 value, less £325,000 NRB (full unused) = £175,000 chargeable at the 20% lifetime rate = £35,000 IHT payable within six months of the end of the month of transfer. Paid by trustees from settlement cash, or by Mr Mitchell from personal funds (with the s.5(2) gross-up rule applying).</li>
<li><strong>CGT (s.169B Condition 1):</strong> trust is settlor-interested because Mr and Mrs Mitchell are both in the beneficiary class. s.260 and s.165(4) holdover both blocked. The £300,000 latent gain crystallises at the residential rate. The Mitchells are higher-rate taxpayers; assume the gain is allocated 50/50 between them per joint legal ownership pre-settlement; net of £3,000 each AEA: net CGT approximately £35,640 each = £71,280 combined, payable within 60 days via the residential property return.</li>
<li><strong>PPR exemption (s.222):</strong> the Mitchells lose their PPR on the home from the date of transfer because the legal owners are now the trustees (not the Mitchells). The s.225 trust-PPR election (allowing the trustees to claim PPR where a beneficiary lives in the trust property) is unavailable because the trust is settlor-interested (the election is restricted by anti-abuse rules). The £300,000 gain is therefore fully chargeable rather than fully PPR-exempt; this loss of PPR is one of the most expensive single mechanical effects of the double-trap structure on a family-home settlement.</li>
<li><strong>GROB:</strong> bites immediately under both routes (IHTM42254 class-membership view AND s.102(1)(b) continued occupation view). No POAT election made; FA 1986 s.102 governs.</li>
<li><strong>Year 0 total cost: £35,000 IHT + £71,280 CGT = £106,280.</strong></li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Years 1 to 10 ongoing.</strong></p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Income tax:</strong> the home generates no rental income (Mitchells continue to occupy), so s.624 attribution has nothing to attribute. If the trust assets were a rental property, s.624 would attribute rents back to the settlor at their marginal rate.</li>
<li><strong>Trust running cost:</strong> trustee fees, accounts, TRS annual maintenance.</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Year 10 periodic charge.</strong> Home worth £700,000. Available NRB at year 10 assumed at £325,000. Hypothetical chargeable transfer value: £700,000 minus £325,000 = £375,000. Effective rate: 30% × 20% × (375,000 / 700,000) ≈ 3.21%. Periodic charge: 3.21% × £700,000 ≈ £22,500. Paid by trustees on form IHT100d.</p>

<p><strong>Year 11 settlor death.</strong> Mr Mitchell dies aged 73. Home worth £700,000 at death. s.102(3) adds £700,000 back to Mr Mitchell's death estate at MV at death (subject to apportionment for Mrs Mitchell's share if joint settlors were both included; for simplicity assume Mr Mitchell alone is the relevant settlor for this side of the analysis). Death-side IHT at 40% above the NRB and RNRB available to the death estate, less SI 1987/1130 Reg 5 credit for the £35,000 lifetime IHT already paid. Net death-side IHT in the order of £125,000 to £140,000 depending on the estate's wider composition and the RNRB-taper position.</p>

<p><strong>Combined IHT and CGT exposure across the structure's life: £35,000 + £71,280 + £22,500 + £130,000 ≈ £258,780.</strong> The counterfactual (Mr Mitchell retains the home, the home passes to Mrs Mitchell under the s.18 spouse exemption on Mr Mitchell's death, the home is then in Mrs Mitchell's estate on second death with two NRBs and two RNRBs potentially available against it depending on the wider estate composition): in the order of £40,000 to £100,000 of IHT depending on estate size, with no CGT entry-side cost at all (the property is in the estate at base cost = death-uplift under s.62(1), removing the £300,000 latent gain entirely). The double-trap structure has cost the Mitchell family £160,000 to £220,000 more than no planning at all.</p>

<aside>
<p>The Mitchell worked example is the structure that an entire generation of family-friend-solicitor estate planning has produced for thousands of UK families.</p>
<p>The arithmetic above is not a worst-case; it is the standard case for a £500,000 home settled on a discretionary trust with the parent in the beneficiary class and continued occupation. Higher-value properties produce proportionally larger IHT and CGT exposure; properties with larger latent gains (long-held inner-London family homes acquired in the 1980s and 1990s) can carry £500,000 to £1 million of dry CGT charge on entry alone. If you are considering a settlement like Mitchell's, or have one in place already, the unwinding playbook below sets out the routes out. The cost of professional review at the decision point is a fraction of the cost of the structural mistake.</p>
</aside>

<h2>The three-part unwinding playbook</h2>

<p>Three steps. The first identifies the problem; the second and third each fix one of the two traps. Both fixes must be completed; either alone leaves the other trap intact.</p>

<h3>Step one: diagnose the structure on both regimes separately</h3>

<p>Test the deed and the practical arrangements against the settlor-interest tests (ITTOIA 2005 s.624 / s.625 for income tax, TCGA 1992 s.169B / s.169F / s.169E for CGT, IHTA 1984 s.49(1A) for IHT-side IIP read-through where relevant). Test the actual occupation and benefit arrangements against the GROB tests (FA 1986 s.102 generally, s.102A for land-interest gifts, s.102B for shared-occupation cases) plus the IHTM42254 class-membership test. The diagnoses may diverge: a trust may be settlor-interested without GROB (where the settlor is in the class but never receives benefit and does not occupy), or GROB without settlor-interest (where the settlor was excluded from the class but continues to occupy with the donees' tolerance), or both.</p>

<p>Document the diagnosis in writing, including the specific clause numbers in the deed that engage each regime and the specific factual arrangements that engage each. The documentation is the foundation for the unwinding strategy and is also the starting evidence pack if HMRC enquires.</p>

<h3>Step two: release the settlor-interest</h3>

<p>Where the deed permits an exclusion deed or a deed of variation, expressly and irrevocably remove the settlor and the settlor's spouse from the beneficiary class. The exclusion takes effect prospectively from the date of the variation; income arising and gains realised before the exclusion remain caught by s.624 / s.169B. The variation may itself trigger an exit charge under IHTA 1984 s.65 if it amounts to a distribution of value from the relevant property regime (typically where the settlor's notional interest is treated as having been distributed away). The variation also triggers a deemed disposal by the trustees for CGT under TCGA 1992 s.71; the gain on the disposal can be held over under s.260 if the post-variation trust is no longer settlor-interested (which by construction it is now not).</p>

<p>This step ends the s.624 income attribution prospectively (future rents are taxed in the trust or beneficiaries, not on the settlor). It ends the s.169B block prospectively (future trustee disposals can now claim s.260 holdover on CLT distributions). It does NOT end the GROB. The settlor remains the originator of the gift; FA 1986 s.102 looks at the donor's continued benefit, not at whether they remain in the beneficiary class.</p>

<h3>Step three: release the reservation (the GROB unwind)</h3>

<p>Independent of step two and structurally necessary on top of it. Three options to actually release the reservation:</p>

<ol>
<li><strong>Cease occupation entirely.</strong> The settlor moves out of the home and does not return as a regular occupier. The cessation date is the trigger for s.102(4): "the donor shall be treated for the purposes of the 1984 Act as having at that time made a disposition of the property by a disposition which is a potentially exempt transfer." A fresh seven-year clock starts. The settlor must survive seven years from the cessation date for the property to fall fully out of the death estate.</li>
<li><strong>Pay full open-market rent to the trustees.</strong> Documented commercial tenancy agreement, regular rent reviews against current market evidence, bank evidence of actual payment, formal landlord-tenant relationship. Below-market rent or informal rent does not break the reservation; HMRC has consistently treated below-market arrangements as continued benefit. The trustees pay corporation tax on the rent receipts (or income tax at trust rates) and may distribute the net to the beneficiaries.</li>
<li><strong>Sch 20 para 6 co-ownership carve-out.</strong> Where applicable: the settlor gifted only a partial interest in the home (not the whole), both settlor and donee actually occupy as co-owners, and the settlor pays their proportionate share of running costs without any element of donee-funded benefit. Narrow application; documentary trail must be solid; covered in detail on our forthcoming <a href="/blog/landlord-tax-essentials/iht-gifts-with-reservation-of-benefit-property">existing GROB walkthrough</a> and our forthcoming Wave 6 B5 on family-home s.102B depth.</li>
</ol>

<p>The release of reservation triggers the s.102(4) deemed PET. The deemed PET is at the market value on the release date (not the original gift date), so a property that has appreciated during the reservation period is now potentially in the seven-year cumulation at the higher value. The settlor must survive seven years from the release date for the property to fall fully out of the death estate.</p>

<p><strong>Critical point:</strong> step two alone is not enough. A deed of variation excluding the settlor from the beneficiary class ends settlor-interest but does NOT end GROB. The donor still enjoys the actual benefit (continues to occupy, continues to receive any indirect benefit deriving from the property), and s.102(1)(b)'s "entire exclusion, or virtually to the entire exclusion, of the donor and of any benefit" test is still failed. Sessions on this cluster must surface this distinction; it is the single most common error in the unwinding-advice community.</p>

<h2>The structural-redesign route: FIC instead of trust</h2>

<p>For families looking at the structure prospectively (not yet committed to a trust), the Family Investment Company route avoids both traps on the entry side. The FIC is a corporate vehicle, not a trust. The property is transferred into the FIC at market value (CGT under TCGA 1992 s.17 bites with no holdover, because no CLT triggers s.260). The founder's shares in the FIC are then gifted as PETs to the next generation; the gift is of shares, not of the property, so FA 1986 s.102 does not bite on the share gift (Ingram v IRC [1999] UKHL 47 and subsequent authorities are clear that share gifts in a property-holding company are not "gifts of property" for s.102 purposes).</p>

<p>The founder may continue to direct the company through their director role and may receive a director's salary (subject to PAYE and the wholly-and-exclusively test), dividends (subject to dividend tax), or rent (where the FIC leases the property back to the founder on full market terms, with the FIC paying corporation tax on the rent receipts and the founder paying full market rent in cash). The settlor-interest s.624 attribution does not bite because there is no settlement (the corporate vehicle is not within the ITTOIA 2005 settlements definition; Arctic Systems share-class structures may have their own s.624 risk on dividend allocations, but that is a separate analysis covered in our forthcoming Wave 6 B6 page).</p>

<p>The trade-off is the CGT entry cost. Where a clean (non-settlor-interested) trust would have given £71,280 of CGT deferral under s.260 holdover, the FIC route pays that £71,280 (or thereabouts; minority-discount valuations on the share gift may reduce the bill substantially in practice). For families with substantial latent gains and an existing structural preference for trustee governance, the clean-trust route is often dominant. For families with mid-life property who want to retain corporate control with the option to receive director-level remuneration, the FIC route is the structural escape from the double-trap.</p>

<h2>POAT as the backstop where GROB does not bite</h2>

<p>Where the structure has been re-engineered to release the GROB (step three of the unwinding playbook achieved, or originally structured so s.102 never engaged), the FA 2004 Sch 15 POAT regime may pick up the benefit on the income-tax side. POAT applies where the donor enjoys continued benefit from a former asset that escapes s.102; the classic case is the cash-gift-buy-house-and-live-there pattern. POAT charges the donor an annual income-tax amount based on the deemed market rent of the property; the rate is the donor's marginal rate against the deemed rental amount.</p>

<p>For a settlor-interested-plus-GROB structure that is mid-unwind (step two done, step three in progress because the settlor is paying full market rent), POAT generally does not bite (the s.102 mechanism is still the active charge until the s.102(4) release triggers). For structures that have completed step three (settlor has ceased occupation entirely, fresh seven-year clock running), POAT may bite if the settlor continues to receive any indirect benefit (e.g. dividend-route distributions from trust-owned shares that derive value from the underlying property). Sessions advising on the post-unwind position should test the POAT analysis independently of the GROB analysis.</p>
