- question: "Can I claim 100% capital allowances on a second hand van?"
  answer: "Yes, in most cases. A used van does not qualify for full expensing or the new 40% first-year allowance, because both of those reliefs require plant that is unused and not second-hand. A second hand van does, however, qualify for the Annual Investment Allowance (AIA), which gives a 100% deduction in the year of purchase on new or second-hand plant, up to the £1,000,000 annual limit. So the headline 100% is still available to a used van, just through the AIA rather than full expensing. The exceptions are if you use the cash basis (no capital allowances on a van at all) or if you bought the van from a connected person (AIA and first-year allowances are denied, leaving the 14% writing-down route)."

  answer: "Yes. If you do not claim the AIA, or your AIA limit is already used up, the van goes into the main capital allowances pool and attracts a writing-down allowance (WDA) at 14% a year on the reducing balance. This is the route that applies, for example, where you bought the van from a relative or your own company and the AIA is denied. It spreads the relief over many years rather than giving it all in year one."
- question: "What writing down allowance applies to a second hand van?"
  answer: "A second hand van sits in the main capital allowances pool, where the writing-down allowance is 14% a year on the reducing balance from April 2026 (it was 18% before then, reduced by Finance Act 2026 s.28). If your accounting period straddles 1 April 2026 for companies or 6 April 2026 for income tax, a hybrid rate between 18% and 14% applies for that one period, time-apportioned by the days falling before and after the change."
- question: "Does full expensing apply to a second hand van?"
  answer: "No. Full expensing (Capital Allowances Act 2001 s.45S) only covers plant and machinery that is unused and not second-hand, and it is available to companies within the charge to corporation tax only. A second hand van fails the unused test and so cannot use full expensing, whether you trade through a company or not. The good news is that the AIA still gives a used van the same 100% first-year deduction in most cases."
- question: "Does the new 40% first-year allowance apply to a used van?"
  answer: "No. The 40% first-year allowance for main-rate plant and machinery (Finance Act 2026 s.29, which inserts s.45U into the Capital Allowances Act 2001, for expenditure from 1 January 2026) also requires the asset to be unused and not second-hand, and it excludes cars. A second hand van does not meet the unused condition, so it cannot use the 40% allowance. It falls back to the AIA (100%) or the 14% writing-down allowance."
- question: "Can a sole trader landlord claim capital allowances on a second hand van?"
  answer: "Yes, if you prepare your property accounts on the accruals basis. A sole trader or partnership can claim the AIA (100%) on a used van used in the property business. The important exception is the cash basis. Under Capital Allowances Act 2001 s.1A, a person using the cash basis is not entitled to capital allowances except on cars, so you cannot claim capital allowances on a van at all. Instead the van's cost is relieved as a deduction in the cash basis computation."
- question: "Can I claim if I bought the van from a relative or my own company?"
  answer: "You can still get relief, but not through the AIA or a first-year allowance. Where you buy plant from a connected person (Capital Allowances Act 2001 ss.214 and 217), no AIA and no first-year allowance can be claimed on that expenditure. The van instead goes into the main pool and attracts the 14% writing-down allowance over time. A spouse, a relative, a business partner and a company you control can all be connected persons, so check before you buy from someone close to you."
- question: "What happens to my capital allowances when I sell the van?"
  answer: "When you dispose of the van (sell it, scrap it or stop using it in the business), the proceeds become a disposal value in your capital allowances pool, capped at the original cost. If you had already claimed 100% through the AIA, that disposal value can create a balancing charge, which is a taxable addition to your profits, broadly reversing the part of the relief that the sale proceeds represent. In short, a generous up-front claim can be partly clawed back when you sell."
- question: "Do I need a mileage log for a second hand van?"
  answer: "Yes, if you use the van for both business and private journeys. HMRC expects you to apportion the claim to the business-use proportion, and a contemporaneous mileage log (date, purpose and miles of each business trip) is the standard evidence. Keep the purchase invoice and proof of payment as well. A van used wholly and exclusively for the property business can be claimed in full, but you still need records to show that."
- question: "Is a used van better than a used car for tax?"
  answer: "For capital allowances, generally yes. A second hand van qualifies for the AIA (100%) because it is not a car. A car is excluded from the AIA and from the 40% first-year allowance, and a used car cannot use the only car first-year allowance (which is for new, unused zero-emission cars). A used car therefore just goes into a pool for writing-down allowances at 14% (CO2 up to 50g/km) or 6% (above 50g/km). Watch out for dual-purpose vehicles such as crew-cab pickups and large SUVs, which HMRC may treat as cars."

  answer: "No. Section 24 of the Finance (No. 2) Act 2015 restricts finance-cost relief (mortgage interest) for individual landlords to a basic-rate tax credit. It has nothing to do with capital allowances on plant and machinery such as a van. They are two separate reliefs and are easy to confuse. Your van claim is governed by the capital allowances rules in the Capital Allowances Act 2001, not by Section 24."
- question: "What is the AIA limit for 2026/27?"
  answer: "The Annual Investment Allowance limit is £1,000,000 a year and is now permanent. That is far more than the cost of a single van, so in practice a used van bought for your property business is comfortably within the limit, provided you have not already used the allowance on other plant and machinery in the same period. Related companies in a group share a single AIA limit."
- question: "Can a limited company claim capital allowances on a second hand van?"
  answer: "Yes. A company can claim the AIA (100%) on a used van in the year of purchase, just like an unincorporated landlord. What a company cannot do on a used van is claim full expensing (s.45S) or the 40% first-year allowance, because both require the van to be unused and not second-hand. For a brand-new van, a company would use full expensing; for a used van, the AIA is the route to the same 100% deduction."

The genuine restriction is the connected-person rule (ss.214 and 217): if the seller is connected to you, the AIA and first-year allowances are denied and you fall back to the 14% writing-down allowance. An ordinary arm's-length purchase from an unconnected business is fine for the AIA."

<p>If you run your property business with a van, the word that changes everything is "second hand". A used van is shut out of the two headline 100% and 40% first-year reliefs that grab all the attention, because both of them only apply to plant that is brand new and unused. So the real question for a second hand van is not "do I get full expensing", it is "do I use the Annual Investment Allowance for 100% in year one, or the 14% writing-down allowance". This guide answers exactly that, with worked examples, the cash basis trap, connected-party purchases and what happens when you sell the van later.</p>

<p>A van used to visit, inspect, maintain and supply your let properties is plant used in the business. It is not plant for use inside a dwelling, so the dwelling-house bar that blocks furniture and fittings inside a let home does not apply to it. This is the used-van page. If you are buying a new van and want the full picture on full expensing and the 40% allowance, see our companion guide on <a href="/blog/landlord-tax-essentials/capital-allowances-on-vans">capital allowances on vans</a>.</p>

<h2>Why "second hand" changes the answer</h2>
<p>Capital allowances let you deduct the cost of a qualifying business asset from your taxable profits, rather than only claiming accounting depreciation, which is not tax deductible <sup><a href="#ref-1" id="cite-1">[1]</a></sup>. For a van there are four possible routes, and "second hand" knocks out two of them before you start.</p>

<p>Full expensing (Capital Allowances Act 2001 s.45S) gives a 100% first-year allowance, but only on plant that is, in the words of the legislation, "unused and not second-hand", and only to a company within the charge to corporation tax <sup><a href="#ref-2" id="cite-2">[2]</a></sup>. The new 40% first-year allowance for main-rate plant (Finance Act 2026 s.29, inserting s.45U into the Capital Allowances Act 2001, for expenditure from 1 January 2026) carries the same "unused and not second-hand" condition and excludes cars <sup><a href="#ref-3" id="cite-3">[3]</a></sup>. A second hand van fails the unused test, so it qualifies for neither.</p>

<p>What a used van does qualify for is the Annual Investment Allowance (AIA), which expressly covers new and second-hand plant and still gives a 100% deduction in year one, and the writing-down allowance (WDA) at 14% in the main pool if the AIA is not used or is unavailable. The table below is the whole topic in one view.</p>

<p>So the headline is reassuring: a used van can still get its full 100% in year one, just through the AIA rather than the first-year allowances. The rest of this guide is about the conditions and the traps.</p>

<h2>The Annual Investment Allowance on a used van: 100% in year one</h2>
<p>The AIA (Capital Allowances Act 2001 s.51A) gives a 100% deduction on qualifying plant and machinery in the period you buy it, up to a limit that is now £1,000,000 a year and permanent <sup><a href="#ref-4" id="cite-4">[4]</a></sup>. Crucially for this page, the AIA contains no restriction limiting it to new assets, which is exactly why it rescues a used van where full expensing and the 40% allowance cannot. It is available to individual landlords and partnerships as well as companies.</p>

<p><strong>Worked example 1, AIA on a used van.</strong> Aisha runs a small portfolio as a sole trader on the accruals basis. In June 2026 she buys a second hand Ford Transit for £14,000 to carry tools and materials between her lets. She has not used her AIA on anything else this year. She claims the AIA and deducts the full £14,000 from her property profits in 2026/27. The van is plant used in the business, not plant for use inside a dwelling, so the dwelling-house restriction does not bite.</p>

<p>If you buy more than one large item in a year, or you have a bigger portfolio, the £1,000,000 limit and the way related companies share a single allowance start to matter. For that detail see our guides to the <a href="/blog/section-24-and-tax-relief/annual-investment-allowance-uk">Annual Investment Allowance</a> and the <a href="/blog/section-24-and-tax-relief/annual-investment-allowance-landlords-uk">AIA for landlords</a>.</p>

<h2>If you do not use the AIA: the 14% writing-down route</h2>
<p>If you choose not to claim the AIA, have already used it elsewhere, or the AIA is denied (for example on a connected-party purchase, below), the van goes into the main capital allowances pool and attracts the writing-down allowance. From April 2026 the main-pool WDA is 14% a year on the reducing balance, reduced from 18% by Finance Act 2026 s.28, which substituted "14%" for "18%" in s.56 of the Capital Allowances Act 2001. The change takes effect for corporation tax from 1 April 2026 and for income tax from 6 April 2026 <sup><a href="#ref-5" id="cite-5">[5]</a></sup>.</p>

<p><strong>Worked example 2, the 14% fallback.</strong> Ben buys a used van for £15,000 and does not claim the AIA on it. In year one he claims 14% of £15,000, which is £2,100. In year two he claims 14% of the reducing balance of £12,900, which is £1,806. The relief continues at 14% of the falling balance each year, so it takes many years to write the cost down fully. That is why most landlords with enough profits prefer the AIA.</p>

<p><strong>Worked example 3, the straddling-period hybrid.</strong> If your accounting period spans the rate change, one period uses a blended rate. Finance Act 2026 s.28 sets a time-apportioned hybrid for a period that begins before and ends on or after the relevant day, found by adding (18 multiplied by the days before the change divided by the total days in the period) to (14 multiplied by the days on or after the change divided by the total days), rounded up to two decimal places. For a company with a year to 31 December 2026, roughly 90 days fall before 1 April 2026 and 275 after, giving a hybrid of about (18 × 90/365) + (14 × 275/365), which is approximately 14.99% for that one transitional period. Periods that start on or after the change use the clean 14%. For the full rate mechanics see our guide to <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/writing-down-allowance-rates">writing-down allowance rates</a>.</p>

<h2>The cash basis trap: when you cannot claim capital allowances at all</h2>
<p>This is the trap that catches sole-trader and partnership landlords most often. Under the cash basis, Capital Allowances Act 2001 s.1A says the person "is not entitled to any allowance" under the Act, with one carve-out, in subsection (4), for cars. The effect is counter-intuitive: a car stays inside the capital allowances regime, but a van does not. For a van bought on the cash basis you make no capital allowances claim at all. Instead the cost of the van is simply relieved as a deduction in the cash basis computation in the period you pay for it.</p>

<p>So if you are on the cash basis, the sections above on the AIA and the WDA do not apply to your van. You do not claim capital allowances; you deduct the cost as cash basis capital expenditure. If you are on the accruals basis (the traditional method), the AIA and WDA rules above are the ones that matter. The practical point is to know which basis you are on before you work out the van's tax treatment, because the answer is genuinely different.</p>

<h2>Buying a used van from a relative or your own company</h2>
<p>Connected-party purchases are where the old advice "you can only claim market value" goes wrong, so this section is worth reading carefully. The real rule is in the anti-avoidance regime at Capital Allowances Act 2001 ss.214 to 218. Section 214 applies where you (the buyer) enter into a relevant transaction with a seller and "B and S are connected with each other". Where it applies, s.217 provides that "no annual investment allowance or first-year allowance is to be made in respect of B's expenditure under the relevant transaction" <sup><a href="#ref-6" id="cite-6">[6]</a></sup>.</p>

<p>In plain terms: if you buy a used van from a connected person, you cannot claim the AIA on it, and you could not have claimed a first-year allowance anyway because it is second-hand. What you can still do is put the van into the main pool and claim the 14% writing-down allowance. You are not blocked from relief; you are just pushed onto the slower route.</p>

<p><strong>Worked example 4, connected-party purchase.</strong> Carol buys a van from a company she controls for £12,000. Because she and the company are connected, the AIA is denied. The £12,000 goes into her main pool and attracts the 14% WDA, so she claims £1,680 in year one and 14% of the reducing balance thereafter, rather than the full £12,000 up front.</p>

<p>A connected person includes a spouse or civil partner, a relative such as a parent, child or sibling, a business partner, and a company you control. If you are buying from someone close to you, or from a business you are involved in, assume the connected-party rule may apply and get the treatment confirmed before you complete.</p>

<h2>Selling or scrapping the van later: balancing charges</h2>
<p>Generous up-front relief has a tail. When you dispose of the van (a sale, a part-exchange, scrapping it, or simply taking it out of the business), the proceeds become a disposal value in your pool, capped at the amount of the original cost on which you claimed. If you had already claimed 100% through the AIA, there is little or no remaining pool value to absorb that disposal value, so it can create a balancing charge, which is a taxable addition to your profits for the year of disposal.</p>

<p><strong>Worked example 5, the balancing charge.</strong> Three years after fully relieving a used van through the AIA, Dan sells it for £4,000. Because the van's cost was already written off in full, the £4,000 of proceeds is brought in as a disposal value and produces a balancing charge of about £4,000, taxed as part of his property profits that year. This is the "free 100% now, partly clawed back on sale" point that most competitor guides leave out entirely. It does not make the AIA a bad idea, but it should shape your planning when you come to replace the van.</p>

<h2>Vans versus cars for a used vehicle</h2>
<p>The van-versus-car distinction is sharper for a used vehicle than most people expect. A van is not a car, so it qualifies for the AIA. A car is excluded from the AIA and from the 40% first-year allowance, and the only car first-year allowance is the 100% allowance for new, unused zero-emission cars, which a used car obviously cannot meet. A used car therefore gets no first-year relief at all and simply goes into a pool for writing-down allowances, at 14% a year if its CO2 emissions are 50g/km or below, or 6% a year in the special rate pool if they are above 50g/km.</p>

<p>So a used van gives you a 100% AIA deduction, while a used car gives you a slow 14% or 6% write-down. The risk is misclassification. HMRC can treat a dual-purpose vehicle, such as a double-cab pickup or a large car-derived SUV, as a car rather than a van, which would collapse the favourable treatment. Check the vehicle's classification before you rely on the van rules. For the car side of this, see our guide to the <a href="/blog/section-24-and-tax-relief/writing-down-allowance-cars">writing-down allowance on cars</a>.</p>

<h2>Sole trader, partnership or limited company: which route for a used van</h2>
<p>For a second hand van the answer is unusually consistent across structures, because the two reliefs that differ by structure (full expensing and the 40% allowance) are both off the table for a used asset.</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Sole traders and partnerships:</strong> the AIA (100%) is available on the accruals basis, with the WDA (14%) as the fallback. On the cash basis there are no capital allowances on a van; the cost is a cash deduction instead.</li>
<li><strong>Limited companies:</strong> the AIA (100%) is available in the same way. Full expensing and the 40% allowance are not, because the van is second-hand. Related companies in a group share a single AIA limit, so plan group purchases together.</li>
</ul>
<p>The structure question really only changes things when you are buying a new van, where companies have full expensing and others have the 40% allowance. <h2>Apportioning for private use and record-keeping</h2>
<p>If the van is used wholly and exclusively for the property business, you can claim in full. If you also use it privately, you must restrict the claim to the business-use proportion. For example, if 8,000 of 10,000 annual miles are business journeys, you claim 80% of the cost. The "wholly and exclusively" test and any private-use adjustment are matters HMRC will look at on enquiry, so the evidence matters.</p>
<p>Keep the purchase invoice and proof of payment, a record of the van's classification as a commercial vehicle, and a contemporaneous mileage log recording the date, purpose and miles of each business trip. A simple spreadsheet or a mileage app is enough. Without this, HMRC can reduce or reject the claim, and the apportionment is exactly the kind of detail that an enquiry tends to focus on.</p>

<h2>Common mistakes landlords make</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Claiming full expensing or the 40% allowance on a used van.</strong> The single biggest error. Both require an unused asset, so a second hand van is excluded from both. Use the AIA for the 100% instead.</li>
<li><strong>Claiming capital allowances on a van while on the cash basis.</strong> Under s.1A you cannot; cars are the only carve-out. The van's cost is a cash deduction, not a capital allowance.</li>
<li><strong>Mis-stating the connected-party rule as "claim limited to market value".</strong> The actual effect (ss.214 and 217) is that AIA and first-year allowances are denied and the van falls to the 14% pool.</li>
<li><strong>Forgetting the balancing charge on sale.</strong> A van fully relieved through the AIA can generate a taxable balancing charge when you sell it.</li>
<li><strong>Using the old 18% writing-down rate.</strong> The main-pool rate is 14% from April 2026 (Finance Act 2026 s.28).</li>
<li><strong>Confusing Section 24 with capital allowances.</strong> Section 24 is finance-cost relief on mortgage interest. It has no bearing on van capital allowances.</li>
</ul>

<p>Claiming capital allowances on a second hand van is a legitimate and often valuable deduction, but the right route depends on details that are easy to get wrong: which accounting basis you use, who you bought the van from, and what happens when you replace it. The recurring theme of this guide is that a used van keeps the 100% prize through the AIA, while losing access to full expensing and the 40% allowance, and that several traps (cash basis, connected parties, balancing charges) sit just under the surface.</p>
