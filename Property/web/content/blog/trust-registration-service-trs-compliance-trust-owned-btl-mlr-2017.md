    answer: "HMRC's published position at TRSM80020 (last updated 13 May 2026 at the time of writing) is a £5,000 maximum penalty per trust, applied on a case-by-case basis.     answer: "Almost never for landlord buy-to-let arrangements. The widely-circulated 'bare trusts are exempt' framing comes from a misreading of Schedule 3A.     answer: "No. This is a persistent misconception that appears in older practitioner content and informal commentary.     answer: "No, they are two separate regimes that must both be addressed for a taxable relevant trust. TRS registration under MLR 2017 reg 45 is the anti-money-laundering identification regime: HMRC holds the beneficial-ownership data centrally and grants access to law-enforcement and other supervisory bodies. Separately, TMA 1970 s.7 requires the trustees of a taxable trust to notify HMRC of liability and to file annual self-assessment trust returns (SA900) reporting the trust's income and gains. The two regimes use different forms, different deadlines (TRS 90 days from trust creation; SA900 31 October on paper or 31 January electronically following the tax year), and different sanctions (TRSM80020 £5,000 discretionary; SA900 late-filing under Sch 55 FA 2009). <h2>The reg 45 classes: which trusts must register?</h2>

<p><a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2017/692/regulation/45">Regulation 45 of the Money Laundering, Terrorist Financing and Transfer of Funds (Information on the Payer) Regulations 2017</a> (SI 2017/692) sets the registration perimeter. Three broad classes fall within scope.</p>

<p><strong>Class 1: taxable relevant trusts.</strong> Any UK trust whose trustees are liable for UK income tax, capital gains tax, inheritance tax, stamp duty land tax, or stamp duty reserve tax. This is the headline category for landlord-trust planning because rental-property trustees pay income tax on the rental profit and CGT on disposals. The Singh trust falls here from day one because the rental income from the three BTL properties flowed to the trustees from 1 March 2026.</p>

<p><strong>Class 2: non-taxable UK express trusts.</strong> Added by the Fifth Money Laundering Directive transposition with effect from 6 October 2020. An express trust is one created deliberately by a settlor (typically by written declaration of trust or under a will), as distinct from a constructive or resulting trust that arises by operation of law. Most landlord-trust planning works through express trusts because the settlor deliberately creates the structure; the non-taxable category catches the small minority of express trusts that have no current UK tax liability but still require registration. Schedule 3A exclusions (see below) carve out a narrow list of trust types.</p>

<p><strong>Class 3: non-UK trusts touching the UK.</strong> Non-UK-resident trusts must register where (a) they acquire UK land (residential or commercial), regardless of trustee residence, for post-1 October 2017 acquisitions, or (b) they have at least one UK-resident trustee while entering a UK business relationship. For landlord-trust planning, an offshore trust holding UK rental property is in this class from the date of acquisition; a UK-trustee-led offshore trust dealing with a UK letting agent or accountancy firm is in this class from the date of that business relationship.</p>

<p>The HMRC manual sets the operational detail at <a href="https://www.gov.uk/hmrc-internal-manuals/trust-registration-service-manual/trsm23020">TRSM23020 (registrable trusts under reg 45)</a> and <a href="https://www.gov.uk/hmrc-internal-manuals/trust-registration-service-manual/trsm24000">TRSM24000 (non-UK trusts under the UK-land-acquisition route)</a>. Both manual pages have been updated for the 5MLD overlay and for the post-2022 non-taxable trust rollout.</p>

<h2>The 90-day deadline: both at creation and on update</h2>

<p>Reg 45 sets the deadline at 90 days from the relevant trigger event. For a taxable relevant trust, the trigger is the trustees first becoming liable to UK tax or the trust being established, whichever is later. For a non-taxable UK express trust, the trigger is the trust being established. For a non-UK trust acquiring UK land, the trigger is the date of acquisition. For a non-UK trust entering a UK business relationship via a UK-resident trustee, the trigger is the date of that relationship.</p>

<p>The 90-day window applies in two directions: initial registration and ongoing updates. The update obligation under reg 45 requires the lead trustee to notify HMRC of any change to the trust's registered details within 90 days of the trustees becoming aware of the change. This rolling 90-day clock is, in practice, the most frequently missed compliance step in landlord-trust administration. A change of trustee, a new named beneficiary added to the class, a death of a settlor, a change in the trust's address for correspondence, or a material change in the asset profile (a property sold or acquired) all trigger the update obligation.</p>

<p>Two historical deadlines applied during the TRS rollout and are now closed: pre-6 April 2021 taxable trusts had a deadline of 31 January following the tax year of first liability; pre-4 June 2022 non-taxable trusts had a deadline of 1 September 2022. Trusts that should have registered then but did not are in late-registration territory now, with the penalty conversation framed by TRSM80020 mitigation (see below).</p>

<h2>The £5,000-discretionary penalty regime: reg 76 + TRSM80020</h2>

<p>The statutory hook for the TRS penalty is <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2017/692/regulation/76">MLR 2017 reg 76</a>, which authorises a designated supervisory authority (HMRC, in the TRS context) to impose a penalty of such amount as it considers appropriate, effective, proportionate, and dissuasive, with a mandatory exemption where the person took all reasonable steps and exercised all due diligence. Reg 76 does not set a fixed maximum.</p>

<p>The operational tariff sits in <a href="https://www.gov.uk/hmrc-internal-manuals/trust-registration-service-manual/trsm80020">HMRC's Trust Registration Service Manual at TRSM80020</a> (last updated 13 May 2026 at the time of writing). The verbatim wording is: "Where a trustee has failed to register a registrable trust a £5,000 penalty may be charged. Penalties for non-compliance will be applied on a case-by-case basis." The penalty is formally issued to the lead trustee. HMRC's published factors in determining the penalty (set out at TRSM80020 itself) include continued failure after warnings, providing inaccurate details with continued failure to amend, and the trustees' overall cooperation.</p>

<p>Three implications fall out of this architecture for landlord-trustee planning.</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>£5,000 is the maximum, not the standard.</strong> HMRC's discretion is case-by-case. First-omission cases voluntarily disclosed before HMRC discovery typically attract no monetary penalty or a substantially below-£5,000 figure. The discretionary nature of the tariff is the load-bearing reason that prompt self-correction is the right operational response.</li>
<li><strong>There is no graduated £100 / £200 / £300 tariff.</strong> Practitioner content that asserts a graduated escalator has confused TRS with the SA late-filing regime under TMA 1970 s.93 read with Sch 55 FA 2009 (where the £100 initial penalty plus 3-month, 6-month, and 12-month triggers do apply). The two regimes are statutorily separate and use different penalty architectures. Landlord-trustee planning that anchors on the wrong tariff structure under-estimates the headline maximum (£5,000 vs the £100-tier opener) and over-estimates the mechanical certainty (HMRC has no fixed escalator on TRS, just discretion).</li>
<li><strong>Lead-trustee designation matters operationally.</strong> The penalty is issued to the lead trustee in their capacity as such. Trust deeds frequently nominate the lead trustee; where they do not, the trustees should agree the designation in writing and document the appointment before TRS submission. Professional trustees frequently take the lead role because the operational discipline (calendar reminders, change-tracking, HMRC correspondence) is routine professional work.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Schedule 3A exclusions: narrow, not "bare trusts are exempt"</h2>

<p>The widespread practitioner shorthand that "bare trusts are exempt from TRS" is wrong as a general statement. <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2017/692/schedule/3A">Schedule 3A</a> sets out the express exclusions. The categories are tightly drawn.</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Legislative trusts.</strong> Trusts imposed or required by an enactment (statutory trusts arising automatically by force of legislation, not by deliberate settlement).</li>
<li><strong>Court-ordered trusts.</strong> Trusts created by, or to satisfy the terms of, an order of a court or tribunal.</li>
<li><strong>Pension scheme trusts.</strong> Trusts holding the assets of a registered pension scheme under Finance Act 2004 Part 4.</li>
<li><strong>Life-policy trusts</strong> (death-payable). Trusts of policies paying out on death, terminal or critical illness, permanent or temporary disablement, or healthcare costs. Also includes a trust of benefits payable on the death of the person assured under a retirement policy.</li>
<li><strong>Charitable trusts.</strong> Trusts registered as charities (or not required to register under the Charities Act 2011 in England and Wales, or registered as charities in Scotland or Northern Ireland).</li>
<li><strong>Pilot trusts.</strong> Trusts holding property valued at no more than £100 created before the applicable regulation date.</li>
<li><strong>Bank-account trusts for minors or those lacking capacity.</strong> Trusts created as account requirements for minors, persons lacking capacity, or those incapable of managing their property.</li>
<li><strong>Bare-trust death-proceeds trusts.</strong> Trusts where the trust holds only benefits received on the death of the assured under a qualifying policy, within two years of death.</li>
<li><strong>Co-ownership identity trusts.</strong> Trusts of jointly-held property where the trustees and the beneficiaries are the same persons. This carve-out is materially narrower than it sounds: it requires identity between the legal owners (trustees) and the beneficial owners (beneficiaries), which is the position for a simple joint legal-and-beneficial co-ownership but is not the position for a declaration of trust that separates legal title from beneficial entitlement (the standard mechanism in landlord-portfolio planning).</li>
</ul>

<p>None of these reach the standard landlord-trust scenario. A bare trust of BTL property held for the benefit of a beneficiary other than the legal owner is not a Schedule 3A excluded trust: the legal-and-beneficial identity carve-out does not apply (the bare trust deliberately separates legal title from beneficial entitlement), the life-policy carve-out does not apply (the trust holds real property, not a policy), and none of the other categories fit. The bare-trust transparency rules under TCGA 1992 s.60 (for CGT) and the corresponding income-tax treatment (rental income taxed on the beneficiary) affect who pays the tax; they do not affect whether the trust must register on TRS. <a href="https://www.gov.uk/hmrc-internal-manuals/trust-registration-service-manual/trsm32010">TRSM32010</a> walks the Schedule 3A categories in HMRC's own framing.</p>

<h2>Information you need before starting the TRS application</h2>

<p>The gov.uk TRS portal asks for six categories of information. Gathering them before opening the application is the difference between a 30-minute submission and a fragmented multi-session exercise.</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Trust details.</strong> Trust name, date of creation, governing law (England and Wales, Scotland, Northern Ireland, or non-UK), trust deed reference if any, trust type (discretionary, IIP, bare, IPDI, charitable, etc.), and the trust's correspondence address.</li>
<li><strong>Settlor details.</strong> Full legal name, date of birth, National Insurance number, nationality, and country of residence. Where the settlor has died, the date of death and last known address.</li>
<li><strong>Trustee details.</strong> Same identity fields for each trustee, plus the designation of the lead trustee.</li>
<li><strong>Beneficiary details.</strong> Named beneficiaries with identity data; class-only descriptions are accepted for discretionary trusts where the class is described in the trust deed. The Singh trust's class description (Anita's two adult children, three grandchildren, and any future grandchildren) is the standard format.</li>
<li><strong>Asset details.</strong> Each significant asset listed with description, market value at registration date, outstanding mortgages or other liabilities, and the basis of valuation. For a landlord trust, this is the property-address-and-value schedule plus any cash and investment holdings.</li>
<li><strong>Beneficial-owner-style declaration.</strong> Identification of the trust's beneficial owners (settlor, trustees, beneficiaries) under the MLR 2017 anti-money-laundering framework.</li>
</ul>

<h2>TRS vs HMRC trust-tax registration: the conflation that catches first-time settlors</h2>

<p>Two separate regimes apply to a taxable relevant trust and both must be addressed.</p>

<p><strong>TRS</strong> under MLR 2017 reg 45 is the anti-money-laundering identification regime. HMRC holds beneficial-ownership data centrally and grants access to law-enforcement bodies and other supervisors. The submission is via the gov.uk Trust Registration Service portal. The deadline is 90 days from trust establishment or first-liability trigger. The penalty regime is TRSM80020 £5,000 discretionary.</p>

<p><strong>Trust SA registration</strong> under TMA 1970 s.7 is the tax-administration regime. Trustees of a taxable trust must notify HMRC of liability and file annual self-assessment trust returns on form SA900 reporting the trust's income and gains. The notification deadline is 5 October following the tax year of first liability. The SA900 filing deadline is 31 October on paper or 31 January electronically following the tax year. Late-filing penalties follow the Sch 55 FA 2009 architecture (£100 initial, escalators at 3, 6, and 12 months), genuinely a graduated tariff and the source of the misattribution to TRS.</p>

<p>A common first-time-settlor misstep is to complete one of the two regimes and assume that the other is satisfied as a consequence. They are not. The Singh trust completed both registrations: TRS by 22 May 2026 (eight days inside the 90-day window) and SA notification on 28 July 2026 (well before the 5 October 2027 deadline for the 2026/27 tax year), with the SA900 itself due 31 January 2028.</p>

<h2>How TRS interacts with IPDI, bare, and FIC routes</h2>

<p>The four trust-route vehicles in our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/putting-rental-property-into-a-trust-decision-pillar-iht-cgt-sdlt-stack">four-vehicle decision pillar</a> each interact with TRS differently.</p>

<p><strong>IPDI trusts</strong> under IHTA 1984 s.49A are registrable taxable relevant trusts. The 90-day clock runs from the date the trustees become liable to UK tax, typically the date probate is granted and the rental income starts flowing through the IPDI. A common practical issue is sequencing: probate may complete weeks or months after death, and the trustees' formal liability may only crystallise once estate administration concludes. Lead trustees should anchor the 90-day clock to the rental-income-receipt date rather than the date of death and document the trigger event explicitly. See our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/immediate-post-death-interest-ipdi-rental-property-tax-iht-1984-s49a">depth page on IPDI rental-property tax</a> for the underlying IHT and income-tax mechanics that drive the registration obligation.</p>

<p><strong>Bare trusts</strong> for adult or minor beneficiaries holding rental property are registrable, as set out above. The bare-trust transparency does not avoid registration. Trustees of bare trusts created for minor-child IHT planning (the standard PET-clock-starting structure) should diarise the registration as a baseline compliance step.</p>

<p><strong>Discretionary trusts</strong> like the Singh trust are the headline registrable category. The settlor-interested exclusion under TCGA 1992 ss.169B to 169G affects whether the s.260 CGT holdover is available on entry, not whether the trust must register; even a settlor-interested discretionary trust is fully within reg 45 (and indeed often more visible to HMRC because the income attribution under ITTOIA 2005 s.624 creates an additional SA-side reporting trigger). See our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/settlor-interested-trust-iht-s49-1a-cgt-s169b-property-attribution-rules">settlor-interested trust three-statute attribution rules</a> for the parallel income-tax and CGT mechanics.</p>

<p><strong>Family Investment Companies</strong> are not trusts and do not register on TRS. The FIC has its own beneficial-ownership disclosure regime through Companies House and the PSC (Person of Significant Control) register, which is the corporate-side analogue of TRS. Where a trust holds shares in an FIC (a hybrid trust-owned-FIC structure), the trust registers on TRS and the FIC registers on Companies House with its PSCs; the two regimes overlap on the beneficial-ownership data but operate independently. See our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/fic-vs-discretionary-trust-property-comparison">FIC vs discretionary trust comparison</a> for the structural detail and our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/bare-trust-vs-nominee-company-vs-formal-trust-decision-property-investors">bare trust vs nominee company vs formal trust decision</a> for the bare-trust mechanics.</p>

<h2>Late registration: rescue mechanics and the penalty-mitigation conversation</h2>

<p>A trust that has missed the 90-day deadline is not in a hopeless position. The discretionary architecture of TRSM80020 means that the right operational sequence reduces the penalty exposure substantially.</p>

<ol>
<li><strong>Register now.</strong> Complete the TRS submission on the gov.uk portal as soon as the omission is identified. The longer the trust remains unregistered, the harder the mitigation conversation becomes.</li>
<li><strong>Document the chronology.</strong> Why was the deadline missed? Common reasons include genuine misunderstanding of the 5MLD overlay for older non-taxable express trusts, change in trusteeship without proper handover, confusion between TRS and SA registration, or simple inadvertence in a family-administered trust. The chronology is the input to HMRC's case-by-case mitigation.</li>
<li><strong>Record cooperative steps.</strong> Any prior compliance steps the trustees took (SA registration, IHT 100 filings on entry, professional advice taken on the trust's tax position) help frame the trust as broadly compliant with a single TRS-side gap.</li>
<li><strong>Engage promptly if HMRC opens a penalty conversation.</strong> The lead trustee should respond inside HMRC's stated window with the chronology and the cooperation record. Reasonable-excuse framing under the general MLR 2017 reg 76 due-diligence defence is the right baseline; even where it does not fully exonerate, it materially reduces the penalty.</li>
</ol>

<p>In practice, first-omission cases voluntarily disclosed before HMRC discovery typically resolve with no monetary penalty or a substantially below-£5,000 figure. The cost of late-registration plus a managed penalty conversation is invariably lower than the cost of HMRC-initiated enquiry plus penalty plus potential ongoing scrutiny. HMRC's discovery channels have widened since 2024 with integration of the TRS data feed with SA records and the Land Registry's enhanced trust-flagging on title; trustees of older trusts that pre-date TRS awareness should treat the question "is this trust on TRS?" as a priority compliance audit item rather than waiting for an HMRC letter.</p>

<p>The Singh trust will not face any of this because the 90-day window was met. The harder cases in our practice are older landlord trusts (typically 2018 to 2021 vintages) where the settlors registered for SA properly, paid the entry IHT properly, and dealt with the 10-year periodic charges properly, but missed the TRS rollout entirely. The right answer in every case we have seen is the same: register now, document the chronology, engage on the mitigation conversation.</p>
