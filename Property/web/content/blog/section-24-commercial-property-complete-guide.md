<p>Section 24 commercial property rules are fundamentally different from residential property restrictions. Unlike residential buy-to-let landlords who face mortgage interest relief caps, commercial property investors can still claim full mortgage interest as a business expense.</p>

<p>This distinction makes commercial property increasingly attractive as the <a href="/blog/section-24-and-tax-relief/section-24-tax-relief-complete-guide">Section 24 restrictions</a> continue to impact residential landlords. However, commercial property investment comes with its own tax complexities and compliance requirements.</p>

<h2>Why Section 24 Doesn't Apply to Commercial Property</h2>

<p>Section 24 legislation specifically targets residential property lettings. The rules introduced in Finance Act 2017 restrict mortgage interest relief to the basic rate of tax (20%) for residential rental properties.</p>

<p>Commercial property falls outside this scope because it's treated as a business activity rather than property investment. Commercial landlords operate under different tax rules that recognise the business nature of their activities.</p>

<p>This means a commercial landlord with £200,000 mortgage interest can claim the full amount as a business expense, whereas a residential landlord would be limited to a 20% tax credit regardless of their marginal tax rate.</p>

<h2>What Qualifies as Commercial Property</h2>

<p>HMRC defines commercial property as premises used wholly for business purposes. This includes:</p>

<ul>
<li>Office buildings and suites</li>
<li>Retail shops and shopping centres</li>
<li>Warehouses and industrial units</li>
<li>Hotels and guest houses (when run as a business)</li>
<li>Restaurants, pubs, and leisure facilities</li>
<li>Mixed-use properties (commercial ground floor, residential upper floors)</li>
</ul>

<p>The key test is whether the property generates income from business activities rather than simple residential occupation. Even a single office let to a business tenant qualifies for full commercial treatment.</p>

<h3>Mixed-Use Properties and Section 24</h3>

<p>Mixed-use properties require careful analysis. If you own a building with commercial units on the ground floor and residential flats above, you'll need to apportion expenses:</p>

<ul>
<li>Commercial portion: full mortgage interest relief</li>
<li>Residential portion: Section 24 restrictions apply</li>
</ul>

<p>This apportionment typically follows floor space or rental value proportions. Professional advice is essential for mixed-use properties to ensure correct treatment.</p>

<h2>Commercial Landlord Mortgage Interest Relief</h2>

<p>Commercial landlords benefit from full mortgage interest relief as a business expense. This applies whether you operate as:</p>

<ul>
<li>A sole trader with commercial property income</li>
<li>A partnership letting commercial premises</li>
<li>A limited company owning commercial property</li>
</ul>

<p>Unlike residential property, there's no restriction on the level of relief available. A higher-rate taxpayer saving 40% on mortgage interest continues to receive this full benefit on commercial property investments.</p>

<h3>How This Affects Tax Planning</h3>

<p>The absence of Section 24 restrictions on business property section 24 rules creates significant tax planning opportunities:</p>

<p><strong>Leverage advantages:</strong> Commercial property investors can still benefit from the full tax shield of mortgage interest, making leveraged investments more attractive than residential equivalents.</p>

<p><strong>Portfolio balance:</strong> Some landlords are shifting from residential to commercial property to avoid Section 24 impacts while maintaining property investment returns.</p>

<p><strong>Incorporation decisions:</strong> The <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/buy-to-let-limited-company-complete-guide-uk">incorporation benefits</a> for commercial property differ from residential property due to the different Section 24 treatment.</p>

<h2>Other Tax Differences for Commercial Property</h2>

<p>Beyond Section 24 exemption, commercial property enjoys several other tax advantages:</p>

<h3>Capital Allowances</h3>

<p>Commercial property qualifies for capital allowances on fixtures and fittings. This includes:</p>

<ul>
<li>Heating and lighting systems</li>
<li>Lifts and escalators</li>
<li>Fitted furniture and equipment</li>
<li>Security systems</li>
</ul>

<p>These allowances provide immediate tax relief on capital expenditure, something not available for residential rental properties.</p>

<h3>VAT Considerations</h3>

<p>Commercial property can be subject to VAT through the "option to tax" election. While this adds complexity, it can provide cash flow benefits through VAT recovery on purchases and improvements.</p>

<p>Most residential property lettings are VAT-exempt, so this represents another area where commercial property offers different tax treatment.</p>

<h3>Business Rates vs Council Tax</h3>

<p>Commercial properties are subject to business rates rather than council tax. While this is often more expensive, business rates are deductible business expenses, whereas council tax on residential rental properties must be borne by either landlord or tenant.</p>

<h2>From April 2027: New Property Income Tax Rates</h2>

<p>From April 2027, the UK introduces separate property income tax rates of 22% (basic), 42% (higher), and 47% (additional rate). These rates apply specifically to residential property income.</p>

<p>Commercial property income will continue to be taxed at standard income tax rates (20%, 40%, 45%), providing another advantage for commercial property investors.</p>

<p>This change further widens the tax treatment gap between residential and commercial property investments.</p>

<h2>Common Commercial Property Tax Mistakes</h2>

<p>Several errors commonly occur with commercial property tax treatment:</p>

<h3>Incorrect Property Classification</h3>

<p>Some landlords incorrectly classify serviced apartments or holiday lets as commercial property. These typically remain residential for tax purposes despite business-like operation.</p>

<h3>Mixed-Use Apportionment Errors</h3>

<p>Failing to properly apportion expenses between commercial and residential elements of mixed-use properties can lead to incorrect Section 24 applications.</p>

<h3>Capital Allowances Oversights</h3>

<p>Not claiming available capital allowances on commercial property represents missed tax relief opportunities. Professional property accountants often identify significant unclaimed allowances.</p>

<h2>Planning Considerations for Commercial Property Investors</h2>

<p>The Section 24 exemption for commercial property creates several planning opportunities:</p>

<h3>Portfolio Diversification</h3>

<p>Adding commercial property to a residential portfolio can improve overall tax efficiency. The combination of full mortgage interest relief and different tax treatment provides balance against Section 24 impacts.</p>

<h3>Timing of Acquisitions</h3>

<p>With residential property facing ongoing Section 24 restrictions and higher SDLT surcharge rates, commercial property acquisitions may offer better after-tax returns.</p>

<h3>Financing Structures</h3>

<p>The ability to claim full mortgage interest relief makes leveraged commercial property investments more attractive than equivalent residential investments for higher-rate taxpayers.</p>
