<p>The UK Register of Overseas Entities has been through three distinct rounds of expansion since it was created in 2022. The 2025 round is the most significant from a disclosure-of-trust-data perspective. For overseas-entity owners of UK property where the beneficial chain runs through a trust, the practical reality is that trustee identity, trust name, trust type, and beneficiary categories that were previously restricted to law enforcement and legitimate-interest applicants are now structurally accessible to the public.</p>

<p>This is a news-led compliance topic, but the structural backbone is the three-layer timeline that places the 2025 expansion in its proper context. Most amateur and competitor content treats this as a single piece of news without explaining the underlying architecture. The architecture matters because the operational obligations on affected entities (annual updates, identity verification, redaction applications) all flow from the layered statutory framework.</p>

<h2>The three-layer timeline</h2>

<h3>Layer 1: ECTEA 2022 baseline</h3>

<p>The <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2022/10" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">Economic Crime (Transparency and Enforcement) Act 2022</a> (ECTEA 2022) Part 1 ss.4-31 established the Register of Overseas Entities in force from 1 August 2022. The architecture:</p>

<ul>
<li><a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2022/10/section/4" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">ECTEA 2022 s.4</a>: application for registration. Overseas entities owning or seeking to acquire UK property must apply to be entered on the register.</li>
<li><a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2022/10/section/7" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">ECTEA 2022 s.7</a>: updating duty. Annual update statement filed within 14 days after the end of each "update period" (defined in s.7), confirming the registered information remains current or notifying changes.</li>
<li><a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2022/10/section/8" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">ECTEA 2022 s.8</a>: failure to comply with updating duty. A criminal offence for the entity and every officer in default, with daily-default mechanic.</li>
<li><a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2022/10/section/34" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">ECTEA 2022 s.34</a>: power to require overseas entity to register if it owns certain UK land. The Secretary of State's compulsory-registration-notice power for unregistered overseas entities.</li>
<li><a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2002/9/schedule/4A" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">LRA 2002 Schedule 4A</a>: HMLR disposition-block. Non-compliant overseas entities cannot transfer, charge, or grant leases over seven years on UK property held by them. This is the practical commercial constraint that drives most compliance behaviour.</li>
</ul>

<p>At Layer 1 the architecture caught the overseas entity itself. Trust information sitting in the beneficial-ownership chain was filed but was accessible only to law enforcement and AML supervisors, plus members of the public who could demonstrate a legitimate interest on application (a process largely opaque in practice).</p>

<h3>Layer 2: ECCTA 2023 amendments, commenced 1 March 2024</h3>

<p>The <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2023/56" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">Economic Crime and Corporate Transparency Act 2023</a> Part 3 amended ECTEA 2022 in several respects. The RoE-relevant amendments commenced on 1 March 2024 per ECCTA Schedule 2. The key changes:</p>

<ul>
<li>Broadened beneficial-ownership disclosure requirements for overseas entities.</li>
<li>Aligned RoE beneficial-owner definitions with the persons-with-significant-control (PSC) framework for UK companies.</li>
<li>Introduced anti-avoidance provisions for nominee arrangements that previously diluted disclosure.</li>
<li>Tightened the trust-information disclosure architecture for entities with trust-chain beneficial owners.</li>
<li>Inserted the identity-verification regime applicable to individuals named in filings, through Companies Act 2006 Part 11A as added by ECCTA.</li>
</ul>

<p>At Layer 2 the depth of information captured on the register increased. Public-versus-restricted access remained largely as at Layer 1: the entity-level information was public; the trust-chain information was restricted.</p>

<h3>Layer 3: 2025 expansion of public access to trust data</h3>

<p>The 2025 expansion regulations, made by the Secretary of State under ECCTA 2023 amending powers, broadened public access to trust information held on the RoE. The previous architecture limited public access to trust information to specifically-applying members of the public who could demonstrate a legitimate interest. The expansion opened structured public access to categories of trust information that include trustee identity, trust name, trust type, and beneficiary names or classes.</p>

<p>The expansion was preceded by a Department for Business and Trade and Companies House consultation in 2024 and reflected the policy choice of preserving (and extending) the UK's broad-public-access posture in contrast to the post-Sovim EU narrowing. The redaction architecture for personal safety and vulnerable persons was preserved.</p>

<p>For the precise SI reference, commencement date, and operative scope, verify against legislation.gov.uk before relying on specific scope claims. The architecture above is stable; the SI specifics are the verification point.</p>

<aside>
<p>The three-layer timeline matters because each layer adds obligations on top of the previous. ECTEA 2022 created the register; ECCTA 2023 deepened it and inserted identity verification; the 2025 expansion opened trust-chain visibility to the public. Affected entities live within the cumulative architecture.</p>

<h2>The RoE versus TRS register distinction</h2>

<p>The single most common point of confusion in amateur and competitor content on this topic is the conflation of the RoE with the Trust Registration Service. They are entirely separate registers.</p>

<table>
<tr><th>Feature</th><th>Register of Overseas Entities (RoE)</th><th>Trust Registration Service (TRS)</th></tr>
<tr><td>Statutory basis</td><td>ECTEA 2022 and ECCTA 2023</td><td>Money Laundering Regulations 2017 SI 2017/692 reg 45</td></tr>
<tr><td>Administrator</td><td>Companies House</td><td>HMRC</td></tr>
<tr><td>What it captures</td><td>Beneficial ownership of overseas entities owning UK property</td><td>Trust-side beneficial ownership information for UK and certain non-UK trusts</td></tr>
<tr><td>Public access posture</td><td>Structured public, with redactions (now expanded for trust data)</td><td>Not publicly accessible; restricted to HMRC, law enforcement, AML supervisors, and legitimate-interest applicants</td></tr>
<tr><td>Filing trigger</td><td>Overseas entity owns or seeks to acquire UK property</td><td>Trust meets the trigger conditions under MLR 2017 reg 45 (UK express trust, certain non-UK trusts with UK touchpoints)</td></tr>
<tr><td>Update cycle</td><td>Annual update statement under ECTEA s.7</td><td>Ongoing change notification under MLR 2017 reg 45 plus periodic confirmation</td></tr>
</table>

<p>A single trust holding a non-UK company that owns UK property will typically need both filings. The Davies-family worked example below illustrates the parallel obligations.</p>

<h2>The Davies-family Jersey-trust worked example</h2>

<p>Persona: a UK-based accountant acts for the Davies family, who are Jersey-resident. The structure:</p>

<ul>
<li>£3,500,000 London residential investment townhouse held as a buy-to-let.</li>
<li>The townhouse is owned by Jersey Property Holdings Ltd, a Jersey company.</li>
<li>Shares of Jersey Property Holdings Ltd are held by Jersey trustees of "The Davies Family Discretionary Trust", a Jersey-law settlor-discretionary trust.</li>
<li>Beneficiaries of the trust: Mr Davies (senior), Mrs Davies, two adult children, three grandchildren (one under 18).</li>
<li>The trust was settled in 2018 by Mr Davies, who was non-UK-domiciled at the time and remains non-LTR under the post-Finance Act 2025 s.6A long-term-resident architecture.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Pre-2025 RoE position (post-ECCTA 2023, from 1 March 2024)</h3>

<ul>
<li>Jersey Property Holdings Ltd is registered on the RoE (since 2023). Entity-level identifying information (registered office, jurisdiction, director identity) is public by default.</li>
<li>Trust information filed in connection with the registration but accessible only to HMRC, law enforcement, AML supervisors, and specifically-applying members of the public who can demonstrate legitimate interest.</li>
<li>Separately, the Davies trust is registered on the HMRC Trust Registration Service (TRS), with non-public access via MLR 2017 reg 45.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Post-2025 expansion position</h3>

<ul>
<li>Jersey Property Holdings Ltd remains registered on the RoE (no change at entity level).</li>
<li>Trustee names, the trust name ("The Davies Family Discretionary Trust"), the trust type (discretionary), and beneficiary class information are now publicly accessible on the RoE in structured form, subject to the redaction architecture.</li>
<li>The under-18 grandchild's residential address and full date of birth remain redacted under the personal safety and vulnerable-persons safeguards.</li>
<li>TRS-side filings remain non-public; no change to the TRS regime.</li>
</ul>

<aside>
<p>The Davies family's structure looks similar from outside, but the public-disclosure profile has changed materially. A journalist, a researcher, or a casual interested party can now look up the trust name and trustee identity from the RoE entry on Jersey Property Holdings Ltd, where previously the same information sat behind a legitimate-interest gate.</p>

<h3>Practical advisory actions</h3>

<p>For the UK-based accountant acting for the Davies family, four practical actions:</p>

<ol>
<li><strong>Brief the family.</strong> A face-to-face or written explanation of what is now publicly accessible, with specific examples of how a third party would access the information. Most non-UK families assume the RoE behaves like a private register; the public posture surprises them.</li>
<li><strong>Assess redaction grounds.</strong> Where any beneficiary has credible personal safety concerns (high-net-worth visibility risks, threats received, vulnerable-person status), the redaction routes should be engaged with supporting evidence. Applications go to Companies House.</li>
<li><strong>Align with the ACSP.</strong> The Authorised Corporate Service Provider submitting the family's RoE filings handles the operational paperwork. The accountant coordinates with the ACSP to ensure the next update statement reflects the family's understanding and any redaction applications are in flight.</li>
<li><strong>Document the audit trail.</strong> A signed acknowledgment from the principal family members confirming they understand the disclosure position. This is both client-protection (clear understanding of public posture) and adviser-protection (record of the briefing in the event of subsequent dispute).</li>
</ol>

<h2>The redaction architecture</h2>

<p>The 2025 expansion preserves redaction routes for narrow safety and vulnerability grounds:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Personal safety.</strong> Where the registered party has a credible risk of serious harm (threats received, public-profile risks, abusive ex-partner situations). Evidence-based application.</li>
<li><strong>Vulnerable persons.</strong> Typically minors and adults at significant risk. Operates through the parent or guardian's application.</li>
<li><strong>Residential addresses.</strong> Default redaction available; service addresses substitute.</li>
<li><strong>Full dates of birth.</strong> Day and month redacted; year of birth typically remains visible for identification.</li>
</ul>

<p>The Secretary of State retains discretion to restrict on application in cases falling outside the standard redaction categories. The presumption is in favour of public access; redaction is the carve-out. Preference alone is not a redaction ground.</p>

<h2>The CJEU Sovim non-application</h2>

<p>In November 2022 the Court of Justice of the European Union ruled in joined cases C-37/20 and C-601/20 (commonly cited as <em>Luxembourg Business Registers</em> or <em>Sovim</em>) that the EU 5th Money Laundering Directive's mandate for unconditional public access to beneficial-ownership information was disproportionate. EU member states have narrowed public access since.</p>

<p>The UK left the EU before that judgment. CJEU rulings do not bind the UK post-Brexit. The UK regime has evolved independently and the 2025 expansion EXPANDS rather than restricts public access. This is a deliberate divergence from the post-Sovim EU position and reflects the UK government's policy choice to prioritise transparency over the privacy interest that Sovim recognised.</p>

<p>For cross-border structures with both UK and EU touchpoints, the divergence matters in practice. The same trust may be publicly disclosed on the UK RoE and privately registered on the equivalent EU member-state register. Reconciling the parallel obligations is an operational reality of cross-border family structures.</p>

<h2>The identity-verification overlay</h2>

<p>Per Companies Act 2006 Part 11A (inserted by ECCTA 2023), individuals named in RoE filings face the same identity-verification requirements as UK-company directors and PSCs. The operational mechanism is the Authorised Corporate Service Provider (ACSP) framework:</p>

<ul>
<li>Only ACSP-registered firms can submit identity verification on behalf of others.</li>
<li>Most UK-based accountants and advisers acting for cross-border family clients need to be ACSP-registered to handle filings for clients.</li>
<li>Direct submission by individuals is possible but operationally fiddly for non-UK-resident parties; the ACSP route is the standard.</li>
</ul>

<p>The identity-verification regime sits alongside the disclosure expansion. Both apply concurrently. For affected entities, the operational picture is: ACSP-handled submission, identity-verified individuals, expanded trust-data disclosure, available redactions, annual update cycle.</p>

<h2>Compliance consequences of non-compliance</h2>

<aside>
<p>The HMLR disposition-block is the lever that drives most compliance behaviour. Where an overseas entity falls non-compliant, the practical commercial constraint (no sale, no charge, no long lease) is usually decisive long before any criminal-offence or civil-penalty consideration kicks in.</p>

<p>The compliance regime has four layers:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>HMLR disposition-block (LRA 2002 Sch 4A).</strong> The decisive commercial constraint. A non-compliant overseas entity cannot transfer, charge, or grant leases over seven years on UK property held by it. Buyers' solicitors will not complete on transactions involving non-compliant entities; lenders will not lend against UK property held by non-compliant entities.</li>
<li><strong>Criminal offences (ECTEA s.8).</strong> Both the entity and every officer in default commit a criminal offence on failure to comply with the updating duty. Conviction carries a fine and a daily default fine. Director and officer liability is personal.</li>
<li><strong>Civil penalties.</strong> Separate Penalties Regulations Statutory Instrument sets the civil penalty quantum. Typical structure: an initial penalty (in the region of £2,500) and a continuing daily-default penalty (in the region of £500 per day of continuing default).</li>
<li><strong>Reputational and tax exposure.</strong> Compliance flags trigger downstream HMRC enquiry and AML supervisor attention. The reputational cost in cross-border family contexts often exceeds the headline penalties.</li>
</ul>

<h2>The LTR-trust-pivot interaction</h2>

<p>The post-Finance Act 2025 long-term-resident architecture (replacing the pre-2025 domicile-based excluded-property regime under the new s.6A long-term-resident test) is a SEPARATE compliance overlay. The RoE expansion sits alongside the LTR pivot but is procedurally distinct.</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>RoE governs WHAT IS PUBLICLY DISCLOSED.</strong> The 2025 expansion changes the disclosure architecture for trust information.</li>
<li><strong>LTR governs WHETHER THE TRUST QUALIFIES for IHT excluded-property treatment.</strong> The pre-2025 protected-trust regime was domicile-based; the post-FA-2025 architecture is residence-based via the s.6A LTR test. The transition rules for pre-6-April-2025-settled trusts by then-non-domiciled settlors are intricate.</li>
<li><strong>Both apply concurrently.</strong> The 2025 RoE expansion does not change tax treatment; the post-FA-2025 LTR architecture does not change disclosure obligations.</li>
</ul>

<h2>What to do now if you are affected</h2>

<p>Affected entities (typically overseas-entity owners of UK property where the beneficial chain runs through a trust) should run a four-step compliance review:</p>

<ol>
<li><strong>Review the trust-chain architecture.</strong> Identify which trust information is now publicly accessible under the 2025 expansion versus what remains restricted. Most cross-border family structures have not run this review since the original 2022 / 2023 / 2024 layered architecture was put in place.</li>
<li><strong>Engage redaction routes where applicable.</strong> Personal safety, vulnerable persons, and the standard residential-address and date-of-birth redactions. Supporting evidence must be prepared.</li>
<li><strong>Align with the ACSP submitting your filings.</strong> Confirm the next annual update statement will reflect accurate information and that any redaction applications are coordinated.</li>
<li><strong>Document the audit trail.</strong> Client-side acknowledgment of the disclosure position; adviser-side record of the briefing; structural review notes maintained for future updates.</li>
</ol>

<p>The expansion is not a one-off event. It changes the standing disclosure posture for affected entities and requires ongoing attention as future filings happen.</p>

<h2>The bigger picture</h2>

<p>The UK's Register of Overseas Entities has moved decisively in the direction of public disclosure across the three rounds of expansion since 2022. The 2025 expansion is the most significant from a trust-information perspective. For cross-border family structures using non-UK companies to hold UK property, with beneficial ownership running through one or more trusts, the disclosure profile has materially changed.</p>

<p>The deliberate divergence from the post-Sovim EU position underlines the policy direction. Where the EU has narrowed public access on privacy grounds, the UK has expanded it on transparency grounds. The redaction architecture preserves narrow personal-safety carve-outs; the default posture is public.</p>

<p>For affected entities the operational reality is the ACSP-mediated submission, the identity-verified individuals, the expanded trust-data disclosure, the available redactions where evidenced, and the annual update cycle. The compliance leverage sits with the HMLR disposition-block: non-compliance closes commercial transactions, regardless of any wider penalty exposure.</p>

<p>The RoE-versus-TRS register distinction remains the single most important conceptual point for advisers in this space. Two registers, two administrators, two public-access postures, often two filings. Conflating them is the most common amateur error and the one that leads to compliance gaps.</p>
