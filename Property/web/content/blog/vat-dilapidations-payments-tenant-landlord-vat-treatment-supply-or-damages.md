<p>At the lease-end handover the VAT question on dilapidations finally has to be answered. Surveyor on site, schedule of dilapidations served, the tenant's counter-quote in the file, a number on the table both sides can live with. Then the invoice question: do you add 20 percent VAT to the settlement, or does the agreed sum stand alone outside the scope of VAT entirely? On a £180,000 settlement that is £36,000 of VAT either charged or not charged, and the money flows very differently for each side depending on the answer.</p>

<p>HMRC's published position has been stable for years and survived the wider 2020 and 2022 reset of the compensation-payments framework. VATSC05910 treats genuine dilapidations as normally outside the scope of VAT. The reasoning in the manual is that a dilapidations settlement lacks the reciprocal link to a supply that VAT requires: the tenant pays for breaching a contractual obligation to maintain, not for any further service you provide in return. Below is the test as it stands in 2026, the evidence that defends an outside-scope treatment, the narrow fact-patterns that can push the payment back inside the scope of VAT, and the lease-drafting choices that protect your position from day one.</p>

<h2>The Lease-End Moment: A VAT Question Most Parties Get Wrong</h2>

<p>Both sides at a commercial lease end care about the dilapidations VAT question, but their accountants often arrive at different answers. As the landlord, you usually want the settlement outside the scope of VAT, because if you have opted to tax on the unit a taxable settlement creates real cashflow friction (output VAT due on your next return, with no equivalent recovery). A tenant who is VAT-registered and fully recovering is indifferent: VAT charged is VAT recovered. A tenant who is partially exempt or not registered has a strong preference for the outside-scope outcome, because to that tenant VAT charged is a real cost.</p>

<p>The settlements that go wrong are the ones drafted in haste at the end of a contested negotiation, where the agreed sum bundles dilapidations, mesne profits for holding over, an in-lieu-of-rent amount for the final quarter, and sometimes a surrender premium. Each head of payment has its own VAT analysis. A composite settlement that lumps them together is exactly the document HMRC will pull apart if a later compliance check picks up the file.</p>

<aside>
<p>Avoid a settlement that bundles heads</p>
<p>If your settlement document combines dilapidations with rent in arrears or a surrender premium, ask your adviser to split the heads explicitly before signing. Mixed settlements are the single most common source of dilapidations VAT disputes.</p>
</aside>

<h2>The Default HMRC Position Until 2020: Compensation Sits Outside VAT</h2>

<p>Before September 2020, HMRC's general framework for compensation payments treated most of them as outside the scope of VAT on the reasoning that compensation does not have the direct link to a supply that VAT requires. The position held for early termination fees, contract cancellation fees, liquidated damages, and dilapidations. The legal anchor was the reciprocity test derived from the Court of Justice decisions in Apple and Pear Development Council (C-102/86), Tolsma (C-16/93), and the cases following them.</p>

<p>For dilapidations the practical reasoning was straightforward. The tenant has a contractual obligation to maintain the property to a defined standard throughout the term. At lease end you either take back a property in the agreed condition (no dilapidations claim) or in a worse one (a claim arises). The tenant's payment restores you to the position the lease promised, no more. You supply nothing in return beyond what was already in the original lease grant. HMRC concluded that this absence of a separate reciprocal supply put the payment outside the scope of VAT.</p>

<h2>What Brief 12/2020 Changed and What Brief 2/2022 Refined</h2>

<p>Revenue and Customs Brief 12/2020 (published 2 September 2020) signalled a sharp reset on early termination fees and compensation payments. Following the Court of Justice decisions in MEO (C-295/17) and Vodafone Portugal (C-43/19), HMRC took the view that many payments described as compensation were in substance further consideration for the original supply, and so within the scope of VAT and standard-rated where the original supply was standard-rated. The Brief was retrospective in tone and unsettled practitioners through late 2020 and 2021.</p>

<p>Revenue and Customs Brief 2/2022 (published 7 February 2022) replaced Brief 12/2020 with a settled, prospective statement. The replacement Brief confirmed the look-through-to-contract test for early termination, contract cancellation, and similar compensation, but it did not extend that test to dilapidations payments at the end of commercial property leases. The VATSC manual at VATSC05910 sets out the residual position: genuine dilapidations remain normally outside the scope of VAT, because they lack the reciprocal link to a supply that VAT requires, and the manual flags only narrow fact-patterns (in particular evidence of value-shifting to avoid VAT) where HMRC will look through to a different analysis.</p>

<p>The practical effect is a two-track framework. Early lease surrender payments now follow the Brief 2/2022 look-through and are VATable where you have opted to tax. Dilapidations settlements continue to follow the VATSC05910 outside-scope analysis. Any settlement that mixes the two heads has to be split.</p>

<h2>The Supply-vs-Damages Diagnostic</h2>

<p>Four questions, in order, settle the VAT treatment of a lease-end payment. The first one that gives a clear answer decides it, and the rest fall away.</p>

<ol>
<li><strong>Is there a separate reciprocal supply?</strong> You must be providing something distinct (a release from a forward repair obligation, a regrant, access to a part of the property the tenant did not previously have) in exchange for the payment. If the answer is no (you simply take back a property in worse condition than the lease required), the payment is compensation and outside scope.</li>
<li><strong>Is the payment quantified by reference to a contractor's cost of reinstatement, or by reference to time or occupation?</strong> A cost-of-reinstatement quantification points to compensation (the payment makes you whole, with no profit element). A time-or-occupation quantification (so many months of rent equivalent) points to consideration for occupation and sits comfortably inside the scope of VAT.</li>
<li><strong>Does the documentation support the label?</strong> Surveyor's schedule, contractor quotes, and the lease's repair covenants together demonstrate that the parties are compensating for breach. A settlement letter that simply states a number without supporting evidence is weaker on the diagnostic, even if the underlying facts would support an outside-scope treatment.</li>
<li><strong>Is there evidence of value-shifting?</strong> Where the parties have artificially relabelled what is in substance rent or a surrender premium as dilapidations, HMRC will reclassify. The fact-pattern to avoid is a dilapidations sum that exceeds the supporting surveyor's quote by a material margin and that arrives at completion of a deal in which the parties are also negotiating rent or surrender terms.</li>
</ol>

<aside>
<p>Test before signing, not after</p>
<p>Walk the four-question diagnostic before the settlement is signed. If question two or four is shaky, restructure the settlement, do not just relabel it. HMRC looks through labels.</p>
</aside>

<h2>How VATSC05910 Treats Dilapidations Specifically</h2>

<p>VATSC05910 is the published position you can point HMRC to. It states that dilapidations are normally outside the scope of VAT because they lack the reciprocal link to a supply that VAT requires. It accepts that dilapidations could in theory be additional consideration in specific circumstances, but confirms HMRC will not treat them that way unless the evidence suggests value-shifting to avoid VAT.</p>

<p>The real force of the manual is the carve-out from the wider compensation framework. A different head of payment (early termination, contract cancellation, an extension fee, a contract variation fee) might be analysed under Brief 2/2022 and the MEO line of cases, but dilapidations keep their own settled treatment. The two frameworks coexist, so a settlement that bundles dilapidations with another head needs the analysis split.</p>

<h2>Evidence That Keeps a Dilapidations Settlement Outside VAT</h2>

<p>The evidence package that defends an outside-scope position in a later HMRC review is built up at three points: lease grant, lease term, and lease end.</p>

<p><strong>At lease grant.</strong> A clear full-repairing-and-insuring covenant, attached Schedule of Condition where the property is taken in a non-pristine state, and an explicit dilapidations clause referencing the statutory damages cap at section 18(1) Landlord and Tenant Act 1927.</p>

<p><strong>During the lease term.</strong> Documentation of any consents granted for tenant alterations, photographs at notable points (mid-term reviews, change of use applications), and a tidy paper trail of any service-charge or fit-out contributions that might otherwise blur the boundary between repair and rent.</p>

<p><strong>At lease end.</strong> An interim schedule of dilapidations served by your surveyor before lease expiry. A final schedule and quantified surveyor's report. Contractor's quotes supporting the quantification. Settlement correspondence that is explicit about the head of payment (compensation for breach of the repair covenant, outside the scope of VAT, no VAT charged). A demand letter or settlement statement, not a VAT invoice, evidencing the receipt.</p>

<h2>Worked Example: Commercial Office Lease, Landlord Opted, £180,000 Settlement</h2>

<p>Carlton Holdings Limited holds an office unit in the Midlands, opted to tax under VATA 1994 Schedule 10 in 2018, leased to a 4,200 sq ft professional-services tenant on a 10-year FRI lease at £62,000 a year (plus VAT at 20 percent on rent because of the OTT).</p>

<p>The lease ends in March 2026. The landlord's surveyor serves a final schedule of dilapidations in February 2026 quantifying the cost of reinstatement at £210,000, comprising:</p>

<ul>
<li>£128,000 of fabric repairs (ceiling tiles, replacement carpets, internal partitioning reinstatement).</li>
<li>£42,000 of M&amp;E works (HVAC service and minor repairs, BMS reset).</li>
<li>£28,000 of redecoration to the contractual standard.</li>
<li>£12,000 of professional fees (surveyor, project manager).</li>
</ul>

<p>The tenant's surveyor counter-quotes at £156,000 reflecting fair-wear-and-tear deductions and a section 18(1) cap argument. The parties settle at £180,000 mid-March 2026. The settlement document is explicit: the payment is for compensation for breach of the repair covenant, supported by the surveyor's schedule and the contractor's quote, with no rent, mesne profits, or surrender element included.</p>

<p><strong>VAT analysis.</strong> The payment is for dilapidations. Carlton Holdings is opted to tax on the unit, so rents and service charges were VAT-able throughout the lease. The dilapidations settlement is separate. The OTT does not extend to it. Following VATSC05910 the settlement is outside the scope of VAT. Carlton Holdings issues a demand, not a VAT invoice, and the £180,000 is received with no output VAT. The tenant treats the £180,000 as a deductible revenue expense in its period of payment (subject to the capital-vs-revenue analysis below).</p>

<p><strong>If the settlement had bundled heads.</strong> Suppose instead the £180,000 had been documented as £130,000 dilapidations and £50,000 in lieu of one quarter's rent. The £50,000 in-lieu-of-rent element is consideration for the lease and follows the rent VAT treatment. Carlton Holdings would charge VAT at 20 percent on the £50,000 (so the tenant pays £180,000 plus £10,000 VAT). The £130,000 dilapidations remains outside scope. Failing to split heads when invoicing is the error to avoid.</p>

<h2>The OTT Cross-Cut: Why the Option to Tax Only Sometimes Matters</h2>

<p>A persistent misconception is that your option to tax under VATA 1994 Schedule 10 automatically pulls every payment from the tenant into the VAT net. It does not. The option converts the grant of the lease (and the supplies that are consideration for the grant: rent, service charge, insurance recharges that are consideration for occupation) from exempt to standard-rated. It does not reach a payment that lacks the reciprocity a supply requires.</p>

<p>So dilapidations are outside the scope of VAT whether or not you have opted. The OTT fixes the VAT treatment of the rent, not of the dilapidations. If you have not opted (a fully exempt commercial let, rare in practice but it happens for newer entities still inside the cooling-off period, or for portfolios where you have chosen not to opt) the analysis is the same: dilapidations sit outside scope on the reciprocity test, not on the exemption test. The mechanics of making, cooling off and revoking the election are covered in full in the option to tax guide below.</p>

<h2>Lease-Drafting Implications: Clauses That Help and Clauses That Hurt</h2>

<p>The lease drafted at the start of the term shapes the settlement document at lease end. Three drafting choices reliably support a clean outside-scope analysis years later:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Separate the repair covenant from the rent covenant.</strong> A lease where the repair obligation is plainly a freestanding tenant covenant, breach of which crystallises a damages claim at lease end, is easy to interpret. A lease where the repair obligation is bundled into the rent (an all-inclusive monthly figure described as covering rent, service, and ongoing maintenance) is harder to argue.</li>
<li><strong>Reference the section 18(1) cap explicitly.</strong> A clause confirming that your damages claim is subject to the statutory cap at section 18(1) Landlord and Tenant Act 1927 reinforces the compensation framing. Damages capped at the diminution in value of the reversion are about as clearly outside-scope as compensation gets.</li>
<li><strong>Attach a Schedule of Condition at grant.</strong> Where the property is not in pristine condition at the start of the lease, attaching a photographic Schedule of Condition fixes the contractual standard the tenant must restore to. The schedule eliminates a common settlement-stage argument and supports a clean cost-of-reinstatement quantification.</li>
</ul>

<p>Two drafting choices to avoid: inclusive-rent structures that combine rent and maintenance recovery in a single periodic charge, and clauses that let you recover dilapidations as a fixed contractual fee unrelated to the actual reinstatement cost. Both blur the line between consideration for occupation and compensation for breach.</p>

<h2>The Capital-vs-Revenue Tax Overlay</h2>

<p>VAT is not the only tax that turns on the substance of a dilapidations settlement. The income-tax or corporation-tax position for the tenant turns on the underlying capital-vs-revenue analysis. Where the dilapidations discharge accrued breaches of the repair covenant during the lease (genuine repair, not capital improvement), the payment is generally revenue, deductible in the period the liability accrues under the matching principle. Where the payment is in substance for capital reinstatement or for the landlord's regrant or release, the payment is capital, non-deductible against trading profits but potentially within the chargeable-gains computation on disposal of the lease.</p>

<p>HMRC's published view at BIM43265 supports the revenue characterisation in the standard case. Keep in mind that the VAT analysis (compensation outside scope) and the income-tax analysis (revenue deduction) sit alongside each other and turn on different tests. Settle both before you sign.</p>

<h2>Residential Lease-End: Why the VAT Question Almost Never Arises</h2>

<p>Residential tenancies are exempt from VAT in their entirety under VATA 1994 Schedule 9 Group 1, so if you let residential property the dilapidations VAT question almost never arises. Deposit deductions for damage (the residential analogue of dilapidations) are administered through the Tenancy Deposit Scheme framework and do not generate VAT invoices. The exception is the rare residential landlord who is VAT-registered for other reasons, typically a serviced-accommodation operator above the £90,000 registration threshold or a holiday-let portfolio operating above the de-registration threshold of £88,000. In those cases the commercial principles above apply: damage-based deductions stay compensation outside scope, and reinstatement charges for items beyond fair-wear-and-tear are documented as such.</p>

<h2>Practical Checklist Before Signing a Dilapidations Settlement</h2>

<p>A short pre-signing review reliably catches the issues that create VAT disputes years later.</p>

<ul>
<li>Is the settlement document explicit that the agreed sum is compensation for breach of the repair covenant, outside the scope of VAT, no VAT charged?</li>
<li>Are surveyor's schedule and contractor quotes on file and consistent with the agreed sum?</li>
<li>Are any other heads of payment (rent in arrears, mesne profits, surrender premium, in-lieu-of-rent) separately identified and separately analysed?</li>
<li>If you are opted to tax, is the dilapidations element being received under a demand rather than a VAT invoice?</li>
<li>Is the tenant's accountant on the same page on the capital-vs-revenue characterisation for income-tax purposes?</li>
<li>If the settlement was preceded by court or tribunal proceedings, does the consent order or settlement deed track the same head-of-payment split as the underlying analysis?</li>
</ul>
