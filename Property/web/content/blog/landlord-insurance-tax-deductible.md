<p>Landlord insurance has two distinct tax stories: premium deductibility against rental income, and the income tax (or CGT) treatment of any payout when a claim is made. The premium side is well understood. The payout side is where landlords most often get the treatment wrong, either by failing to report a taxable receipt or by treating a capital receipt as if it were ordinary rental income.</p>

<p>This guide covers both. HMRC's position is set out in the <a href="https://www.gov.uk/hmrc-internal-manuals/property-income-manual/pim2068">Property Income Manual at PIM2068</a> (insurance receipts) and the surrounding pages, with statutory authority in <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2005/5">ITTOIA 2005</a> for income, and sections 22 and 23 of the <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12">TCGA 1992</a> for capital sums derived from assets.</p>

<h2>Which premiums are deductible</h2>

<p>The starting point is section 34 ITTOIA 2005: an expense is deductible if it is incurred wholly and exclusively for the purposes of the property rental business. The following standard landlord cover types meet that test:</p>

<table>
<thead>
<tr><th>Cover</th><th>Deductible?</th><th>Notes</th></tr>
</thead>
<tbody>
<tr><td>Buildings insurance (let property)</td><td>Yes, in full</td><td>Includes lender-required cover</td></tr>
<tr><td>Contents insurance (furnished let)</td><td>Yes, in full</td><td>For unfurnished lets, usually not relevant</td></tr>
<tr><td>Public/property owner's liability</td><td>Yes, in full</td><td>Often bundled with buildings cover</td></tr>
<tr><td>Rent guarantee / loss of rent</td><td>Yes, in full</td><td>Payout is taxable income (see below)</td></tr>
<tr><td>Legal expenses (evictions, disputes)</td><td>Yes, in full</td><td>Premium is allowable; covered legal costs ditto</td></tr>
<tr><td>Malicious damage cover</td><td>Yes, in full</td><td>Specialist landlord product</td></tr>
<tr><td>Unoccupied property cover</td><td>Yes, in full</td><td>Allowable for void periods within a letting business</td></tr>
<tr><td>Boiler/appliance breakdown</td><td>Yes, in full</td><td>Allowable; deductible against rental profit</td></tr>
<tr><td>Life insurance (covering mortgage)</td><td>No</td><td>Personal expense regardless of underlying purpose</td></tr>
<tr><td>Personal income protection</td><td>No</td><td>Not wholly and exclusively for the business</td></tr>
<tr><td>Motor insurance</td><td>Apportion</td><td>Business-use percentage only; HMRC simplified expense rate may be used</td></tr>
<tr><td>Mixed-use buildings (landlord lives in part)</td><td>Apportion</td><td>By value, floor area or other reasonable basis</td></tr>
</tbody>
</table>

<p>The "wholly and exclusively" test is purpose-based rather than benefit-based. A premium with an incidental personal benefit (you happen to feel calmer knowing the building is insured) does not become non-deductible. What disqualifies a deduction is a premium with a non-trivial personal purpose, typically because the policy covers something other than the rental property.</p>

<h2>How insurance claim payouts are taxed</h2>

<p>Payouts are not automatically tax-free. The treatment depends on what the payout is replacing. The framework is:</p>

<table>
<thead>
<tr><th>Payout replaces</th><th>Tax treatment</th><th>Authority</th></tr>
</thead>
<tbody>
<tr><td>Lost rents (rent guarantee, loss of rent)</td><td>Taxable rental income</td><td>PIM2068; receipts test</td></tr>
<tr><td>Revenue repair costs (reimbursement)</td><td>Trading receipt that offsets the repair expense</td><td>PIM2068; matching principle</td></tr>
<tr><td>Capital damage (rebuild, reinstatement)</td><td>Capital sum derived from asset, adjusts CGT base cost</td><td>s.22 TCGA 1992</td></tr>
<tr><td>Capital payout where capital repair is undertaken</td><td>Rollover relief into repair under conditions</td><td>s.23 TCGA 1992</td></tr>
<tr><td>Contents replacement (furnished lets)</td><td>Trading receipt; offsets replacement-of-domestic-items relief</td><td>s.311A ITTOIA 2005</td></tr>
</tbody>
</table>

<p>The "income vs capital" question is the same one that runs through repairs themselves: is the work restoring an existing asset (revenue) or creating a substantively new or improved asset (capital)? A new boiler that replaces a like-for-like boiler is revenue; an extension is capital. The insurance receipt follows the underlying expenditure.</p>

<h2>Worked example 1: rent guarantee payout</h2>

<p>A landlord has a £1,200 per month tenancy with rent guarantee cover. The tenant defaults and the policy pays out four months of rent (£4,800) less an excess of £200, net £4,600. The landlord paid £400 in rent guarantee premium for the year.</p>

<ul>
<li>Premium paid: £400 (allowable expense)</li>
<li>Payout received: £4,600 (taxable rental income, replacing the rent that did not arrive)</li>
<li>Original rent that would have been received but for the default: £4,800 (no entry, never received)</li>
</ul>

<p>The landlord's rental account for the year shows £4,600 of receipts in place of the £4,800 of contractual rent, plus the £400 premium as a deduction. Net result: rental profit lower by £600 (the £200 excess plus the £400 premium) compared to the position if the tenant had paid in full. The insurance evens out the cashflow but does not produce a tax-free outcome.</p>

<h2>Worked example 2: revenue repair reimbursement</h2>

<p>A storm damages the roof on a let property. The landlord pays a roofer £4,000 to repair the damaged section (revenue repair, not a new roof). The buildings insurer pays out £4,000 less a £250 excess, net £3,750.</p>

<ul>
<li>Repair invoice: £4,000 (allowable revenue expense)</li>
<li>Insurance payout: £3,750 (trading receipt, offsets the repair expense)</li>
<li>Net cost to landlord, reflected in rental account: £250</li>
</ul>

<p>The mechanics in the accounts can be either (a) show the gross expense of £4,000 and the gross receipt of £3,750, or (b) net the two off and show £250. HMRC accepts both, but the gross approach is cleaner for audit trail and matches the bank statements more directly.</p>

<h2>Worked example 3: capital payout following a major fire</h2>

<p>A major fire destroys most of a let property. The buildings insurer pays out £180,000 to fund a full rebuild. The rebuild cost is £180,000. The original property cost £130,000 with £15,000 of capital improvements over the years (total CGT base cost: £145,000).</p>

<ul>
<li>Insurance receipt: £180,000 (capital sum derived from the asset under s.22 TCGA 1992)</li>
<li>Application of receipt to capital repair: rollover available under s.23 TCGA 1992, treating the receipt as not giving rise to an immediate gain provided the receipt is used to restore the asset within the statutory window</li>
<li>Adjusted CGT base cost: original £145,000 plus £180,000 of rebuild expenditure minus £180,000 of insurance receipt = £145,000 unchanged</li>
</ul>

<p>The landlord has no immediate income tax or CGT charge on the £180,000. The original CGT base cost survives, so when the property is eventually sold, the gain is computed against the original £145,000 cost. The election under s.23 is the route by which the payout does not trigger a chargeable gain at the point of receipt.</p>

<p>Where the payout exceeds the capital repair cost (for example, the landlord chooses not to rebuild and pockets the proceeds), the excess is treated as a part-disposal for CGT purposes, with a chargeable gain crystallising. Where there is no rebuild at all, the entire receipt is treated as a part-disposal.</p>

<h2>Section 24 and insurance: not restricted</h2>

<p>Section 24 ITA 2007 restricts relief for residential property finance costs (mortgage interest, loan interest, finance fees, related arrangement costs) to a basic-rate tax credit. Insurance premiums are not finance costs. They remain fully deductible at the landlord's marginal rate of income tax.</p>

<p>From 6 April 2027 individual residential landlords' marginal rates on rental profit move to 22% basic, 42% higher and 47% additional rate. Insurance premiums continue to be deductible against those higher rates, meaning the tax-relief value of a premium goes up slightly for higher-rate landlords from that date. The Section 24 pillar guide at <a href="/blog/section-24-and-tax-relief/section-24-tax-relief-complete-guide">Section 24 tax relief complete guide</a> sets out the finance-cost restriction in detail.</p>

<h2>Apportionment for mixed-use and multi-property policies</h2>

<p>Two common apportionment scenarios:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Mixed-use building.</strong> Where the landlord lives in part of the property and lets the other part (a converted house with a self-contained let flat, for example), apportion the premium by reference to the let part. Floor area is the most defensible basis where the rooms are similar; capital value or rental value can be appropriate where one part is materially more valuable. Document the basis chosen.</li>
<li><strong>Portfolio policy.</strong> A blanket policy covering multiple properties produces a single premium. The premium is fully deductible against the combined property rental business. There is no need to apportion across properties for SA105 purposes because the SA105 reports the rental business in aggregate, not per property. For MTD quarterly submissions, the standard categorisation is the same regardless of how many properties the policy covers.</li>
</ul>

<p>Where a single policy covers personal motor use and business use (a car used for property viewings, maintenance trips, agent meetings), apportion by mileage records or use HMRC's simplified expenses flat-rate, which can be more efficient for small business mileage.</p>

<h2>Filing mechanics: SA105 and MTD</h2>

<p>For 2025/26 and earlier, insurance premiums go in the "Rent, rates, insurance, ground rents" box on the SA105 UK Property pages (box 25 for non-FHL residential property in recent SA105 versions). Where insurance is bundled into a managing agent's invoice or a service charge, separate out the insurance element so the SA105 categorisation is accurate.</p>

<p>From 6 April 2026, sole-trader landlords with rental income above £50,000 enter the <a href="/blog/making-tax-digital-mtd/making-tax-digital-landlords-april-2026-deadline">MTD for Income Tax</a> regime. The threshold drops to £30,000 from April 2027 and £20,000 from April 2028. In-scope landlords keep digital records and submit quarterly updates using HMRC's category schema. Insurance is one of the standard categories. The year-end finalisation reconciles to the equivalent of the SA105 boxes.</p>

<p>Insurance payouts go into the same accounting framework as expenses. A revenue receipt (rent guarantee payout, revenue repair reimbursement) is recorded in the rental income workings for the period. A capital receipt is kept out of the income workings and tracked on the CGT side for the eventual disposal.</p>

<aside>
<p>Setting MTD records up to handle insurance cleanly</p>
<p>The MTD category schema does not have a separate "insurance payout" income category. In-scope landlords need to record payouts under the existing rental income category (for revenue receipts) or outside MTD entirely (for capital receipts). We help in-scope clients set this up at the start of the MTD year so the year-end is clean.</p>
</aside>

<h2>Limited company landlords</h2>

<p>The same income/capital distinction applies for company-owned rental businesses, adapted for corporation tax. Premiums are deductible against the company's trading profit at corporation tax rates of 19% (small profits, profits under £50,000) or 25% (main rate, over £250,000), with marginal relief between. Payouts follow the same analysis: revenue receipts are part of the trading account; capital receipts feed the chargeable gains computation on eventual disposal.</p>

<p>Companies do not face the Section 24 restriction at all, so the relative tax-relief value of insurance premiums inside a company is unaffected by the 2027 income tax change. This is one element of the wider personal-vs-company comparison set out in the <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/buy-to-let-limited-company-complete-guide-uk">BTL limited company complete guide</a>.</p>

<h2>Timing of the deduction</h2>

<p>For cash-basis accounting (the default for landlords with rental receipts below £150,000 unless they elect out), the premium is deductible in the tax year it is paid. A premium paid in March 2026 covering policy year April 2026 to March 2027 is deductible in 2025/26.</p>

<p>For accruals-basis accounting (mandatory above the £150,000 threshold and available by election), the premium is spread over the policy period. The March 2026 premium for an April 2026 to March 2027 policy is spread across 2025/26 (12 days) and 2026/27 (353 days).</p>

<p>Most individual landlords are on the cash basis by default. The choice matters at the margin for landlords paying large premiums in advance close to a year-end.</p>

<h2>Common landlord mistakes around insurance and tax</h2>

<p>Five errors that come up repeatedly in our work:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Treating rent guarantee payouts as tax-free.</strong> They replace rent and are taxable. Reporting the premium but omitting the payout is a textbook error.</li>
<li><strong>Treating a rebuild payout as ordinary rental income.</strong> It is a capital sum and goes into the CGT computation, not the rental income account.</li>
<li><strong>Claiming life insurance as a deductible premium.</strong> It is not, even where the policy is explicitly aligned to a buy-to-let mortgage.</li>
<li><strong>Failing to apportion mixed-use insurance.</strong> Only the let-business element is deductible.</li>
<li><strong>Net-rather-than-gross presentation that hides the payout.</strong> HMRC enquiries pick up on bank statements showing insurance receipts that don't appear anywhere in the rental income figures. Gross presentation, with the offset visible, is more defensible.</li>
</ul>

<h2>What this means in practice</h2>

<p>Premium deductibility for routine landlord cover is one of the more settled areas of property tax: get the wholly-and-exclusively test right, apportion where personal use exists, and claim everything that is allowable. The payout side is where most landlords benefit from professional input, particularly on capital claims where the rollover and CGT base cost interaction can materially affect the eventual disposal liability. For routine premiums, in-house bookkeeping with MTD-compliant software is usually sufficient. For significant claims (any meaningful capital event, contested rent guarantee, or major repair reimbursement), getting the treatment right at the time of the claim avoids a much harder reconstruction exercise at year-end or on disposal.</p>
