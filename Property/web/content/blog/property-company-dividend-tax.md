<p>Many property investors choose to <a href="/incorporation">incorporate their property business</a> to benefit from lower corporation tax rates. However, when you take money out of your property company as dividends, you'll face dividend tax on top of the corporation tax already paid.</p>

<p>Understanding dividend tax rates and how they apply to property company distributions is essential for effective tax planning. The rates differ significantly from income tax on rental income, and there are specific allowances and calculations to consider.</p>

<h2>Current Dividend Tax Rates 2025/26</h2>

<p>Dividend tax operates on a sliding scale based on your total income. Unlike salary or rental income, dividends don't attract National Insurance contributions, but they do face dividend tax at these rates:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Basic rate (up to £37,700):</strong> 8.75%</li>
<li><strong>Higher rate (£37,701 to £125,140):</strong> 33.75%</li>
<li><strong>Additional rate (over £125,140):</strong> 39.35%</li>
</ul>

<p>These rates apply after you've used your £500 dividend allowance for 2025/26. The dividend allowance was reduced from £1,000 in previous years, meaning more of your dividends now face tax.</p>

<h2>How Dividend Tax Works for Property Companies</h2>

<p>When your property company makes a profit, it pays corporation tax at 25% (or 19% on profits up to £50,000 with marginal relief up to £250,000). Any remaining profit can then be distributed as dividends to shareholders.</p>

<p>The dividend tax you pay depends on your other income. If you're a basic rate taxpayer with no other significant income, your first £37,700 of total income (including dividends) faces the 8.75% dividend tax rate.</p>

<p>However, if you have salary income, pension income, or rental income from personally-owned properties, these count towards your basic rate band first. Dividends are treated as the "top slice" of your income.</p>

<h2>Worked Example: Property Company Dividend Tax</h2>

<p>Let's consider Sarah, who owns a property company that made £60,000 profit in 2025/26. She also has a part-time salary of £20,000.</p>

<p><strong>Company level:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Property company profit: £60,000</li>
<li>Corporation tax (19% on first £50,000, 25% on remaining £10,000): £12,000</li>
<li>Available for dividends: £48,000</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Personal level:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Salary income: £20,000</li>
<li>Dividend income: £48,000</li>
<li>Total income: £68,000</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Dividend tax calculation:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Dividend allowance: £500 (tax-free)</li>
<li>Remaining dividends: £47,500</li>
<li>Basic rate band remaining after salary: £17,700 (£37,700 - £20,000)</li>
<li>Dividends taxed at 8.75%: £17,700 × 8.75% = £1,549</li>
<li>Dividends taxed at 33.75%: £29,800 × 33.75% = £10,058</li>
<li><strong>Total dividend tax: £11,607</strong></li>
</ul>

<p>Sarah's effective tax rate on the company's original £60,000 profit is 39.3% when combining corporation tax and dividend tax.</p>

<h2>Dividend Tax vs Salary: The Trade-Off</h2>

<p>Property company owners often take a small salary (typically up to the National Insurance threshold of £12,570 for 2025/26) and the remainder as dividends. This approach minimises National Insurance contributions while managing overall tax efficiency.</p>

<p>However, dividend tax rates can be punitive for higher earners. A dividend taxed at 33.75% plus 25% corporation tax gives an effective rate of 52.1% on company profits. In comparison, salary income faces income tax and National Insurance but qualifies for various reliefs and pension contribution opportunities.</p>

<h2>Managing Your Dividend Tax Liability</h2>

<p>Several strategies can help optimise your dividend tax position:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Timing dividends:</strong> Spread dividends across tax years to utilise lower rate bands</li>
<li><strong>Spousal dividends:</strong> If your spouse has unused basic rate band, they could receive dividends at 8.75%</li>
<li><strong>Retain profits:</strong> Keep money in the company and take dividends in lower-income years</li>
<li><strong>Pension contributions:</strong> Company pension contributions reduce corporation tax and can free up basic rate band for dividend tax</li>
</ul>
