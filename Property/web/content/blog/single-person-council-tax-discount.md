<p>If you live alone, your council tax bill is reduced by 25% under <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/14/section/11" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">Local Government Finance Act 1992 s.11(1)(a)</a>. The reduction is statutory, applies regardless of income or wealth, and is granted day by day. The route is free, direct to your local authority. There is no legitimate role for a paid third-party "discount recovery" firm.</p>

<p>This page is the operational applicant primer for the single-person discount. It walks the reader through who counts as a "resident," who is "disregarded" under <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/14/schedule/1" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">LGFA 1992 Schedule 1</a>, how to apply, the evidence required, the mid-year change-of-circumstances discipline, and how to defend a discount that the local authority later reviews. For the complete statutory architecture, the case-law on "sole or main residence," and every interaction with the exemption classes, see the companion <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/single-person-council-tax-discounts-a-complete-guide">complete guide page</a>. For the wider catalogue of legitimate routes to a reduced council tax bill, see the <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/reduce-your-council-tax-bill-in-the-uk">reduction-routes hub</a>.</p>

<h2>How the discount works in one read</h2>

<p>The mechanic has four moving parts:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>The trigger.</strong> Only one non-disregarded adult is resident in the dwelling. "Resident" is defined by s.11(5) as the dwelling being the person's sole or main residence.</li>
<li><strong>The reduction.</strong> 25% off the council tax bill, fixed by s.11(3). The local authority cannot vary the rate.</li>
<li><strong>The basis.</strong> Non-means-tested. Income, savings, and assets are irrelevant. A solo-occupier in a £4m Band H home qualifies on the same basis as a solo-occupier in a Band A flat.</li>
<li><strong>The clock.</strong> Day-by-day. A mid-year change (a partner moves in; a student finishes their course; you separate from a partner) triggers pro-rata recalculation.</li>
</ul>

<p>The single-person discount sits at the top of the most common council-tax-bill reductions because it is the simplest to claim and the most widely-applicable. But it is also the most-misunderstood: residents routinely (i) miss the discount entirely because they wrongly assume living with a student or carer disqualifies them, (ii) over-claim because they mis-judge the sole-or-main-residence test, or (iii) walk into a five-figure overpayment-recovery letter because they failed to notify the local authority when circumstances changed.</p>

<h2>Who counts as a "resident"?</h2>

<p>The starting point is s.11(5): an individual is resident in a dwelling on a particular day if it is their sole or main residence on that day. The test is a question of fact, applied holistically. No single factor is determinative. Relevant factors typically include:</p>

<ul>
<li>Where the person sleeps most nights over a reasonable comparison period.</li>
<li>Where personal possessions are kept.</li>
<li>Where the person's family, partner, or dependants are.</li>
<li>Voter registration.</li>
<li>Mail address (driving licence, bank statements, GP registration).</li>
<li>Work location, if local.</li>
<li>The person's intentions, treated as objective facts where supportable.</li>
</ul>

<p>A person can have only ONE sole or main residence at a time, even if they have multiple homes. The case-law line developed across Williams v Horsham DC and the subsequent Valuation Tribunal authorities anchors the holistic test. For the case-law walk-through, see the <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/single-person-council-tax-discounts-a-complete-guide">complete guide page</a>.</p>

<h2>Who is "disregarded" under Schedule 1?</h2>

<p>The most-misunderstood corner of the discount is the disregard mechanism. A dwelling with two adult residents qualifies for the discount if one of the adults falls within a Schedule 1 disregard category. The Schedule lists adult categories whose presence in the dwelling does NOT count toward the resident headcount for the discount.</p>

<p>The principal categories are:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Persons in detention</strong> (prison, immigration detention, mental-health detention).</li>
<li><strong>Severely mentally impaired persons</strong> with medical certification and entitlement to a qualifying state benefit per SI 1992/552.</li>
<li><strong>Persons for whom child benefit is payable</strong>, capturing 18 and 19 year olds in continuing non-advanced education.</li>
<li><strong>Full-time students and student nurses</strong> on a qualifying course at a UK or EEA institution.</li>
<li><strong>Apprentices and youth-training trainees</strong> on prescribed schemes.</li>
<li><strong>Patients in hospitals</strong> where the hospital admission becomes their sole or main residence.</li>
<li><strong>Persons in care homes</strong>, on equivalent terms.</li>
<li><strong>Religious community members</strong> in qualifying orders.</li>
<li><strong>Persons resident in hostels or night-shelters.</strong></li>
<li><strong>Carers</strong> providing 35 or more hours per week of care to a co-resident severely mentally impaired or disabled person in receipt of a qualifying benefit.</li>
<li><strong>Members of visiting forces and diplomats.</strong></li>
</ul>

<p>A household of one working adult plus one full-time student (the student child returns home each evening) qualifies for the single-person discount. A household of one working adult plus one 18-year-old in continuing non-advanced education for whom child benefit is still payable qualifies. A household of one working spouse plus one carer providing 35-plus hours of care to a co-resident severely mentally impaired family member qualifies (the carer is disregarded under Schedule 1; the cared-for is disregarded under the severely mentally impaired category). The headline framing "you only get the discount if you live alone" is wrong in many practical fact patterns.</p>

<h2>How to apply</h2>

<p>Three routes, all free, all direct to the local authority:</p>

<ol>
<li><strong>Online.</strong> Most local authorities have an online application form on their council tax page. Search "single person discount" plus your local authority name; the form is usually one or two pages. Upload supporting evidence at the same time. Confirmation typically arrives within 5 to 15 working days.</li>
<li><strong>Phone.</strong> Call the local authority's council tax team. They will take the application verbally and either grant the discount on the call or request supporting evidence by post or email.</li>
<li><strong>Post.</strong> Download the local authority's discount application form, complete it, and return with copies of supporting evidence. Slower than online but appropriate where digital access is limited.</li>
</ol>

<p>You do not need a paid intermediary. The official route via <a href="https://www.gov.uk/apply-for-council-tax-discount" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">gov.uk's apply-for-council-tax-discount page</a> routes directly to your local authority's application form. Citizens Advice and the Low Incomes Tax Reform Group have published warnings against the third-party "discount recovery" firms that charge 30 to 50 per cent of recovered amounts for work the resident can do themselves in under an hour.</p>

<h2>What evidence is required?</h2>

<p>For a straightforward solo-occupier claim, the local authority typically asks for:</p>

<ul>
<li>Proof of identity (driving licence or passport).</li>
<li>Proof of residence at the property (council tax bill or utility bill in your name; tenancy agreement or title deeds if you own).</li>
<li>A declaration that no other adult is resident at the property.</li>
</ul>

<p>For a claim involving a Schedule 1 disregard of another adult resident, additional evidence is required:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Student disregard:</strong> a student certificate from the institution confirming the course, the course length, and the weekly hours.</li>
<li><strong>Severely mentally impaired disregard:</strong> medical evidence from a registered medical practitioner plus evidence of entitlement to a qualifying state benefit.</li>
<li><strong>Child benefit (18 or 19 year old) disregard:</strong> child benefit notice from HMRC.</li>
<li><strong>Carer disregard:</strong> evidence of the cared-for's qualifying benefit, plus a declaration of the 35-plus hours per week of care.</li>
<li><strong>Hospital or care home disregard:</strong> admission letter or care plan from the institution.</li>
</ul>

<p>Keep copies of all evidence. A future local authority review may revisit the original application years later; the evidential file is the defence against an overpayment-recovery claim.</p>

<aside>
<p>The discount is statutory. A properly-evidenced application cannot be refused. But local authorities increasingly run discount-review programs (cross-referencing electoral roll data, utility billing data, and tenancy records to identify potential mis-claims). The discipline of a contemporaneous evidence file at the date of application is the strongest defence to any subsequent review.</p>

<h2>The 21-day notification discipline</h2>

<p>Once the discount is in place, the law requires notification of any relevant change of circumstances within 21 days. The duty sits in <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/14/section/16" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">LGFA 1992 s.16(6)</a>. Examples of relevant changes:</p>

<ul>
<li>Another adult moves in (a partner; an adult child returning from university to live full-time at home; a lodger).</li>
<li>A disregarded adult ceases to be disregarded (a student completes their course and starts work; a hospital patient is discharged and returns; a carer ceases caring responsibilities).</li>
<li>You move to a new property (the discount does NOT transfer automatically; a fresh application is required at the new address).</li>
<li>Civil partnership formation or dissolution.</li>
<li>Marriage or separation, where it changes the sole-or-main-residence position of either party.</li>
</ul>

<p>The 21-day clock starts the day the change occurs, not the day you notice or remember to act. Failure to notify is a criminal offence under s.16(6), attracts a £70 penalty plus interest, and triggers the local authority's investigation and recovery powers.</p>

<h2>Two properties: what happens?</h2>

<p>The discount applies only to your sole or main residence. A second property is treated under LGFA 1992 s.11C as a second home. Most local authorities now charge a second-home premium of up to 100 per cent on second homes, following the LURA 2023 expansion of the premium powers. This is the opposite of a discount.</p>

<p>For a single owner-occupier with a London flat used Monday to Friday and a country home used at weekends, the sole or main residence is the property that meets the holistic test. Voter registration, where personal possessions are kept, family location, and intentions all matter. The local authority cannot grant single-person discounts on both properties; only on the sole or main residence.</p>

<h2>Cohabitees, separated couples, and the residence question</h2>

<p>A cohabiting couple is two adult residents, so the single-person discount does not apply unless one partner is disregarded. The disregard route is the key planning question for couples in which one partner is a student, a carer, or severely mentally impaired.</p>

<p>On separation, the question is when the moving-out partner ceases to be "resident" at the original property. The test is the sole-or-main-residence test, applied holistically. A partner who has moved out and rented a separate property, changed voter registration, redirected mail, and removed possessions has clearly ceased to be resident. A partner who has moved out but still uses the original property as a postal address, keeps possessions there, and returns intermittently may not have ceased to be resident in the local authority's view. Document the change clearly; notify the local authority within the 21-day window.</p>

<h2>The overpayment-review risk and defence</h2>

<p>Local authorities increasingly run discount-review programs. The trigger is typically a tip-off, an electoral roll inconsistency, or a data match against utility billing. The local authority writes to the resident asking for evidence that the discount remains correctly applied. In the worst case, the local authority decides the discount was not properly entitled at some past point and seeks to recover up to 6 years of misclaimed discount under <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/14/schedule/4" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">LGFA 1992 Schedule 4</a>.</p>

<p>Defence strategy:</p>

<ol>
<li><strong>Contemporaneous evidence.</strong> Produce the original application file, the supporting evidence at the time, and any subsequent updates. A timeline of the residence pattern, with utility bills, bank statements, driving licence address, and electoral roll registration, is the strongest defence.</li>
<li><strong>Disregard evidence.</strong> Where a Schedule 1 disregard underpinned the discount, produce the student certificate, the medical certificate, the child benefit notice, or the care evidence. Multiple years may need separate evidence files.</li>
<li><strong>21-day notification record.</strong> Show that any relevant change of circumstances was notified within the 21-day window. This is the single most-load-bearing administrative discipline.</li>
<li><strong>Appeal route.</strong> If the local authority's recovery decision stands after the evidence has been considered, the appeal route is the Valuation Tribunal under LGFA 1992 s.16(1). Time limits apply; act promptly.</li>
</ol>

<aside>
<p>An overpayment-recovery letter is high-stakes. Six years of mis-applied discount on a Band D bill is roughly £3,250 plus interest plus the local authority's recovery costs. The right first step is to read the letter carefully, gather the contemporaneous evidence, and respond with a structured rebuttal. Where the facts are genuinely complex (multi-disregard household; separated-couple residence dispute; HMO-versus-single-dwelling boundary; second-home / sole-residence boundary), specialist advice is appropriate before responding.</p>

<h2>Interaction with Council Tax Reduction (CTR)</h2>

<p>The single-person discount and Council Tax Reduction (CTR) are separate routes. The discount applies first; CTR applies to the residual bill. Apply for both if eligible.</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Pensioner CTR</strong> is centrally prescribed under <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2012/2885/contents" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">SI 2012/2885</a> and broadly equivalent to the abolished Council Tax Benefit. A pensioner on Pension Credit (guarantee) typically receives 100% CTR.</li>
<li><strong>Working-age CTR</strong> is locally determined under LGFA 1992 Schedule 1A in England (Schedule 1B in Wales). Each local authority sets its own scheme. Maximum reduction varies.</li>
</ul>

<p>A pensioner widow alone in a Band C dwelling on Pension Credit, post-2025 illustrative figures: pre-discount bill £1,950; single-person discount 25% to £1,462.50; pensioner CTR may then reduce the residual close to zero depending on income and savings. Apply for both routes simultaneously.</p>

<h2>What to do today</h2>

<p>Three-step action list:</p>

<ol>
<li><strong>Check whether you are eligible.</strong> Are you the only non-disregarded adult resident in the dwelling? If you live with another adult, is that adult disregarded under any Schedule 1 category? If yes to either, you qualify.</li>
<li><strong>Apply directly to your local authority.</strong> Use the online form on the local authority's council tax page, or the official route at <a href="https://www.gov.uk/apply-for-council-tax-discount" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">gov.uk/apply-for-council-tax-discount</a>. Do not use third-party paid firms. Keep copies of the application and supporting evidence.</li>
<li><strong>Build a notification discipline.</strong> Diary the 21-day rule. Any change in household composition, any change in a disregarded resident's status, any move, any partnership change requires notification within 21 days. Failure to notify is a criminal offence and triggers recovery.</li>
</ol>
