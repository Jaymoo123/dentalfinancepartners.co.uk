<p>If you're earning income from Airbnb or other short-term rental platforms in the UK, you need to understand how <strong>Airbnb tax UK</strong> rules apply to your situation. Unlike traditional buy-to-let properties, short-term rentals come with specific tax considerations that can significantly impact your liability.</p>

<p>The abolition of the Furnished Holiday Lettings (FHL) regime from April 2025 has fundamentally changed the tax landscape for short-term rental operators. This guide explains exactly how your Airbnb income is taxed and what you need to know for compliance in 2026 and beyond.</p>

<h2>How Is Airbnb Income Taxed in the UK?</h2>

<p>Your Airbnb income is typically taxed as <strong>property income</strong> under UK tax law. This means it's subject to income tax at your marginal rate, just like rental income from a traditional buy-to-let property.</p>

<p>For the 2026/27 tax year, income tax rates are:</p>
<ul>
<li>Personal allowance: £0-£12,570 (0%)</li>
<li>Basic rate: £12,571-£50,270 (20%)</li>
<li>Higher rate: £50,271-£125,140 (40%)</li>
<li>Additional rate: £125,140+ (45%)</li>
</ul>

<p>From April 2027, a major change takes effect: separate property income tax rates will apply at 22% basic rate, 42% higher rate, and 47% additional rate specifically for property income. This will increase the tax burden on all property investors, including Airbnb hosts.</p>

<h3>Property Income vs Trading Income</h3>

<p>The distinction between property income and trading income is crucial for <strong>short term rental tax</strong> purposes. Most Airbnb activities are treated as property rental, but intensive serviced accommodation operations might be classified as a trade.</p>

<p>If your activity is deemed trading income, you'll pay income tax plus Class 2 and Class 4 National Insurance contributions, but you'll have access to trading deductions and potentially better capital gains treatment.</p>

<h2>End of Furnished Holiday Lettings Relief</h2>

<p>The FHL regime provided significant tax advantages for qualifying short-term rentals, including:</p>
<ul>
<li>Capital allowances on furniture and equipment</li>
<li>Business asset disposal relief on capital gains</li>
<li>Pension contribution eligibility</li>
<li>Relief from Section 24 mortgage interest restrictions</li>
</ul>

<p>These benefits ended on 5 April 2025. Properties that previously qualified as FHL now follow standard property rental rules, meaning owners face the full impact of Section 24 restrictions and lose access to enhanced capital allowances.</p>

<h2>Section 24 Impact on Airbnb Properties</h2>

<p>Like traditional buy-to-let landlords, Airbnb hosts are subject to <a href="/blog/section-24-and-tax-relief/section-24-tax-relief-complete-guide">Section 24 mortgage interest restrictions</a>. This means mortgage interest on your Airbnb property is no longer deductible as an expense.</p>

<p>Instead, you receive a basic rate tax credit (currently 20%) on your mortgage interest payments. For higher and additional rate taxpayers, this creates a significant tax disadvantage compared to the pre-2017 rules.</p>

<p>For example, if you have £10,000 annual mortgage interest on your Airbnb property and you're a higher rate taxpayer, you only get a £2,000 tax credit instead of a £4,000 tax deduction under the old system.</p>

<h2>Allowable Expenses for Airbnb Income</h2>

<p>You can deduct legitimate business expenses when calculating your taxable <strong>holiday rental income tax</strong>. Common allowable expenses include:</p>

<ul>
<li>Property management and cleaning costs</li>
<li>Utilities (gas, electricity, water when paid by you)</li>
<li>Council tax and business rates</li>
<li>Insurance premiums</li>
<li>Repairs and maintenance</li>
<li>Advertising and platform commission fees</li>
<li>Professional fees (accountants, legal costs)</li>
<li>Safety equipment and compliance costs</li>
</ul>

<h3>Furniture and Equipment</h3>

<p>With the end of FHL relief, you can no longer claim capital allowances on furniture and equipment. Instead, you'll typically use the replacement furniture relief, allowing you to deduct the cost of replacing worn-out items (but not the initial purchase).</p>

<p>Alternatively, you might choose to include furniture costs in your property's capital gains base cost, though this won't provide immediate tax relief.</p>

<h2>Capital Gains Tax on Airbnb Properties</h2>

<p>When you sell an Airbnb property, you'll typically face <a href="/blog/capital-gains-tax/capital-gains-tax-property-complete-guide-uk">capital gains tax</a> at property rates:</p>
<ul>
<li>18% for basic rate taxpayers</li>
<li>24% for higher and additional rate taxpayers</li>
</ul>

<p>The annual exempt amount for 2026/27 is £3,000. Without FHL relief, you won't qualify for business asset disposal relief (formerly entrepreneurs' relief), meaning the full gain is taxed at property CGT rates.</p>

<h2>Record Keeping Requirements</h2>

<p>HMRC expects detailed records for all short-term rental activities. You should maintain:</p>

<ul>
<li>Booking records with dates and income received</li>
<li>All receipts for allowable expenses</li>
<li>Bank statements showing rental income and payments</li>
<li>Property management agreements</li>
<li>Insurance documentation</li>
<li>Mortgage statements (for tax credit claims)</li>
</ul>

<h3>Making Tax Digital Compliance</h3>

<p>If your gross rental income (including Airbnb) exceeds £10,000 annually, you'll need to comply with <a href="/blog/making-tax-digital-mtd/making-tax-digital-landlords-april-2026-deadline">Making Tax Digital rules from April 2026</a>. This requires:</p>
<ul>
<li>Digital record keeping using MTD-compatible software</li>
<li>Quarterly submissions to HMRC</li>
<li>Annual declaration through your Self Assessment</li>
</ul>

<h2>Multiple Property Considerations</h2>

<p>If you operate several Airbnb properties, each property should be treated separately for tax purposes. However, you can offset losses from one property against profits from another within your overall property business.</p>

<p>For landlords with mixed portfolios (traditional BTL and Airbnb), the same tax principles apply to both. The key difference is typically higher management costs and more frequent tenant turnover with short-term rentals.</p>

<h2>Company vs Personal Ownership</h2>

<p>Some Airbnb hosts consider incorporating their short-term rental business. <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/buy-to-let-limited-company-complete-guide-uk">Limited company ownership</a> can provide benefits including:</p>

<ul>
<li>Corporation tax rates (19% small profits rate, 25% main rate)</li>
<li>No Section 24 restrictions on mortgage interest</li>
<li>Greater flexibility in timing income and expenses</li>
<li>Potential for dividend planning</li>
</ul>

<p>However, incorporation also brings additional compliance costs, restrictions on capital gains relief, and potential exit charges when extracting profits or disposing of properties.</p>

<h2>Regional and Licensing Considerations</h2>

<p>Different areas of the UK have varying requirements for short-term rentals:</p>

<p><strong>Scotland:</strong> Short-term let licensing is mandatory, with additional safety and insurance requirements affecting your deductible costs.</p>

<p><strong>England:</strong> Some local authorities require planning permission or licensing for short-term rentals, particularly in London boroughs and tourist areas.</p>

<p><strong>Wales:</strong> Similar planning considerations apply, with some areas restricting short-term lets to preserve housing stock.</p>

<p>Compliance costs for licensing, safety certificates, and planning applications are typically deductible as business expenses.</p>

<h2>VAT Considerations</h2>

<p>Most residential short-term rental income is exempt from VAT. However, if you provide significant additional services (meals, cleaning, concierge services), part of your income might be standard-rated for VAT.</p>

<p>If your total taxable supplies exceed the VAT threshold (£90,000 for 2026/27), you'll need to register for VAT and charge VAT on applicable services while potentially recovering VAT on business expenses.</p>

<h2>Planning for the Future</h2>

<p>The tax environment for short-term rentals continues evolving. Key considerations for 2026 and beyond include:</p>

<ul>
<li>The new property income tax rates from April 2027</li>
<li>Potential further restrictions on short-term rental operations</li>
<li>MTD compliance requirements</li>
<li>Ongoing Section 24 restrictions</li>
</ul>

<p>Given these complexities, many Airbnb hosts benefit from professional advice. <a href="/blog/property-accountant-services/what-does-a-property-accountant-do">Property accountants</a> can help optimize your tax position, ensure compliance, and plan for future changes in the regulatory environment.</p>

<p>The abolition of FHL relief has significantly increased the tax burden on short-term rental operators. Understanding these rules and planning accordingly is essential for maintaining profitability in an increasingly regulated market.</p>
