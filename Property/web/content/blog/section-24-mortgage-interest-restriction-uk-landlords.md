<p>Section 24 has fundamentally changed how individual landlords can claim tax relief on mortgage interest. Introduced in April 2017 and fully implemented by April 2020, this legislation restricts mortgage interest relief to the basic rate of tax (20%) only.</p>

<p>For higher rate taxpayers, this represents a significant increase in their tax burden. Understanding how Section 24 works is crucial for any landlord with mortgage debt on their rental properties.</p>

<h2>What is Section 24 Mortgage Interest Restriction?</h2>

<p>Section 24 mortgage interest restriction limits the tax relief available on mortgage interest and other finance costs for residential rental properties owned by individuals. Previously, landlords could deduct full mortgage interest as an expense before <a href="/blog/section-24-and-tax-relief/section-24-calculator">calculating</a> taxable profit.</p>

<p>Now, mortgage interest is treated as a tax credit rather than a deductible expense. This means you can only reduce your tax bill by 20% of your mortgage interest, regardless of your marginal tax rate.</p>

<p>The restriction applies to residential rental properties owned personally. It does not apply to commercial properties or properties owned through limited companies.</p>

<h2>How Section 24 Works in Practice</h2>

<p>Under the old system, a higher rate taxpayer could effectively claim 40% relief on mortgage interest. Under Section 24, everyone is limited to 20% relief.</p>

<p>Here's a practical example: A landlord has rental income of £30,000 and mortgage interest of £15,000. Their other allowable expenses total £5,000.</p>

<h3>Before Section 24:</h3>
<ul>
<li>Rental income: £30,000</li>
<li>Less: Mortgage interest: £15,000</li>
<li>Less: Other expenses: £5,000</li>
<li>Taxable profit: £10,000</li>
</ul>

<h3>After Section 24:</h3>
<ul>
<li>Rental income: £30,000</li>
<li>Less: Other expenses: £5,000</li>
<li>Taxable profit: £25,000</li>
<li>Tax credit: £15,000 × 20% = £3,000</li>
</ul>

<p>The landlord now pays tax on £25,000 instead of £10,000, but gets a £3,000 tax credit. For a higher rate taxpayer, this significantly increases their tax bill.</p>

<h2>Who is Affected by Section 24?</h2>

<p>Section 24 mortgage interest restriction affects individual landlords who own residential rental properties with mortgages. You're impacted if you:</p>

<ul>
<li>Own buy-to-let properties in your personal name</li>
<li>Have mortgages or loans secured against these properties</li>
<li>Pay higher rate tax (currently 40% or 45%)</li>
<li>Are pushed into higher rate tax by the restriction itself</li>
</ul>

<p>The restriction is particularly painful for landlords who are pushed from basic rate to higher rate tax. This happens because mortgage interest can no longer be deducted before calculating your tax band.</p>

<h2>Calculating Your Section 24 Impact</h2>

<p>To understand your Section 24 impact, you need to calculate your tax under both the old and new systems. The difference shows your additional tax burden.</p>

<p>Consider a landlord with total income of £55,000 (including £35,000 rental income), mortgage interest of £20,000, and other property expenses of £8,000.</p>

<h3>Old System Tax Calculation:</h3>
<ul>
<li>Total income: £55,000</li>
<li>Less: Personal allowance: £12,570</li>
<li>Less: Mortgage interest: £20,000</li>
<li>Less: Other expenses: £8,000</li>
<li>Taxable income: £14,430</li>
<li>Tax due: £1,443 (all at 20%)</li>
</ul>

<h3>Section 24 Tax Calculation:</h3>
<ul>
<li>Total income: £55,000</li>
<li>Less: Personal allowance: £12,570</li>
<li>Less: Other expenses: £8,000</li>
<li>Taxable income: £34,430</li>
<li>Tax due: £6,886 (£2,143 at 20% + £4,743 at 40%)</li>
<li>Less: Tax credit: £4,000 (£20,000 × 20%)</li>
<li>Net tax due: £2,886</li>
</ul>

<p>The additional tax burden is £1,443 per year – a 100% increase. For help with these calculations, you might find our <a href="/calculators">tax calculators</a> useful.</p>

<h2>Solutions to Section 24</h2>

<p>Several strategies can help mitigate Section 24's impact, though each comes with its own considerations and costs.</p>

<h3>Limited Company Incorporation</h3>

<p>The most common solution is transferring properties to a limited company. Companies are not subject to Section 24 and can still deduct full mortgage interest as an expense.</p>

<p>However, incorporation involves significant costs and considerations:</p>
<ul>
<li>Capital gains tax on transfer (though incorporation relief may apply)</li>
<li>Stamp duty land tax at commercial rates</li>
<li>Corporation tax on company profits</li>
<li>Personal tax when extracting funds from the company</li>
</ul>

<p>Our <a href="/incorporation">incorporation service</a> can help you understand whether this strategy makes sense for your portfolio.</p>

<h3>Pension Contributions</h3>

<p>Increasing pension contributions can help reduce your marginal tax rate. The annual allowance for 2024/25 is £60,000, though this may be reduced for high earners.</p>

<p>Regular pension contributions can keep you in the basic rate band, ensuring Section 24 has minimal impact.</p>

<h3>Spouse/Partner Transfer</h3>

<p>If your spouse or partner has a lower marginal tax rate, transferring properties or shares in properties might reduce the overall tax burden. This can be particularly effective if one partner is a basic rate taxpayer.</p>

<h2>Long-term Property Strategy</h2>

<p>Section 24 has changed the fundamentals of property investment for individual landlords. Many investors are now considering whether the traditional buy-to-let model remains viable.</p>

<p>Key considerations for your long-term strategy include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Future mortgage interest rates and their impact</li>
<li>Your overall income and tax position</li>
<li>The costs and benefits of incorporation</li>
<li>Alternative investment structures</li>
</ul>

<p>The restriction has made property investment through companies increasingly attractive, particularly for higher rate taxpayers with significant mortgage debt.</p>

<h2>Threshold Effects and Tax Band Interactions</h2>
<p>Section 24 can push landlords into higher tax bands by increasing taxable income. A landlord earning £45,000 from employment with £8,000 rental profit now has £53,000 taxable income, crossing into the higher rate band.</p>

<p>This threshold effect can trigger:</p>

<ul>
<li>Loss of personal allowance (income over £100,000)</li>
<li>Higher rate tax on rental profits</li>
<li>Reduced child benefit entitlement</li>
<li>Impact on pension annual allowance</li>
</ul>

<h2>Strategies for Managing Section 24</h2>
<h3>Property Company Incorporation</h3>

<p>Many landlords consider <a href="/incorporation">incorporation</a> to avoid Section 24. Companies can still deduct mortgage interest as a business expense, but this route involves corporation tax, potential capital gains on transfer, and ongoing compliance costs.</p>

<h3>Portfolio Restructuring</h3>

<p>Some landlords reduce mortgage debt by:</p>

<ul>
<li>Selling highly mortgaged properties</li>
<li>Using sale proceeds to pay down other mortgages</li>
<li>Focusing on lower loan-to-value investments</li>
<li>Switching to commercial or furnished holiday lets</li>
</ul>

<h3>Pension Contributions</h3>

<p>Increasing pension contributions can reduce taxable income, potentially bringing landlords back into the basic rate band and reducing the Section 24 impact.</p>

<h2>Section 24 and Joint Ownership</h2>
<p>Married couples and civil partners can allocate rental income between them to optimise tax positions. If one spouse is a basic rate taxpayer, allocating more income to them reduces the Section 24 impact.</p>

<p>However, this requires completing Form 17 and affects both income and mortgage interest allocation proportionally.</p>

<h2>What Changed with Mortgage Interest Tax Relief</h2>
<p>Before Section 24, landlords could deduct their full mortgage interest payments from their rental income as a business expense. This reduced their taxable profit pound-for-pound.</p>

<p>The old system meant if you earned £20,000 in rental income and paid £8,000 in mortgage interest, you were only taxed on £12,000 of profit. Simple and straightforward.</p>

<p>Section 24 changed this completely. Now, individual landlords can only claim a 20% tax credit on their mortgage interest payments, regardless of their marginal tax rate.</p>

<h2>How the 20% Tax Credit Works</h2>
<p>Under the current system, mortgage interest is no longer deducted from your rental income. Instead, it's added back to your profit calculation, then you receive a 20% tax credit.</p>

<p>Here's a practical example: A landlord with £30,000 rental income and £12,000 mortgage interest payments.</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Old system:</strong> Taxable profit = £18,000 (£30k - £12k)</li>
<li><strong>New system:</strong> Taxable profit = £30,000, but you get a £2,400 tax credit (20% of £12k)</li>
</ul>

<p>For higher-rate taxpayers, this creates a significant tax burden. You're paying 40% tax on the full £30,000 but only getting 20% relief on the mortgage interest.</p>

<h2>The Real Impact on Different Tax Brackets</h2>
<p>The mortgage interest tax relief changes affect landlords differently depending on their total income and tax bracket.</p>

<h3>Basic Rate Taxpayers</h3>

<p>If your total income (including rental profit) keeps you in the 20% tax band, the impact is neutral. You pay 20% tax and get 20% relief – the same net effect as before.</p>

<h3>Higher Rate Taxpayers</h3>

<p>This is where Section 24 bites hardest. Higher-rate taxpayers pay 40% tax on their full rental income but only receive 20% relief on mortgage interest.</p>

<p>Take a landlord earning £60,000 from their day job plus £25,000 rental profit (after all expenses except mortgage interest of £10,000):</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Total taxable income:</strong> £95,000 (£60k + £35k rental income)</li>
<li><strong>Additional tax on rental income:</strong> 40% on £35,000 = £14,000</li>
<li><strong>Mortgage interest relief:</strong> 20% of £10,000 = £2,000</li>
<li><strong>Net additional tax:</strong> £12,000</li>
</ul>

<h3>Pushed Into Higher Rate Tax</h3>

<p>Perhaps most frustrating are landlords pushed from basic rate into higher rate tax by the gross rental income calculation. You might have modest actual profits, but Section 24 inflates your taxable income significantly.</p>

<h2>Additional Consequences You Need to Consider</h2>
<p>The mortgage interest tax relief changes create several knock-on effects that many landlords overlook initially.</p>

<h3>Child Benefit Clawback</h3>

<p>Higher taxable income can trigger the High Income Child Benefit Charge. This affects anyone earning over £50,000, with child benefit fully clawed back at £60,000.</p>

<h3>Personal Allowance Reduction</h3>

<p>Once your adjusted net income exceeds £100,000, you start losing your personal allowance. This creates an effective 60% tax rate on income between £100,000 and £125,140.</p>

<h3>Student Loan Repayments</h3>

<p>Higher reported income can increase student loan repayments, even if your actual cash profit hasn't changed.</p>

<h2>Strategies to Manage the Impact</h2>
<p>While Section 24 is here to stay for individual landlords, several strategies can help manage its impact.</p>

<h3>Property Company Structure</h3>

<p>Limited companies aren't affected by Section 24. They can still deduct mortgage interest as a business expense, paying corporation tax on actual profits.</p>

<p>For many landlords, <a href="/incorporation">incorporation</a> has become an attractive option, though it brings its own complexities around extraction of profits and capital gains.</p>

<h3>Pension Contributions</h3>

<p>Increasing pension contributions can reduce your adjusted net income, potentially keeping you in a lower tax bracket or avoiding benefit clawbacks.</p>

<h3>Spouse Income Splitting</h3>

<p>If your spouse is a basic rate taxpayer, transferring properties to them (or holding jointly) can reduce the overall tax burden.</p>

<h3>Portfolio Optimization</h3>

<p>Consider whether lower-yielding, less mortgaged properties make more sense in the current environment. The math has fundamentally changed for highly geared investments.</p>
