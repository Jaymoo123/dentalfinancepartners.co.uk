faqs:
  - question: "Why can a minor not hold legal title to property in their own name?"
    answer: "Settled Land Act 1925 s.1(7) excludes minors from holding legal estate in land in England and Wales, and the Trusts of Land and Appointment of Trustees Act 1996 (TOLATA) preserves the exclusion in its new trusts-of-land structure. A conveyance purporting to vest legal title in a minor takes effect as a declaration that the land is held on trust for the minor. The trustees hold legal title; the minor is the beneficial owner. Scotland uses age 16 for legal capacity in property matters under the Age of Legal Capacity (Scotland) Act 1991, but the practical answer is the same: under 16 in Scotland, under 18 in England and Wales and Northern Ireland, the minor cannot hold legal estate. Bare-trust legal title vesting in adults (typically parents or appointed trustees) is the standard route to put property in a minor's beneficial estate."
  - question: "Does the bare-trust transparency block ITTOIA 2005 s.629 attribution?"
    answer: "No. This is the single biggest misconception in family-planning content on minor-child gifts. The bare-trust mechanism affects CGT (TCGA 1992 s.60 transparency) and IHT (the property is not 'settled property' for IHT under IHTA 1984 s.43). It does not affect the income-tax attribution under ITTOIA 2005 s.629. Where the parent is the settlor of the bare trust and the beneficiary is the parent's unmarried minor child, s.629 attributes the rental income back to the parent regardless of how the bare-trust deed is drafted. The drift catch is in our house position §22.15 ('Bare trust for minor child escapes settlements legislation for income tax' is on the do-not-write list)."
  - question: "What is the £100 per-settlement de-minimis under s.629(3)?"
    answer: "s.629(3) carves out income arising under a settlement where the total amount of the relevant settlement income paid to or for the benefit of the settlor's relevant child(ren) in a tax year does not exceed £100. The £100 is per settlement-settlor relationship, not per child, and HMRC's published practice at TSEM4205 onwards treats any practical income flow above £100 as triggering full s.629 attribution on the entire settlement income for the tax year (not just the excess above £100). The £100 figure has been unchanged since the de-minimis was first inserted; it is not RPI-uprated and does not adjust for inflation. For any property-based settlement producing meaningful rental income (typically £8,000-£25,000 per year on a single BTL share), the £100 de-minimis is irrelevant and the full attribution applies."
  - question: "How does the grandparent route differ from the parent route?"
    answer: "Where a grandparent (not parent) settles property on a minor grandchild, the grandparent is the 'settlor' for s.629 purposes. s.629(1) attributes income to 'the settlor', so income is attributed to the grandparent, not the parent. Where the grandparent is retired (often basic-rate or below the personal allowance) the s.629 outcome is materially better than the parent-route outcome (where attribution typically bites at the parent's higher-rate or additional-rate marginal cost). Where the grandparent has no other income, the rental income from a single-flat settlement may fall within the grandparent's personal allowance (£12,570 for 2026/27) and produce no tax at all. The grandparent route is the cleanest property-gifting structure for minor-grandchild planning, and its existence is the reason the parent route should be a deliberate choice rather than a default."
  - question: "Does s.629 attribute capital growth as well as income?"
    answer: "No. s.629 attributes 'income arising under a settlement', which on its statutory wording is income only. Capital gains arising on disposal of the settlement property are not attributed under s.629. The child's CGT base cost is the deemed-MV at the gift date under TCGA 1992 s.17; subsequent appreciation accrues in the child's beneficial estate. On the child's eventual disposal (whether during minority via trustee sale or after the 18th-birthday vesting), the gain is computed on the child's base cost with the child's annual exempt amount and personal CGT rates. The capital-growth-not-attributed feature is the operative loophole used by parents who want to crystallise base cost in the child's hands at gift-date MV (using the parent's annual exempt amount on a small slice, or accepting the dry CGT on a larger slice) accepting that the rental income attributes back."
  - question: "What happens at the child's 18th birthday?"
    answer: "Three things change simultaneously. First, s.629 ceases to apply prospectively from the 18th birthday; rental income arising on the property from that date forward is the child's income at the child's marginal rate, with no further attribution back to the parent. The s.629 attribution does not run retrospectively: income that has been attributed up to the 18th birthday stays attributed; income arising after the 18th birthday is the child's. Second, the bare-trust legal-title structure becomes unwindable: the beneficiary can call for legal title under the rule in Saunders v Vautier (1841). The trustees can choose to maintain the bare-trust holding pattern indefinitely with the beneficiary's consent, but the beneficiary's right to demand title means there is no longer a structural reason for the bare-trust to exist. Third, the property starts to feature in the child's own income tax personal allowance, savings allowance, and dividend allowance, which may produce a more efficient income tax footprint than the parent's marginal rate."
  - question: "Is the gift into a bare trust a PET or a CLT for IHT?"
    answer: "A PET. The transfer of property into a bare trust is treated as a direct PET to the named beneficiary under IHTA 1984 s.3A, because the bare trust is not a 'settlement' for IHT purposes per IHTA 1984 s.43(2) (the bare-trust mechanism treats the beneficiary as the absolute owner from gift date). The seven-year clock runs from the gift date; if the settlor parent (or grandparent) survives seven years, the gift drops out of the donor's estate entirely. If the donor dies within seven years, the gift falls back into the death estate at the gift-date MV with taper relief at years 3-7 under IHTA 1984 s.7(4). The bare-trust route therefore combines the income-tax-attribution disadvantage (s.629 on rental income) with the IHT-clock advantage (PET running from gift date and the property outside the relevant property regime)."
  - question: "What about a discretionary trust for the minor child instead?"
    answer: "Different statutory frame. A discretionary trust for the minor child is a settled trust within IHTA 1984 s.43, with the transfer into the trust being a chargeable lifetime transfer (CLT) at 20% on the value above the settlor's available NRB. The trust then operates within the relevant property regime: 10-year periodic charge up to 6% under IHTA 1984 s.64, exit charges on capital distributions. CGT entry can be held over under TCGA 1992 s.260 where the trust is non-settlor-interested (settlor and spouse expressly excluded). Income tax: the trust pays at the trust rate (45% from £500 of income from 6 April 2024) until the trustees distribute, with the same s.629 minor-child attribution applying where the beneficiary is the settlor's minor child. The discretionary route delivers more trustee control past age 18 but costs more in entry IHT and ongoing tax than the bare-trust route. The choice between the two is driven by the family's appetite for governance control versus tax efficiency."
  - question: "Does TRS registration apply to a bare trust holding UK land for a minor?"
    answer: "Yes. Money Laundering Regulations 2017 reg 45ZA requires registration of any UK express trust that acquires an interest in UK land, regardless of whether the trust pays tax. A bare trust for a minor over UK land falls within scope. The 90-day registration clock runs from the trust's creation; the penalty for failure to register is up to £5,000. Trustees should register on the Trust Registration Service at gov.uk via a Government Gateway login, supplying settlor details, beneficiary details (the minor child by name, date of birth, and country of residence), trustee details, and the property description. Updates within 90 days are required when any of the registered details change (typically when the minor's address changes or when trustees are added or removed)."
  - question: "Can the parent reclaim the s.629-attributed tax from the child later?"
    answer: "No. The attribution is final; the parent is the taxpayer on the income for the year of attribution, and there is no statutory mechanism by which the child becomes responsible for the income tax retrospectively. The parent pays the tax out of the parent's resources (the rent may have been received and retained for the child's benefit, or used to pay property expenses, but the income-tax liability sits with the parent regardless). Where the parent wants to recover the cash position from the child, the parent must do so by drawing the post-tax rent from the child's bank account (with the child's consent, which a minor cannot legally give in their own right, so via the trustees), or accept the cost as the price of the structure. The 'parent-as-tax-paying-vehicle' arrangement is one reason families with substantial portfolios often prefer the grandparent route or the FIC alternative."
  - question: "How does s.629 interact with the parent's child benefit?"
    answer: "s.629 attributes the rental income to the parent's total income; that total income figure feeds into the High Income Child Benefit Charge (HICBC) computation under ITEPA 2003 ss.681B-H. The HICBC kicks in at £60,000 of adjusted net income for the 2026/27 tax year (the threshold was raised from £50,000 from 6 April 2024) and tapers full child benefit reclaim at £80,000. For a parent with £45,000 of employment income who picks up an additional £20,000 of attributed rental income via s.629, the combined £65,000 falls into the HICBC band; the family loses approximately half of the child benefit they would otherwise have received. The HICBC interaction with s.629 attribution is one of the under-flagged cost lines on the parent-route gift; many parents calculate the structure as 'parent's marginal rate on the rent' without modelling the HICBC ladder."
  - question: "What if both parents settle a share each on the same child?"
    answer: "s.629 looks at each parent separately as a potential settlor; the question is whether each parent is a settlor of property income attributable to the child. Where both parents have settled property shares on the child, each is a separate settlor, with the £100 de-minimis applying per settlement (so two settlements get two £100 thresholds, but the practical income from a property settlement almost always exceeds £100 anyway). The attribution to each parent is on the parent's share of the income; where the property shares are 50/50, each parent has half the rental income attributed to them at their respective marginal rate. The arrangement does not aggregate; one parent's HICBC threshold does not factor in the other parent's attributed income. The practical effect of two-parent settlements is to split the income tax bill between the parents' personal allowances rather than concentrate it on one."
  - question: "How does PRR interact with a gifted home held on bare trust for a minor?"
    answer: "PRR under TCGA 1992 ss.222-226 requires the property to be the taxpayer's only or main residence. Where the property is held on bare trust for a minor and the minor occupies it as their main residence, the beneficial owner (the minor) can in principle claim PRR on a subsequent disposal during minority via the trustees. In practice this is rare: minors typically occupy the family home as part of the parents' household, not as their own main residence in any meaningful sense, and HMRC's TCGA Sch 1 paragraph 13 specifically restricts PRR for trustees to cases where the beneficiary is treated as having a right to occupy under the trust terms. For a parent gifting their family home to a minor child via bare trust, PRR analysis is complex and depends on the actual occupation pattern; the residual answer is often that PRR is preserved during minority but the planning question is whether the gift achieves anything useful given the s.629 issue and the contingent nature of the future disposal."
  - question: "Does the gift trigger SDLT if the property has a mortgage?"
    answer: "FA 2003 Schedule 4 paragraph 8 treats assumed debt as chargeable consideration for SDLT, with the cap at market value under paragraph 8(2). A debt-free gift to a bare-trust structure for a minor has no SDLT consequence; a gift where the trustees take title subject to the existing mortgage (with the mortgage assumed by the trustees as a condition of the lender's release) triggers SDLT on the assumed debt. For a £400,000 BTL with £180,000 outstanding mortgage assumed by the trustees, the chargeable consideration is £180,000 and SDLT at the higher-rate residential surcharge (5% from 31 October 2024) produces approximately £9,000. The standard fix is for the parent to refinance the mortgage out before the transfer, leaving the trustees to take clean title with no debt assumption. The refinance carries its own fees but is normally cheaper than the SDLT it avoids."

<p>Gifting a buy-to-let or a flat to a minor child is not "gifting to an adult child with extra paperwork". The statutory frame is materially different. Three blocks change the analysis. First, Settled Land Act 1925 s.1(7) read with the Trusts of Land and Appointment of Trustees Act 1996 stops a minor from holding legal estate in UK land, so the bare-trust route is not a choice, it is the only legal way to put property in a minor's hands. Second, ITTOIA 2005 s.629 attributes rental income arising under any parent-funded settlement back to the parent at the parent's marginal rate; the bare-trust transparency that works for capital gains tax and inheritance tax does not block this attribution. Third, s.629 is asymmetric: it bites on income but not on capital growth, which opens the operative loophole that capital appreciation accrues in the child's hands free of any attribution mechanism.</p>

<p>This page walks the three statutory blocks, the parent-route mechanics with a worked example, the grandparent-route variant that changes the answer materially, the capital-growth loophole and how to plan for it, the 18th-birthday prospective-only cliff, the inheritance tax seven-year clock running from gift date, and the common mistakes families make.</p>

<h2>Block 1: a minor cannot hold legal estate in land</h2>

<p><a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1925/18/section/1">Settled Land Act 1925 s.1(7)</a> states that "a legal estate is not capable of subsisting or of being created in an undivided share in land or of being held by an infant." The Trusts of Land and Appointment of Trustees Act 1996, which restructured the law of co-ownership and trusts of land, preserved the exclusion. The Land Registry will not register a minor as proprietor. A conveyance purporting to vest legal title in a minor takes effect as a declaration that the land is held on trust for the minor.</p>

<p>The structural consequence: any gift of UK land to a minor must vest legal title in an adult (typically the parent, a grandparent, or appointed corporate trustees) who holds on bare trust for the named minor. The bare-trust mechanism is the standard solution. Three points follow.</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>The trustee is operationally the legal owner.</strong> The trustee deals with the letting agent, signs the AST, banks the rent, instructs maintenance contractors, and engages with HMRC on the bare-trust's behalf. The trustee files the trust on the Trust Registration Service within 90 days of creation under Money Laundering Regulations 2017 reg 45ZA. None of this changes the beneficial-ownership analysis; the minor is the beneficial owner throughout.</li>
<li><strong>The bare-trust deed must be precise.</strong> Any language that gives the trustee discretion over who benefits, or that defers vesting past age 18, takes the structure out of the bare-trust regime and into the formal-trust regime (with materially different tax consequences). The standard wording is "to hold for [the named minor] absolutely, contingent only on attaining the age of majority". A deed that says "to be paid at age 25" creates a discretionary trust, not a bare trust; see our separate page on the <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/bare-trust-vs-nominee-company-vs-formal-trust-decision-property-investors">bare trust vs nominee vs formal trust structural decision</a>.</li>
<li><strong>Scotland uses age 16.</strong> The Age of Legal Capacity (Scotland) Act 1991 gives 16-year-olds legal capacity in property matters. The under-18 analysis above applies to England and Wales and Northern Ireland; in Scotland the equivalent test is under-16. The s.629 attribution rules use "unmarried child under 18" for all four UK jurisdictions, so the income-tax analysis is broadly aligned.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Block 2: ITTOIA 2005 s.629 attributes the rental income</h2>

<p><a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2005/5/section/629">ITTOIA 2005 s.629</a> is the statutory attribution mechanism that catches parent-to-minor-child property arrangements. Verbatim s.629(1): "Income arising under a settlement is treated as income of the settlor alone if, during the settlor's life, it is paid to or for the benefit of a relevant child or would otherwise be treated as that child's income."</p>

<p>Three operative points.</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>"Settlement" is defined broadly under s.620.</strong> Any disposition, trust, covenant, agreement, arrangement or transfer of assets is a settlement for s.629 purposes. A bare trust over UK land is unambiguously a settlement for income-tax purposes (it is not "settled property" for IHT under IHTA 1984 s.43, but it is a "settlement" for ITTOIA 2005 s.629). A declaration of trust over an existing property recording a beneficial-share gift to a minor child is a settlement. An informal family arrangement under which a parent buys a property "in trust for" a minor child is a settlement.</li>
<li><strong>"Relevant child" is defined under s.629(7) as an unmarried minor not in a civil partnership.</strong> The definition includes step-children. The status of being a relevant child ceases on the 18th birthday or on the child's earlier marriage or civil partnership.</li>
<li><strong>The bare-trust transparency does not block s.629.</strong> The bare-trust mechanism affects CGT (TCGA 1992 s.60: trustee's acts are deemed beneficiary's acts) and IHT (IHTA 1984 s.43(2): bare-trust property is treated as the beneficiary's directly). It does not affect the income-tax attribution under s.629. The relevant statutory hook is ITTOIA 2005 s.629(1), which operates on the settlor-and-relevant-child relationship independently of the bare-trust mechanic. This is the single biggest drift in family-planning content on minor-child gifts and is on our house position §22.15 do-not-write list.</li>
</ul>

<p>The mechanical consequence: where a parent settles a BTL on bare trust for their minor child, the rental income produced by the BTL share is the parent's income for income-tax purposes, taxed at the parent's marginal rate. The parent files the income on the parent's self-assessment return; the parent pays the tax. The bare-trust structure does nothing to mitigate this position.</p>

<h2>The £100 de-minimis under s.629(3)</h2>

<p>s.629(3) carves out income arising under a settlement where the total amount of the relevant settlement income paid to or for the benefit of the settlor's relevant child(ren) in a tax year does not exceed £100. The threshold has been at £100 since the de-minimis was introduced; it has not been RPI-uprated and does not adjust for inflation.</p>

<p>HMRC's published practice at <a href="https://www.gov.uk/hmrc-internal-manuals/trusts-settlements-and-estates-manual/tsem4205">TSEM4205</a> and the worked examples at <a href="https://www.gov.uk/hmrc-internal-manuals/trusts-settlements-and-estates-manual/tsem4300">TSEM4300</a> treat any income above the £100 threshold as triggering full s.629 attribution on the entire settlement income for the year, not just the excess. For property settlements that produce material rental income (typically £8,000 to £25,000 per year on a single BTL share), the £100 threshold is irrelevant. The de-minimis is useful for tiny incidental income streams (small interest balances on a bare-trust bank account, modest dividend streams on bare-trust share holdings) but is not a planning tool for property income.</p>

<h2>Block 3: the grandparent-as-settlor route</h2>

<p>s.629(1) attributes income to "the settlor". Where the grandparent (not the parent) is the settlor of the bare trust, the grandparent is the s.629 settlor; income is attributed to the grandparent, not pushed through to the parent.</p>

<p>The structural advantage: where the grandparent is retired, lives within or below the personal allowance and basic-rate band, or has tax characteristics that produce a low effective rate, the s.629 outcome can be materially better than the parent route. A retired grandparent with no other income (£12,570 personal allowance for 2026/27) can typically absorb £10,000-£15,000 of attributed rental income from a single-flat settlement without paying any tax at all, or paying basic-rate tax on the slice above the personal allowance.</p>

<p>Three constraints on the grandparent route.</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>The grandparent must be the genuine settlor.</strong> HMRC's substance-over-form approach (set out at TSEM4300 with the Mr J example, where HMRC looked through the grandmother's nominal gift to identify Mr J as the true wealth-creating settlor) means the grandparent's settlement must reflect their own resources, not a parent's wealth funnelled through the grandparent. A parent-funded gift channelled via the grandparent is liable to be characterised as a parent-settled arrangement on a substance basis.</li>
<li><strong>The grandparent's seven-year IHT clock runs.</strong> The PET attached to the grandparent's gift carries the grandparent's seven-year clock; if the grandparent dies within seven years the gift may fall back into the grandparent's death estate (with taper relief at years 3-7). The grandparent's IHT estate planning may be affected by the gift more than the parent's would have been.</li>
<li><strong>The grandparent retains no continuing interest.</strong> Any structure under which the grandparent (or grandparent's spouse) retains a benefit triggers GROB on the grandparent's death-side and may trigger s.624 settlor-with-interest on the income-tax side. The clean grandparent route has the grandparent making a complete gift with no continuing benefit; the structural fix for a grandparent who wants to retain rent is to use a different structure (loan to bare trustees, or commercial arrangement with the bare trust under which the grandparent provides services for value).</li>
</ul>

<h2>Worked scenario 1: parent-to-minor bare trust</h2>

<p>Mr and Mrs Patel own a £400,000 BTL outright in their joint names. They want to start the IHT seven-year clock running and crystallise the CGT base cost in their 12-year-old daughter's hands. They execute a declaration of trust transferring 10% of the beneficial share (£40,000) to their daughter, with Mr Patel as bare trustee for the daughter's interest. The arrangement runs as follows.</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>CGT on gift:</strong> 10% of latent gain (latent gain on the property is £180,000; the daughter's slice is £18,000). Connected-person market-value disposal under TCGA 1992 s.17. Higher-rate residential CGT at 24% on £15,000 (after £3,000 AEA) = £3,600. Reported and paid within 60 days under TCGA Schedule 2.</li>
<li><strong>IHT on gift:</strong> £40,000 PET; seven-year clock starts on the gift date. The daughter does not "accept" the gift in any formal sense (minors cannot legally accept gifts but the bare-trust structure operates regardless); the PET is the Patels' gift.</li>
<li><strong>Rental income post-gift:</strong> 10% of the annual rent (typically £18,000-£24,000 on a £400,000 BTL) = £1,800-£2,400 per year, payable to the daughter's bare-trust account. ITTOIA 2005 s.629 attributes this back to the Patels at their marginal rate. Mr Patel is the settlor (he funded the gift); s.629 attributes to him. Mr Patel's higher-rate marginal income tax of 40% on £2,400 = £960 of income tax per year, due from Mr Patel personally.</li>
<li><strong>Capital growth post-gift:</strong> 10% of the property's continuing appreciation accrues in the daughter's hands free of attribution. Where the property appreciates 5% per year over the next six years (to the daughter's 18th birthday), the daughter's £40,000 starting share grows to approximately £53,600. The £13,600 of growth accrues in the daughter's beneficial estate; no s.629 attribution; the daughter's CGT base cost remains the £40,000 gift-date MV.</li>
<li><strong>18th-birthday cliff:</strong> from the daughter's 18th birthday, s.629 ceases prospectively. Rental income arising on the 10% share from the 18th birthday onwards is the daughter's income at her marginal rate (subject to her personal allowance and savings allowance). The bare-trust structure may continue with the daughter's consent; the daughter can call for legal title at any point past her 18th birthday under the rule in Saunders v Vautier.</li>
</ul>

<p>The Patels' overall position: £3,600 dry CGT at gift date plus approximately £6,000 of income tax over the six pre-18 years (£960 a year × 6, plus a small annual uplift as rent grows). Total Year 0 to Year 6 cost: approximately £9,600. In exchange, the daughter has a £40,000 share with growth running at full appreciation, and the seven-year PET clock is running. If both Patels survive seven years, the £40,000 gift drops out of their joint estate, saving £16,000 in IHT (40% of £40,000 at second death). The structure is marginally net-positive on the joint-estate IHT side, with the trade-off that the parents are paying the income tax in the meantime.</p>

<h2>Worked scenario 2: grandparent-to-grandchild bare trust</h2>

<p>Mrs Williams (retired, personal allowance only, no other income) settles a £200,000 flat on bare trust for her 8-year-old grandson. Mrs Williams is the bare trustee and the s.629 settlor. The arrangement runs as follows.</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>CGT on gift:</strong> latent gain on the flat is £80,000 (Mrs Williams bought it at £120,000 some years earlier). Connected-person market-value disposal under TCGA s.17. Mrs Williams is a basic-rate taxpayer on her personal allowance; her residential CGT rate is 18% on the gain after AEA. CGT = 18% × £77,000 = £13,860. Reported and paid within 60 days.</li>
<li><strong>IHT on gift:</strong> £200,000 PET from Mrs Williams' estate; seven-year clock runs from gift date.</li>
<li><strong>Rental income post-gift:</strong> the flat produces £12,000 a year in rent, payable to the grandson's bare-trust account. s.629 attributes the rental income to Mrs Williams (the grandparent settlor), not to either parent. Mrs Williams uses her £12,570 personal allowance to absorb the £12,000 of attributed rental income; she pays £0 of income tax on the rent. Where the rent grows above the personal allowance, she would pay basic-rate tax (20%) on the excess.</li>
<li><strong>Capital growth post-gift:</strong> the grandson's beneficial estate captures the full appreciation of the flat from gift date. At a typical 4% annual appreciation, the £200,000 share grows to approximately £253,000 over the 10 years to the grandson's 18th birthday. The £53,000 of growth accrues in the grandson's hands; no s.629 attribution; the grandson's CGT base cost is the £200,000 gift-date MV.</li>
<li><strong>18th-birthday cliff:</strong> at age 18, s.629 ceases. From then on, rental income on the flat is the grandson's income at his own marginal rate with his own personal allowance available.</li>
</ul>

<p>Mrs Williams' overall position: £13,860 dry CGT at gift date, £0 of income tax over the 10 pre-18 years (assuming the rent stays within her personal allowance). Total Year 0 to Year 10 cost: approximately £13,860. The grandson has a £200,000 share with full growth running and the seven-year PET clock is running on Mrs Williams' estate. If Mrs Williams survives seven years, the £200,000 gift drops out of her estate; IHT saving (40% of £200,000) is £80,000. The structure is materially net-positive: £13,860 of dry CGT in exchange for £80,000 of IHT saving on death, plus £53,000 of capital growth accruing in the grandson's hands rather than Mrs Williams'.</p>

<h2>The capital-growth loophole</h2>

<p>s.629 attributes "income arising under a settlement". It does not attribute capital gains. The asymmetry is the planning lever for parents and grandparents who want to crystallise CGT base cost in the child's hands at gift-date MV and let appreciation run free of any attribution.</p>

<p>Three structural ways to exploit the asymmetry.</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Pre-appreciation gift.</strong> Gift the property to the child before any further appreciation occurs. The child's base cost is the gift-date MV; subsequent growth accrues in the child's hands. The dry CGT bite at gift is calculated on the latent gain to date, which the gift "freezes" at the parent's level; the child takes the property at the new higher base cost.</li>
<li><strong>Phased gift via declarations of trust.</strong> Stage the gift in tranches over years, using the parent's annual exempt amount each year to shelter small slices of gain. Each annual slice transfers a fresh chunk of the beneficial estate to the child's growing share, with the income attribution still applying on each slice but the capital base-cost transfer accumulating over time.</li>
<li><strong>FIC variant for substantial portfolios.</strong> For families with multiple BTLs and a long planning horizon, the FIC route described in our sibling page on <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/gifting-property-to-adult-children-decision-tree-cgt-iht-occupancy-mechanics">adult-child gifting</a> can be adapted for minor-child planning. The shares are settled on bare trust for the minor; s.629 attributes the dividend income but the shares appreciate in the minor's hands. The structure has more moving parts but the principle is the same: income attributes back, capital growth runs in the child's hands.</li>
</ul>

<p>The loophole has limits. The gift is a real transfer of beneficial ownership; the parent is not getting the asset back. The income attribution is a real tax cost paid out of the parent's resources. The 18th-birthday cliff means the child gains full control at majority, regardless of the parent's wishes. For families whose primary planning goal is IHT mitigation plus base-cost reset in the next generation, the loophole works. For families whose primary goal is asset retention or governance control, the bare-trust route is not the right structure; the formal-discretionary-trust route with s.260 holdover is the alternative.</p>

<h2>The 18th-birthday cliff and what changes</h2>

<p>Three things change simultaneously on the child's 18th birthday.</p>

<p>First, ITTOIA 2005 s.629 ceases to apply prospectively. Rental income arising on the property from the 18th birthday onwards is the now-adult child's income at the child's own marginal rate. The cliff is hard: income arising on 17th-birthday-eve is attributed to the settlor; income arising the next morning is the child's. Pre-cliff attribution is not unwound retrospectively; the parent (or grandparent) keeps the past attribution as their final position for those years.</p>

<p>Second, the bare-trust legal-title arrangement becomes unwindable on the beneficiary's demand. The rule in Saunders v Vautier (1841) gives an absolutely-entitled beneficiary of full age and capacity the right to call for the trust to be wound up and the property transferred to them. The trustees can maintain the bare-trust holding pattern with the beneficiary's consent but cannot resist a Saunders v Vautier demand. Families that want to delay the beneficiary's full control past 18 should use a formal discretionary or interest-in-possession trust, not a bare trust.</p>

<p>Third, the property starts to feature in the now-adult child's own income tax structure: personal allowance (£12,570 for 2026/27), basic-rate band (£37,700 to £50,270), savings allowance, dividend allowance. For most 18-year-olds, the marginal rate is low or zero on the early-career income, which makes the post-18 income-tax position materially more efficient than the parent-route attribution that ran during minority.</p>

<h2>IHT mechanics: PET runs from gift date, not from the child's birth</h2>

<p>The seven-year PET clock runs from the gift date, not from the child's 18th birthday or from any other later milestone. Settling property on bare trust for a 10-year-old means the seven-year clock completes when the child is 17; for a 15-year-old, the clock completes during the child's first year of adulthood; for an 8-year-old, the clock completes when the child is 15.</p>

<p>Where the donor (parent or grandparent) survives the seven years, the gift drops out of the donor's estate entirely; the property is in the child's beneficial estate from the gift date and stays there. Where the donor dies within seven years, the gift falls back into the death estate at the gift-date MV with taper relief at years 3-7 under IHTA 1984 s.7(4): 20% relief in year 3-4, 40% in year 4-5, 60% in year 5-6, 80% in year 6-7. The property's continuing appreciation post-gift does not enlarge the IHT-on-death exposure; the relevant figure is the gift-date MV, frozen.</p>

<p>The bare-trust mechanism does not put the property into the relevant property regime; no 10-year periodic charges apply under IHTA 1984 s.64. The property is the child's at general law from gift date; the trustees' role is purely custodial. For IHT purposes the child holds the property absolutely.</p>

<h2>TRS registration and the 90-day clock</h2>

<p>Money Laundering Regulations 2017 reg 45ZA requires registration of any UK express trust that acquires an interest in UK land, with the Trust Registration Service running from gov.uk. The bare-trust-for-minor structure falls within scope. The 90-day clock runs from the date the trust acquires the land interest (which may be the purchase completion date for new acquisitions, or the date of the declaration of trust for transfers of existing property). Failure to register can result in a £5,000 penalty per trust.</p>

<p>The information required: settlor name and date of birth and country of residence (parent or grandparent), beneficiary name and date of birth and country of residence (the minor child), trustee names and dates of birth, property description with title number, and the trust's tax-resident status. Updates within 90 days are required when any of these details change (new trustees, additional property, transfers out).</p>

<h2>Common mistakes to avoid</h2>

<ol>
<li><strong>Assuming bare-trust transparency escapes s.629.</strong> The single most common drift in family-planning content. The bare-trust mechanism is CGT-and-IHT transparency; it does not block income-tax attribution under s.629. The parent (or other settlor) is the income-tax taxpayer on the rent regardless of the bare-trust structure.</li>
<li><strong>Confusing s.624 with s.629.</strong> The two statutory mechanisms are separate. s.624 catches arrangements where the settlor retains an interest (settlor-with-interest); s.629 catches arrangements where the beneficiary is the settlor's minor child. The two can overlap (a parent retaining an interest in a bare trust for their minor child triggers both) but they are not the same hook. Practitioner content frequently conflates them.</li>
<li><strong>Routing a parent-funded gift through the grandparent for tax purposes.</strong> HMRC's substance-over-form approach (TSEM4300 with the Mr J example) treats arrangements where the grandparent is a paper settlor of the parent's wealth as parent-settled for s.629 purposes. The genuine grandparent route requires the gift to come from the grandparent's own resources.</li>
<li><strong>Ignoring the HICBC interaction.</strong> Where a parent picks up attributed rental income that pushes them into the £60,000-£80,000 adjusted-net-income band, the family loses some or all of their child benefit. The HICBC interaction with s.629 attribution is one of the under-flagged cost lines on the parent-route gift.</li>
<li><strong>Drafting a "bare trust" that defers vesting past 18.</strong> A deed that defers the beneficiary's absolute entitlement past majority (typically to 21, 25, or 30) is not a bare trust; it is a discretionary or contingent trust with materially different tax consequences (CLT entry, 10-year periodic charges, trust-rate income tax). Drafting precision matters.</li>
<li><strong>Forgetting the TRS clock.</strong> The 90-day window from trust creation closes quickly and missing it produces penalties up to £5,000 per trust. Bare-trust structures over UK land are within scope regardless of whether the trust pays tax.</li>
</ol>

<h2>Closing the loop</h2>

<p>The minor-child gift decision is shaped by the three statutory blocks more than by the family's preferences. The bare-trust route is required by Block 1; the income-tax attribution is forced by Block 2; the capital-growth loophole is opened by the asymmetry of Block 3. Within those constraints, the parent-versus-grandparent settlor choice is the single most valuable variable. Where a grandparent is available as settlor, the income-tax outcome can be materially better than the parent route. Where the family must use the parent route, the structure works as a base-cost-reset and IHT-clock-starting mechanism, with the income-tax-attribution cost as the price. The cleanest answer for a long-horizon family is often the grandparent route layered with the FIC alternative for substantial portfolios; the cleanest answer for a single-property family with a parent-as-settlor constraint is the bare trust with eyes open about the s.629 attribution. The structural choice merits a conversation rather than a default.</p>
