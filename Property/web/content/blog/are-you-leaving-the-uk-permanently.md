  - question: "What does 'leaving the UK permanently' actually mean for tax purposes?"
    answer: "For Capital Gains Tax purposes specifically, the operative line is the TCGA 1992 s.10A temporary-non-residence trap. You are a 'temporary non-resident' (with gains realised during non-residence deemed to arise in the year of return) where your period of non-UK residence is 5 years or less AND you were UK-resident in 4 or more of the 7 tax years before departure. To shed the s.10A trap on non-UK gains realised during your time away, you need MORE than 5 complete tax years of non-UK-residence. For Inheritance Tax purposes, the FA 2025 long-term-resident regime is the operative tail: a long-term UK resident (UK-resident in 10 of the previous 20 tax years) remains within UK IHT on worldwide assets for a tail period scaling 3 to 10 years post-departure. For Income Tax purposes (UK rental income), the Non-Resident Landlord scheme operates whenever you are UK-non-resident, regardless of permanence."
  - question: "How does the Statutory Residence Test apply in the departure year?"
    answer: "FA 2013 Schedule 45 cascade: automatic overseas tests (any one met means non-UK resident); automatic UK tests (any one met without overseas test triggered means UK resident); sufficient ties test (where no automatic test met, ties combined with UK days determine residence). For someone leaving the UK partway through a tax year, split-year treatment under Schedule 45 Part 3 may apply if Case 1 (starting full-time overseas work), Case 2 (partner of Case-1 individual) or Case 3 (ceasing to have any UK home) is satisfied. Split-year is not optional; the cases either apply or they do not, and statute determines priority where multiple cases compete."
  - question: "What is split-year treatment and how does Case 3 work for a UK home-seller?"
    answer: "Split-year treatment under FA 2013 Schedule 45 Part 3 splits a tax year that would otherwise be wholly UK-resident or wholly non-resident into a pre-departure UK part and a post-departure non-UK part. Case 3 (ceasing to have any UK home) applies where you have a UK home at the start of the tax year, you cease to have any UK home during the year, you are not UK-resident for the following tax year, and you spend no more than 15 days in the UK between the cease-to-have-home date and the end of the tax year. Selling or letting out your UK home before departure is the operative trigger. Case 1 (starting full-time overseas work) and Case 2 (partner of Case-1 individual) are alternative entry routes."
  - question: "The s.10A trap: why does selling a non-UK asset during non-residence not give CGT escape if I return within 5 years?"
    answer: "Because TCGA 1992 s.10A (per HMRC Capital Gains Manual CG26540) deems gains realised on assets owned at departure (and disposed during non-residence) to arise in the year of return when you become UK-resident again. The gateway test is that you were UK-resident in 4 or more of the 7 tax years before departure. The escape test is that your period of non-UK residence exceeds 5 complete tax years. Anyone who fails either test (gateway met, escape not met) has the gains recaptured in the return year at UK CGT rates prevailing in that year. ITA 2007 s.812 applies the same architecture to certain income (dividend and pension lump-sum income during the temporary-non-residence window)."
  - question: "Does s.10A catch gains on UK property sold during non-residence?"
    answer: "Generally not, because UK land is already in NRCGT under TCGA 1992 s.1A. Non-resident sellers of UK land file the 60-day NRCGT return and pay UK CGT at 18% (basic) and 24% (higher band) on residential disposals (post-30 October 2024). The s.10A overlay is most material for non-UK situs gains (non-UK shares, non-UK businesses, non-UK investment portfolios) realised during non-residence. There can be pre-NRCGT base-cost issues for UK land acquired before the 5 April 2015 (residential) or 5 April 2019 (non-residential / indirect disposal) rebasing dates that interact with s.10A, but the standard case is UK land in NRCGT, non-UK assets in s.10A."
  - question: "What is the NRL position for a landlord keeping UK rentals after the move?"
    answer: "20% basic-rate withholding by your letting agent or tenant unless you hold NRL1, NRL2 or NRL3 approval to receive rent gross. NRL is statutory under FA 1995 Schedule 23 and SI 1995/2902; treaty residence does not displace it. Apply for NRL1 BEFORE the move, ideally 6 weeks before departure, so the gross-payment approval is in place from the day of move. Even with gross-payment approval, you still register for UK Self-Assessment and file SA100 plus SA105 (UK property pages) annually. The NRL framework operates regardless of permanent vs temporary intent; UK rental income is UK-taxable throughout your non-residence period."
  - question: "What about the 60-day NRCGT return on UK property disposals during non-residence?"
    answer: "Required for every UK land disposal by a non-resident, regardless of whether tax is due. The 60-day clock starts on the day of legal completion (not exchange). Residential rates are 18% basic and 24% higher band (post-30 October 2024); AEA £3,000 for individuals retaining UK personal allowance, nil for non-resident companies. Indirect disposals of UK property-rich entity shares (75% UK land-value test plus 25% holder-stake test) are also caught under TCGA 1992 Schedule 1A Part 4. Schedule 4AA gives default 5 April 2015 rebasing for residential property acquired before that date and default 5 April 2019 rebasing for non-residential and indirect-disposal interests acquired before then."
  - question: "How does the new IHT long-term-resident regime apply post-departure?"
    answer: "Under IHTA 1984 ss.6A-6C (FA 2025 architecture, in force from 6 April 2025), a long-term UK resident is UK-resident in 10 of the previous 20 tax years. LTR status extends post-departure through a tail period: roughly 3 years if you were UK-resident in 13 or fewer of the preceding 20 years, scaling up to 10 years if you were UK-resident in all 20. The tail keeps worldwide assets within UK IHT for the LTR-tail-period duration. UK situs property (UK BTL portfolios) stays in UK IHT regardless of LTR status under IHTA 1984 Schedule A1 (in force since 6 April 2017). For long-term UK residents the LTR tail rewrites the historic 'stay away 3 years to escape UK IHT' planning script."
  - question: "What is the FIG regime for someone who left and now returns?"
    answer: "ITTOIA 2005 ss.845A-845J (inserted by FA 2025 s.37) gives Foreign Income and Gains relief to new arrivals who were not UK-resident in any of the 10 preceding tax years (the 10-of-prior-10-years gateway under s.845B(1)). FIG runs for up to 4 tax years with a per-year claim under s.845A. Foreign income and gains arising in claim years are UK-exempt. Costs: lost UK personal allowance, dividend allowance, and CGT AEA in each claim year. Claim deadline: 31 January 12 months after the end of the tax year. For someone who left the UK and returns within 10 years, FIG is not available; the 10-year non-residence gateway fails."
  - question: "What about the TRF for someone who claimed remittance basis pre-2025-26?"
    answer: "The Temporary Repatriation Facility under FA 2025 s.41 and Schedule 10 allows individuals who claimed the remittance basis pre-6-April-2025 to designate previously unremitted foreign income and gains for UK taxation at concessionary rates: 12% for designation in 2025/26 or 2026/27, and 15% for designation in 2027/28. The window closes 5 April 2028. Designation deadline per Schedule 10 paragraph 8(1) is 31 January 12 months after the end of the relevant tax year. The TRF is a transitional bridge for non-doms moving out of the historic remittance regime; for departing non-doms with substantial accumulated unremitted income or gains, TRF designation pre-departure can clear the historic position at a known rate."
  - question: "What about the CGT rebasing election for non-doms?"
    answer: "The CGT rebasing election under FA 2025 s.42 and Schedule 11 is narrow. Five conditions in Schedule 11 paragraph 1(1) including (importantly) condition 3: the asset is NOT UK situs at any time between 6 March 2024 and 5 April 2025. UK property is therefore NOT eligible for the FA 2025 rebasing election. UK land follows its own NRCGT rebasing dates: 5 April 2015 for residential, 5 April 2019 for non-residential and indirect-disposal interests, under Schedule 4AA TCGA 1992. The FA 2025 rebasing election is for non-UK assets only and helps non-doms who held non-UK assets through the pre-2025 remittance regime to step up the base cost to 5 April 2017 value."
  - question: "What is the destination-country DTA position?"
    answer: "Specific to the destination state, but a few general points apply across most modern UK DTAs. Article 6 of the OECD-model treaty allocates UK source-state taxing rights on UK immovable property income. Article 13 allocates UK source-state rights on direct disposals of UK immovable property and (in modern OECD-form treaties from 2018 onwards) on indirect disposals of UK property-rich entity shares. Article 4 tie-breaker resolves residence where dual-resident, but the tie-breaker affects treaty allocation only, not domestic-law UK residence under the Statutory Residence Test. For the specific UK-Isle of Man, UK-Spain and UK-India bilateral positions, see our country-specific DTA pages."

    answer: "Six steps. (1) Apply for NRL1 6 weeks before departure so gross-payment approval is in place from move date. <p>Permanent emigration is not the same thing as temporary non-residence for UK tax purposes, and the difference catches well-intentioned landlords who later return within 5 years. The operative line is the TCGA 1992 s.10A temporary-non-residence trap: anyone who has been UK-resident in 4 or more of the 7 tax years before departure and who returns within a period of non-UK residence of 5 years or less has the gains realised on non-UK assets during non-residence deemed to arise in the year of return and chargeable to UK CGT then. Genuine clean-break emigration means more than 5 complete tax years non-UK-resident.</p>
<p>The FA 2025 long-term-resident IHT regime adds a second tail. A long-term UK resident under IHTA 1984 ss.6A-6C (UK-resident in 10 of the previous 20 tax years) remains within UK Inheritance Tax on worldwide assets for a tail period scaling 3 to 10 years post-departure, depending on the prior-UK-resident-year count. UK situs property (any UK BTL portfolio retained through the move) stays in UK IHT regardless of LTR status under IHTA 1984 Schedule A1. The historic "stay away 3 years to escape UK IHT" planning script anchored on deemed-domicile is obsolete for anyone with 14 or more prior UK-resident years.</p>

<h2>The permanently-vs-temporarily decision tree</h2>
<p>In the first conversation about emigration, work through this nine-step sequence.</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Identify your departure year.</strong> When does your UK residence year-by-year actually break? SRT determines this, not intent. Use FA 2013 Sch 45 Part 1 cascade: automatic overseas tests first, then automatic UK tests, then sufficient ties.</li>
<li><strong>Check the split-year cases (Cases 1-3 for leaving).</strong> Case 1 (starting full-time overseas work); Case 2 (partner of Case-1 individual); Case 3 (ceasing to have any UK home). Split-year is NOT optional; statute determines application and priority per Sch 45 Part 3.</li>
<li><strong>Decide your minimum non-UK-resident period.</strong> To shed the s.10A 5-year recapture trap you need MORE than 5 complete tax years non-UK-resident (and you must have been UK-resident in 4 or more of the 7 preceding tax years to engage s.10A at all). If you cannot commit to 5+ complete non-UK tax years you are at risk of return-year recapture on gains realised during non-residence.</li>
<li><strong>Inventory your UK property holdings.</strong> Each UK rental property continues to be UK-taxable on rental income under ITTOIA 2005 Part 3 (individuals) or CTA 2009 Part 4 (companies); the domestic charge survives any treaty allocation. Each UK property disposal triggers NRCGT under TCGA 1992 s.1A plus the 60-day return regardless of whether tax is due.</li>
<li><strong>Apply for NRL1 (or NRL2 or NRL3) before the move.</strong> Without approval, your letting agent or tenant must withhold 20% basic rate. With approval you receive rent gross and self-assess on the UK return.</li>
<li><strong>Check your IHT LTR position under IHTA 1984 s.6A and run the tail-period table.</strong> If you have been UK-resident in 10 of the preceding 20 tax years you are LTR. On departure you remain LTR (worldwide-asset IHT exposure) for a tail period scaling 3-10 years depending on prior-UK-resident-year count. UK property is in UK IHT regardless of LTR status (UK situs).</li>
<li><strong>If you previously claimed remittance basis, consider TRF designation under FA 2025 s.41 and Schedule 10.</strong> Rates 12% / 12% / 15% across 2025/26, 2026/27, 2027/28; window closes 5 April 2028.</li>
<li><strong>Identify your destination-country DTA position.</strong> Article 4 tie-breaker may apply where dual-resident; Articles 6 and 13 allocate UK source-state rights on UK property regardless. See our country-specific DTA pages for bilateral detail.</li>
<li><strong>Engage specialist representation before the move.</strong> Pre-departure planning, tax-year-end disposals, and post-departure income and gain timing matter operationally.</li>
</ol>

<aside><p>The 5-year s.10A line is the single most important number in any departure conversation.</p><p>We work with landlords planning a permanent move overseas on the residence-year-by-year SRT analysis, the split-year case for the departure year, the NRL1 pre-move application, the IHT LTR tail-period modelling, and (where applicable) TRF designation for accumulated unremitted income or gains. The discipline matters because the s.10A trap and the LTR tail interact: a 4-year stint with planned return triggers BOTH the s.10A recapture and an LTR tail that may run for years more. Get in touch before the departure, not after.</p></aside>

<h2>Example one: Mrs Carter, UK landlord moving to Portugal permanently</h2>
<p>Mrs Carter was UK-resident throughout 2018/19 to 2025/26 (8 of preceding 20 tax years), UK-born and UK-domiciled-of-origin. She sells her UK consulting business in 2025 and moves to a Portuguese coastal town in May 2026 intending to settle for life. She retains a London BTL flat and a Manchester BTL flat through the move.</p>
<p>SRT in 2026/27 departure year: depending on Portugal arrival date, UK day-count, workdays and ties, the year likely splits. Case 3 (ceasing to have any UK home) applies if she sells her London home before moving (her primary UK home goes); otherwise Case 1 if she starts full-time overseas work in Portugal. Either way, split-year treatment for 2026/27 treats her as UK-resident only for the pre-departure part-year.</p>
<p>s.10A position: she was UK-resident in 4 or more of the 7 preceding tax years (well above the gateway). To shed s.10A she needs more than 5 complete non-UK tax years (2027/28, 2028/29, 2029/30, 2030/31, 2031/32 gives 5 complete; she needs 2032/33 too, so 6 complete non-UK tax years to be safe). NRL on continuing UK rentals: she applies for NRL1 6 weeks before the May 2026 move; HMRC approves; her letting agents pay her gross from the approval date; she self-assesses UK rental income each year via SA100 plus SA105 with NRL6 evidence on file. NRCGT on continuing UK property: if she sells either UK BTL during non-residence, the 60-day NRCGT return is required regardless of whether tax is due. Residential rates 18% and 24% from 30 October 2024; AEA £3,000 (preserved for individuals retaining UK personal allowance under ITA 2007 s.56 because she is a UK national).</p>
<p>IHT LTR position: Mrs Carter has been UK-resident for 8 of the preceding 20 years, BELOW the 10-of-20 LTR threshold under IHTA 1984 s.6A. She is NOT LTR. On departure, she is immediately outside UK IHT for worldwide assets. UK property remains in UK IHT regardless (UK situs). Pre-FA-2025 contrast: under historic deemed-domicile, Mrs Carter would have shed UK-domicile (UK-domicile-of-origin) only through acquiring a Portuguese domicile of choice, a high common-law evidential bar (intent plus physical move plus cutting UK ties). Post-FA-2025 LTR is residence-based; domicile is irrelevant; the LTR 10-of-20 test resolves cleanly. Destination DTA: UK-Portugal Convention 1968 (verify currency at consultation date; check whether MLI-modified). Article 6 allocates UK source-state rights on UK property income; Article 13 on UK property gains; Article 4 tie-breaker available where dual-resident.</p>

<h2>Example two: Mr Ellis returns to UK after 4 years in Australia</h2>
<p>Mr Ellis was UK-resident throughout 2018/19 to 2025/26 (satisfies the s.10A 4-of-7-preceding gateway). He moves to Sydney in June 2026 for a 5-year corporate role intending eventually to settle in Australia. In 2028/29, while Australian-resident, he sells a portfolio of pre-departure-acquired Australian shares for a £210,000 gain. Plans change; he and his family return to the UK in May 2030 after 4 complete non-UK tax years (2026/27, 2027/28, 2028/29, 2029/30).</p>
<p>s.10A position: Mr Ellis's non-UK residence period is 4 complete tax years, within the "5 years or less" window. He had been UK-resident in 4 or more of the 7 preceding tax years. He is a temporary non-resident. Effect: the £210,000 gain on Australian shares is deemed to arise in his 2030/31 return year and chargeable to UK CGT in that year. UK property gain (separate analysis): if Mr Ellis had sold a UK BTL during his non-residence, NRCGT under TCGA 1992 s.1A applied at the time of disposal (60-day return plus 18% / 24% residential). s.10A does not overlay UK land (already in NRCGT); s.10A primarily catches non-UK situs assets.</p>
<p>Australian-side credit: Australia taxed him on the £210,000 gain in 2028/29 (Australian-resident at the time of disposal). UK CGT on the same gain in 2030/31 is creditable against the Australian CGT already paid, but only up to the lower of the two amounts. The Australian-side procedural mechanic for retrospective credit may be cumbersome; specialist Australian and UK advice needed. Planning lesson: to break s.10A, Mr Ellis needed MORE than 5 complete non-UK tax years. A 4-year stint with planned return crystallises the recapture risk on non-UK gains realised during non-residence. The planning rule is straightforward: if you are uncertain about staying away for 5 or more complete tax years, defer non-UK asset disposals until you have committed to the 6 or more year minimum, or realise them BEFORE departure to claim UK CGT at exit-year rates with PRR, AEA and loss-offset planning.</p>

<aside><p>The s.10A trap catches almost every emigrant who later returns within 5 years.</p><p>Where the realistic non-residence horizon is uncertain or short, we model two alternative scenarios with the client: defer non-UK asset disposals to the sixth complete non-UK tax year or beyond, OR realise the gains before departure at UK CGT exit-year rates with planning. The pre-departure realisation route often wins on simple arithmetic because UK CGT at 18% or 24% residential is below most foreign CGT regimes and you avoid the Australian-or-equivalent FTC-recovery friction entirely. Run both arms before the disposal decisions crystallise.</p></aside>

<h2>Example four: Sra Marquez, departing non-dom with accumulated unremitted income</h2>
<p>Sra Marquez is a Spanish citizen, UK-resident from 2016/17 to 2025/26 (claimed the remittance basis annually until 6 April 2025), with £620,000 of unremitted Spanish rental and Spanish dividend income accumulated across that period. She moves back to Madrid permanently in October 2026 (becomes Spanish-resident from 2026/27; split-year treatment may apply to the 2026/27 departure year).</p>
<p>TRF position under FA 2025 s.41 and Schedule 10: Sra Marquez is eligible to designate previously unremitted foreign income and gains for UK taxation at concessionary rates: 12% for designation in 2025/26 or 2026/27, 15% for designation in 2027/28. The window closes 5 April 2028. If she designates the full £620,000 in 2026/27, the UK tax bill is £74,400 (12% of £620,000) versus a worst-case marginal-rate-plus-historic-remittance position that could materially exceed that amount. Designation deadline per Schedule 10 paragraph 8(1) is 31 January 12 months after the end of the relevant tax year, so 31 January 2028 for a 2026/27 designation.</p>
<p>LTR position: Sra Marquez has been UK-resident for 10 of preceding 20 years (2016/17 to 2025/26 is exactly 10 years), so she just satisfies the LTR threshold under IHTA 1984 s.6A. Her LTR tail per the tail-period mechanism is roughly 3 consecutive non-UK tax years. Worldwide assets stay in UK IHT until the end of approximately 2029/30. CGT rebasing election: she qualifies for the FA 2025 Schedule 11 rebasing election on her non-UK assets (Spanish residential and commercial holdings not in the UK situs) provided the five Schedule 11 paragraph 1(1) conditions are met. UK property held during her UK residence (if any) is NOT eligible for the rebasing because of condition 3 (asset not UK-situs between 6 March 2024 and 5 April 2025). Practical sequencing: TRF designation in 2026/27 to clear the historic unremitted position at 12%, rebasing election where appropriate for non-UK assets, IHT planning for the 3-year LTR tail, and Spanish-side tax registration on arrival.</p>

<h2>The clean-break checklist in practice</h2>
<p>For the standard permanent-emigration case, the operational sequence in the 12 months before move is consistent across destinations. Six steps. First, apply for NRL1 (or NRL2 if a UK letting agent holds the rental account, or NRL3 if you let directly without an agent) six weeks before departure so gross-payment approval is in place from the move date. Second, inform HMRC of leaving via form P85 (or via the Self-Assessment return at the next year-end). Third, close or let your UK home before or immediately on move if you want Case 3 split-year treatment for the departure year; otherwise rely on Case 1 (full-time overseas work) or Case 2 (partner of a Case-1 individual).</p>
<p>Fourth, plan any post-departure non-UK asset disposals to AVOID falling within the 5-year s.10A window: defer until your sixth complete non-UK tax year or realise the gains BEFORE departure at UK CGT exit-year rates with PRR, AEA and loss-offset planning. Fifth, check your IHT LTR position under IHTA 1984 s.6A and run the tail-period analysis if you are LTR; consider lifetime gifting in the pre-departure window to use the 7-year PET clock on assets that will be in your estate during the LTR tail. Sixth, engage destination-country tax advice early because the FTC mechanics on UK rental income and any UK CGT paid run through the destination return, not the UK return; pre-arrival planning on the destination side typically saves more tax than post-arrival planning.</p>
