<p>Many UK property landlords consider incorporating their rental business to benefit from lower corporation tax rates and overcome Section 24 restrictions. However, when transferring properties to a limited company, <strong>incorporation relief section 162 landlords</strong> often find this valuable CGT relief doesn't apply to their situation.</p>

<p>Section 162 incorporation relief can defer capital gains tax when transferring a business to a company, but HMRC takes a strict view on what constitutes a "business" versus investment activity. For most property landlords, this distinction is crucial and often disappointing.</p>

<h2>What Is Section 162 Incorporation Relief?</h2>

<p>Section 162 of the Taxation of Chargeable Gains Act 1992 allows individuals to defer capital gains tax when transferring their business to a company in exchange for shares. The relief works by reducing the base cost of the shares received, effectively rolling over the gain until the shares are eventually sold.</p>

<p>For the relief to apply, three key conditions must be met:</p>

<ul>
<li>The transfer must be of a business (not just assets)</li>
<li>The business must be transferred as a going concern</li>
<li>The consideration must be wholly or mainly in shares</li>
</ul>

<p>The relief is automatic if these conditions are satisfied, but for property landlords, the first condition typically presents an insurmountable hurdle.</p>

<h2>Why Property Landlords Usually Don't Qualify</h2>

<p>HMRC's position is clear: ordinary buy-to-let activity is considered investment rather than trading. The key distinction lies in the level of activity and services provided to tenants.</p>

<p>Typical rental activity that HMRC considers investment includes:</p>

<ul>
<li>Collecting rent from tenants</li>
<li>Basic property maintenance</li>
<li>Finding replacement tenants</li>
<li>Managing a portfolio of buy-to-let properties</li>
</ul>

<p>Even landlords with substantial portfolios often fail to meet the "business" test. A landlord with 20 buy-to-let properties generating £200,000 annual rental income would typically still be considered an investor rather than trader for <strong>section 162 property</strong> relief purposes.</p>

<h3>HMRC's Business Test</h3>

<p>HMRC applies several factors when determining whether property activity constitutes a business:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Services provided:</strong> Are substantial services provided to tenants beyond basic landlord duties?</li>
<li><strong>Level of activity:</strong> Is there significant day-to-day involvement and decision-making?</li>
<li><strong>Commercial organisation:</strong> Is the activity organised and systematic?</li>
<li><strong>Risk and management:</strong> Are there business risks beyond property value fluctuations?</li>
</ul>

<p>Standard residential buy-to-let activity typically fails these tests, regardless of the number of properties owned.</p>

<h2>When Might Property Activity Qualify?</h2>

<p>Some property-related activities may qualify for <strong>CGT relief incorporating property business</strong>, though these scenarios are relatively uncommon:</p>

<h3>Serviced Accommodation</h3>

<p>Operating serviced apartments with substantial additional services might qualify:</p>

<ul>
<li>Daily cleaning and housekeeping</li>
<li>Concierge services</li>
<li>Business facilities provision</li>
<li>Meal services or catering</li>
</ul>

<p>Note that the <a href="/blog/landlord-tax-essentials/property-investment-tax-uk-complete-guide-2026">furnished holiday lettings regime was abolished from April 2025</a>, removing one potential route to business classification.</p>

<h3>Property Development</h3>

<p>Systematic property development for profit typically constitutes trading rather than investment. Developers who purchase, renovate, and sell properties as their main activity often qualify for incorporation relief.</p>

<h3>Property Management Services</h3>

<p>Landlords who provide substantial management services to other property owners might qualify, though this would be a separate business from their own rental portfolio.</p>

<h2>Tax Consequences of Incorporation Without Relief</h2>

<p>When Section 162 relief doesn't apply, landlords face immediate capital gains tax on the transfer to their company. This can create significant cash flow challenges.</p>

<h3>CGT Calculation Example</h3>

<p>Consider a landlord incorporating three buy-to-let properties:</p>

<ul>
<li>Purchase prices: £150,000, £180,000, £200,000 (total £530,000)</li>
<li>Current values: £250,000, £280,000, £320,000 (total £850,000)</li>
<li>Total gain: £320,000</li>
<li>CGT at 24% (higher rate): £76,800</li>
</ul>

<p>This immediate tax bill must be funded separately from the incorporation, as the properties transfer to the company rather than generating cash for the landlord.</p>

<h3>Annual Exempt Amount</h3>

<p>The CGT annual exempt amount of £3,000 provides minimal relief for substantial property transfers. Landlords with significant gains should consider timing transfers across multiple tax years if possible.</p>

<h2>Alternative Reliefs and Planning Options</h2>

<p>While Section 162 relief typically isn't available, other options may reduce the incorporation tax cost:</p>

<h3>Gift Relief (Section 165)</h3>

<p>Gift relief may apply if properties are gifted to the company rather than sold. However, this typically requires the company to issue no consideration, which may not align with commercial objectives.</p>

<h3>Staged Incorporation</h3>

<p>Transferring properties gradually over several years can utilise multiple years' annual exempt amounts, though the total tax saving is limited given the £3,000 annual allowance.</p>

<h3>Principal Private Residence Relief</h3>

<p>Former residential properties may qualify for <a href="/blog/capital-gains-tax/principal-private-residence-relief-landlords">partial principal private residence relief</a>, reducing the gain subject to CGT on incorporation.</p>

<h2>Planning Around Section 24 Restrictions</h2>

<p>Many landlords consider incorporation primarily to escape <a href="/blog/section-24-and-tax-relief/section-24-tax-relief-complete-guide">Section 24 restrictions on mortgage interest relief</a>. When Section 162 relief isn't available, the decision becomes more complex.</p>

<p>Key factors to consider include:</p>

<ul>
<li>Annual rental income and mortgage costs</li>
<li>Current and projected tax rates</li>
<li>CGT cost of incorporation</li>
<li>Long-term exit strategy</li>
</ul>

<p>For some landlords, the ongoing tax savings from <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/buy-to-let-limited-company-complete-guide-uk">operating through a limited company</a> justify paying CGT upfront, even without incorporation relief.</p>

<h2>Professional Advice Is Essential</h2>

<p>The interaction between incorporation relief rules, capital gains tax, and property taxation is complex. HMRC's interpretation of what constitutes a "business" can be subjective, and each landlord's circumstances are unique.</p>

<p>Before making any incorporation decisions, landlords should:</p>

<ul>
<li>Obtain specialist tax advice on their specific circumstances</li>
<li>Model the total tax cost over several years</li>
<li>Consider alternative structures and timing</li>
<li>Review their long-term property investment strategy</li>
</ul>

<p>A <a href="/blog/property-accountant-services/what-does-a-property-accountant-do">specialist property accountant</a> can provide detailed calculations and help navigate the complex rules around incorporation relief and property taxation.</p>

<h2>Future Changes and Considerations</h2>

<p>The property tax landscape continues to evolve. From April 2027, separate tax rates will apply to property income (22% basic rate, 42% higher rate, 47% additional rate), potentially affecting incorporation decisions.</p>

<p>Additionally, <a href="/blog/making-tax-digital-mtd/making-tax-digital-landlords-april-2026-deadline">Making Tax Digital requirements from April 2026</a> will increase compliance burdens for landlords, potentially making corporate structures more attractive despite the incorporation costs.</p>

<p>Landlords should regularly review their structure as tax rules and personal circumstances change. What doesn't make sense today may become beneficial in future years.</p>

<h2>Conclusion</h2>

<p>Section 162 incorporation relief rarely applies to property landlords because HMRC treats most rental activity as investment rather than trading. While this means landlords typically face immediate CGT on incorporating their property portfolio, other factors may still justify the incorporation cost.</p>

<p>The decision to incorporate should consider the total tax position over several years, not just the immediate incorporation relief availability. With proper planning and professional advice, many landlords find incorporation beneficial despite paying CGT upfront.</p>

<p>Each situation is unique, and the rules around business classification can be subjective. Landlords considering incorporation should seek specialist advice to understand their options and ensure they're making the most tax-efficient decisions for their circumstances.</p>
