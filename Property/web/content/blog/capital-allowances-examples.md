- question: What is the writing-down allowance rate for capital allowances now?
  answer: The main-pool writing-down allowance is 14% per year on a reducing-balance basis. It was cut from 18% to 14% by Finance Act 2026 section 28, which substitutes 14% into CAA 2001 section 56, with effect for chargeable periods beginning on or after 1 April 2026 for corporation tax and 6 April 2026 for income tax. A period that straddles the start date uses a hybrid, time-apportioned rate between 18% and 14%. The special-rate pool, which holds integral features, stays at 6% and was not changed.
- question: Is the Annual Investment Allowance still £1 million?
  answer: Yes. The Annual Investment Allowance is £1,000,000 and it is permanent. CAA 2001 section 51A(5) sets the maximum at £1,000,000, made permanent from 1 April 2023 by Finance (No.2) Act 2023 section 8. The earlier framing of the £1 million as temporary, due to revert to £200,000, is out of date. The allowance is one figure per business or per related group of companies, not per property.
- question: Can individual landlords claim full expensing?
  answer: No. Full expensing, the 100% first-year allowance for new and unused main-rate plant under CAA 2001 section 45S, is available only to companies within the charge to corporation tax. Sole traders, partnerships and individual landlords cannot claim it. The route for unincorporated businesses is the new 40% first-year allowance instead, alongside the £1 million Annual Investment Allowance.
- question: What is the new 40% first-year allowance?
  answer: Finance Act 2026 section 29 introduced a 40% first-year allowance on new and unused main-rate plant and machinery, for expenditure incurred on or after 1 January 2026 (it inserts CAA 2001 section 45U). It excludes cars, second-hand assets and special-rate (integral-feature) expenditure. It is not restricted by incorporation status, but in practice it is the route for those who cannot use full expensing, that is unincorporated businesses and leasing, because a company buying qualifying new main-rate plant normally claims 100% full expensing instead.
- question: Can I claim capital allowances on a standard buy-to-let?
  answer: "Generally no. CAA 2001 section 35 says expenditure is not qualifying expenditure if it is incurred on plant or machinery for use in a dwelling-house. That bars plant and machinery allowances on boilers, kitchens and bathrooms inside a let dwelling. Residential landlords instead claim Replacement of Domestic Items Relief under ITTOIA 2005 section 311A on like-for-like replacements of furniture, furnishings and appliances. Two carve-outs survive: plant in the common parts of a block (a communal boiler, lift or lighting) and integral features in qualifying non-dwelling areas."
- question: How much is the special-rate pool written down each year?
  answer: The special-rate pool is written down at 6% per year on a reducing-balance basis. It holds integral features under CAA 2001 section 33A (electrical and lighting systems, cold water systems, space and water heating, ventilation and air cooling, lifts and escalators, and external solar shading) plus long-life assets. This 6% rate was not changed by Finance Act 2026; only the main pool moved, from 18% to 14%.
- question: Why do I need a section 198 election when buying a commercial property?
  answer: When a commercial property is sold with fixtures, the buyer can only claim allowances on those fixtures if two gates are passed. First, the past owner must have pooled the expenditure (the pooling requirement). Second, the value must be fixed within two years of the purchase, normally by a joint section 198 election. CAA 2001 section 187A(3) treats the new expenditure as nil if the fixed-value requirement applies but is not satisfied, so missing the election can lose the allowances permanently.
- question: How long do I have to make a section 198 election?
  answer: Two years. CAA 2001 section 201 sets a two-year time limit running from the date the buyer acquires the qualifying interest (or, for a lease, from the grant of the lease). If the deadline passes with no joint election, written statements or First-tier Tribunal determination, the fixed-value requirement is failed and the buyer is treated as having incurred nil qualifying expenditure on those fixtures.
- question: Were furnished holiday lets able to claim capital allowances?
  answer: They were, until the regime was abolished. The furnished holiday lettings regime was abolished from 1 April 2025 for corporation tax and 6 April 2025 for income tax by Finance Act 2025 Schedule 5. Before that, an FHL was a separate qualifying activity, so plant and machinery in the property qualified. From the commencement date, no new FHL plant qualifies and the property is treated as an ordinary residential property business with the section 35 dwelling-house bar applying.
- question: What happens to an FHL capital allowances pool after April 2025?
  answer: "The pool is grandfathered. Plant and machinery balances built up on a furnished holiday let before the abolition date transfer into the ordinary property business pool and continue to be written down at the normal rates, 14% on the main pool and 6% on the special-rate pool. What stops is new claims: no new FHL plant and machinery expenditure qualifies after commencement, because the property is now an ordinary residential let subject to section 35."
- question: How are mixed-use property costs apportioned?
  answer: Where a single asset serves both a commercial part and a residential dwelling, the cost is apportioned on a just and reasonable basis, usually by floor area or usage. CAA 2001 section 35 permits apportionment so that the commercial share can qualify while the dwelling share cannot. The dwelling portion instead falls under Replacement of Domestic Items Relief if it is a qualifying replacement. Communal common parts of the building can qualify even where the units themselves are dwellings.
- question: What is the difference between an SBA allowance statement and a section 198 election?
  answer: "They are different documents for different reliefs. A section 198 election fixes the value of plant and machinery fixtures between buyer and seller on a commercial purchase. A Structures and Buildings Allowance statement is the document required under CAA 2001 section 270IA for the separate 3% allowance on the building structure itself, and without it the SBA qualifying expenditure is treated as nil. They are not interchangeable: a buyer needs the section 198 election for fixtures and a separate allowance statement for SBA."
- question: Can I claim capital allowances on a property I already own?
  answer: You may be able to. If qualifying plant or machinery expenditure was incurred in earlier years and was never claimed, and was not already treated as a repair or revenue cost, a capital allowances review can identify it and bring it into a pool. The figures must be supported by the original cost. For a commercial property bought from a previous owner, the section 198 and pooling history governs what is still claimable, so the review needs the transaction documents, not just the build cost.
- question: Do the corporation tax rates change the value of a company's claim?
  answer: Yes, the value of an allowance depends on the rate it offsets. For 2026/27 the main rate of corporation tax is 25%, the small-profits rate is 19% for profits up to £50,000, and marginal relief tapers between £50,000 and £250,000. A capital allowance claimed by a company reduces taxable profits, so the cash benefit is the allowance multiplied by the rate that would otherwise apply, which is higher for companies in the main-rate band than for those on the small-profits rate.

<p>Capital allowances are easiest to understand in numbers. This page is the worked-example hub for the property capital allowances cluster: every section below is built around a concrete computation, so you can see how the relief actually lands on a tax bill rather than reading another definition. For the full decision framework (what qualifies, which pool an item falls into, and how the reliefs interact), see our <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/capital-allowances-property-investors-complete-pillar-2026-27-caa-2001-decision-framework">complete CAA 2001 pillar guide</a>. This page gives the numbers.</p>

<p>A capital allowance writes off the cost of qualifying plant and machinery against your taxable profits over time. It is different from a repair, which is a revenue deduction in the year you spend it. Capital allowances cover assets with a lasting function: heating and electrical systems, lifts, fitted equipment and the integral features of a commercial building. The figures here reflect the post-Finance-Act-2026 position, verified against legislation.gov.uk in May 2026.</p>

<p>Two points carry most of the misunderstanding. First, the main-pool writing-down allowance is now <strong>14%</strong>, not 18%: Finance Act 2026 section 28 substitutes 14% into CAA 2001 section 56, with effect for chargeable periods beginning on or after 1 April 2026 (corporation tax) and 6 April 2026 (income tax), and a period straddling that date uses a hybrid time-apportioned rate. Second, the <strong>£1 million AIA is permanent</strong>, fixed at £1,000,000 in CAA 2001 section 51A(5) from 1 April 2023. The special-rate pool stays at <strong>6%</strong>.</p>

<h2>Example 1: commercial plant and machinery, AIA plus the 14% tail</h2>
<p>An investor owns a small office building let to a local business. In the year she spends £50,000 on a new heating system, £15,000 on a powered ventilation system and £10,000 on general fitted plant. These are capital costs, not repairs, and the property is commercial, so the section 35 dwelling-house bar does not apply.</p>

<p>The heating and ventilation systems are integral features under CAA 2001 section 33A, so they are special-rate expenditure (£65,000). The general plant (£10,000) is main-rate. She uses the Annual Investment Allowance, which gives a 100% deduction up to £1,000,000 on most plant and machinery, including special-rate items. Total spend is £75,000, comfortably under the cap, so the whole £75,000 is written off in the year.</p>

<p>If her rental profit before the claim was £100,000, the £75,000 claim reduces it to £25,000. The cash value of the relief is the deduction multiplied by her marginal rate. As a higher-rate individual taxpayer that is £75,000 at 40%, a £30,000 reduction in tax for the year. The point to note is what happens <em>after</em> the AIA in a bigger spending year: anything that is not covered by AIA does not disappear, it rolls into the pools and is written down at 14% (main pool) or 6% (special rate) on a reducing-balance basis from then on. For the detail of which costs fall into which pool on a commercial building, see <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/capital-allowances-commercial-property-what-can-claim">what you can claim on commercial property</a> and the <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/integral-features-capital-allowances">integral features guide</a>.</p>

<aside>
<p>Most commercial-property capital allowances claims are won or lost on the apportionment between main-rate plant, special-rate integral features, and the non-qualifying building shell. Get the split right and the AIA covers the immediate spend while the pool tail is written down at the correct rate. If you have refurbished or bought a commercial building and are not sure the claim was mapped properly, a review before the accounts close is worth booking.</p>
</aside>

<h2>Example 2: when the AIA cap bites in a big refurbishment year</h2>
<p>Suppose a refurbishment year is larger: £1,300,000 of qualifying plant and machinery across a commercial portfolio held in one company. The Annual Investment Allowance is one £1,000,000 figure per business (or per related group), not per property, so it cannot be claimed afresh on each building.</p>

<p>The company claims the full £1,000,000 AIA in the year. That leaves £300,000 of qualifying spend above the cap. That excess is allocated to the pools: main-rate plant goes into the main pool and is written down at 14% reducing balance, and special-rate integral features go into the special-rate pool at 6%. Say £200,000 of the excess is main-rate and £100,000 is integral features. The first-year writing-down allowance on the excess is £200,000 at 14% (£28,000) plus £100,000 at 6% (£6,000), so £34,000 in year one, with the balances carrying forward and reducing each year after.</p>

<p>This is where allocation strategy matters: a company can often direct the AIA at the special-rate spend (which would otherwise only get 6%) and let the faster-writing-down main-rate plant fall into the pool, or claim full expensing on the new main-rate plant instead and reserve the AIA for the integral features. The mechanics of the £1 million cap, including the related-company association rules, are covered in the <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/aia-1m-cap-property-investors-allocation-strategy-and-association-rules-cta-2010">AIA cap and allocation guide</a>, and the AIA basics in <a href="/blog/section-24-and-tax-relief/what-is-aia-in-tax">what AIA means in tax</a>.</p>

<h2>Example 3: full expensing for a company, and the 40% FYA for everyone else</h2>
<p>There are two first-year allowances that sit alongside the AIA, and which one is available depends on who you are.</p>

<p>A company buying <strong>new and unused main-rate plant</strong> normally claims <strong>full expensing</strong>: a 100% first-year allowance under CAA 2001 section 45S, available only to companies within the charge to corporation tax, with no annual cap. Take a property SPV that spends £400,000 on new main-rate plant in a commercial fit-out. It can write off the full £400,000 in the year through full expensing, leaving the £1 million AIA free to cover the special-rate integral features (which full expensing does not reach at 100%, though a companion 50% first-year allowance applies to new special-rate plant). The full-expensing and 50% special-rate mechanics for SPVs are set out in the <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/full-expensing-50-percent-fya-commercial-property-spvs-mechanics-fa-2023">full expensing and 50% FYA guide</a>.</p>

<p>An <strong>individual landlord, a partnership or a sole trader cannot claim full expensing</strong>. For them, Finance Act 2026 section 29 introduced a new <strong>40% first-year allowance</strong> (inserting CAA 2001 section 45U) on new and unused main-rate plant, for expenditure incurred on or after 1 January 2026. It excludes cars, second-hand assets and special-rate expenditure. Take an unincorporated investor who owns a commercial unit and spends £100,000 on qualifying new main-rate plant in a year where the £1 million AIA is already committed elsewhere: the 40% FYA gives an immediate £40,000 deduction, and the remaining £60,000 enters the main pool and is written down at 14% from the following period. The 40% allowance is not legally restricted to unincorporated persons, but in practice a company would use full expensing at 100% instead, so the 40% route is the practical one for those who cannot or would not use full expensing, including leasing.</p>

<h2>Example 4: mixed-use property and the section 35 apportionment</h2>
<p>Mixed-use buildings are where the dwelling-house bar does its real work. Take a building where the ground floor is a retail unit and the first floor is a residential flat. The owner installs a new boiler serving both parts at a cost of £6,000.</p>

<p>CAA 2001 section 35 says expenditure is not qualifying expenditure if it is incurred on plant or machinery for use in a dwelling-house, but it permits a just and reasonable apportionment where an asset serves both. If the commercial unit occupies 60% of the floor area, the owner can treat £3,600 (60% of £6,000) as qualifying for capital allowances on the commercial side. The remaining £2,400 relates to the flat and is barred: it instead falls under Replacement of Domestic Items Relief (ITTOIA 2005 section 311A) if and when it is a qualifying like-for-like replacement.</p>

<p>The carve-outs matter here. Plant in the <strong>common parts</strong> of a block (a communal boiler, a lift, communal lighting) can qualify even though the individual units are dwellings, and integral features in genuine non-dwelling areas qualify. The interaction of section 35 with multi-tenant and HMO common parts is worked through in the <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/hmo-common-parts-capital-allowances-s35-claim-mechanics-multi-tenant-property">HMO common parts guide</a>, and the residential alternative is covered in the <a href="/blog/section-24-and-tax-relief/landlord-tax-deductions-uk-2026-complete-list">landlord deductions list</a>.</p>

<h2>Example 5: a former furnished holiday let after April 2025</h2>
<p>The furnished holiday lettings regime was abolished from 1 April 2025 for corporation tax and 6 April 2025 for income tax by Finance Act 2025 Schedule 5. Before abolition, an FHL was a separate qualifying activity, so plant and machinery in the cottage (kitchen, beds, white goods) qualified for allowances in a way that ordinary residential lets never could.</p>

<p>Take an owner who had built up a £13,000 plant and machinery pool on a Lake District holiday cottage before April 2025. After commencement, the property is treated as an ordinary residential property business and the section 35 dwelling-house bar applies in full. The pool is not lost: the grandfathered balance transfers into the ordinary property business pool and continues to be written down at the normal rates, the main pool at 14% and any integral features at 6%. What stops is new claims. No new FHL plant and machinery expenditure qualifies after commencement, so a replacement washing machine bought in 2026 is no longer a capital allowances item; it falls under Replacement of Domestic Items Relief instead. The full roll-forward mechanics are in the <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/fhl-capital-allowances-post-april-2025-grandfathered-claims-mechanics">grandfathered FHL claims guide</a>.</p>

<h2>Example 6: a section 198 fixtures claim on a commercial purchase</h2>
<p>Buying a commercial property is the example most landlords get wrong, because the fixtures already in the building are only claimable if two gates are passed.</p>

<p>An investor buys an office building for £900,000. A survey identifies £120,000 of plant and machinery fixtures (heating, lighting, ventilation, lifts) embedded in the price. To claim allowances on them, the buyer must satisfy:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>The pooling requirement.</strong> The past owner must have pooled the expenditure (claimed AIA, full expensing or writing-down allowances on it). If a previous owner in the chain never pooled the fixtures, they are permanently stranded and no section 198 election can rescue them.</li>
<li><strong>The fixed-value requirement.</strong> The value allocated to the fixtures must be fixed within two years, normally by a <strong>joint section 198 election</strong> signed by buyer and seller (CAA 2001 section 198), or failing agreement by First-tier Tribunal determination.</li>
</ul>

<p>Say the parties agree a section 198 election at £120,000. That becomes the buyer's qualifying expenditure: the main-rate fixtures can attract the £1 million AIA or be written down at 14%, and the integral features at 6%. Now the warning. CAA 2001 section 187A(3) treats the new expenditure as <strong>nil</strong> if the fixed-value requirement applies but is not satisfied, and CAA 2001 section 201 imposes a strict two-year deadline running from the date the buyer acquires the interest. If the election is agreed in principle but never executed within two years, the buyer is treated as having incurred nil qualifying expenditure on the fixtures, and the relief on £120,000 of value is lost for good. The buyer-side mechanics, including the negotiation between a high-value and a nominal election, are in the <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/commercial-property-fixtures-claim-s198-election-purchase-mechanics">section 198 fixtures claim guide</a>.</p>

<aside>
<p>The single most common deal-handling failure we see on commercial completions is a section 198 election that everyone intended to make and nobody executed before the two-year window closed. The obligation belongs in the heads of terms and the sale agreement, not in a post-completion intention. If you are buying a commercial property with fixtures, get the election quantum and the deadline pinned down before exchange. We work alongside the conveyancing solicitors to make sure the allowances are not left on the table.</p>
</aside>

<h2>Capital allowances and incorporation</h2>
<p>If you hold property in a limited company, the same pools and allowances apply, but the value of a claim is set by the corporation tax rate it offsets. For 2026/27 the main rate is 25% on profits over £250,000, the small-profits rate is 19% for profits up to £50,000, and marginal relief tapers between £50,000 and £250,000. A £100,000 capital allowance therefore saves a main-rate company £25,000 and a small-profits company £19,000.</p>

<p>Incorporation also unlocks full expensing, which individuals cannot use, so a property business planning a heavy commercial fit-out sometimes finds the relief profile is part of the incorporation case. Full expensing is companies-only, and it does not reach special-rate integral features at 100% (a 50% first-year allowance applies to those instead). The wider incorporation picture is covered in our <a href="/incorporation">incorporation guide</a>; the capital allowances position is one lever among several, not a reason in itself.</p>

<h2>What does not qualify for capital allowances</h2>
<p>The boundaries are as important as the claims:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>The building structure and shell</strong> (walls, floors, ceilings, doors, windows) is not plant and machinery. It may instead attract the separate 3% Structures and Buildings Allowance under CAA 2001 section 270AA if construction began on or after 29 October 2018 and an allowance statement is held. See the <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/structures-and-buildings-allowance-sba-3-percent-claim-mechanics-fa-2018-onward">SBA mechanics guide</a> for the boundary.</li>
<li><strong>Repairs and maintenance</strong> are revenue deductions, not capital allowances.</li>
<li><strong>Plant and machinery in a dwelling-house</strong> (a let flat or house) is barred by section 35; use Replacement of Domestic Items Relief instead.</li>
<li><strong>Cars</strong> are excluded from both the AIA and the new 40% FYA. Only new, unused zero-emission cars (0 g/km) get a 100% first-year allowance under CAA 2001 section 45D; other cars go into the main pool (≤50 g/km) or special-rate pool (over 50 g/km).</li>
<li><strong>Land</strong> and the cost of acquiring it.</li>
</ul>

<h2>How to claim, and reviewing a property you already own</h2>
<p>Capital allowances are claimed through your self-assessment or corporation tax return, in the capital allowances computation. Where the AIA, full expensing or the 40% FYA applies, you take the deduction in the period the expenditure is incurred; the balance of any pooled expenditure is written down each year at 14% or 6%.</p>

<p>For a property you already own, a capital allowances review can identify qualifying plant and machinery from earlier years that was never pooled, provided it was not already treated as a repair or revenue cost. On a commercial property bought from a previous owner, the review turns on the section 198 and pooling history, so the transaction documents matter as much as the build cost. When fixtures are sold with a building, the seller-side disposal values and any balancing charge are governed by the pool mechanics; the disposal and balancing-charge detail is in the <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/balancing-allowance-balancing-charge-on-disposal-property-capital-allowances-mechanics">balancing allowance guide</a>. For a relief-level overview of the landlord position, see <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/capital-allowances-on-property">landlord capital allowances and tax relief</a> and <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/capital-allowances-on-property">capital allowances on property</a>.</p>

<h2>Common mistakes landlords make</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Claiming AIA on a single-let dwelling.</strong> Section 35 bars plant and machinery allowances on boilers, kitchens and bathrooms inside a let house or flat. The residential route is Replacement of Domestic Items Relief.</li>
<li><strong>Quoting the writing-down allowance at 18%.</strong> The main-pool rate is 14% from April 2026; only legacy periods used 18%.</li>
<li><strong>Treating the AIA as £1 million per property.</strong> It is one £1 million figure per business or related group.</li>
<li><strong>Assuming a company can rely on the 40% FYA.</strong> A company normally uses full expensing at 100% instead; the 40% FYA is the practical route where full expensing is unavailable.</li>
<li><strong>Missing the section 198 two-year deadline.</strong> An unexecuted election loses the fixtures allowances permanently under section 187A(3).</li>
<li><strong>Confusing an SBA allowance statement with a section 198 election.</strong> They are different documents for different reliefs and you may need both on a commercial purchase.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Frequently Asked Questions</h2>
<p>Below are answers to common questions landlords and property investors ask about capital allowances, with the worked numbers above as the reference.</p>
