<p>If you let property in the UK, the heart of your tax return is the <strong>SA105 UK property pages</strong>. For the 2025/26 tax year these are the supplementary pages where you declare rental income, claim allowable expenses, apply the Section 24 finance-cost rules, and arrive at the property profit that feeds your overall Self Assessment. This guide covers which Self Assessment forms you need, the key deadlines and penalties, allowable expenses and a full Section 24 calculation, Capital Gains Tax on a sale, and how Making Tax Digital changes the routine from April 2026; for the box-by-box detail of every SA105 entry, see our dedicated SA105 guide linked below.</p>
<p>Two things have moved since older guides were written, and both matter for 2025/26. The furnished holiday lettings regime has been abolished, so there is no separate holiday-let tax treatment any more. And Making Tax Digital for Income Tax is now live for the largest landlords, with the first £50,000 threshold biting from 6 April 2026. We flag both in context below.</p>

<h2>Which forms a landlord needs for 2025/26</h2>
<p>Self Assessment for landlords is built from the main return plus the property supplement.</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>SA100</strong> is the main Self Assessment return that everyone completes.</li>
<li><strong>SA105</strong> is the UK property supplement. You complete one set of SA105 pages covering all your UK rental income. You do not file a separate SA105 for each property; you aggregate the figures.</li>
<li><strong>SA106</strong> is the foreign pages, used for overseas rental property and other foreign income.</li>
<li><strong>SA108</strong> is the Capital Gains Tax summary, used in the year you sell a property at a gain (separate from the in-year 60-day reporting described later).</li>
<li><strong>SA109</strong> is the residence and remittance pages, relevant to non-UK residents and to anyone arriving in or leaving the UK part-way through the year.</li>
</ul>
<p>HMRC publishes <strong>SA105 Notes</strong> for each tax year that explain every box. Box numbers occasionally shift between years, so when you are reading any guide, including this one, cross-check the box references against the SA105 Notes for the exact year you are filing. The walkthrough below uses the 2025/26 layout.</p>

<h2>Key deadlines and penalties</h2>
<p>Missing a filing date triggers automatic penalties, so the timeline is worth pinning down early. For a fuller treatment see our guide to the <a href="/blog/making-tax-digital-mtd/landlord-tax-return-deadline-2026">landlord tax return deadline for 2026</a>.</p>
<h3>2025/26 Self Assessment dates</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>31 October 2026:</strong> deadline if you file a paper return by post.</li>
<li><strong>31 January 2027:</strong> deadline to file online, and to pay any tax owed for 2025/26.</li>
<li><strong>31 January 2027:</strong> first payment on account for 2026/27, if you are within the payments-on-account regime.</li>
<li><strong>31 July 2027:</strong> second payment on account for 2026/27, if applicable.</li>
</ul>
<h3>Late filing penalties</h3>
<ul>
<li>£100 fixed penalty the day after the deadline, even if no tax is due.</li>
<li>Daily penalties of £10 a day (up to £900) once the return is three months late.</li>
<li>Further tax-geared penalties at six and twelve months.</li>
<li>Separate penalties and interest apply to tax paid late.</li>
</ul>
<p>If you are newly letting and have not filed before, you must register for Self Assessment by 5 October following the end of the tax year in which the rental income arose.</p>

<h2>The SA105 in brief (full box-by-box in our dedicated guide)</h2>
<p>The SA105 UK property pages are where your rental figures land: rent received in box 20, the £1,000 property allowance in box 20.1 (claimed instead of actual expenses, never alongside them), any tax already withheld in box 22, your expense categories across boxes 24 to 29, and residential finance costs in box 44. Box 44 is the one to understand: that figure is <em>not</em> deducted from profit, it drives your basic-rate Section 24 tax reducer instead. Your adjusted profit is income less the box 24 to 29 expenses, with finance costs handled separately through the reducer, and brought-forward property losses set against the result.</p>
<p>For a full walkthrough of every box, the exact entries and the common filing mistakes, see our dedicated reference: <a href="/blog/landlord-tax-essentials/sa105-property-income-form-2026-complete-guide">SA105 property income form 2026: every box explained</a>.</p>

<h2>What counts as rental income</h2>
<p>Report all income from the property business for the year, not just the headline rent:</p>
<ul>
<li>Rent received from tenants.</li>
<li>Deposits you keep to cover damage, cleaning or unpaid rent.</li>
<li>Insurance payouts that compensate for lost rent.</li>
<li>Charges to tenants for services such as cleaning, gardening or utilities you arrange.</li>
<li>Grants and incentive payments connected with the letting, including some reverse premiums.</li>
</ul>
<p>Most unincorporated landlords use the <strong>cash basis</strong>, which is the default: you report rent when it is received and expenses when they are paid. You must use the accruals (GAAP) basis instead if your cash receipts for the year exceed £150,000, or you can elect into accruals voluntarily (ITTOIA 2005 section 271A). Under the cash basis, December 2025 rent that a tenant actually pays in January 2026 falls into the 2025/26 tax year, because that is when you received it.</p>

<h2>Allowable expenses and deductions</h2>
<p>You can deduct costs incurred wholly and exclusively for the rental business. Getting this right is the difference between a fair tax bill and an inflated one, and the most common HMRC enquiries into landlords start with expense errors.</p>
<h3>Fully deductible expenses</h3>
<ul>
<li>Letting agent and property management fees.</li>
<li>Buildings and contents insurance.</li>
<li>Ground rent, service charges and rents you pay out.</li>
<li>Repairs and maintenance (see the repairs-versus-improvements distinction below).</li>
<li>Accountancy, legal and other professional fees relating to the letting.</li>
<li>Advertising for tenants and tenant referencing or credit checks.</li>
<li>Utilities and council tax you pay (for example during void periods or where bills are not recharged).</li>
<li>The running cost of visits to the property for management purposes.</li>
</ul>
<h3>The £1,000 property allowance</h3>
<p>The property allowance (ITTOIA 2005 Part 6A) gives a £1,000 deduction in place of actual expenses. If your gross rents are £1,000 or less, the allowance usually means you have nothing to report. If your rents are higher, you choose each year between deducting the £1,000 allowance or your actual expenses, whichever leaves the lower taxable profit. You cannot use both against the same income, and the allowance is per person, not per property.</p>
<h3>Repairs versus improvements</h3>
<p>A <strong>repair</strong> restores the property to its previous condition and is deductible: replacing a failed boiler with a similar model, repainting between tenancies, or fixing guttering. An <strong>improvement</strong> goes beyond the original and is capital expenditure, not deductible against rental profit: an extension, or fitting a substantially superior kitchen where there was a basic one. A like-for-like replacement is still a repair even if the modern equivalent is a modest upgrade on the old item. Capital improvement spending is usually added to the property's base cost and reduces the Capital Gains Tax bill when you sell.</p>
<h3>The Section 24 finance-cost restriction</h3>
<p>Since 6 April 2020 individual residential landlords cannot deduct mortgage interest and other finance costs from rental profit. Instead, the finance costs go in box 44 and HMRC gives a <strong>basic-rate tax reducer</strong> against your overall income tax. The reducer is 20% of the <em>lowest</em> of three figures: the finance costs for the year, the residential property profit, and your adjusted total income above the personal allowance (ITTOIA 2005 sections 272A and 274A). Any finance cost not relieved because of the cap carries forward indefinitely. This is the rule that pushes leveraged higher-rate landlords into a higher effective tax position, and it is set out in detail in our <a href="/blog/section-24-and-tax-relief/section-24-mortgage-interest-restriction-uk-landlords">Section 24 mortgage interest restriction guide</a> and the practical mechanics in <a href="/blog/section-24-and-tax-relief/claim-mortgage-interest-rental-property-uk-section-24">how to claim mortgage interest relief under Section 24</a>.</p>
<p><strong>Looking ahead:</strong> the reducer is not frozen at 20%. From 2027/28, Finance Act 2026 (which received Royal Assent on 18 March 2026) gives the reducer at the new 22% property basic rate for England, Wales and Northern Ireland (Scotland sets its own), alongside separate property income rates of 22%, 42% and 47%. A basic-rate landlord sees no new wedge from this change because the reducer tracks the 22% rate; a higher-rate landlord's relief rises from 20% to 22% but still sits well below their 42% marginal rate.</p>

<h2>Section 24 worked example</h2>
<p>Numbers make Section 24 concrete. Take Priya, a higher-rate-taxpayer landlord with a single buy-to-let. Her 2025/26 figures are:</p>
<ul>
<li>Rental income: £18,000</li>
<li>Allowable expenses (agent fees, insurance, repairs): £3,000</li>
<li>Mortgage interest: £9,000</li>
</ul>
<p>Under the current rules the calculation runs like this:</p>
<table>
<thead>
<tr><th>Step</th><th>Amount</th></tr>
</thead>
<tbody>
<tr><td>Rental income</td><td>£18,000</td></tr>
<tr><td>Less allowable expenses (boxes 24 to 29)</td><td>(£3,000)</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>Taxable rental profit</strong> (mortgage interest not deducted)</td><td><strong>£15,000</strong></td></tr>
<tr><td>Income tax on £15,000 at her 40% marginal rate</td><td>£6,000</td></tr>
<tr><td>Less Section 24 reducer: 20% of £9,000 interest (box 44)</td><td>(£1,800)</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>Net income tax on the rental profit</strong></td><td><strong>£4,200</strong></td></tr>
</tbody>
</table>
<p>Before Section 24, Priya would have deducted the full £9,000 interest, leaving a £6,000 profit taxed at 40%, so £2,400. Under the restriction she pays £4,200, which is £1,800 more on the same economic position. The reducer is capped at 20% of the lowest of her interest (£9,000), her property profit (£15,000), and her income above the personal allowance, so in this case the full £1,800 is given. The wider the gap between her interest bill and her profit, the more the cap can bite, which is why heavily mortgaged higher-rate landlords feel Section 24 most. Several respond by reviewing whether a <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/buy-to-let-limited-company-complete-guide-uk">limited company structure</a>, where interest remains fully deductible, suits their portfolio.</p>

<h2>Capital allowances: narrower than landlords expect</h2>
<p>Capital allowances on plant and machinery are <strong>not</strong> available for items inside a residential dwelling let on an ordinary tenancy. You cannot claim the Annual Investment Allowance on a new cooker, carpets or furniture in a standard buy-to-let. Instead, the cost of replacing furnishings, appliances and similar movable items in a residential let is dealt with through <strong>replacement of domestic items relief</strong>, which gives a deduction for the cost of replacing (not initially providing) such items, restricted to a like-for-like standard.</p>
<p>Capital allowances do still apply in specific situations:</p>
<ul>
<li>Plant and machinery in the <strong>communal areas</strong> of a residential block (for example a lift or shared boiler) can qualify.</li>
<li><strong>Commercial</strong> lettings can claim capital allowances on qualifying fixtures and plant in the normal way.</li>
<li>Capital allowances <strong>pooled before 6 April 2025</strong> on a former furnished holiday let continue to receive writing-down allowances within the ongoing property business, even though the FHL regime has gone.</li>
</ul>

<h2>The end of the furnished holiday lettings regime</h2>
<p>The furnished holiday lettings (FHL) regime was <strong>abolished from 6 April 2025</strong>. For 2025/26 there is no separate holiday-let category on the return: a former holiday let is reported on the SA105 as an ordinary residential let. In practice that means the Section 24 finance-cost restriction now applies to it, the old FHL capital allowances and Business Asset Disposal Relief routes are no longer available, and the profits no longer count as relevant earnings for pension contributions. Two transitional points survive: capital allowances already pooled before abolition keep their writing-down allowances, and unused FHL losses carry forward against the continuing property business. If you previously filed holiday-let pages, do not carry that treatment into your 2025/26 return.</p>

<h2>Non-resident landlords and the SA109</h2>
<p>If you live abroad but let UK property, you still pay UK tax on that rental income and report it on the SA105. The <strong>SA109</strong> residence pages confirm your residence status, and the <strong>Non-Resident Landlord (NRL) scheme</strong> may require your letting agent or tenant to deduct basic-rate tax from the rent before paying you, unless HMRC has approved you to receive rents gross (using forms NRL1, NRL2 or NRL3). Any tax withheld is reported in box 22 of the SA105 and credited against your liability. Making Tax Digital still applies to non-resident landlords where the qualifying income threshold is met, so the NRL scheme and MTD operate side by side rather than as alternatives.</p>

<h2>Capital Gains Tax when you sell</h2>
<p>The annual return covers rental income, but a sale brings Capital Gains Tax into play. For 2025/26 a gain on a residential investment property is taxed at <strong>18%</strong> within your remaining basic-rate band and <strong>24%</strong> above it (TCGA 1992 section 1H), after the <strong>£3,000</strong> annual exempt amount. Where Capital Gains Tax is due, UK residents must file a <strong>CGT-on-UK-property return and pay the estimated tax within 60 days of completion</strong>, which is a separate filing from the annual Self Assessment. The gain is then also reported on the SA108 pages of that year's return, with the 60-day payment credited. Non-UK residents must file the 60-day return for every UK property disposal, whether or not tax is due. The full picture, including the current rates and reliefs, is in our <a href="/blog/capital-gains-tax/cgt-rates-property-2026-27-current-rates-explained">CGT rates on property guide</a> and the <a href="/blog/capital-gains-tax/capital-gains-tax-property-complete-guide-uk">complete Capital Gains Tax guide for landlords</a>.</p>

<h2>Record-keeping for the return and for MTD</h2>
<p>Good records make the SA105 straightforward and protect you if HMRC opens an enquiry. Keep:</p>
<ul>
<li>Rent statements and bank records showing income received.</li>
<li>Receipts and invoices for every expense you claim.</li>
<li>Annual mortgage interest certificates, which feed box 44.</li>
<li>Letting agent statements showing gross rent, commission and deductions.</li>
<li>Purchase, legal and improvement paperwork, which you will need to calculate Capital Gains Tax years later when you sell.</li>
</ul>
<p>A practical retention rule for landlords is to keep records for at least five years after the 31 January filing deadline. Under Making Tax Digital those records must be kept <strong>digitally</strong>, with bank-feed data, app-captured receipt images or accounting-software entries all acceptable; loose paper receipts must be digitised. Building that habit now makes the move to quarterly reporting far less disruptive.</p>

<h2>Getting ready for Making Tax Digital</h2>
<p>Making Tax Digital for Income Tax (MTD for ITSA) replaces the single annual return with digital records, quarterly updates and a year-end final declaration. The mandate is phased by <strong>qualifying income</strong>, which is gross rents plus gross self-employment turnover, measured before expenses:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>From 6 April 2026:</strong> mandatory where qualifying income is above <strong>£50,000</strong>.</li>
<li><strong>From 6 April 2027:</strong> threshold drops to <strong>£30,000</strong>.</li>
<li><strong>From 6 April 2028:</strong> threshold drops to <strong>£20,000</strong>.</li>
</ul>
<p>Because the test is on <em>gross</em> income, a landlord with £55,000 of rent but only £10,000 of profit is in scope at the April 2026 mandate. Limited companies are outside MTD for ITSA entirely, and general partnerships are deferred to a date still to be confirmed. Joint owners test the threshold against their own share of the gross rents, not the property's total, so the way income is split (including any Form 17 election) affects who is mandated and when. Our <a href="/blog/making-tax-digital-mtd/mtd-itsa-qualifying-income-test-gross-vs-net">MTD qualifying income gross-versus-net guide</a> works through the threshold test in detail, and the <a href="/blog/making-tax-digital-mtd/making-tax-digital-landlords-april-2026-deadline">April 2026 MTD deadline guide</a> covers what you need in place.</p>
<p>In-scope landlords file <strong>quarterly updates</strong> using HMRC-recognised software (or a spreadsheet linked to bridging software), followed by a final declaration by 31 January after the tax year. The standard quarters run to 5 July, 5 October, 5 January and 5 April, with updates due by the 7th of the following month, although calendar-quarter end dates can be elected. Late submissions move to a points-based regime, with a £200 penalty once you reach the four-point threshold, and MTD late-payment interest is charged on an accelerated schedule from 6 April 2026.</p>

<h2>Common mistakes that trigger enquiries</h2>
<ul>
<li>Treating a capital improvement as a repair (or vice versa).</li>
<li>Still deducting mortgage interest from profit instead of routing it through the box 44 Section 24 reducer.</li>
<li>Reporting rent net of agent fees as gross income, which understates both income and expenses and can make a landlord wrongly conclude they are below the MTD threshold.</li>
<li>Carrying forward old furnished holiday let treatment into a 2025/26 return.</li>
<li>Mixing personal and rental costs, or claiming the full cost of an asset used partly privately.</li>
<li>Forgetting to report retained deposits or insurance recoveries.</li>
<li>Missing the separate 60-day CGT return on a sale.</li>
</ul>
