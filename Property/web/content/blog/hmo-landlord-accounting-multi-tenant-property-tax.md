<p>Houses in multiple occupation pose a different problem from single-let property. The tax rules are not exotic, but the <strong>bookkeeping</strong> is: one building, several rooms, several tenancies, shared running costs and a tax figure that depends on what you actually received room by room. This guide is the operational side of HMO accounting, how to set up and run the books for a multi-tenant property and meet Making Tax Digital for Income Tax, rather than a list of what is and is not deductible.</p>

<p>For the deductions list itself, what counts as an allowable expense and how Section 24 restricts finance costs, read our companion <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/hmo-tax-guide-rental-income-deductions-multi-tenant">HMO tax guide to rental income and deductions</a>. Here the focus is the system: a chart of accounts, room-level income recording, a documented allocation method, void and arrears tracking, and the records pack that keeps you ready if HMRC ever asks.</p>

<h2>Setting up a chart of accounts for an HMO</h2>

<p>Good HMO bookkeeping starts with structure. Before you record a single transaction, decide how the accounts are organised so that every figure has an obvious home. A workable HMO chart of accounts separates four things:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Income, by room or tenancy.</strong> An income line per let unit (Room 1, Room 2, and so on, or per tenancy where a couple shares a room) so you can see what each unit produced and where the voids fell.</li>
<li><strong>Room-specific costs.</strong> Repairs, redecoration and furniture that belong to one room and need no allocation.</li>
<li><strong>Shared costs.</strong> Utilities, communal cleaning, garden, kitchen and bathroom maintenance, insurance and council tax, costs that serve the whole house and have to be allocated.</li>
<li><strong>Finance costs, kept on their own.</strong> Mortgage and loan interest in a dedicated account, because finance costs are not deducted in the normal way under Section 24 and have to be reported separately for the basic-rate tax reducer.</li>
</ul>

<p>Keeping finance costs ring-fenced from day one saves real work later, both for the Section 24 calculation and for the MTD category split. It is also worth marking each cost as capital or revenue at entry: revenue costs (ordinary repairs, running expenses) reduce this year's profit, while capital costs (improvements, the original purchase, structural additions) are held for capital gains tax on a future disposal. Sorting that at the point of entry is far easier than untangling it at year end. Expense deductibility for a property business runs on the basis in <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2005/5/section/272" rel="nofollow">section 272 of ITTOIA 2005</a>, and furniture and appliance renewals are claimed under replacement of domestic items relief in <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2005/5/section/311A" rel="nofollow">section 311A</a> (the old 10% wear-and-tear allowance was abolished and no longer applies).</p>

<h2>Recording HMO rental income room by room</h2>

<p>Property income is taxed on what you actually receive in the tax year, not what you invoice. For an HMO that means your books have to capture, for each room: the rate, the occupied dates, the rent received, any arrears, and the deposit (which is not income while it is held in a protection scheme). Aggregate those room lines to one property total at the end, but keep the detail underneath, because it is the detail that explains voids, arrears and per-room profitability and that supports your figures in an enquiry.</p>

<p>If a letting agent manages the property, report the <strong>gross</strong> rent the agent collected, then the agent's commission and any management charge as separate expenses. Do not record only the net amount the agent paid into your account: as the landlord you are the filer, and netting off the agent's fee understates both your income and your costs and breaks the category split MTD expects.</p>

<h2>A documented expense-allocation methodology</h2>

<p>This is the load-bearing part of HMO bookkeeping. Room-specific costs are easy, they go straight to the room. The judgement is in the <strong>shared</strong> costs: utilities, communal cleaning, the garden, kitchen and bathroom maintenance, insurance. HMRC does not prescribe how you split them. It expects a method that is fair and reasonable, applied consistently, and documented. The three common bases are:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Equal split per room.</strong> Simple and defensible where rooms are broadly similar. Five rooms means each bears one fifth.</li>
<li><strong>Floor-area weighting.</strong> Each room (and a share of communal space) bears a percentage based on square metres. Fairer where rooms differ a lot in size.</li>
<li><strong>Rental-value weighting.</strong> Each room bears a share proportional to its rent, which tracks the economic benefit each room derives.</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Worked example 2, the same shared cost under each basis.</strong> Take a shared annual cost of 1,000 pounds (say communal electricity) across the same five rooms, where Room 3 is the largest and highest-rent and Room 5 is the smallest:</p>

<p>The total is the same under every basis (it has to be, you are dividing one cost), so the choice does not change your overall deduction. What it changes is per-room profitability and, more importantly, how defensible your books look. Pick the basis that genuinely fits the property, write down why, and apply it the same way every year. Switching basis annually to flatter a particular room is precisely the pattern an enquiry looks for. If a layout change later makes your original basis unreasonable, you can change it, just record the reason and the date.</p>

<h2>Tracking void periods, arrears and the council-tax cost line</h2>

<p>Voids and arrears are where HMO books most often go wrong. Track a void as a gap in that room's occupancy log, not as a negative income entry. Track arrears as a memo, rent due but not yet paid, so you can chase it and reconcile it when it arrives, but do <strong>not</strong> include unpaid rent in your taxable income, because property income is taxed on the cash received in the year.</p>

<p>Council tax is the cost line that catches many HMO landlords out, and it is now firmly a landlord cost. Since <strong>1 December 2023</strong> in England, an HMO is treated as a <strong>single dwelling</strong> for council tax: article 3C of the Council Tax (Chargeable Dwellings) Order 1992 (inserted by SI 2023/1175) means one band and one bill for the whole property, and that bill falls on the <strong>owner</strong> under Class C of the <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/1992/551" rel="nofollow">Council Tax (Liability for Owners) Regulations 1992</a> (SI 1992/551). Because the landlord pays it, council tax, including the cost of a void room, is a deductible running expense of the letting business. This is settled current law from that date, not a proposal. For the full statutory mechanic and the review route for older per-room bandings, see our dedicated page on the <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/government-to-end-council-tax-on-hmo-rooms">end of per-room council tax on HMO rooms</a>.</p>

<h2>Capital versus revenue, and the communal-parts point</h2>

<p>Your books should split capital from revenue as costs go in. Revenue items reduce this year's rental profit. Capital items (the purchase, improvements, structural work) sit on the property's capital gains base cost for a future disposal. One narrow capital-allowances point is worth flagging without re-deriving it here: plant and machinery allowances are generally barred for plant inside a dwelling-house under <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2001/2/section/35" rel="nofollow">section 35 of the Capital Allowances Act 2001</a>, with a narrow exception for plant in the communal common parts of a multi-let property (shared hallways, landings and the like). For how that claim actually works on an HMO, see our page on the <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/hmo-common-parts-capital-allowances-s35-claim-mechanics-multi-tenant-property">section 35 communal-parts capital-allowances claim</a>.</p>

<h2>MTD for Income Tax: what multi-tenant record-keeping actually requires</h2>

<p>Making Tax Digital for Income Tax is now live and phasing in by income. The mandation timeline is gross qualifying income over <strong>50,000 pounds from 6 April 2026</strong>, over <strong>30,000 pounds from 6 April 2027</strong>, and over <strong>20,000 pounds from 6 April 2028</strong>. The obligation to keep digital records and send quarterly updates sits in the <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2026/336" rel="nofollow">Income Tax (Digital Obligations) Regulations 2026</a> (SI 2026/336), which replaced the earlier 2021 instrument from 1 April 2026.</p>

<p>The qualifying-income test is on <strong>gross</strong> income, total property (and any sole-trade) turnover before expenses, not profit. This is the trap for HMO landlords, who run high gross rents against high running costs.</p>

<p><strong>Worked example 3, a low-profit HMO that is still in scope.</strong> A landlord with one HMO producing roughly 52,000 pounds of gross rent a year and, after heavy running costs and finance, only about 12,000 pounds of net profit, is tested on the <strong>52,000</strong>. They are mandated into MTD for Income Tax from 6 April 2026 despite a modest profit. The mechanic is set out in our guide to the <a href="/blog/making-tax-digital-mtd/mtd-itsa-qualifying-income-test-gross-vs-net">gross-versus-net qualifying income test</a>.</p>

<p>Practically, MTD for a multi-tenant property requires:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Digital records of every transaction</strong> (date, amount, category) kept in functional compatible software, with room-level income captured underneath the property total.</li>
<li><strong>Category discipline.</strong> Map everything to the standard property pages categories: gross rental income, agent fees, repairs, insurance, council tax, finance costs, other. A spreadsheet works if its columns mirror these categories.</li>
<li><strong>A digital link</strong> all the way to the quarterly update: data moving between programs electronically (cell references, formulas, CSV import, API), with no copy-paste or manual re-keying of a subtotal anywhere in the chain.</li>
<li><strong>Quarterly updates</strong> of cumulative income and expenses to HMRC, then a year-end finalisation, in place of the single annual return.</li>
</ul>

<p>Limited companies are outside MTD for Income Tax (they report through the corporation tax route instead), which is one reason some HMO landlords look at incorporation. If you are weighing that up, our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/buy-to-let-limited-company-complete-guide-uk">buy-to-let limited company guide</a> covers the trade-offs.</p>

<h2>Choosing software for HMO bookkeeping</h2>

<p>There is no single right product, and HMRC does not endorse one. For multi-tenant property, judge software on the features that actually matter:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Room or property tagging</strong>, so income and costs attach to the correct unit.</li>
<li><strong>Category mapping</strong> to the standard property pages categories, so the quarterly figures fall out automatically.</li>
<li><strong>A digital-link export</strong> (no manual re-keying) into the submission step.</li>
<li><strong>MTD-compatible submission</strong>, ideally a direct API filing or clean bridging.</li>
</ul>

<p>Check what is currently listed on the <a href="https://www.gov.uk/guidance/find-software-thats-compatible-with-making-tax-digital-for-income-tax" rel="nofollow">HMRC compatible-software finder</a> rather than relying on a vendor's marketing. A spreadsheet plus bridging software is acceptable provided it is properly categorised and the link is digital end to end; our page on <a href="/blog/making-tax-digital-mtd/mtd-itsa-spreadsheets-with-bridging-software-allowed-mechanics">spreadsheets with bridging software</a> sets out the mechanics. <h2>The audit-ready records pack</h2>

<p>The point of running clean HMO books is that an enquiry becomes a quick exercise in producing documents, not a scramble to reconstruct them. A complete HMO records pack holds:</p>

<ul>
<li>Per-room tenancy agreements with start and end dates.</li>
<li>The occupancy and arrears log (the source of your received-rent figure).</li>
<li>Your written allocation method, with floor plans or area measurements if you allocate by space.</li>
<li>Receipts and invoices mapped to a specific room or to shared areas.</li>
<li>Deposit-protection records for each tenancy.</li>
<li>Licensing documentation for the property.</li>
<li>Finance-cost statements (kept separate for Section 24).</li>
</ul>

<p>Held digitally and in order, this pack is what makes you enquiry-resilient, and most of it is the same data MTD already requires you to keep.</p>

<h2>Section 24 in the HMO books</h2>

<p>Section 24 restricts relief on finance costs to a basic-rate (20%) tax reducer rather than a deduction from profit, which is why your chart of accounts keeps mortgage interest in its own line. HMOs tend to run higher yields and higher gearing, so the restriction can bite harder relative to net profit. We do not re-derive the mechanics here; for the calculation see <a href="/blog/section-24-and-tax-relief/claim-mortgage-interest-rental-property-uk-section-24">claiming mortgage interest under Section 24</a>, and for the deductions context the <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/hmo-tax-guide-rental-income-deductions-multi-tenant">HMO deductions guide</a>. The bookkeeping job is simply to isolate the finance costs cleanly so the reducer is straightforward to compute.</p>
