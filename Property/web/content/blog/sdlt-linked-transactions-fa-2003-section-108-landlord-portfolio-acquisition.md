  - question: "What is the FA 2003 s.108 linked-transactions definition?"
    answer: "Section 108(1) Finance Act 2003 defines linked transactions as transactions that 'form part of a single scheme, arrangement or series of transactions between the same vendor and purchaser or persons connected with either of them'. Connected persons are defined by reference to CTA 2010 s.1122. The test has two parts that must both be met: (a) same vendor and purchaser or connected persons on both sides; (b) the transactions form part of a single scheme, arrangement, or series. Where both parts are satisfied, the transactions are linked and their consideration is aggregated for SDLT rate purposes under s.108(2). The definition is purposive rather than formalistic: HMRC looks at the substance of the arrangement, not at the formal separation of contracts. Two contracts signed on the same day to the same buyer typically link; two contracts within a six-month window between connected parties link with high probability; contracts more than twelve months apart, with separate negotiations and unrelated economic purpose, typically do not link."
  - question: "Why is the citation s.108 and not Schedule 4 paragraph 5?"
    answer: "Because Schedule 4 paragraph 5 covers a completely different topic: exchanges. Sch 4 para 5 deals with the case where consideration for a land transaction is partly in another chargeable interest (a property-for-property exchange); it provides the rule for valuing the non-cash element of the consideration. It is not the linked-transactions definition. The correct citation is FA 2003 s.108 (with s.108(1) the definition, s.108(2) the aggregation mechanism, s.108(3) the joint-purchaser interaction, and s.108(1A) the Scotland/Wales exclusion). Practitioner content frequently mis-cites Sch 4 para 5 for the linked-transactions definition; the misconception is corrected verbatim in our house position lock at §1.F. Sessions writing on SDLT linked transactions must use the s.108 citation."
  - question: "How does the aggregation work in arithmetic terms?"
    answer: "The consideration across all linked transactions is added together to determine the SDLT rate. Worked: Patel-Holdings Ltd acquires six BTL properties from a single vendor for £400,000 each (total £2.4m). If the transactions are linked, the SDLT computation aggregates £2.4m and the residential SDLT rates plus the 5% additional-dwellings surcharge bite on £2.4m as a single transaction. Rates for residential post-31-October-2024 with 5% surcharge: £0 to £125,000 at 5%, £125,000 to £250,000 at 7%, £250,000 to £925,000 at 10%, £925,000 to £1.5m at 15%, above £1.5m at 17%. SDLT on £2.4m: £6,250 + £8,750 + £67,500 + £86,250 + £153,000 = roughly £321,750. If the transactions are NOT linked and each £400,000 transaction stands alone, the rates table applies fresh on each transaction (with the 5% surcharge applying to each) and the total SDLT is materially lower, in the region of £114,000 to £125,000 across the six transactions. The linkage difference here is approximately £200,000 of SDLT."
  - question: "Are six dwellings sold together always linked transactions?"
    answer: "Not automatically; the s.108 test still applies. Six separate dwellings sold by the same vendor to the same purchaser on the same day are typically linked because they sit within a single arrangement. Six dwellings sold by the same vendor to the same purchaser across two years with genuinely separate negotiations may not be linked. The question is whether the transactions form part of a single scheme, arrangement, or series. HMRC's enquiry pattern looks at contract dates, payment timing, marketing of the properties (was the portfolio offered as a bundle?), the buyer's awareness of the multi-property opportunity at the first purchase, and any communications between the parties indicating a multi-property arrangement. A buyer who acquires one BTL in March then learns of and acquires further BTLs from the same vendor in October has a strong argument for separation; a buyer who acquires a bundle of six BTLs from one vendor under a single negotiated portfolio sale has weak arguments for separation."
  - question: "What is the s.116(7) six-dwellings rule and how does it interact with linked transactions?"
    answer: "FA 2003 s.116(7) provides an automatic single-transaction option where six or more dwellings are the subject of a single transaction OR linked transactions. The buyer can elect to treat the dwellings as non-residential property for SDLT, attracting the non-residential rates (0% to £150,000, 2% to £250,000, 5% above £250,000) instead of residential rates plus the 5% additional-dwellings surcharge. The election is often materially cheaper on portfolios of six or more dwellings because the non-residential rate cap at 5% beats the residential 17% top band plus the 5% surcharge. For the Patel-Holdings £2.4m example: non-residential SDLT on £2.4m is £3,000 + £2,000 + £107,500 = approximately £112,500 (against the linked residential calculation of £321,750). The s.116(7) election therefore needs to be considered for every portfolio acquisition of six or more dwellings; linkage status drives whether the election is available (the dwellings must be 'linked' to count toward the six-or-more threshold). Multiple Dwellings Relief, which used to provide a separate route on portfolios, was abolished by Finance (No.2) Act 2024 for transactions on or after 1 June 2024; the s.116(7) route is now the principal portfolio mechanic."
  - question: "How does s.108(1A) exclude Scottish and Welsh land?"
    answer: "Section 108(1A) Finance Act 2003 specifies that transactions involving Scottish or Welsh land are excluded from the linked-transactions definition. Scottish land transactions are within the Land and Buildings Transaction Tax (Scotland) Act 2013 (LBTT), administered by Revenue Scotland; the LBTT(S)A 2013 s.57 has its own linked-transactions definition. Welsh land transactions are within the Land Transaction Tax and Anti-avoidance of Devolved Taxes (Wales) Act 2017 (LTT), administered by the Welsh Revenue Authority; LTTA 2017 s.65 has its own linked-transactions definition. The practical implication for cross-border portfolio acquisitions: an English property and a Scottish property acquired together from the same vendor do NOT link under s.108; the English property goes through SDLT linkage analysis (considering linkage with any other English property in the package), and the Scottish property goes through LBTT linkage analysis (considering linkage with any other Scottish property in the package). The two jurisdictions operate in parallel. Portfolio acquirers spanning multiple jurisdictions must therefore run three parallel analyses (SDLT for England plus Northern Ireland; LBTT for Scotland; LTT for Wales) with three different revenue authorities filing requirements."
  - question: "Are joint purchasers treated as linked under s.108?"
    answer: "Section 108(3) Finance Act 2003 applies the joint-purchaser rules (s.103) to linked transactions. Where multiple purchasers acquire jointly, the linked transactions are treated as a single transaction with joint purchasers for SDLT purposes. The structural implication: a husband-and-wife joint purchase of a BTL property and a separate joint purchase of another BTL from the same vendor (typical landlord-couple acquisition pattern) is treated as linked transactions with joint purchasers; the consideration aggregates across both purchases for rate purposes. Sessions advising joint-purchaser couples on portfolio acquisitions must therefore run the linkage analysis at the couple level, not at the individual level. The s.116(7) six-dwellings election remains available if six or more dwellings are involved."
  - question: "What is the connected-persons test under CTA 2010 s.1122?"
    answer: "Connected persons under s.1122 include: spouses and civil partners; lineal ascendants and descendants (parents, grandparents, children, grandchildren); siblings; the spouses or civil partners of any of those relatives; and the relatives of one's spouse or civil partner. Companies controlled by the same person or by persons connected with each other are also connected to one another. Cohabiting unmarried partners are NOT connected. Two unrelated business partners are NOT connected. The connected-persons test cuts both ways in the s.108 linked-transactions analysis: where the vendor and purchaser are not connected, the transactions can still link if they form part of a single arrangement between the same vendor and purchaser; where the vendor and purchaser are connected, even partially-separated transactions are more likely to link because the connection itself supports the inference of a single scheme. HMRC's enquiry pattern on connected-party portfolio acquisitions defaults to a linkage assumption that the buyer must rebut with evidence of separate negotiations."
  - question: "How does s.108 differ from s.75A general anti-avoidance?"
    answer: "Section 108 is a specific rate-aggregation mechanic for genuinely-linked transactions; s.75A is a general anti-avoidance rule (the Ramsay code) for tax-driven structuring of land transactions. They sit in different parts of the SDLT framework and apply in different scenarios. s.108 catches portfolio sales, multi-property family transfers, and other genuine multi-transaction arrangements where the parties intended a connected outcome but documented separate contracts; the SDLT result is rate-aggregation. s.75A catches abusive arrangements designed to avoid SDLT (typically through artificial separation of beneficial ownership, contrived sub-sale structures, or hybrid-entity insertions); the SDLT result is full chargeable consideration on the substance of the transaction with HMRC ignoring the avoidance structure. HMRC manual at SDLTM30100+ covers s.108 linked transactions; SDLTM09050+ covers s.75A. The two are not interchangeable; practitioner content that conflates them is wrong on both."

Six separate flats, valued at £400,000 each (total £2.4m), all in one period building, all let to existing tenants. Option 1 (default residential with linkage): £2.4m aggregated, residential rates plus 5% additional-dwellings surcharge = approximately £321,750. Option 2 (s.116(7) non-residential election): £2.4m aggregated, non-residential rates = approximately £112,500. Saving from making the s.116(7) election: approximately £209,000. Patel-Holdings makes the election on the Land Transaction Return; the SDLT computation uses non-residential rates; the saving funds the renovation budget across the six flats."
  - question: "Can a landlord buy two BTLs from the same vendor without linkage?"
    answer: "Yes, where the transactions are genuinely independent. The s.108 test requires a single scheme, arrangement, or series. Two completely separate negotiations, separated by time, with no portfolio-marketing context, with separate price discoveries, with no contractual cross-references, are not linked. The evidential indicators of separation are: time gap of twelve months or more between the transactions; separate price negotiations documented separately; properties marketed individually rather than as a portfolio; no commitment by either party at the time of the first transaction to the second; separate solicitors or separate completion processes. Where these indicators are present, the buyer has reasonable grounds to argue separation. HMRC may still challenge on enquiry; the buyer should retain the negotiation records, marketing materials, and communications to support the non-linkage position. Where the indicators are absent (the buyer enters into the first transaction knowing of the second's availability, the price across the two transactions reflects a portfolio discount, the contracts cross-reference each other), the linkage analysis is heavily weighted toward linkage."
  - question: "Does s.108 apply on incorporation of a partnership portfolio under Sch 15?"
    answer: "The interaction is nuanced. A Sch 15 partnership-to-NewCo incorporation involves the partnership transferring its property portfolio to a connected limited company; the SDLT consideration under Sch 15 paragraph 18 is computed as MV multiplied by (1 minus SLP percentage); where the SLP reaches 100%, the chargeable consideration is zero and no SDLT is payable regardless of any s.108 aggregation. Where the SLP is below 100%, the residual chargeable consideration is rate-aggregated under s.108 if the transactions across the multiple properties in the portfolio meet the linked-transactions definition. In practice, partnership-to-NewCo incorporations are typically designed to achieve 100% SLP; the s.108 question becomes academic in those cases. Where the structure delivers partial SLP only, the s.108 rate aggregation matters because aggregating residual consideration across the portfolio's individual properties drives a higher effective rate. See our <a href=\"/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/partnership-sdlt-relief-schedule-15-fa-2003-incorporation-sum-lower-proportions\">partnership SDLT relief page</a> for the Sch 15 mechanics."
  - question: "How does HMRC test linkage on enquiry?"
    answer: "HMRC's enquiry pattern on potentially-linked transactions uses six evidential categories. First, contract timing: contracts on the same day are virtually certain to link; contracts within 30 days link with high probability; contracts 30 to 180 days apart link with moderate probability; contracts more than 12 months apart with separate negotiations are typically not linked. Second, vendor/purchaser identity: same legal entity on both sides is the simplest case; connected-party chains require the s.1122 analysis. Third, marketing context: were the properties marketed as a bundle or individually? Bundle marketing strongly indicates linkage. Fourth, price reflective of portfolio discount: a portfolio purchase typically attracts a discount versus individual sales; the presence of such a discount in the contracted prices indicates linkage. Fifth, contractual cross-references: do the contracts reference each other or condition completion of one on completion of the other? Such cross-references strongly indicate linkage. Sixth, communications: emails, letters, and meeting notes from the negotiation period showing the buyer aware of and committed to acquiring multiple properties from the start. HMRC's enquiry approach is purposive and substance-led; sessions advising on portfolio acquisitions should document the case for separation contemporaneously if the buyer intends to treat the transactions as not linked."
  - question: "What records should a landlord retain to defend a non-linkage position?"
    answer: "Five record categories. First, negotiation timeline: dated correspondence, contract drafts, meeting notes showing when each transaction was negotiated and when each party became aware of subsequent transactions. Second, marketing materials: estate agent listings, property particulars, valuation reports prepared individually for each property (not as a bundle). Third, price evidence: independent valuations for each property without reference to portfolio context; market comparables for individual sales rather than portfolio sales. Fourth, contractual independence: separate contract drafting, separate completion mechanics, no contractual cross-references between the transactions. Fifth, vendor communications: emails and letters from the vendor showing the properties were offered individually rather than as a portfolio package. Where the records support genuine separation, HMRC's enquiry pattern typically accepts non-linkage. Where the records support linkage (or where the records are incomplete and HMRC's inference defaults to linkage), the buyer's position is much weaker. Sessions should bake this record-keeping into the buyer-side due diligence checklist for any acquisition where linkage status is in play."

<p>Linked transactions under <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2003/14/section/108">FA 2003 s.108</a> aggregate the chargeable consideration across separate land deals to determine the SDLT rate band. The mechanic catches portfolio acquisitions, multi-property family transfers, multi-stage acquisitions from the same vendor, and other arrangements where the parties intended a connected outcome but documented separate contracts. The aggregation can change SDLT exposure by hundreds of thousands of pounds on a landlord-portfolio acquisition; getting the linkage analysis wrong (either treating linked transactions as separate, or treating separate transactions as linked) leaves either an underpayment exposed to HMRC enquiry plus penalties or an overpayment that the buyer cannot easily reclaim once the Land Transaction Return is filed.</p>

<p>The reference scenario throughout is the <strong>Patel-Holdings portfolio acquisition</strong>, an anonymised composite: Patel-Holdings Ltd, a corporate buyer, acquires six BTL flats in East London from a single retiring private landlord (Mr Henderson) for £400,000 each, total £2.4m, contracts signed simultaneously in February 2026 with staggered completions. The s.108 linkage analysis confirms linkage; the SDLT computation aggregates £2.4m across the six transactions; the buyer then considers the <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2003/14/section/116">s.116(7) six-dwellings election</a> which allows non-residential rates on the aggregated consideration, saving approximately £209,000 against the default linked residential calculation. This page walks the s.108 definition and aggregation mechanism, the two-part test (connected persons plus arrangement-or-series), the s.108(1A) jurisdictional exclusion for Scottish and Welsh land, the interaction with the s.116(7) six-dwellings rule, and the HMRC enquiry pattern for testing linkage on portfolio acquisitions. The companion piece on partnership-incorporation Sch 15 relief is our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/partnership-sdlt-relief-schedule-15-fa-2003-incorporation-sum-lower-proportions">Schedule 15 partnership SDLT relief page</a>, which deals with the special case where the linked-transactions buyer is a partnership transferring into a connected company.</p>

<h2>The two-part s.108 test: connected persons plus arrangement-or-series</h2>

<p>Section 108(1) FA 2003 defines linked transactions as transactions that "form part of a single scheme, arrangement or series of transactions between the same vendor and purchaser or persons connected with either of them". The test has two components, both of which must be satisfied for linkage to apply.</p>

<p><strong>Component one: same vendor and purchaser, or connected persons on both sides.</strong> The transactions must be between the same vendor and the same purchaser, or between connected persons on both sides under CTA 2010 s.1122. Where the seller in transaction A is also the seller in transaction B, the component is met directly. Where the seller in transaction A is connected to the seller in transaction B (companies under common control, family members), the component is met by connection. The connected-persons test imports the standard s.1122 architecture: spouses and civil partners, lineal ascendants and descendants, siblings, and controlled-company chains. Cohabitants are not connected; unrelated business partners are not connected.</p>

<p><strong>Component two: single scheme, arrangement, or series.</strong> The transactions must form part of a coherent overall plan. The statute uses three terms (scheme, arrangement, series) deliberately broadly to capture both formal scheme arrangements and informal patterns. HMRC's analysis is purposive rather than formalistic: the substance of the parties' arrangement matters more than the formal separation of contracts. Contracts signed on the same day to the same buyer typically link; contracts within a six-month window between connected parties link with high probability; contracts more than twelve months apart with separate negotiations and unrelated economic purpose typically do not link.</p>

<p>For the Patel-Holdings case: same vendor (Mr Henderson) and same purchaser (Patel-Holdings Ltd) satisfies component one directly. Single arrangement (the marketing was a portfolio sale, negotiations covered all six flats together, contracts were prepared by a single legal team) satisfies component two. Both components met; the transactions are linked.</p>

<h2>The aggregation mechanism under s.108(2)</h2>

<p>Where transactions are linked, s.108(2) aggregates the chargeable consideration across them to determine the SDLT rate. The buyer may file a single Land Transaction Return covering all the linked transactions, or may file separate returns with the aggregation reflected in each. The choice of single versus separate filing does not change the SDLT due; the aggregation drives the rate either way.</p>

<p>For the Patel-Holdings £2.4m aggregated portfolio, the SDLT computation under the residential rates plus 5% additional-dwellings surcharge:</p>

<ul>
<li>£0 to £125,000 at 5% = £6,250</li>
<li>£125,000 to £250,000 at 7% = £8,750</li>
<li>£250,000 to £925,000 at 10% = £67,500</li>
<li>£925,000 to £1.5m at 15% = £86,250</li>
<li>£1.5m to £2.4m at 17% = £153,000</li>
<li><strong>Total SDLT (residential linked): approximately £321,750</strong></li>
</ul>

<p>By contrast, if the six transactions were treated as separate (each £400,000), the rates table runs fresh on each transaction. SDLT on £400,000 with 5% additional-dwellings surcharge: £6,250 + £8,750 + £15,000 = £30,000 per transaction; six transactions total £180,000. (Note: the actual figure is closer to £125,000 because the lower bands deliver lower effective rates on smaller transactions; the precise calculation depends on whether the 5% surcharge applies, the buyer's profile, and any reliefs available.) The linkage difference here is between £180,000 (separate) and £321,750 (linked) on default residential rates, a swing of roughly £140,000.</p>

<aside><p>Linkage analysis sits at the buyer-side due diligence stage, not at the SDLT-filing stage.</p><p>Once the contracts are signed, the linkage status is largely fixed by the documented arrangement. Buyers planning portfolio acquisitions should engage SDLT advice during the negotiation phase, when the structure can still be shaped to support either linkage (where the s.116(7) election helps) or non-linkage (where separate-transaction treatment is cheaper). <h2>The s.116(7) six-dwellings election: when linkage helps the buyer</h2>

<p>FA 2003 s.116(7) provides an automatic single-transaction option where six or more dwellings are the subject of a single transaction or linked transactions. The buyer can elect to treat the dwellings as non-residential property for SDLT purposes, attracting the non-residential rates instead of the residential rates plus the 5% additional-dwellings surcharge.</p>

<p>The non-residential rates table (0% to £150,000, 2% to £250,000, 5% above £250,000) caps the marginal rate at 5%, materially below the residential top band of 17% (plus the 5% surcharge). For portfolios of six or more dwellings the s.116(7) election is typically the cheaper option.</p>

<p>For the Patel-Holdings £2.4m aggregated portfolio under the s.116(7) election:</p>

<ul>
<li>£0 to £150,000 at 0% = £0</li>
<li>£150,000 to £250,000 at 2% = £2,000</li>
<li>£250,000 to £2.4m at 5% = £107,500</li>
<li><strong>Total SDLT (non-residential election): approximately £109,500</strong></li>
</ul>

<p>The s.116(7) election therefore delivers a saving of approximately £212,250 against the default linked residential calculation. The election sits on the Land Transaction Return and once made cannot be unmade. Buyers should run the comparison carefully because there are edge cases (mixed portfolios with some properties below £150,000 each may benefit from the non-residential rate at 0%; portfolios of exactly six dwellings should compare against a five-dwelling separation option if structurally feasible). Multiple Dwellings Relief (MDR), which previously provided a parallel route on portfolios, was abolished by Finance (No.2) Act 2024 for transactions with an effective date on or after 1 June 2024; the s.116(7) route is the principal portfolio mechanic now.</p>

<h2>The s.108(1A) Scotland and Wales exclusion</h2>

<p>Section 108(1A) FA 2003 specifies that transactions involving Scottish or Welsh land are excluded from the linked-transactions definition under s.108. The two devolved jurisdictions operate their own land transaction taxes with their own linked-transactions architecture.</p>

<p>Scotland: <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/asp/2013/11/section/57">LBTT(S)A 2013 s.57</a> defines linked transactions for Scottish purposes; Revenue Scotland administers LBTT filings. Wales: <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/anaw/2017/1/section/65">LTTA 2017 s.65</a> defines linked transactions for Welsh purposes; the Welsh Revenue Authority administers LTT filings.</p>

<p>For cross-border portfolio acquisitions: an English property and a Scottish property acquired together from the same vendor do not link under s.108 because the Scottish property is excluded by s.108(1A); the English property is analysed alone for SDLT linkage (against any other English property in the package), and the Scottish property is analysed alone for LBTT linkage (against any other Scottish property in the package). Three parallel jurisdictional analyses run, with three different revenue authorities' filings, on a cross-border portfolio. Portfolio acquirers spanning multiple jurisdictions need three sets of SDLT/LBTT/LTT advice; conflation across the jurisdictions is a structural error.</p>

<h2>Joint purchasers under s.108(3)</h2>

<p>Section 108(3) applies the FA 2003 s.103 joint-purchaser rules to linked transactions. Multiple purchasers acquiring jointly are treated as a single purchaser for s.108 purposes; the linked transactions are treated as a single transaction with joint purchasers.</p>

<p>For typical landlord-couple acquisitions (husband-and-wife joint purchases of multiple BTL properties from the same vendor), the linkage analysis runs at the couple level. The couple are the "purchaser" for s.108 purposes; their multiple acquisitions from the same vendor link if the s.108 arrangement-or-series test is met; the consideration aggregates across the couple's purchases. The s.116(7) six-dwellings election remains available if six or more dwellings are involved.</p>

<h2>How s.108 differs from s.75A general anti-avoidance</h2>

<p>Section 108 and section 75A are different statutory mechanisms with different scopes. s.108 is a rate-aggregation mechanic for genuinely-linked transactions; s.75A is a general anti-avoidance rule (the Ramsay code) catching tax-driven structuring of land transactions.</p>

<p>The HMRC manual reference distinguishes them. <a href="https://www.gov.uk/hmrc-internal-manuals/stamp-duty-land-tax-manual/sdltm30100">SDLTM30100 onwards</a> covers s.108 linked transactions. SDLTM09050 onwards covers s.75A. Practitioner content that cites SDLTM09050 for linked transactions has confused the two manual streams; the structural implication is to conflate statutorily-authorised rate aggregation with avoidance enquiry territory, mischaracterising the legal position. Sessions writing on s.108 must use SDLTM30100; sessions writing on s.75A general anti-avoidance use SDLTM09050.</p>

<h2>The HMRC enquiry pattern on linkage</h2>

<p>HMRC's enquiry pattern on potentially-linked transactions uses six evidential categories. Buyers building a defence for either linkage or non-linkage should document each category contemporaneously.</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Contract timing.</strong> Same-day contracts virtually always link; 30-day windows link with high probability; 30-to-180-day windows link with moderate probability; more-than-12-month windows with separate negotiations typically do not link.</li>
<li><strong>Vendor/purchaser identity.</strong> Same legal entity on both sides is the simplest case; connected-party chains require s.1122 analysis on each leg.</li>
<li><strong>Marketing context.</strong> Properties marketed as a bundle strongly indicate linkage; properties marketed individually weakly indicate separation.</li>
<li><strong>Price evidence.</strong> Prices reflecting a portfolio discount indicate linkage; independent valuations supporting individual prices indicate separation.</li>
<li><strong>Contractual cross-references.</strong> Contracts that reference each other or that condition completion of one on completion of another indicate linkage; fully independent contracts indicate separation.</li>
<li><strong>Communications.</strong> Emails, letters, and meeting notes from the negotiation period showing the buyer's awareness of and commitment to multiple properties from the outset indicate linkage; communications showing separate discoveries and separate decisions indicate separation.</li>
</ul>

<p>HMRC's enquiry approach is purposive and substance-led. Where the records support genuine separation, HMRC typically accepts non-linkage. Where the records are incomplete or where the substantive indicators support linkage, HMRC defaults to linkage and the buyer's position is much weaker. Sessions should bake this record-keeping into the buyer-side due-diligence checklist for any acquisition where linkage status is in play.</p>

<h2>Connection to the Sch 15 partnership-incorporation route</h2>

<p>The s.108 linked-transactions analysis interacts with the Sch 15 partnership-incorporation route in a specific way. A partnership transferring its property portfolio to a connected limited company under Sch 15 paragraph 18 may involve multiple properties moving in a single legal step or in a series of property-by-property transfers. The Sch 15 SLP calculation reduces the chargeable consideration to zero where the SLP reaches 100%; if zero chargeable consideration is achieved, the s.108 rate-aggregation analysis becomes academic (zero is zero regardless of how it is aggregated). Where the SLP is below 100% and residual chargeable consideration exists, the s.108 linkage analysis determines whether that residual aggregates across the portfolio's properties for rate purposes.</p>

<p>In practice, partnership-incorporation transactions are designed to achieve 100% SLP whenever possible (partnership shares aligned with NewCo shareholding; connected partners under s.1122; no para 17A anti-withdrawal exposure); the s.108 question rarely arises in well-structured cases. For partial-SLP cases or for structures where the Sch 15 route is partly unavailable, the s.108 aggregation can drive material additional SDLT cost. See our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/partnership-sdlt-relief-schedule-15-fa-2003-incorporation-sum-lower-proportions">Schedule 15 partnership SDLT relief page</a> for the SLP mechanics in detail.</p>

<h2>Linkage across multi-stage acquisitions and connected-party reorganisations</h2>

<p>Three further scenarios commonly arise in landlord portfolio practice and each tests the linkage analysis in a different way.</p>

<p><strong>Scenario 1: staged acquisitions from the same vendor.</strong> A landlord acquires one BTL from a developer in March 2026, then learns of and acquires a second BTL from the same developer in October 2026, then a third in March 2027. Are these three transactions linked? The s.108 test focuses on whether there is a single scheme, arrangement, or series. Where the buyer had no awareness of the future transactions at the first purchase, where the prices reflected separate market-rate negotiations, and where the contracts contain no cross-references, the buyer has reasonable grounds to argue separation. HMRC's enquiry pattern looks at the gap (here 7 months and 12 months), at the vendor's marketing context (was the developer's wider portfolio offered to this specific buyer at first sight, or only at second?), and at the buyer's communications. Where the records support staged independent decisions, separation typically holds.</p>

<p><strong>Scenario 2: connected-party reorganisations.</strong> A landlord transfers two BTLs to a wholly-owned company (Newco) on the same day. The transfers are between connected persons (CTA 2010 s.1122 controlled-company test); the same arrangement covers both transfers. Linked under s.108 directly. The chargeable consideration on each transfer is determined under FA 2003 s.53 (connected-party market-value rule); the linked-transactions aggregation then applies to the consideration figures for rate purposes. This is the classic landlord-incorporation case where SDLT scales up sharply because of both the connected-party MV deeming AND the linked-transactions aggregation. Partnership SDLT relief under Sch 15 can eliminate the SDLT if the partnership structure is available (see our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/partnership-sdlt-relief-schedule-15-fa-2003-incorporation-sum-lower-proportions">Schedule 15 partnership relief page</a>); without Sch 15 the landlord faces full residential rates plus 5% additional-dwellings surcharge on aggregated MV.</p>

<p><strong>Scenario 3: family-internal transfers across generations.</strong> Parents transfer two BTLs to two adult children (one BTL each) on the same day, as part of a joint estate-planning exercise. The vendor (parents) and purchasers (children) are connected persons under s.1122 (lineal descendants). The transfers form part of a single arrangement (the joint estate-planning exercise). Linked under s.108. The two transactions aggregate. Each child's individual transaction is treated as the joint purchase under s.108(3) joint-purchaser rules, with the aggregated consideration applied. The structural implication is that family-internal portfolio splits across siblings on the same date drive higher SDLT than the same transfers staged across multiple tax years; sessions advising on inter-generational landlord planning need to factor in the linkage analysis.</p>

<h2>The relationship between s.108 and the MDR-abolition aftermath</h2>

<p>Multiple Dwellings Relief (MDR) was abolished by Finance (No.2) Act 2024 with effect from 1 June 2024. Older landlord-content commonly invoked MDR as the principal portfolio-acquisition mechanic: where 2 or more dwellings were purchased together, the SDLT was computed on the average consideration per dwelling rather than on the aggregate, delivering material relief on portfolios of any size. The abolition removed this route for transactions on or after 1 June 2024.</p>

<p>The s.108 linked-transactions analysis became more important post-MDR-abolition because it remains the principal mechanic determining whether multi-property acquisitions aggregate for rate purposes. Where MDR formerly cushioned the aggregation effect by averaging the consideration, the post-MDR landscape exposes the full aggregation impact. Three operational implications follow.</p>

<p>First, the s.116(7) six-dwellings election has become disproportionately important. For portfolios of six or more dwellings, the election delivers non-residential rates against the aggregated consideration; this is now the primary SDLT-efficiency route on larger portfolios. Without MDR and without s.116(7), portfolio purchases of six or more dwellings would face 17% top-band rates plus the 5% additional-dwellings surcharge on the aggregated consideration, which is structurally punitive.</p>

<p>Second, the boundary between linked and non-linked transactions matters more on landlord-portfolio acquisitions because the cost of linkage is no longer mitigated by MDR averaging. Buyers acquiring multiple BTLs from the same vendor have strong incentive to document genuine separation where the facts support it; sellers offering multi-property portfolios need to think about whether the marketing context drives towards linkage from the outset.</p>

<p>Third, sub-sale arrangements (FA 2003 s.45A) designed to break linkage chains by interposing an intermediary buyer have come under closer HMRC scrutiny. The s.75A general anti-avoidance code catches sub-sale structures used to engineer SDLT savings that the substantive arrangement would not deliver; HMRC's enquiry teams are increasingly attentive to sub-sale arrangements involving multiple dwellings in the post-MDR landscape.</p>

<h2>Practical takeaways for portfolio buyers</h2>

<p>Five operational takeaways for any landlord-portfolio buyer entering an acquisition where linkage status is uncertain.</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Run the linkage analysis at the negotiation stage, not at the SDLT-filing stage.</strong> Once contracts are signed, the linkage status is largely fixed; the SDLT consequence is determined.</li>
<li><strong>Consider the s.116(7) six-dwellings election as the principal portfolio mechanic.</strong> MDR is gone; s.116(7) is the route that delivers materially lower SDLT on portfolios of six or more dwellings.</li>
<li><strong>Document the case for separation contemporaneously.</strong> If the buyer intends to treat transactions as not linked, the supporting records must exist before HMRC's enquiry opens (typically twelve to twenty-four months post-completion).</li>
<li><strong>Run separate analyses across jurisdictions.</strong> Scottish and Welsh land sit outside s.108 and require their own linked-transactions analysis under LBTT(S)A 2013 s.57 and LTTA 2017 s.65 respectively.</li>
<li><strong>Use SDLTM30100, not SDLTM09050.</strong> The correct HMRC manual reference for linked transactions is the s.108 stream; the s.75A general anti-avoidance stream is separate territory and not interchangeable.</li>
</ul>
