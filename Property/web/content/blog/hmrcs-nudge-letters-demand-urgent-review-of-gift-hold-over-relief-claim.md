<p>You opened the envelope and HMRC has asked you to review a hold-over relief claim from a return you filed years ago. The letter does not allege wrongdoing. It is not the start of a formal enquiry. It invites you, within 60 days, to confirm the prior claim or amend the return if you now think the claim was wrong. The letter is a nudge: HMRC's published One-Too-Many campaign mechanic, deployed at scale against population groups where HMRC's risk analysis points at a recurring claim type that often fails on scrutiny.</p>

<p>For landlord-cohort recipients, the recurring claim type is hold-over relief on a property gift. The three failure points are well established: a buy-to-let gift where section 165 was claimed despite the rental being investment activity rather than trading; a gift into a discretionary trust where the trust is settlor-interested and so disqualifies the section 260 claim; and a mixed-use estate gift where the agricultural element under Schedule 7 Part 1 was not properly separated from the residential and paddock elements. This page sets out the statutory framework that determines whether your prior claim survives, the 60-day operational response sequence, the Digital Disclosure Service route if the claim was wrong, and the penalty and appeal architecture that follows.</p>

<h2>The statutory architecture of hold-over relief</h2>

<p>Two separate statutory routes provide hold-over relief on CGT for gifts of property, under different conditions and against different recipient profiles.</p>

<p><strong>Section 165 TCGA 1992 (gifts of business assets):</strong> applies to gifts of assets used for the purposes of a trade, profession or vocation carried on by the donor or by their personal company. Schedule 7 Part 1 extends the relief to agricultural property held for agricultural purposes. Schedule 7 Part 2 contains restrictions on settled property (preventing claims that would route around the trust regime). Section 165 is the route used for business-property gifts within a family.</p>

<p><strong>Section 260 TCGA 1992 (gifts on which IHT is chargeable):</strong> applies to chargeable transfers within the meaning of IHTA 1984, typically chargeable lifetime transfers (CLTs) into discretionary or interest-in-possession trusts. Section 260 is the route used for gifts into trust where IHT enters the picture. Where section 260 applies, it operates on the same hold-over mechanic as section 165: the donor's CGT is deferred and the recipient's base cost is reduced.</p>

<p>The two routes can both engage on the same transaction (a gift of business assets into a discretionary trust). Where both engage, section 165 typically takes priority in the operative analysis. The recipient (trustees) inherits the held-over gain in their base cost; the eventual disposal by the trustees crystallises the gain at residential rates (18% or 24% per the current TCGA 1992 section 1H and section 4 architecture).</p>

<h2>Why section 165 fails for buy-to-let property</h2>

<p>The most common landlord-cohort nudge-letter failure point. A donor gifts a BTL property to an adult child. The donor's prior return shows a section 165 hold-over claim, treating the rental activity as a "business" carried on for section 165 purposes. The 2026 nudge letter asks the donor to confirm or amend.</p>

<p>Section 165(2) requires the asset to be used for the purposes of a trade carried on by the donor. The settled HMRC view, supported by the Upper Tribunal analysis in Pawson v HMRC [2013] UKUT 50 (a Business Property Relief case decided on the same trade-versus-investment line by analogy), is that pure residential letting is INVESTMENT activity, not trading. A buy-to-let landlord is investing in property and earning rental yield; they are not carrying on a trade for tax purposes.</p>

<p>The Aldridge-Estate scenario. In 2022/23 the landlord gifted a £400,000 BTL property (acquired 2010 for £180,000) to an adult child. The donor claimed section 165 on the £220,000 gain. The 2024/25 SA return reflected the held-over gain as deferred to the child's base cost. In 2026 the nudge letter arrives.</p>

<p>The section 165 claim fails. The gain crystallises on the 2022/23 disposal date at residential CGT rates: 28% pre-6-April-2024 (still operative for early 2022/23 disposals before the rate change for residential) or 24% post-6-April-2024. On a £220,000 gain at 24% the consequential tax is around £52,800. Schedule 24 careless-unprompted floor at 0% applies if the donor self-corrects within the 60-day window. The Schedule 24 prompted-disclosure floor of 15% applies if HMRC has to escalate.</p>

<p>The failure point is not "the donor was sloppy" or "the donor lied". It is structural: the section 165 gate was misread to include investment property. The remediation route is the Digital Disclosure Service, used within the 60-day window to secure the unprompted-disclosure mitigation floor.</p>

<h2>The settlor-interested trust trap on section 260</h2>

<p>The second common failure point. A donor settles property into a discretionary trust as a CLT. The donor claims section 260 hold-over on the gain. The trust deed includes the donor's spouse among the discretionary beneficiaries.</p>

<p>Sections 169B and 169C of TCGA 1992 disapply section 260 hold-over relief where the trust is settlor-interested. A trust is settlor-interested if the donor, the donor's spouse, or (in defined cases) the donor's minor children can benefit from the trust property. The exclusion catches the most common form of family-property trust: a discretionary settlement in which the donor's spouse is a named potential beneficiary, even where no distribution to the spouse has occurred or is intended.</p>

<p>The Mawell-Estate scenario. In 2023/24 the donor settled a £600,000 commercial-property holding into a discretionary trust as a CLT. The donor claimed section 260 on the £350,000 gain. The trust deed includes the donor's spouse among the discretionary beneficiaries. The 2026 nudge letter asks the donor to review the section 260 claim.</p>

<p>The trust is settlor-interested under section 169B. Section 169C disapplies section 260 on these facts. The £350,000 gain crystallises in the donor's hands at the date of the CLT. Commercial property attracts the standard CGT rate (20% post-6-April-2024 for higher-rate taxpayers), so the consequential CGT is around £70,000. Schedule 24 mitigation floors apply on unprompted disclosure within the 60-day window.</p>

<p>A drafting note on the statute. The Wave 6 lock for this site (F-4) confirms that section 169G subsections (2) to (5), which historically set out the settlor definition, were OMITTED by Finance Act 2009. Sessions writing on the settlor-interested-trust exclusion must NOT cite section 169G(2) to (5) as the live settlor definition; the operative definition runs through section 169E and references the ITTOIA 2005 section 625 trust-settlor framework as adapted. Pre-2009 commentary and many older textbooks still carry the omitted-subsections framing.</p>

<h2>The agricultural bifurcation under Schedule 7 Part 1</h2>

<p>The third common failure point. A donor gifts a mixed estate (farmland plus paddock plus residential garden plus farmhouse) to an adult child. The donor claims section 165 plus the Schedule 7 Part 1 agricultural extension on the whole disposal.</p>

<p>Schedule 7 Part 1 extends section 165 to agricultural property held for agricultural purposes. The extension is precise. Only the genuinely agricultural element of the estate qualifies. Non-agricultural paddock (used for recreational equestrian activity rather than commercial agriculture), residential garden, and any element with no commercial-agricultural use sit OUTSIDE the extension.</p>

<p>The Singh-Estate scenario. In 2023/24 the donor gifted a 12-hectare property to an adult child: 8 hectares of agricultural land plus a farmhouse, 3 hectares of paddock with no commercial-agricultural activity, and 1 hectare of residential garden. The donor claimed section 165 plus Schedule 7 on the whole disposal.</p>

<p>The section 165 claim survives on the 8-hectare agricultural element plus the farmhouse (subject to the farmhouse meeting the character-of-the-property and occupier tests). The claim fails on the 3 hectares of paddock and the 1 hectare of garden. The donor must amend the return to bifurcate: hold-over applies to the agricultural portion only; the non-agricultural portion crystallises as a standard section 17 TCGA 1992 market-value disposal. The arithmetic of bifurcation is fact-specific (acreage proportions, valuation by element) and typically requires a chartered surveyor's apportionment.</p>

<h2>The 60-day operational response sequence</h2>

<p>The 60-day window is HMRC operational practice, not statutory. Within the period the taxpayer should:</p>

<ol>
<li><strong>Recover the underlying return.</strong> Pull the SA return for the year of the gift, the supporting CGT computation, and the original agent's working papers if the claim was filed via an agent.</li>
<li><strong>Characterise the underlying property type and trust profile.</strong> Was the property investment or trading? Was the trust settlor-interested? Was the estate mixed-use, requiring agricultural bifurcation?</li>
<li><strong>Reach a view.</strong> Either the prior claim was valid (in which case write to HMRC confirming, with the supporting analysis) OR the prior claim was wholly or partly invalid (in which case self-correct via the Digital Disclosure Service within the window).</li>
<li><strong>Quantify the consequential tax.</strong> Where the claim was wholly or partly invalid, compute the gain that crystallises (at the appropriate residential or commercial rate per the current TCGA 1992 section 1H and section 4 architecture), the interest that runs from the original SA payment date, and the Schedule 24 penalty band that applies (mitigated by the unprompted-disclosure floor where the self-correction is within the 60-day window).</li>
<li><strong>Submit the DDS disclosure or the confirmation letter.</strong> Pay the consequential tax plus interest plus penalty on or before submission of the DDS disclosure.</li>
</ol>

<p>The Carmichael-Estate scenario illustrates the operational difficulty where the recipient cannot quickly characterise the original claim. The recipient receives a nudge letter referring to a 2018/19 hold-over claim and does not remember the underlying transaction. The 60-day window starts from receipt. The recipient must request a copy of the 2018/19 return and computation from the original agent (or from HMRC under TMA 1970 access powers), characterise the property type, and respond. If the recipient cannot meet the deadline, the operational discipline is to communicate with HMRC promptly to seek an extension. Silence is the worst outcome: it risks a section 9A enquiry or, where the original return is out of enquiry-window, a section 29 discovery assessment.</p>

<h2>Schedule 24 penalty exposure and the unprompted-disclosure floor</h2>

<p>Schedule 24 FA 2007 governs the penalty regime where a return understated tax. Standard maxima:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Careless behaviour:</strong> maximum 30%, minimum floor 0% (unprompted) or 15% (prompted).</li>
<li><strong>Deliberate behaviour:</strong> maximum 70%, minimum floor 20% (unprompted) or 35% (prompted).</li>
<li><strong>Deliberate-concealed behaviour:</strong> maximum 100%, minimum floor 30% (unprompted) or 50% (prompted).</li>
</ul>

<p>The unprompted floor of 0% on careless behaviour is the operative target for a landlord self-correcting within the 60-day window. There is NO 12-month qualifier on the Schedule 24 unprompted floor; the qualifier exists on Schedule 41 paragraph 13 (which governs failure-to-notify rather than inaccuracy), and the two should not be conflated.</p>

<p>Offshore Category 2 (1.5x) and Category 3 (2x) uplifts apply where overseas elements are involved. For most landlord hold-over claims the property is UK-located so the uplifts do not engage; offshore-property gifts or non-resident-donor disposals can attract the uplift on the underlying penalty.</p>

<p>The behavioural characterisation (careless vs deliberate) is fact-specific. A donor who relied on professional advice that turned out to be wrong typically falls on the careless side. A donor who knew the BTL was investment but wrote "business" on the claim form deliberately falls on the deliberate side. The behavioural characterisation drives the floor; the timing of self-correction drives whether the unprompted floor or the prompted floor applies.</p>

<h2>If HMRC issues an amended assessment</h2>

<p>Where the taxpayer does not respond to the nudge letter, or responds confirming a claim that HMRC ultimately rejects on review, HMRC will issue an amended return or a discovery assessment. The 30-day appeal window starts from the date of the amendment or assessment under TMA 1970 section 31A.</p>

<p>The escalation route:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Internal review:</strong> available within the 30-day window. The taxpayer can request a statutory review by an HMRC officer not involved in the original decision.</li>
<li><strong>Alternative Dispute Resolution (ADR):</strong> available alongside or in place of internal review. ADR is a facilitated discussion led by an HMRC mediator, useful where the dispute turns on a fact-pattern question (the agricultural bifurcation acreage, the trust settlor-interest characterisation, the trade-versus-investment line for a marginal case).</li>
<li><strong>First-tier Tribunal:</strong> the statutory appeal route. The Tribunal hears the merits of the case on the statutory framework. Late appeals are admitted under the framework in Martland v HMRC [2018] UKUT 178.</li>
<li><strong>Upper Tribunal:</strong> appeals from the FTT on points of law. Perrin v HMRC [2018] UKUT 156 sets the reasonable-excuse four-stage test that applies to late-response penalty appeals.</li>
</ul>

<p>The reasonable-excuse defence is typically not available on the substantive hold-over claim (the claim was either statutorily valid or it was not). The defence is more often relevant on the procedural side: a late response to the nudge letter caused by serious illness, a death in the family, or comparable factor.</p>

<h2>The boundary against the parallel BADR nudge campaign</h2>

<p>HMRC operates a parallel nudge-letter campaign on prior Business Asset Disposal Relief (BADR) claims under TCGA 1992 sections 169H to 169SA. The campaigns share the same One-Too-Many mechanic but operate on entirely different statutory regimes.</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>The hold-over campaign</strong> targets section 165 and section 260 claims. Failure points: the trade-versus-investment line for section 165; the settlor-interested-trust trap for section 260; the Schedule 7 agricultural bifurcation.</li>
<li><strong>The BADR campaign</strong> targets section 169H to 169SA claims. Failure points: the trading-company test, the 2-year qualifying-period under section 169I(3), and the tri-conditional 5% economic-interest gate under section 169S(3).</li>
</ul>

<p>A landlord with BOTH a hold-over claim history AND a BADR claim history may receive nudge letters on both. The responses are separate. The statutory frameworks are separate. The remediation routes (DDS, Counter Avoidance team where appropriate) operate the same way but on different underlying tax computations.</p>

<h2>What to take away from a hold-over nudge letter</h2>

<p>Three operational disciplines that the 60-day window typically asks of a landlord recipient:</p>

<ol>
<li><strong>Engage immediately.</strong> The 60-day window is operational HMRC practice, not a statutory deadline, but silence is the worst outcome. Communicate with HMRC even if the substantive response will take longer; ask for an extension if the response cannot be ready in time.</li>
<li><strong>Characterise the original transaction precisely.</strong> Was the property trading or investment? Was the trust settlor-interested? Was the estate mixed-use, requiring agricultural bifurcation? The answer drives the substantive response.</li>
<li><strong>Use the unprompted-disclosure floor.</strong> Where the prior claim was wrong, self-correct via the Digital Disclosure Service within the 60-day window. The 0% careless-unprompted floor on Schedule 24 is materially better than the 15% prompted floor that applies once HMRC has escalated.</li>
</ol>
