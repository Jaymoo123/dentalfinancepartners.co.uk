faqs:
- question: Can sole traders or individual landlords claim full expensing?
  answer: No. Full expensing (the 100% first-year allowance) and the 50% first-year allowance are restricted to companies within the charge to corporation tax under CAA 2001 s.45S. Sole traders, partnerships and individual landlords cannot use them. If you are unincorporated, your routes are the annual investment allowance (up to £1 million a year), writing-down allowances, and from 1 January 2026 the new 40% first-year allowance under CAA 2001 s.45U.
- question: Is full expensing temporary, and does it expire in March 2026?
  answer: No. Full expensing is permanent. CAA 2001 s.45S, inserted by Finance (No. 2) Act 2023, contains no sunset or expiry clause, and permanence was confirmed at Autumn Statement 2023 and again at Autumn Budget 2024. There is no end date to plan around. Any guidance still describing it as temporary or as ending on 31 March 2026 is out of date.
- question: What is the new 40% first-year allowance and who is it for?
  answer: The 40% first-year allowance is a new relief introduced by Finance Act 2026 s.29, which inserts CAA 2001 s.45U. It applies to main-rate plant and machinery that is new and unused, for expenditure incurred on or after 1 January 2026, in cases not falling within full expensing (s.45S). In practice it is the route for unincorporated businesses (sole traders, partnerships) and for plant acquired for leasing, which is precisely where full expensing does not reach. It is not a fallback for company assets that fail the 100% test.
- question: Can I claim full expensing on a boiler or kitchen in a let flat?
  answer: Usually no. CAA 2001 s.35 bars plant and machinery allowances on plant for use in a dwelling-house within an ordinary property business, so even a company landlord generally cannot claim on a boiler, kitchen or bathroom inside a let flat or house. The narrow exception is plant in the communal common parts of a multi-let building (a block boiler, a lift, communal lighting), which can qualify because the common parts are not a dwelling-house.
- question: How does full expensing compare with the annual investment allowance?
  answer: The annual investment allowance (AIA) gives 100% relief on up to £1 million of qualifying plant and machinery a year and is available to both companies and unincorporated businesses, subject to the s.35 dwelling-house bar. Full expensing also gives 100% relief but only to companies, only on new and unused main-rate plant, and with no annual cap. For a company spending within the £1 million AIA limit the cash result is often the same, but full expensing matters above the cap and keeps the AIA free for second-hand or special-rate assets.
- question: What corporation tax rate does the relief save?
  answer: Each pound of full-expensing relief reduces taxable profits by a pound, so the cash saving depends on the company's corporation tax rate. The main rate of 25% applies to profits above £250,000, the small profits rate of 19% applies to profits under £50,000, and marginal relief tapers the effective rate between those two thresholds. A common error is to say the 19% rate applies under £250,000; it does not.
- question: What happens for capital allowances when the Furnished Holiday Lettings rules ended?
  answer: The Furnished Holiday Lettings (FHL) regime was abolished from 6 April 2025 (1 April 2025 for corporation tax) by Finance Act 2025 Schedule 5. FHLs are no longer a separate qualifying activity, so the old route to plant and machinery allowances on furniture and equipment inside the holiday let has gone. Former FHLs are now treated as an ordinary property business, where the s.35 dwelling-house bar applies in the normal way.
- question: Do cars qualify for full expensing or the 40% first-year allowance?
  answer: No. Cars are excluded from full expensing, from the annual investment allowance and from the new 40% first-year allowance by the general exclusions in CAA 2001 s.46. The only car first-year allowance is the 100% allowance for new and unused zero-emission cars (0 g/km CO2) under CAA 2001 s.45D. Cars above 0 g/km get no first-year allowance and are written down in the main pool (14%) or special rate pool (6%) depending on emissions.
- question: Does full expensing ever help a residential landlord?
  answer: Rarely on the dwellings themselves, because of the s.35 dwelling-house bar. Where it can help a company landlord is on qualifying plant in the communal common parts of a block of flats or an HMO, and on commercial premises the company holds for its own qualifying activity. For most residential portfolios held personally, the practical reliefs are repairs as revenue deductions, the AIA or 40% FYA on common-parts plant, and writing-down allowances.
- question: What is special rate expenditure, and which relief applies to it?
  answer: Special rate expenditure covers integral features (such as lifts, electrical and lighting systems, cold water systems, and air conditioning), thermal insulation and long-life assets. Companies can claim a 50% first-year allowance on this in the year of purchase under the full-expensing rules, with the balance entering the special rate pool. The annual investment allowance can also cover special-rate items. Anything left in the pool is written down at 6% a year, a rate unchanged by Finance Act 2026.
- question: What happens when a company sells an asset it fully expensed?
  answer: There is an immediate balancing charge on disposal. Where the 100% first-year allowance was claimed the charge is broadly the full disposal value, and where the 50% allowance was claimed there are specific disposal rules for the special rate pool. The effect is that the relief is recovered in the year of sale, so the disposal needs to be planned for. The detailed clawback mechanics, including the disposal-value rules and anti-avoidance, are covered in our companion guide on full expensing for commercial property companies.
- question: Should I incorporate just to access full expensing?
  answer: Not on its own. Incorporation is a major decision with stamp duty, capital gains tax and ongoing cost consequences, and the s.35 dwelling-house bar means a company still cannot claim full expensing on plant inside a let dwelling. Full expensing usually only changes the picture for substantial company-held commercial property or large common-parts plant. The capital-allowance saving should be one input into the incorporation decision, not the driver of it.
- question: What rate are writing-down allowances after Finance Act 2026?
  answer: From the first chargeable period beginning on or after 1 April 2026 for corporation tax (6 April 2026 for income tax), the main pool writing-down allowance is 14%, reduced from 18% by Finance Act 2026 s.28. The special rate pool stays at 6%. Periods straddling the change use a hybrid, time-apportioned rate. Writing-down allowances apply to expenditure that does not, or cannot, attract a first-year allowance or the AIA.

<p>The real question behind a search for "full expensing capital allowances" as a property investor is not what the relief is, but whether you can actually use it. The short answer is that full expensing is a 100% first-year deduction for <strong>companies only</strong> (CAA 2001 s.45S), and even then it does not reach plant inside a let dwelling. If you hold property personally, or your plant sits in a residential flat, full expensing is closed to you, but there are other routes, including the new 40% first-year allowance from January 2026. This guide maps which route applies to you by entity and property type, then shows where full expensing genuinely helps. It sits within our wider <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/capital-allowances-property-investors-complete-pillar-2026-27-caa-2001-decision-framework">capital allowances decision framework for property investors</a>.</p>

<h2>Which capital allowance route applies to you?</h2>
<p>Capital allowances for property are decided by three things at once: your <strong>entity</strong> (company or unincorporated), your <strong>property type</strong> (commercial, a residential dwelling, or the common parts of a multi-let), and the <strong>use</strong> of the asset (used in your own qualifying activity or held for leasing). The table below is the routing layer. Find your row first, then read the detail underneath.</p>

<p>Three lines do most of the work. <strong>Only companies claim full expensing.</strong> <strong>Plant inside a dwelling is barred</strong> for everyone (s.35). And <strong>the new 40% first-year allowance is the unincorporated route</strong>, not a company fallback. The rest of this guide explains each branch.</p>

<aside><p><strong>Not sure which route is yours?</strong></p><p>The answer turns on how you hold the property and what the plant is for. A property tax specialist can map your portfolio to the right relief before you spend, so you are not relying on an allowance you cannot claim.</p></aside>

<h2>What full expensing is, in one screen</h2>
<p>Full expensing is a 100% first-year allowance under CAA 2001 s.45S. It lets a company within the charge to corporation tax deduct the entire cost of qualifying plant and machinery from its taxable profits in the year of purchase, with no upper limit. To qualify, the expenditure must be incurred on or after 1 April 2023, by a company within the charge to corporation tax, on plant or machinery that is <strong>unused and not second-hand</strong>, and not caught by the disqualifying-arrangements rule in s.45T or the general exclusions in s.46 (which exclude cars and plant held for leasing).</p>
<p>A companion 50% first-year allowance applies to <strong>special rate</strong> expenditure, such as integral features and long-life assets, that would otherwise be written down slowly in the special rate pool. You cannot claim both allowances against the same expenditure.</p>
<p>Crucially, full expensing is <strong>permanent</strong>. The section as enacted has no sunset clause, and permanence was confirmed at Autumn Statement 2023 and again at Autumn Budget 2024. Any source still describing it as temporary, or as expiring on 31 March 2026, is out of date. For the detailed s.45S mechanics, the disposal-value clawback, the leasing question and the anti-avoidance rules, see our companion guide on <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/full-expensing-50-percent-fya-commercial-property-spvs-mechanics-fa-2023">full expensing and the 50% FYA for commercial property companies</a>. This page stays at the decision layer.</p>

<h2>Who can actually claim it: the entity fork</h2>
<p>Full expensing is restricted to companies within the charge to corporation tax. Sole traders, partnerships and limited liability partnerships cannot use it, and neither can an individual who holds buy-to-let property in their own name. This is the point at which most property investors fall out of the relief, because the majority of UK rental property is still held personally.</p>
<p>Take a landlord who holds two flats personally. They cannot claim full expensing on anything, full stop, because they are not a company. Their capital-allowance routes are the annual investment allowance, writing-down allowances, and from January 2026 the new 40% first-year allowance, all subject to the dwelling-house bar covered below. The same landlord operating the identical portfolio through a limited company would have full expensing available in principle, though the dwelling-house bar would still block most of the plant inside the flats themselves.</p>
<p>Because the entity choice is what unlocks (or blocks) full expensing, it cannot be separated from the wider incorporation decision. Our guide to running a <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/buy-to-let-limited-company-complete-guide-uk">buy-to-let limited company</a> sets out the stamp duty, capital gains and ongoing cost trade-offs that should drive that call. Full expensing is one input, not the reason to incorporate.</p>

<h2>The residential dwelling-house bar: section 35</h2>
<p>This is the section most generic full-expensing guides skip, and it is the one that matters most to property investors. CAA 2001 s.35 removes plant and machinery used in a <strong>dwelling-house</strong> from the definition of qualifying activity for an ordinary property business. In plain terms, you cannot claim capital allowances (full expensing, the 50% FYA, the AIA or the 40% FYA) on a boiler, a fitted kitchen, a bathroom suite or white goods that are installed for use inside a let flat or house. This is true whether you are a company or an individual.</p>
<p>The narrow exception is plant in the <strong>communal common parts</strong> of a multi-let building. A block boiler that heats the common hallways, a lift, communal lighting and a communal entry system serve the building rather than any single dwelling, so they fall outside the s.35 bar and can qualify for allowances. For a company, that opens the door to full expensing on new and unused common-parts plant; for an unincorporated landlord, the AIA or the 40% FYA applies instead. We cover this in detail in our guide to <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/hmo-common-parts-capital-allowances-s35-claim-mechanics-multi-tenant-property">HMO common-parts capital allowances and the s.35 claim mechanics</a>.</p>
<p>One related change: the Furnished Holiday Lettings regime, which used to give a separate route to plant and machinery allowances inside the holiday let, was abolished from 6 April 2025 (1 April 2025 for corporation tax) by Finance Act 2025 Schedule 5. FHLs are no longer a qualifying activity, so former holiday lets now fall under the ordinary s.35 bar like any other dwelling.</p>

<h2>If you cannot use full expensing: the 40% first-year allowance</h2>
<p>If you are unincorporated, the route built for you is the new <strong>40% first-year allowance</strong>. Finance Act 2026 s.29 inserts CAA 2001 s.45U, giving a 40% first-year deduction on main-rate plant and machinery for expenditure incurred on or after <strong>1 January 2026</strong>. It applies in cases not falling within full expensing (s.45S), which is the deliberate design point: it reaches the sole traders, partnerships and individual landlords that the company-only full-expensing rules cannot.</p>
<p>The plant must be <strong>new and unused</strong>. Second-hand assets are excluded, special rate expenditure is excluded (so integral features do not get the 40% rate), and the general exclusions in s.46 still bite, which means cars and plant held for leasing do not qualify. It is important to read the 40% FYA correctly: it is the unincorporated and main-rate route, not a residual fallback for company assets that fail the 100% full-expensing test.</p>
<p>For most unincorporated landlords, the 40% FYA sits alongside two longer-standing reliefs. The <strong>annual investment allowance</strong> gives 100% relief on up to £1 million of qualifying plant a year and is available to both companies and unincorporated businesses, and we cover its application to landlords in our guide to the <a href="/blog/section-24-and-tax-relief/annual-investment-allowance-landlords-uk">annual investment allowance for landlords</a> and the broader <a href="/blog/section-24-and-tax-relief/annual-investment-allowance-uk">annual investment allowance</a> rules. Anything not relieved by a first-year allowance or the AIA is written down through the pools.</p>

<h2>Full expensing, AIA, the 40% FYA and WDA: how they fit together</h2>
<p>These four reliefs are not alternatives you choose freely; each has its own gateway, and they layer in a set order. The practical question is which one delivers the relief for a given asset.</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Full expensing (s.45S):</strong> companies only, 100% in year one, new and unused main-rate plant, no annual cap.</li>
<li><strong>50% FYA:</strong> companies only, on special-rate expenditure (integral features, long-life assets), with the balance to the special rate pool.</li>
<li><strong>Annual investment allowance:</strong> companies and unincorporated businesses, 100% on up to £1 million a year, covers most plant including special-rate items, subject to s.35.</li>
<li><strong>40% FYA (s.45U):</strong> the unincorporated and main-rate route, new and unused, from 1 January 2026, no cars or leasing or second-hand.</li>
<li><strong>Writing-down allowances:</strong> the catch-all for expenditure that does not, or cannot, get a first-year allowance or the AIA. From Finance Act 2026 s.28 the main pool rate is <strong>14%</strong> (down from 18%, for chargeable periods beginning on or after 1 April 2026 for corporation tax and 6 April 2026 for income tax), and the special rate pool stays at <strong>6%</strong>.</li>
</ul>
<p>A company spending under the £1 million AIA limit on new main-rate plant often gets the same year-one result whether it labels the claim full expensing or AIA. Full expensing matters above the cap, or where the company wants to keep the AIA free for second-hand or special-rate items. Our guide to <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/writing-down-allowance-rates">writing-down allowance rates</a> covers how the pools work once first-year reliefs are exhausted.</p>

<h2>Corporation tax: what the relief actually saves</h2>
<p>Because full expensing reduces taxable profits pound for pound, the cash value of the relief is the profit saved multiplied by the company's corporation tax rate. The current rates are the <strong>main rate of 25%</strong> on profits above £250,000, the <strong>small profits rate of 19%</strong> on profits under £50,000, and <strong>marginal relief</strong> tapering the effective rate between £50,000 and £250,000.</p>
<p>So a company paying the main rate saves 25p of tax for every pound of qualifying expenditure, while a small company at the 19% rate saves 19p. A common and costly error in older guidance is to say the 19% small profits rate applies to profits under £250,000; it does not. The £250,000 figure is the upper threshold above which the full 25% applies, not the small-profits ceiling.</p>

<h2>Where full expensing genuinely helps a property investor</h2>
<p>Strip out the dwellings, and the case for full expensing comes down to a company holding <strong>commercial premises for its own qualifying activity</strong>: an office, a warehouse, a shop fit-out, or a trading business that owns its building. New and unused main-rate plant in those premises can be fully expensed, and special-rate integral features can take the 50% FYA.</p>
<p>The other live area is <strong>fixtures on the purchase of a commercial building</strong>. When commercial property changes hands, the buyer and seller can agree a s.198 election to fix the value transferred for fixtures already in the building, which then governs what the buyer can claim going forward. That is its own discipline, and we cover it in our guide to <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/commercial-property-fixtures-claim-s198-election-purchase-mechanics">claiming capital allowances on commercial property fixtures and the s.198 election</a>. The full SPV-side mechanics, including the disposal clawback, live in the <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/full-expensing-50-percent-fya-commercial-property-spvs-mechanics-fa-2023">commercial full-expensing companion guide</a>.</p>

<h2>A worked routing example</h2>
<p>Consider an investor who owns a block of six flats and is weighing whether to keep it in personal ownership or move it into a company. The block needs a new communal boiler serving the shared hallways and common areas. The boiler is new and unused, and it is plant in the common parts, so the s.35 dwelling-house bar does not apply to it.</p>
<p>Held <strong>personally</strong>, the investor cannot touch full expensing, because they are not a company. Their routes for the communal boiler are the annual investment allowance (100% in year one, well within the £1 million cap), or, from 1 January 2026, the 40% first-year allowance under s.45U on this new and unused main-rate plant, with any balance written down at 14%.</p>
<p>Held in a <strong>company</strong>, the same communal boiler could be fully expensed at 100% under s.45S, since it is new, unused, main-rate plant used in the company's property business and not held out for separate leasing. Note what does <em>not</em> change: a new boiler or kitchen installed <em>inside</em> one of the individual flats remains barred by s.35 under either structure. The right route here depends on entity and on which part of the building the plant serves, not on the size of the spend alone.</p>

<aside><p><strong>Planning a capital spend on a portfolio?</strong></p><p>Whether a boiler, a lift, a commercial fit-out or a building purchase qualifies, and at what rate, depends on entity and property type. Get the route confirmed before you commit the cost.</p></aside>

<h2>Common mistakes to avoid</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Claiming full expensing as a sole trader or individual landlord.</strong> It is company-only (s.45S). Unincorporated businesses use the AIA, WDA, or the 40% FYA (s.45U) from January 2026.</li>
<li><strong>Claiming any first-year allowance or the AIA on plant inside a let dwelling.</strong> The s.35 dwelling-house bar blocks it; only common-parts and commercial plant qualify.</li>
<li><strong>Treating the 40% FYA as a company route.</strong> It is the unincorporated and main-rate route, not a residual fallback for company assets.</li>
<li><strong>Assuming full expensing is temporary.</strong> It is permanent, with no expiry date in s.45S.</li>
<li><strong>Using the wrong corporation tax band.</strong> The 19% small profits rate applies under £50,000, not under £250,000.</li>
<li><strong>Forgetting the disposal charge.</strong> Selling a fully expensed asset triggers a balancing charge that recovers the relief, so disposals need planning.</li>
</ul>

<h2>How to claim and what to keep</h2>
<p>Capital allowances are claimed through the relevant tax return: a company tax return for full expensing and the 50% FYA, or the self-assessment return for the AIA, the 40% FYA and writing-down allowances. There is no separate application; the allowances are computed in the capital allowances section of the return. Keep invoices, purchase dates, and a clear description of each asset and where it is used in the property, because the entitlement to most of these reliefs (and especially the s.35 dwelling-house question) turns on exactly that detail in the event of an HMRC enquiry.</p>
