<p>From 6 April 2026 you no longer "register" for landlord tax reporting in the old postal sense. You <strong>sign up for Making Tax Digital for Income Tax</strong>, and that sign-up is a quick, self-service step done in your browser using the same Government Gateway login you already use for Self Assessment. The myth that it takes seven to ten working days, or that HMRC posts you a confirmation you have to wait for, is exactly that. The real work happens before you sign up: confirming you are over the threshold and getting compatible software in place.</p>
<p>This guide walks the genuine HMRC sign-up flow, step by step, for landlords. It corrects the menu paths that do not exist, covers what joint owners and non-residents actually do, explains how an accountant signs you up through an Agent Services Account, and sets out the penalties for missing the deadlines. For the wider picture of how the regime works once you are in it, our <a href="/blog/making-tax-digital-mtd/making-tax-digital-property-income-2026-complete-guide">complete guide to Making Tax Digital for property income</a> is the overview this page sits under.</p>

<h2>Who must sign up, and who does not</h2>
<p>You are mandated into MTD for Income Tax once your <strong>qualifying income</strong> crosses the threshold for your tax year. Qualifying income is your <em>gross</em> property rent (and any sole-trade turnover) before any deductions, with the two streams added together. The phased thresholds are set in the Income Tax (Digital Obligations) Regulations 2026 (<a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2026/336/contents/made">SI 2026/336</a>, in force 1 April 2026, which replaced the revoked 2021 regulations):</p>
<table>
<thead>
<tr><th>Mandated from</th><th>Who</th><th>Qualifying income (gross)</th><th>Tested against</th></tr>
</thead>
<tbody>
<tr><td>6 April 2026</td><td>Sole traders and landlords</td><td>Over GBP 50,000</td><td>2024/25 Self Assessment return</td></tr>
<tr><td>6 April 2027</td><td>Sole traders and landlords</td><td>Over GBP 30,000</td><td>2025/26 return</td></tr>
<tr><td>6 April 2028</td><td>Sole traders and landlords</td><td>Over GBP 20,000</td><td>2026/27 return</td></tr>
</tbody>
</table>
<p>Because the test is on gross, a landlord taking GBP 52,000 in rent with GBP 40,000 of mortgage interest, repairs and agent fees (a GBP 12,000 net profit) is <strong>in scope</strong> from April 2026. Net-low, gross-high landlords are the largest group HMRC's outreach targets, and they are the ones most likely to assume, wrongly, that they are below the line.</p>
<p>A few categories sit outside this:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Limited companies are not in MTD for Income Tax at all.</strong> They report under separate Corporation Tax rules. If you are weighing up incorporation, our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/buy-to-let-limited-company-complete-guide-uk">complete guide to buy-to-let limited companies</a> sets out the trade-offs.</li>
<li><strong>General partnerships and LLPs are deferred.</strong> They are not mandated from April 2026; HMRC has pushed partnerships to a later date that has not been confirmed. Partners who also hold property or a sole trade in their own name are still in MTD through that separate income.</li>
<li><strong>Trustees are outside MTD for Income Tax;</strong> trust property income continues via the trust return.</li>
<li><strong>Non-resident individuals are in scope</strong> where the threshold is met. More on that below.</li>
</ul>
<p>Joint owners are <em>not</em> a partnership. Two people who co-own a rental are taxed as individuals on their shares, and each tests the threshold against their own share of the gross rent. We cover how they sign up separately further down.</p>

<h2>Before you sign up: the three things to have ready</h2>
<p>The sign-up screen assumes you have already done the groundwork. Get these in order first and the actual sign-up takes minutes.</p>
<p><strong>1. Confirm you are over the threshold, on gross.</strong> Add up your gross rents across every property (plus any self-employment turnover) before deducting anything. If you report net of agent commission, gross it back up first, because reporting net understates your qualifying income and can make you think you are out of scope when you are in. For the detail on what counts, see our note on the <a href="/blog/making-tax-digital-mtd/mtd-itsa-qualifying-income-test-gross-vs-net">qualifying-income test (gross versus net)</a>.</p>
<p><strong>2. Be registered for Self Assessment with a UTR.</strong> You sign up for MTD using your Self Assessment Government Gateway account, so you have to be in Self Assessment first. Most established landlords already are. If you have been letting under the GBP 1,000 property allowance and have never filed, register for Self Assessment for UK property income and wait for your Unique Taxpayer Reference (UTR) to arrive before you go further. This is usually the longest part of the timeline for a brand-new landlord, so start it early.</p>
<p><strong>3. Have HMRC-recognised compatible software in place.</strong> This is the step the old version of this page underplayed. MTD for Income Tax runs on software, so sort it out <em>before</em> sign-up, not after. You can use a full accounting package or a spreadsheet linked to recognised bridging software; both are fine if they are on HMRC's list. HMRC keeps the current list at its <a href="https://www.gov.uk/guidance/find-software-thats-compatible-with-making-tax-digital-for-income-tax">find-compatible-software</a> page. We deliberately do not name products here, because the right choice depends on your portfolio; our <a href="/blog/making-tax-digital-mtd/mtd-software-landlords-free-vs-paid-options-compared">free versus paid MTD software comparison for landlords</a> walks through the choice, from free tiers up to fuller paid packages.</p>

<h2>Your Government Gateway login and the identity check</h2>
<p>You sign up with the <strong>same Government Gateway user ID and password you use for Self Assessment</strong>. There is no separate MTD login to create. If you already file online, you have everything you need.</p>
<p>If you have lost your Government Gateway details, use HMRC's recovery service to reset them rather than creating a brand-new account. Duplicate accounts are one of the most common causes of sign-up and filing problems later, because your tax records sit under the original credentials.</p>
<p>When you sign up, HMRC may ask you to <strong>confirm your identity</strong>. The current route is the GOV.UK ID Check app, which matches a photo of your face to a passport or driving licence, or a set of security questions about information HMRC already holds (such as figures from a recent payslip, P60 or passport). This is a quick in-app or on-screen check. It is not the old "wait for an activation code in the post" routine that some guides still describe.</p>

<h2>How to sign up for MTD for Income Tax: the actual steps</h2>
<p>Here is the real flow, taken from HMRC's live <a href="https://www.gov.uk/guidance/sign-up-your-business-for-making-tax-digital-for-income-tax">sign-up guidance</a>. There is a single journey behind one <strong>Sign up now</strong> button.</p>
<ul>
<li>Go to the gov.uk "sign up your business for Making Tax Digital for Income Tax" page and select <strong>Sign up now</strong>.</li>
<li>Sign in with your existing Self Assessment Government Gateway user ID and password.</li>
<li>Confirm your identity if prompted (ID Check app or security questions).</li>
<li>Work through the journey. HMRC shows you the income sources it already holds for you. <strong>Check each one and add any that are missing.</strong> If you have more than one property business or a sole trade alongside your lettings, each is signed up individually.</li>
<li>Confirm, and you are signed up.</li>
</ul>
<p>That is it. There is <strong>no</strong> "Business Tax Account, then Manage your Income Tax, then Making Tax Digital, then Sign up" menu chain (a path that simply does not exist in the live service), and there is <strong>no</strong> postal confirmation you have to wait on. Sign-up is self-service and effectively immediate. The one practical caveat is planned HMRC maintenance windows, when the service is briefly unavailable, so do not leave it to the very last day before a deadline.</p>

<h2>Connect your software to HMRC and start filing</h2>
<p>Once you have signed up, the final setup step is to authorise your software to talk to HMRC over the MTD link. You do this from inside your chosen software, signing in with your Government Gateway credentials to grant access. After that the software can pull and push your quarterly figures through HMRC's MTD interface.</p>
<p>If the connection fails, the cause is almost always one of two things: you have entered the wrong Government Gateway credentials, or your sign-up has not finished confirming. Re-check both before contacting your software provider. For what filing actually looks like across the year, see our <a href="/blog/making-tax-digital-mtd/mtd-quarterly-reporting-landlords-step-by-step-guide">step-by-step guide to MTD quarterly reporting for landlords</a>.</p>

<h2>Signing up as a joint owner or through an accountant</h2>
<p><strong>Joint owners.</strong> Each owner signs up in their own name; there is no joint sign-up. Each tests the threshold against <em>their share</em> of the gross rent (the default 50/50 for spouses absent a Form 17 election, or the actual beneficial shares for other co-owners). That means one co-owner can be mandated while the other is still below the line. Each owner needs their own Government Gateway access and their own software, and each files their own quarterly updates on their share. For the running mechanics, see our page on <a href="/blog/making-tax-digital-mtd/mtd-itsa-joint-property-owners-quarterly-filing-mechanics-each-spouse">joint-owner quarterly filing, spouse by spouse</a>.</p>
<p><strong>Through an accountant.</strong> If your accountant handles this, they sign you up using an <strong>Agent Services Account (ASA)</strong>, which is the route HMRC requires for MTD for Income Tax. The old 64-8 authorisation you may have in place for Self Assessment does not extend to MTD. Your accountant raises an authorisation request from their ASA, you receive an email link, and you approve it by signing into Government Gateway with your own credentials. After that they can sign you up and file on your behalf. Two points catch people out: each joint owner has to authorise separately (there is no spouse-implies-spouse rule), and ASA authorisations do not transfer if you switch firm, so a new accountant has to request fresh authorisation. Our <a href="/blog/making-tax-digital-mtd/mtd-itsa-agent-services-account-asa-authorisation-walkthrough">ASA authorisation walkthrough</a> covers the landlord side in full.</p>

<h2>Non-resident landlords</h2>
<p>If you live abroad but let UK property, you sign up through the same flow once your qualifying income is over the threshold. The <strong>Non-Resident Landlord scheme runs alongside MTD, not instead of it.</strong> If a UK letting agent (or a tenant paying you directly above the limit) handles your rent, basic-rate tax is withheld at source unless you hold NRL approval, but you still file the MTD quarterly updates and the year-end declaration yourself when you are in scope.</p>
<p>The practical wrinkle is software. Not every package handles the non-resident and foreign-element fields cleanly, so check that your chosen software supports non-resident reporting before you rely on it. Our note on <a href="/blog/making-tax-digital-mtd/mtd-itsa-foreign-property-income-quarterly-reporting-rules">foreign property income inside MTD</a> covers the reporting detail and the foreign-tax-credit timing.</p>

<h2>What happens if you do not sign up or file on time</h2>
<p>Missing the obligation is not free, and the rules changed for MTD filers, so the figures you may have seen for ordinary Self Assessment no longer apply.</p>
<p><strong>Late submissions are points-based.</strong> Under the regime in <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2021/26/schedule/24">Finance Act 2021, Schedule 24</a>, each missed quarterly update earns one point. Once you reach the four-point threshold for quarterly obligations, a <strong>GBP 200 penalty</strong> applies for each further failure while you remain at the threshold. A single late update on its own does not trigger an automatic GBP 200; that headline is wrong. Points reset only after a clean run, broadly twelve months of compliance with all submissions due in the preceding 24 months also made.</p>
<p><strong>Late payment of tax is charged on an accelerated schedule</strong> for MTD for Income Tax: 3% of the unpaid tax once it is 15 days late, a further 3% at 30 days, then 10% per year from day 31 (the timetable confirmed at Spring Statement 2025). That replaces the older 2%/2%/4% schedule on the 31/46/91-day triggers, which still applies to VAT and to non-MTD income tax. For the worked detail, see our page on <a href="/blog/making-tax-digital-mtd/mtd-itsa-late-submission-points-late-payment-15-30-31-worked">MTD late-submission points and the 15/30/31-day late-payment penalties</a>, and on the broader consequences our guide to <a href="/blog/making-tax-digital-mtd/mtd-penalties-landlords-miss-deadline">MTD penalties when landlords miss a deadline</a>.</p>

<h2>After sign-up: your quarterly cycle in brief</h2>
<p>Sign-up is the start, not the finish. From the next tax year onward you keep digital records and file four quarterly updates plus a year-end wrap-up. On the default UK tax-year quarters the deadlines are 7 August, 7 November, 7 February and 7 May, followed by your final declaration by 31 January after the tax year ends. The quarterly updates are just running totals of income and expenses, not tax calculations; HMRC works out the tax at the final declaration. We keep the full mechanics on the <a href="/blog/making-tax-digital-mtd/mtd-quarterly-reporting-landlords-step-by-step-guide">quarterly reporting guide</a> rather than duplicating them here.</p>

<h2>Common sign-up problems and how to fix them</h2>
<p><strong>Government Gateway access.</strong> If you cannot get in, recover the existing account rather than creating a new one. A duplicate account splits your records and causes filing failures later.</p>
<p><strong>UTR delays.</strong> If you registered for Self Assessment and the UTR has not arrived within about 15 working days, chase HMRC. You sign up for MTD using that account, so a missing UTR holds up everything.</p>
<p><strong>Business start date.</strong> When asked when your property business began, use the date you <em>first</em> started letting any property, even if that was years ago and the portfolio has since changed.</p>
<p><strong>Software will not connect.</strong> Check you are using the correct Government Gateway credentials and that your sign-up has finished confirming. Those two account for most connection failures.</p>
