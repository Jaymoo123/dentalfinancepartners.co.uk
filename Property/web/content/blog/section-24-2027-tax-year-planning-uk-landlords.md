<p>The 2027/28 tax year brings continued <strong>Section 24 restrictions</strong> for UK landlords, but with a significant twist. From April 2027, separate property income tax rates will fundamentally change how rental profits are taxed. Understanding these changes is crucial for <strong>section 24 2027 tax year planning</strong>.</p>

<p>This guide examines what stays the same, what changes dramatically, and how landlords can prepare their portfolios for the new tax landscape.</p>

<h2>Section 24 Restrictions and the New Property Tax Rates</h2>

<p>The core Section 24 rules remain unchanged in the 2027/28 tax year. Mortgage interest relief is still capped at the basic rate tax credit of 20%, regardless of your marginal tax rate. This restriction applies to individual landlords only. <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/buy-to-let-limited-company-complete-guide-uk">Limited companies remain unaffected by Section 24</a> and can still deduct mortgage interest as a business expense.</p>

<h3>What Section 24 Still Covers</h3>
<ul>
<li>Mortgage interest payments on buy-to-let properties</li>
<li>Interest on loans for property improvements or purchases</li>
<li>Arrangement fees and early repayment charges</li>
<li>Interest on bridging loans for property transactions</li>
</ul>

<h3>The Big Change: Separate Property Tax Rates from April 2027</h3>
<p>The most significant development for <strong>property tax 2027/28</strong> planning is the introduction of separate tax rates specifically for property income. From 6 April 2027, rental profits will be taxed at:</p>
<ul>
<li>22% basic rate (instead of 20%)</li>
<li>42% higher rate (instead of 40%)</li>
<li>47% additional rate (instead of 45%)</li>
</ul>
<p>This means property income faces a 2-percentage-point premium over other income types. The change affects all rental profits after allowable expenses, including the restricted mortgage interest relief under Section 24.</p>

<h3>Combined Impact: Section 24 Plus Higher Rates</h3>
<p>Consider a higher-rate taxpayer earning £60,000 employment income plus £30,000 rental profit with £15,000 mortgage interest:</p>
<p><strong>Under current rules (2026/27):</strong><br>
Rental profit: £30,000<br>
Less mortgage interest deduction: £0 (Section 24 restriction)<br>
Tax on rental profit: £30,000 × 40% = £12,000<br>
Tax credit for interest: £15,000 × 20% = £3,000<br>
Net tax: £12,000 - £3,000 = £9,000</p>
<p><strong>From April 2027:</strong><br>
Rental profit: £30,000<br>
Less mortgage interest deduction: £0 (Section 24 continues)<br>
Tax on rental profit: £30,000 × 42% = £12,600<br>
Tax credit for interest: £15,000 × 20% = £3,000<br>
Net tax: £12,600 - £3,000 = £9,600</p>
<p>The additional tax burden is £600 annually in this example, purely from the higher property tax rates.</p>

<h2>Making Tax Digital (MTD) Requirements</h2>
<p>Making Tax Digital (MTD) for Income Tax becomes mandatory from April 2026 for landlords with gross property income over £10,000. By 2027/28, this will be the established system for property tax reporting.</p>
<p>Key MTD requirements continuing into 2027/28:</p>
<ul>
<li>Quarterly digital record-keeping using approved software</li>
<li>Quarterly income and expense submissions to HMRC</li>
<li>Annual tax return submission through digital channels</li>
<li>Penalties for late submissions or incorrect data</li>
</ul>
<p><a href="/blog/making-tax-digital-mtd/making-tax-digital-landlords-april-2026-deadline">MTD compliance will be essential</a> for managing the new property tax rates effectively and avoiding penalties.</p>

<h3>Quarterly Tax Planning with MTD</h3>
<p>With MTD quarterly reporting requirements, tax planning becomes an ongoing process rather than an annual exercise. Regular reviews allow for:</p>
<ul>
<li>Real-time monitoring of property income against tax bands</li>
<li>Timing of repairs and improvement expenditure</li>
<li>Management of other income sources to optimize overall tax position</li>
<li>Early identification of incorporation trigger points</li>
</ul>

<h2>Strategic Planning and Incorporation</h2>
<p>The introduction of separate property tax rates may tip the balance towards incorporation for many landlords. Limited companies remain unaffected by Section 24 and won't face the higher property tax rates.</p>
<p>Corporation tax rates for 2027/28 are expected to remain at 19% for profits up to £250,000 and 25% above that threshold. This creates a significant rate differential compared to the new 42% higher rate on individual property income.</p>
<p>However, <a href="/incorporation">incorporation planning</a> must consider the capital gains tax implications of transferring properties and the ongoing compliance costs.</p>

<h3>Portfolio Restructuring Options</h3>
<p>Some landlords may consider restructuring their portfolios before April 2027. Options include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Transferring properties to lower-rate taxpayer spouses</li>
<li>Realizing capital gains before higher rates apply to property income</li>
<li>Converting to commercial property investments (unaffected by Section 24)</li>
<li>Exploring furnished holiday lettings alternatives post-FHL abolition</li>
</ul>

<h2>Expense Optimization and Record-Keeping</h2>
<p>With higher tax rates on property income, maximizing allowable deductions becomes even more valuable. <a href="/blog/section-24-and-tax-relief/landlord-tax-deductions-uk-2026-complete-list">Review all possible expense categories</a> including:</p>
<ul>
<li>Repairs and maintenance costs</li>
<li>Professional fees and property management charges</li>
<li>Insurance premiums and safety certificates</li>
<li>Marketing and void period costs</li>
<li>Capital allowances on furnished lettings</li>
</ul>
<p>Effective record-keeping becomes critical under the new system. Maintain detailed records of:</p>
<ul>
<li>All rental income by property and date received</li>
<li>Mortgage interest payments and loan balances</li>
<li>Expenses by category for MTD reporting</li>
<li>Capital improvements for future CGT calculations</li>
<li>Time spent on property activities (relevant for trading vs investment distinction)</li>
</ul>
<p>Digital record-keeping through MTD-compatible software will be mandatory and should integrate seamlessly with tax calculations under the new rates.</p>

<h2>Cash Flow and Tax Planning</h2>
<p>The combination of Section 24 restrictions and higher property tax rates will impact cash flow planning significantly. Landlords should model their 2027/28 tax liability early to avoid payment on account shortfalls.</p>
<p>Consider setting aside additional reserves for:</p>
<ul>
<li>Higher income tax on rental profits</li>
<li>Potential payments on account increases</li>
<li>Professional advice costs for restructuring decisions</li>
<li>MTD software and compliance expenses</li>
</ul>
