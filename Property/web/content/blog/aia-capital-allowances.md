<p>If you own rental property through a limited company or as a sole trader, you can claim tax relief on the cost of certain assets you buy for your business. One of the most valuable reliefs available is the Annual Investment Allowance (AIA). AIA capital allowances let you deduct the full cost of qualifying plant and machinery from your profits before tax, up to an annual limit of £1 million <sup><a href="#ref-1" id="cite-1">[1]</a></sup>.</p>

<p>This guide explains how AIA capital allowances work for UK landlords, what qualifies, what doesn't, and how to make a claim. Whether you are a portfolio landlord or just starting out, understanding this relief can reduce your tax bill significantly.</p>

<h2>What Are AIA Capital Allowances?</h2>
<p>Capital allowances are a type of tax relief that lets you deduct some or all of the value of an item from your profits before you pay tax <sup><a href="#ref-2" id="cite-2">[2]</a></sup>. The Annual Investment Allowance (AIA) is effectively a 100% first-year allowance for business expenditure on qualifying plant or machinery <sup><a href="#ref-3" id="cite-3">[3]</a></sup>. This means you can write off the entire cost in the year you buy the asset, rather than spreading the relief over several years.</p>

<p>The current AIA limit is £1 million. This has been made permanent following the Growth Plan 2022, which confirmed the temporary £1 million level would not revert to £200,000 after 31 March 2023 <sup><a href="#ref-4" id="cite-4">[4]</a></sup>. For the great majority of UK businesses, this limit significantly exceeds their annual expenditure on plant and machinery, meaning they can claim full tax relief up front <sup><a href="#ref-4" id="cite-4">[4]</a></sup>.</p>

<h2>Who Can Claim AIA Capital Allowances?</h2>
<p>AIA is available to sole traders, partnerships, and limited companies that carry on a qualifying business activity <sup><a href="#ref-1" id="cite-1">[1]</a></sup>. For landlords, this typically means you can claim if you let property as a business, rather than as a passive investment. If you own property through a limited company, you are almost certainly eligible. If you are a sole trader or partnership, you can claim provided you are not using the cash basis for your rental income.</p>

<p>If you use the cash basis, you can only claim capital allowances on business cars <sup><a href="#ref-2" id="cite-2">[2]</a></sup>. Most landlords using the accruals basis can claim AIA on a wide range of assets.</p>

<h2>What Qualifies for AIA Capital Allowances?</h2>
<p>Qualifying expenditure includes most plant and machinery used in your property business. This covers items such as:</p>
<ul>
<li>Fixtures and fittings in rental properties (e.g., kitchens, bathrooms, heating systems)</li>
<li>Furniture and white goods (e.g., sofas, beds, fridges, washing machines)</li>
<li>Office equipment used to manage your portfolio (e.g., computers, printers, desks)</li>
<li>Tools and equipment for maintenance (e.g., ladders, power tools)</li>
<li>Motorcycles, lorries, vans, and trucks used for your business <sup><a href="#ref-3" id="cite-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Importantly, business cars do not qualify for AIA. However, motorcycles, vans, and trucks are not considered cars and can be included <sup><a href="#ref-3" id="cite-3">[3]</a></sup>.</p>

<h2>What Does Not Qualify for AIA?</h2>
<p>You cannot claim AIA on:</p>
<ul>
<li>Assets not used immediately in your trade or property business</li>
<li>Business cars</li>
<li>Assets bought in the chargeable period in which the qualifying activity is permanently discontinued</li>
<li>Transactions with a connected person <sup><a href="#ref-3" id="cite-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
<li>Buildings and structures (though integral features may qualify)</li>
<li>Land and parts of buildings</li>
</ul>
<p>If you are unsure whether a specific asset qualifies, speak to a specialist property accountant. Our team at <a href="/services">Property Tax Partners</a> can help you identify eligible expenditure.</p>

<h2>How Much Is the AIA Limit?</h2>
<p>The current AIA limit is £1 million. This has been the case since 1 January 2019 for sole traders, partnerships, and limited companies <sup><a href="#ref-1" id="cite-1">[1]</a></sup>. The limit was temporarily increased from £200,000 to £1 million from 1 January 2019 to 31 March 2023, and then made permanent <sup><a href="#ref-4" id="cite-4">[4]</a></sup>.</p>

<p>If your accounting period is shorter than 12 months, the AIA limit is proportionally reduced. For example, if your accounting period is 9 months, the AIA will be 9/12 x £1,000,000 = £750,000 <sup><a href="#ref-1" id="cite-1">[1]</a></sup>.</p>

<h2>When Can You Claim AIA?</h2>
<p>You can only claim AIA in the period you bought the item. The date you bought it is when you signed the contract, if payment is due within less than 4 months <sup><a href="#ref-1" id="cite-1">[1]</a></sup>. This is important for year-end planning, if you sign a contract just before your year end but pay later, you may still be able to claim in the current period.</p>

<p>You can claim first year allowances in addition to AIA; they do not count towards your AIA limit <sup><a href="#ref-3" id="cite-3">[3]</a></sup>. However, the super-deduction and 50% special rate first-year allowance only applied to assets bought from 1 April 2021 up to and including 31 March 2023 <sup><a href="#ref-2" id="cite-2">[2]</a></sup>. From 1 April 2023, full expensing and 50% first-year allowance are available for qualifying plant and machinery investments <sup><a href="#ref-2" id="cite-2">[2]</a></sup>.</p>

<h2>How to Claim AIA Capital Allowances</h2>
<p>You claim AIA through your tax return. For sole traders and partnerships, this is done on the self-assessment tax return. For limited companies, it is claimed on the company tax return (CT600). You need to list the qualifying expenditure and calculate the AIA deduction.</p>

<p>If you are a landlord with a portfolio, it is worth keeping detailed records of all capital purchases. This includes invoices, contracts, and dates of acquisition. A <a href="/blog/property-accountant-services/what-does-a-property-accountant-do">property accountant</a> can ensure your claims are accurate and maximise your relief.</p>

<h2>AIA for Partnerships: Special Rules</h2>
<p>HMRC has clarified that for partnerships, the AIA is available to each individual partner, not the partnership as a whole <sup><a href="#ref-5" id="cite-5">[5]</a></sup>. This means each partner can claim up to £1 million of qualifying expenditure, subject to their share of the partnership's assets. This can be a significant advantage for property partnerships with multiple partners.</p>

<p>However, the rules are complex, and you should seek professional advice to ensure you apply them correctly. Our <a href="/contact">team at Property Tax Partners</a> can guide you through partnership capital allowances.</p>

<h2>AIA and Property Developers</h2>
<p>If you are a property developer, you may also qualify for AIA on plant and machinery used in your development business. This could include tools, equipment, and vehicles. However, the distinction between trading income and capital gains is important, if you are developing property as a trade, you can claim AIA on business assets. If you are investing in property for rental income, the rules differ.</p>

<p>For more on how property development is taxed, see our guide on <a href="/blog/landlord-tax-essentials/property-investment-tax-uk-complete-guide-2026">property investment tax</a>.</p>

<h2>Common Mistakes Landlords Make</h2>
<p>One common mistake is claiming AIA on assets that are not used immediately in the business. For example, buying furniture for a property that is not yet let may not qualify until the property is available for rent. Another mistake is failing to keep proper records of when assets were bought and how much they cost.</p>

<p>Landlords also sometimes confuse revenue expenses (which are deductible in full) with capital expenditure (which qualifies for capital allowances). If you replace a boiler, that is likely capital expenditure and qualifies for AIA. If you repair a boiler, that is a revenue expense and is deductible in full without needing to claim AIA.</p>

<p>For a full list of deductible expenses, see our guide on <a href="/blog/section-24-and-tax-relief/landlord-tax-deductions-uk-2026-complete-list">landlord tax deductions</a>.</p>

<h2>Planning for the Future</h2>
<p>The AIA limit has changed frequently. Between 2008 and 2022 there were 7 different AIA limits, with some decreases as well as increases <sup><a href="#ref-4" id="cite-4">[4]</a></sup>. While the current £1 million limit is now permanent, it is always possible that future governments could change it. If you are planning significant capital expenditure, it may be worth bringing it forward while the limit is high.</p>

<p>From April 2027, separate property income tax rates will apply, 22% basic, 42% higher, and 47% additional rate on property income. This makes AIA even more valuable, as the relief will be at these higher rates for many landlords.</p>

<h2>Final Thoughts</h2>
<p>AIA capital allowances are a powerful tax relief for UK landlords. With a £1 million annual limit, most landlords can claim full relief on their qualifying plant and machinery expenditure in the year of purchase. The key is to understand what qualifies, keep good records, and claim correctly.</p>
