faqs:
  - question: "What is a property HoldCo group?"
    answer: "A HoldCo group is a parent company (the HoldCo) sitting above one or more property SPV subsidiaries (each SPV typically holding one or two properties, or a defined geographic portfolio). The HoldCo owns the shares in each SPV; the SPVs own the properties. The parent-subsidiary relationship for tax purposes runs through Companies Act 2006 s.1162 (the standard parent-undertaking test, requiring the HoldCo to hold a majority of voting rights, control the board, or have a dominant influence over the subsidiary). Most property HoldCo groups in the UK are formed with the HoldCo holding 100% of each SPV's ordinary share capital. The group's investment in properties is concentrated in the SPVs; the HoldCo holds the shares and receives dividends from the SPVs."
  - question: "Why do SPV dividends to HoldCo avoid corporation tax?"
    answer: "Under CTA 2009 Part 9A, dividends and other distributions received by a UK company from another company are exempt from corporation tax in the receiving company, provided the distribution falls within one of the statutory exemption categories. s.931A is the gateway provision: a distribution is taxable unless exempt under Chapter 2 (for receiving companies that are small) or Chapter 3 (for receiving companies that are not small). The categories cover most ordinary dividends from UK SPVs to UK HoldCos. The economic effect is that the SPV's after-tax profits flow up to HoldCo without a second layer of corporation tax. A single layer of personal dividend tax then applies when the HoldCo declares a dividend to the individual founder. The net cost of extracting £100,000 of underlying profit from the bottom of a HoldCo group is approximately the same as extracting £100,000 from a single SPV, ignoring the associated-companies squeeze."
  - question: "What is the difference between the Chapter 2 and Chapter 3 dividend exemption?"
    answer: "Chapter 2 applies where the company receiving the distribution is a small company (broadly, a stand-alone company with fewer than 50 employees and turnover or balance-sheet total below approximately £10 million; the precise test runs through CTA 2010 ss.931E to 931H). Chapter 3 applies where the receiving company is not small. The detail differs in the conditions and anti-avoidance overlays, but for a typical UK property HoldCo with UK SPV subsidiaries the effect is the same: the dividend is exempt. HMRC's International Manual at INTM164000 onwards covers the operational guidance. Practitioners often quote the rule as 'inter-company dividends in the UK are exempt' as a working approximation; the precise route to that result runs through whichever chapter applies."
  - question: "What is the associated-companies test in CTA 2010 s.18E?"
    answer: "Two companies are associated companies in an accounting period if one is associated with the other for any part of that period. The base test (in subsection (4)) is that one company is under the control of the other, or both are under the control of the same person or group of persons. For a HoldCo holding 100% of multiple property SPVs, all the SPVs are under the common control of the HoldCo, so all the SPVs are associated companies of each other. The HoldCo is also an associated company of each SPV. The total count of associated companies is therefore the HoldCo plus each SPV; a HoldCo with 9 SPVs has 10 associated companies. The s.18E test runs by the calendar of the accounting period: an SPV acquired or disposed of partway through the period still counts for any part of the period it was in the group."
  - question: "How aggressive is the band-slicing for a 10-SPV group?"
    answer: "Severe. The £50,000 small-profits-rate lower limit and the £250,000 marginal-relief upper limit in CTA 2010 s.18D are divided by the number of associated companies. A 10-SPV group means 10 associated companies, so each SPV's lower limit becomes £5,000 and the upper limit becomes £25,000. An SPV with £15,000 of taxable profit, which would have sat comfortably in the 19% small-profits-rate band at standalone level, is now in the marginal-relief band at 26.5% effective rate. An SPV with £30,000 of profit, again easily in the small-profits-rate band at standalone level, is over the sliced upper limit and into the 25% main rate. The arithmetic effect on a 10-SPV group is that almost no SPV in the group can access the small-profits-rate band, and most profits are taxed at the 25% main rate. The HoldCo group structure is therefore not unambiguously efficient on the corporation-tax side; the multi-company overlay can move profits into a higher CT bracket."
  - question: "Can I use alphabet shares at HoldCo level for income shifting?"
    answer: "Yes, with the standard settlements-legislation discipline. At HoldCo level, alphabet shares (A class, B class, C class) with differential dividend rights allow the founder to split dividends across family shareholders (spouse on B, adult child on C, and so on). The settlements legislation in ITTOIA 2005 s.624 catches arrangements where the income-generating asset is gifted but the income is enjoyed by the donor; the Jones v Garnett (Arctic Systems) [2007] UKHL 35 carve-out preserves outright spouse share-ownership arrangements with full beneficial rights. Minor children remain attributed back to the settlor under ITTOIA 2005 s.629. The advantage of alphabet shares at HoldCo level over single-SPV level is that the alphabet structure applies across the whole group's dividend stream: a single declaration of dividend on the B class delivers a share of all the SPVs' combined post-CT profits to the spouse, rather than requiring per-SPV declarations. See our <a href=\"/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/alphabet-shares-property-spv-dividend-splitting-spouse-children\">alphabet shares page</a> for the full mechanic at single-SPV level; the same discipline applies at HoldCo level."
  - question: "Are HoldCos always close investment-holding companies?"
    answer: "No. A close company is a close investment-holding company under CTA 2010 s.18N unless it exists wholly or mainly for one or more of the permitted purposes in subsection (2). Subsection (2)(c) covers holding shares in qualifying companies, where qualifying companies are defined in s.18N(6) as those existing wholly or mainly for trading or commercial land-letting purposes. The standard route for a property HoldCo to escape CIHC status is that its SPVs are land-letting businesses (residential or commercial let to unconnected third parties), and the HoldCo therefore exists for the permitted purpose of holding shares in qualifying land-letting companies. The escape works for most BTL HoldCo groups. Where the SPVs let predominantly to connected parties (the founder's family, related companies) or where the activity has shifted to passive cash holding, the HoldCo can fall into CIHC status."
  - question: "What does CIHC status do at HoldCo level if it bites?"
    answer: "The HoldCo loses access to the small-profits rate and the marginal-relief band. The 25% main rate applies regardless of the HoldCo's profit level. Personally-extracted dividend rates are not affected (the personal-side rates apply by reference to the founder's other income, not to the company's CIHC status). The practical exposure at HoldCo level is usually small because most HoldCo profits are dividends from SPVs (CT-exempt under Part 9A) rather than direct trading or rental profits. A pure-conduit HoldCo with no operating profit has nothing to charge CT on, so the CIHC main-rate denial has no bite. The CIHC trap is more serious for HoldCos that have any direct operating activity (own a property, manage on behalf of group, hold cash earning interest)."
  - question: "How does the AIA allocation work across the group?"
    answer: "The £1 million Annual Investment Allowance is shared across associated companies under CAA 2001 s.51E and the related-companies test in s.51G. The shared-AIA mechanism requires the group to nominate how the £1 million is allocated across the SPVs; the default position is that allocation is by group agreement, with the s.51K rules applying if no allocation is made. The shared cap means a 10-SPV group does not each get £1 million of AIA; the total across the group is £1 million. For most property HoldCo groups, AIA usage is light (because residential plant-and-machinery expenditure is mostly excluded from AIA by the dwelling-house restriction in CAA 2001 s.35), so the shared cap rarely bites. The cap does bite for commercial-property HoldCo groups (offices, warehouses, mixed-use) where qualifying integral-features expenditure can be substantial. For the full mechanics of the shared AIA cap, see our <a href=\"/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/aia-1m-cap-property-investors-allocation-strategy-and-association-rules-cta-2010\">AIA across associated SPVs page</a>."
  - question: "What about full expensing on intra-group transfers?"
    answer: "Full expensing under CAA 2001 s.45S applies to companies acquiring new (not used) qualifying plant and machinery, with no upper limit. Intra-group transfers of plant on which full expensing was previously claimed are subject to anti-avoidance carve-outs: the transferee cannot claim full expensing on the transferred asset (because the asset is not new in their hands), and there is a balancing-event-type clawback risk if the asset moves between connected companies in ways HMRC characterises as engineered. The detailed mechanics including the connected-company restrictions in CAA 2001 Schedule A1 are on our <a href=\"/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/full-expensing-50-percent-fya-commercial-property-spvs-mechanics-fa-2023\">full expensing for commercial SPVs page</a>. For a HoldCo group, the practical point is that full expensing relief is asset-by-asset and stays with the SPV that originally acquired the new plant; restructuring the group by transferring assets between SPVs to redistribute relief is unlikely to deliver an additional CT saving."
  - question: "Should I form a HoldCo retrospectively or at the start?"
    answer: "At the start where possible. Retrospectively inserting a HoldCo above existing SPVs involves a share-for-share exchange under TCGA 1992 s.135 (CGT roll-over for the founder's existing SPV shares as consideration for new HoldCo shares), which is mechanically routine but requires careful structuring. The substantial harder issue is SDLT: the SPV's underlying properties do not change hands on a share-for-share, so no SDLT triggers, but if the restructure involves transferring properties between SPVs to align the group on a HoldCo basis, SDLT does apply on each property transfer at the connected-party deemed market value under FA 2003 s.53. For a group founder planning a HoldCo from the start, the SPVs are formed first as subsidiaries of the HoldCo and the properties are acquired directly into the SPVs at standard SDLT rates (no connected-party uplift). The early-formation route avoids the retrospective SDLT cost."
  - question: "What is the SDLT cost of inserting a HoldCo above existing SPVs?"
    answer: "Two cost vectors. First, the share-for-share exchange itself: no SDLT, because shares are not a land transaction. Second, any property transfer that the restructure triggers: SDLT at the residential or non-residential rate on the deemed market value of the property, with the higher rates for additional dwellings potentially applying. The typical pattern that triggers material SDLT is a restructure that consolidates properties across SPVs (for example, moving a property from SPV-1 to SPV-2 so that SPV-1 can be liquidated). Each such transfer is a land transaction; SDLT applies. The defensive structure is to leave the underlying properties in their current SPVs and let the share-for-share work do the restructuring; the SPVs continue to hold the same properties they held before, just under a new HoldCo parent. The SDLT cost is then nil. For the detail on connected-party SDLT mechanics, see our <a href=\"/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/section-162-incorporation-relief-property-landlords\">s.162 incorporation page</a> which covers the comparable mechanic for partnership-to-SPV transfers."
  - question: "How does HoldCo extraction compare with a single-SPV setup?"
    answer: "Three differences. First, the dividend conduit (Part 9A exemption) means cash can move freely from any SPV up to the HoldCo without CT leakage, supporting cross-SPV pooling that a single-SPV cohort cannot replicate. Second, the associated-companies squeeze means individual SPVs in the group lose access to the small-profits rate and the marginal-relief band, raising the effective CT on each SPV's profits. Third, alphabet shares at HoldCo level cover the whole group's dividend stream, supporting more efficient family income-splitting than per-SPV alphabet structures. The net economic comparison depends on the size of the portfolio: a 3-SPV group with small per-SPV profits sees the associated-companies squeeze as a meaningful cost; a 20-SPV group with each SPV well into the marginal-relief band at standalone level sees the squeeze as immaterial because all the profits would have been at the higher rates anyway. For property founders considering whether to form a HoldCo, the maths is portfolio-size-dependent rather than uniformly favourable."
  - question: "What does the decision tree look like for a founder considering a HoldCo?"
    answer: "Four entry points. First, group size: HoldCo only makes structural sense above 3-4 SPVs; below that the associated-companies squeeze cost outweighs the dividend-conduit benefit. Second, exit path: HoldCo is the natural staging structure for a future SSE-eligible share sale of a property development subsidiary (see our SSE for property companies page) and for a multi-SPV MVL on retirement (see our MVL page); founders with a clear exit plan should consider HoldCo earlier. Third, family income-splitting: HoldCo-level alphabet shares are materially more efficient than per-SPV alphabet shares for groups above 2-3 SPVs. Fourth, succession planning: HoldCo is the natural vehicle for a FIC-style growth-share design with founders on preference shares and next generation on growth shares; single-SPV cohorts cannot replicate the structure. Founders failing all four tests should think hard before adding the HoldCo layer."

<p>A 10-SPV property portfolio held under a HoldCo is not ten independent extraction questions, it is one extraction question with three load-bearing multi-company mechanics that change the maths versus a solo SPV. First, the dividend conduit: SPVs pay dividends up to HoldCo without corporation tax leakage under CTA 2009 Part 9A. Second, the associated-companies squeeze: every SPV in the group counts as an associated company under CTA 2010 s.18E, slicing the £50,000 small-profits-rate lower limit and the £250,000 marginal-relief upper limit by the count of associated companies. Third, alphabet shares at HoldCo level allow cross-SPV income shifting across family shareholders in a single declaration.</p>

<p>The HoldCo group structure is not uniformly favourable. The dividend conduit is a clear benefit, but the associated-companies squeeze can move profits into a higher CT bracket that more than offsets the conduit saving. The maths is portfolio-size-dependent; the decision tree for a founder considering whether to form a HoldCo runs through group size, exit path, family income-splitting goals, and succession planning. This page walks each mechanic with verified statute citations, a 10-SPV worked extraction, the CAA capital-allowances cross-references for AIA and full expensing, the CIHC risk at HoldCo level under s.18N, and the practical decision tree.</p>

<h2>The HoldCo group context: when this structure exists and why</h2>

<p>A property HoldCo group has a parent company (the HoldCo) holding 100% of the ordinary share capital of one or more property SPV subsidiaries. The HoldCo's only assets are typically the shares in the SPVs, a working cash balance, and occasionally intra-group loans receivable. Each SPV holds one or more properties (or a defined geographic portfolio), borrows on its own balance sheet, and runs its own rental and operating P&L. The parent-subsidiary relationship is established under <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2006/46/section/1162">Companies Act 2006 s.1162</a>, which sets the parent-undertaking test by reference to majority voting rights, board-control, or dominant influence.</p>

<p>Property founders form HoldCo groups for four common reasons. First, ring-fencing: each SPV holds a defined set of properties with its own lender and its own liabilities, so a covenant breach or insolvency on one SPV does not propagate to the rest. Second, exit optionality: the HoldCo can sell shares in any individual SPV under SSE (where the SPV is a trading company; pure-investment SPVs fail the Pawson test), or wind down individual SPVs through MVL, without affecting the others. Third, family planning: HoldCo-level shareholding structures (alphabet shares, preference / growth share designs for FIC-style transfers) deliver outcomes single-SPV cohorts cannot match. Fourth, lender preference: many BTL portfolio lenders prefer to lend to a single-property SPV structure for security clarity, and a HoldCo above the SPVs is the natural shareholder vehicle.</p>

<h2>Mechanic 1: the dividend conduit (CTA 2009 Part 9A)</h2>

<p><a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2009/4/section/931A">CTA 2009 s.931A</a> is the gateway provision: "The charge to corporation tax on income applies to any dividend or other distribution of a company, but only if the distribution is not exempt." The exemption regime sits in Part 9A of CTA 2009 across two chapters. Chapter 2 covers distributions received by small companies (the qualifying tests at ss.931E to 931H), Chapter 3 covers distributions received by non-small companies. For a typical property HoldCo group with UK SPV subsidiaries paying ordinary dividends, the exemption applies and the dividend is received tax-free by HoldCo.</p>

<p>The economic effect is that SPV after-tax profits flow up to HoldCo without a second layer of corporation tax. A £150,000 dividend declared by SPV-3 out of post-CT profits is received by HoldCo as £150,000 of cash, with no CT charge on HoldCo. The £150,000 sits in HoldCo's cash balance until the HoldCo declares a dividend to the founder personally. At that point the standard personal dividend tax applies (£500 allowance, 10.75% / 35.75% / 39.35% by income band). The net layer of tax on extracting £150,000 from the bottom of a HoldCo group through to the founder is therefore: SPV-level CT on the underlying profit (depending on which bracket), plus personal dividend tax on the eventual founder dividend. There is no HoldCo-level layer.</p>

<p>The exemption does not extend to all distributions. Distributions of capital from an SPV (for example, a capital reduction or share buyback for cancellation) are outside Part 9A. Distributions from a non-UK subsidiary may need to satisfy additional Chapter 3 conditions (the chapter has anti-avoidance overlays for distributions from non-treaty jurisdictions). The standard pattern (UK SPV dividends to UK HoldCo) is exempt; the unusual patterns need a specialist look. HMRC's published guidance at <a href="https://www.gov.uk/hmrc-internal-manuals/international-manual/intm164000">INTM164000</a> covers the operational detail.</p>

<h2>Mechanic 2: the associated-companies squeeze (CTA 2010 s.18E)</h2>

<p><a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2010/4/section/18E">CTA 2010 s.18E</a> defines associated companies for the purposes of the small-profits-rate / marginal-relief band-slicing in s.18D. The verbatim test: "For the purposes of section 18D, a company is another company's associated company in an accounting period if it is an associated company for any part of the accounting period." The underlying associated-company test in subsection (4) covers companies under common control. For a HoldCo holding 100% of multiple SPVs, all the SPVs are under common control (HoldCo's control), so each SPV is associated with each other SPV and with HoldCo. A HoldCo with 9 SPVs therefore has 10 associated companies (HoldCo + 9 SPVs).</p>

<p>The consequence for the small-profits-rate band: the £50,000 lower limit and the £250,000 upper limit in s.18D are divided by the number of associated companies. The arithmetic, working from a 10-SPV group (10 associated companies, each SPV faces a divisor of 10), is:</p>

<ul>
<li>Per-SPV small-profits-rate lower limit: £50,000 ÷ 10 = £5,000.</li>
<li>Per-SPV marginal-relief upper limit: £250,000 ÷ 10 = £25,000.</li>
<li>Per-SPV main-rate threshold: above £25,000 of profit.</li>
</ul>

<p>An SPV in the group with £30,000 of profit is now over its sliced £25,000 upper limit and pays CT at the 25% main rate on the whole £30,000. At standalone level (no associated companies), the same SPV would have sat comfortably in the small-profits-rate band at 19% on all £30,000. The CT cost difference per SPV is £30,000 × (25% - 19%) = £1,800. Across 10 SPVs in the same position the annual CT cost of the squeeze is £18,000. The 10-SPV worked scenario later in this page sets out the full implications.</p>

<p>The associated-companies count includes any SPV in the group for any part of the accounting period. An SPV acquired in the third quarter of the period still counts as an associated company for the full period (because it was associated for part of the period). An SPV disposed of in the first quarter still counts for the full period of disposal. The s.18E test is mechanical: presence at any time means inclusion in the count.</p>

<p>Dormant SPVs are generally excluded from the count by s.18E(5) (verify exact wording at write time), provided they meet the dormancy test. Property SPVs that have no rental activity in a period (between exchange and completion, between tenants for short windows that span no rent receipts) are not necessarily dormant for s.18E purposes; HMRC reads the dormancy test strictly. Most property SPVs in a HoldCo group are active in the relevant sense and therefore counted.</p>

<h2>Mechanic 3: alphabet shares at HoldCo level for cross-SPV income shifting</h2>

<p>At HoldCo level, alphabet share structures (A class held by founder, B class held by spouse, C class held by adult child, and so on) deliver cross-SPV income shifting in a single dividend declaration. The mechanic is the same as the single-SPV alphabet structure covered on our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/alphabet-shares-property-spv-dividend-splitting-spouse-children">alphabet shares page</a>, but operates across the whole group's dividend stream rather than per-SPV.</p>

<p>The advantages over per-SPV alphabet structures are administrative and arithmetic. Administratively, a single board minute at HoldCo level declares a dividend on (say) the B class for £25,000; the spouse receives £25,000 of dividend income from HoldCo, with the underlying funding coming from any combination of SPV up-dividends. There is no per-SPV declaration discipline. Arithmetically, the alphabet structure can rebalance income flows year by year as portfolio profitability shifts across SPVs; a poor year for SPV-1 and a strong year for SPV-3 are smoothed at HoldCo level into the household dividend mix.</p>

<p>The settlements legislation discipline is unchanged. <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2005/5/section/624">ITTOIA 2005 s.624</a> catches arrangements where the income-generating asset is gifted but the income is enjoyed by the donor. The <em>Jones v Garnett</em> (Arctic Systems) [2007] UKHL 35 carve-out preserves outright spouse share-ownership arrangements with full beneficial rights. Minor children remain attributed back to the settlor under s.629 unless the structure runs through a properly-constituted formal trust. The HoldCo-level alphabet structure has to be drafted with the same care as a single-SPV alphabet: full beneficial transfer to the spouse, no settlement-back arrangement, no condition that the spouse must pay the dividends back. The Arctic Systems pattern works at HoldCo level on the same basis as at single-SPV level.</p>

<h2>AIA shared allowance across the group (CAA 2001 ss.51E to 51K)</h2>

<p>The £1 million Annual Investment Allowance is shared across associated companies under <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2001/2/section/51E">CAA 2001 s.51E</a> (single AIA for related companies) and the related-companies test in s.51G (shared premises or similar activities). For a HoldCo group where all SPVs are associated (per s.18E), the same companies are typically also related for s.51G AIA purposes, and the shared £1 million AIA cap applies across the group as a single pool.</p>

<p>The allocation discipline runs through s.51K: the related companies must specify how the £1 million is allocated between them; in the absence of an agreement, the default allocation rules apply. For most property HoldCo groups, AIA usage is light: residential plant-and-machinery expenditure is mostly excluded from AIA by the dwelling-house restriction in CAA 2001 s.35, so the shared cap rarely bites for a pure-residential portfolio. The cap does bite for commercial-property HoldCo groups (office buildings, warehouses, mixed-use) where qualifying integral-features expenditure on lifts, electrical systems, hot and cold water, heating, air conditioning, and lighting can be substantial.</p>

<p>For the full mechanics of AIA allocation across associated SPVs, the s.51K default allocation rules, and worked examples of how a HoldCo group should structure the allocation in a commercial-portfolio context, see our <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/aia-1m-cap-property-investors-allocation-strategy-and-association-rules-cta-2010">AIA shared cap page</a>. The C4 page in this Wave 6 cluster is the depth treatment; A7 here is the HoldCo-level summary for the extraction-focused reader.</p>

<h2>Full expensing intra-group transfer carve-out (CAA 2001 s.45S + Schedule A1)</h2>

<p>Full expensing under <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2001/2/section/45S">CAA 2001 s.45S</a> (companies acquiring new qualifying plant and machinery from 1 April 2023, permanent under Finance (No. 2) Act 2023) does not apply to used plant. Intra-group transfers of plant on which full expensing was previously claimed by SPV-1 to SPV-2 are subject to the connected-company restrictions in CAA 2001 Schedule A1: SPV-2 acquires the plant in a connected-party transaction at the deemed market value, but cannot claim full expensing on the asset (because it is not new in SPV-2's hands; the new test in s.45S(1) requires the company to have acquired the plant new and unused). The asset enters SPV-2's special-rate pool at the deemed value with WDA at 6% per year going forward.</p>

<p>The further wrinkle is the balancing-event treatment on the transferor's side. SPV-1's disposal of the asset to SPV-2 is a disposal event under CAA 2001 s.61 at the deemed market value; SPV-1's pool is reduced by the disposal value, with balancing-charge or balancing-allowance treatment depending on the pool position. Where the connected-company restrictions in Schedule A1 apply, additional anti-avoidance overlay can deny or recapture full-expensing relief previously claimed in SPV-1.</p>

<p>The practical implication for a HoldCo group: restructuring the group by transferring assets between SPVs to redistribute full-expensing relief is unlikely to deliver an additional CT saving and may trigger clawback. Full expensing is asset-by-asset and stays with the SPV that originally acquired the asset new. For the full mechanics including the worked numerical examples for connected-company restrictions on full-expensing assets, see our <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/full-expensing-50-percent-fya-commercial-property-spvs-mechanics-fa-2023">full expensing for commercial SPVs page</a>. The C5 page in this Wave 6 cluster is the depth treatment for intra-group full-expensing mechanics; A7 here surfaces it as one of the HoldCo group's CAA-side considerations.</p>

<h2>CIHC risk at HoldCo level (CTA 2010 s.18N)</h2>

<p>A close company is a close investment-holding company under <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2010/4/section/18N">CTA 2010 s.18N</a> unless it exists wholly or mainly for one or more of the permitted purposes in subsection (2). The relevant permitted purpose for a HoldCo is at (2)(c): "holding shares in and securities of, or making loans to, one or more companies each of which" is a qualifying company under subsection (6). A qualifying company is defined in (6) as one existing wholly or mainly for trading or commercial land-letting purposes.</p>

<p>For most property HoldCo groups the route to escape CIHC status runs through (2)(c) plus (6). The SPVs are land-letting businesses, the lettings are commercial in the broad sense (residential or commercial properties let to unconnected third parties), the SPVs therefore qualify as "qualifying companies" under (6), and the HoldCo exists for the permitted purpose of holding their shares. The escape works for most BTL HoldCo groups. The exception is where the SPVs let predominantly to connected parties (founder's family, related companies) or where the activity has shifted to passive cash or share investment; in those cases the SPVs may not qualify and the HoldCo can fall into CIHC status.</p>

<p>The bite of CIHC status at HoldCo level is usually small. CIHC denies access to the small-profits rate and the marginal-relief band, so a CIHC HoldCo pays the 25% main rate on all profits regardless of profit level. Most HoldCos in property groups have very little direct operating profit: the dividends received from SPVs are CT-exempt under Part 9A, intra-group interest income is typically modest, and the HoldCo has few direct expenses. A HoldCo with effectively zero taxable profit has nothing to charge CT on, and the CIHC main-rate denial has no practical effect. The CIHC bite is more material for HoldCos that hold cash earning meaningful interest, or that directly own one or more properties, or that take advisory fees from group companies.</p>

<aside><p>HoldCo CIHC status is a check-once-then-monitor exercise.</p><p>Most BTL HoldCo groups escape CIHC by the s.18N(2)(c) qualifying-shares route. The check is worth doing at formation and re-checking if the SPV tenant base shifts toward connected parties or if the HoldCo starts holding material direct activity. Use the form at the bottom of this page if you want a one-off review of your group's CIHC status.</p></aside>

<h2>Worked extraction: a 10-SPV HoldCo group</h2>

<p>HoldCo holds 100% of 10 BTL SPVs. Each SPV holds two flats, generates net rental profit of £18,000 (after mortgage interest, repairs, agent fees), and has no other income. The HoldCo has £50,000 cash on its balance sheet, no operating activity, and no taxable profit of its own. The founder owns 100% of HoldCo's A class shares; spouse owns 100% of B class; both classes have equal rights to dividends and capital.</p>

<p><strong>CT at SPV level.</strong> 10 associated companies (HoldCo + 9 other SPVs from each SPV's perspective; 10 total in the group). Per-SPV small-profits-rate lower limit: £50,000 ÷ 10 = £5,000. Per-SPV marginal-relief upper limit: £250,000 ÷ 10 = £25,000. Each SPV's £18,000 of profit sits in the marginal-relief band (between £5,000 and £25,000). The effective CT rate on £18,000 of profit in this band is approximately 26.5% (the marginal-relief formula peaks at 26.5%; the average rate at this profit level is roughly the same). CT per SPV: approximately £4,770. CT across the 10 SPVs: £47,700.</p>

<p><strong>Post-CT cash flow up to HoldCo.</strong> Each SPV declares a dividend of (£18,000 - £4,770) = £13,230 up to HoldCo. The aggregate dividend received by HoldCo is £132,300. Under CTA 2009 Part 9A (s.931A and the small-company exemption in Chapter 2), the dividends are exempt from CT in HoldCo. HoldCo's cash balance increases by £132,300 to £182,300.</p>

<p><strong>Personal extraction from HoldCo.</strong> Founder and spouse each have £8,000 of other personal income. HoldCo declares a dividend of £50,000 on the A class (founder) and £50,000 on the B class (spouse), totalling £100,000. Each receives £50,000 of dividend income. Founder's total income £8,000 + £50,000 = £58,000. After the £12,570 personal allowance and the £500 dividend allowance, founder pays dividend tax on £44,930: £37,200 within the basic-rate dividend band at 10.75% (£3,999) and £7,730 in the higher-rate band at 35.75% (£2,763). Founder's dividend tax £6,762. Spouse on identical numbers: £6,762. Total household dividend tax £13,524.</p>

<p><strong>Total tax cost.</strong> SPV-level CT £47,700; personal dividend tax £13,524; total £61,224. The household receives £100,000 net of dividend tax. The aggregate tax on £180,000 of underlying SPV profit is therefore £61,224 + £13,524 = approximately £61,224 of CT plus £13,524 of personal tax = £74,748 (net household cash £105,252 against gross £180,000 underlying profit; effective tax rate approximately 41.5%).</p>

<p><strong>Comparison: same portfolio without HoldCo, 10 standalone SPVs.</strong> Without a HoldCo above them, the SPVs would still be associated companies through common ownership by the founder, so the band-slicing under s.18E applies identically. SPV-level CT is unchanged at £47,700. The dividends would be declared directly from each SPV to the founder personally; no HoldCo layer; dividend tax on the same £100,000 of total dividend is the same £13,524. The arithmetic is therefore approximately equal. The HoldCo benefit is not on the tax side in this scenario; it is on the structural side (ring-fencing, exit optionality, alphabet-share efficiency at HoldCo level vs per-SPV).</p>

<p><strong>Comparison: smaller portfolio, 3 SPVs each with £40,000 of profit, no HoldCo.</strong> 3 associated companies. Per-SPV lower limit £50,000 ÷ 3 = £16,667; per-SPV upper limit £250,000 ÷ 3 = £83,333. Each SPV's £40,000 of profit sits in the marginal-relief band (above £16,667). CT effective rate approximately 26.5%; per-SPV CT approximately £10,600; across 3 SPVs £31,800. Compare to 1 SPV with £120,000 of profit (no associated companies, full £50,000 lower limit and £250,000 upper limit): CT on £120,000 in the band would be approximately 26.5% × (£120,000 - £50,000 × 19% / 25%) blended, working out at approximately £24,500. The 3-SPV split costs approximately £7,300 of extra CT compared to a single £120,000-profit SPV. Adding a HoldCo above the 3 SPVs would change nothing on the CT side (still 4 associated companies after HoldCo) but does add the structural benefits.</p>

<h2>Decision-tree quick reference</h2>

<p>The HoldCo group structure makes sense in the property context when at least one of the four entry-point tests is positively passing.</p>

<ol>
<li><strong>Group size.</strong> 4+ SPVs supporting a meaningful aggregate profit; below that the associated-companies squeeze cost outweighs the dividend-conduit and alphabet-share benefits.</li>
<li><strong>Exit path.</strong> Clear plan for SSE-eligible share sale of a future development subsidiary or for multi-SPV MVL on retirement (see our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/mvl-members-voluntary-liquidation-property-company-cgt-vs-income-treatment">MVL page</a>). Forming a HoldCo earlier supports the future exit mechanics.</li>
<li><strong>Family income-splitting.</strong> Alphabet shares at HoldCo level are materially more efficient for groups above 2-3 SPVs than per-SPV alphabet structures. Where the founder has a spouse and one or more adult children who would receive distributions, the HoldCo-level alphabet is the cleaner route.</li>
<li><strong>Succession planning.</strong> The HoldCo is the natural vehicle for a FIC-style growth-share design (founders on preference shares with frozen value, next generation on growth shares with future appreciation). Single-SPV cohorts cannot replicate this structure.</li>
</ol>

<p>Founders failing all four tests should think hard before adding the HoldCo layer; the associated-companies squeeze is a real cost on a small portfolio, the structural benefits do not yet outweigh it, and the formation cost (legal drafting, accountancy setup, share-for-share roll-over filings) is a friction. The right answer for a 2-3 SPV founder with no specific exit plan is usually to stay at the standalone-SPV-per-property level and revisit when the portfolio grows or the exit picture clarifies.</p>
