faqs:
  - question: "What does FA 2003 Schedule 7 group relief actually do?"
    answer: "Schedule 7 of the Finance Act 2003 provides relief from SDLT on intra-group transfers of property between members of the same 75%-controlled corporate group. Where the transfer qualifies, the SDLT on the property transfer is reduced to zero (the standard residential or non-residential rates, including any additional-dwellings surcharge, do not apply). The relief must be claimed in the Land Transaction Return for the transfer; non-claim within 12 months of the effective date can be remedied via an overpayment claim under FA 2003 Schedule 11A within four years, but late claims invite HMRC enquiry. The relief is the most-used SDLT mitigation route for landlord LtdCo portfolio restructuring (consolidating multi-SPV portfolios under a single HoldCo, moving properties between operating subsidiaries, reorganising for finance or refinancing purposes)."
  - question: "What is the 75% group test?"
    answer: "Paragraph 1(3) of Schedule 7 defines a 75% group by reference to three limbs that must all be met. First: ownership of at least 75% of the ordinary share capital (excluding preference shares with fixed-rate dividends under CTA 2010 s.1119). Second: beneficial entitlement to at least 75% of the distributable profits. Third: entitlement to at least 75% of the assets on a winding-up. Indirect ownership through intermediate companies is calculated under CTA 2010 ss.1155 to 1157 using the standard 75% subsidiary multiplication rules. All three limbs must be met by all relevant intermediate companies in the chain. The structural implication is that a parent owning 80% of an intermediate which owns 90% of the target results in a 72% indirect ownership (0.8 multiplied by 0.9), failing the 75% test. Sessions advising on group structures need to map the indirect-ownership chain carefully because mid-chain dilutions break the group-relief eligibility downstream."
  - question: "When does paragraph 3 trigger SDLT claw-back?"
    answer: "Paragraph 3 imposes claw-back where the transferee company ceases to be a member of the same group as the transferor within 3 years of the SDLT effective date. The triggering events include: the transferee being sold to a third party (the typical case); the transferee being sold within the group such that the transferor and transferee are no longer in the same 75% group; the transferee becoming subject to insolvency proceedings in a way that breaks the group-relationship; a reorganisation in which the transferee moves into a different group structure. The claw-back recomputes the SDLT as if the original transfer had not benefited from group relief: the consideration at the original effective date is taxed at the market-value rates that would have applied (typically residential rates plus 5% additional-dwellings surcharge for residential property, or non-residential rates for commercial property). Interest runs from the original effective date; penalties may apply where the claw-back is not declared promptly."
  - question: "Can the claw-back be avoided by partial group restructuring?"
    answer: "Only in narrow cases. Paragraph 3(2) carve-outs include some statutory restructurings (transferee leaving the group due to insolvency outside the control of the parties; transferee leaving the group due to court order under the Companies Act 2006 or insolvency legislation; technical group reorganisations where the underlying economic ownership chain is preserved). The standard commercial third-party sale is NOT carved out. Where the transferee is sold to a third party within 3 years, the claw-back applies regardless of the commercial rationale or the financial difficulty of the transferee. Sessions advising on group restructuring should treat the 3-year period as a hard commitment from the transferee company; any plan to dispose of the transferee within the period needs to factor in the claw-back cost from the outset."
  - question: "What is the para 5 connected-party recovery mechanism?"
    answer: "Paragraph 5 of Schedule 7 provides that where the SDLT on a clawed-back transfer remains unpaid 6 months after assessment, HMRC may recover the unpaid amount from three categories of connected party. First: the vendor company in the original transfer (the transferor). Second: any company that was the parent company of the group at the time of the original transfer. Third: any individual who was a controlling director of the group at the time of the original transfer. The recovery is joint and several across these categories. The structural implication is materially serious: a director who signed the original intra-group transfer in 2024 may, in 2027 when the transferee SPV is sold to a third party and the claw-back is unpaid by that SPV, face personal liability for the clawed-back SDLT regardless of whether they remain involved with the group at the recovery date. The risk is invisible at the time of the original transfer but operationally important; directors approving group-relief restructurings need to understand this exposure."
  - question: "What is the para 2 arrangements-defeating-relief trap?"
    answer: "Paragraph 2 denies relief at the point of original transfer where, at the SDLT effective date, certain arrangements exist. Specifically: arrangements for the transferee to leave the group; arrangements for the transferor or transferee to enter another group; or arrangements for consideration to flow from a non-group party (effectively converting the intra-group transfer into a third-party sale dressed up as intra-group). The denial is at the time of the original transfer, not at any subsequent event. The structural implication is that group-relief restructurings cannot be used as a stepping stone toward a planned third-party sale: where the parties intend the transferee to leave the group as part of the broader transaction, para 2 catches the arrangement and disallows the relief at the outset. HMRC's enquiry pattern looks for board minutes, mandate letters, broker engagements, and external advisory work indicating a sale plan was in place at or near the intra-group transfer date."

  - question: "What does an SPA covenant pack look like for a group-relief restructuring?"
    answer: "The defensive SPA covenant pack used by specialist advisers on group-relief restructurings has four core elements. First: a 3-year continuity covenant prohibiting the transferee from leaving the group within the para 3 claw-back period, with breach triggering a break-fee equal to the claw-back exposure (typically the original SDLT plus an estimated interest amount). Second: a guarantee from the parent company covering the transferee's claw-back exposure during the 3-year period (so if claw-back triggers and the transferee cannot pay, the parent's guarantee covers the amount before HMRC invokes the para 5 statutory recovery from connected parties). Third: a director-side indemnity where individual controlling directors of the group are protected against personal liability under para 5, typically backed by directors-and-officers insurance with an explicit SDLT-claw-back endorsement. Fourth: a notification covenant requiring any change in group structure that may affect the para 3 status to be notified to all signing parties promptly so the financial implications can be addressed. The pack is bespoke for each restructuring; the precise drafting depends on the group structure and the commercial rationale for the original transfer."
  - question: "What if the transferee company is later sold to another company in the same group?"
    answer: "Schedule 7 paragraph 4 addresses the case where the transferee is sold to another company that is itself in the same group as the original transferor and transferee. Where the move preserves the 75%-group connection between the transferor and the company that now controls the transferee, claw-back does NOT apply because the underlying group relationship is preserved. The structural implication is that intra-group reorganisations (transferring shares between holding companies, or restructuring the corporate chain in ways that preserve ultimate ownership) do not trigger claw-back even if they look on paper like the transferee has changed hands. Sessions advising on multi-stage group restructures need to map the corporate chain at each stage to confirm whether each transfer preserves the 75%-group connection or breaks it; the answer drives whether claw-back is triggered at each step."
  - question: "Does the 5% additional-dwellings surcharge apply to clawed-back SDLT?"
    answer: "Yes. Where the original intra-group transfer would have attracted the 5% additional-dwellings surcharge had the relief not been claimed (typical for residential portfolios moving between corporate entities), the claw-back recomputes the SDLT at the rates that would have applied, including the surcharge. The structural implication is that residential portfolios with substantial market value moving between corporate entities face a particularly large claw-back exposure: the 5% surcharge on top of the residential rates (capped at 17% in the top band) means the clawed-back rate can effectively reach 22% on portions of the consideration. For the Mitchell-Group £3.2m residential portfolio: the £435,000 claw-back includes the 5% additional-dwellings surcharge applied across the residential rates table. Sessions advising on residential group-relief restructurings need to factor in the additional-dwellings surcharge exposure in the claw-back risk register."
  - question: "Can the original SDLT relief be preserved if the transferee enters insolvency?"
    answer: "Paragraph 3(2) of Schedule 7 includes carve-outs for insolvency-related events where the transferee's departure from the group is caused by genuine insolvency rather than commercial sale. Specifically, where the transferee enters administration, liquidation, or other formal insolvency proceedings outside the control of the parties, the claw-back is disapplied. The HMRC enquiry pattern on insolvency-based carve-outs looks at whether the insolvency is genuine (involuntary creditor-driven process with proper supervisor appointment) versus engineered (pre-pack arrangement designed to break the group connection while preserving the underlying business under new ownership). Engineered insolvencies invite HMRC challenge under both para 2 (arrangements-defeating-relief) and the general anti-avoidance code at FA 2003 s.75A. Sessions advising on distressed group situations need careful navigation of the insolvency carve-out boundary."
  - question: "How does group relief interact with the para 2 arrangements test?"
    answer: "Paragraph 2 is the entry-level gate for relief; paragraph 3 is the exit-level claw-back. Para 2 denies relief at the original transfer date where arrangements exist for the transferee to leave the group; para 3 claws back relief where the transferee actually leaves within 3 years even without prior arrangements. The two together create a 3-and-a-half-year exposure window: from any pre-transfer planning that touched on the transferee's future disposal (catchable under para 2) through to the 3-year anniversary of the transfer (catchable under para 3). Sessions advising on group restructurings need to document the commercial rationale for the transfer (intra-group consolidation, finance optimisation, refinancing efficiency) and clearly separate this from any subsequent sale planning. Board minutes recording the transfer purpose, the absence of sale-of-transferee planning at the date of transfer, and the rationale for the timing all support the para 2 defence; the absence of such records leaves the relief vulnerable to HMRC enquiry."

    answer: "Yes. Paragraph 5 attaches liability based on the director's role at the time of the original transfer, not at the time of claw-back. A director who signed off on the original transfer in 2024 may face personal liability under para 5 in 2027 even if they have left the group entirely by 2026, sold their shares, and have no remaining involvement with the entities. The recovery follows the historic connection. The practical implication for directors of landlord-LtdCo groups: any director approving a Sch 7 group-relief restructuring carries a 3-year contingent personal liability that survives their subsequent departure from the group. The defensive SPA covenant pack typically includes ongoing director-side indemnities post-departure, plus director-and-officer insurance maintenance for the 3-year period. Sessions advising directors on group restructurings should make this multi-year contingent exposure explicit."

<p>SDLT group relief under FA 2003 Schedule 7 is the workhorse mechanism for intra-group property transfers within a 75%-controlled corporate group. The relief itself is structurally simple: a qualifying intra-group transfer of property is free of SDLT at the point of transfer. The two complications that reshape the risk profile sit at the anti-avoidance gates. Paragraph 3 imposes a three-year claw-back: if the transferee company leaves the group within 36 months of the SDLT effective date, the original relief is withdrawn and the SDLT becomes payable at market value rates. Paragraph 5 then provides wide connected-party recovery powers if the transferee cannot or will not pay, reaching the original transferor, the parent company, and any controlling director of the group. These two gates together create a 3-and-a-half-year compliance window with personal-liability exposure for directors that survives their subsequent departure from the group.</p>

<p>This page is the operational complement to our higher-level <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/sdlt-group-relief-for-corporate-landlord-portfolios">SDLT group relief overview page</a>. The depth focus here is three specific operational risks. First, the para 3 claw-back mechanics: what triggers it, how the recomputation works, what the carve-outs are. Second, the para 5 connected-party recovery: who can be pursued, when the liability attaches, why it survives a director's departure from the group. Third, the defensive SPA covenant pack that Property Tax Partners uses to manage both risks on landlord-LtdCo group-relief restructurings. The reference scenario throughout is the <strong>Mitchell-Group restructuring</strong>, an anonymised composite walking the full mechanic from initial transfer to a hypothetical claw-back 30 months later.</p>

<h2>The relief gate at paragraph 2: arrangements defeat relief from the start</h2>

<p>Before the 3-year claw-back analysis even arises, paragraph 2 imposes an entry-level gate. The relief is denied at the SDLT effective date itself where, at that date, certain arrangements exist. Specifically: arrangements for the transferee to leave the group; arrangements for the transferor or transferee to enter another group; or arrangements for consideration to flow from a non-group party. The denial is contemporaneous with the transfer; no claw-back analysis is needed because the relief never applied.</p>

<p>HMRC's enquiry pattern on para 2 looks at the documentation. Board minutes recording the rationale for the intra-group transfer (consolidation, refinancing efficiency, intra-group reorganisation) without contemporaneous discussion of subsequent disposal support the relief. Mandate letters with brokers, sale-side advisory engagements, or external sale processes running concurrently with the intra-group transfer undermine the relief. The Mitchell-Group transfer in April 2026 was supported by a board minute recording the rationale as "centralisation of residential investment within the group" with no contemporaneous discussion of any sale of any group entity; the para 2 gate passes.</p>

<aside><p>Para 2 is the silent failure mode on planned multi-stage transactions.</p><p>Where the broader commercial plan envisages selling part of the group after a period of pre-sale restructuring, the documentation at the intra-group transfer stage needs to be unimpeachable. Property Tax Partners runs documentation reviews for group-relief restructurings to confirm para 2 defensibility; this is particularly important for landlord families where the group restructuring sits within a broader exit or refinancing strategy. The form below routes to the corporate-restructuring team.</p></aside>

<h2>Paragraph 3 in operation: the three-year claw-back mechanics</h2>

<p>Paragraph 3 imposes claw-back where the transferee company ceases to be a member of the same group as the transferor within 3 years of the SDLT effective date. The triggering events include the transferee being sold to a third party, the transferee being moved into a different group structure that breaks the 75% group relationship, the transferee being subject to insolvency proceedings outside the para 3(2) carve-outs, or any other event that breaks the group connection.</p>

<p>The recomputation. Where claw-back triggers, the SDLT is recomputed as if the original transfer had not benefited from group relief. The chargeable consideration at the original effective date is taxed at the market-value rates that would have applied, including the 5% additional-dwellings surcharge for residential property. Interest runs from the original effective date to the claw-back assessment date at HMRC's then-current rate (currently around 8% per annum, varying with Bank of England base rate movements). Penalties may apply where the claw-back is not declared promptly by the transferee on the relevant Land Transaction Return supplementary filing.</p>

<p>The 3-year period runs from the SDLT effective date of the original transfer, not from completion of the transferee's subsequent transaction. The window therefore closes exactly 3 years after the original transfer; a transferee sold on the 3-year anniversary plus one day escapes claw-back. Timing of any subsequent disposal of the transferee matters operationally: where the transferor and transferee anticipate a future sale of the transferee, structuring the timing to fall outside the 3-year window can preserve the original group relief. The structural risk is that commercial circumstances may force a sale within the window despite the parties' best intentions.</p>

<h2>The Mitchell-Group worked claw-back walkthrough</h2>

<p><strong>Initial transfer, April 2026.</strong> Mitchell-Group Holdings Ltd owns 100% of three property SPVs (Mitchell-Residential, Mitchell-Commercial, Mitchell-Mixed) plus operating company Mitchell-Property-Management. The group reorganises: Mitchell-Residential transfers an 8-BTL portfolio (market value £3.2m, outstanding mortgages £1.4m) to Mitchell-Holdings to consolidate residential investment under the parent. SDLT group relief under Sch 7 is claimed on the Land Transaction Return; SDLT reduced from approximately £435,000 (residential rates plus 5% additional-dwellings surcharge on £3.2m) to zero. The transfer is documented as a centralisation of residential investment with board-minuted rationale; no para 2 arrangements exist.</p>

<p><strong>Operating period, April 2026 to October 2028.</strong> Mitchell-Holdings holds the 8-property portfolio for 30 months. Rental income from the portfolio flows through Mitchell-Holdings; Mitchell-Residential operates as a dormant SPV pending the next phase of the group plan.</p>

<p><strong>Sale event, October 2028.</strong> Mitchell-Group Holdings sells Mitchell-Residential Ltd shares to a third-party buyer (an external property investment firm wishing to acquire the SPV legacy entity for tax-loss carry-forward and historical-property-record purposes). Mitchell-Residential leaves the Mitchell-Group; para 3 claw-back triggers because the transferee from the original 2026 transfer has left the group within 3 years.</p>

<p><strong>Claw-back assessment.</strong> SDLT due on the original April 2026 transfer recomputed: approximately £435,000 at market value rates including the 5% additional-dwellings surcharge. Interest from April 2026 to October 2028: 30 months at approximately 8% per annum on £435,000, roughly £87,000. Total claw-back: approximately £522,000. Payable by Mitchell-Holdings as transferee.</p>

<p><strong>Recovery if Mitchell-Holdings cannot pay.</strong> Six months after the claw-back assessment, if unpaid, HMRC may invoke para 5 to recover from Mitchell-Residential (the original transferor in 2026), from Mitchell-Group Holdings (the parent at the time of original transfer), or from the controlling director of the group at the time of original transfer (Mr Mitchell personally, even if he has since reduced his role or sold his shares).</p>

<h2>Paragraph 5: the silent connected-party recovery risk</h2>

<p>Paragraph 5 of Schedule 7 provides that where the SDLT on a clawed-back transfer remains unpaid 6 months after assessment, HMRC may recover from three categories of connected party.</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>The vendor company in the original transfer (the transferor).</strong> In the Mitchell-Group case, this is Mitchell-Residential Ltd. Note that this is the company that BENEFITED from the relief at the original date (no SDLT was paid by it) but now faces the recovery if the transferee defaults.</li>
<li><strong>Any company that was the parent company of the group at the time of the original transfer.</strong> In the Mitchell-Group case, this is Mitchell-Group Holdings (which is also the transferee in this particular case, but in other structures could be a separate parent further up the chain).</li>
<li><strong>Any individual who was a controlling director of the group at the time of the original transfer.</strong> In the Mitchell-Group case, this is Mr Mitchell personally. The director-side liability attaches based on the director's role at the time of the original transfer, not at the time of claw-back. A director who left the group in 2027 still faces para 5 liability in 2028 for a transfer they signed off in 2026.</li>
</ul>

<p>The recovery is joint and several across the three categories. HMRC can pursue any one or any combination. The director-side exposure is the most operationally significant for landlord-LtdCo groups because directors typically have personal assets and unlimited personal liability (the corporate liabilities of the entities are limited to the corporate net worth). Sessions advising directors on group-relief restructurings need to surface this exposure at the planning stage.</p>

<h2>The SPA covenant pack: managing para 3 and para 5 together</h2>

<p>The defensive SPA covenant pack used by Property Tax Partners on landlord-LtdCo group-relief restructurings has four core elements. Each element addresses a specific risk in the para 3 / para 5 framework.</p>

<p><strong>1. Three-year continuity covenant.</strong> The SPA prohibits the transferee from leaving the group within the para 3 claw-back period (3 years from the SDLT effective date of the original transfer). Breach of the covenant triggers a break-fee equal to the claw-back exposure, typically the original SDLT plus an estimated interest amount calculated at HMRC's then-current rate. Where the parties wish to permit transferee disposal within the period (because commercial flexibility matters more than the SDLT-side risk), the break-fee provides a contractual mechanism to absorb the cost rather than leaving it as an unfunded exposure.</p>

<p><strong>2. Parent-company guarantee.</strong> The parent company at the time of original transfer guarantees the transferee's claw-back exposure during the 3-year period. Where claw-back triggers and the transferee cannot pay, the parent's guarantee covers the amount before HMRC invokes the statutory para 5 recovery. The guarantee is contractual (between the parent and the relevant counterparties); the para 5 recovery is statutory (HMRC's right). The two operate together: the contractual guarantee gives the transferee the ability to call on the parent first, preserving relationships, before HMRC's statutory recovery kicks in.</p>

<p><strong>3. Director-side indemnity and D&amp;O insurance maintenance.</strong> Individual controlling directors of the group at the time of original transfer are indemnified by the parent company against personal liability under para 5. The indemnity is supported by directors-and-officers insurance with an explicit SDLT-claw-back endorsement maintained for the 3-year period (and ideally for an additional 12-month tail to cover late-discovered claw-back events). The structural challenge is that D&amp;O insurance markets vary in their willingness to include SDLT-claw-back coverage; specialist underwriters such as those serving the corporate property sector are usually familiar with the risk and can quote.</p>

<p><strong>4. Notification covenant.</strong> Any change in group structure that may affect the para 3 status of the original transfer (transferee disposal, intra-group reorganisation that breaks the 75% chain, insolvency event) must be notified to all signing parties promptly. The notification triggers the financial-implications review and any contractual mechanics (break-fee payment, guarantee call, indemnity activation). Without the notification covenant, parties may discover the claw-back trigger only after HMRC's assessment lands, by which point the contractual mechanics may be too late to activate.</p>

<p>The four elements together convert an open-ended director-side personal-liability risk into a structured contractual framework where each party's exposure is defined, funded, and time-limited. The SPA pack is bespoke for each restructuring; the precise drafting depends on the group structure, the commercial rationale for the original transfer, the directors' personal asset position, and the parent company's creditworthiness for the guarantee.</p>

<h2>Group relief in the wider SDLT mitigation landscape</h2>

<p>Three SDLT-mitigation routes operate in different fact patterns and are not interchangeable.</p>

<p><strong>Schedule 7 group relief.</strong> Intra-group transfers within an established 75%-controlled corporate group. Relief subject to para 3 3-year claw-back and para 5 connected-party recovery. No apportionment calculation (zero SDLT on the transfer, subject to the claw-back). The route covered in this page.</p>

<p><strong>Schedule 15 partnership SLP relief.</strong> Genuine partnership-to-NewCo incorporation. SLP calculation reduces chargeable consideration potentially to zero. Subject to para 17A 3-year anti-withdrawal rule (separate from but conceptually parallel to the Sch 7 para 3 claw-back). See our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/partnership-sdlt-relief-schedule-15-fa-2003-incorporation-sum-lower-proportions">Schedule 15 partnership SDLT relief page</a> for the SLP mechanics.</p>

<p><strong>Incorporation-side CGT reliefs.</strong> TCGA s.162 incorporation relief (for trading businesses; rarely available for property investment per the Pawson line); TCGA s.165 gift holdover (business asset only); FA 2003 s.53 connected-party market-value rule (always applies on connected-party transfers; not a relief but a chargeable-consideration determination). See our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/section-162-incorporation-relief-property-landlords">section 162 incorporation relief page</a> for the CGT-side mechanics.</p>

<p>The three routes are layered: incorporation reliefs cover the CGT side; Schedule 15 covers SDLT on partnership incorporation; Schedule 7 covers SDLT on intra-group restructuring within an already-corporatised group. Landlord-family planning often involves all three at different stages: Schedule 15 at initial incorporation from partnership to LtdCo; section 162 (or, more commonly, market-value crystallisation) on the CGT side at the same step; Schedule 7 at subsequent intra-group restructuring as the corporate group grows. Each stage carries its own risk register; sessions advising landlord families need to map the full sequence.</p>

<h2>Operational patterns where Schedule 7 group relief is used in landlord-LtdCo restructuring</h2>

<p>Five recurring patterns dominate landlord-LtdCo group restructurings where Schedule 7 group relief is the operative SDLT mechanism. Each pattern carries its own para 3 claw-back profile depending on the post-restructure commercial plan.</p>

<p><strong>Pattern 1: consolidation of multi-SPV portfolio under a HoldCo.</strong> A landlord family has accumulated multiple SPVs over years, each holding one to three properties. The structure has grown organically without an overarching HoldCo. The family wants to consolidate operational and financing decisions at a single HoldCo level. The restructure transfers each SPV's property portfolio up to a newly-formed HoldCo using Schedule 7 relief at each stage. The post-restructure plan is to operate the HoldCo permanently, with no sale planned for any SPV. The para 3 claw-back risk is low because no transferee is expected to leave the group. SPA covenants in this pattern focus on the parent-guarantee element (covering the unlikely case where a transferee SPV needs to be sold for refinancing or commercial reasons within the 3-year window).</p>

<p><strong>Pattern 2: residential / commercial separation.</strong> A landlord group holds a mixed portfolio across one or more SPVs. The family wants to separate residential investment (subject to s.24 mortgage interest restriction at the individual level when extracted but tax-stable at the corporate level) from commercial investment (different cashflow profile, different financing characteristics, different exit horizons). The restructure transfers residential properties to a residential-only SPV and commercial properties to a commercial-only SPV, with both held under a common parent. Schedule 7 group relief applies to both transfers. The post-restructure plan typically envisages permanent separation; para 3 claw-back risk depends on whether either separated SPV is later sold (commercial property sales are common; residential investment is often held longer-term).</p>

<p><strong>Pattern 3: refinancing-driven restructure.</strong> A landlord group needs to refinance and the new lender requires a specific group structure (single SPV holding all secured properties; HoldCo with no operating activity; or similar). The restructure shapes the group to fit the lender requirement. Schedule 7 applies to the necessary property transfers. The para 3 claw-back risk is typically low because the restructure is driven by the refinancing not by a planned sale; however, the 3-year window must outlast the refinancing horizon to be safe (a refinancing planned for re-refinancing in 24 months is fine; one expecting a sale-and-replacement in 18 months may trigger claw-back).</p>

<p><strong>Pattern 4: family-succession reorganisation.</strong> An older-generation landlord wants to bring adult children into the corporate structure. The restructure typically uses Schedule 7 to move properties between corporate entities such that the children's holdings emerge in the right place after the share-class changes. The post-restructure plan envisages permanent family ownership; para 3 risk is theoretical unless an adult child later wants to exit and take a property out (which would trigger claw-back if within 3 years of the underlying Schedule 7 transfer).</p>

<p><strong>Pattern 5: exit-preparation restructure.</strong> A landlord group is preparing for an exit (sale of the group or a substantial portion) and uses Schedule 7 to shape the group into a saleable structure (cleaning the corporate chain, separating saleable from non-saleable assets, simplifying intra-group balances). This pattern carries the highest para 2 risk because the exit motive sits at the heart of the restructure; HMRC's enquiry teams view exit-prep restructures with the most scrutiny. The defensive documentation must clearly establish a non-sale-driven rationale for the specific transfer (operational consolidation, refinancing efficiency, etc.) even if the broader group is heading for an exit; otherwise para 2 catches the relief at the outset.</p>

<h2>Multi-stage restructures and the rolling 3-year window</h2>

<p>Where a landlord-LtdCo group conducts multiple intra-group transfers in sequence (typical in larger corporate landlord structures), each transfer carries its own 3-year claw-back window starting from its own effective date. The windows roll forward in parallel: a transfer on 1 April 2026 has its window closing 31 March 2029; a follow-on transfer on 15 October 2027 has its window closing 14 October 2030. Where the transferee in a later transfer is itself the transferee from an earlier transfer, the windows overlap and a single sale event affecting that company can trigger multiple claw-backs.</p>

<p>The operational implication is that any group with multiple Schedule 7 transfers in flight has a portfolio of contingent claw-back exposures with staggered expiry dates. Sessions advising on such groups should maintain a claw-back register: each transfer with its effective date, its claw-back expiry, its claw-back amount, and any contractual mitigations in place. The register provides the audit trail for HMRC enquiries and a single-source-of-truth for directors making decisions about subsequent disposals or restructures.</p>

<p>HMRC's manual at <a href="https://www.gov.uk/hmrc-internal-manuals/stamp-duty-land-tax-manual/sdltm23000">SDLTM23000 onwards</a> is the authoritative reference for Schedule 7 group relief mechanics. Sub-pages cover the 75% group test (SDLTM23010), para 2 arrangements (SDLTM23030), para 3 claw-back (SDLTM23040 onwards), and para 5 recovery (SDLTM23070 onwards). Practitioner content that cites SDLTM09050 (s.75A Ramsay general anti-avoidance) or SDLTM33500 (Schedule 15 partnership mechanics) for Schedule 7 issues has confused the manuals; the three streams operate in distinct territories. Correct citation discipline is the principal differentiator between authoritative content and drift-prone content on landlord-SDLT topics.</p>
