<p><strong>Replacement domestic items relief</strong> allows UK landlords to claim tax relief when they replace furniture, appliances, and other domestic items in furnished rental properties. This relief replaced the old 10% wear and tear allowance in April 2016, fundamentally changing how landlords can claim for furnishing costs.</p>

<p>Unlike the previous system, replacement domestic items relief only provides tax relief when you actually replace items — not as an automatic allowance. This means landlords need to keep detailed records and understand exactly what qualifies for relief.</p>

<h2>What Is Replacement Domestic Items Relief?</h2>

<p>Replacement domestic items relief is a tax deduction that allows landlords to claim the cost of replacing moveable domestic items in furnished rental properties. The relief covers the cost of the replacement item, minus any proceeds from selling or part-exchanging the old item.</p>

<p>This relief applies to both residential and commercial furnished lettings, but the item being replaced must have been provided when the property was first let as a furnished property.</p>

<p>The key principle is that you're claiming relief for maintaining the standard of furnishing in your property, not improving it. HMRC treats this as a revenue expense rather than capital expenditure, making it deductible against rental income.</p>

<h2>What Items Qualify for Replacement Domestic Items Relief?</h2>

<p>The relief covers moveable domestic items that would normally be found in a home. HMRC provides specific guidance on what qualifies:</p>

<h3>Items That Qualify</h3>

<ul>
<li><strong>Furniture:</strong> Sofas, chairs, beds, wardrobes, dining tables, bookcases</li>
<li><strong>Furnishings:</strong> Curtains, carpets, rugs, blinds, pictures, ornaments</li>
<li><strong>Household appliances:</strong> Washing machines, refrigerators, freezers, dishwashers, televisions</li>
<li><strong>Kitchen equipment:</strong> Cookers, microwaves, kettles, toasters, crockery, cutlery</li>
<li><strong>Other domestic items:</strong> Lamps, mirrors, vacuum cleaners, garden furniture for garden flats</li>
</ul>

<h3>Items That Don't Qualify</h3>

<ul>
<li><strong>Integral fixtures:</strong> Built-in wardrobes, fitted kitchens, bathroom suites</li>
<li><strong>Structural items:</strong> Doors, windows, radiators, boilers</li>
<li><strong>Decorative fixtures:</strong> Light fittings, ceiling fans (unless easily removable)</li>
<li><strong>Improvements:</strong> Items that improve the property rather than replace like-for-like</li>
</ul>

<p>The distinction between moveable items and fixtures can be complex. Generally, if you could take the item with you when moving house, it's likely to qualify for replacement domestic items relief.</p>

<h2>How to Calculate Replacement Domestic Items Relief</h2>

<p>The calculation for replacement domestic items relief follows a specific formula:</p>

<p><strong>Relief = Cost of replacement item - Any proceeds from disposal of old item</strong></p>

<h3>Example Calculation</h3>

<p>A landlord replaces a worn sofa in their furnished rental property:</p>

<ul>
<li>Cost of new sofa: £800</li>
<li>Old sofa sold on Facebook Marketplace: £50</li>
<li>Replacement domestic items relief claim: £800 - £50 = £750</li>
</ul>

<p>If the old item has no value and is disposed of without sale, you can claim the full cost of the replacement item.</p>

<h3>Part-Exchange Situations</h3>

<p>When you part-exchange an old item for a new one, you need to identify the trade-in value:</p>

<ul>
<li>Cost of new washing machine: £600</li>
<li>Part-exchange allowance for old machine: £100</li>
<li>Amount paid: £500</li>
<li>Relief claim: £600 - £100 = £500 (same as amount paid)</li>
</ul>

<h2>When Does Replacement Domestic Items Relief Apply?</h2>

<p>The relief only applies when you're replacing an existing item, not when you're furnishing a property for the first time or adding new items.</p>

<h3>Property Must Be Furnished</h3>

<p>The property must have been let as furnished when you first let it. If you later decide to furnish an unfurnished property, the initial furniture costs don't qualify for this relief — they're capital expenditure.</p>

<h3>Like-for-Like Replacement</h3>

<p>HMRC expects replacements to be broadly similar to the original item. You can replace a three-seater sofa with a different three-seater sofa, but replacing a small TV with a large smart TV might be seen as an improvement rather than a replacement.</p>

<p>However, normal technological advancement is accepted. Replacing an old standard-definition TV with a modern HD TV of similar size would typically qualify, as would replacing a basic washing machine with a modern equivalent.</p>

<h2>Record Keeping Requirements</h2>

<p>Proper record keeping is essential for replacement domestic items relief claims. HMRC may request evidence during an enquiry, particularly given the increase in <a href="/blog/making-tax-digital-mtd/making-tax-digital-landlords-april-2026-deadline">Making Tax Digital</a> scrutiny from April 2026.</p>

<h3>Essential Records</h3>

<ul>
<li><strong>Purchase receipts:</strong> For both original and replacement items</li>
<li><strong>Disposal evidence:</strong> Sales receipts, charity donation confirmations, or waste disposal evidence</li>
<li><strong>Photos:</strong> Before and after photos showing the condition of items being replaced</li>
<li><strong>Property inventory:</strong> Original inventory showing what was provided when first let</li>
<li><strong>Dates:</strong> When items were purchased, when they were replaced, and how long they lasted</li>
</ul>

<h3>Digital Record Keeping</h3>

<p>With MTD requirements coming into effect, consider using cloud-based accounting software to store digital receipts and photos. This makes it easier to link replacement costs to specific properties and rental periods.</p>

<h2>How Does This Relief Compare to the Old Wear and Tear Allowance?</h2>

<p>Before April 2016, furnished property landlords could claim a wear and tear allowance of 10% of rent (excluding services charges). This was an automatic deduction whether you replaced anything or not.</p>

<h3>Key Differences</h3>

<p>For many landlords, the actual replacement costs exceed what they would have claimed under the old 10% allowance, particularly in the early years when furniture needs frequent replacement. However, in later years when items are more established, the relief may be less generous.</p>

<h2>Tax Planning Considerations</h2>

<p>Understanding replacement domestic items relief helps with broader <a href="/blog/landlord-tax-essentials/property-investment-tax-uk-complete-guide-2026">property investment tax planning</a>, especially as tax rules continue to evolve.</p>

<h3>Timing of Replacements</h3>

<p>Consider the timing of major replacements in relation to your overall tax position. If you're having a particularly profitable year, accelerating necessary replacements might provide useful tax relief.</p>

<p>However, don't replace items unnecessarily just for tax relief — the net cost is still significant even after tax relief.</p>

<h3>Section 24 Impact</h3>

<p>Replacement domestic items relief reduces your profit before applying <a href="/blog/section-24-and-tax-relief/section-24-tax-relief-complete-guide">Section 24 restrictions</a>, making it more valuable than mortgage interest relief which is restricted to basic rate.</p>

<p>For example, a higher-rate taxpayer saves 40% on replacement costs through this relief, whereas mortgage interest relief is capped at 20%.</p>

<h3>Company vs Individual Ownership</h3>

<p>The relief works the same way whether you own properties personally or through a <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/buy-to-let-limited-company-complete-guide-uk">buy-to-let limited company</a>. However, the effective tax rate on the relief differs due to different tax rates.</p>

<h2>Common Mistakes to Avoid</h2>

<p>Several common errors can lead to HMRC challenges or rejected claims:</p>

<h3>Claiming Improvements as Replacements</h3>

<p>The biggest risk is claiming relief for improvements rather than true replacements. Upgrading from basic to luxury items, or adding items not previously provided, doesn't qualify.</p>

<h3>Poor Record Keeping</h3>

<p>Many landlords fail to keep adequate records, making it difficult to prove claims during HMRC enquiries. Always retain receipts and document the condition of items being replaced.</p>

<h3>Double Claiming</h3>

<p>Don't claim both replacement domestic items relief and capital allowances on the same item. Each item should follow only one tax treatment route.</p>

<h3>Not Deducting Disposal Proceeds</h3>

<p>Always reduce your claim by any money received for the old item, even small amounts from car boot sales or eBay.</p>

<h2>Practical Examples</h2>

<h3>Example 1: Student Property</h3>

<p>Sarah owns a furnished student house with rental income of £24,000 per year. During the tax year, she replaces:</p>

<ul>
<li>Broken washing machine: £450 (old one scrapped, no proceeds)</li>
<li>Worn carpets in two bedrooms: £800 (old carpets disposed of)</li>
<li>Damaged dining table and chairs: £300 (old set sold for £40)</li>
</ul>

<p>Total replacement domestic items relief: £450 + £800 + (£300 - £40) = £1,210</p>

<p>This reduces her taxable rental profit by £1,210, saving £484 in tax (assuming 40% tax rate).</p>

<h3>Example 2: HMO Property</h3>

<p>James operates a licensed HMO with six bedrooms, each furnished separately. He replaces items room by room as tenants leave:</p>

<ul>
<li>Room 1: Bed and mattress £200, wardrobe £150</li>
<li>Room 3: Desk and chair £180</li>
<li>Communal area: Sofa £600 (old sofa part-exchanged for £80)</li>
</ul>

<p>Total relief: £200 + £150 + £180 + (£600 - £80) = £1,050</p>

<h2>Future Changes and Considerations</h2>

<p>While replacement domestic items relief hasn't been directly affected by recent tax changes, landlords should be aware of broader developments:</p>

<h3>Separate Property Tax Rates</h3>

<p>From April 2027, separate property income tax rates (22% basic, 42% higher, 47% additional) will apply. This makes replacement domestic items relief more valuable as it reduces profit before these higher rates apply.</p>

<h3>Making Tax Digital</h3>

<p>From April 2026, landlords with gross property income over £10,000 must use MTD-compatible software. Ensure your record-keeping system can handle replacement domestic items relief claims and link them to specific properties.</p>
