  - question: "Do I have to pay inheritance tax on a house I'm inheriting?"
    answer: "No. The personal representatives (executor or administrator) pay any IHT from the estate before distributing it to you. By the time the title transfers, the IHT bill has been settled. Where the house is the main asset and the estate is illiquid, the personal representatives may elect the 10-year instalment option under IHTA 1984 s.227 so the IHT is paid annually rather than up front. Instalments accelerate if the property is sold. You as beneficiary do not receive an IHT bill at any point. The estate's IHT is the estate's obligation, not yours."
  - question: "Do I pay income tax or CGT just for receiving the property?"
    answer: "No to both. An inheritance itself is not income, so no income tax arises on receipt. And per TCGA 1992 s.62(1)(a), assets vested in beneficiaries on death are treated as acquired at market value at the date of death. No CGT is charged on the deceased, and no CGT is charged on you on receipt. The gain that accrued during the deceased's ownership is wiped for CGT purposes. Your CGT base cost going forward is the probate value (the so-called ascertained value under s.274 where IHT applied to the estate)."
  - question: "What is the probate value and why does it matter to me?"
    answer: "The probate value is the market value of the property at the date of death, used by the personal representatives to compute IHT on the estate. Per TCGA 1992 s.62(1)(a), it also sets YOUR CGT base cost for any future disposal. If you sell at the probate value (which is common within the first year), there is no gain and no CGT. If you sell years later at a much higher value, CGT is charged on the gain above probate value, not above the deceased's original acquisition cost. That earlier gain was wiped out by the uplift. Get the probate value documented properly. It is the number you will need years from now."
  - question: "What are my three main options for an inherited house?"
    answer: "(a) Keep and occupy. Move in as your main residence; PPR runs from the date of actual occupation forward; the pre-occupation void between the date of death and your move-in does NOT qualify (except for the last 9 months of ownership at eventual sale under s.223). (b) Keep and let. Rental income is taxed at your marginal rate on the ITTOIA 2005 property-business basis; future disposal triggers CGT against probate value; if you take a buy-to-let mortgage Section 24 finance-cost restriction applies. (c) Sell. Usually at or close to probate value if within the first year, in which case there is typically no chargeable gain, no 60-day return, and no CGT. Each route has different downstream tax consequences."
  - question: "When does the 60-day CGT reporting requirement apply?"
    answer: "Per Finance Act 2019 Schedule 2 as amended by Finance Act 2021 s.23, UK residents disposing of UK residential property must file a CGT-on-property-disposal return and pay a payment on account within 60 days of completion. This applies ONLY where there is a chargeable gain to report. Sales at or close to probate value usually produce no gain (or a gain within the annual exempt amount), so no return is required. The 60-day reporting trap bites mainly where the inheritor sells years later at a materially higher value than probate value. Final reconciliation runs through the annual self-assessment return."
  - question: "What is a deed of variation and when should I consider it?"
    answer: "Per IHTA 1984 s.142 and TCGA 1992 s.62(6), beneficiaries can redirect their inheritance within two years of death. The variation reads back to the deceased for both IHT and CGT. There is no fresh disposal by you. Common uses: skip a generation by redirecting to your own children to keep the house outside your own future estate; equalise between siblings where one inherited disproportionately; redirect to qualifying charity to trigger the Sch 1A 36% reduced IHT rate where at least 10% of net estate passes to charity; redirect into a discretionary trust for asset protection. The two-year window is hard. Miss it and the redirection becomes a fresh PET or chargeable transfer by you."
  - question: "What is the difference between a deed of variation and a disclaimer?"
    answer: "A disclaimer relinquishes the inheritance entirely. The asset passes to the next person in line under the will (or under the intestacy rules if there was no will). You have no control over the destination. A deed of variation redirects to a person of your choosing. Disclaimer is one-way; DoV is targeted. Both can read back to the deceased for tax purposes if executed within the two-year window with the right elections. For most planning scenarios the deed of variation is the more useful tool because you can specify who receives the redirected inheritance."
  - question: "What happens if there is a mortgage on the inherited house?"
    answer: "The mortgage is paid from the estate before distribution, per Administration of Estates Act 1925 s.34 and Schedule 1 Part 2 (the statutory order of application of assets). Secured creditors have first call on the secured asset. If the estate is illiquid (for example £550,000 house, £20,000 cash, £200,000 mortgage) the personal representatives may need to sell the house to satisfy the mortgage. Or you may take over the mortgage personally with lender consent (lender will assess your income to service the loan). Where the lender declines and you cannot refinance, forced sale is common. Life insurance written in trust outside the estate is the standard solution in planned cases."
  - question: "What if I'm inheriting jointly with siblings and we disagree?"
    answer: "You inherit as tenants in common (TIC), each owning a defined undivided share, typically equal unless the will specifies otherwise. Where co-inheritors disagree on whether to keep or sell, any one of you can apply to court under the Trusts of Land and Appointment of Trustees Act 1996 s.14 for an order of sale. Per Bagum v Hafiz [2015] EWCA Civ 801, the court has discretion to order sale, looking at factors including whether any party is bona fide occupying the property and whether the others have alternative accommodation. Litigation costs erode the estate's value quickly. Agreement is usually preferable. Buy-out arrangements between siblings, partition where physically possible, or staged sale plans all sit beneath the court route."
  - question: "What if there was no will (intestacy)?"
    answer: "The Administration of Estates Act 1925 ss.45-49 (as amended by the Inheritance and Trustees' Powers Act 2014) sets the distribution hierarchy. Where the deceased left a spouse and issue: the spouse takes personal chattels, a statutory legacy of £322,000 (SI 2023/758 from 26 July 2023), and half the residue absolutely; children share the other half on statutory trusts. Spouse and no issue: spouse takes everything. No spouse: issue, parents, siblings, nieces and nephews, in the statutory hierarchy at s.46. The surviving spouse can elect under AEA 1925 Schedule 2 to take the matrimonial home in or towards satisfaction of their entitlement, paying equality money if the home exceeds their share."
  - question: "If I move into the inherited house, does PPR apply from the date of death?"
    answer: "No. Per TCGA 1992 s.223(1) and s.223(2), private residence relief runs from the date you actually occupy as your main residence. The last 9 months of any period of ownership qualifies for PPR regardless of occupation, but the pre-occupation void between the date of death and your move-in does NOT qualify by itself. If the gain accrues mostly in that void you face a part-CGT calculation at sale. The narrow s.225 trustees relief can sometimes preserve PPR where the personal representative retains the property and a beneficiary occupies it before assent, but the conditions are tight and the route is operationally fiddly."
  - question: "What if the property is in Scotland?"
    answer: "Scottish succession law (Succession (Scotland) Act 1964 and Succession (Scotland) Act 2016) is materially different from the English and Welsh framework. The fractions of moveable estate going to spouse and children depend on whether the deceased was testate or intestate. For Scottish-situs property, the rules above do NOT apply. Refer to a Scottish-law solicitor early. Land Registration Act 2002 does not apply; Land Registers of Scotland and Scots conveyancing law govern title."

<p>The first thing to know about inheriting a UK house is what is NOT your problem. Inheritance tax on the estate is the executor's problem, settled from the estate's assets before the title transfers to you. Receipt of the property is not a taxable event for you. No income tax, no capital gains tax, no inheritance tax bill arrives in your name.</p>

<p>The second thing to know is the single most important tax fact about an inheritance: the probate value at the date of death becomes your capital gains tax base cost. Per <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12/section/62" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">TCGA 1992 s.62(1)(a)</a>, you are deemed to acquire the asset at market value at the date of death. The gain accrued during the deceased's ownership is wiped out. Years from now, when you might sell, the chargeable gain is measured against probate value, not against whatever the deceased originally paid.</p>

<p>From there your decision splits three ways. Keep and occupy as your main residence (PPR runs from your move-in date). Keep and let (rental income at your marginal rate; future CGT against probate value). Or sell, typically within the first year (usually at or near probate value with no chargeable gain and no 60-day reporting obligation).</p>

<p>This page is the beneficiary's decision pillar. The Latimer-family £550,000 worked example threads all three routes plus the deed of variation variant.</p>

<h2>What the executor does (and what you don't)</h2>

<p>Most beneficiaries arrive at this page confused about who pays IHT. The answer is the estate, through the personal representatives (executors named in the will, or administrators where there is no will or no acting executor).</p>

<p>The executor process at orientation depth:</p>

<ol>
<li>Personal representatives apply for the grant of probate (will) or letters of administration (intestacy). The grant gives them legal authority to deal with the deceased's assets.</li>
<li>They complete the IHT400 IHT return (or the shorter IHT205/IHT207 for excepted estates), pay any IHT due (or elect the 10-year instalment option under <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1984/51/section/227" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">IHTA 1984 s.227</a> on land), and obtain IHT421 / IHT442 clearance.</li>
<li>They settle estate debts in the statutory order of application of assets under <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/Geo5/15-16/23" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">Administration of Estates Act 1925</a> s.34 + Schedule 1 Part 2 (secured creditors first; then funeral and administration costs; then unsecured creditors; then specific legacies; then residuary legacies).</li>
<li>They execute the Assent (form AS1 at HM Land Registry) transferring legal title of the property to you. No SDLT on the assent (the transfer is not for consideration in the SDLT sense).</li>
<li>You become registered proprietor at HM Land Registry. The legal and beneficial title now sits with you.</li>
</ol>

<p>Throughout the process you are largely a spectator. Your contact with the executor is for documentation (sometimes asked to consent to certain decisions) and eventually for distribution. The executor is liable for IHT under <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1984/51/section/226" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">IHTA 1984 s.226</a>; you are not.</p>

<aside>
<p>The single most useful step you can take in the early weeks is to confirm in writing with the executor what the probate value of the property will be. This is the number that sets your CGT base cost.</p>

<h2>The Latimer-family worked example</h2>

<p>Persona: Mr Latimer, son, aged 47, owns his own house in Leeds (worth £350,000, with PPR fully covering any prior gain), salaried higher-rate taxpayer. His mother died in March 2026. Her estate: family home in Harrogate at probate value £550,000 (her 1995 acquisition cost £180,000); cash and ISA £150,000. Mother had full NRB available (£325,000) plus full RNRB (£175,000) where the home passes to lineal descendants, giving £500,000 of combined allowance.</p>

<p>Net estate £700,000. Above combined allowances by £200,000. IHT at 40% on £200,000 = £80,000 paid by the executor from the cash element (or by 10-year instalments under s.227 on the land slice if cash is short). After IHT settled, the son receives the house plus the residual cash of approximately £70,000.</p>

<p>The son's three routes:</p>

<h3>Route A: keep and occupy (PPR)</h3>

<p>The son sells his Leeds house in late 2026 (no CGT, full PPR on his prior occupation under <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12/section/222" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">TCGA 1992 s.222</a>) and moves into the Harrogate house in February 2027. From the move-in date, PPR runs prospectively under s.222 and s.223.</p>

<ul>
<li>The pre-occupation void (March 2026 to February 2027, approximately 11 months) does NOT qualify for PPR by itself.</li>
<li>The last 9 months of any future period of ownership qualifies for PPR regardless of occupation under <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12/section/223" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">TCGA 1992 s.223(1) and s.223(2)</a>.</li>
<li>On eventual sale, PPR covers the occupied period plus the last 9 months. The non-occupied period (death to move-in, minus the last 9 months overlap if applicable) is chargeable proportionately.</li>
<li>If the son occupies for 15 years and then sells at £900,000: gain £350,000 (above probate value £550,000); chargeable proportion = void months over total ownership months, after subtracting any last-9-months overlap. Typically the void is a small fraction of total ownership and most of the gain is PPR-covered.</li>
</ul>

<p>Route A is the right answer where the son actually wants to live in the Harrogate house and is willing to give up his Leeds residence. The void-period CGT is a small price for full prospective PPR.</p>

<h3>Route B: keep and let</h3>

<p>The son retains his Leeds residence and lets out the Harrogate house to long-term tenants from June 2026.</p>

<ul>
<li>Rental income taxed at the son's marginal rate (40% higher rate) on <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2005/5" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">ITTOIA 2005</a> Part 3 property-business basis.</li>
<li>No Section 24 finance-cost restriction applies if there is no mortgage on the inherited property; the issue arises only if the son later remortgages to extract equity.</li>
<li>Future disposal triggers CGT on the gain above probate value £550,000. No PPR (it is not the son's main residence).</li>
<li>On sale at £900,000 ten years later: gain £350,000 at 24% higher residential rate = £84,000 CGT, with 60-day CGT-on-property-disposal return and payment on account required.</li>
</ul>

<p>Route B is the right answer where the son wants the rental yield (and the long-term capital appreciation) but does not want to live there. The route trades immediate tax efficiency for ongoing landlord administration and future CGT.</p>

<h3>Route C: sell within twelve months</h3>

<p>The son lists the house immediately, sells four months after probate vests for £555,000.</p>

<ul>
<li>Chargeable gain £5,000 (£555,000 minus £550,000 probate value), minus selling expenses (estate-agent and legal fees ~£10,000) = no chargeable gain.</li>
<li>No 60-day CGT-on-property-disposal return required because no chargeable gain.</li>
<li>Cash proceeds £545,000 net land in the son's account.</li>
<li>No further tax. The son adds the cash to his own assets and the inheritance is fully closed.</li>
</ul>

<p>Route C is the most common route in practice. It is the right answer where the son does not want to live in the house and does not want to become a landlord. Sold near probate value, the transaction is typically tax-free for the beneficiary.</p>

<aside>
<p>The three-route decision is not abstract. The Latimer-family figures show Route C as tax-efficient and operationally simple; Route A as best where genuine occupation makes sense; Route B as best for the would-be landlord. Many beneficiaries default to Route B without modelling the long-term CGT against probate value.</p>

<h3>Variant: deed of variation to skip a generation</h3>

<p>Suppose the son already has substantial assets and is in his late 40s. Adding £550,000 of property to his estate compounds his own future IHT exposure. Within two years of his mother's death, the son executes a deed of variation under <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1984/51/section/142" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">IHTA 1984 s.142</a> redirecting the inheritance to his own two children (his mother's grandchildren, aged 22 and 25).</p>

<ul>
<li>For IHT (s.142): the variation reads back to the mother's estate. The grandchildren are treated as direct beneficiaries. No fresh PET by the son; no fresh seven-year clock.</li>
<li>For CGT (TCGA 1992 s.62(6)): the redirection is NOT a disposal by the son. The grandchildren acquire at probate value (£550,000), preserving the s.62(1)(a) death uplift.</li>
<li>Each grandchild owns a 50% TIC share at probate value £275,000. They can hold (using their own NRB and RNRB on eventual death decades hence) or sell now at break-even.</li>
<li>The son's own estate is £550,000 lower. The IHT exposure on the son's eventual death falls by £220,000 at 40% rates.</li>
</ul>

<p>The deed of variation is the only mechanism in UK tax that achieves both CGT uplift preservation AND IHT savings (the next-generation saving on the son's eventual death) in one structure. For full mechanics see our <a href="/blog/landlord-tax-essentials/deed-of-variation-property-estate-redirecting-inheritance-iht-saving">deed of variation property estate</a> page.</p>

<h2>The 60-day CGT reporting trap</h2>

<p>For UK residents disposing of UK residential property, <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2019/1/schedule/2" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">Finance Act 2019 Schedule 2</a> as amended by <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2021/26/section/23" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">Finance Act 2021 s.23</a> requires a CGT-on-property-disposal return AND payment on account within 60 days of completion, where there is a chargeable gain.</p>

<p>Three points the amateur reader often misses:</p>

<ul>
<li>The 60-day window runs from completion (the date you receive the proceeds), not from exchange.</li>
<li>The obligation triggers ONLY where there is a chargeable gain. Sales at or close to probate value generally produce no gain (or a gain within the annual exempt amount, currently £3,000 for 2025/26) and no return is required.</li>
<li>The 60-day return is not the final settlement. Final reconciliation runs through the annual self-assessment return (SA108 CGT supplementary pages), where adjustments are made and any over-payment is refunded.</li>
</ul>

<p>The practical filter: if you are selling the inherited house within twelve months at near-probate-value, you almost certainly have no 60-day obligation. If you are selling years later at materially above probate value, you almost certainly do. The 60-day window is short and the penalties for missing it (interest plus late-filing penalty stack) are real.</p>

<h2>Joint inheritance and TIC mechanics</h2>

<p>Where the will (or intestacy) leaves the house to multiple beneficiaries, you each take a defined undivided share, typically equal unless the will specifies otherwise. The default is tenants-in-common (TIC); each beneficiary owns a separate share that can be sold, gifted, or willed.</p>

<p>Practical issues with joint inheritance:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Disagreement on whether to keep or sell.</strong> Any one beneficiary can apply under <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1996/47" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">TOLATA 1996 s.14</a> for an order of sale. Per <em>Bagum v Hafiz</em> [2015] EWCA Civ 801, the court exercises discretion looking at occupation, alternative accommodation for other parties, and the purpose for which the property was acquired.</li>
<li><strong>Buy-out by one beneficiary.</strong> Common where one wants to occupy and another wants the cash. Requires agreed valuation; the buying beneficiary pays cash to the seller; legal title is transferred via TR1 from the joint registered proprietors to the buying party.</li>
<li><strong>Tenancy or letting between siblings.</strong> One sibling occupies; pays rent at market value to the others; the others declare rental income on SA. Rare but workable.</li>
<li><strong>Phased sale or partition.</strong> Where the property can be physically divided (a duplex, a property with separable units), partition orders are available under TOLATA. Operationally fiddly and rare in practice.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Mortgage on the inherited house</h2>

<p>The mortgage is paid from the estate before distribution per AEA 1925 s.34 and Schedule 1 Part 2. Secured creditors have first call on the secured asset.</p>

<p>Three common scenarios:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Solvent estate with liquidity.</strong> Cash in the estate clears the mortgage; the unencumbered property passes to the beneficiary. Most common case.</li>
<li><strong>Solvent estate without liquidity.</strong> £550,000 house, £20,000 cash, £200,000 mortgage. Executor must either sell the house to clear the mortgage (and you inherit the residual cash) or you arrange to take over the mortgage with the lender's consent. Lender assesses your income to service the loan; if you cannot service it, forced sale is common.</li>
<li><strong>Insolvent estate.</strong> Estate's debts exceed its assets. The property goes to satisfy creditors; the beneficiary receives nothing from the estate. The IHT calculation never gets started because there is no chargeable estate to tax.</li>
</ul>

<p>The standard planning answer for the illiquid-estate case is a life insurance policy written in trust outside the estate, which provides liquidity for the mortgage payoff at exactly the moment it is needed. Uninsured mortgages on family homes are the most common cause of forced sale in inheritance.</p>

<h2>Intestacy: when there is no will</h2>

<p>Under the Administration of Estates Act 1925 ss.45-49 (as amended by the Inheritance and Trustees' Powers Act 2014), the distribution where there is no will:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Spouse and issue.</strong> Spouse takes personal chattels, statutory legacy £322,000 (<a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2023/758" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">SI 2023/758</a> from 26 July 2023), and half the residue absolutely. Issue (children) share the other half on statutory trusts.</li>
<li><strong>Spouse, no issue.</strong> Spouse takes the entire estate.</li>
<li><strong>No spouse, issue.</strong> Issue take the entire estate equally on statutory trusts.</li>
<li><strong>No spouse, no issue.</strong> Parents, then full siblings, then half siblings, then grandparents, then full aunts and uncles, then half aunts and uncles, in the hierarchy at s.46.</li>
</ul>

<p>The surviving spouse can elect under AEA 1925 Schedule 2 to take the matrimonial home in or towards satisfaction of their entitlement, paying equality money if the home exceeds their share. Without that election the home is sold and the proceeds distributed in line with the statutory shares.</p>

<p>For most beneficiaries the practical takeaway is that intestacy rarely matches what the deceased would have wanted. If you are reading this page because someone in your family does not have a will, the cheapest single piece of estate planning is to encourage them to make one.</p>

<aside>
<p>The two-year deed of variation window is the most often missed beneficiary planning move. It costs little to execute and can save substantial IHT across the next generation. The window is hard: 24 months from the date of death, no extensions.</p>

<h2>If the property is in Scotland</h2>

<p>Scottish succession law differs materially from the English and Welsh framework. Under the Succession (Scotland) Act 1964 and the Succession (Scotland) Act 2016:</p>

<ul>
<li>The surviving spouse has <strong>prior rights</strong>: statutory entitlements to dwelling house, plenishings (furniture and household contents), and a cash sum.</li>
<li>The surviving spouse and children have <strong>legal rights</strong>: a statutory share of the moveable estate (i.e. not heritable land but cash, investments, chattels).</li>
<li>Where there is a will, legal rights can be exercised against the will (the spouse or child can elect to take their legal rights instead of any testamentary provision).</li>
<li>Scots conveyancing law (the Land Register of Scotland, Sasines, dispositions) replaces the Land Registry / Assent / TR1 framework of England and Wales.</li>
</ul>

<p>For Scottish-situs property, refer to a Scottish-law solicitor early. The English and Welsh framework above does NOT apply. Where the deceased had assets in both jurisdictions, the cross-border framework needs coordinated handling.</p>

<h2>The decision sequence</h2>

<p>If you have just been told you are inheriting a UK house, work through these questions in order:</p>

<ol>
<li>Is there a will? If yes, confirm the executor has been identified and probate has been (or is being) applied for. If no, the intestacy rules apply and the personal representative is the administrator.</li>
<li>What is the probate value of the property? Get this in writing from the executor as soon as the estate's IHT400 is filed. This is your CGT base cost.</li>
<li>What is the IHT position of the estate? The executor settles it; you do not. But understanding whether IHT is paid in full up-front or by 10-year instalments under s.227 affects when you can expect clear title.</li>
<li>Is there a mortgage on the property? If yes, what is the estate's liquidity position? If illiquid, plan for the possibility of forced sale or owner-occupier refinancing.</li>
<li>Do you want to occupy, let, or sell? The decision sets the downstream tax position for years. Reconfirm against the three-route framework above.</li>
<li>Are you in your 40s or 50s with substantial assets of your own? If yes, consider a deed of variation within two years to skip a generation.</li>
<li>Are you inheriting with siblings or other beneficiaries? Have the conversation early about whether you all want the same thing. TOLATA litigation is expensive and erodes the value you are inheriting.</li>
<li>Is the property in Scotland? If yes, the rules above do not apply. Get Scots-law advice early.</li>
</ol>

<h2>The bigger picture</h2>

<p>Inheriting a UK house is a receiving-side event. The executor handles inheritance tax from the estate. You receive the title clean (or, where the estate is illiquid, encumbered by an existing mortgage that the lender will need to consent to your assumption of). Your CGT base cost is the probate value. Your three structural routes are keep and occupy, keep and let, or sell. Your one piece of post-receipt flexibility is the two-year deed of variation window.</p>

<p>The single most overlooked move in beneficiary planning is the deed of variation. Within two years of death, with the right elections, you can redirect your inheritance to your own children, equalise between siblings, redirect to charity, or settle into a trust. The redirection reads back to the deceased for both IHT and CGT purposes. There is no fresh seven-year clock and no fresh CGT crystallisation. For mid-life beneficiaries with substantial assets of their own, the DoV is often the right answer.</p>

<p>The single most common mistake is the assumption that the inheritance compounds the beneficiary's own estate without thinking through whether the receiving family wants the asset at all. Many inherited properties are sold within twelve months; many are converted to rentals without proper modelling of the long-term CGT against probate value; some are kept as second homes that accumulate void-period CGT. The choice is yours. The framework above is the structure inside which the choice gets made cleanly.</p>
