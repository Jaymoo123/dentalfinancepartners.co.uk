<p>Six legitimate statutory routes can reduce a council tax bill in the UK. Each is anchored in primary legislation or a 1992 statutory instrument. Each is free to apply for through the local authority or the Valuation Office Agency. No third-party firm is needed for any of them, despite a thriving cottage industry that suggests otherwise. This page walks the six routes in turn, with statute citations, application steps, worked examples, and the operative position post-Levelling-up and Regeneration Act 2023, under which the empty-property and second-home discount routes have been replaced by premium surcharges.</p>

<p>The audience is broad: homeowners, tenants, landlords, carers, students, persons with disabilities, bereaved family members, persons going through bankruptcy or repossession. Every route below is statute-anchored and depends on the specific facts of your situation. The framework is the same; the route that applies depends on who lives in the dwelling and how it is being used.</p>

<h2>The six routes in one read</h2>

<ol>
<li><strong>Discounts under section 11 LGFA 1992.</strong> The 25 per cent single-occupant discount and the no-resident discount under section 11(1)(a) and section 11(2).</li>
<li><strong>Exemptions under SI 1992/558.</strong> Class-based exemptions (Class N student, Class W annex, Class M HMO, Class F post-death, and others) that eliminate council tax entirely for the period the exemption applies.</li>
<li><strong>Disabled-band reduction under SI 1992/554.</strong> The dwelling assessed at one band lower than its actual band where the disability conditions are met.</li>
<li><strong>VOA banding challenge under section 16 LGFA 1992.</strong> Correction of a band that is or has become incorrect.</li>
<li><strong>Council Tax Reduction Scheme under Schedule 1A LGFA 1992.</strong> Means-tested reduction operated by each LA, up to 100 per cent for low-income households.</li>
<li><strong>Specific class-based reliefs.</strong> Including the dependant-relative annex exemption (Class W) and the family-annex reduction.</li>
</ol>

<p>Several of these routes can stack. A disabled household in a low-income position with one full-time student adult can claim the disability-band reduction (one band lower), the single-person discount (25 per cent off the lower band for the non-student adult), and CTR (means-tested further reduction).</p>

<h2>Route 1: Discounts under section 11 LGFA 1992</h2>

<h3>The single-occupant discount (s.11(1)(a))</h3>

<p><a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/14/section/11" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">Section 11(1)(a) of the Local Government Finance Act 1992</a> reduces the council tax by 25 per cent where there is only one resident in the dwelling, or where the only other residents are persons disregarded under Schedule 1.</p>

<p>The 25 per cent figure is statutory and fixed by section 11(3). It is not means-tested. It applies regardless of band. The single condition is that the dwelling is the resident's sole or main residence (the operative test under section 11(5)).</p>

<p>Schedule 1 disregards include:</p>

<ul>
<li>Full-time students (under section 4 and Schedule 1 paragraph 4).</li>
<li>Student nurses.</li>
<li>Apprentices.</li>
<li>Severely mentally impaired persons (under section 4 and Schedule 1 paragraph 2).</li>
<li>Persons in detention.</li>
<li>Persons resident in hospital or in a care home.</li>
<li>18 or 19 year olds in respect of whom child benefit is payable.</li>
<li>Members of religious communities.</li>
<li>Foreign-language assistants.</li>
<li>Members of visiting forces.</li>
<li>Diplomats with diplomatic immunity from UK taxation.</li>
</ul>

<p>The disregards collapse multi-resident households into "single-occupant" treatment for council tax purposes. A two-adult household where one adult is a full-time student is treated as a single-occupant household; the working adult is the only "non-disregarded resident"; the household gets the 25 per cent discount.</p>

<h3>The no-resident discount (s.11(2))</h3>

<p>Section 11(2) provides a 50 per cent no-resident discount where the dwelling has no resident, or where all residents are disregarded under Schedule 1. The 50 per cent figure is, however, subject to LA discretion. Under the LGFA 2012 reforms most LAs have:</p>

<ul>
<li>Eliminated the empty-property discount entirely, or</li>
<li>Reduced it to a short window (commonly one month from when the property became unoccupied).</li>
</ul>

<p>The historic 50 per cent no-resident discount is therefore not a reliable route in 2026. Where the LA has eliminated it, no discount applies. Where the LA retains a short window, the discount typically applies only for the first month of vacancy.</p>

<aside>
<p>The single-person discount under s.11(1)(a) is the most-claimed reduction in the council tax system. If you live alone, or only with a disregarded person (typically a full-time student spouse or partner), you should be claiming the 25 per cent discount. The application is free and direct to the LA.</p>

<h2>Route 2: Exemptions under SI 1992/558</h2>

<p><a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/1992/558" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">The Council Tax (Exempt Dwellings) Order 1992 (SI 1992/558)</a> lists classes of dwellings that are exempt from council tax entirely for the period the class applies. The classes run from A to W with specific conditions for each.</p>

<h3>Class N: dwelling occupied solely by full-time students</h3>

<p>The principal student exemption. Where every occupant of the dwelling is a full-time student (within the statutory definition), the dwelling is exempt: council tax bill is zero. The exemption is widely used in student housing, particularly in HMOs occupied by students sharing a kitchen.</p>

<p>The single most-common trip-wire: if one occupant is not a full-time student (a working housemate, a graduate working a temporary year, a non-student partner), Class N fails. The household reverts to standard council tax with single-person-discount treatment for the non-student resident if they are the only non-disregarded person, or to full council tax if there are multiple non-disregarded residents.</p>

<h3>Class W: annex occupied by a dependant relative</h3>

<p>An annex occupied by a dependant relative of the main-dwelling occupier, where the dependant is aged 65 or over, severely mentally impaired, or substantially disabled. The annex is treated as a separate dwelling for banding (where it is structurally separate) and the Class W exemption eliminates the annex council tax entirely. The main dwelling remains separately liable on its own band.</p>

<h3>Class M: HMO occupied solely by Schedule 1 disregarded persons</h3>

<p>Where the HMO (under SI 2023/1175 a single dwelling for council tax) is occupied solely by persons disregarded under Schedule 1 (typically a fully-student HMO), the Class M exemption applies. Note the interaction with SI 2023/1175 single-dwelling rule: the HMO is one dwelling, banded as one dwelling, and Class M operates at the dwelling level. See our <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/government-to-end-council-tax-on-hmo-rooms">HMO single-dwelling page</a> for the framework.</p>

<h3>Class F: dwelling unoccupied because the owner has died</h3>

<p>Up to 6 months after grant of probate or letters of administration, the dwelling is exempt. Class F is the post-death council-tax position for the bereaved family during the probate-pending and immediate post-grant period. The 6-month window is statutory; beyond it, the dwelling reverts to standard council tax with the empty-property and empty-homes-premium framework as applicable.</p>

<h3>Class E: resident moved to hospital or care home</h3>

<p>Where a dwelling is unoccupied because the previous resident has moved permanently to a hospital or care home, Class E applies. The exemption is open-ended in time while the resident remains in care.</p>

<h3>Other classes</h3>

<p>The full list also includes:</p>

<ul>
<li>Class B (unoccupied charity-owned property, up to 6 months).</li>
<li>Class D (resident in detention).</li>
<li>Class G (occupation prohibited by law).</li>
<li>Class H (held for a minister of religion).</li>
<li>Class K (left unoccupied by a student).</li>
<li>Class L (mortgagee in possession).</li>
<li>Class O (armed forces accommodation).</li>
<li>Class Q (left unoccupied by a bankrupt or trustee in bankruptcy).</li>
<li>Class S (occupied solely by persons under 18).</li>
<li>Class T (annex unable to be let separately due to a planning condition).</li>
<li>Class U (occupied solely by persons severely mentally impaired).</li>
</ul>

<p>Each class has specific conditions and an LA application process. Several classes (notably A and C) have been substantially abolished or made discretionary by the LGFA 2012 reform package; check the LA's current policy.</p>

<h2>Route 3: Disabled-band reduction under SI 1992/554</h2>

<p>One of the most under-claimed reductions in the council tax system. <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/1992/554" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">SI 1992/554 (the Council Tax (Reductions for Disabilities) Regulations 1992)</a> provides a reduction where a substantially and permanently disabled person resides in the dwelling AND one of three conditions applies:</p>

<ul>
<li>A room (other than a kitchen, bathroom or toilet) is predominantly used by and required by the disabled person (typically a downstairs bedroom adapted for wheelchair use, or a treatment room).</li>
<li>An additional bathroom or kitchen is required for meeting the needs of the disabled person.</li>
<li>There is sufficient floor space for use of a wheelchair indoors.</li>
</ul>

<p>The effect is that the dwelling is assessed at one band lower than its actual band. A Band E dwelling is assessed at Band D. A Band D dwelling is assessed at Band C. For Band A dwellings, the reduction is calculated as 5/9ths of the Band B charge (the standard band-relativity ratio applied to a Band A baseline).</p>

<p>The reduction applies regardless of income and regardless of the value of the property. The condition is that the disability is substantial and permanent, and that one of the three accommodation features is required for the disability. Medical evidence is needed for the application.</p>

<aside>
<p>The disabled-band reduction is widely under-claimed. Households with a wheelchair user, a downstairs bedroom adapted for wheelchair use, or an additional accessibility bathroom are often eligible but unaware of the route. The application is free and direct to the LA with medical evidence.</p>

<h2>Route 4: Banding challenge to the VOA under section 16 LGFA 1992</h2>

<p>The banding of every dwelling in England by reference to its 1 April 1991 value can produce results that the current owner believes are wrong. <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/14/section/16" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">Section 16 LGFA 1992</a> with the proposal regulations under SI 1992/290 provides the banding-challenge route to the Valuation Office Agency.</p>

<h3>When can you challenge?</h3>

<ul>
<li>Within 6 months of you first becoming the taxpayer for the property. This is the "new taxpayer" window.</li>
<li>Where one of the grounds in section 24 LGFA 1992 arises: a material reduction in the dwelling's value (typically following demolition or partial destruction); the commencement of business use; a material physical change in the locality affecting the dwelling's value.</li>
</ul>

<h3>How does the challenge work?</h3>

<p>Submit a proposal to the VOA via the gov.uk Check, challenge, appeal service. Set out the grounds, supply supporting evidence (typically the prices of comparable dwellings sold around 1 April 1991, evidence of any physical changes since 1991, evidence of the dwelling's specification at 1991). The VOA reviews and either amends the list or refuses.</p>

<p>If the VOA refuses, appeal to the <a href="https://valuationtribunal.gov.uk/council-tax-appeals/" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">Valuation Tribunal for England</a>. The Tribunal hearing is typically 6 to 18 months after the initial proposal.</p>

<p>Where the band is reduced, council tax is adjusted retrospectively from the date of the original (incorrect) banding decision, and the LA refunds overpayment. Where the band is increased, the LA bills the underpayment from the date of the change. Banding challenges should therefore only be made where there is real evidence supporting a lower band.</p>

<h2>Route 5: Council Tax Reduction Scheme under Schedule 1A LGFA 1992</h2>

<p>The CTR scheme replaced the centrally-administered Council Tax Benefit in 2013. Schedule 1A LGFA 1992 (inserted by the LGFA 2012) provides the framework. The scheme has two strands:</p>

<h3>Pensioner CTR</h3>

<p>Centrally-prescribed under <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2012/2885" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">SI 2012/2885 (the Council Tax Reduction Schemes (Prescribed Requirements) (England) Regulations 2012)</a>. Broadly equivalent to the abolished Council Tax Benefit. Pensioners on Pension Credit, or with low income, can receive a reduction of up to 100 per cent of their council tax bill. Eligibility is means-tested across income, capital, and household composition. The scheme is the same in every English LA.</p>

<h3>Working-age CTR</h3>

<p>Locally-determined. Each LA designs its own working-age CTR scheme within the LGFA 2012 framework. Schemes vary materially LA-by-LA:</p>

<ul>
<li>Some LAs operate a near-100 per cent scheme for low-income working-age households (broadly mirroring the pensioner scheme).</li>
<li>Many LAs require a minimum council tax contribution (often 10 to 30 per cent) even from the lowest-income households.</li>
<li>Capital thresholds, income tapers, household-composition rules vary.</li>
</ul>

<p>For a working-age household, apply via the specific LA's website. Eligibility is typically tied to receipt of Universal Credit, Income Support, JSA, ESA, or to a means-test on household income and capital.</p>

<h2>Route 6: Specific class-based reliefs</h2>

<p>Several specific reliefs sit outside the main framework:</p>

<h3>Dependant-relative annex exemption (Class W of SI 1992/558)</h3>

<p>Already covered above under exemptions. The annex council tax is eliminated entirely where the dependant relative is aged 65 or over, severely mentally impaired, or substantially disabled.</p>

<h3>Family-annex reduction</h3>

<p>Where an annex is occupied by a family member who is not within the Class W definition (for example a working adult child living in the annex), the annex council tax can be reduced by 50 per cent under the Council Tax (Reductions for Annexes) (England) Regulations 2013 (SI 2013/2977). The conditions: the annex must form part of a property where the main dwelling is occupied by a person who is a relative of the annex occupier; the annex must be used by the relative as their sole or main residence.</p>

<h2>The post-LURA 2023 surcharge framework</h2>

<p>One context point that affects the discount calculus. The Levelling-up and Regeneration Act 2023 added two new premium charges that work in the opposite direction to discounts:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Section 11B (empty-homes premium):</strong> reduced trigger to 12 months from 1 April 2024; premium up to 100 per cent in years 1 to 5 of vacancy.</li>
<li><strong>Section 11C (second-homes premium):</strong> inserted from 26 October 2023; adopted by participating LAs typically from 1 April 2025; premium up to 100 per cent on furnished second homes.</li>
</ul>

<p>The combined effect is that empty properties and second homes are now in surcharge territory, not discount territory. Any "reduce my council tax bill" strategy that relies on legacy empty-property discounts or second-home discounts is materially out of date. See our <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/is-increasing-council-tax-damaging-the-housing-market">council tax and housing market analysis</a> for the wider policy context.</p>

<h2>The scam warning</h2>

<p>A thriving cottage industry of firms offers paid council-tax-reduction services. Common patterns:</p>

<ul>
<li>Cold-call or unsolicited-mail approach offering to "review your council tax band" or "claim discounts you may be entitled to".</li>
<li>Fee charged on a percentage of any refund secured, sometimes 30 to 50 per cent.</li>
<li>The fee firm does no work that the taxpayer could not do directly.</li>
</ul>

<p>The legitimate reduction routes are all free and direct:</p>

<ul>
<li>Discount or exemption applications go to the LA's council-tax department. Free.</li>
<li>Banding challenges go to the VOA via the gov.uk Check, challenge, appeal service. Free.</li>
<li>CTR applications go to the LA via its CTR application form. Free.</li>
</ul>

<p>No third-party fee is needed for any of the routes on this page. <a href="https://www.citizensadvice.org.uk/housing/council-tax/" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">Citizens Advice</a> publishes a warning on the council-tax-challenge cold-call industry. If a firm is asking for a fee or commission to claim a reduction on your behalf, the same outcome is available through the LA or VOA at no cost.</p>

<p>The scam-firm pattern is the single most-common reason households end up paying for council tax advice they did not need. The LA's council-tax department, the VOA's gov.uk service, and the LA's CTR application are all free and direct.</p>

<h2>Worked examples</h2>

<h3>Single-person discount: widowed pensioner in Band C</h3>

<p>Patel-pensioner is recently widowed and now lives alone in a Band C dwelling. Pre-discount annual council tax: 1,950 pounds. After the s.11(1)(a) single-person discount (25 per cent): 1,463 pounds. Patel-pensioner also has a low income (state pension plus small private pension); she applies for pensioner CTR under SI 2012/2885 and qualifies for a further reduction of approximately 700 pounds depending on her exact income. Final annual council tax: approximately 760 pounds.</p>

<h3>Student-disregard: Band D household with one full-time student</h3>

<p>Singh-household consists of one working adult and one adult full-time MBA student. Band D annual council tax: 2,170 pounds. The student is disregarded under Schedule 1 paragraph 4. The working adult is the only "non-disregarded resident", so the household qualifies for the single-person discount. Final annual council tax: 1,628 pounds (saving 542 pounds annually).</p>

<h3>Disabled-band reduction: Band E household with wheelchair adaptation</h3>

<p>The Kumar household is in a Band E semi-detached property. One household member is a wheelchair user, with a downstairs adapted bedroom and an adapted ground-floor bathroom. The dwelling qualifies for the disabled-band reduction under SI 1992/554. Council tax is assessed at one band lower (Band D). Band E annual council tax was 2,660 pounds; Band D assessment is 2,170 pounds. Saving: 490 pounds annually.</p>

<h3>Class N student exemption: 4-bed shared house</h3>

<p>A 4-bedroom shared house occupied solely by full-time university students. Class N exemption applies. The dwelling is exempt from council tax: bill is zero. The Class N exemption operates at the dwelling level (and post-SI 2023/1175 the HMO is a single dwelling) so a fully-student HMO has a zero council tax bill for the period of the all-student occupancy. If one student leaves and a non-student moves in, Class N fails immediately and the dwelling reverts to standard council tax with single-person-discount treatment for the non-student.</p>

<h3>Annex relief: Class W dependant relative</h3>

<p>An annex within the curtilage of the main dwelling is occupied by the homeowner's 78-year-old mother. The annex is separately banded at Band A. Class W applies because the dependant relative is aged 65 or over. The annex is exempt from council tax: bill on the annex is zero. The main dwelling remains separately liable on its own band.</p>

<h2>How to apply</h2>

<p>The application path depends on the route:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Discounts and exemptions:</strong> apply to the LA's council-tax department, typically online via the LA website. Supporting evidence varies by route.</li>
<li><strong>Disabled-band reduction:</strong> apply to the LA with medical evidence (typically a GP letter or other clinical evidence of the disability and the accommodation requirement).</li>
<li><strong>Banding challenge:</strong> apply to the VOA via the <a href="https://www.gov.uk/challenge-council-tax-band" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">gov.uk Check, challenge, appeal service</a>. The window is 6 months from becoming the taxpayer or from a section 24 ground arising.</li>
<li><strong>CTR scheme:</strong> apply to the LA via the LA's CTR application form. Different from the standard council tax discount route.</li>
</ul>

<p>Processing times vary. Cooperative discount applications typically resolve within 28 days. Banding challenges typically take 6 to 18 months. CTR applications typically resolve within 4 to 8 weeks.</p>

<h2>Backdating and time limits</h2>

<p>Statutory entitlements (s.11 discount, SI 1992/558 exemption, SI 1992/554 disability reduction) are typically backdated to the date the entitlement arose, subject to the LA's evidential satisfaction. The council tax collection limitation period under Schedule 4 LGFA 1992 is typically 6 years.</p>

<p>CTR scheme backdate windows are shorter and vary LA-by-LA. Many working-age CTR schemes allow only one or three months of backdate. Apply promptly to avoid losing entitlement to time-bar effects.</p>

<p>For the banding challenge, the 6-month window from becoming the taxpayer is strict. Outside that window, the only routes are the section 24 grounds (material reduction in value, commencement of business use, physical change in locality). Many readers miss the 6-month new-taxpayer window because they did not realise it existed.</p>
