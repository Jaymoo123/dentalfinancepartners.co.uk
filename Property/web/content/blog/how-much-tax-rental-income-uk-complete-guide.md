<p>Understanding <strong>how much tax you'll pay on rental income in the UK</strong> is crucial for every property investor. Your <strong>rental income tax bill</strong> depends on several factors: your total income, allowable expenses, mortgage interest restrictions, and significant changes coming in April 2027.</p>

<p>This guide explains exactly how <strong>tax on rent received</strong> is calculated, what rates apply, and how to minimise your liability legally. The tax landscape for landlords is changing dramatically, so getting the calculations right matters more than ever.</p>

<h2>Current Rental Income Tax Rates 2025/26</h2>

<p>For the 2025/26 tax year, rental income is taxed as part of your total income using standard income tax rates:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Basic rate (20%):</strong> Income between £12,571-£50,270</li>
<li><strong>Higher rate (40%):</strong> Income between £50,271-£125,140</li>
<li><strong>Additional rate (45%):</strong> Income above £125,140</li>
</ul>

<p>However, this changes dramatically from April 2027. From that point, property income will be taxed at separate rates: 22% basic rate, 42% higher rate, and 47% additional rate. This means landlords will pay more tax on their rental profits than other forms of income.</p>

<h2>How Rental Income Tax Is Calculated</h2>

<p>Your <strong>tax on rent received</strong> isn't calculated on gross rental income. HMRC taxes your net rental profit after deducting allowable expenses and applying Section 24 mortgage interest restrictions.</p>

<h3>Step 1: Calculate Gross Rental Income</h3>

<p>Include all rental income received during the tax year:</p>

<ul>
<li>Monthly rent payments</li>
<li>Deposits retained for damages</li>
<li>Letting fees paid by tenants</li>
<li>Premium payments for lease assignments</li>
</ul>

<h3>Step 2: Deduct Allowable Expenses</h3>

<p>You can deduct legitimate property expenses from your rental income:</p>

<ul>
<li>Property management fees</li>
<li>Repairs and maintenance</li>
<li>Insurance premiums</li>
<li>Safety certificates (gas, electrical, EPC)</li>
<li>Professional fees (legal, accounting)</li>
<li>Advertising for tenants</li>
<li>Council tax (if landlord responsibility)</li>
</ul>

<p>For a comprehensive list of what you can claim, see our guide on <a href="/blog/section-24-and-tax-relief/landlord-tax-deductions-uk-2026-complete-list">landlord tax deductions</a>.</p>

<h3>Step 3: Apply Section 24 Restrictions</h3>

<p>Mortgage interest is no longer fully deductible. Instead, you receive a basic rate tax credit (20%) on your mortgage interest payments. This significantly increases the effective tax rate for leveraged landlords.</p>

<p>For detailed information on how this works, read our <a href="/blog/section-24-and-tax-relief/section-24-tax-relief-complete-guide">complete Section 24 guide</a>.</p>

<h2>Worked Example: Rental Income Tax Calculation</h2>

<p>Let's calculate the <strong>rental income tax bill</strong> for Sarah, who owns two buy-to-let properties and has a full-time job earning £45,000.</p>

<h3>Sarah's Property Income 2025/26:</h3>
<ul>
<li>Gross rental income: £18,000</li>
<li>Allowable expenses: £3,500</li>
<li>Mortgage interest: £8,000</li>
<li>Net rental profit (before Section 24): £6,500</li>
</ul>

<h3>Tax Calculation:</h3>
<p><strong>Step 1:</strong> Add back mortgage interest for income tax calculation<br>
Taxable rental income: £6,500 + £8,000 = £14,500</p>

<p><strong>Step 2:</strong> Calculate total income<br>
Employment income: £45,000<br>
Rental income: £14,500<br>
Total income: £59,500</p>

<p><strong>Step 3:</strong> Apply income tax rates<br>
£50,270 at 20% = £10,054<br>
£9,230 (£59,500 - £50,270) at 40% = £3,692<br>
Total income tax: £13,746</p>

<p><strong>Step 4:</strong> Deduct Section 24 tax credit<br>
Mortgage interest tax credit: £8,000 × 20% = £1,600<br>
Final tax liability: £13,746 - £1,600 = £12,146</p>

<p>Without rental income, Sarah would pay £6,454 in income tax. Her <strong>additional tax on rent received</strong> is therefore £5,692 on £6,500 of actual rental profit - an effective rate of 87.5%.</p>

<h2>The April 2027 Property Tax Changes</h2>

<p>From April 2027, the tax calculation becomes more straightforward but potentially more expensive. Property income will be taxed at dedicated rates:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Basic rate landlords:</strong> 22% on rental profits</li>
<li><strong>Higher rate landlords:</strong> 42% on rental profits</li>
<li><strong>Additional rate landlords:</strong> 47% on rental profits</li>
</ul>

<p>Using Sarah's example above, under the 2027 system she would pay 42% on her £6,500 rental profit = £2,730. This is significantly less than the current effective rate of 87.5%.</p>

<h2>Factors That Affect Your Rental Income Tax Bill</h2>

<h3>Your Total Income Level</h3>

<p>The more you earn from all sources, the higher your marginal tax rate on rental income. A basic rate taxpayer pays far less <strong>tax on rent received</strong> than someone in the additional rate band.</p>

<h3>Property Type</h3>

<p>Different property types have varying tax implications:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Residential BTL:</strong> Subject to Section 24 restrictions</li>
<li><strong>Commercial property:</strong> Full mortgage interest relief still available</li>
<li><strong>HMOs:</strong> Higher expenses but potential business rates liability</li>
<li><strong>Holiday lets:</strong> Furnished Holiday Lettings regime abolished from April 2025</li>
</ul>

<h3>Leverage and Mortgage Interest</h3>

<p>Highly leveraged landlords are hit hardest by Section 24. Cash buyers or those with small mortgages face lower effective tax rates on their <strong>rental income tax bill</strong>.</p>

<h3>Incorporation Status</h3>

<p>Limited companies don't face Section 24 restrictions and pay corporation tax at 19% (up to £250k profit) or 25% (above £250k). This can result in significant tax savings for portfolio landlords. Learn more about <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/buy-to-let-limited-company-complete-guide-uk">buy-to-let limited companies</a>.</p>

<h2>How to Reduce Your Rental Income Tax</h2>

<h3>Maximise Allowable Expenses</h3>

<p>Keep detailed records of all property-related expenses. Many landlords miss legitimate deductions that could reduce their <strong>tax on rent received</strong>.</p>

<h3>Consider Incorporation</h3>

<p>Moving properties into a limited company can provide significant tax savings, especially for higher-rate taxpayers with mortgaged properties. However, there are costs involved including SDLT on the transfer.</p>

<h3>Spouse/Civil Partner Planning</h3>

<p>If your partner has unused basic rate band, consider transferring property ownership to balance income and reduce the overall <strong>rental income tax bill</strong>.</p>

<h3>Pension Contributions</h3>

<p>Personal pension contributions can reduce your adjusted net income, potentially keeping you in a lower tax band. This is particularly valuable for landlords pushed into higher rate tax by rental income.</p>

<h2>Record Keeping and Compliance</h2>

<p>From April 2026, <a href="/blog/making-tax-digital-mtd/making-tax-digital-landlords-april-2026-deadline">Making Tax Digital becomes mandatory</a> for landlords with gross property income over £10,000. You'll need to:</p>

<ul>
<li>Keep digital records of all income and expenses</li>
<li>Submit quarterly updates to HMRC</li>
<li>File an annual property business summary</li>
</ul>

<p>Proper record keeping is essential for calculating your exact <strong>rental income tax bill</strong> and claiming all available deductions.</p>
