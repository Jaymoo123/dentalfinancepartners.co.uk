  - question: "How does GROB on a gifted let property differ from GROB on a gifted family home?"
    answer: "The statutory rule is identical (s.102 Finance Act 1986), but the typical fact pattern that triggers a reservation is different. On a family-home gift, the reservation is almost always continued occupation by the donor. On a let-property gift, the donor never occupied the property to begin with, so the reservation is found instead in cash flow (keeping any share of the rent), in continuing donor liability under the mortgage that the children indemnify against, or in continued operational control where the donor manages the property and makes the decisions that the legal owner ought to be making. The s.102(1)(b) requirement that the property be enjoyed to the entire exclusion of the donor and of any benefit by contract or otherwise reads onto a let asset through its income stream rather than through its walls.   - question: "If I gift my BTL to my children but keep collecting the rent into my account, what happens?"
    answer: "You have made a textbook reservation. The s.102(1)(b) test is breached the moment any post-gift rent ends up in the donor's hands or under the donor's control. HMRC's enquiry will not stop at the legal title transfer or the deed of gift; the question is where the rent actually went and who declared it for income tax. The property is treated under s.102(3) as remaining in your estate for IHT at its open-market value at the date of death, the lifetime gift is ineffective for IHT purposes, and the seven-year PET clock that you thought was running is running on a void gift. The same applies where the rent passes through the children's account on paper but is then transferred or paid to the donor monthly. HMRC's manual at IHTM14333 onwards treats the substance of the cash flow as decisive."
  - question: "Can I gift a BTL and stay on the mortgage as guarantor without triggering GROB?"
    answer: "It depends on the substance of the guarantee arrangement. A bare guarantor obligation by itself, with the children making all the mortgage payments from their account and the lender treating the children as the primary borrowers, is usually not a reservation, because the donor enjoys no benefit. The position changes where the children's payments are being subsidised by the donor, where the children indemnify the donor against any call under the guarantee in a way that puts the donor in a better position than at the gift date, or where the donor remains as a named borrower on the mortgage and continues to bear actual repayment liability. HMRC will look at the practical and contractual position together: the lender's documentation, the actual payment flows, and any side arrangement between donor and donee. If the donor is in any way better off after the gift than a stranger guarantor would be, the reservation argument is on the table."
  - question: "What if my child cannot get a mortgage on the gifted property and I have to remain a joint borrower?"
    answer: "The cleanest GROB position is to refinance the property fully into the children's names before the gift, with the donor coming off the mortgage entirely. Where the child cannot qualify on income or affordability and the donor has to remain a joint borrower, the gift is at high risk of being a continuing GROB because the donor's name remains on the legal liability. HMRC's view, supported by tribunal cases on home-gift facts, is that the test looks at whether the donor enjoys any continuing benefit from the property, and being a named borrower on a mortgage secured against the property is hard to characterise as anything other than a continuing connection. Alternative routes worth exploring before the gift: a longer planning horizon during which the child's borrowing capacity is built up, a sale-and-replacement structure where the donor receives cash and the children buy on their own borrowing, or a Family Investment Company route where the donor remains a director without holding the equity (see our FIC IHT framing for the value-freeze comparison)."
  - question: "Can I gift a BTL and still act as managing agent for the children?"
    answer: "Possibly yes if the role is properly arms-length, almost certainly no if it is not. The bright line is the management fee. Where the donor charges the donees a market-rate management fee (typically 8% to 12% of the rent for a single-property arrangement, less for portfolio managers, documented in a written management agreement that resembles what a professional letting agent would issue), and the donor declares the fee as trading or property-management income on self-assessment, HMRC will usually accept that the donor enjoys no benefit from the property itself; the donor is being paid for a service. Where the donor charges no fee and provides the management as a continuing favour, the donor's role looks like a constructive reservation: the donor remains the de facto controller of the asset. Where the donor charges an above-market fee, the excess over market rate is a reservation by way of over-paid management. The documentary trail (the management agreement, the time records, the fee invoices, the donee's bank statements showing payments to the donor) is what HMRC will request on enquiry."
  - question: "Does receiving a small management fee from my children create GROB?"
    answer: "At a genuine market rate, no; above market rate, yes to the extent of the excess. Section 102(1)(b) speaks of any benefit to the donor by contract or otherwise, and an over-paid management fee is a benefit by contract. The test HMRC applies is comparative: what would a third-party letting agent charge for the same property in the same location with the same scope of services? If the donor's fee is in line with that benchmark, the fee is consideration for a service and the property itself is enjoyed to the exclusion of the donor. If the fee is materially above benchmark, the difference is a continuing benefit derived from the property and the reservation argument is open. Practical discipline: get two or three written quotes from local letting agents at the time of the gift, set the fee at or below the lowest quote, review every two to three years against fresh quotes, document each review."
  - question: "What if I want to keep collecting half the rent and gift only half the property?"
    answer: "A gift of an undivided share in the property, with the donor retaining the other share and continuing to take their share of the rent, is normally clean for s.102 because the donor is keeping what the donor kept; the gift is only of the gifted share. The reservation question is then about the gifted share alone: does the donor enjoy any benefit from the donee's share beyond the donor's own retained share? If the donor takes only their own legal share of the rent (set against the joint-ownership percentage), reports it on their own self-assessment, bears their share of costs (mortgage interest on their share, repairs proportionate to ownership, ground rent in proportion), and the donee bears the donee's share of the same costs and reports the donee's share of rent, the half-gift is usually outside s.102. Where the donor takes the donee's share of the rent (whether by side agreement, undocumented arrangement, or because the donee deposits all rents into a joint account that the donor controls), the donor has reserved a benefit and the gifted share is back in the donor's estate."
  - question: "How does the Schedule 20 paragraph 6 carve-out work for a let property?"
    answer: "The paragraph 6 carve-out works clearly for shared occupation of a residence; for a let property it is much less helpful. The carve-out requires both donor and donee to occupy the land after the gift, and to share the running costs proportionately. A let property by definition is occupied by a third-party tenant, not by the donor and donee, so the central condition is not met. Paragraph 6 may have application in the unusual case where donor and donee hold an undivided share in a property as joint landlords (each is a legal owner, each takes their proportionate share of the rent, each bears their proportionate share of the costs) and the donor was a co-owner before the gift to begin with. Even then, the structure is rarely simpler or safer than a clean undivided-share gift with proper income and cost allocation."
  - question: "When does Pre-Owned Assets Tax apply instead of GROB on a let-property gift?"
    answer: "POAT under Schedule 15 Finance Act 2004 applies where the donor escapes s.102 on the gift itself but still enjoys a benefit derived from the gifted asset. For let property, the most common POAT trigger is the cash-gift-then-property-purchase route: the donor gifts cash to the children, the children buy a BTL with the cash, the children let it out, and some portion of the post-let cash flow returns to the donor by side arrangement. Section 102 does not catch the original gift because the original gift was of cash. POAT applies an annual income tax charge on the donor based on the deemed market rent of the property in proportion to the gifted cash funding, even though the property itself was never owned by the donor. The donor can elect on form IHT500 by 31 January after the tax year the benefit first arises to be treated as a GROB instead; the property then sits in the donor's estate for IHT and the annual POAT charge falls away. The election is irrevocable for the period it covers."
  - question: "Do I need to refinance the mortgage fully into the children's names before the gift?"
    answer: "If the children can qualify on their own income and affordability, yes, refinance fully and then make the gift; that is the cleanest position. If the children cannot qualify, the GROB risk on a continuing joint-borrower structure is high and worth quantifying before the gift goes ahead. Two intermediate routes are sometimes considered. First, a phased plan in which the children build up borrowing capacity over a defined window (typically two to four years through job stability, deposit accumulation, and credit history) and the gift waits for the refinance moment. Second, a smaller initial gift of an undivided share (say 25%) where the children share the mortgage liability proportionately to that share, with further share gifts following as the children's borrowing capacity grows. Both routes have CGT and SDLT implications that need running through; the SDLT charge on a transfer of an undivided share encumbered by a mortgage is the practical sting (HMRC SDLTM04042 onwards on assumption of consideration by transferee)."
  - question: "What documentation will HMRC actually request in a GROB enquiry on a let-property gift?"
    answer: "On a typical enquiry the inspector will request, in roughly this order: the deed of gift or transfer with stamp evidence, the contemporaneous mortgage documentation showing the lender's position on the donor's and donee's roles, the rental bank account statements for the 12 months either side of the gift showing where the rent actually flowed, the donee's self-assessment returns showing the rental income reported and the income tax paid, any management agreement between donor and donee with the fee schedule, contemporaneous market-rate evidence for the management fee where one was charged, the tenancy agreements and rent receipts for the period either side of the gift, the council tax and utility records showing the donee as bill-payer or arrangements with tenants, and any correspondence between donor and donee evidencing the gift's intent. A weak documentary trail is the single most common cause of an adverse GROB finding; the s.102 test is fact-led and the facts live in the documents."
  - question: "How does GROB on a direct let-property gift compare to gifting FIC growth shares?"
    answer: "The route is different, but the IHT logic overlaps. On a direct property gift, the donor passes the legal title and economic interest in a specific asset; GROB risk lives in cash flow, mortgage liability and management, as set out on this page. On a FIC growth-share gift, the donor retains preference shares with a frozen-value coupon and gifts growth shares to the next generation; the 7-year PET clock starts on the growth-share gift, not on FIC formation. GROB risk on the FIC route lives in the underlying property that the FIC holds: if the FIC's main asset is a property the donor occupies or uses personally, s.102 can read through to the donor's continuing benefit even via the corporate wrapper. The CGT position diverges: direct property gift is a deemed disposal at market value under s.17 TCGA 1992 with no holdover for non-business BTL; FIC growth-share gift is a deemed disposal at market value too, but minority-discounted valuation can keep the CGT bill smaller.   - question: "What is the best documentary discipline to support a clean let-property gift?"
    answer: "Six items, all dated at or close to the gift date, and all kept on file for at least seven years after death (longer is better). One, a contemporaneous valuation of the property at the gift date by an RICS-qualified surveyor; this supports the s.17 TCGA 1992 deemed-disposal value for the donor's CGT and the gift value for the donor's IHT PET schedule. Two, the deed of gift or transfer, properly drafted, with no side letters reserving any interest in the property to the donor. <p>Section 102 Finance Act 1986 is the IHT anti-avoidance rule that catches the most common piece of bad amateur estate planning on the family home: gift the house, carry on living there, hope the seven-year PET clock runs the gift out of the estate. The rule says no, and the home stays in the estate at its open-market value at the date of death. Our Wave 2 walkthrough of <a href="/blog/landlord-tax-essentials/iht-gifts-with-reservation-of-benefit-property">GROB on Property Gifts: s.102 FA 1986</a> takes that family-home variant through the statute subsection by subsection.</p>

<p>This page is the let-property companion. The fact pattern that brings landlords here is different: parent owns a tenanted buy-to-let, the children are in their thirties, the parent gifts the property to the children intending the seven-year clock to do its work, but somewhere in the post-gift arrangement the parent reserves a thread of benefit. Sometimes the parent quietly keeps some or all of the rent. Sometimes the parent remains on the mortgage as a borrower or guarantor that the children indemnify. Sometimes the parent stays on as effective managing agent without a proper fee, calling all the operational decisions while the children's role on paper is to hold the legal title. Each of these is the let-property version of the reservation, and each puts the property back into the donor's estate at death under s.102(3).</p>

<p>Below, the cash-flow test, three triggers worked through three personas, the narrow Schedule 20 paragraph 6 carve-out applied to a let asset, the Pre-Owned Assets Tax back-stop and the IHT500 election, and the documentation discipline that an HMRC enquiry will actually test against.</p>

<h2>The cash-flow test: how s.102 reads onto a let property</h2>

<p>The statute does not draw a distinction between residential and let property. Section 102(1)(b) requires that the gifted property be enjoyed to the entire exclusion, or virtually to the entire exclusion, of the donor and of any benefit to the donor by contract or otherwise. On a family-home gift, that test reads through the front door: did the donor continue to live there. On a let-property gift, it reads through the bank statements: did the donor continue to enjoy any part of the income stream, retain any economic liability, or exercise any continuing control.</p>

<p>HMRC's Inheritance Tax Manual makes the point explicitly. IHTM14333 onwards walks through the "exclusion of donor" question for income-producing assets, and the substance of the cash flow is decisive. The legal title transfer is a starting point, not an answer. The deed of gift is the cover sheet. What HMRC wants to know is where the money went after the gift, who declared it for income tax, and what continuing financial relationship the donor and donee maintained in respect of the property.</p>

<p>That gives three lines of enquiry that come up repeatedly in practice, and three corresponding triggers for s.102 on a let property:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Trigger one: continuing rent receipt.</strong> Any post-gift rent that ends up in the donor's hands, or under the donor's effective control, or is paid into a joint account where the donor is a signatory, is a benefit by contract or otherwise. The trigger does not require the whole rent; a partial share is enough to put the gifted share back into the estate.</li>
<li><strong>Trigger two: continuing donor liability under the mortgage.</strong> Where the donor remains a named borrower or guarantor on the mortgage and the children indemnify the donor against any call under that liability, the donor has retained an economic position in the property that a stranger would not have. HMRC will read the mortgage documentation, the lender's correspondence, the actual payment flows and any side indemnity between donor and donee together.</li>
<li><strong>Trigger three: continuing operational control by the donor.</strong> Where the donor remains the effective decision-maker (signs the tenancy renewals, instructs the repairs, holds the keys, deals with the letting agent) without charging a market-rate management fee, the donor is enjoying a benefit derived from the property in the form of continuing influence. The line is not crossed by a one-off conversation or a relative giving advice; it is crossed where the donor remains the practical landlord and the donee is the legal landlord on paper only.</li>
</ul>

<p>None of these triggers requires the donor to have any direct enjoyment of the property as a building. The donor has never set foot in it, never stays there, never uses it. The reservation lives in the income, the liability, or the control. That is the difference from the family-home GROB pattern and the reason the analysis sits on a separate page.</p>

<h2>Worked example one: the share-of-rent trip</h2>

<p>The Khan-estate persona. Mr Khan owns a BTL flat in Reading worth £290,000 at the gift date, generating £14,400 of annual rent against a £170,000 outstanding mortgage that he pays from the rent net of agent and insurance costs. He gifts the flat to his daughter at age 67, intending the PET to clear the seven-year clock. The lender is paid off and the legal title transferred to her. The agent's instructions are updated to credit the rent to the daughter's bank account. Six months later, the daughter starts transferring £600 a month to her father's account "to help with bills", continuing for the next eight years.</p>

<p>Mr Khan dies aged 75, eight years after the gift. The family expect the property to be outside the estate (seven-year PET clock cleared). HMRC's enquiry traces the post-gift bank flows, identifies the £600 monthly transfers, and treats them as a continuing benefit derived from the property. The transfers track roughly half the net rent and are described in the daughter's notes as "Dad's share". Under s.102(1)(b), the reservation has been continuous from the gift date to the date of death. Under s.102(3), the property is in Mr Khan's estate at its open-market value at the date of death (now £370,000). Roughly £370,000 is added back into the estate; depending on the rest of the estate's value and the available nil-rate bands, the IHT exposure is in the range of £100,000 to £148,000 that the gift was intended to remove.</p>

<p>The thread to notice is how little it took to reserve the benefit. The daughter held legal title, the lender treated her as the borrower, the rental tax return was in her name, the agent corresponded with her, and the documentary trail above the bank account looked clean. The monthly cash transfer was modest in absolute terms but it tracked the rent in proportion, in timing, and in narrative. The whole gift collapsed on the cash flow, not on the title.</p>

<aside>
<p>If a gift of a let property is in your plan and any post-gift cash flow runs back to the donor, the seven-year clock is running on a void gift.</p>
<p>The position is fixable in lifetime if it is caught early; far harder for executors to mitigate after the fact. We work alongside specialist estate-planning advisers who can model the position and find the cleanest remediation route.</p>
</aside>

<h2>Worked example two: the mortgage-indemnity trap</h2>

<p>The O'Brien-estate persona. Ms O'Brien owns two BTL terraced houses in Coventry, total value £420,000 at the gift date, with a combined mortgage balance of £240,000 held jointly with her late husband's estate (now in her sole name). She wants to gift both properties to her son, aged 32, but he cannot qualify for a £240,000 BTL mortgage on his own income. The plan: she transfers legal title to him, the lender consents to him being added as a joint borrower without releasing her, and a side agreement records that he indemnifies her against any call on the joint borrowing.</p>

<p>On the face of the documentation, the gift is clean: legal title transferred, the son named on the mortgage, an indemnity in his favour. The rent flows to his bank account and he reports it on self-assessment. He pays the mortgage from the rent.</p>

<p>The position fails the s.102 test for two reasons. First, Ms O'Brien remains a named borrower on a mortgage secured against the gifted properties; the lender's position is that her assets stand behind the debt. A stranger guarantor with no connection to the property would not normally be on the legal liability, and the donor's continuing presence on the borrowing reads as a continuing economic interest in the asset. Second, the indemnity in her favour is itself a benefit by contract within s.102(1)(b): she enjoys protection against the mortgage call that is funded, ultimately, by the income from the gifted property. HMRC's view on similar facts in the home-gift case law has been that the donor's enjoyment of indemnity protection derived from the gifted asset is a reservation.</p>

<p>If Ms O'Brien dies within the relevant period, both properties (now worth, say, £510,000) come back into her estate under s.102(3). The IHT exposure on the death-date value can be on the order of £130,000 to £200,000 depending on the available nil-rate bands. The cleaner alternatives that should have been considered before the gift: refinance the properties into the son's name only (where his income supports it, with a multi-year build-up if not), reduce the gift to an undivided share matching the son's borrowing capacity with further share gifts to follow, sell the properties and gift the net cash proceeds (with POAT then in scope if he buys replacement property the donor uses), or restructure into a Family Investment Company with the donor retaining preference shares.</p>

<h2>Worked example three: full transfer with management remaining</h2>

<p>The Lewis-estate persona. Mr Lewis owns five BTL flats in Cardiff, total value £980,000 at the gift date, no mortgage debt, generating £52,000 of annual rent. He gifts the portfolio to his two daughters in equal shares at age 70. The legal title transfers cleanly, the lender position is moot (no debt to assume), the rent flows to the daughters' bank accounts and they report the rental income on self-assessment. So far, so clean.</p>

<p>The problem is operational. Mr Lewis was the de facto manager of the portfolio for fifteen years before the gift. He continues to be the de facto manager after the gift: he handles the letting agent relationship, sources and approves the repair contractors, makes the rent-review decisions, deals with tenant disputes, signs the tenancy renewals (the daughters give him power of attorney). He does this for no fee. His daughters describe him in conversation as "still running the portfolio" and refer most decisions to him.</p>

<p>HMRC's enquiry, prompted by an unrelated routine review of the daughters' rental returns, asks how the portfolio is managed and quickly identifies Mr Lewis's continuing operational role. The inspector's argument: the donor retains an economic benefit derived from the property in the form of continuing influence and control, exercised in his own interest as much as in the daughters'. The absence of a market-rate management fee converts a service into a continuing benefit by contract or otherwise within s.102(1)(b).</p>

<p>Section 102 then re-includes the gifted portfolio in Mr Lewis's estate. The fix, identifiable in advance, would have been one of three: a proper market-rate management agreement between donor and donees (typically 8% to 12% of the gross rent for a five-flat portfolio, with periodic review and proper documentation), an arms-length handover to a third-party letting agent immediately on the gift date, or a clean step-back where the daughters take operational responsibility (with or without their own agent) and Mr Lewis's role reduces to ordinary family advice.</p>

<aside>
<p>Operational control is the trigger that surprises most landlords. Cash flow is intuitive; mortgage liability is intuitive; continuing to "run the portfolio" the day after the gift often feels too normal to be a reservation.</p>
<p>If lifetime gifting is in your plan, the operational handover needs as much thought as the legal title transfer. Get in touch via the form at the foot of the page for a specialist review of the proposed gift structure.</p>
</aside>

<h2>The Sch 20 para 6 carve-out applied to let property</h2>

<p>Schedule 20 paragraph 6 FA 1986 carves out the shared-occupation case from the s.102 reservation rule, on three cumulative conditions: donor and donee both occupy the land after the gift; the donor receives no benefit (beyond a negligible one) provided by or at the expense of the donee in connection with the gift; the donor bears their share of the cost of running the property proportionate to use. The carve-out reads cleanly onto a co-occupied family home where parent and adult child both live in the gifted property thereafter.</p>

<p>It does not read cleanly onto a let property. A let property is by definition occupied by a third-party tenant, not by the donor and donee. The first condition (donor and donee both occupy) is not met where the gifted asset is a tenanted property. The paragraph 6 route is therefore not generally available to let-property gifts. The narrow case where it could in principle have application is the unusual one in which the donor and donee already hold the property in joint legal ownership and the gift is of the donor's undivided share, with both donor and donee continuing as co-landlords each taking their share of the rent and bearing their share of the costs. Even there, paragraph 6's "both occupy" condition is hard to satisfy on a tenanted property: occupation in the statute is read in the residential sense, not in the legal-ownership sense.</p>

<p>In practice, for the great majority of let-property gifts, paragraph 6 is not the analytical route. The cleaner analysis is whether the gifted share is enjoyed to the entire exclusion of the donor (the s.102(1)(b) primary test) by reference to the cash flow, the mortgage position, and the management role.</p>

<h2>Pre-Owned Assets Tax as the back-stop</h2>

<p>The Pre-Owned Assets Tax regime in Schedule 15 Finance Act 2004 is the income-tax catch for arrangements that escape s.102 on the gift itself but still leave the donor enjoying a benefit derived from the gifted asset. On a family-home gift, the canonical POAT case is the cash-gift route: donor gifts cash, donee buys the home, donor moves in.</p>

<p>On a let-property gift, the analogous POAT pattern is the cash-gift-into-let-property route. Donor gifts £400,000 of cash to two adult children, the children pool the cash to buy a £400,000 BTL house in their joint names, the children let the house out, and some portion of the post-let cash flow returns to the donor by an undocumented family arrangement. Section 102 does not catch the original gift (the gift was of cash, not of the property; the donor never owned the house). POAT applies an annual income tax charge on the donor based on the deemed market rent of the property in proportion to the gifted cash funding, with a £5,000 de minimis below which no charge arises and a five-yearly indexation of the deemed rent.</p>

<p>The donor's election out of POAT into GROB is on form IHT500 by 31 January after the tax year in which the benefit first arises. The election removes the annual income tax charge but puts the property into the donor's estate for IHT under s.102. The trade-off is age-and-survival dependent: for a donor in their early 60s in good health, the long POAT income-tax bill (potentially 20-plus years of charges) usually exceeds the eventual IHT cost, so the election is often the right move; for a donor in their late 70s with health concerns, the few years of POAT may be cheaper than full IHT on a property in the estate at death.</p>

<h2>The clean direct-gift route and the FIC alternative</h2>

<p>The clean direct-gift route for a let property is the one in which the donor passes the legal title without retaining any thread of benefit. Mortgage refinanced fully into the donee's name. Rental bank account changed to the donee. Tenancy agreements re-signed by the donee. Self-assessment rental return filed by the donee. No management role retained by the donor without a market-rate fee. The seven-year PET clock then runs from the gift date; if the donor survives seven years, the property is fully outside the estate; if the donor dies between three and seven years, taper relief under s.7(4) IHTA 1984 reduces the IHT payable.</p>

<p>The CGT cost of the gift is the trade-off and needs to be modelled before the gift goes ahead. A gift of a let property between connected persons is a deemed disposal at open-market value under s.17 TCGA 1992, with no holdover relief under s.165 because non-business BTL is not "business assets" for s.165 purposes. The donor pays CGT on the gain (deemed proceeds minus base cost) at 18% or 24% depending on their marginal-rate position for the tax year, due via the 60-day return for UK residential property. The donee's base cost is reset to the gift-date market value. Our companion page on the <a href="/blog/landlord-tax-essentials/iht-lifetime-gifts-7-year-rule-property-taper">7-Year Rule and Property Gifting</a> walks the PET mechanics through alongside this CGT cost.</p>

<p>The Family Investment Company route is the structural alternative. The donor transfers the property portfolio into a FIC at open-market value, takes preference shares with a fixed-coupon dividend (frozen value), and gifts growth shares to the next generation as PETs. The seven-year clock starts on the growth-share gift, not on the FIC formation. The CGT position on the share gift is a deemed disposal at market value (no s.165 holdover for an investment FIC) but minority-discounted valuation typically keeps the CGT bill below the equivalent direct-property gift. The IHT framing comparison and the GROB risk on the underlying FIC property are covered in our <a href="/blog/landlord-tax-essentials/business-property-relief-rental-property-iht">Business Property Relief on Rental Property</a> pillar and (forthcoming) FIC value-freeze page.</p>

<h2>Documentation that survives an HMRC enquiry</h2>

<p>The s.102 test is fact-led and the facts live in the documents. A weak documentary trail is the single most common cause of an adverse GROB finding, irrespective of the actual underlying intent. Six items, all dated at or close to the gift date and all retained on file for at least seven years after the donor's death (longer is better):</p>

<ol>
<li><strong>Contemporaneous RICS valuation of the property at the gift date.</strong> This supports the s.17 TCGA 1992 deemed-disposal value for the donor's CGT calculation and the gift value for the donor's IHT PET schedule. Without it, HMRC will value at their own estimate, usually higher.</li>
<li><strong>Deed of gift or transfer, properly drafted, with no side letters.</strong> Side letters reserving any continuing interest in the property to the donor are the most direct evidence of a reservation. Counsel-drafted gift deeds are routine for any portfolio above £500,000 in value.</li>
<li><strong>Lender documentation showing the mortgage position post-gift.</strong> Either a redemption statement (where the mortgage is paid off before the gift), or a complete release of the donor from the loan (where the donee assumes a fresh mortgage), or, in the joint-borrower fallback case, full transparency on the lender's position and the practical payment flows.</li>
<li><strong>Bank account changes evidencing the rent flow.</strong> The rental account renamed to the donee, signatories changed, no joint account giving the donor continuing access. The first 12 months of post-gift bank statements are what HMRC will request first.</li>
<li><strong>Donee's first post-gift self-assessment return.</strong> Rental income reported by the donee, income tax paid by the donee. The return is documentary evidence that the rental income flowed to the donee, not the donor.</li>
<li><strong>Market-rate management agreement, if the donor continues as managing agent.</strong> Written agreement, fee set against contemporaneous third-party-agent quotes, periodic review with fresh quotes every two to three years, fee invoices issued and paid, donee's payment of the fee documented in their bank statements and tax-deductible against their rental income.</li>
</ol>

<p>The combination of these six items produces a documentary trail that survives the standard HMRC enquiry pattern. Each missing item raises the risk of a finding that the gift was subject to a reservation, regardless of the underlying intent. The doctrine HMRC applies is that good documentation evidences good substance; weak documentation evidences a fact pattern that the inspector is entitled to read against the taxpayer.</p>
