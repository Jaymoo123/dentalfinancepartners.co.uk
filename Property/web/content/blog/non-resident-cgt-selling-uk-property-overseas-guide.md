<p>If you're a <strong>non-resident landlord selling UK property</strong>, you face specific capital gains tax (CGT) obligations that differ significantly from UK residents. The rules around <strong>non resident CGT UK property</strong> sales include mandatory reporting deadlines, different tax rates in some cases, and additional compliance requirements that many overseas property investors overlook.</p>

<p>This guide explains everything you need to know about CGT when selling UK property as a non-resident, including the 60-day reporting rule, tax calculations, exemptions, and practical compliance steps to avoid HMRC penalties.</p>

<h2>Who Is Considered Non-Resident for CGT Purposes?</h2>

<p>For CGT purposes, you're typically non-resident if you spend fewer than 183 days in the UK during the tax year of disposal. However, the rules are more complex than this simple test suggests.</p>

<p>HMRC uses the Statutory Residence Test (SRT) to determine your tax status. You may be non-resident even if you spend more than 183 days in the UK if you have no UK ties and were non-resident in previous years. Conversely, you could be UK resident despite spending less time here if you have strong UK connections.</p>

<h3>Key Factors in the Residence Test</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Days spent in UK:</strong> Physical presence counting towards the 183-day test</li>
<li><strong>UK ties:</strong> Family, accommodation, work, and 90-day ties from previous years</li>
<li><strong>Previous residence status:</strong> Your status in the three preceding tax years affects the current year's determination</li>
<li><strong>Full-time work overseas:</strong> Can override other factors if you work sufficient hours abroad</li>
</ul>
<p>If you're unsure about your residence status, consider seeking advice from a <a href="/services">specialist property accountant</a> before proceeding with any property sale.</p>

<h2>CGT Rates, Calculations and the 60-Day Reporting Rule</h2>
<p>Non-residents pay CGT on UK property disposals at the same rates as UK residents: 18% for basic rate taxpayers and 24% for higher rate taxpayers. However, the calculation process and reporting requirements differ significantly.</p>

<h3>CGT Rates and Annual Exempt Amount</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Basic rate:</strong> 18% on gains up to the basic rate threshold</li>
<li><strong>Higher rate:</strong> 24% on gains above the basic rate threshold</li>
<li><strong>Annual exempt amount:</strong> £3,000 (same as UK residents)</li>
</ul>
<p>The rate you pay depends on your total UK income and gains for the tax year. If your UK income plus gains exceed the basic rate threshold (£50,270 for 2026/27), you'll pay the higher 24% rate on the excess.</p>

<h3>Calculating the Gain</h3>
<p>Your capital gain is calculated as: <strong>Sale proceeds minus (original purchase price + allowable costs + improvements)</strong>.</p>
<p>Allowable costs include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Purchase costs (legal fees, stamp duty, survey fees)</li>
<li>Sale costs (estate agent fees, legal fees, marketing costs)</li>
<li>Capital improvements (extensions, major renovations)</li>
<li>Professional fees for CGT advice and compliance</li>
</ul>
<p>You cannot deduct routine maintenance, repairs, or <a href="/blog/section-24-and-tax-relief/landlord-tax-deductions-uk-2026-complete-list">normal landlord expenses</a> from the capital gain calculation.</p>

<h3>The 60-Day Reporting Requirement and Penalties</h3>
<p>Non-residents must report and pay CGT within 60 days of completion when selling UK residential property. The deadline applies regardless of whether you owe any tax. Even if your gain is covered by the annual exempt amount or losses, you must still file a return and notify HMRC of the disposal.</p>
<p>Late filing penalties apply immediately:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Day 61-90:</strong> £100 penalty</li>
<li><strong>Day 91-180:</strong> Additional £300 penalty</li>
<li><strong>Day 181+:</strong> Further penalties of up to 5% of the tax due</li>
<li><strong>Interest:</strong> Charged on unpaid tax from day 61</li>
</ul>
<p>HMRC has limited discretion to waive penalties, and "didn't know about the rule" is rarely accepted as a reasonable excuse.</p>

<h2>Exemptions, Reliefs and Double Taxation</h2>
<p>Several exemptions and reliefs can reduce or eliminate your CGT liability when selling UK property as a non-resident.</p>

<h3>Principal Private Residence Relief</h3>
<p>If the property was your main home at any point during ownership, you may qualify for <a href="/blog/capital-gains-tax/principal-private-residence-relief-landlords">Principal Private Residence Relief</a>. This can exempt part or all of the gain from CGT.</p>
<p>The relief applies to periods when you actually lived in the property, plus:</p>
<ul>
<li>The last 9 months of ownership (automatic exemption)</li>
<li>Up to 3 years if you were working elsewhere in the UK</li>
<li>Up to 4 years if you were working abroad</li>
<li>Any period up to 3 years when you couldn't sell due to work conditions</li>
</ul>

<h3>Letting Relief</h3>
<p>If you let out a property that qualifies for Principal Private Residence Relief, you may also claim Letting Relief of up to £40,000. This relief is only available if you shared occupation with tenants or the property was your main residence at some point.</p>

<h3>Double Taxation Relief</h3>
<p>If you're also liable to pay capital gains tax in your country of residence on the same property disposal, you may be able to claim relief under a double taxation treaty. The UK has tax treaties with most countries that prevent double taxation. Typically, you pay the higher of the two tax amounts and claim credit for tax paid in the other jurisdiction. The mechanics of claiming relief vary by country, so you'll likely need advice from tax specialists in both jurisdictions.</p>

<h2>Reporting Process, Documentation and Payment</h2>
<p>The non-resident CGT return must be filed online using HMRC's digital service. You'll need to create a Government Gateway account if you don't already have one.</p>

<h3>Required Information and Supporting Documentation</h3>
<p>You will need to provide the following information and keep detailed records of all transactions:</p>
<ul>
<li>Property address and description</li>
<li>Completion date and sale proceeds</li>
<li>Original purchase date and cost</li>
<li>Details of all allowable costs</li>
<li>Your UK address (if any) and overseas address</li>
<li>National Insurance number or Unique Taxpayer Reference</li>
<li>Sale contract and completion statement</li>
<li>Original purchase documents</li>
<li>Invoices for all allowable costs</li>
<li>Bank statements showing payment of costs</li>
<li>Professional valuations (if relevant for relief claims)</li>
</ul>

<h3>Payment Methods and Currency</h3>
<p>CGT must be paid in pounds sterling, regardless of the currency in which you received the sale proceeds. You're responsible for currency conversion at the appropriate exchange rate. HMRC typically accepts the exchange rate on the completion date or the date you received the proceeds. Keep records of the exchange rates used for future reference.</p>
<p>Payment options include:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Online banking:</strong> Direct from a UK bank account</li>
<li><strong>International transfer:</strong> SWIFT payment to HMRC's account</li>
<li><strong>Agent payment:</strong> Through a UK-based accountant or agent</li>
</ul>

<h2>Multiple Property Disposals and Company Ownership</h2>

<h3>Multiple Property Disposals</h3>
<p>If you sell multiple UK properties in the same tax year, each disposal has its own 60-day reporting requirement. However, you can aggregate gains and losses across all disposals when calculating your tax liability. For example, if you sell three properties - making gains of £50,000 and £30,000 on two properties but a loss of £20,000 on the third - your net gain is £60,000. You can then deduct the £3,000 annual exempt amount, leaving £57,000 subject to CGT.</p>

<h3>Companies and Non-Resident CGT</h3>
<p>If you own UK property through an overseas company, different rules apply. The company may be liable to UK Corporation Tax on the gain rather than CGT, depending on its activities and connection to the UK. Companies with UK property assets may also need to comply with the Annual Tax on Enveloped Dwellings (ATED) regime if individual properties are worth more than £500,000. Many overseas property investors consider <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/buy-to-let-limited-company-complete-guide-uk">UK company structures</a> to simplify compliance and potentially reduce overall tax liabilities.</p>

<h2>Common Mistakes and Planning for Future Disposals</h2>

<h3>Common Mistakes to Avoid</h3>
<p><strong>Overseas landlords selling property CGT</strong> compliance often goes wrong in predictable ways. Here are the most common errors:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Missing the 60-Day Deadline:</strong> This is by far the most expensive mistake. Even if you owe no tax, the reporting requirement applies. Set reminders immediately after exchange of contracts.</li>
<li><strong>Incorrect Cost Basis Calculation:</strong> Many non-residents forget to include all allowable costs in their calculations. Professional fees, improvements, and purchase costs can significantly reduce your taxable gain.</li>
<li><strong>Currency Conversion Errors:</strong> Using incorrect exchange rates or inconsistent conversion dates can lead to disputes with HMRC. Document your methodology clearly.</li>
<li><strong>Assuming Residence Status:</strong> Residence rules are complex, and assumptions often prove wrong. If you spend significant time in the UK or have strong ties here, get professional advice on your status.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Planning for Future Disposals</h3>
<p>If you're planning to sell UK property as a non-resident, advance planning can reduce your tax liability and compliance burden.</p>
<p><strong>Timing Considerations:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Tax year planning:</strong> Consider which tax year to complete in based on your other UK income</li>
<li><strong>Multiple properties:</strong> Spread disposals across tax years to use multiple annual exemptions</li>
<li><strong>Residence planning:</strong> Your residence status can significantly affect tax liabilities</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Record Keeping:</strong> Start organizing your records before marketing the property. You'll need detailed documentation of all costs from the original purchase onwards. A specialist <a href="/blog/property-accountant-services/what-does-a-property-accountant-do">property accountant</a> can help with both advance planning and compliance when the time comes to sell.</p>

<h2>Getting Professional Help</h2>
<p>Non-resident CGT compliance is complex, with severe penalties for errors or delays. The interaction between UK tax rules, your home country's tax system, and residence planning requires specialist knowledge.</p>
<p>Consider professional advice if:</p>
<ul>
<li>You're unsure about your residence status</li>
<li>The property was ever your main home</li>
<li>You own multiple properties or complex structures</li>
<li>You need to coordinate with tax obligations in another country</li>
<li>The gain is substantial enough to justify planning opportunities</li>
</ul>
<p>The cost of professional advice is often far less than the penalties for getting compliance wrong, and good planning can frequently reduce the overall tax liability.</p>
