  - question: "What makes an IPDI different from any other interest in possession trust?"
    answer: "The four conditions at IHTA 1984 s.49A, all of which must be met for the IIP to qualify. Condition 1 (s.49A(2)): the settlement was effected by will or under the law relating to intestacy. Lifetime-created settlements cannot be IPDIs; they fall into the relevant property regime under FA 2006. Condition 2 (s.49A(3)): the beneficiary became beneficially entitled to the interest in possession on the death of the testator or intestate. The interest must crystallise on death, not on a later trustee resolution. Condition 3 (s.49A(4)): the property in which the interest subsists is not the subject of a section 71A trust for bereaved minors, and the interest is not a disabled person's interest under s.89B. Condition 4 (s.49A(5)): condition 3 has been satisfied at all times since the beneficiary became entitled to the IIP. Where all four are met the IIP is an IPDI and the s.49(1) deemed beneficial entitlement applies; where any fails, the trust falls into the relevant property regime with 10-year periodic charges, exit charges, and trust-rate taxation."
  - question: "How is rental income taxed where the property sits in an IPDI?"
    answer: "On the IIP holder at their personal income tax rates. IHTA 1984 s.49(1) deems the IIP holder to be beneficially entitled to the underlying property for the purposes of the Act, and the corresponding income-tax treatment under ITTOIA 2005 s.272 (property income) read with the IIP transparency rules at ITA 2007 makes the IIP holder the taxpayer on the rental profit. The trustees are interposed for legal-title purposes but are not the taxpayer on the rental income. The practical implication for the surviving spouse on a spouse-IPDI arrangement is that the rental income is taxed at the spouse's marginal rate (10.75 / 35.75 / 39.35 percent post-6-April-2026 dividend bands, with rental income following the standard non-savings non-dividend bands), with personal allowance, the £1,000 property allowance, and the section 24 mortgage interest restriction all applying as they would on direct ownership."
  - question: "Does Section 24 mortgage interest restriction apply to rental income in an IPDI?"
    answer: "Yes. Because the rental income is transparently taxed on the IIP holder under s.49(1) and the corresponding ITTOIA 2005 mechanic, the section 24 ITA 2007 mortgage interest restriction applies to the IPDI holder as it would on direct ownership. The 20% basic-rate tax-reducer for finance costs (rather than full deduction against rental income) bites on the IIP holder's personal tax computation. The structural implication is that an IPDI structure does not avoid section 24 for a surviving spouse who steps into the income stream of a previously-mortgaged BTL portfolio. Where the testator died with substantial finance costs on the portfolio, the IPDI mechanic preserves the section 24 restriction at IIP-holder level. Sessions advising on IPDI design should make this clear to the testator pre-death: the IPDI delivers excellent IHT outcomes but does not solve the section 24 problem."
  - question: "Can the trustees claim Principal Private Residence (PPR) relief where the IPDI holder occupies the property?"
    answer: "Yes, under TCGA 1992 s.225. Section 225 extends PPR to settlements where the property is occupied as the only or main residence of an individual entitled to occupy under the terms of the settlement. The IPDI holder satisfies this requirement: the s.49A IPDI gives the holder beneficial entitlement to the IIP, and the trust's terms (set out in the will) determine the occupation right. Where the surviving spouse moves into a former rental property held in IPDI as their main residence, the trustees can claim PPR on any subsequent disposal during the period of spouse occupation. This is materially useful for portfolios where one of the properties was the deceased's BTL but becomes the spouse's main residence after death. The trustees document the spouse's occupation, claim PPR for the relevant period, and the gain attributable to the occupation period is exempt. Cross-reference our spouse PPR mechanics page for the calculation detail."
  - question: "What happens to the CGT base cost on the testator's death?"
    answer: "TCGA 1992 s.72 imposes no CGT charge on death and re-bases the assets to market value at the date of death for the trustees holding the IPDI. The IPDI mechanic therefore inherits the date-of-death market value as its base cost, just as direct beneficiaries on a death-bed-distribution would. The structural implication for landlords with substantial latent gains on their BTL portfolio: those gains are washed out at death under s.72 regardless of whether the property passes directly to beneficiaries or into an IPDI trust. On any subsequent disposal by the trustees during the life of the IPDI, the gain is computed against the date-of-death base cost, not the original acquisition cost. On the IPDI holder's death (second death in a spouse-IPDI structure), another s.72 re-base applies because the IPDI brings the property into the holder's death estate for IHT under s.49(1), and the corresponding CGT treatment mirrors absolute-ownership."
  - question: "Does the IPDI trust need to register on the Trust Registration Service?"
    answer: "Yes, in almost every case. An IPDI is a taxable relevant trust under MLR 2017 reg 45 from the date the trustees become liable to UK tax. For a rental-property IPDI, the trustees become liable to UK tax from the date rental income starts flowing through the trust (typically the date probate is granted and the rental income redirects from the executor's estate-administration account into the trust account). The 90-day TRS registration deadline runs from that trigger date. The lead trustee designation, the partnership of TRS with HMRC SA registration (separate regime for trust tax returns on SA900), and the £5,000-maximum-discretionary penalty under TRSM80020 all apply to IPDI trusts on the same basis as discretionary trusts. See our <a href=\"/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/trust-registration-service-trs-compliance-trust-owned-btl-mlr-2017\">TRS compliance pillar</a> for the full registration mechanics."
  - question: "How does an IPDI work for the executor administration sequence?"
    answer: "Four steps run in parallel after death. Step one: probate. The executor obtains the Grant of Probate (or Letters of Administration on intestacy) and assumes legal control of the estate assets. For an IPDI structure, the will identifies the rental portfolio as the IPDI subject and the surviving spouse as the IPDI holder; the children (or other remaindermen) take the remainder interest. Step two: estate administration. The executor settles debts, pays any outstanding income tax to date of death, files the final SA return for the deceased, and prepares the IHT account on IHT400. The s.18 spouse exemption removes the IPDI subject from the IHT charge on first death because the property passes to a spouse for IHT purposes (the IPDI treats the surviving spouse as beneficially entitled). Step three: transfer to trustees. Once the IHT clearance certificate (form IHT421) is obtained, the executor transfers the rental portfolio to the trustees of the IPDI. Step four: IPDI trustee operation. The trustees register on TRS within 90 days, register for SA (form SA900 trust return), open trustee bank accounts, transfer the rental income stream from the estate-administration account to the trust account, and pay the IPDI holder (the surviving spouse) the rental income net of trust expenses. The income then drops out for tax purposes via s.49(1) transparency and is taxed in the spouse's hands."
  - question: "What is the deed-of-variation-into-IPDI mechanic and when does it help?"
    answer: "Two routes. First, IHTA 1984 s.142 (general deed of variation): beneficiaries can vary a will within 2 years of death, redirecting the deceased's estate, with the variation reading back to the deceased for IHT and CGT purposes. A common use case is varying a will that left the rental portfolio to the children outright (potentially with full IHT on first death) into an IPDI for the surviving spouse with remainder to the children, capturing both the spouse exemption and the spouse-IPDI architecture retrospectively. Second, IHTA 1984 s.144 (discretionary will-trust distribution reading back): where the will established a discretionary trust holding the rental portfolio, the trustees can distribute the property out within 2 years of death (with the distribution reading back to the deceased for IHT) into a structure that creates an IPDI for the surviving spouse. Both routes require the beneficiary or trustee to elect the reading-back treatment within 2 years; missed deadlines mean the variation is a fresh lifetime gift and loses the s.18 spouse exemption opportunity. Sessions advising on estate restructure within 2 years of death should consider both routes systematically."
  - question: "Robinson worked example: how does the IPDI mechanic play out on a £1.4m BTL portfolio?"
    answer: "Mr Robinson dies in March 2026 owning four rental properties (combined market value £1.4m, no mortgages, generating £52,000 net rental income before tax). His will leaves the portfolio on IPDI for his wife Mrs Robinson (aged 66) for life, with remainder to their two adult children equally. Estate administration. The executor obtains probate by July 2026 and prepares IHT400 showing the rental portfolio as the only significant asset. The s.18 spouse exemption applies because the IPDI gives Mrs Robinson beneficial entitlement to the property for IHT under s.49(1). First-death IHT: nil. CGT on death: TCGA 1992 s.72 re-bases the four properties to their market value at March 2026 (combined £1.4m); no CGT charge on death. IPDI in operation. Mrs Robinson receives the £52,000 rental income each year, taxed at her marginal rate (£12,570 personal allowance plus £37,700 at 20% plus residual at 40%, with section 24 mortgage interest restriction inapplicable here because there are no mortgages). Trustees register on TRS in October 2026 (within 90 days of the rental income starting to flow). Mrs Robinson dies in 2034. Second death: the £1.4m portfolio (or its then-market value) sits in her death estate under s.49(1) because the IPDI brings the property into her IHT position. NRB plus transferable NRB (Mr Robinson's unused £325,000) plus RNRB plus transferable RNRB (assuming both children inherit) gives £325,000 plus £325,000 plus £175,000 plus £175,000 = £1m of nil-rate band. If the portfolio has appreciated to £1.6m at second death, IHT at 40% on £600,000 = £240,000. The s.18 spouse exemption on first death plus the second-death architecture together deliver a clean outcome that direct individual ownership could not achieve."
  - question: "Can an IPDI work where the testator did not own the property at death?"
    answer: "No, in the literal sense. The IPDI mechanic requires the deceased's will (or intestacy rules) to settle property on trust at death. Where the testator never owned the relevant property, there is nothing for the will to settle into IPDI. The structurally adjacent scenarios where the IPDI concept can be reached via other routes: a deed of variation under s.142 redirecting OTHER assets in the estate into a structure that holds property acquired by the trustees post-death (using cash from the estate to purchase property); a s.144 distribution from a discretionary will-trust where the trustees purchase property and then create an IPDI for the surviving spouse. Both are creative routes and need careful drafting. The mainstream case is the property already in the testator's estate at death; everything else is bespoke."
  - question: "How does an IPDI compare to a discretionary will-trust for landlord estates?"
    answer: "Three axes of difference. First, IHT treatment. IPDI: s.49(1) deemed beneficial entitlement, spouse exemption on first death if IPDI is for spouse, property in IIP holder's death estate on second death. Discretionary will-trust: enters the relevant property regime, no spouse exemption (the trust is not a spouse), 10-year periodic charges, exit charges on capital distributions. The IPDI delivers cleaner IHT outcomes for spouse-then-children architectures. Second, income tax. IPDI: rental income taxed on IIP holder at personal rates via s.49(1) transparency. Discretionary: rental income taxed at trust rates (45% on non-dividend income above the £500 trust tax-free amount under ITA 2007 s.479; for property income from 6 April 2027 the new 47% property trust rate per FA 2026 applies). The IPDI delivers materially better income-tax outcomes. Third, flexibility. IPDI: rigid (the IPDI holder has fixed entitlement; trustees cannot redirect income or capital to other beneficiaries). Discretionary: highly flexible (trustees decide on distributions case by case). Where the family values flexibility over tax efficiency, the discretionary route wins; where the family wants tax efficiency for a known surviving-spouse-then-children pattern, the IPDI wins."
  - question: "What is a Qualifying Interest in Possession (QIIP) and how does it relate to IPDI?"
    answer: "QIIP is the broader category at IHTA 1984 s.59 that captures all IIPs preserving the s.49(1) treatment after the 22 March 2006 reform. The QIIP category includes IPDIs (s.49A), Transitional Serial Interests (TSIs, ss.49B-49E), Disabled Person's Interests (s.89B), and pre-22-March-2006 IIPs that were already in existence on the reform date (grandfathered for the lifetime of the original IIP holder). Post-22-March-2006, only those four sub-categories qualify as QIIPs. Wider IIPs created post-22-March-2006 outside these carve-outs fall into the relevant property regime and are not QIIPs. The structural implication for landlord estate planning is that the IPDI is the principal route to create a new QIIP post-2006 because TSIs were a transitional rescue mechanism that has now largely closed (TSI deadline was October 2008 with limited subsequent extensions), Disabled Person's Interests require a qualifying disability, and pre-2006 IIPs cannot be created new. The IPDI is the only flexible route to a QIIP for current planning."
  - question: "Did Finance Act 2025 change the IPDI mechanic?"
    answer: "No, the IPDI mechanic itself is unchanged by FA 2025. FA 2025 s.45 reformed the IHTA 1984 s.48 excluded-property test for offshore trusts (omitting s.48(3)-(3F) and inserting new s.48ZA based on a long-term-residence test from 6 April 2025), but s.49 / s.49A / s.49(1A) carve-outs all continue to operate as before. The post-22-March-2006 architecture preserving the s.49(1) treatment for IPDIs, TSIs, and Disabled Person's Interests is not modified by FA 2025. Landlord estate planning using the IPDI route under a UK will with UK-resident trustees and UK-situs property is wholly unaffected by the FA 2025 reforms; only the offshore-trust excluded-property territory is reshaped (and that is C2 out-of-scope; see our settlor-interested trust attribution rules page for s.48ZA context if needed)."
  - question: "Should we ask the testator's solicitor to draft the will with an IPDI specifically?"
    answer: "Yes, where the family fits the spouse-then-children pattern and the rental portfolio is the principal estate asset. The IPDI structure must be expressly created in the will; will-drafting templates for older clients may default to either an outright bequest to the spouse (delivering the s.18 spouse exemption on first death but losing the second-death architecture) or a discretionary will-trust (delivering trust-side IHT inefficiency on rental income). Specific IPDI drafting requires the will to (a) place the rental portfolio (or specific properties) on trust on the testator's death, (b) give the surviving spouse an interest in possession for life (or for a defined period), (c) define the remainder in favour of the children or other beneficiaries, and (d) make clear that the IPDI conditions are met (no s.71A bereaved-minor trust, no disabled person's interest). The drafting solicitor should also coordinate with the estate-planning adviser on the lifetime gifting strategy that complements the IPDI: lifetime PETs reducing the testator's death estate further before the IPDI structure activates."

<p>An Immediate Post-Death Interest (IPDI) is the single most operationally important section 49(1A) carve-out for landlord estates because it preserves the transparent income-tax treatment of pre-22-March-2006 interests in possession while sitting inside a trust structure created by will. This page is the operational complement to our broader <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/interest-in-possession-iht-treatment-iipi-iht49a-life-tenant-rental-property">IIP architecture page on the s.49A and s.49(1A) carve-outs</a>; it focuses on the three operational tax mechanics that determine how an IPDI works in practice for a rental portfolio, the executor administration sequence end to end, and the deed-of-variation routes that allow post-death rescue where the will did not originally create an IPDI.</p>

<p>The reference scenario throughout is the <strong>Robinson estate</strong>, anonymised composite. Mr Robinson, aged 71, dies in March 2026 owning four rental properties (combined market value £1.4m at death, no outstanding mortgages, generating £52,000 of net rental income before tax). His will, drafted in 2019 and reviewed with his solicitor in 2024, leaves the rental portfolio on IPDI for his wife Mrs Robinson (then aged 66) for life, with remainder to their two adult children equally on her death. Our IPDI compliance pillar requires the trustees to register the trust with HMRC under MLR 2017 reg 45 within 90 days of becoming liable to UK tax (see our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/trust-registration-service-trs-compliance-trust-owned-btl-mlr-2017">TRS compliance pillar</a>); the broader IHT context around spouse exemption, NRB / RNRB transferability, and the FA 2025 reforms is set out in our IIP architecture page above; and the FIC and discretionary-trust alternatives sit in our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/fic-vs-discretionary-trust-property-comparison">FIC vs discretionary trust comparison</a> and <a href="/blog/landlord-tax-essentials/iht-clt-property-discretionary-trust-20-percent-entry-charge">CLT entry-charge mechanics</a> pages respectively.</p>

<h2>The four s.49A conditions and what they mean operationally</h2>

<p><a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1984/51/section/49A">IHTA 1984 s.49A</a> sets four conditions, all of which must be met for the IIP to qualify as an IPDI. The verbatim wording from legislation.gov.uk (verified 2026-05-24):</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>s.49A(2): "the settlement was effected by will or under the law relating to intestacy."</strong> Lifetime-created settlements cannot be IPDIs. This is the structural reason the IPDI route requires advance will drafting; it cannot be created during the testator's lifetime as a planning move.</li>
<li><strong>s.49A(3):</strong> the beneficiary "became beneficially entitled to the interest in possession on the death of the testator or intestate". The interest must crystallise on death, not at a later trustee decision or distribution event.</li>
<li><strong>s.49A(4):</strong> two requirements both apply: "section 71A below does not apply to the property in which the interest subsists" and "the interest is not a disabled person's interest". The IPDI is mutually exclusive with the bereaved-minors trust and the disabled-person's-interest carve-outs.</li>
<li><strong>s.49A(5): "Condition 3 has been satisfied at all times since L became beneficially entitled to the interest in possession."</strong> The s.71A and s.89B exclusions must persist throughout the IPDI; any subsequent operation that brings the property into s.71A or s.89B territory disqualifies the IPDI prospectively.</li>
</ul>

<p>The Robinson will meets all four conditions cleanly. The settlement is effected by Mr Robinson's will (condition 1). Mrs Robinson becomes beneficially entitled to the IIP on Mr Robinson's death (condition 2). Neither s.71A nor s.89B applies to the rental portfolio (condition 3). Mrs Robinson is an adult (above 25), is not disabled within s.89B, and the trust is not a bereaved-minors trust, so condition 4 is preserved throughout her IIP.</p>

<h2>Mechanic 1: rental income tax transparency under s.49(1)</h2>

<p>The IPDI mechanic preserves the s.49(1) deemed beneficial entitlement: the IIP holder is treated for IHT purposes as beneficially entitled to the underlying property. The corresponding income-tax treatment is set out in ITTOIA 2005 and ITA 2007, with the trustees being interposed for legal-title purposes but not being the taxpayer on the rental income. The taxpayer is the IIP holder.</p>

<p>For Mrs Robinson on the £52,000 annual rental income from the four-property portfolio: the income is taxed in her hands at her personal rates. Assuming she has a state pension of £11,500 plus modest investment income of £4,000 plus the £52,000 rental income, her total income is around £67,500. Personal allowance of £12,570 covers the pension portion; basic-rate band of £37,700 captures most of the remainder; the residual £17,000 falls into the higher-rate band at 40%. Total income tax for 2026/27: approximately £15,500 to £16,000 depending on exact allowance interactions. The trustees pay no income tax on the rental income because the income drops out to Mrs Robinson via s.49(1) transparency.</p>

<p>Compare with the discretionary alternative. Had the will established a discretionary will-trust over the same portfolio, the trustees would pay income tax at the trust rate (45% on non-dividend income above the £500 trust tax-free amount per ITA 2007 s.479, with the new 47% property trust rate applying from 6 April 2027 per Finance Act 2026). On £52,000 of rental income: roughly £23,000 of trust-level tax. Distribution to Mrs Robinson would then carry a 45% tax credit and net £40,000 to her after grossing-up and her marginal-rate adjustment. The IPDI route saves roughly £7,000 to £9,000 of annual income tax against the discretionary alternative.</p>

<aside><p>Wills drafted before the 2006 IIP reform may not explicitly create an IPDI even where the testator intended an IIP-style structure.</p><p>Reviewing the will with an IPDI lens (do the four s.49A conditions clearly apply on the testator's death?) is a high-value pre-death exercise. Property Tax Partners runs will reviews against the IPDI carve-out architecture for landlord families with substantial rental portfolios; the form below routes to the estate-planning team.</p></aside>

<h2>Mechanic 2: section 24 mortgage interest restriction still bites</h2>

<p>The transparency under s.49(1) carries through to the section 24 ITA 2007 mortgage interest restriction. Where the rental portfolio includes mortgaged properties, the IIP holder is the taxpayer for the rental profit computation and the section 24 restriction (finance costs allowable only as a 20% basic-rate tax-reducer, not as a deduction against rental income) applies on the IIP holder's tax return.</p>

<p>The Robinson estate is mortgage-free, so section 24 does not bite. The structural point matters for landlord families considering IPDI design while the testator is still alive: the IPDI is excellent for IHT and for income-tax-rate optimisation, but it does not solve the section 24 problem on mortgaged portfolios. If the testator wants to leave a mortgaged rental portfolio in a structure that escapes section 24, the discretionary trust route does NOT help (trusts also pay section 24 on rental income) and the principal escape is incorporation into a company before death (CT on rental income net of full finance-cost deduction, with the company then forming part of the death estate as company shares).</p>

<h2>Mechanic 3: PPR via trustees under TCGA 1992 s.225</h2>

<p><a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12/section/225">TCGA 1992 s.225</a> extends Principal Private Residence relief to settlements where the property is occupied as the only or main residence of an individual entitled to occupy under the terms of the settlement. The IPDI holder satisfies this requirement because the s.49A IPDI gives them beneficial entitlement to the IIP, and the will's terms govern the occupation right.</p>

<p>Operational implication for landlord IPDI. Where the surviving spouse moves into one of the former rental properties held in IPDI as their main residence after the testator's death, the trustees can claim PPR on any subsequent disposal during the period of spouse occupation. The Robinson family might decide that Mrs Robinson, post-death, sells her own (smaller) home and moves into the largest of the four BTL properties as her main residence. From that move-in date, PPR applies via s.225 on that property; the rental income from the other three properties continues to be taxed in Mrs Robinson's hands via s.49(1) transparency; the structural saving on the eventual disposal of the now-PPR property can be substantial (the residential CGT rate of 18% / 24% saved on the gain attributable to the occupation period).</p>

<h2>Mechanic 4: TCGA 1992 s.72 CGT no-charge on death and the base-cost re-base</h2>

<p>The IPDI mechanic does not change the standard CGT-on-death position. <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12/section/72">TCGA 1992 s.72</a> imposes no CGT charge on death and re-bases the assets to market value at the date of death for the personal representatives, and (where the will places the assets into a settlement) the trustees inherit the re-based cost.</p>

<p>For the Robinson portfolio at March 2026 death: the four properties (combined market value £1.4m at death, original combined acquisition cost £620,000 in 2008 with various enhancements totalling £80,000, latent gain £700,000 immediately before death) are washed by s.72 to a £1.4m base cost. The trustees of the IPDI hold the portfolio at £1.4m base cost; any subsequent disposal during Mrs Robinson's life is computed against £1.4m.</p>

<p>On Mrs Robinson's death (second death), the s.49(1) deemed beneficial entitlement brings the portfolio into her death estate for IHT, and the corresponding CGT s.72 re-base operates again because the property is treated as her property for the purposes of the CGT-on-death rules. The remaindermen (her two adult children) inherit the portfolio at the second-death market value. The IPDI therefore delivers a double base-cost uplift (once on first death, once on second death) that direct individual ownership would only deliver once. The structural advantage on long-held appreciating portfolios is material.</p>

<h2>Mechanic 5: TRS registration within 90 days of trustees' first tax liability</h2>

<p>An IPDI is a taxable relevant trust under MLR 2017 reg 45 because the trustees become liable to UK tax (income tax on rental income flowing through the trust, capital gains tax on any disposal during the life of the IPDI). The 90-day TRS registration deadline runs from the date the trustees first become liable to UK tax, which is typically the date probate is granted and the rental income redirects from the executor's estate-administration bank account into the IPDI trust account.</p>

<p>For the Robinson estate: probate granted July 2026; rental income starts flowing through the trust account from August 2026; 90-day TRS deadline expires October 2026. The trustees designate Mrs Robinson's brother (acting as one of the two trustees, alongside an independent professional trustee firm) as the lead trustee, complete the gov.uk TRS portal submission in late September 2026, and receive the URN in early October. Separately, the trustees register for self-assessment trust tax returns (form SA900) by 5 October 2027 for the 2026/27 tax year, with the first SA900 filing due 31 January 2028. The dual-registration discipline (TRS plus SA900) is required for every taxable relevant IPDI trust; missing either gate creates exposure under TRSM80020 or the Schedule 55 FA 2009 SA late-filing regime. The detail is covered in our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/trust-registration-service-trs-compliance-trust-owned-btl-mlr-2017">TRS compliance pillar</a>.</p>

<h2>The Robinson estate worked sequence: executor administration to IPDI operation</h2>

<p>The end-to-end administration sequence over the first eighteen months looks as follows.</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>March 2026 to July 2026 (executor administration).</strong> Executor obtains probate; settles outstanding debts; prepares the final SA return for Mr Robinson to date of death; prepares IHT400 listing the £1.4m rental portfolio plus minor personal estate. The s.18 spouse exemption removes the IPDI property from the IHT charge on first death because the IPDI gives Mrs Robinson beneficial entitlement for IHT. First-death IHT: nil. The IHT clearance certificate (form IHT421) is obtained.</li>
<li><strong>July 2026 to October 2026 (transfer to trustees and TRS registration).</strong> Trustees take legal title of the four properties; rental income redirects to the trust account; TRS registration completed within 90 days; SA900 registration scheduled for 2026/27 tax-year cycle. Mrs Robinson's first rental income payment from the trust lands in September 2026.</li>
<li><strong>2026/27 tax year onwards (steady-state IPDI operation).</strong> Trustees collect rent, pay property-running costs, distribute net income to Mrs Robinson. Mrs Robinson reports the rental profit on her own SA return at her personal rates. The trustees file SA900 annually showing the income flow-through and confirming the IIP holder's identity.</li>
<li><strong>Mrs Robinson's death (estimated mid-2030s).</strong> Second-death IHT applies under s.49(1) with the portfolio in her death estate at then-market-value. NRB (£325,000) plus transferable NRB from Mr Robinson (£325,000) plus RNRB (£175,000) plus transferable RNRB (£175,000) gives £1m of nil-rate cover. CGT s.72 second re-base. Remaindermen (the two adult children) inherit the portfolio at second-death market value.</li>
</ul>

<aside><p>The executor administration of an IPDI is materially more involved than for a will-leaves-everything-to-spouse outright bequest.</p><p>The TRS registration window, the SA900 trustee tax returns, the trust-bank-account discipline, the dual-trustee designation, and the documentation of Mrs Robinson's IIP entitlement all add complexity. The IHT and income-tax savings on a long-running IPDI typically dwarf the administration costs (the Robinson IPDI delivers roughly £7,000 to £9,000 of income-tax saving per year against the discretionary alternative, against trustee administration costs of £2,000 to £4,000 per year), but families considering the route should budget the operational time and cost realistically. The form below routes to the estate-planning team.</p></aside>

<h2>The deed-of-variation-into-IPDI rescue routes</h2>

<p>Where the testator's will did not create an IPDI but the family later concludes the IPDI route would have been better, two rescue mechanics are available within 2 years of death.</p>

<p><strong>IHTA 1984 s.142 deed of variation.</strong> Beneficiaries can vary a will within 2 years of death, redirecting the estate to a different structure, with the variation reading back to the deceased for IHT and CGT purposes (the deceased is treated for tax purposes as having made the varied disposition). A common landlord variation: the will leaves the rental portfolio to the children outright (no IPDI; full IHT exposure if first-death IHT was payable; loss of the s.18 spouse exemption opportunity). Within 2 years, the children and the surviving spouse jointly execute a deed of variation directing the portfolio into a spouse-IPDI structure with remainder to themselves. The variation reads back: first-death IHT is recomputed under the variation (typically zero because the s.18 spouse exemption now applies); CGT is recomputed (no immediate CGT crystallises because the variation is treated as a death disposition). The retrospective IPDI then operates as if it had been in the original will.</p>

<p><strong>IHTA 1984 s.144 discretionary will-trust distribution.</strong> Where the will established a discretionary trust holding the rental portfolio, the trustees can distribute the property within 2 years of death (with the distribution reading back to the deceased for IHT under s.144) into a structure that creates an IPDI for the surviving spouse. The mechanic is more flexible than s.142 because the trustees decide rather than requiring beneficiary consensus.</p>

<p>Both routes have strict 2-year deadlines from the date of death; missed deadlines mean the variation is a fresh lifetime gift from the relevant beneficiary, losing the IHT and CGT reading-back treatment. Families considering retrospective IPDI creation should engage estate-planning advice promptly post-death; the 2-year window passes quickly during the grieving and administrative period.</p>

<h2>How the IPDI compares operationally with the three principal alternatives</h2>

<p>Four estate structures compete for landlord-portfolio planning: outright bequest to spouse, IPDI for spouse with remainder to children, discretionary will-trust, and lifetime-incorporation pre-death into a Family Investment Company.</p>

<p><strong>Outright bequest to spouse.</strong> The simplest structure. The s.18 spouse exemption removes the portfolio from the IHT charge on first death. The spouse owns the portfolio absolutely and runs the rental income through their own SA return at personal rates. On second death, the portfolio is fully in the spouse's death estate at then-market-value. The downside is loss of remainderman control: the surviving spouse can sell, gift, or remarry without the children having any contingent interest. For families where the surviving spouse may remarry, may make substantial lifetime gifts to a new partner, or may consume capital, the outright bequest loses inter-generational asset protection. The IPDI preserves the remainderman interest while delivering essentially identical first-death and second-death tax outcomes.</p>

<p><strong>Discretionary will-trust.</strong> The most flexible structure but the most tax-inefficient for rental portfolios. Trustees decide year-by-year on distributions. No spouse exemption applies on first death because the trust is not a spouse (although a Spouse-of-Settlor trustee can preserve some flexibility). Rental income is taxed at trust rates (45% on non-dividend above £500 trust tax-free amount; 47% property trust rate from 6 April 2027 per Finance Act 2026). The 10-year periodic charge bites every decade at up to 6% on the chargeable value above the NRB. The structure suits families where the surviving spouse's needs are unpredictable, where there are family members with special circumstances (substance dependency, vulnerability) requiring trustee discretion, or where the family wants intentionally non-spouse beneficiaries (a long-time friend, a charity, a step-relative). For a mainstream spouse-then-children pattern, the discretionary route is materially more expensive than the IPDI.</p>

<p><strong>Lifetime incorporation pre-death.</strong> The testator incorporates the rental portfolio into a Family Investment Company while alive. Rental income is taxed at corporation tax (25% main rate; 19% small profits rate up to £50,000; marginal relief in between) inside the company; the s.24 mortgage interest restriction does not apply (full finance-cost deduction at corporate level). On death, the FIC shares form part of the estate. The structure delivers excellent ongoing-tax efficiency on the rental income (particularly on mortgaged portfolios where s.24 bites hardest) but has substantial entry-side costs (SDLT on the property-to-company transfer unless Sch 15 partnership relief applies; CGT on any latent gain unless s.162 incorporation relief is available, which is rare for letting businesses). The lifetime-incorporation route is the right answer where the testator has many years of life expectancy and a heavily-mortgaged portfolio; the IPDI route is the right answer where the testator's death is more imminent or the portfolio is unmortgaged.</p>

<p>The decision among these four routes is fact-specific. The IPDI dominates the spouse-then-children pattern on a mortgage-free or lightly-mortgaged portfolio; the outright bequest dominates the same pattern where the family rejects trustee complexity and accepts the spouse's absolute control; the discretionary route dominates where flexibility is paramount; the FIC route dominates where the portfolio is heavily mortgaged and the testator has time to run the structure. Sessions advising on will drafting for landlord families should walk all four against the client's specific facts.</p>

<h2>FA 2025 does not change the IPDI mechanic</h2>

<p>Finance Act 2025 s.45 reformed the IHTA 1984 s.48 excluded-property test for offshore-trust IHT planning, omitting s.48(3) to (3F) and inserting new s.48ZA based on a long-term-residence test from 6 April 2025. The reform reshapes excluded-property territory for non-UK-domiciled settlors and non-UK-resident trustees, but does not modify s.49, s.49A, or the s.49(1A) carve-outs. The IPDI mechanic, the QIIP definition at s.59, and the transparency under s.49(1) are all unchanged.</p>

<p>For UK-domiciled (or UK-long-term-resident) testators settling UK-situs rental property on IPDI under a UK will with UK-resident trustees, FA 2025 has no operational impact. The IPDI continues to function as the principal spouse-IPDI vehicle for landlord estate planning, with the architecture unchanged from the post-22-March-2006 settlement. Older practitioner content that bundles FA 2025 reform commentary with IPDI mechanics typically conflates two separate territories (excluded property under s.48 / s.48ZA versus IIP transparency under s.49 / s.49A); the IPDI route is on the s.49 side and is wholly unaffected by FA 2025.</p>
