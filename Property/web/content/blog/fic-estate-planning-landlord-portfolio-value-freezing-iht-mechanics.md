faqs:
  - question: "What does 'value-freeze' actually mean in a FIC context?"
    answer: "The founder restructures the property portfolio inside a FIC with two share classes that separate value from growth. Preference shares carry a fixed-coupon dividend and are valued at the £-amount of their coupon entitlement (typically £1 nominal value plus the right to receive the coupon, with a market value tracking the discounted income stream). The preference shares stay in the founder's estate, but their value is frozen at the structural level: even as the FIC's underlying portfolio doubles in value over the following 15 years, the preference shares' value does not grow because they only ever entitle the holder to the fixed coupon, not to any capital growth. Growth shares carry no fixed coupon but receive all the capital growth of the FIC's underlying portfolio above the preference-share entitlement; they also typically carry the voting control. Growth shares are gifted to the next generation. After the gift, all future growth in the underlying portfolio accrues to the growth shareholders, not to the founder. The founder's estate is 'frozen' at the preference-share value plus any non-FIC assets they still hold."
  - question: "Why is FIC IHT planning typically considered above £2,000,000 portfolio size?"
    answer: "Three reasons converge above approximately £2 million. One: the £2,000,000 RNRB taper threshold under s.8D IHTA 1984 begins withdrawing residence nil-rate band at £1 for every £2 above the threshold, so the marginal IHT cost on portfolio growth above £2m rises materially. The value-freeze removes future growth from the founder's estate and avoids worsening the taper position. Two: the structural cost of running a FIC (incorporation, articles, ongoing governance, accountancy, professional fees, annual filings) is meaningful in absolute terms but small relative to a portfolio of £2 million plus. Below £2m the structural cost ratio rises and the value-freeze benefit shrinks. Three: portfolio landlords at this scale typically have multi-generational planning ambitions (passing the portfolio to children and grandchildren intact) that the FIC route serves better than direct gifting of individual properties. For portfolios below £1.5m, the direct-PET route (covered on our 7-year clock page) or simply life cover in trust is usually more proportionate."
  - question: "When does the seven-year PET clock actually start?"
    answer: "On the date of the growth-share gift, not on the date of the FIC formation. The FIC formation itself is not a chargeable transfer for IHT (the founder transfers property into a company they own; there is no transfer of value to a third party at that point). The seven-year clock starts when the founder gifts the growth shares to the next-generation shareholders. From that date, the gift is a Potentially Exempt Transfer under s.3A IHTA 1984; full IHT exemption applies on the gift if the founder survives seven complete years. Within seven years, the gift fails as a PET and comes back into the estate at the value at the date of gift, with taper relief under s.7(4) IHTA 1984 between years 3 and 7 reducing the tax payable (not the gift value). For the share-gift-mechanics-at-point-of-gift in detail (TCGA 1992 s.17 valuation, ITTOIA 2005 s.624 + s.629 settlements treatment, the 8-document checklist), see Wave 4 A9 on FIC growth-share gifting."
  - question: "What is the IHT footprint after a successful value-freeze structure is in place?"
    answer: "The founder's IHT-exposed assets after 7 years of survival from the share gift are capped at: the preference-share value (which tracks the discounted coupon stream and grows slowly if at all) plus any non-FIC assets the founder still holds personally (cash, family home, ISAs, pensions post-2027). The underlying property portfolio inside the FIC has been removed from the founder's estate; the future growth of that portfolio accrues to the growth shareholders. For a £2.5 million portfolio that grows to £4 million over 15 years post-incorporation, the founder's IHT exposure on the portfolio component is capped at the preference-share value (typically £100,000 to £500,000 depending on coupon design), against an unmanaged £4 million exposure (i.e., £1.6 million of IHT at 40%) on the held-to-death counterfactual. The IHT footprint cap is the structural value of the route."
  - question: "Does the property FIC qualify for Business Property Relief?"
    answer: "Almost never. A FIC holding residential investment property is on the investment side of the s.105 IHTA 1984 test; Pawson v HMRC [2013] UKUT 050 (TCC) is the settled authority that residential letting is investment, not trading, regardless of the legal wrapper. The corporate structure does not change the underlying business character. BPR is denied on the founder's preference shares (which represent an interest in the investment business) and on the gifted growth shares (which also represent an interest in the same investment business). The value-freeze route does not deliver BPR; it delivers the structural separation of value from growth, with the strategic IHT outcome from removing growth from the estate via the 7-year-PET-clock mechanic. The April 2026 £1m BPR/APR cap is therefore not directly relevant to a property FIC, because there was no BPR to cap in the first place. Our Wave 4 page at /blog/landlord-tax-essentials/iht-1m-bpr-apr-cap-mixed-trading-investing-landlord-allocation covers the BPR/APR cap mechanics for genuine BPR-qualifying assets (a separate analysis from the FIC route)."
  - question: "Does GROB under s.102 FA 1986 apply to a property FIC?"
    answer: "Potentially, where the founder retains both the preference-share coupon AND continues to use the underlying property personally. The classic GROB trap: founder retains preference shares paying an annual coupon AND lives in (or holiday-stays in, or otherwise uses) one of the properties held in the FIC. The reservation argument is that the founder enjoys a benefit derived from the gifted growth shares (continuing use of the underlying property), and the s.102(1)(b) entire-exclusion test fails. The counter is straightforward in design: founder takes preference-coupon income only (no use of underlying property), or where founder must use one of the properties, pays full market rent to the FIC for the use (the FIC declares the rent as commercial rental income and pays CT on it). The discipline is documentary: tenancy agreement, market-rate rent reviewed periodically, bank evidence of rent paid, FIC's CT return showing the rent as income. Our Wave 4 page on GROB for gifted let property at /blog/landlord-tax-essentials/iht-gift-with-reservation-letting-children-paying-rent-mechanics walks the cash-flow-test mechanics; the corporate-wrapper variant of the same test applies here."
  - question: "How does the value-freeze compare to a direct property PET gift?"
    answer: "Three structural differences. One: the direct PET transfers a specific property at deemed market value under s.17 TCGA 1992 with no s.165 holdover for non-business BTL; the immediate CGT cost is therefore the full latent gain on that property at 24% residential rate. The FIC growth-share gift transfers shares (not property); the share value reflects a minority discount (typically 25% to 40% below the pro-rata underlying asset value) plus any marketability discount, so the CGT bill on the gift is materially smaller than on equivalent direct property gifting. Two: the direct PET removes a specific property completely; the founder no longer has any economic interest in that property. The FIC value-freeze separates value (preference shares, retained) from growth (growth shares, gifted); the founder retains the income stream via the coupon. The retention of income matters for the founder's retirement plan. Three: the 7-year clock runs on both routes, but the size of the IHT-exempt amount after 7 years differs: the direct PET removes one property's value; the FIC route removes all future growth on the entire portfolio. For a portfolio expected to grow materially over 15+ years, the FIC removes a much larger total amount over time. The direct PET is the right route for a single high-gain property; the FIC value-freeze is the right route for a multi-property portfolio with a long planning horizon. For the direct PET mechanics, see Wave 4 C4 at /blog/landlord-tax-essentials/iht-7-year-clock-property-gifting-mid-life-landlord-strategy."
  - question: "How does the value-freeze compare to a CLT into a discretionary trust?"
    answer: "Four structural differences. One: entry IHT. CLT into trust pays 20% IHT immediately on the value above the founder's NRB (e.g., £75,000 chargeable element on a £400,000 settlement = £15,000 lifetime IHT). The FIC value-freeze has no entry IHT (FIC formation is not a chargeable transfer; the growth-share gift is a PET, not a CLT). Two: CGT on the transfer in. CLT into trust unlocks s.260 TCGA 1992 holdover (if non-settlor-interested), so no immediate CGT for the settlor. FIC incorporation does not have an automatic CGT holdover for investment property (s.162 incorporation relief requires the property to be a genuine business by substance, which Pawson and HMRC's reading typically deny for BTL); CGT may crystallise at incorporation. The subsequent growth-share gift is also a market-value CGT event (minority-discounted), so the FIC route generally has a larger total CGT bill than the CLT route. Three: ongoing structure. The trust pays 10-year periodic charges (up to 6% under s.64) and exit charges on capital distributions. The FIC pays standard CT on rental profits at the main 25% rate, plus dividends to shareholders subject to dividend tax. Four: control. The trust gives the trustees discretion; the founder has no ongoing control as settlor. The FIC gives the founder control via director powers and the voting rights attached to share classes (often retained on preference shares while growth shares get economic upside). For the CLT-into-trust mechanic, see Wave 4 C10 at /blog/landlord-tax-essentials/iht-clt-property-discretionary-trust-20-percent-entry-charge. For the side-by-side tax comparison, see our existing page at /blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/fic-vs-discretionary-trust-property-comparison."
  - question: "What happens if the founder dies within seven years of the share gift?"
    answer: "The growth-share PET fails and the gift comes back into the founder's estate at the value at the date of the gift, not at the value at death. This is a critical feature of the value-freeze: even if the underlying portfolio has doubled in value between the gift date and the founder's death, the failed PET is computed on the gift-date growth-share valuation, not on the death-date valuation. The taper relief schedule under s.7(4) IHTA 1984 then reduces the IHT payable (not the gift value) between years 3 and 7: 80% of full rate at 3 to 4 years, 60% at 4 to 5, 40% at 5 to 6, 20% at 6 to 7, zero at 7+. For a fit founder in their 50s or 60s with normal life expectancy, the probability of clearing 7 years is high; for a founder in their 70s or 80s, the value-freeze route may be the wrong tool and a CLT-into-trust route (which provides immediate certainty at the 20% lifetime IHT rate) is often preferred. Mortality-hedging via 7-year decreasing-term life cover in trust is the standard parallel arrangement; the premium typically prices at £200 to £500 per year for a £200,000 sum-assured policy on a fit 55-year-old. The founder's normal-expenditure-out-of-income exemption (s.21 IHTA 1984) usually covers the premium without additional IHT consequences."
  - question: "Can the founder continue to take rental income from the FIC properties after the value-freeze?"
    answer: "Yes, via the preference-share coupon, with three operational requirements. One: the coupon must be a genuine preference-share entitlement, not a discretionary distribution. The founder is entitled to the coupon as a matter of share rights; the directors of the FIC have no discretion to vary or withhold the coupon. Two: the coupon is paid as a dividend, taxed in the founder's hands at the dividend tax rates (8.75% / 33.75% / 39.35% from 6 April 2025) above the £500 dividend allowance, with the founder receiving the net-of-tax amount as retirement income. The FIC pays the dividend out of post-CT profits at the standard 25% main rate. Three: the founder must not use the underlying property in addition to taking the coupon, otherwise GROB s.102 risk arises (as set out above). The founder's residence is typically held outside the FIC (the family home is separate from the BTL portfolio in most landlord estates); FIC properties are let to third-party tenants in the normal way. The retirement-income mechanics in operational detail are on Wave 4 A8 at /blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/fic-property-retirement-decumulation-mechanics-uk."
  - question: "What records must the founder keep at the share-gift date?"
    answer: "Six items, retained on the founder's planning file for at least the founder's life plus 7 years after death. One: a contemporaneous formal share valuation by an independent valuer at the date of the gift, supporting the s.17 TCGA 1992 deemed-disposal market value figure for the donor's CGT and the gift value for the IHT PET schedule. Two: the share transfer documents (stock transfer form or equivalent for unquoted shares, signed and dated). Three: the company's updated register of members showing the growth-share transfer to the next-generation recipients on the gift date. Four: the donor's CGT computation showing base cost of growth shares (typically zero or nominal where growth shares were issued at incorporation at £1 par), market value at gift, minority-and-marketability discounts applied, chargeable gain, annual exempt amount, rate applied, tax due.     answer: "Pre-incorporation: founder's estate £2.5 million (£2.1m property portfolio + £400k other), exposed to the full 40% IHT charge above NRB + RNRB allowances, total exposure roughly £600,000 to £800,000 depending on RNRB taper. IHT on £750k after allowances: approximately £100,000 (against the £600k-800k counterfactual). Net IHT saving £500k-700k across the 15-year window, against the CGT-on-incorporation-and-gift cost of approximately £150,000-250,000. The value-freeze pays comfortably on portfolios at this scale and time horizon."

<p>The Family Investment Company is one of the most-discussed and most-misunderstood structures in UK landlord IHT planning. The structural move it enables, the value-freeze, is straightforward in concept and powerful in effect: the founder restructures their property portfolio inside a corporate wrapper, retains preference shares with a fixed coupon (value frozen at the structural level), and gifts growth shares (entitled to all future capital growth in the portfolio) to the next generation. After seven years, all future growth in the underlying portfolio accrues outside the founder's estate; the founder's IHT footprint on the portfolio component is capped at the frozen preference-share value.</p>

<p>This page is the strategic IHT framing of that mechanic. It is deliberately not the operational walkthrough. The operational layers (articles drafting, governance discipline, retirement-income coupon design, the share-gift mechanics at the point of gift, the blended-family planning use case) live on the Bucket A FIC pages, and we cross-link each at the appropriate moment without re-walking their ground. The intent is to keep this page narrow on what the FIC does for IHT and where it sits against the direct PET gift (Wave 4 C4) and the CLT into discretionary trust (Wave 4 C10) routes in the broader landlord IHT toolkit.</p>

<p>For the wider planning lens that connects FIC, direct gifting, trust settlement, life cover in trust, deed of variation, charitable legacy, and the pension overlay from April 2027, see <a href="/blog/landlord-tax-essentials/iht-property-investors-decision-framework-2026-onwards">An IHT Decision Framework for UK Landlords</a>. The IHT-specific BPR-and-FIC question (why a property FIC almost never qualifies for BPR despite the corporate wrapper) is at <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/fic-iht-treatment-bpr-myth">FIC IHT Treatment: The BPR Myth</a>.</p>

<h2>The value-freeze in one paragraph</h2>

<p>The founder restructures the property portfolio inside a FIC with two share classes that separate value from growth. Preference shares carry a fixed-coupon dividend (typically a defined £-amount per share per year), pay out of the FIC's post-CT profits, and are valued at the discounted income stream they represent. Their value is structurally capped: even as the FIC's underlying portfolio doubles in value, the preference shares' market value does not move in proportion because their entitlement is fixed at the coupon rate. Growth shares carry no fixed coupon but are entitled to all the capital growth of the FIC's underlying portfolio above the preference-share entitlement, and typically also carry the voting control. The founder retains the preference shares; the growth shares are gifted to the next generation as Potentially Exempt Transfers under s.3A IHTA 1984.</p>

<p>The result, after seven years of survival from the share gift: the founder's estate is "frozen" at the preference-share value plus any non-FIC assets they still hold personally. The portfolio's future growth is in the growth shareholders' hands outside the founder's IHT base. The portfolio itself is intact inside the FIC, running normally with the founder typically as a director retaining day-to-day control.</p>

<h2>Why this works above approximately £2,000,000</h2>

<p>The value-freeze structure has structural costs (incorporation, articles-of-association drafting, ongoing corporate governance, annual filings, accountancy, professional fees on the share-gift) that scale roughly linearly with the complexity of the structure but only loosely with the portfolio size. The IHT saving from the value-freeze scales with the portfolio's future growth, which is a fraction of the portfolio's current value compounded over the planning horizon. The economics work above approximately £2,000,000 of portfolio value with a planning horizon of 10+ years; below that scale the structural cost ratio rises and simpler routes (direct PET on individual properties, life cover in trust to fund eventual IHT, deed of variation on first death) are usually more proportionate.</p>

<p>The £2,000,000 threshold also lines up with the RNRB taper. Section 8D(5) IHTA 1984 measures the estate at the section 5 broad definition and withdraws RNRB at £1 for every £2 of estate value above £2 million. A portfolio landlord at £2.5 million today, projected to grow to £4 million over 15 years, would lose £1 million of RNRB to the taper at that point under no-action. The value-freeze removes the future growth from the estate and preserves the RNRB available at second death (assuming the spouse exemption defers IHT through the founder's death).</p>

<h2>The seven-year PET clock on the share gift</h2>

<p>The growth-share gift to the next generation is a Potentially Exempt Transfer under s.3A IHTA 1984. The seven-year clock starts on the date of the share gift, not on the date of the FIC formation. The FIC formation itself is not a chargeable transfer for IHT purposes: the founder transfers property into a company they own, which is not a transfer of value to a third party.</p>

<p>Full IHT exemption applies on the share gift if the founder survives seven complete years from the gift date. Within seven years, the gift fails as a PET and comes back into the estate at the value at the date of the gift (not at the date of death). The s.7(4) IHTA 1984 taper relief reduces the IHT payable (not the gift value) on a sliding scale between years 3 and 7: 80% of the full IHT rate at 3 to 4 years, 60% at 4 to 5, 40% at 5 to 6, 20% at 6 to 7, zero at 7+.</p>

<p>The taper relief mechanic is the most-commonly-misunderstood feature of the 7-year clock: the gift value does not reduce with time. What reduces is the proportion of the full-rate IHT payable. For a £1.4 million growth-share gift that has used the donor's £325,000 NRB and is now in the failed-PET chargeable band, a year-5 death attracts 40% taper-adjusted on £1,075,000 chargeable = £172,000 of IHT against the £430,000 (full-rate) counterfactual. The taper is real but only partial within the 7-year window.</p>

<p>For the share-gift mechanics at the point of gift (the s.17 TCGA 1992 valuation with hurdle / minority / marketability discounts, the s.165 unavailability for investment FIC under Sch 7, the ITTOIA 2005 s.624 + s.629 settlements treatment of dividend rights on growth shares, the 8-document checklist), see Wave 4 A9 at <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/fic-gifting-shares-children-property-7-year-iht-mechanics">FIC Growth-Share Gifting: 7-Year IHT Mechanics</a>.</p>

<h2>The IHT footprint after the value-freeze</h2>

<p>After the structure is in place and the 7-year clock has cleared, the founder's IHT-exposed assets are:</p>

<ul>
<li>The preference shares (capped at the discounted-coupon-stream value, typically £100,000 to £500,000 on a portfolio of £2 to £5 million depending on coupon design).</li>
<li>Any non-FIC assets the founder still holds personally (family home, cash, pensions in scope from April 2027, ISAs).</li>
<li>Any non-FIC property that was not transferred in at incorporation.</li>
</ul>

<p>The founder's IHT footprint is the sum of these. The underlying property portfolio inside the FIC, and all its future growth, is in the growth shareholders' hands outside the founder's estate. For a successful value-freeze on a £2.5 million portfolio with a 15-year horizon to projected £4 million at the founder's death, the IHT-exposed estate at death is typically in the £500,000 to £1,000,000 range (preference shares plus family home plus non-FIC assets), against the £4 million unmanaged counterfactual.</p>

<h2>Why a property FIC does not get BPR</h2>

<p>The s.105(3) IHTA 1984 test for Business Property Relief requires the business to be wholly or mainly trading. Pawson v HMRC [2013] UKUT 050 (TCC) is the settled authority that residential letting is investment, not trading, regardless of the level of owner involvement. The corporate wrapper does not change the underlying business character: a FIC holding residential investment property is itself an investment business by substance. The s.105(3) test reads through to the underlying activity, not the legal structure.</p>

<p>BPR is therefore denied on both the founder's preference shares (which represent an interest in the investment business) and the gifted growth shares (which also represent an interest in the same investment business). The value-freeze route does not deliver BPR; it delivers the structural separation of value from growth and removes future growth from the founder's estate via the 7-year-PET-clock mechanic on the growth-share gift. Our Wave 4 page at <a href="/blog/landlord-tax-essentials/bpr-pure-btl-pawson-test-why-buy-to-let-fails-investment-line">Why Pure Buy-to-Let Fails BPR</a> covers the Pawson investment line in detail, and <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/fic-iht-treatment-bpr-myth">FIC IHT Treatment: The BPR Myth</a> covers the corporate-wrapper version specifically.</p>

<p>The April 2026 £1m BPR/APR cap is therefore not directly relevant to a property FIC, because there was no BPR to cap in the first place. The cap matters for genuine BPR-qualifying assets (qualifying trading businesses, agricultural property, AIM portfolios, serviced accommodation businesses clearing the Pawson trading bar). For the cap mechanics see Wave 4 C8 at <a href="/blog/landlord-tax-essentials/iht-1m-bpr-apr-cap-mixed-trading-investing-landlord-allocation">£1m BPR/APR Cap: Allocation Across Mixed Landlord Estates</a>.</p>

<h2>GROB risk on a property FIC</h2>

<p>Section 102 FA 1986 reservation-of-benefit applies to the growth-share gift if the founder retains a benefit derived from the gifted shares. The headline scenarios where GROB bites on a property FIC:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Founder uses one of the underlying properties personally.</strong> The classic case: the FIC holds a coastal property that the founder occasionally uses as a holiday home. The reservation argument is that the founder enjoys a benefit derived from the gifted growth shares (continuing use of the underlying property), and s.102(1)(b) entire-exclusion test fails.</li>
<li><strong>Founder lives in a property the FIC owns.</strong> The FIC holds the family home (or a property the founder occupies as principal residence). The reservation argument is the same as above but stronger because the use is permanent rather than occasional.</li>
<li><strong>Founder has discretionary access to FIC's cash beyond the preference coupon.</strong> The FIC's surplus cash is, at the founder's instruction as director, distributed to the founder in ways that go beyond the preference-share entitlement. The reservation argument is that the founder retains effective economic control over the FIC's value.</li>
</ul>

<p>The counter for each is structural and documentary:</p>

<ul>
<li>Founder pays full market rent to the FIC for any personal use of FIC properties, with a written tenancy agreement at commercial rate, reviewed periodically, with bank evidence of rent paid and the FIC declaring the income on its CT return. Mirrors the rent-payment-out test under s.102 for direct property gifts (see our Wave 4 page on GROB for gifted let property).</li>
<li>The family home is held outside the FIC. FIC properties are exclusively let to third-party tenants in the normal way. The founder's residence is separate from the FIC's investment portfolio.</li>
<li>The FIC's cash is distributed via the preference-share coupon only. Discretionary distributions to the founder beyond the coupon are not made. The governance discipline (covered on Wave 4 A7) maintains the line.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Comparison table: FIC value-freeze vs CLT into trust vs direct property PET</h2>

<p>The three routes positioned on a £2,000,000 portfolio for a founder in their early 60s with normal life expectancy:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Entry IHT.</strong> FIC value-freeze: zero (FIC formation not a transfer; growth-share gift is a PET). CLT into discretionary trust: 20% IHT on value above NRB (£325,000 above NRB = £65,000 entry IHT on £2m settlement, though typical trust settlements are partial rather than full portfolio so the figure varies). Direct property PET: zero (PET).</li>
<li><strong>Entry CGT.</strong> FIC value-freeze: CGT crystallises on incorporation (without s.162 incorporation relief, which is typically unavailable for investment BTL by substance), plus CGT on the share gift at market value (minority-discounted). Total CGT typically £100,000 to £200,000 on the worked £2m portfolio with mixed base costs. CLT into discretionary trust: zero immediate CGT under s.260 holdover (where non-settlor-interested). Direct property PET: CGT at s.17 market value (£40,000 to £80,000 on a single £400,000 property in the portfolio).</li>
<li><strong>Ongoing structure cost.</strong> FIC value-freeze: CT on rental profits at 25% main rate, corporate filings, annual governance. Trust: 10-year periodic charges to 6% of chargeable trust value above NRB, exit charges on capital distributions. Direct property PET: none (property is in the donee's hands).</li>
<li><strong>Founder retains income?</strong> FIC value-freeze: yes, via preference-share coupon. Trust: no (the trust is for beneficiaries other than the settlor in the typical non-settlor-interested structure). Direct property PET: no (property is gone).</li>
<li><strong>Founder retains control?</strong> FIC value-freeze: yes, typically via director powers and preference-share voting rights, retained while growth shares get economic upside. Trust: no (trustees have discretion). Direct property PET: no.</li>
<li><strong>7-year clock?</strong> FIC value-freeze: yes, runs on the growth-share gift. Trust: 7-year top-up of additional IHT to bring rate to 40% if settlor dies within 7 years, but the trust property itself is in the trust regardless. Direct property PET: yes, runs on the gift date.</li>
<li><strong>Future growth removed from estate?</strong> FIC value-freeze: yes (growth shareholders take the future growth). Trust: yes (trust takes the future growth). Direct property PET: yes (donee takes the future growth on that single property).</li>
</ul>

<p>The right route depends on the founder's profile and the family's priorities. For a £2m+ portfolio landlord wanting to retain income and control while removing future growth from the estate, the FIC value-freeze is typically the best fit. For a smaller settlement of a single high-gain property where the founder wants zero immediate CGT, the CLT-into-trust route with s.260 holdover is often better. For a single property the founder wants out of the estate quickly with no ongoing structure, the direct PET works.</p>

<p>The decision is rarely a clean either-or. Many landlord estates combine routes: a FIC value-freeze on the core portfolio, supported by direct PET gifts of individual non-FIC properties, and a deed-of-variation route on first death to settle a portion of the surviving spouse's inheritance into a discretionary trust within the deceased's NRB. The combined approach gives flexibility across time horizons and family circumstances.</p>
