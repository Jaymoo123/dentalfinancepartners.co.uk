  - question: "What changes on 6 April 2027 that flips the decumulation order?"
    answer: "From 6 April 2027 unused defined-contribution pension funds (and most unused DB lump-sum death benefits) form part of the deceased's estate for inheritance tax. The reform was announced at Autumn Budget 2024, with the technical consultation outcome published in 2025, and the effective date is on the face of the upcoming Finance Act 2026. Pre-2027 the pension fund was IHT-free, so the textbook decumulation order for a landlord with rental income plus a pension was to spend the rental income first (taxable at marginal income tax rates) and leave the pension untouched (IHT-free legacy). From April 2027 both asset classes are in the IHT base. The optimal sequence is no longer obvious and depends on the landlord's marginal income tax rate in retirement, the CGT position on the BTL portfolio, the second-death RNRB taper position, and the time over which the decumulation plays out. The rule-mechanic background is on our Wave 2 page; this page is the sequencing strategy applied."
  - question: "Why does 65 to 80 matter as the planning window?"
    answer: "Because most decumulation sequencing happens in that 15-year band and because the £2,000,000 RNRB taper threshold (s.8D IHTA 1984) is the strategic floor below which most landlord couples want to land their estate by the second death. A 65-year-old landlord with a £900,000 pension and a £1,600,000 BTL portfolio is well above the £2,000,000 threshold today; without active decumulation the position deteriorates each year as both asset classes grow. By age 80 (the typical median life-expectancy band for a fit 65-year-old today) the unmanaged estate is likely to be £3,500,000-plus, with the full second-death IHT bill in the £900,000 to £1,200,000 range. The decumulation sequencing decision made at 65 sets the trajectory: 15 years of pension drawdown plus measured property disposals can land the estate at second death below the taper threshold with full RNRB preserved. The same starting position with passive decumulation lands above the threshold with RNRB tapered out and a six-figure IHT bill that could have been avoided."
  - question: "What does sell-property-first mean and how does the sequence work?"
    answer: "Sell-property-first means liquidating BTL properties in measured tranches over the early retirement years (typically ages 65 to 75), realising the latent CGT gains under the 18% / 24% rate structure for residential property, using the proceeds to fund living costs, and leaving the pension to draw down at basic-rate income tax later in retirement (typically 76 to 84). The sequence pairs with the pre-2027 BTL CGT base-cost reset that does not happen if held to death post-2027 (because the pension reform leaves the BTL death-uplift under TCGA 1992 s.62 intact, but the IHT base on the BTL is unchanged either way). Worked: sell two BTL flats per three-year window, total realised £1.6 million over a decade, CGT bill £160,000 to £220,000 across the period at the marginal residential rate. Pension drawn at basic-rate band (£12,570 personal allowance plus £37,700 basic band) in years 11 to 15 at minimal income tax cost. Estate at second death back to family home plus residual pension plus accumulated cash, often below the £2,000,000 taper threshold."
  - question: "What does drawdown-first mean and how does the sequence work?"
    answer: "Drawdown-first means using the pension as the primary income source in early retirement (ages 65 to 77), drawing within the basic-rate income tax band to minimise the income tax friction (£37,700 of basic-band plus £12,570 personal allowance gives effective gross pension drawdown of £50,270 a year at 20% marginal cost on the gross above the personal allowance). Combined with continuing rental income from the unchanged BTL portfolio, the landlord lives comfortably and the pension is drawn down to zero by age 77 to 80. The BTL portfolio is preserved intact during this window. At second death, the pension is gone (no IHT on it), the BTL portfolio is intact (£1.6 million plus growth, deemed disposal at death uplifted base under s.62 so no CGT for the beneficiaries on inheritance), and the IHT bill is computed on property plus residual cash. The route leaves a heavier property-side estate but no pension-side IHT. The trade-off versus sell-property-first depends on the marginal rates and the family's preference for inheriting properties versus cash."

  - question: "Why is the sell-property-first route so much cheaper on the Reid figures?"
    answer: "The £600,000 latent CGT gain on the BTL portfolio is taxed at 24% (£144,000) if realised in life under the sell-property route, versus £0 if held to death (s.62 base cost uplift). But the BTL portfolio that is held to death faces 40% IHT on its full death-date value (£2.5m), against the much smaller IHT on the realised-cash-and-residual-pension estate at second death. The 24% versus 40% rate gap, combined with the loss of RNRB to the taper on the held-portfolio route, produces the £800,000 spread. The arithmetic is sensitive to inputs: a lower CGT base cost on the portfolio (higher latent gain) makes the sell-now route relatively more expensive; a higher property growth rate makes the held-portfolio route relatively more expensive on the IHT side. For most mid-to-large landlord estates above £2m the sell-property-first route wins; for small estates near the NRB or RNRB threshold the calculus can be close or reversed."
  - question: "What about a hybrid sequence (mix of pension draw and property sales)?"
    answer: "Often the optimal route in practice. A pure sell-property-first route concentrates the CGT bill in years 1 to 10, which can push the donor's income above the higher-rate threshold and trigger additional rate exposure on rental income. A pure drawdown-first route leaves the full BTL portfolio exposed to second-death IHT. A measured hybrid (sell one BTL every two to three years across ages 65-78, draw pension within the basic-rate band each year, gift smaller amounts annually within the £3,000 annual exemption) usually produces a lower lifetime total tax bill than either extreme. The hybrid takes more advisory work to model annually. The principle: each year of retirement is its own tax-band optimisation, and the right sequence is one that uses the basic-rate band on pension drawdown each year and times CGT realisations in lower-income years if possible. Our forthcoming page on FIC value-freezing (Wave 4 C7) covers the structural alternative where the BTL portfolio is moved into a corporate wrapper at age 65 to 70, separating the value-freeze from the decumulation question entirely."
  - question: "How does the pension liability get reported to HMRC after April 2027?"
    answer: "The Personal Representatives of the deceased's estate carry primary liability for reporting and paying the IHT on the pension component, per the published consultation outcome. The mechanic is integrated into the standard IHT400 process: the pension fund value at death is reported on a dedicated IHT400 schedule (the technical detail of which schedule and which form sits where is being finalised in HMRC's consultation cycle), the pension scheme administrator provides a death-value statement to the PRs, and the IHT is paid as part of the overall estate liability either from estate funds or by the PRs requesting payment from the pension scheme administrator under the Direct Payment Scheme. The scheme administrator is not the primary debtor (industry's lobbied position succeeded), but the PRs may need information from the scheme administrator within the IHT400 deadlines (typically 12 months from end of month of death). Practical implication: PRs need scheme administrator contact details on file with the deceased's records, and the scheme administrator should be informed of the death promptly so the death-value statement is available."
  - question: "Does the spouse exemption still apply where I leave the pension to my spouse?"
    answer: "Yes. Section 18 IHTA 1984 spouse exemption applies to pension assets passing to the surviving spouse on death, exactly as it does to property and other estate assets. The reform changes what is in the estate, not the exemptions that operate on it. A landlord who nominates the surviving spouse as the beneficiary of the unused DC pension pays no first-death IHT on the pension; the spouse inherits it and the IHT exposure defers to second death. For most couples this is the operationally simplest position because it removes any pension-side IHT charge at first death entirely. The second-death effect is that the pension (plus growth) is then in the surviving spouse's estate at second death, and the decumulation strategy that the surviving spouse runs in their remaining lifetime drives the eventual IHT outcome. Spouse exemption applies symmetrically to pre-A-Day registered scheme arrangements and to post-A-Day SIPPs and personal pensions."
  - question: "Does drawing down the pension in life remove it from the estate?"
    answer: "Yes for the amount actually drawn (the cash drawn becomes part of the landlord's lifetime spending, gifting, or saving balance), but the cash retains the same IHT character as any other cash holding. Drawn pension cash sat in a bank account at death is in the estate at 40% IHT. Drawn pension cash gifted as a PET and surviving 7 years is outside the estate (subject to the s.21 IHTA 1984 normal expenditure out of income exemption for regular small gifts, which is a separate route). The reform-driven planning route is to draw down the pension during the landlord's lifetime, pay basic-rate income tax on the drawdown (20% on the band above the personal allowance), and then use the drawn cash actively (lifestyle spending, gifts as PETs, or charitable contributions). The combination of drawing the pension at 20% income tax plus the IHT-free outcome on PET-survival is materially cheaper than the 40% IHT on the un-drawn pension at death."
  - question: "What about charity nominations on the pension?"
    answer: "Pension benefits nominated to a UK-registered charity (or EEA-equivalent under the post-Brexit transitional rules) pass to the charity free of IHT under s.23 IHTA 1984. The reform does not change the charity exemption. For estates well above the £2,000,000 threshold where the RNRB is being tapered out anyway, a charity nomination of 10% of the pension (or 10% of the relevant baseline amount on the estate as a whole) can also trigger the Sch 1A 36% reduced rate on the chargeable estate. The 36% rate mechanics are covered in our forthcoming page on charitable legacy and the reduced rate (Wave 4 C9). The combination of basic-rate-band pension drawdown in life plus a charity nomination on residual pension at death plus a charity-funded 36% rate on the rest of the estate is a strong route for landlord estates above £2.5 million."
  - question: "How does gifting BTL property compare as a decumulation tool?"
    answer: "Gifting a BTL property to the next generation as a PET (with the 7-year clock) is a third decumulation route that pairs cleanly with the sell-property route or replaces it on properties with high latent gains and low rental yields. The CGT cost is the same as selling (s.17 deemed market value disposal between connected persons, no s.165 holdover for non-business BTL), but the property leaves the estate at gift rather than at realised cash. The route is most valuable in mid-life (45 to 58) where survival probability is high (covered in our 7-year-clock page); at 65-plus the survival probability over 7 years drops materially and the gift route becomes less efficient than the sale route. For the 65-year-old landlord the typical pattern is: sell one to two BTL properties in early retirement (cash for living costs), gift one to two properties (start the clock, hedged with 7-year decreasing-term life cover in trust), draw down pension at basic rate, leave the residual portfolio for the spouse and the children to deal with on the spouse-exemption-deferred second death."
  - question: "What if my pension is mostly in a defined-benefit scheme?"
    answer: "The reform applies primarily to defined-contribution unused funds and most DB lump-sum death benefits. A DB scheme that pays a recurring dependant's pension (rather than a lump sum) on the member's death is largely outside the reform: the dependant's pension is income to the dependant, taxed at the dependant's marginal income tax rate, with no IHT. The decumulation sequence question is therefore narrower for DB-heavy landlords: the BTL portfolio is still in the IHT base, but the DB pension is not (provided the death benefit is structured as a recurring pension, not a lump sum). The right sequence for a DB-heavy landlord with a BTL portfolio is usually a measured property-sale-first route over early retirement, with the DB recurring pension covering basic living costs from year one and the realised property cash funding additional spending and lifetime gifts. The hybrid analysis above still applies; the DC versus DB mix simply changes the relative magnitude of the pension-side IHT exposure."

<p>From 6 April 2027 unused defined-contribution pension funds and most unused defined-benefit lump-sum death benefits enter the deceased's estate for inheritance tax. The reform was announced at Autumn Budget 2024 and the effective date sits on the upcoming Finance Act 2026. Our Wave 2 walkthrough at <a href="/blog/landlord-tax-essentials/pension-iht-april-2027-landlord-estate-planning">Pension IHT from April 2027</a> sets out the rule mechanics, the spouse-exemption position, the post-75 income-tax overlay, and the RNRB taper interaction.</p>

<p>This page answers a different question. With both the pension fund and the BTL portfolio inside the IHT base from age 67 onwards, which asset class does the retiring-age landlord draw on first? The pre-2027 textbook answer ("use pension last") is structurally undermined and the new sequence is not obvious without working through the numbers. Below: why 65 to 80 is the planning window, the two pure decumulation routes (sell property first vs draw pension first), a worked total-cost comparison on the Reid persona (£2.9m joint estate), the hybrid route that usually wins in practice, the pension-liability reporting mechanics on death so families know what happens administratively, and the cross-route comparison against direct property gifting (Wave 4 C4) and FIC value-freezing (Wave 4 C7).</p>

<h2>Why 65 to 80 is the planning window</h2>

<p>Most decumulation sequencing decisions for landlord couples play out in the 65 to 80 age band. The defaults set at 65 (when the pension fund first becomes accessible and the landlord typically starts thinking about reducing rental management workload) drive the trajectory through to second death. The £2,000,000 RNRB taper threshold under s.8D(5) IHTA 1984 is the strategic floor: a couple's combined estate landing below the threshold at second death keeps RNRB available in full (£175,000 plus £175,000 transferable = £350,000 against the residence); landing above the threshold withdraws RNRB at £1 for every £2 over and triggers a six-figure additional IHT exposure on a typical landlord estate.</p>

<p>A 65-year-old portfolio landlord with a £900,000 SIPP, a £1,600,000 BTL portfolio, and a £400,000 family home is at a joint estate of £2,900,000 today, well above the £2,000,000 threshold. Passive holding sees the estate grow at roughly 3% real over the 15-year decumulation window, landing at second death (typical age 85) at £3,500,000 to £4,000,000. The second-death IHT bill on the passive position, against £650,000 combined NRB and zero RNRB (tapered out), is in the order of £1,000,000 to £1,200,000. The same starting position with active decumulation can land below the threshold with full RNRB preserved and the second-death IHT bill at £200,000 or less. The decision made at 65 (which asset class to draw on, in what sequence, over what timescale) is the binding constraint on the eventual outcome.</p>

<h2>Route one: sell property first</h2>

<p>The sell-property-first sequence runs measured BTL disposals across the first decade of retirement and leaves the pension to draw down at basic-rate income tax in the second decade. The pattern looks like this for a five-flat £1,600,000 portfolio held since the early 2000s with a £600,000 aggregate latent gain:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Ages 65 to 68:</strong> sell two of the five BTL flats (the highest-yield and most-management-intensive). Realised proceeds approximately £640,000, latent gain on these two approximately £240,000. CGT bill at 24% residential rate on the gain (the landlord is still in higher rates given the remaining rental income) approximately £56,000. Net proceeds £584,000 added to cash reserves.</li>
<li><strong>Ages 69 to 72:</strong> sell a third flat. Proceeds £350,000, gain £130,000, CGT £30,000. Two flats remain.</li>
<li><strong>Ages 73 to 75:</strong> sell a fourth flat. Proceeds £400,000, gain £160,000, CGT £38,000. One flat remains (often kept for the surviving spouse's ongoing income or for legacy purposes).</li>
<li><strong>Ages 76 to 85:</strong> pension drawdown begins. Annual drawdown £40,000 each (within the basic-rate band of £12,570 personal allowance plus £37,700 basic band for each spouse). Income tax £5,486 each per year (basic rate of 20% on the £27,430 above personal allowance). Cumulative income tax over 10 years: approximately £55,000 each, total £110,000 across the couple. Pension fund drawn to near-zero by age 85.</li>
</ul>

<p>Aggregate decumulation tax cost across the 20-year window: CGT £124,000 plus income tax £110,000 = £234,000. Second-death position (age 85): residual one BTL flat £550,000 (assumed 3% growth on the held-to-death property), family home £550,000, cash £400,000 (mid-window spent down from the realised proceeds), residual pension £100,000. Total estate £1,600,000. Below the £2,000,000 threshold. RNRB available £350,000 in full. NRB available £650,000. Taxable estate £600,000. IHT at 40% = £240,000. Total tax across decumulation plus death: £474,000.</p>

<h2>Route two: draw pension first</h2>

<p>The pension-first sequence preserves the BTL portfolio intact through retirement and runs the pension as the primary income source. Same Reid couple, same starting position:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Ages 65 to 77:</strong> pension drawdown £45,000 each per year (basic-rate band plus a small slice of higher-rate). Cumulative income tax over 12 years approximately £130,000 across the couple. Pension fund of £900,000 drawn to near-zero by age 77. Combined with continuing rental income from the unchanged £1,600,000 BTL portfolio, the couple's living standard is comfortable throughout.</li>
<li><strong>Ages 78 to 85:</strong> rental income alone funds remaining retirement. No further pension to draw. No active property sales. Modest annual gifting within the £3,000 annual exemption (£6,000 across the couple per year, cumulating to roughly £42,000 over the seven years, gifted to adult children outside the IHT base).</li>
</ul>

<p>Aggregate decumulation tax cost across the 20-year window: income tax £130,000. CGT zero (no property sales). Second-death position (age 85): five BTL flats now worth approximately £2,500,000 (3% growth on the £1.6m original portfolio), family home £550,000, cash £100,000, residual pension zero. Total estate £3,150,000. Well above the £2,000,000 threshold. RNRB tapered out entirely (£1,150,000 over the threshold withdraws £575,000 of combined RNRB, which exceeds the £350,000 available, so RNRB is zero). NRB available £650,000. Taxable estate £2,500,000. IHT at 40% = £1,000,000. CGT zero for the beneficiaries on inheritance (death uplift under s.62 TCGA 1992 wipes the latent gain). Total tax across decumulation plus death: £1,130,000.</p>

<h2>The comparison: £656,000 cheaper to sell property first</h2>

<p>On the Reid figures: sell-property-first total tax £474,000; draw-pension-first total tax £1,130,000; the sell-property route is £656,000 cheaper. The headline driver is the £2,500,000 of BTL property remaining in the estate on the draw-pension route, which faces 40% IHT on its full death-date value, against the £550,000 single residual flat on the sell-property route. The CGT cost of selling (£124,000 in life at 24% effective rate) is much smaller than the IHT cost of holding to death (£1,000,000 at 40% on the un-realised position), and that 16-percentage-point gap, applied to £1.6m of property value, is the structural reason the sell route wins on this estate size.</p>

<p>The arithmetic is sensitive to inputs. Three sensitivities matter most:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Property growth rate.</strong> Higher growth makes the held-portfolio route relatively more expensive on the IHT side. Lower growth (or a downturn) reduces the IHT exposure on the held portfolio and narrows the spread.</li>
<li><strong>CGT base cost.</strong> A higher base cost (smaller latent gain) reduces the CGT cost on the sell route. A lower base cost (larger latent gain) makes the sell route relatively more expensive. On a portfolio acquired recently with little latent gain, the sell route is very cheap; on a portfolio held since the 1990s with high latent gains, the CGT cost on the sell route can rise to 30% to 35% of realised proceeds and the spread narrows.</li>
<li><strong>Family structure and legacy preference.</strong> Some landlord families have a strong preference for inheriting the actual properties rather than cash, on family-business or sentimental grounds. The sell-property route delivers cash to the next generation (clean, divisible, easily invested or spent); the draw-pension route delivers the property portfolio intact (operationally heavier, but the property itself passes to the family). The £656,000 difference on the Reid figures is the price of that preference at this estate size.</li>
</ul>

<h2>The hybrid: usually the right answer in practice</h2>

<p>A pure sell-property-first route concentrates CGT in the early retirement years, potentially pushing the landlord into the higher-rate band on rental income, which raises the effective CGT rate on the next disposal. A pure draw-pension-first route exposes the full BTL portfolio to the worst-case IHT outcome. The hybrid that usually wins in practice:</p>

<ol>
<li><strong>Annual pension drawdown to fill the basic-rate band.</strong> Each spouse draws roughly £37,700 of taxable pension income per year above their personal allowance, paying 20% on the gross. Across the couple this consumes approximately £900,000 of pension over 12 years at low marginal income tax cost.</li>
<li><strong>One BTL disposal every 2 to 3 years across the early-to-mid retirement window.</strong> Realises CGT in measured chunks, uses the £3,000 annual CGT exemption each year, keeps the landlord below the higher-rate threshold for the year of disposal, and steadily reduces the property estate exposure.</li>
<li><strong>Annual gifts of £6,000 (combined £3,000 each spouse annual exemption) plus larger gifts under the normal-expenditure-out-of-income exemption (s.21 IHTA 1984) where the rental income comfortably exceeds normal living costs.</strong> Each gift starts its own 7-year clock and reduces the eventual second-death estate.</li>
<li><strong>One or two larger BTL gifts to adult children as PETs in mid-retirement (ages 65 to 68 ideally), hedged with 7-year decreasing-term life cover in trust.</strong> The mid-life corridor for these gifts is covered on our companion page at <a href="/blog/landlord-tax-essentials/iht-7-year-clock-property-gifting-mid-life-landlord-strategy">The 7-Year Clock for Mid-Life Landlords</a>.</li>
</ol>

<p>The hybrid is more advisory-intensive (each year's tax position is its own optimisation) and benefits from a five-year rolling plan that is revised annually against the actual realised positions. The output is usually a total-tax cost 10% to 25% lower than either pure route.</p>

<h2>The FIC alternative: structural value-freeze instead of decumulation</h2>

<p>For landlords with portfolios of £1.5 million plus and a strong preference for keeping the family in the property business rather than realising it to cash, the Family Investment Company route is the structural alternative. The donor transfers the BTL portfolio into a FIC at incorporation (incorporation relief under s.162 TCGA 1992 is rarely available for pure BTL but worth checking against the Ramsay business-substance test), retains preference shares with a frozen-coupon dividend providing retirement income, and gifts growth shares to the next generation as PETs starting the 7-year clock.</p>

<p>The FIC route does not solve the pension-IHT-from-April-2027 question in itself (the pension is separate from the FIC structure). It does change the property-side decumulation question: with the portfolio inside a FIC, the founder is taking dividend income on preference shares (frozen value, no growth, fully in the founder's estate as the share value) rather than realising property to cash. The growth in property value over retirement accrues to the growth shareholders (the next generation) outside the founder's estate from the gift date plus 7 years. The pension-side decision can then run on its own merits: drawdown to basic rate, charity nominations on the residual, spouse-exemption deferral to second death. The FIC route's IHT framing is on our forthcoming page (Wave 4 C7).</p>

<h2>The pension-liability reporting mechanics on death</h2>

<p>From 6 April 2027 the Personal Representatives of the deceased's estate carry primary liability for reporting and paying the IHT on the unused pension fund, per the published consultation outcome. The mechanic integrates into the existing IHT400 estate-return process:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Within 12 months of the end of the month of death,</strong> the PRs file the IHT400 with a dedicated schedule for the pension component (the form numbering is being finalised by HMRC in the implementation guidance; the principle is that the pension is on the IHT400 return, not on a separate trust or pension form).</li>
<li><strong>The pension scheme administrator provides a death-value statement</strong> to the PRs giving the unused fund value at the date of death. The statement is the source document for the IHT400 schedule; PRs need scheme administrator contact details on file with the deceased's records.</li>
<li><strong>The IHT on the pension component is part of the overall estate IHT liability.</strong> The PRs pay it from estate funds in the normal way, or they can request payment from the pension scheme administrator under the Direct Payment Scheme that exists for current pre-2027 in-scope pensions (some discretionary scheme death benefits already fall in IHT). The Direct Payment Scheme avoids the PRs having to advance the IHT from their own funds.</li>
<li><strong>The PRs are the primary debtor for the IHT.</strong> The scheme administrator is not. Industry lobbied for the scheme administrator to be primary debtor in the original consultation; the published outcome confirmed PR primacy. Practical implication: PRs need to plan for the pension-IHT liability as part of the overall estate IHT cash-flow position, and the executors' insurance market is adapting to provide cover for the extended PR liability.</li>
</ul>

<p>The post-2027 estate administration is operationally heavier than the pre-2027 process. Families running a landlord estate should ensure that the deceased's will appoints PRs who are willing to take on the extended responsibility (typically professional executors charge more for post-2027 estates with pension components) and that the pension scheme administrator details are kept on file alongside the BTL portfolio documents and the family home title.</p>
