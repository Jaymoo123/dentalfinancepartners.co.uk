Paper Form ATED51 is the alternative, and it suits amendments that span multiple chargeable periods or interact with a disposal; you email it to paperforms.ated@hmrc.gov.uk with the supporting evidence as PDF attachments. Your amended return reports the new chargeable amount and relief position, and HMRC expects evidence behind it: the corrected valuation, the days-held calculation, the relief documentation, the tenancy agreements where relevant. A covering letter setting out why you are amending and your unprompted-disclosure position is good practice. The form does not strictly require it, but it helps a lot if HMRC opens an enquiry."

    answer: "Yes, on the same 12-month window. The usual change to a claim-only return is a switch from one relief to another (for example from Property Rental Business Relief under s.133 to Property Developer Relief under s.138 once the dwelling moves into a development pipeline), or a partial-year split between two reliefs. The amendment reports the new relief mix and the days each relief applied."
  - question: "We discovered a clawback event after the 12-month window had closed. What now?"
    answer: "Voluntary disclosure. The amendment route has closed at 12 months, so you put it right through an unprompted voluntary disclosure to HMRC's ATED Helpline. That is usually a letter setting out the facts, the corrected calculation, the additional tax due, and a proposed penalty position under FA 2009 Sch 24 or Sch 55 as applicable. Pay the tax and interest with the disclosure where you can. Unprompted disclosure carries lower penalty floors than HMRC-prompted disclosure throughout. Our <a href=\"/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/ated-relief-clawback-occupation-non-qualifying-individual\">clawback page</a> walks through the most common scenario in detail."
  - question: "Is filing an amendment an admission of inaccuracy?"
    answer: "Not in itself. Amending is exactly the mechanism HMRC built for correcting genuine errors and updating a return to reflect new facts (a relief you first missed, a valuation re-tested, days-held re-calculated). HMRC draws a line between (a) corrections you make before it has opened an enquiry, which are unprompted disclosures, and (b) corrections you make in response to an enquiry, which are prompted. The unprompted route engages the lower end of the FA 2009 Sch 24 penalty range and the unprompted-disclosure reductions; in many cases the careless-and-unprompted reduction takes the penalty to zero."
  - question: "Will HMRC charge interest on additional tax due via an amendment?"
    answer: "Yes. Late-paid ATED runs interest from the original 30 April due date until you pay, at HMRC's published rate (currently 7.75 per cent at the time of writing, though it moves with the Bank of England base rate plus 2.5 per cent). The interest is statutory, so HMRC has no discretion to waive it. Send payment of both the additional tax and the accrued interest with your amendment where you can; if not, HMRC will work out the interest and bill it separately."
  - question: "We overpaid ATED on the original return. How do we recover the overpayment?"
    answer: "Two routes. Inside the 12-month amendment window, amend the return to the correct figures and HMRC refunds the overpayment automatically (set against any other outstanding ATED liabilities first). Past 12 months, you claim overpayment relief under HMRC's standard framework, by letter to the ATED Helpline within four years of the end of the chargeable period. That four-year window is far longer than the 12-month amendment one, and it is well worth a look back over closed periods; recoveries on long-settled years are more common than you might think."
  - question: "A property was sold during the year. Does that trigger an amendment?"
    answer: "Yes. A disposal means you must file a Return of Adjusted Chargeable Amount (RACA) within 30 days of the disposal date, reporting the actual days you held the dwelling in the chargeable period and the matching apportioned charge. The RACA works as an in-year amendment to your original return. The buyer files their own acquisition-day-apportioned return within 30 days, and together the two filings cover the chargeable period with no gap."
  - question: "Will filing an amendment trigger an HMRC enquiry?"
    answer: "Sometimes. HMRC's enquiry-risk scoring treats an amended return as one input, but the amendment itself is not the trigger; what may prompt an enquiry is the underlying facts (the band change, the relief mix, the valuation re-test). Clean documentary support and a clear unprompted-disclosure covering letter bring your risk profile down a good deal. If an enquiry does open against an amended return, it runs on the standard FA 1998 Sch 18 timeline: a Notice of Enquiry within 12 months of the amendment date, with the closure notice timing depending on the case."
  - question: "Can we amend the same return more than once?"
    answer: "Yes. You can amend a single return more than once inside the 12-month window, each version updating the figures from the last. Keep the count down, though: a flurry of amendments in quick succession can read as data-quality trouble to HMRC's enquiry team. Where you can, gather the corrections into one amendment with all the supporting evidence behind it. And if genuinely new facts surface after a first amendment, a second is perfectly fine."
  - question: "HMRC has opened an enquiry. Can we still amend?"
    answer: "Not in the usual way. Once HMRC has opened an enquiry under FA 1998 Sch 18 paragraph 24, you cannot file an amendment that touches the enquiry's scope. You can still cooperate, hand over more information, and reach an agreed settlement, but the formal amendment route is suspended while the enquiry is live. If it settles, the closure notice reflects the agreed figures; if it does not, the appeal route is your next step. Our <a href=\"/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/ated-late-filing-penalty-appeal-reasonable-excuse\">appeals page</a> covers the FTT route."
  - question: "Does filing an amendment restart the FA 2009 Sch 55 penalty clock?"
    answer: "No. The penalty clock under FA 2009 Sch 55 runs from the original 30 April deadline, not from the amendment date. Amending inside the 12-month window does not extend or reset the underlying penalty calculation for any failure-to-file on the original return. If that return was late, the late-filing penalties keep running on their original schedule; amending a late-filed return does not cure the late-filing position."
  - question: "Are amendments visible to shareholders or directors via Companies House or other public records?"
    answer: "No. ATED returns and their amendments are confidential to HMRC and are not filed at Companies House. The directors of the holding company will see them through the company's tax compliance process; shareholders are not separately notified unless the company's own governance requires it. For overseas structures with several corporate layers, the trust deed or shareholders' agreement may set out reporting obligations to the ultimate beneficial owner."

<p>You filed the ATED return, then the facts moved: a tenant turns out to have been a connected person, a valuation re-tests over a band boundary, a relief no longer holds for the whole year. Correct it and the cost is usually just the right tax plus interest. Leave it for HMRC to find and the penalty band shifts from unprompted (which can be nil) to prompted, adding hundreds or thousands on top. The good news is that the amendment route is straightforward inside the 12-month window from the original 30 April deadline. Past that, two routes pick up the slack: overpayment relief if you paid too much, and unprompted voluntary disclosure if you underpaid.</p>

<p>If you need the wider regime first (who is chargeable, the bands, the reliefs, the return itself), our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/ated-complete-guide-2026-27">2026/27 ATED guide</a> sets it out. The single most common reason you will be amending, a non-qualifying individual occupying a relief-claimed dwelling, is covered step by step on our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/ated-relief-clawback-occupation-non-qualifying-individual">ATED relief clawback page</a>.</p>

<h2>When do you need to amend a filed ATED return?</h2>

<p>Six triggers come up again and again:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Relief mis-claimed.</strong> You claimed Property Rental Business Relief, then a review shows the tenant was a connected person for part of the year, or the commercial-terms paperwork does not actually support the claim. Your amended return reports the corrected relief days and pays the apportioned charge.</li>
  <li><strong>Valuation re-tested.</strong> You used a Knight Frank valuation of £1.95m, putting the dwelling in the £1m to £2m band. A later comparable sale, or a PRBC challenge, re-tests the value at £2.05m. The amended return moves the dwelling into the £2m to £5m band for the affected period.</li>
  <li><strong>Days-held correction.</strong> A mid-year acquisition return apportioned the charge over 197 days, but completion actually fell four days later, making the right figure 193 days. The amended return corrects the days and anything that flows from them.</li>
  <li><strong>Relief mix change.</strong> A dwelling sat under Property Rental Business Relief for the first half of the year, then switched to Property Developer Relief on 1 October when you decided to redevelop. The original return claimed one relief for the whole year; the amended return apportions days between the two.</li>
  <li><strong>Information error.</strong> A Land Registry title number went in wrong, the property address has a typo, or the ATED Reference Number for an earlier period was mis-stated. Errors that do not change the chargeable amount are still amendments.</li>
  <li><strong>Disposal during the year.</strong> Selling mid-period triggers a Return of Adjusted Chargeable Amount (RACA) within 30 days, which works as an in-year amendment to your original return.</li>
</ul>

<h2>How long is the ATED amendment window?</h2>

<p>You have <strong>12 months from the 30 April filing deadline</strong> of the original return. File the 2026/27 return by 30 April 2026 and you can amend it any time up to 30 April 2027. That holds whether the return was tax-due or claim-only relief. You can amend more than once inside the window, and each amendment supersedes the last.</p>

<p>If you filed the original return late, the window extends to the later of (a) 12 months from the 30 April original deadline or (b) three months from the date you actually filed. Say you filed the 2026/27 return late on 1 December 2026: the window runs to 30 April 2027 (the standard 12 months) or 1 March 2027 (three months from late filing), whichever is later. Here the 12-month date wins, so you have until 30 April 2027.</p>

<h2>How to amend: online service vs paper Form ATED51</h2>

<p>The online ATED service handles most single-period amendments. You sign in with the company's Government Gateway credentials, go to the relevant chargeable period, open the existing return and edit the figures. The system records the amendment, recalculates the chargeable amount, and either prompts for the extra tax or generates a refund where you have overpaid.</p>

<p>The paper route uses Form ATED51, emailed to <code>paperforms.ated@hmrc.gov.uk</code> with supporting evidence as PDF attachments. Reach for paper where the amendment spans multiple chargeable periods, involves a fiddly relief recalculation, or rests on significant valuation re-testing that needs a fuller documentary file. It also helps where Government Gateway access is limited, or where a different agent filed the original return and online continuity is awkward.</p>

<p>Whichever route you use, include:</p>

<ul>
  <li>The corrected figures with the calculation showing how they were derived.</li>
  <li>Evidence supporting the amendment (the new valuation, the tenancy documentation, the days-held log, the relief switch documentation).</li>
  <li>A covering letter setting out the reason for the amendment, the unprompted-disclosure position, and the proposed penalty position if any.</li>
  <li>Payment instruction or refund nomination where applicable.</li>
</ul>

<h2>What does HMRC expect to see?</h2>

<p>HMRC's published amendment-processing guidance (ATED Manual, ATED41200 onwards) points to three things:</p>

<ol>
  <li><strong>A clear reason for the amendment.</strong> What changed, and why. A bare numerical correction with no explanation pushes up your enquiry-risk score. A clear statement brings it down: "the original return claimed Property Rental Business Relief for the full year; review of the tenancy chain shows the director's adult child occupied the dwelling for 60 days; the amended return claims relief for the remaining 305 days and pays the apportioned charge for the 60 NQI days plus look-back / look-forward periods".</li>
  <li><strong>Documentary support.</strong> The amendment evidence in the categories above. Photocopies are fine; HMRC does not ask for certified originals.</li>
  <li><strong>Payment or refund nomination.</strong> Where extra tax is due, send payment (and interest) with the amendment where you can; where a refund is due, put the nomination details on the form.</li>
</ol>

<h2>What can you do after the 12-month window has closed?</h2>

<p>The amendment route shuts at 12 months, but you are not stuck. Which of the two remaining routes you use depends on whether you paid too much or too little.</p>

<h3>Overpayment relief (you paid more tax than was due)</h3>

<p>If the original return paid more than was due, say you missed a relief entirely or used the wrong band, you can claim overpayment relief by letter to HMRC's ATED Helpline within four years of the end of the chargeable period. So for the 2024/25 chargeable period (ending 31 March 2025) you have until 31 March 2029. The letter sets out the original position, the corrected figures, how much you overpaid, and why it was paid in the first place (usually an oversight or a misread of relief eligibility). HMRC reviews it and refunds, subject to satisfactory evidence. That four-year window is far longer than the 12-month amendment one, so it is well worth checking long-closed periods for tax you should never have paid.</p>

<h3>Unprompted voluntary disclosure (you underpaid tax)</h3>

<p>If the original return underpaid (a clawback you spotted after the window, an undisclosed band shift, a missed relief revocation), the route is unprompted voluntary disclosure to HMRC's ATED Helpline. The disclosure letter sets out:</p>

<ul>
  <li>The facts: what happened, when, and why it was not picked up sooner.</li>
  <li>The corrected calculation, with the additional tax due per period.</li>
  <li>The interest position (FA 2009 Sch 53 interest from the original due date).</li>
  <li>Your proposed penalty position under FA 2009 Sch 24 (inaccuracy) or Sch 55 (failure to file), with the prompted-versus-unprompted argument.</li>
  <li>Payment with the disclosure where you can.</li>
</ul>

<p>Unprompted disclosure carries lower penalty floors right across the FA 2009 schedules; the careful-and-unprompted band starts at <strong>zero per cent</strong>. Disclose within 12 months of the original deadline and you get the lowest floor of all; even beyond 12 months, the unprompted floor still sits well below the HMRC-prompted one. The point is simple: going to HMRC first almost always costs you less than HMRC coming to you.</p>

<h2>Amend or sit tight? What the penalty rules cost you</h2>

<p>The penalty maths is the strongest reason to put your hand up, through an amendment or a voluntary disclosure, rather than wait and hope HMRC never notices. Take one set of facts and run it three ways.</p>

<p><strong>Scenario A: you self-correct within the 12-month window.</strong> Your return claimed full relief; a review finds a 60-day NQI occupation. You amend within 12 months. Additional tax £1,553. Interest from 30 April. Penalty under FA 2009 Sch 24: careful, unprompted, within 12 months = 0 per cent. Total exposure: £1,553 plus modest interest, and HMRC most likely processes it without an enquiry.</p>

<p><strong>Scenario B: you self-correct beyond the 12-month window (unprompted disclosure).</strong> Same facts, but you spot it 18 months after the original deadline. Voluntary disclosure letter, additional tax £1,553, interest accrued for around 18 months. Penalty under FA 2009 Sch 24: careful, unprompted, beyond 12 months = 0 to 15 per cent depending on the quality of your disclosure, and usually settled at 0 to 5 per cent. Total exposure: tax, interest, and a modest penalty.</p>

<p><strong>Scenario C: HMRC finds it first, via an OTM letter or enquiry.</strong> A One-to-Many letter campaign or a discovery assessment turns up the same 60-day NQI occupation. Now the prompted band bites: careless, prompted, within 12 months = 15 to 30 per cent; beyond 12 months = 20 to 30 per cent or higher depending on behaviour. The same £1,553 of additional tax now carries a £233 to £466 penalty on top of the interest. The cost of waiting is exactly that gap between the unprompted and prompted bands, multiplied by the tax at stake.</p>

<h2>Three worked examples</h2>

<h3>Example 1: relief mis-claim caught internally, within the window</h3>

<p>A Ltd holds a £1.5m flat and filed its 2026/27 return on 25 April 2026 claiming Property Rental Business Relief in full. An internal compliance review on 15 December 2026 finds that the director's adult son occupied the flat from 1 September 2026 to 30 October 2026 (60 days), with no one revisiting the relief. The team puts together an amendment package: the corrected days (60 NQI + 14 look-back + 18 look-forward = 92 days at full charge), the additional tax (£2,381), interest from 30 April to the amendment date (small), and a covering letter explaining the discovery and the unprompted-disclosure position. The amendment goes in online on 18 December 2026, with payment of the additional tax. HMRC processes it without an enquiry. Total cost: £2,381 plus minor interest. No penalty.</p>

<h3>Example 2: a valuation re-test forces a multi-period amendment</h3>

<p>B Ltd holds a £4.95m townhouse just under the £5m band boundary, and filed its 2026/27 return in band 3 (£32,200 charge) on 28 April 2026. In autumn 2026 the company commissions a fresh PRBC for an unrelated reason, and HMRC's review settles the value at 1 April 2022 at £5.05m, pushing the dwelling into band 4 (£75,450 charge). B Ltd files an amended 2026/27 return moving to band 4 and paying the extra £43,250 plus interest. The same analysis reaches back to 2025/26, where the amendment window has already closed, so B Ltd makes an unprompted voluntary disclosure for 2025/26 and 2024/25 (both inside the four-year window for past periods). Total exposure: three years of band uplift (£43,250 + £41,650 + ~£40,000) plus interest plus a penalty that depends on the behaviour analysis. The unprompted-disclosure position keeps the penalty range low, with settlement typically at 0 to 10 per cent of the additional tax.</p>

<h3>Example 3: a mid-year disposal triggers a RACA</h3>

<p>C Ltd holds a £2.6m flat and files its 2026/27 return on 30 April 2026 claiming Property Rental Business Relief in full. On 15 October 2026 it sells the flat to an unconnected individual. The disposal triggers a Return of Adjusted Chargeable Amount (RACA) within 30 days (by 14 November 2026). The RACA reports the actual days C Ltd held the dwelling (1 April to 14 October 2026 = 197 days), the apportioned charge for those days under Property Rental Business Relief (so nil tax due if relief still applies across the holding period), and refunds any tax prepaid on the assumption of a full-year holding. The buyer files their own acquisition-day-apportioned return within 30 days of completion.</p>
