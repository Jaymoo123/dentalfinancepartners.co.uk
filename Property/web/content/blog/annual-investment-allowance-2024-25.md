Vans and lorries are not cars for this purpose and can qualify for AIA if used in a qualifying business.

  answer: Finance Act 2026 section 29 introduced a new 40% first-year allowance on new and unused main-rate plant and machinery, for expenditure incurred from 1 January 2026. It excludes cars, second-hand or used assets, and assets for overseas leasing. AIA gives 100% relief to businesses of any structure, including individuals and partnerships, but is capped at GBP1 million a year. Plant serving those common parts can therefore qualify for capital allowances, including AIA where the wider activity qualifies. The dividing line between a dwelling-house and the common parts is fact-specific and is the central judgement in HMO and block-of-flats claims.

<p>This is a review of the Annual Investment Allowance (AIA) as it stood for the 2024/25 tax year, what it meant for property businesses, and what has changed since. If you are reconstructing a 2024/25 claim, checking a return before an amendment window closes, or simply trying to understand how the rules you read about for that year line up with the position today, this page is the bridge. It is deliberately time-anchored: AIA in 2024/25, what carried into 2025/26, and what changed from April 2026 and 1 January 2026.</p>

<h2>AIA in 2024/25 at a glance</h2>
<p>For 2024/25 the headline numbers were stable. The Annual Investment Allowance gave 100% first-year relief on qualifying plant and machinery up to a permanent cap of GBP1 million, set by CAA 2001 section 51A(5). That cap had been made permanent by Finance (No. 2) Act 2023 section 8 from 1 April 2023 for companies and 6 April 2023 for unincorporated businesses, removing the long-running uncertainty about a reversion to GBP200,000. Plant and machinery within that GBP1 million was written off in full against profits in the year of spend, rather than spread over several years through writing-down allowances.</p>

<h2>Who could (and could not) claim AIA in 2024/25</h2>
<p>The central rule that shaped every property AIA claim in 2024/25 is the dwelling-house bar. CAA 2001 section 35(2) provides that a person's expenditure is not qualifying expenditure if it is incurred in providing plant or machinery for use in a dwelling-house within a property business. A standard buy-to-let house or flat is a dwelling-house, so the fittings inside it (the boiler, the bathroom, the fitted kitchen) did not attract AIA. This is the single most misunderstood point in the cluster, and it applied equally whether you let personally or through a company.</p>

<p>Where AIA did reach property businesses in 2024/25 was:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Commercial property letting</strong>, such as shops, offices, warehouses and industrial units. Plant and integral features serving commercial premises are not in a dwelling-house, so they could qualify.</li>
<li><strong>Furnished holiday lettings</strong>, which still qualified in 2024/25 as a deemed trade with its own capital allowances treatment. This was the last full tax year in which FHLs sat outside the dwelling-house bar.</li>
<li><strong>Mixed-use property</strong>, where the commercial element could qualify even though the residential element could not. The apportionment between the two is a matter of fact and evidence.</li>
<li><strong>Common parts of multi-let buildings</strong>, such as the shared hallways, stairwells and central plant of a block of flats or an HMO, which are generally not part of any single dwelling-house.</li>
</ul>
<p>For the deeper treatment of the landlord-specific eligibility question, see our guide on whether <a href="/blog/section-24-and-tax-relief/annual-investment-allowance-landlords-uk">landlords can claim the Annual Investment Allowance</a>. The HMO and block-of-flats line, which turns on the meaning of a dwelling-house, is covered in detail in our piece on <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/hmo-common-parts-capital-allowances-s35-claim-mechanics-multi-tenant-property">HMO common-parts capital allowances and the section 35 claim mechanics</a>.</p>

<h2>What counted as plant and machinery in 2024/25</h2>
<p>Plant and machinery is a broad category, but it has firm edges. It does not include land or the building structure. You could not claim AIA on the purchase price of a property or on the cost of constructing the shell. What could qualify, where the wider activity qualified, was the plant and machinery within the building.</p>

<p>Qualifying items typically included integral features under CAA 2001 section 33A (the five statutory categories: electrical and lighting systems, cold water systems, space and water heating and ventilation, lifts and escalators, and external solar shading), loose plant and machinery, and equipment used in the business. Integral features sit in the special rate pool, which wrote down at 6% to the extent it exceeded any AIA claimed against it, so the order in which AIA is allocated across pools matters.</p>

<p>This is mostly evergreen territory and is covered comprehensively in the main <a href="/blog/section-24-and-tax-relief/annual-investment-allowance-uk">Annual Investment Allowance guide</a> and the <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/capital-allowances-property-investors-complete-pillar-2026-27-caa-2001-decision-framework">capital allowances for property investors pillar guide</a>. The key 2024/25-specific point is simply that the categories and exclusions above were unchanged that year; what changed afterwards were the rates and the new allowance, dealt with below.</p>

<h2>The GBP1 million cap and the single-allowance rule</h2>
<p>The GBP1 million cap applies per business, not per property. A landlord with several commercial units aggregates qualifying expenditure across the whole property business and tests it against one GBP1 million limit. The cap is proportionately adjusted only where the chargeable period is longer or shorter than 12 months, under section 51A(6).</p>

<p>Where ownership is more layered, the single-allowance rules bite. Under CAA 2001 sections 51B to 51N, companies that are related (through common control, or through shared premises or similar activities) do not each get a full GBP1 million. They share a single allowance and must allocate it between them. The same logic constrains how individuals and partnerships with controlling links between their businesses allocate the cap. This is easy to overlook when a property empire is spread across several SPVs, and getting the allocation wrong can mean a clawback.</p>

<p>The allocation strategy across a group, and the precise association tests, are a specialist topic in their own right. Our detailed guide on the <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/aia-1m-cap-property-investors-allocation-strategy-and-association-rules-cta-2010">GBP1 million AIA cap, allocation strategy and association rules</a> works through the planning. The point for 2024/25 is that the cap was GBP1 million per business, shared where the association rules applied.</p>

<h2>How AIA interacted with Section 24 in 2024/25</h2>
<p>It helps to be clear about what AIA is and is not. AIA is a capital allowance, not a revenue expense. It is given as a deduction in the capital allowances computation and reduces the taxable profit of a qualifying business directly. That is fundamentally different from the Section 24 finance-cost restriction, which from 6 April 2020 has limited relief on residential mortgage interest to a basic-rate (20%) tax reducer rather than a deduction from profit.</p>

<p>Because AIA only ever applied to commercial, FHL or common-parts activity, never to the residential lettings that Section 24 targets, the two rules generally operated on different parts of a mixed portfolio. AIA reduces the profit of the qualifying (often commercial) part; Section 24 restricts interest relief on the residential part. For a landlord with both, separating the two activities cleanly in the records is what keeps each rule applied correctly. Our <a href="/blog/section-24-and-tax-relief/section-24-tax-relief-complete-guide">complete guide to Section 24</a> sets out how the finance-cost restriction now works in full.</p>

<h2>What carried into 2025/26</h2>
<p>Two things carried forward unchanged from 2024/25 into 2025/26: the GBP1 million AIA cap and the special rate pool at 6%. The headline change for property businesses was the abolition of the furnished holiday lettings regime.</p>

<p>Finance Act 2025 Schedule 5 removed the special FHL treatment that had classed qualifying holiday accommodation as a deemed trade. The income tax changes have effect for the 2025-26 tax year onwards, and the corporation tax changes for accounting periods beginning on or after 1 April 2025. From those dates, former FHL properties fall into the ordinary property business and are caught by the section 35 dwelling-house bar, so no new AIA is available on plant inside them.</p>

<p>Plant already pooled before abolition is not lost. Existing pools continue to attract writing-down allowances within the now-ordinary property business; the regime change stops new qualifying expenditure rather than stripping out historic allowances. The transitional mechanics, including how grandfathered pools are carried forward, are covered in our guide on <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/fhl-capital-allowances-post-april-2025-grandfathered-claims-mechanics">FHL capital allowances after April 2025 and grandfathered claims</a>. If you ran an FHL in 2024/25, that year was the last clean window to make a fresh AIA claim on holiday-let plant.</p>

<h2>What changed from April 2026: the writing-down allowance cut</h2>
<p>The most significant rate change since 2024/25 is the cut to the main pool writing-down allowance. Finance Act 2026 section 28 reduced the main pool rate from 18% to 14% per year. CAA 2001 section 56(1) now reads 14%, annotated on legislation.gov.uk as substituted by Finance Act 2026 section 28(1). Finance Act 2026 received Royal Assent on 18 March 2026, so this is enacted law, not a proposal.</p>

<p>The change takes effect from 1 April 2026 for corporation tax and 6 April 2026 for income tax. For a chargeable period that straddles the start date, a hybrid time-apportioned rate applies, blending 18% and 14% in proportion to the days before and after the change. The special rate pool is unchanged at 6%.</p>

<p>This matters wherever qualifying expenditure exceeds the GBP1 million AIA cap, because the excess writes down at these pool rates rather than getting 100% relief, and the slower 14% rate stretches relief over a longer period. It also matters for cars, which are excluded from AIA and sit in the pools by emissions. The full detail of the rate change and the straddling calculation is in our guide on <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/writing-down-allowance-rates">writing-down allowance rates</a>.</p>

<h2>The new 40% first-year allowance (from 1 January 2026)</h2>
<p>The change that a 2024/25 reader will not have met, and the most material new relief for unincorporated landlords in years, is the 40% first-year allowance. Finance Act 2026 section 29 introduced it for expenditure incurred from 1 January 2026 on new and unused main-rate plant and machinery.</p>

<p>What makes the 40% FYA distinct is who can use it. Full expensing under CAA 2001 section 45S is restricted to companies within the charge to corporation tax. The 40% FYA was deliberately designed to reach the businesses that full expensing leaves out: it is available to all businesses, including unincorporated landlords (sole traders and partnerships) and assets bought for leasing. The gov.uk measure describes its purpose as encouraging investment where the existing first-year allowances are not available, such as for assets bought for leasing and by unincorporated businesses.</p>

<p>The exclusions are important. The 40% FYA does not apply to cars, to second-hand or used assets, or to assets for overseas leasing. And, critically for property, the section 35 dwelling-house bar still applies. The 40% FYA does not unlock relief on plant inside a residential dwelling. It is most relevant to commercial property plant and to the common parts of multi-let buildings, and to the leasing of qualifying assets. For a company that has already used its full expensing, or for an unincorporated landlord that has used its GBP1 million AIA, the 40% FYA can give a meaningful first-year deduction on additional qualifying spend. The contrast with company-only full expensing is set out in our guide on <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/full-expensing-50-percent-fya-commercial-property-spvs-mechanics-fa-2023">full expensing and first-year allowances for commercial property</a>.</p>

<h2>Worked examples</h2>
<p>The following examples use anonymised, illustrative figures to show how the rules apply. They are not advice on your own position.</p>

<p><strong>Example 1: commercial unit, AIA in 2024/25.</strong> A landlord lets a commercial retail unit. In 2024/25 they spend GBP25,000 on a new heating system and GBP15,000 on electrical upgrades, both qualifying plant and machinery in commercial premises. They can claim the full GBP40,000 as an AIA deduction against the rental business profit. If the profit before the claim is GBP60,000, it reduces to GBP20,000. At a 40% marginal rate, the GBP40,000 deduction is worth up to GBP16,000 in tax relief that year. The relief is illustrative only and depends on the individual's marginal rate and other income.</p>

<p><strong>Example 2: unincorporated landlord, AIA cap used, spend after 1 January 2026.</strong> A sole-trader landlord with a commercial portfolio has already used the full GBP1 million AIA in the year on a large refurbishment. In February 2026 they incur a further GBP100,000 on new and unused qualifying main-rate plant for a commercial unit. AIA is exhausted, and full expensing is not available to an individual. The new 40% first-year allowance under FA 2026 section 29 applies because the spend is from 1 January 2026, the assets are new and unused, and they are not cars, second-hand or for overseas leasing. The landlord can claim a 40% first-year deduction of GBP40,000, with the remaining GBP60,000 entering the main pool to write down at 14% from April 2026.</p>

<p><strong>Example 3: a straddling period and the hybrid WDA rate.</strong> A company has a 12-month accounting period running from 1 January 2026 to 31 December 2026. The main pool writing-down allowance change takes effect for corporation tax from 1 April 2026. Roughly three months of the period fall before the change at 18% and nine months after it at 14%, so a hybrid rate around 15% applies to the pool for that period, calculated by time-apportioning the days before and after 1 April 2026. The exact figure depends on the day count and is set out in the writing-down allowance guide linked above.</p>

<h2>How to claim and keep records</h2>
<p>The claim mechanics did not change. AIA is claimed within the capital allowances computation that forms part of the tax return: the self-assessment return (SA103 for sole traders, SA104 for partnerships) for unincorporated landlords, and the company tax return (CT600) for companies. There is no separate AIA application form; you calculate the qualifying expenditure and claim it in the computation.</p>

<p>Good records are what make a claim defensible: dated invoices, an asset schedule identifying each item and its pool, and, for property, evidence of the apportionment between qualifying plant and the non-qualifying building structure or dwelling-house element. The evergreen step-by-step for any year is in the main <a href="/blog/section-24-and-tax-relief/annual-investment-allowance-uk">Annual Investment Allowance guide</a>.</p>

<h2>Common mistakes (2024/25 and beyond)</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Claiming AIA on residential dwelling plant.</strong> The section 35(2) dwelling-house bar means the boiler, bathroom and fitted kitchen of a standard buy-to-let do not qualify. This is the most common error in the cluster.</li>
<li><strong>Including building or purchase costs.</strong> AIA covers plant and machinery only, never the land, the building shell or the purchase price.</li>
<li><strong>Assuming FHLs still qualify.</strong> They did in 2024/25, but the regime was abolished by Finance Act 2025 Schedule 5 from April 2025. New FHL plant no longer attracts AIA.</li>
<li><strong>Missing the new 40% first-year allowance.</strong> For qualifying spend from 1 January 2026, unincorporated landlords and leasing businesses now have a route that did not exist in 2024/25. It is easy to default to AIA or the pools and overlook it.</li>
<li><strong>Treating a car as AIA-qualifying.</strong> Cars are excluded from both AIA and the 40% FYA and must go through the pools by emissions.</li>
<li><strong>Forgetting the single-allowance rule.</strong> Related companies and connected businesses share one GBP1 million cap under sections 51B to 51N; they do not each get a full GBP1 million.</li>
</ul>
