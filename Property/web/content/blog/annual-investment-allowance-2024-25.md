<p>The Annual Investment Allowance (AIA) is one of the most generous capital allowances available to UK businesses. For the 2024/25 tax year, it allows you to claim 100% tax relief on qualifying plant and machinery costs up to £1 million. But does it apply to landlords? The answer is yes, but only in specific circumstances.</p>

<p>This guide explains how the AIA works for UK property investors, who qualifies, what types of expenditure qualify, and how to claim it correctly. If you are a landlord with a portfolio of buy-to-let properties, understanding the AIA can reduce your tax bill significantly.</p>

<h2>What Is the Annual Investment Allowance?</h2>
<p>The Annual Investment Allowance is a capital allowance that gives you 100% tax relief on qualifying plant and machinery costs in the year you incur them. For the 2024/25 tax year, the AIA threshold is permanently set at £1 million. This means you can deduct the full cost of qualifying assets from your taxable profits immediately, rather than spreading the relief over several years through writing down allowances.</p>

<p>The AIA applies to most businesses, including sole traders, partnerships, and limited companies. However, the rules for landlords are more restrictive. You cannot claim the AIA on residential property unless you are running a qualifying business such as a furnished holiday letting (FHL) or a commercial property letting business.</p>

<h2>Who Can Claim the AIA as a Landlord?</h2>
<p>The key distinction is between residential and commercial property. The AIA is not available for standard buy-to-let residential properties. This is because HMRC treats residential property letting as an investment activity, not a trade. As a result, plant and machinery costs in residential properties are not eligible for the AIA.</p>

<p>However, the AIA is available for:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Furnished holiday lettings (FHLs)</strong>, but note the FHL regime was abolished from April 2025. For the 2024/25 tax year, FHLs still qualify for the AIA if they meet the qualifying conditions.</li>
<li><strong>Commercial property letting</strong>, such as shops, offices, warehouses, and industrial units.</li>
<li><strong>Mixed-use properties</strong>, where you let both residential and commercial space, the AIA can apply to the commercial element.</li>
<li><strong>Property development and trading businesses</strong>, if you are a property developer or trader, the AIA applies to plant and machinery used in your trade.</li>
</ul>
<p>If you let residential property through a limited company, the same restriction applies. The company cannot claim the AIA on residential property unless it is running a qualifying FHL or commercial letting business.</p>

<h2>What Qualifies as Plant and Machinery for the AIA?</h2>
<p>Plant and machinery is a broad category under capital allowances. For the AIA, qualifying assets include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Fixtures and fittings in commercial properties, such as heating systems, air conditioning, lifts, and electrical systems.</li>
<li>Kitchen and bathroom installations in FHLs or commercial properties.</li>
<li>Furniture, furnishings, and equipment in FHLs (e.g., beds, sofas, fridges, washing machines).</li>
<li>Solar panels and other energy-saving equipment.</li>
<li>Office equipment used in your property business, such as computers, desks, and printers.</li>
<li>Vehicles used for business purposes (cars are excluded from the AIA, but vans and lorries qualify).</li>
</ul>
<p>It is important to note that the AIA does not apply to buildings themselves. You cannot claim the AIA on the cost of buying or constructing a property. Only the integral fixtures and fittings within the building qualify.</p>

<h2>How Much Can You Claim?</h2>
<p>For the 2024/25 tax year, the AIA threshold is £1 million. This means you can claim 100% relief on qualifying expenditure up to £1 million. If your qualifying expenditure exceeds £1 million, the excess is claimed through writing down allowances at a lower rate (typically 6% or 14% per year from April 2026 (the main pool rate was 18% before then), depending on the asset pool).</p>
<p>The £1 million threshold applies per business, not per property. If you have multiple properties, you aggregate all qualifying expenditure across your property business. If you operate multiple separate businesses, each business has its own £1 million AIA limit.</p>

<h2>How to Claim the AIA</h2>
<p>Claiming the AIA is straightforward. You include the qualifying expenditure in your capital allowances computation as part of your tax return. For sole traders and partnerships, this is done on the self-assessment tax return (SA103 or SA104). For limited companies, it is included in the company tax return (CT600).</p>
<p>You do not need to submit a separate form. Simply calculate the total qualifying expenditure and deduct it from your taxable profits. If you are unsure whether an asset qualifies, it is worth consulting a specialist property accountant. <a href="/services">Our property accountant services</a> can help you identify eligible expenditure and maximise your claim.</p>

<h2>Interaction with Section 24</h2>
<p>Section 24 restricts mortgage interest relief for residential landlords to a basic rate tax credit. However, the AIA is a capital allowance, not a revenue expense. It reduces your taxable profits directly, which in turn reduces the amount of mortgage interest you can claim relief on. This interaction can be complex, particularly for landlords with both residential and commercial properties.</p>
<p>If you are a landlord with a mixed portfolio, it is essential to separate your residential and commercial letting activities for tax purposes. The AIA applies only to the commercial or FHL part of your business. <a href="/blog/section-24-and-tax-relief/section-24-tax-relief-complete-guide">Our complete guide to Section 24</a> explains how mortgage interest relief works alongside capital allowances.</p>

<h2>What About the 2025/26 Tax Year?</h2>
<p>The AIA threshold of £1 million is permanent as of the 2023/24 tax year. It remains at £1 million for 2025/26 and future years, unless the government changes it. However, the abolition of the FHL regime from April 2025 means that many landlords who previously claimed the AIA on FHL properties will no longer qualify. From April 2025, FHL properties are treated as standard residential lettings, and the AIA is no longer available.</p>
<p>If you currently operate FHLs, you should consider claiming the AIA on any qualifying expenditure before 6 April 2025. After that date, you will need to rely on other capital allowances or revenue deductions.</p>

<h2>Common Mistakes Landlords Make</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Claiming the AIA on residential property</strong>, This is the most common error. The AIA does not apply to standard buy-to-let residential properties.</li>
<li><strong>Including building costs</strong>, The AIA only covers plant and machinery, not the cost of the building itself.</li>
<li><strong>Forgetting to claim on office equipment</strong>, If you use a home office or separate office for your property business, you can claim the AIA on computers, desks, and other equipment.</li>
<li><strong>Not keeping detailed records</strong>, HMRC may ask for evidence of qualifying expenditure. Keep invoices, receipts, and a schedule of assets.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Example: Claiming the AIA on a Commercial Property</h2>
<p>Sarah owns a commercial property in Manchester that she lets to a retail tenant. In 2024/25, she spends £25,000 on a new heating system and £15,000 on electrical upgrades. Both are qualifying plant and machinery. She can claim the full £40,000 as an AIA deduction against her rental profits. If her rental profit before the claim is £60,000, her taxable profit reduces to £20,000, saving her up to £8,000 in tax (at 40% higher rate).</p>
