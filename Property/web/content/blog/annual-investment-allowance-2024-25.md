From those dates, former FHL properties are treated as ordinary property businesses, which are caught by the section 35 dwelling-house bar, so no new AIA is available on plant inside them. If you had qualifying FHL expenditure in 2024/25, you should have claimed it in that year's return. Plant already pooled before abolition continues to attract writing-down allowances within the ordinary property business."
- question: "Can I claim the AIA on a car used for my property business?"
  answer: "No. Cars are specifically excluded from the Annual Investment Allowance under CAA 2001 section 38B. A car used in a qualifying business is dealt with through the capital allowance pools instead, at the special rate pool rate of 6% per year for higher-emission cars or the main pool rate (now 14% per year from April 2026, having been 18% before then) for lower-emission cars, depending on CO2 emissions. The only first-year allowance a car can attract is the 0 g/km new-car allowance under section 45D, which is itself set to end on 31 March 2027 for corporation tax and 5 April 2027 for income tax. Vans and lorries are not cars for this purpose and can qualify for AIA if used in a qualifying business."
- question: "What is the new 40% first-year allowance, and can individual landlords use it?"
  answer: "Finance Act 2026 section 29 introduced a 40% first-year allowance on new and unused main-rate plant and machinery for expenditure incurred from 1 January 2026, with the qualifying conditions set out in the inserted CAA 2001 section 45U. It is available to qualifying persons generally; section 45U contains no incorporation test. In practice it is the route for unincorporated businesses (sole traders, partnerships and individual landlords) and for assets bought for leasing, because a company spending on the same new main-rate plant will normally claim 100% full expensing under section 45S instead, which is company-only and unavailable for leasing. It excludes cars, second-hand or used assets, and assets for overseas leasing. The section 35 dwelling-house bar still applies, so it does not unlock relief on plant inside residential dwellings; it is most relevant to commercial property plant and the common parts of multi-let buildings."
- question: "Did the writing-down allowance change after 2024/25?"
  answer: "Yes. Finance Act 2026 section 28 reduced the main pool writing-down allowance from 18% to 14% per year. The change takes effect from 1 April 2026 for corporation tax and 6 April 2026 for income tax, with a hybrid time-apportioned rate for chargeable periods that straddle the start date. The special rate pool remains at 6%. This matters for any qualifying expenditure above the £1 million AIA cap, which writes down at these pool rates rather than getting 100% relief."
- question: "Is the £1 million AIA cap still £1 million, or has it reverted to £200,000?"
  answer: "It is still £1 million. Finance (No. 2) Act 2023 section 8 made the £1,000,000 cap in CAA 2001 section 51A(5) permanent from 1 April 2023, removing the temporary uplift and the previously scheduled reversion to £200,000. Older guidance that warns of a drop back to £200,000 is out of date. For 2024/25, 2025/26 and onwards, the cap is £1 million, proportionately adjusted only where the chargeable period is longer or shorter than 12 months."
- question: "How does AIA differ from full expensing for a property company?"
  answer: "Full expensing under CAA 2001 section 45S gives companies a 100% first-year deduction on qualifying new main-rate plant and machinery with no annual cap, but it is restricted to companies within the charge to corporation tax. AIA gives 100% relief to businesses of any structure, including individuals and partnerships, but is capped at £1 million a year. A company can use full expensing for unlimited qualifying spend and keep its AIA for assets full expensing does not reach, such as second-hand plant. An unincorporated landlord cannot use full expensing at all and relies on AIA, plus, from 1 January 2026, the new 40% first-year allowance."
- question: "Do associated companies get a full £1 million AIA each?"
  answer: "No. Where companies are related under the AIA single-allowance rules in CAA 2001 sections 51B to 51N, they share a single £1 million allowance between them rather than each receiving a full £1 million. The tests look at common control and at whether the companies are related through shared premises or similar activities. The group must then allocate the single allowance between its members. The same idea constrains how individuals and partnerships with controlling links allocate AIA. The allocation strategy is a specialist area worth planning before incurring large qualifying spend."
- question: "Can I claim AIA on plant in the communal areas of an HMO or block of flats?"
  answer: "Often yes. The section 35 dwelling-house bar applies to plant for use in a dwelling-house, but the communal areas of a multi-let building, such as shared hallways, stairwells, lifts and a central heating boiler serving the whole block, are generally not part of any single dwelling-house. Plant serving those common parts can therefore qualify for capital allowances, including AIA where the wider activity qualifies. The dividing line between a dwelling-house and the common parts is fact-specific and is the central judgement in HMO and block-of-flats claims."
- question: "Does AIA cover the cost of buying or building the property itself?"
  answer: "No. AIA, like all plant and machinery allowances, covers plant and machinery, not land or the building structure. You cannot claim AIA on the purchase price of a property or on the cost of constructing the shell. Only qualifying plant and machinery within the building, such as integral features (electrical and cold water systems, heating, lifts and similar) under CAA 2001 section 33A and loose plant, can qualify. Integral features are dealt with in the special rate pool and write down at 6% to the extent they exceed any AIA claimed against them."

<p>This page reviews the Annual Investment Allowance (AIA) as it stood for the 2024/25 tax year, what it meant for property businesses, and how every point on it has moved since. Most readers who land here are doing one of three things: reconstructing a 2024/25 capital-allowances claim, checking a return that has already been filed before the amendment window shuts, or trying to reconcile guidance written "for 2024/25" against the position today. AIA gives 100% first-year relief on qualifying plant and machinery up to an annual cap, and for 2024/25 that cap was £1 million. The rest is detail, and the detail is where the year-anchored points matter.</p>

<p>This is deliberately a backward-looking page. For the evergreen mechanics of who can claim and how AIA works in any year, see our main guide on the <a href="/blog/section-24-and-tax-relief/annual-investment-allowance-uk">Annual Investment Allowance for UK property investors</a>; it does not need repeating here. For the current-year position and the full 1 January 2026 to 6 April 2026 transition timeline, see the <a href="/blog/landlord-tax-essentials/annual-investment-allowance-2025">2025/26 AIA guide</a>. This page sits behind both of those: it fixes the 2024/25 position and tracks what changed afterwards, so you can tell at a glance whether what was true then is still true now.</p>

<h2>Annual Investment Allowance 2024/25 at a glance</h2>
<p>For 2024/25 the headline numbers were stable. AIA gave 100% first-year relief on qualifying plant and machinery up to a permanent cap of £1 million, set by <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2001/2/section/51A">CAA 2001 section 51A(5)</a> ("The maximum allowance is £1,000,000"). That cap had been made permanent by Finance (No. 2) Act 2023 section 8 from 1 April 2023 for companies and 6 April 2023 for unincorporated businesses, which ended years of uncertainty about a reversion to £200,000. Expenditure within the £1 million was written off in full against profits in the year it was incurred, rather than spread over several years through writing-down allowances.</p>

<p>The single most useful thing this page can give a reader reviewing 2024/25 is a clean before-and-after. The table sets the 2024/25 position against where each point sits today, with the statute behind each line, so the sections that follow are easy to navigate.</p>

<h2>Capital allowances on investment property in the UK: who could claim AIA in 2024/25</h2>
<p>The central rule that shaped every property AIA claim in 2024/25 is the dwelling-house bar. <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2001/2/section/35">CAA 2001 section 35(2)</a> provides that a person's expenditure is not qualifying expenditure if it is incurred on providing plant or machinery for use in a dwelling-house within a property business. A standard buy-to-let house or flat is a dwelling-house, so the fittings inside it (the boiler, the bathroom, the fitted kitchen) did not attract AIA. This is the single most misunderstood point in property capital allowances, and it applied equally whether you let personally or through a company.</p>

<p>Where AIA did reach property businesses in 2024/25 was:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Commercial property letting</strong>, such as shops, offices, warehouses and industrial units. Plant and integral features serving commercial premises are not in a dwelling-house, so they could qualify.</li>
<li><strong>Furnished holiday lettings</strong>, which still qualified in 2024/25 as a deemed trade with its own capital allowances treatment. This was the last full tax year in which FHLs sat outside the dwelling-house bar.</li>
<li><strong>Mixed-use property</strong>, where the commercial element could qualify even though the residential element could not. The apportionment between the two is a matter of fact and evidence.</li>
<li><strong>Common parts of multi-let buildings</strong>, such as the shared hallways, stairwells and central plant of a block of flats or an HMO, which are generally not part of any single dwelling-house.</li>
</ul>
<p>For the deeper treatment of the landlord-specific eligibility question, see our guide on whether <a href="/blog/section-24-and-tax-relief/annual-investment-allowance-landlords-uk">landlords can claim the Annual Investment Allowance</a>. The HMO and block-of-flats line, which turns on the meaning of a dwelling-house, is covered in our piece on <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/hmo-common-parts-capital-allowances-s35-claim-mechanics-multi-tenant-property">HMO common-parts capital allowances and the section 35 claim mechanics</a>.</p>

<h2>What counted as plant and machinery in 2024/25</h2>
<p>Plant and machinery is a broad category with firm edges. It does not include land or the building structure. You could not claim AIA on the purchase price of a property or on the cost of constructing the shell. What could qualify, where the wider activity qualified, was the plant and machinery within the building.</p>

<p>Qualifying items typically included integral features under <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2001/2/section/33A">CAA 2001 section 33A</a> (the five statutory categories: electrical and lighting systems, cold water systems, space and water heating and ventilation, lifts and escalators, and external solar shading), loose plant and machinery, and equipment used in the business. Integral features sit in the special rate pool, which wrote down at 6% to the extent it exceeded any AIA claimed against it, so the order in which AIA is allocated across pools matters.</p>

<p>This is mostly evergreen territory, set out in full in the main <a href="/blog/section-24-and-tax-relief/annual-investment-allowance-uk">Annual Investment Allowance guide</a> and the <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/capital-allowances-property-investors-complete-pillar-2026-27-caa-2001-decision-framework">capital allowances for property investors pillar guide</a>. The 2024/25-specific point is narrow: the categories and exclusions above were unchanged that year. What moved afterwards were the rates and the arrival of a new allowance, both dealt with below.</p>

<h2>The £1 million cap and the single-allowance rule</h2>
<p>The £1 million cap applies per business, not per property. A landlord with several commercial units aggregates qualifying expenditure across the whole property business and tests it against one £1 million limit. The cap is proportionately adjusted only where the chargeable period is longer or shorter than 12 months, under section 51A(6).</p>

<p>Where ownership is more layered, the single-allowance rules bite. Under CAA 2001 sections 51B to 51N, companies that are related (through common control, or through shared premises or similar activities) do not each get a full £1 million. They share a single allowance and must allocate it between them. The same logic constrains how individuals and partnerships with controlling links between their businesses allocate the cap. This is easy to overlook when a portfolio is spread across several SPVs, and getting the allocation wrong can mean a clawback.</p>

<p>The allocation strategy across a group, and the precise association tests, are a specialist topic in their own right; our detailed guide on the <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/aia-1m-cap-property-investors-allocation-strategy-and-association-rules-cta-2010">£1 million AIA cap, allocation strategy and association rules</a> works through the planning. For 2024/25 the rule to hold in mind is simple: the cap was £1 million per business, shared where the association rules applied.</p>

<h2>How AIA interacted with Section 24 in 2024/25</h2>
<p>It helps to be precise about what AIA is and is not. AIA is a capital allowance, not a revenue expense. It is given as a deduction in the capital allowances computation and reduces the taxable profit of a qualifying business directly. That is a different mechanism from the Section 24 finance-cost restriction, which since 6 April 2020 has limited relief on residential mortgage interest to a basic-rate (20%) tax reducer rather than a deduction from profit.</p>

<p>Because AIA only ever applied to commercial, FHL or common-parts activity, never to the residential lettings that Section 24 targets, the two rules generally operated on different parts of a mixed portfolio. AIA reduced the profit of the qualifying (often commercial) part; Section 24 restricted interest relief on the residential part. For a landlord running both, separating the two activities cleanly in the records is what keeps each rule applied correctly. Our <a href="/blog/section-24-and-tax-relief/section-24-tax-relief-complete-guide">complete guide to Section 24</a> sets out how the finance-cost restriction works now.</p>

<h2>What carried into 2025/26</h2>
<p>Two things carried forward unchanged from 2024/25 into 2025/26: the £1 million AIA cap and the special rate pool at 6%. The headline change for property businesses was the abolition of the furnished holiday lettings regime.</p>

<p>Finance Act 2025 Schedule 5 Part 3 removed the special FHL treatment that had classed qualifying holiday accommodation as a deemed trade, omitting the FHL limbs from the qualifying-activity gateway in <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2001/2/section/15">CAA 2001 section 15</a>. The income tax changes have effect for the 2025-26 tax year onwards, and the corporation tax changes for accounting periods beginning on or after 1 April 2025. From those dates, former FHL properties fall into the ordinary property business and are caught by the section 35 dwelling-house bar, so no new AIA is available on plant inside them.</p>

<p>Plant already pooled before abolition is not lost. Existing pools continue to attract writing-down allowances within the now-ordinary property business; the regime change stops new qualifying expenditure rather than stripping out historic allowances. The transitional mechanics, including how grandfathered pools are carried forward, are covered in our guide on <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/fhl-capital-allowances-post-april-2025-grandfathered-claims-mechanics">FHL capital allowances after April 2025 and grandfathered claims</a>. The practical takeaway for this page is blunt: if you ran an FHL in 2024/25, that year was the last clean window to make a fresh AIA claim on holiday-let plant, which is exactly why the amendment-in-time section below matters. For the complete 2025/26 picture, see the <a href="/blog/landlord-tax-essentials/annual-investment-allowance-2025">2025/26 AIA guide</a>.</p>

<h2>What changed from April 2026: the writing-down allowance cut</h2>
<p>The most significant rate change since 2024/25 is the cut to the main pool writing-down allowance. Finance Act 2026 section 28 reduced the main pool rate from 18% to 14% per year. <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2001/2/section/56">CAA 2001 section 56(1)</a> now reads 14%, annotated on legislation.gov.uk as substituted by Finance Act 2026 section 28(1). Finance Act 2026 received Royal Assent on 18 March 2026, so this is enacted law, not a proposal.</p>

<p>The change takes effect from 1 April 2026 for corporation tax and 6 April 2026 for income tax. For a chargeable period that straddles the start date, a hybrid time-apportioned rate applies under section 28(2) to (6), blending 18% and 14% in proportion to the days before and after the change. The special rate pool is unchanged at 6% under section 104D; guidance suggesting it falls to 4% is wrong.</p>

<p>This matters wherever qualifying expenditure exceeds the £1 million AIA cap, because the excess writes down at these pool rates rather than getting 100% relief, and the slower 14% rate stretches that relief over a longer period. It also matters for cars, which are excluded from AIA and sit in the pools by emissions. The full detail of the rate change and the straddling calculation is in our guide on <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/writing-down-allowance-rates">writing-down allowance rates</a>.</p>

<h2>The new 40% first-year allowance (from 1 January 2026)</h2>
<p>The change a 2024/25 reader will not have met is the 40% first-year allowance. Finance Act 2026 section 29 introduced a 40% FYA on new and unused main-rate plant and machinery for expenditure incurred from 1 January 2026. The 40% rate is set by section 29 (through the first-year allowance table in CAA 2001 section 52); the qualifying conditions are set out in the inserted <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2001/2/section/45U">CAA 2001 section 45U</a>, which requires the expenditure to be incurred on or after 1 January 2026 on unused, not second-hand main-rate plant and not to be caught by the section 45V disqualifying-arrangements rule or the general exclusions in section 46.</p>

<p>The point that trips people up is who can use it. Section 45U sets no incorporation test: the 40% FYA is available to qualifying persons generally. In practice, though, it is the route for unincorporated businesses (sole traders, partnerships and individual landlords) and for assets bought for leasing, because a company spending on the same new main-rate plant will normally claim 100% full expensing under <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2001/2/section/45S">CAA 2001 section 45S</a> instead. Full expensing is restricted to companies within the charge to corporation tax and is unavailable for leasing, so the 40% FYA fills the gap that full expensing leaves; it is not a relief from which companies are barred. The contrast with company-only full expensing is set out in our guide on <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/full-expensing-50-percent-fya-commercial-property-spvs-mechanics-fa-2023">full expensing and first-year allowances for commercial property</a>.</p>

<p>The exclusions are important. The 40% FYA does not apply to cars, to second-hand or used assets, or to assets for overseas leasing. And, critically for property, the section 35 dwelling-house bar still applies, so it does not unlock relief on plant inside a residential dwelling. It is most relevant to commercial property plant, the common parts of multi-let buildings, and the leasing of qualifying assets. For a company that has already used its full expensing, or for an unincorporated landlord that has used its £1 million AIA, the 40% FYA can give a meaningful first-year deduction on additional qualifying spend.</p>

<aside><p>Working out whether a particular spend belongs in AIA, full expensing or the new 40% first-year allowance is genuinely fact-specific, and the wrong route can lock relief into a slow pool. If you are reconstructing or planning a claim, it is worth a short conversation before the position is fixed.</p></aside>

<h2>Amending a 2024/25 return: claiming AIA in time</h2>
<p>Because 2024/25 was the last clean year for FHL plant, and because property capital-allowances claims are easy to miss on a self-filed return, the timing question is real for this page's readers. Capital allowances, including AIA, are claimed in the return computation; there is no standalone AIA form. If a 2024/25 return was filed without a valid claim that should have been made, the route back depends on which window is still open.</p>

<p>For a self-assessment return, a taxpayer can amend it within 12 months of the filing deadline under <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1970/9/section/9ZA">TMA 1970 section 9ZA</a>. For a 2024/25 return due on 31 January 2026, that means the amendment window runs to 31 January 2027. A company amends its CT600 within 12 months of the statutory filing date under FA 1998 Schedule 18 paragraph 15. Once the amendment window has closed, the out-of-time route is an overpayment relief claim under TMA 1970 Schedule 1AB, made within the ordinary four-year time limit in <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1970/9/section/34">TMA 1970 section 34</a>, which for 2024/25 means by 5 April 2029.</p>

<p>The distinction between the 12-month amendment window and the four-year out-of-time route matters because they are not interchangeable: an in-time amendment simply corrects the return, while overpayment relief is a separate claim with its own conditions and is not available in every case. The table sets out the windows for a 2024/25 capital-allowances claim.</p>

<h2>Worked examples</h2>
<p>The following examples use anonymised, illustrative figures to show how the rules apply. They are not advice on your own position.</p>

<p><strong>Example 1: commercial unit, AIA in 2024/25.</strong> A landlord lets a commercial retail unit. In 2024/25 they spend £25,000 on a new heating system and £15,000 on electrical upgrades, both qualifying plant and machinery in commercial premises. They can claim the full £40,000 as an AIA deduction against the rental business profit. If the profit before the claim is £60,000, it reduces to £20,000. The cash value of a deduction tracks the landlord's marginal rate: at a 40% marginal rate the £40,000 deduction is worth up to £16,000, and from 2027/28 the separate property income rates enacted in Finance Act 2026 (22% basic, 42% higher, 47% additional, for England, Wales and Northern Ireland) mean an equivalent deduction is worth more at the higher property rates. The relief is illustrative only and depends on the individual's rate and other income.</p>

<p><strong>Example 2: unincorporated landlord, AIA cap used, spend after 1 January 2026.</strong> A sole-trader landlord with a commercial portfolio has already used the full £1 million AIA in the year on a large refurbishment. In February 2026 they incur a further £100,000 on new and unused qualifying main-rate plant for a commercial unit. AIA is exhausted, and full expensing is not available to an individual. The 40% first-year allowance under FA 2026 section 29 applies because the spend is from 1 January 2026, the assets are new and unused, and they are not cars, second-hand or for overseas leasing. The landlord claims a 40% first-year deduction of £40,000, with the remaining £60,000 entering the main pool to write down at 14% from April 2026.</p>

<p><strong>Example 3: a straddling period and the hybrid WDA rate.</strong> A company has a 12-month accounting period running from 1 January 2026 to 31 December 2026. The main pool writing-down allowance change takes effect for corporation tax from 1 April 2026. Roughly three months of the period fall before the change at 18% and nine months after it at 14%, so a hybrid rate of around 15% applies to the pool for that period, calculated by time-apportioning the days before and after 1 April 2026. The exact figure depends on the day count and is set out in the writing-down allowance guide linked above.</p>

<p><strong>Example 4: a missed 2024/25 claim, amended in time.</strong> A landlord fitted out a commercial unit in 2024/25 at a qualifying cost of £30,000 but filed the 2024/25 return without claiming AIA. The return was due on 31 January 2026, so the amendment window under TMA 1970 section 9ZA runs to 31 January 2027. The landlord amends the return inside that window, adds the £30,000 AIA deduction to the capital-allowances computation, and the corrected return recovers the relief without needing an overpayment relief claim. Had the amendment window already closed, the claim would instead have run through TMA 1970 Schedule 1AB by 5 April 2029.</p>

<h2>How to claim and keep records</h2>
<p>The claim mechanics did not change. AIA is claimed within the capital allowances computation that forms part of the tax return: the self-assessment return (SA103 for sole traders, SA104 for partnerships) for unincorporated landlords, and the company tax return (CT600) for companies. There is no separate AIA application form; you calculate the qualifying expenditure and claim it in the computation.</p>

<p>Good records are what make a claim defensible: dated invoices, an asset schedule identifying each item and its pool, and, for property, evidence of the apportionment between qualifying plant and the non-qualifying building structure or dwelling-house element. The evergreen step-by-step for any year is in the main <a href="/blog/section-24-and-tax-relief/annual-investment-allowance-uk">Annual Investment Allowance guide</a>.</p>

<h2>Common mistakes (2024/25 and beyond)</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Claiming AIA on residential dwelling plant.</strong> The section 35(2) dwelling-house bar means the boiler, bathroom and fitted kitchen of a standard buy-to-let do not qualify. This is the most common error in property capital allowances.</li>
<li><strong>Including building or purchase costs.</strong> AIA covers plant and machinery only, never the land, the building shell or the purchase price.</li>
<li><strong>Assuming FHLs still qualify.</strong> They did in 2024/25, but the regime was abolished by Finance Act 2025 Schedule 5 from April 2025. New FHL plant no longer attracts AIA.</li>
<li><strong>Overlooking the 40% first-year allowance.</strong> For qualifying spend from 1 January 2026, unincorporated businesses and leasing now have a route that did not exist in 2024/25. It is easy to default to AIA or the pools and miss it; companies will usually reach for full expensing first, but the 40% FYA is not closed to them.</li>
<li><strong>Treating a car as AIA-qualifying.</strong> Cars are excluded from both AIA and the 40% FYA and go through the pools by emissions.</li>
<li><strong>Forgetting the single-allowance rule.</strong> Related companies and connected businesses share one £1 million cap under sections 51B to 51N; they do not each get a full £1 million.</li>
<li><strong>Letting the amendment window close.</strong> A 2024/25 AIA claim missed on a filed return can usually still be recovered by amending within 12 months of the filing deadline; leaving it past that pushes you into the slower overpayment relief route.</li>
</ul>
