faqs:
  - question: "How broad is the s.620 definition of 'settlement'?"
    answer: "Very broad, by deliberate statutory design. s.620(1) verbatim: 'settlement' includes any disposition, trust, covenant, agreement, arrangement or transfer of assets (with one carve-out for charitable loan arrangements defined at s.620(5)). The width is the load-bearing point: there is no requirement for a formal trust deed. An informal family arrangement to share rental income, a deed of gift, a covenant, a declaration of trust, an oral understanding, or an asset transfer can each constitute a 'settlement' for s.624 purposes. The case law (Crossland v Hawkins [1961] 1 Ch 537; Arctic Systems = Jones v Garnett [2007] UKHL 35) confirms that the section catches commercial arrangements as well as estate-planning structures where the necessary 'bounty' element is present. For property cases, any arrangement to share rental income or capital with a family member at less than market value is potentially within s.620."
  - question: "What does s.624 actually attribute?"
    answer: "Verbatim s.624(1): 'Income which arises under a settlement is treated for income tax purposes as the income of the settlor and of the settlor alone if it arises (a) during the life of the settlor, and (b) from property in which the settlor has an interest.' Subsection (1A) adds that trustee expenses cannot be used to reduce the attributed income. Subsection (3) cross-refers to the three carve-outs at ss.626 to 628. The attribution is to the settlor, not to the named beneficiary; the named beneficiary's tax position on the income is unaffected by the attribution (no double taxation), but the settlor's marginal rate determines the tax bill. The retained-interest test at s.625 governs whether the settlor 'has an interest' in the property; the test is broad (any circumstances in which the property is or may become payable to or applicable for the benefit of the settlor or spouse)."
  - question: "What is the s.626 spouse carve-out (Arctic Systems)?"
    answer: "s.626(1) carves out from s.624 attribution any 'outright gift' of income-producing property between spouses or civil partners, subject to two conditions. Condition A: the gift carries a right to the whole of the income (i.e. the donee receives all the income arising from the gifted property, not a fixed sum). Condition B: the property is not wholly or substantially a right to income (i.e. the gift includes a meaningful capital interest, not just a right to receive income). The carve-out is the statutory grounding for the Arctic Systems decision (Jones v Garnett [2007] UKHL 35), where ordinary shares with full dividend rights gifted from husband to wife were held outside s.624 because both conditions were satisfied. For property contexts: a gift of an undivided share of legal title in a BTL between spouses (under a clean declaration of trust with no donor-benefit reservation) typically meets both conditions and is outside s.624. Preference shares with frozen dividend rights or partial-income gifts are at higher risk on Condition B."
  - question: "What does s.628 actually say (and what does it NOT say)?"
    answer: "s.628 is the charities exception. It carves out from s.624 attribution qualifying income arising under a UK settlement where the income is given to charity in the tax year of arising, or where a charity is entitled to the income under the trust terms. It is NOT a '5% rule'. There is no 5% threshold anywhere in s.628. The persistent misreading in older blog content (treating s.628 as a 5%-de-minimis on settlor-attribution generally) is wrong; the section addresses charities only. The closest the settlements legislation comes to a numerical de-minimis is the £100 figure at s.629(3) for minor-child distributions, which is a different mechanism for a different cohort. Sessions writing on the cluster must be precise: s.628 is charities; £100 is s.629(3); these are distinct."
  - question: "What is s.629 and how does it differ from s.624?"
    answer: "s.629 is a separate statutory attribution rule for distributions from a settlement to a minor unmarried child of the settlor. Verbatim s.629(1): 'Income which arises under a settlement is treated for income tax purposes as the income of the settlor and of the settlor alone for a tax year if, in that year and during the life of the settlor, it (a) is paid to, or for the benefit of, a relevant child of the settlor, or (b) would otherwise be treated (apart from this section) as income of a relevant child of the settlor.' Subsection (2) deconflicts with s.624 (the attribution is one or the other, not both). Subsection (3) carves out the £100 de-minimis: the section does not apply where the relevant settlement income paid to or for the relevant child in the tax year does not exceed £100 per settlement. Subsection (7)(d) defines 'relevant child' as a minor child who is unmarried or not in a civil partnership. Critical conceptual point: s.624 (settlor-with-interest) and s.629 (minor-child-of-settlor) are SEPARATE mechanisms. A bare trust for a minor child of the settlor where the settlor has no retained interest engages s.629 (income paid to child = attributed back to settlor) but not s.624 (settlor has no retained interest). The two rules are independent triggers."
  - question: "How does the £100 de-minimis at s.629(3) actually work?"
    answer: "The £100 figure is per settlement, per tax year, per relevant child. Where the relevant settlement income paid to or for the benefit of the relevant child in the tax year does not exceed £100, s.629 does not attribute the income to the settlor; the income is taxed (or not) under normal rules in the recipient's hands (typically not taxed if it is the only income and is within the child's personal allowance). The figure has not been uprated since the section was first enacted; HMRC's TSEM4300 manual confirms the literal £100 per settlement. The de-minimis is genuinely small for landlord cases: a £200,000 BTL share generating £6,000 of net rent for a minor child blasts straight past the £100 threshold, with the full £6,000 attributed to the settlor under s.629. The de-minimis is workable only for trust arrangements designed around very modest income streams (e.g. small cash deposits in a bare-trust JISA, where the gross interest at modern rates may be under £100 per year on small balances)."
  - question: "What is the grandparent-route exception to s.629?"
    answer: "s.629 attributes income to the 'settlor', defined for the section in the same way as for the rest of Part 5 Chapter 5 (any person by whom the settlement was made). Where the grandparent (not the parent) is the settlor of a trust for a minor grandchild, the grandparent is the s.629 settlor; income is attributed to the grandparent, not to the parent. This is materially better than the parent-route in three common scenarios: (a) where the grandparent is retired and on a lower marginal income tax rate than the parent (basic rate 20% vs higher rate 40% saves £1,200 on a £6,000 attributed-rent figure); (b) where the grandparent has unused personal allowance against which the attributed income can sit tax-free; (c) where the planning objective is to fund the grandchild's expenses (school fees, university costs) and the parent would otherwise have funded them out of post-tax marginal-rate income. The grandparent-route is the statutory architecture behind JISA contributions from grandparents (in their own name as settlor of the JISA arrangement, with the JISA income tax-exempt at source so s.629 has nothing to attribute) and the same logic applies to property bare-trusts settled by grandparents."
  - question: "Does a bare trust for a minor child escape s.629?"
    answer: "No, on the income-tax side. The bare trust mechanism affects CGT and IHT (TCGA 1992 s.60 treats the beneficiary as the direct CGT owner; the IHT transfer is a PET, not a CLT into the relevant property regime) but does not affect s.629 income-tax attribution. The s.629 trigger is income arising under the settlement that is paid to or treated as paid to a relevant child of the settlor; the bare-trust mechanism does not change this. A parent who gifts a BTL share to a minor child via bare trust still faces full s.629 attribution on the child's share of rental income (subject to the £100 de-minimis). The bare-trust route is structurally useful for parent-to-minor-child gifts on IHT grounds (PET clock running, no relevant property regime exposure) and on CGT grounds (full s.60 transparency, beneficiary is direct owner). It is NOT useful as an income-tax-saving structure. See our forthcoming Wave 6 B9 page on gifting property to minor children for the full operational treatment."
  - question: "What about s.631 on retained or accumulated income?"
    answer: "s.631 closes an obvious avoidance route: rather than paying income out to a minor child each year (and attracting s.629 attribution on the parent-settlor), the trustees accumulate the income inside the trust; when the child reaches 18 (and is no longer a 'relevant child' under s.629(7)(d)), the accumulated capital is distributed. s.631 catches this by treating any payment of accumulated income to a former 'relevant child' as a distribution under s.629(1) to the extent of retained-or-accumulated income available. The retained-or-accumulated-income figure is computed cumulatively across the trust's life. So a 15-year-old beneficiary whose trust accumulates £5,000 a year of rental income for three years (£15,000 retained), then receives a £20,000 capital distribution at age 19, has £15,000 of the £20,000 treated as a s.629 distribution attributable to the parent-settlor in the year of distribution. The mechanism preserves the s.629 attribution effectively for the trust's full life, regardless of distribution timing."
  - question: "Worked scenario 1: bare trust for minor child of settlor (the typical case)"
    answer: "Mr Patel, age 45, higher-rate taxpayer, gifts a 25% share of a £400,000 BTL (worth £100,000) to his 12-year-old daughter via a bare trust. The BTL generates £24,000 of net rental income per year (after allowable expenses but before finance costs; assume unmortgaged for simplicity). The daughter's 25% share of rental income is £6,000 per year. Income-tax attribution analysis: s.620 width: the bare-trust declaration is a 'transfer of assets' within the s.620 definition, so it is a settlement. s.624: Mr Patel has no retained interest in the gifted share (the daughter is the absolute owner under TCGA 1992 s.60 transparency, the property is hers from the gift date for CGT and trust purposes), so s.624 does not attribute. s.629: the £6,000 rental income is paid to (or treated as paid to) the daughter, who is a minor unmarried child of Mr Patel ('relevant child' under s.629(7)(d)); s.629(1) attributes the full £6,000 to Mr Patel; the £100 de-minimis is exceeded; the attribution is the full £6,000. Mr Patel's tax: £6,000 attributed × 40% higher-rate = £2,400 of additional income tax per year. The IHT-side benefit (PET clock running from gift) and CGT-side benefit (£100,000 minority-discounted gain held over) remain, but the income-tax outcome is identical to direct retention of the share by Mr Patel."
  - question: "Worked scenario 2: declaration of trust to adult child living rent-free in settled property"
    answer: "Mrs Khan, age 60, declares a trust over her former family home (a now-vacant £350,000 property) in favour of her 28-year-old daughter, who will live in the property as her main residence. The declaration is a transfer of assets within the s.620 width. The daughter receives no rental income (she occupies as her PPR); s.629 does not apply (the daughter is an adult, not a 'relevant child'); s.624 does not apply if the declaration is a true outright gift with no retained interest. The arrangement is outside the settlements legislation entirely. The CGT side: Mrs Khan triggers a TCGA 1992 s.17 deemed-MV disposal at the date of the declaration; latent gain crystallises (no s.260 holdover because no CLT trigger). The IHT side: PET starting 7-year clock. The structure is clean on the settlements legislation but the entry-side CGT bill must be funded. Comparison with the parent-to-minor-child bare trust above: the adult-child scenario has different statutory analysis on the income-tax side (no s.629 attribution because no minor child) but the same entry-side CGT analysis. Family circumstances drive which scenario applies."
  - question: "Worked scenario 3: the grandparent-route bypass"
    answer: "Mr Williams's father (the grandfather), aged 72, retired, basic-rate taxpayer with unused personal allowance, settles a £100,000 cash sum on bare trust for his 8-year-old grandson (Mr Williams's son). The trustees invest the cash in a buy-to-let property, which generates £6,000 of net rental income per year after allowable expenses. s.629 attribution analysis: the grandfather is the settlor (he made the settlement, not Mr Williams). s.629 attributes the £6,000 of attributable rental income to the grandfather, not to Mr Williams (the parent). The grandfather's tax: assume he has £8,000 of unused personal allowance after his other income (state pension + small workplace pension). The £6,000 attributed sits within his personal allowance: zero income tax. Annual tax saving versus the parent-route alternative: £2,400 (if Mr Williams is a higher-rate taxpayer). The structure is a textbook example of the grandparent-route bypass: same beneficiary (the grandson), same property, same rental income, but the settlor identity drives the s.629 attribution to a more favourable marginal rate. <p>The settlements legislation at ITTOIA 2005 Part 5 Chapter 5 is the income-tax anti-avoidance code that catches arrangements designed to shift rental income from a higher-rate landlord to a lower-rate family member. The single point that defines the entire code is the s.620 definition of "settlement": verbatim text reads "any disposition, trust, covenant, agreement, arrangement or transfer of assets" (with one carve-out for charitable loan arrangements). The width is deliberate. There is no requirement for a formal trust deed; an informal family understanding can be a "settlement" for s.624 purposes, and the case law confirms it.</p>

<p>The rest of the chapter elaborates the consequence. s.624 attributes income arising under the settlement back to the settlor where the settlor retains an interest. s.625 defines retained interest broadly (any circumstances in which the property is or may become payable to or applicable for the benefit of the settlor or spouse). The three carve-outs at ss.626 to 628 are narrow. s.629 is a separate statutory mechanism for minor-child distributions with a £100 per-settlement de-minimis. s.631 closes the obvious accumulate-then-distribute-at-18 avoidance route.</p>

<p>This page walks the architecture section by section with verbatim statutory wording verified against legislation.gov.uk at write time. It addresses the most common conceptual error in landlord planning (conflating s.624 settlor-with-interest with s.629 minor-child attribution as a single rule; they are independent triggers and a single arrangement can engage one, the other, both, or neither). And it sets out three worked attribution scenarios in property context: a bare trust for a minor child, a declaration of trust to an adult child living rent-free, and a grandparent-route bypass that delivers the income to the grandchild at a substantially better marginal rate.</p>

<h2>The architecture of Part 5 Chapter 5 in one paragraph</h2>

<p>s.620 defines settlement and settlor. s.624 attributes income from settled property where the settlor retains an interest (with s.625 defining "retained interest" widely). The three carve-outs at ss.626 (outright gifts between spouses), 627 (separation/commercial/pension), and 628 (charities) restrict the s.624 net. s.629 is a separate attribution rule for minor-child distributions, with the £100 de-minimis at s.629(3). s.631 backstops s.629 against accumulate-and-distribute-at-18 arrangements. The chapter operates independently of the CGT-side anti-avoidance at TCGA 1992 s.169B (settlor-interested holdover block, covered on our Wave 6 B4 page) and the IHT-side carve-out at IHTA 1984 s.49(1A) (IPDI read-through, also covered on our B4 page). Income tax, CGT, and IHT each have their own statutory hook for settlor-interested arrangements; sessions that conflate them mislead readers.</p>

<h2>s.620: the deliberately broad definition</h2>

<p>s.620(1) verbatim: "settlement" includes any disposition, trust, covenant, agreement, arrangement or transfer of assets (except that it does not include a charitable loan arrangement). s.620(5) defines charitable loan arrangements as loans of money made by individuals to charities for no consideration or for consideration consisting only of interest.</p>

<p>The width is the entire point. The legislation predates many of the family-tax-planning structures it now catches and was drafted (in its pre-2005-rewrite form) to give HMRC the widest possible reach. The case law has confirmed the literal reading: <em>Crossland v Hawkins</em> [1961] 1 Ch 537 held that a commercial arrangement between an actor's company and the actor's family was a settlement; <em>Copeman v Coleman</em> [1939] 22 TC 594 held that a salary arrangement could be a settlement; <em>Jones v Garnett</em> (the Arctic Systems case) [2007] UKHL 35 confirmed that a share gift from husband to wife was a settlement (although outside the s.624 attribution by force of s.626 because the conditions were met).</p>

<p>For property cases, any arrangement to share rental income or capital with a family member at less than market value is potentially within s.620. A declaration of trust over a beneficial share, a deed of gift of legal title, an informal agreement to direct rents to a family member, a covenant to pay a fixed share of profits: all can be settlements. The threshold question is whether there is the necessary element of "bounty" (a transfer of value made gratuitously or at undervalue); arms-length commercial dealings between unconnected parties are usually outside the section.</p>

<h2>s.624 and s.625: settlor-with-interest attribution</h2>

<p>s.624(1) verbatim: "Income which arises under a settlement is treated for income tax purposes as the income of the settlor and of the settlor alone if it arises (a) during the life of the settlor, and (b) from property in which the settlor has an interest." Subsection (1A) provides that trustee expenses cannot be used to reduce the attributed income. Subsection (3) cross-refers to the carve-outs at ss.626 to 628.</p>

<p>The retained-interest test is at s.625. s.625(1) verbatim: a settlor has an interest in property if there are "any circumstances in which the property or any related property (a) is payable to the settlor or the settlor's spouse or civil partner, (b) is applicable for the benefit of the settlor or the settlor's spouse or civil partner, or (c) will, or may, become so payable or applicable." Spouse / civil partner is treated as an extension of the settlor for the retained-interest test. "Related property" at s.625(5) means income from that property or any other property representing proceeds of, or income from, that property.</p>

<p>The "in any circumstances" formulation is the test's load-bearing language. A discretionary trust where the trustees have power to benefit the settlor, even on the most circumscribed contingency, is caught. A trust where the settlor's spouse is in the beneficiary class is caught (the spouse is treated as the settlor for the test). A trust where value can route back to the settlor by any indirect mechanism (a side letter, a pattern of trustee behaviour, an informal expectation) is caught under s.625 read with the s.169G "arrangement" definition for the parallel CGT-side test.</p>

<p>The mechanic for landlord pages: rental income from a BTL property settled on a trust where the settlor or settlor's spouse is in the beneficiary class is taxed on the settlor at the settlor's marginal rate, regardless of who the named beneficiaries are or how the trustees actually distribute the income. The income is treated as the settlor's for income tax; the trustees may still hold the cash; the income-tax liability sits on the settlor. The named beneficiary's tax position on any actual distribution is unaffected by the attribution (no double taxation), but the settlor cannot reclaim the tax paid out of any future trust distribution.</p>

<h2>s.626: the Arctic Systems spouse carve-out</h2>

<p>s.626(1) carves out from s.624 attribution any "outright gift" of income-producing property between spouses or civil partners, subject to two conditions:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Condition A:</strong> the gift carries a right to the whole of the income. The donee must receive all the income arising from the gifted property, not a fixed sum or a fraction. A gift of an ordinary share with full dividend rights meets Condition A; a gift of a preference share with a frozen dividend right does not (the dividend is capped, not "the whole of the income").</li>
<li><strong>Condition B:</strong> the property is not wholly or substantially a right to income. The gift must include a meaningful capital interest, not just an income stream. A gift of an ordinary share carrying both dividend rights and capital rights meets Condition B; a gift of just a right to receive future dividends without any capital entitlement does not.</li>
</ul>

<p>The Conditions A and B test was the centre of the Arctic Systems case (<em>Jones v Garnett</em> [2007] UKHL 35). Mr Jones gifted ordinary shares in his contracting company to his wife; HMRC argued the gift was within s.624 because the dividends she received were really his income for services rendered. The House of Lords held that the s.626 carve-out applied: the ordinary shares carried the right to whole income (Condition A) and were not wholly or substantially a right to income because they carried capital rights as well (Condition B). The outright-gift mechanic placed the dividends outside the s.624 net.</p>

<p>For property cases, a gift of an undivided share of legal title in a BTL between spouses (under a clean declaration of trust with no donor-benefit reservation) typically meets both conditions: the donee receives their share of the rental income (Condition A); the gift includes their share of capital ownership (Condition B). Form 17 elections by joint owners to split income unequally to match unequal beneficial shares can engage the same statutory framework; the s.626 carve-out usually applies if the gift creating the beneficial-share imbalance was a clean outright gift. See our Wave 5 Form 17 cluster for the operational detail.</p>

<aside>
<p>The s.626 Conditions A and B test is the single most-misread provision in the settlements legislation.</p>
<p>The Arctic Systems carve-out is genuine but narrow. It is income-tax-only (CGT and IHT consequences of the same arrangement are independent and may still bite). It applies only to outright gifts (not partial gifts, not gifts with retained interest, not gifts subject to conditions). It applies only between spouses or civil partners (not parent-child, not sibling, not cohabitant). If you are designing an alphabet-share or beneficial-share BTL structure between spouses, the Conditions A and B test must be applied precisely; failing either condition removes the s.626 shelter and the s.624 attribution bites. Get in touch via the form at the foot of the page for a structure review.</p>
</aside>

<h2>s.627: separation, commercial, pension carve-outs</h2>

<p>s.627 carves out four sub-categories of income from s.624 attribution: (a) income payable to a former spouse or civil partner under a separation or divorce provision; (b) annual payments made by an individual for commercial reasons in connection with a trade, profession, or vocation; (c) qualifying gift-aid donations; (d) benefits under a relevant pension scheme. The carve-outs are mostly outside typical landlord planning but the separation/divorce carve-out matters where rental property is settled in connection with a divorce settlement (the income from the settled property is taxed on the receiving ex-spouse, not the settlor, by force of s.627(a) rather than by the s.626 spouse carve-out which only applies to current spouses).</p>

<h2>s.628: the charities exception (NOT a 5% rule)</h2>

<p>s.628 carves out from s.624 attribution qualifying income arising under a UK settlement where the income is given to charity in the tax year of arising, or where a charity is entitled to the income under the trust terms. The carve-out is mechanical and applies to the charity-bound income only; non-charity income from the same settlement remains within s.624.</p>

<p>There is no 5% threshold in s.628. The persistent misreading in older blog content (treating s.628 as a 5%-de-minimis on settlor-attribution generally) is wrong. Sessions across the property-trust cluster must be precise: s.628 is the charities exception, with no numerical threshold; the £100 figure that does exist in the settlements legislation is at s.629(3) and is a different mechanism (per-settlement annual de-minimis on minor-child distributions). The two should never be conflated.</p>

<h2>s.629: minor-child attribution (the separate mechanism)</h2>

<p>s.629 is a structurally separate attribution rule for distributions from a settlement to a minor unmarried child of the settlor. Verbatim s.629(1): "Income which arises under a settlement is treated for income tax purposes as the income of the settlor and of the settlor alone for a tax year if, in that year and during the life of the settlor, it (a) is paid to, or for the benefit of, a relevant child of the settlor, or (b) would otherwise be treated (apart from this section) as income of a relevant child of the settlor."</p>

<p>Subsection (2) deconflicts with s.624: the attribution is one or the other, not both. Where both sections potentially apply, s.624 takes precedence (i.e. if the settlor has a retained interest under s.625, s.624 attributes; s.629 then has nothing to attribute). For pure parent-to-minor-child gift structures where the settlor has no retained interest (the classic bare-trust-for-minor-child case), s.624 does not apply and s.629 is the operative attribution rule.</p>

<p>Subsection (3) is the £100 per-settlement de-minimis: the section does not apply where the relevant settlement income paid to or for the relevant child in the tax year does not exceed £100. The figure has not been uprated since the section was first enacted; HMRC's TSEM4300 manual confirms the literal £100. For property cases, the de-minimis is structurally too small to be useful: a £200,000 BTL share generating £6,000 of net rent for a minor child blasts past the £100 threshold and the full £6,000 is attributed.</p>

<p>Subsection (7)(d) defines "relevant child" as a minor child of the settlor who is unmarried or not in a civil partnership. The cliff-edge is the child's 18th birthday: from that date prospectively, s.629 ceases to apply (the child is no longer a "relevant child" because no longer a minor). Income arising after the 18th birthday is taxed in the (now-adult) child's hands at the child's marginal rate. The cliff is prospective only; there is no retrospective re-attribution to the parent of income that was attributed during the minor years.</p>

<h2>s.631: retained and accumulated income (anti-bunching backstop)</h2>

<p>s.631 closes the obvious avoidance route of accumulating settlement income inside the trust during the child's minor years (avoiding s.629(1) attribution on those years because no distribution is made), then distributing the accumulated capital to the child after age 18 (when s.629 has ceased to apply prospectively). The mechanic: where trustees retain or accumulate settlement income and later make a payment to a relevant child, the payment is treated as a distribution under s.629(1) to the extent of retained-or-accumulated income available.</p>

<p>The retained-or-accumulated-income figure is computed cumulatively across the trust's life, less amounts already treated as settlor income, already paid to other beneficiaries, or applied to trustee expenses properly chargeable to income. So a trust that accumulates £15,000 over three years and distributes £20,000 at the child's 19th birthday has £15,000 of the £20,000 treated as a s.629 distribution attributable to the parent-settlor in the year of the £20,000 distribution. The mechanism preserves the s.629 attribution effectively for the trust's full life regardless of distribution timing.</p>

<p>The s.631 backstop is the structural reason the parent-to-minor-child trust route does not deliver income-tax savings even with sophisticated accumulation strategies. The income-tax outcome is the same whether the trust pays out annually (s.629(1) attributes annually) or accumulates and pays out later (s.631 catches the accumulated income on payment). For income-tax purposes, the parent-to-minor-child trust route is genuinely equivalent to direct retention by the parent; the route's value is on the IHT (7-year PET clock) and CGT (base-cost roll-up in child's hands) sides only.</p>

<h2>Three worked attribution scenarios in property context</h2>

<h3>Scenario 1: Patel bare trust for minor child (s.629 bites)</h3>

<p>Mr Patel, age 45, higher-rate taxpayer (marginal income tax rate 40%), gifts a 25% beneficial share of a £400,000 BTL property (gift value £100,000) to his 12-year-old daughter via a clean bare-trust declaration. The bare trustee is Mr Patel's brother (his daughter's uncle). The BTL generates £24,000 of net rental income per year (after allowable expenses, before any finance costs; assume unmortgaged). The daughter's 25% share of net rental income is £6,000 per year.</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>s.620 width:</strong> the bare-trust declaration is a "transfer of assets" within the s.620 definition. The arrangement is a settlement.</li>
<li><strong>s.624 attribution:</strong> Mr Patel has no retained interest in the gifted share (TCGA 1992 s.60 transparency places the daughter as the direct CGT owner from the gift date; the property is hers for trust purposes; the bare trustee holds legal title but cannot benefit Mr Patel). s.624 does not attribute.</li>
<li><strong>s.629 attribution:</strong> the £6,000 of rental income is paid to (or treated as paid to) the daughter, who is a minor unmarried child of Mr Patel ("relevant child" under s.629(7)(d)). s.629(1) attributes the full £6,000 to Mr Patel. The £100 de-minimis at s.629(3) is exceeded. Mr Patel reports the £6,000 on his self-assessment as attributed settlement income; tax at 40% = £2,400 per year.</li>
<li><strong>Outcome:</strong> identical income-tax outcome to direct retention of the BTL share by Mr Patel. IHT benefit (PET starts 7-year clock from gift date) and CGT benefit (gain held over into daughter's base cost? No: bare trust does not unlock s.260 holdover because no CLT trigger; the gift triggers a s.17 deemed-MV disposal with CGT bite on the £100,000 share value at residential rates) remain in play. Net of the entry-side CGT cost, the bare-trust route delivers IHT benefit (after 7 years) but no income-tax benefit at all.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Scenario 2: Khan declaration of trust to adult child living rent-free</h3>

<p>Mrs Khan, age 60, has moved out of her former family home (a £350,000 property in good condition). Her 28-year-old daughter has been renting elsewhere and would benefit from the property as her main residence. Mrs Khan declares a clean trust over the property in favour of the daughter; the daughter moves in as her principal private residence.</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>s.620 width:</strong> the declaration of trust is a "transfer of assets" within the s.620 definition. The arrangement is a settlement.</li>
<li><strong>s.624 attribution:</strong> Mrs Khan has no retained interest (clean outright gift; daughter is the direct beneficial owner). s.624 does not attribute. Even if s.624 did apply, there is no rental income to attribute (the daughter occupies as her PPR and pays no rent).</li>
<li><strong>s.629 attribution:</strong> the daughter is 28, not a minor; she is not a "relevant child" under s.629(7)(d). s.629 does not apply.</li>
<li><strong>Outcome:</strong> the arrangement is outside the settlements legislation entirely. The CGT-side analysis is separate: Mrs Khan triggers a TCGA 1992 s.17 deemed-MV disposal at the date of the declaration; latent gain crystallises (no holdover because no CLT trigger and no business-asset element). The IHT-side analysis: PET starting 7-year clock. The income-tax-side analysis: clean (the daughter occupies as PPR, no rental income to attribute). The arrangement is structurally cheap on income tax but expensive on entry-side CGT.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Scenario 3: Williams grandparent-route bypass</h3>

<p>Mr Williams (age 42, higher-rate taxpayer at 40% marginal rate) wants to fund his 8-year-old son's school fees and future university costs. His father (the grandfather, age 72, retired, basic-rate taxpayer with £8,000 of unused personal allowance after his state pension and small workplace pension) is willing to act as the funding mechanism.</p>

<p>The grandfather settles £100,000 of cash on bare trust for his grandson, with the grandfather's daughter (the grandson's aunt) as bare trustee. The trustees invest the cash in a BTL property worth £100,000 (a single-bedroom flat in a regional city). The flat generates £6,000 of net rental income per year.</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>s.620 width:</strong> the settlement of cash on trust is a clear "transfer of assets". Settlement exists.</li>
<li><strong>s.624 attribution:</strong> the grandfather has no retained interest. s.624 does not attribute.</li>
<li><strong>s.629 attribution:</strong> the £6,000 of rental income is treated as paid to (or for the benefit of) the grandson, who is a minor unmarried child of the GRANDFATHER (not of Mr Williams). s.629(1) attributes the income to the grandfather (the settlor of the trust), NOT to Mr Williams. The grandfather is "the settlor" for s.629 purposes because he made the settlement.</li>
<li><strong>Tax outcome:</strong> the grandfather receives the £6,000 as attributed settlement income. His total income for the year before attribution is, say, £12,000 (state pension + workplace pension), with £570 of personal allowance already used. He has £12,000 of personal allowance remaining (£12,570 standard 2026/27 less £570 used = £12,000 effective). The £6,000 attributed sits entirely within his unused personal allowance: zero income tax. Annual saving versus the parent-route alternative (where Mr Williams would have funded the £6,000 of school costs out of his own £6,000 of post-tax income, requiring £10,000 of pre-tax higher-rate earnings): £4,000 of effective income-tax saving per year.</li>
<li><strong>Outcome:</strong> the grandparent-route bypass delivers genuine income-tax savings within the statutory architecture. The structure is fully compliant; HMRC accepts the grandparent-route position in TSEM4300 onwards. The mechanism works because the s.629 settlor identity drives the attribution to a more favourable marginal rate.</li>
</ul>
