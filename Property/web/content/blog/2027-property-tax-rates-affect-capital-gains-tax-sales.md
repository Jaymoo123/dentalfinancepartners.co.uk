<p>From April 2027, the UK tax system introduces separate property income tax rates for the first time. While these <strong>2027 property tax rates CGT</strong> changes primarily affect rental income taxation, they create important interactions with capital gains tax (CGT) that every property investor needs to understand.</p>

<p>The new property tax rates don't directly change CGT rates themselves, but they significantly affect tax planning strategies around property disposals. Understanding how these systems work together is crucial for timing sales and structuring your property portfolio effectively.</p>

<h2>What Are the 2027 Property Tax Rate Changes?</h2>

<p>From 6 April 2027, rental income will be taxed at separate rates rather than your marginal income tax rate:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Basic rate:</strong> 22% (instead of 20% general income tax rate)</li>
<li><strong>Higher rate:</strong> 42% (instead of 40% general income tax rate)</li>
<li><strong>Additional rate:</strong> 47% (instead of 45% general income tax rate)</li>
</ul>

<p>These rates apply specifically to property income - dividends, employment income, and other sources continue using the existing income tax bands. This creates a dual tax system where your property income and other income are taxed differently.</p>

<h2>Current CGT Rates on Property (Unchanged for Now)</h2>

<p>Capital gains tax rates on residential property disposals remain:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Basic rate taxpayers:</strong> 18% CGT on property gains</li>
<li><strong>Higher rate taxpayers:</strong> 24% CGT on property gains</li>
</ul>

<p>Your CGT rate depends on your total income (including the capital gain) for the tax year. The government hasn't announced any changes to these CGT rates alongside the property income tax changes.</p>

<h2>How Property Tax Rates Affect CGT Planning</h2>

<h3>Determining Your CGT Rate Band</h3>

<p>Here's where the interaction becomes complex. When calculating which CGT rate applies, HMRC looks at your total income including:</p>

<ul>
<li>Employment/self-employment income (taxed at standard rates)</li>
<li>Rental income (from 2027: taxed at property rates)</li>
<li>Other investment income</li>
<li>The capital gain itself</li>
</ul>

<p>If your combined income (excluding the gain) plus the capital gain pushes you into higher rate territory, you pay 24% CGT. The question is: which income thresholds apply?</p>

<h3>Expected Threshold Application</h3>

<p>While HMRC hasn't published final guidance, tax practitioners expect CGT rate determination will still use the general income tax thresholds (£12,570 personal allowance, £50,270 higher rate threshold for 2026/27). This means:</p>

<ul>
<li>If your total income excluding property income, plus any capital gain, exceeds £50,270, you pay 24% CGT</li>
<li>Your property income taxed at the new rates doesn't directly determine your CGT rate band</li>
</ul>

<p>However, this interpretation awaits confirmation in Treasury guidance.</p>

<h2>CGT 2027 Changes: Strategic Implications</h2>

<h3>Timing Property Sales</h3>

<p>The separate property tax rates create new considerations for sale timing:</p>

<p><strong>Before April 2027:</strong> If you're a higher rate taxpayer primarily due to property income, you might benefit from realising gains while property income is still taxed at 40% rather than 42%.</p>

<p><strong>After April 2027:</strong> Your CGT rate calculation becomes more complex, but potentially more favourable if the new property rates don't affect CGT band determination.</p>

<h3>Portfolio Restructuring Opportunities</h3>

<p>The dual tax system creates opportunities for tax-efficient restructuring. For example, a landlord with £60,000 rental income might consider:</p>

<ul>
<li>Selling one property before April 2027 to reduce ongoing property income subject to the higher 42% rate</li>
<li>Using CGT annual exempt amount (£3,000 for 2025/26) more strategically</li>
<li>Considering <a href="/incorporation">incorporation</a> to access corporation tax rates instead</li>
</ul>

<h2>Corporation Tax vs Personal CGT After 2027</h2>

<p>The new property tax rates make limited company structures more attractive for property investment. Companies pay:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Corporation tax on rental profits:</strong> 19% (up to £250k) or 25% (above £250k)</li>
<li><strong>Corporation tax on capital gains:</strong> Same rates as trading profits</li>
</ul>

<p>A higher rate taxpayer facing 42% tax on rental income and 24% CGT might find <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/buy-to-let-limited-company-complete-guide-uk">company ownership</a> more tax-efficient, despite the complications of extracting funds.</p>

<h2>Capital Gains Tax Property 2027: Practical Examples</h2>

<h3>Example 1: Basic Rate Taxpayer</h3>

<p>Sarah has employment income of £30,000 and rental income of £15,000. She sells a buy-to-let property in 2027/28 making a £25,000 gain after costs and reliefs.</p>

<p><strong>Income tax position:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Employment income: £30,000 at 20% standard rate</li>
<li>Rental income: £15,000 at 22% property rate</li>
<li>Total income: £45,000 (still basic rate overall)</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>CGT calculation:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Total income plus gain: £45,000 + £25,000 = £70,000</li>
<li>Exceeds higher rate threshold, so 24% CGT applies</li>
<li>CGT: £25,000 × 24% = £6,000</li>
</ul>

<h3>Example 2: Property-Heavy Portfolio</h3>

<p>David has employment income of £25,000 and rental income of £40,000. He sells a property making a £30,000 gain.</p>

<p><strong>Income tax position:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Employment income: £25,000 at 20% standard rate</li>
<li>Rental income: £40,000 at mixed rates (22% basic, 42% higher)</li>
<li>Higher rate taxpayer due to combined income</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>CGT:</strong> 24% rate applies due to higher rate status, regardless of which income pushed him into that band.</p>

<h2>Planning Strategies for 2027 Changes</h2>

<h3>Pre-2027 Actions</h3>

<p>Consider these steps before the new rates take effect:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Accelerate disposals:</strong> If planning to sell anyway, bringing sales forward avoids higher ongoing property tax rates</li>
<li><strong>Use CGT allowances:</strong> Maximise annual exempt amounts in 2025/26 and 2026/27</li>
<li><strong>Review joint ownership:</strong> Married couples can use both CGT allowances and income tax bands</li>
<li><strong>Consider incorporation:</strong> Moving properties into a company before 2027 could provide long-term tax savings</li>
</ul>

<h3>Post-2027 Strategies</h3>

<p>Once the new system is in place:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Stagger disposals:</strong> Spread gains across multiple tax years to use annual exemptions</li>
<li><strong>Coordinate with other income:</strong> Time sales for years with lower non-property income</li>
<li><strong>Review relief claims:</strong> Ensure you're claiming all available <a href="/blog/capital-gains-tax/principal-private-residence-relief-landlords">reliefs</a> to reduce taxable gains</li>
</ul>

<h2>Record Keeping and Compliance</h2>

<p>The dual tax system increases compliance complexity. You'll need to maintain separate records for:</p>

<ul>
<li>Property income and expenses (for property tax rate calculations)</li>
<li>Other income sources (for standard tax rate calculations)</li>
<li>Capital gains computations (potentially affected by both)</li>
<li>Relevant dates for transitional rules</li>
</ul>

<p>With <a href="/blog/making-tax-digital-mtd/making-tax-digital-landlords-april-2026-deadline">Making Tax Digital</a> for income tax starting in April 2026, digital record-keeping becomes even more critical for navigating these changes.</p>

<h2>Professional Advice Considerations</h2>

<p>The interaction between 2027 property tax rates and CGT creates unprecedented complexity in UK property taxation. The government hasn't published complete guidance on how these systems will interact, leaving several questions unanswered:</p>

<ul>
<li>Exact mechanics of CGT rate determination under the dual system</li>
<li>Transitional rules for gains accruing before 2027 but realised after</li>
<li>Impact on reliefs and allowances</li>
<li>Administrative procedures for mixed-rate calculations</li>
</ul>

<p>Given this uncertainty, professional advice becomes essential for significant property transactions. <h2>Looking Ahead: Potential Further Changes</h2>

<p>The introduction of separate property tax rates represents a major shift in UK tax policy. It's possible that future changes could extend to CGT rates themselves, creating fully separate capital gains rates for different asset classes.</p>

<p>Property investors should monitor Treasury consultations and budget announcements for potential further changes to the tax treatment of property investment. The current CGT rates of 18% and 24% could be adjusted as the government seeks to balance revenue raising with maintaining investment incentives.</p>
