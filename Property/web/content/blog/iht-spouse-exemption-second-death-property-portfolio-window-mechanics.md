  - question: "Is the spouse exemption really unlimited?"
    answer: "Between two long-term-resident spouses or civil partners, yes. Section 18(1) IHTA 1984 exempts any transfer of value between spouses or civil partners from IHT, with no cap on the amount and no requirement that the recipient survive any minimum period. The exemption applies to lifetime transfers and to transfers on death, and it applies regardless of asset class (residential property, BTL portfolios, pensions, ISAs, business interests). The one-line answer to most clients' first IHT question is: on first death, leaving everything to the surviving spouse means no IHT is payable. The complication is what happens on second death, when the consolidated estate faces full IHT and the £2,000,000 RNRB taper threshold (see our RNRB taper page) is much more likely to bite."
  - question: "What counts as a spouse for section 18 purposes?"
    answer: "Legally married couples and registered civil partners qualify. Section 18 does not extend to unmarried partners, regardless of how long they have lived together, how many children they share, or whether they are described in beneficiary nominations as life partners. Cohabitee estates pay full IHT on transfers to each other on death. Separated spouses still qualify for section 18 until the decree absolute (or final order of dissolution) is granted; the legal-separation-while-still-married state preserves the exemption. After divorce, the exemption is lost; transfers between former spouses are full IHT events. A widowed spouse who remarries gains the exemption with the new spouse but does not regain the unused first-marriage allowances beyond those already transferred."
  - question: "What if my spouse is not UK-domiciled or not a long-term resident?"
    answer: "Section 18(2) IHTA 1984 caps the spouse exemption at £325,000 (matching the NRB amount) for transfers from a long-term-resident donor to a non-long-term-resident recipient. The post-April-2025 long-term-resident test (replacing the historic domicile concept) is set out at section 267A. An individual is long-term resident if they have been UK resident for at least 10 of the previous 20 tax years OR for 10 consecutive UK tax years. Where one spouse is long-term resident and the other is not, transfers from the LTR spouse to the non-LTR spouse exceeding £325,000 attract IHT. The fix is the section 267ZA election."
  - question: "What is the section 267ZA election and when should the non-UK spouse make it?"
    answer: "Section 267ZA IHTA 1984 lets a non-long-term-resident spouse elect to be treated as long-term resident for IHT purposes. Effect: the £325,000 limited exemption is removed and the spouse exemption becomes unlimited as it would be for a UK-LTR-to-UK-LTR transfer. The cost: the electing spouse's worldwide assets are brought within UK IHT scope for as long as the election applies. The election can be made in lifetime or by the personal representatives within 2 years of the LTR-spouse's death. Practical sense: the election is usually worthwhile where the non-LTR spouse holds limited overseas assets and the inheriting estate is a substantial UK property portfolio (the £325,000 cap would otherwise expose £1,000,000+ to immediate IHT on first death). The election is less attractive where the non-LTR spouse holds material overseas wealth that would now be drawn into UK IHT scope."
  - question: "How do my personal representatives claim transferable NRB on my second death?"
    answer: "Section 8A IHTA 1984 governs the transferable nil-rate band claim. The form is IHT402, filed by the personal representatives within 2 years of the second deceased's death (HMRC commonly accepts late claims where the delay is reasonable). The PRs need: the first deceased's name and date of death, the date of their marriage or civil partnership, the value of their chargeable estate, the value of the NRB at the first death, and the proportion of that NRB used by chargeable transfers on the first death. The unused proportion (often 100% where the first death was wholly spouse-exempt) is applied to the NRB at the date of the second death (currently £325,000). A 100% unused TNRB therefore stacks £325,000 onto the second-death estate's own £325,000 NRB, producing the combined £650,000 of NRB allowances quoted in most IHT planning advice."
  - question: "How do my personal representatives claim transferable RNRB on second death?"
    answer: "Form IHT436, filed alongside the IHT400 and IHT435 within 2 years of the second death. The mechanic mirrors IHT402: the PRs calculate the percentage of the first deceased's residence nil-rate band that was unused, and apply that percentage to the RNRB available at the second death. Where the first death was post-6-April-2017 and wholly spouse-exempt, the unused percentage is typically 100% (the first deceased's RNRB went unused because there was no chargeable transfer to a direct lineal descendant). The transferable RNRB applies even where the first death pre-dated the RNRB's introduction (deaths before 6 April 2017) on a deemed-RNRB-amount basis. The claim is then subject to the £2,000,000 taper test on the second-death estate, as covered in our RNRB taper page."
  - question: "How is the percentage calculated where the first death used some of the NRB?"
    answer: "Suppose the first deceased made a £100,000 chargeable gift in their lifetime that fell within their NRB at death, and left the residue to the surviving spouse. Of the £325,000 NRB available at the first death, £100,000 was used (the chargeable lifetime gift); £225,000 was unused. The unused percentage is 225/325 = 69.23%. On the second death (assuming the NRB is still £325,000), the TNRB available is £325,000 × 69.23% = £225,000. Combined with the second deceased's own £325,000 NRB, the available allowance is £550,000. The percentage method is set out in IHTM43021 onwards and gives the right answer even where the NRB amount has changed between the two deaths."
  - question: "What is the 2-year window for action after the first death?"
    answer: "Two related 2-year clocks. First, a deed of variation under section 142 IHTA 1984 can rewrite the first deceased's will (or apply to an intestacy) within 2 years of death, with read-back to the deceased for both IHT (section 142) and CGT (section 62(6) TCGA 1992). The variation must be in writing, signed by the beneficiary giving up the entitlement, and made for no consideration; an election is included to invoke the read-back. Second, the TNRB and TRNRB claims at the second death must be filed within 2 years of the second death on IHT402 and IHT436. The first window (DoV) is the planning interval that landlord couples should be active in; the second (claims at second death) is procedural and falls on the surviving spouse's PRs."
  - question: "Should the surviving spouse gift down the portfolio in life or leave it for second death?"
    answer: "Two different mechanics that need to be weighed together. Gifting in lifetime starts the 7-year clock under section 3A IHTA 1984: full PET exemption if the donor survives 7 years, taper relief on the IHT charged if death falls between years 3 and 7. The CGT cost is the trade-off (section 17 TCGA 1992 deemed disposal at market value, no section 165 holdover for non-business BTL). Leaving the estate intact for second death means no CGT cost but full IHT at 40% above the available NRB and RNRB allowances. The right answer depends on the surviving spouse's age, health, the CGT inherent gain in the portfolio, and the projected gap between current estate value and £2,000,000 (the RNRB taper threshold). For surviving spouses in their late 60s or early 70s with material gains in the portfolio, the IHT saving on full survival of a PET typically dominates the CGT cost; for surviving spouses in their 80s with health concerns, lifetime gifting is harder to justify on probability-weighted grounds and life cover in trust often makes more sense."
  - question: "Can a deed of variation in the 2-year window equalise allowances between the spouses?"
    answer: "Yes, this is one of the most-used applications of section 142. Suppose Mr A dies leaving everything to Mrs A under a standard mirror-will. The first death is wholly spouse-exempt, Mr A's £325,000 NRB and £175,000 RNRB go unused, and the consolidated portfolio in Mrs A's hands is £2,400,000 (above the £2,000,000 taper threshold). Within 2 years of Mr A's death, Mrs A can execute a DoV redirecting (for example) £500,000 of the portfolio to their adult children. The DoV reads back for IHT: the £500,000 is treated as having passed from Mr A to the children, using £325,000 of his NRB at zero IHT and triggering a chargeable transfer of £175,000. The chargeable transfer attracts IHT at 40% (£70,000) but: (a) the second-death estate is now £1,900,000, below the taper threshold, so the full £350,000 of combined RNRB on the second death is preserved (£140,000 of IHT saved), and (b) the £325,000 that was unused at first death is now used, so the 100% TNRB is reduced (lost benefit £130,000 of TNRB on second death). Net effect is fact-specific; the calculation often comes out positive for estates in the £2,200,000 to £2,800,000 band. Full DoV mechanics are covered separately in this Wave 4 series."
  - question: "Does the portfolio consolidation in my hands push me over the £2m RNRB taper threshold?"
    answer: "Most landlord couples with a portfolio of 4 or more BTL properties plus a family home find that the consolidated estate after first death crosses £2,000,000 even if neither standalone estate did. A typical example: Mr and Mrs B each held 50% of a £700,000 family home and 50% of a £1,800,000 BTL portfolio (net of mortgages). Each standalone estate at first death was £1,250,000, well below the £2,000,000 wall. On Mr B's death the entire £2,500,000 sits in Mrs B's name (£700,000 home + £1,800,000 portfolio). On Mrs B's later death, the £2,500,000 gross estate triggers the taper at section 8D(5) IHTA 1984 and withdraws £250,000 of RNRB. The £100,000 of IHT cost (40% of the lost RNRB) is the consolidation trap. The planning routes that avoid it are (1) lifetime equalisation pre-first-death (transferring assets between spouses to use the lower-equity spouse's NRB and RNRB on first death if they die first); (2) a DoV in the 2-year window after first death to redirect part of the estate away from the survivor; (3) lifetime gifts by the survivor after first death to shrink the estate below £2,000,000 before second death."
  - question: "What if I remarry after my spouse's death?"
    answer: "Remarriage does not retroactively change the first death; the first deceased's NRB and RNRB unused at that death remain claimable as TNRB and TRNRB by your PRs on your eventual second death. Section 8A(4) IHTA 1984 caps the TNRB at 100% of the current NRB (you cannot stack TNRB from multiple deceased spouses to get 200% or more); the lifetime maximum transferable is one full additional NRB. Remarriage to a new long-term-resident spouse restores the unlimited section 18 exemption with the new spouse, so transfers to the new spouse in life or on your death are exempt. Practical implication: a person on their second marriage has access to potentially their own NRB + RNRB plus one transferable NRB + one transferable RNRB from one prior deceased spouse, regardless of which prior spouse the transferable allowances came from. Sequencing of deaths and claims becomes complex; an estate solicitor with IHT specialisation is usually needed where multiple deceased spouses are in scope."
  - question: "How does the April 2027 pension-IHT change affect the second-death window?"
    answer: "Materially. From 6 April 2027 (per Autumn Budget 2024 and the Finance Act 2026 cycle), unused defined-contribution pension funds enter the deceased's estate for IHT. A pension that previously sat outside IHT and passed to the surviving spouse via beneficiary nomination tax-free now sits in the chargeable estate at first death (with section 18 spouse exemption applying to the transfer to spouse, same as the portfolio). On the second death the pension fund is in the chargeable estate and counts towards the £2,000,000 RNRB taper threshold (calculation at section 8D(5) IHTA 1984 uses gross E before exemptions). A couple at £1,800,000 of property + £600,000 of pensions sits below the wall on current-rules deaths; on post-April-2027 deaths the £2,400,000 gross E crosses the wall and triggers £200,000 of RNRB withdrawal. The second-death window planning conversation post-2027 must include pension drawdown sequencing, not just property gifting. See our pension-IHT April 2027 page for the detail."
  - question: "Should I plan liquidity for the second-death IHT bill?"
    answer: "For most landlord estates with consolidated portfolios above £1,500,000, yes. Property is illiquid; the IHT400 charge is payable within 6 months of the second death (with the option to pay BPR-qualifying or land charges in 10 annual instalments under section 227 IHTA 1984, interest-bearing for non-business assets). A landlord estate forced to sell a BTL property at probate is exposed to forced-sale prices, transaction costs (estate agent, solicitor, CGT on the deceased's pre-death gain if there has been one), and the income loss during the sale window. Joint-life-second-death policies are the standard product. <p>The headline IHT-on-first-death story for a landlord couple is short. Section 18 IHTA 1984 gives an unlimited inheritance tax exemption on transfers between long-term-resident spouses or civil partners; on first death the entire portfolio passes to the surviving spouse tax-free, and the IHT400 reports a chargeable estate of zero. The complication is the second death. The portfolio has consolidated in the surviving spouse's hands, and the next time the IHT door opens, it opens on the whole estate with no spouse to pass through.</p>

<p>This page is the window between those two deaths. It covers the spouse-exemption mechanics that defer the first-death charge, the limited £325,000 cap that applies where the receiving spouse is not long-term resident (and the section 267ZA election that switches it off), the transferable NRB and RNRB claim mechanics on IHT402 and IHT436 within 2 years of the second death, and the surviving spouse's decision tree for the period between the deaths: gift down in lifetime under the 7-year PET clock, redirect via a deed of variation in the 2-year section 142 window, strip value through a charitable legacy, or accept the second-death exposure and plan liquidity with life cover in trust. The page closes on two worked examples for portfolios at £1,500,000 and £2,800,000.</p>

<h2>What section 18 actually does (and what it does not)</h2>

<p>Section 18(1) IHTA 1984 reads: "A transfer of value is an exempt transfer to the extent that the value transferred is attributable to property which becomes comprised in the estate of the transferor's spouse or civil partner." The provision applies to lifetime transfers and to transfers on death, and there is no upper limit on the value transferred between two long-term-resident spouses. A landlord couple with a £3,000,000 combined estate can pass the entirety from the first dying to the survivor with no IHT consequence at the first death.</p>

<p>What section 18 does not do is move the underlying asset out of the IHT regime. The portfolio that passes to the survivor is in the survivor's estate from the moment it arrives. The IHT is deferred, not avoided. For a landlord couple, the consolidated estate after the first death is almost always larger than either standalone estate before the first death (the survivor now owns 100% of what was previously 50%-50%), and on the second death the IHT charge falls on the full consolidated value above the available NRB and RNRB allowances.</p>

<p>The exemption also does not extend to unmarried partners, however long-standing the relationship and however many children are shared. Cohabitee estates pay full IHT on transfers between partners on death; the only routes available to a cohabiting couple are the same routes available to any pair of unrelated individuals (annual exemption, lifetime gifts under the 7-year clock, charity legacy, life cover in trust). The marriage or civil partnership is the gating fact for section 18; conversion of a long cohabitation into a marriage or civil partnership unlocks the exemption from that date onward.</p>

<h2>The non-long-term-resident spouse exception</h2>

<p>From 6 April 2025 the historic domicile concept for IHT was replaced by a residence-based long-term-resident (LTR) test (per the Autumn Budget 2024 reform legislated in the Finance Act 2025). An individual is long-term resident for IHT purposes where either they have been UK resident for 10 of the previous 20 UK tax years, or they have been UK resident for 10 consecutive UK tax years (the two-route test set out at section 267A). The long-term-resident concept replaces the old "deemed domicile" and "domicile" framing entirely.</p>

<p>Where the transferring spouse is long-term resident but the receiving spouse is not, section 18(2) caps the spouse exemption at £325,000 (matching the current NRB amount). Above £325,000, transfers to the non-LTR spouse attract IHT on the same basis as transfers to a non-spouse. For a landlord couple where one spouse has been resident in the UK long enough to be LTR (typically the working-life spouse) and the other has spent recent years living abroad or has never been UK-resident, the consolidated portfolio that "obviously" should pass spouse-exempt on first death actually walks straight into a £1,000,000 or larger chargeable transfer.</p>

<p>The fix is section 267ZA. The non-LTR spouse can elect (in lifetime, or by the PRs within 2 years of the LTR spouse's death) to be treated as long-term resident for IHT purposes. The election lifts the £325,000 cap and the section 18 exemption becomes unlimited as it would be for two LTRs. The cost is that the electing spouse's worldwide assets are brought within UK IHT scope; for a non-LTR spouse holding limited overseas assets the trade is almost always worthwhile, for a non-LTR spouse holding material overseas wealth it requires careful modelling. Our <a href="/blog/non-resident-landlord-tax/iht-non-resident-uk-property-april-2025-residence-test">long-term-resident IHT test page</a> covers the residence test and the section 267ZA election in detail.</p>

<h2>Why first-death relief is second-death exposure</h2>

<p>The structural problem for landlord couples is consolidation. Before first death, each spouse typically holds 50% of the family home and 50% of the BTL portfolio (joint tenancy or tenancy in common). Each standalone estate is half the family wealth. After first death, with everything passing under section 18 to the surviving spouse, the survivor now holds 100% of the family wealth in one estate. The same asset base, the same total IHT exposure on death, but concentrated.</p>

<p>The headline £1,000,000 of combined allowances quoted in IHT advice (£325,000 NRB each + £175,000 RNRB each, transferable on second death) is preserved by the consolidation: the first deceased's unused NRB and RNRB transfer to the second-death estate via IHT402 and IHT436. That much is symmetric. What is not symmetric is the section 8D(5) RNRB taper at £2,000,000, which uses the gross value of the estate (E) at death, before any spouse or charity exemption. A standalone estate of £1,250,000 was nowhere near the wall; the consolidated £2,500,000 is £500,000 over it.</p>

<p>For a couple with a £700,000 family home and a £1,800,000 BTL portfolio split 50/50, the standalone first-death E is £1,250,000 (no taper, full RNRB available, but RNRB unused because the spouse exemption took the QRI off the table). On the survivor's second death, E is £2,500,000 (£500,000 over the threshold), the RNRB withdrawal is £250,000, and the resulting IHT on the lost allowance is £100,000. The full taper math is in the <a href="/blog/landlord-tax-essentials/iht-residence-nil-rate-band-2m-taper-property-portfolios">RNRB taper page</a>; this page is the planning window before it bites.</p>

<h2>The transferable allowances claim on second death</h2>

<p>The PRs of the second deceased file two transferable-allowance claims alongside the IHT400 estate account: IHT402 for the transferable nil-rate band, IHT436 for the transferable residence nil-rate band. Both forms are filed within 2 years of the second death; HMRC commonly accepts late claims where the delay is reasonable.</p>

<p><strong>IHT402 (TNRB).</strong> Section 8A IHTA 1984 carries the rule. The claim is the percentage of the first deceased's NRB that was unused on the first death, applied to the NRB at the date of the second death. Where the first death was wholly spouse-exempt (the default for a mirror-will landlord couple), the unused percentage is 100% and the second deceased's estate stacks £325,000 of own NRB + £325,000 of TNRB = £650,000 of combined NRB allowances. Where the first death used some of the NRB (a chargeable lifetime gift in the seven years before first death, or a non-spouse-exempt portion of the estate left to children), the unused percentage drops and the TNRB claim is correspondingly reduced. The percentage formula handles the case where the NRB amount has changed between the two deaths.</p>

<p><strong>IHT436 (TRNRB).</strong> The mirror form for the residence nil-rate band, also a percentage-based claim. Where the first deceased's RNRB went unused (typical for a spouse-exempt first death), 100% of the first deceased's RNRB transfers. The transferable RNRB applies even where the first death pre-dated 6 April 2017 (when the RNRB was introduced); HMRC deems an RNRB to have been available for the calculation and the percentage applies as for post-2017 deaths. The transferable RNRB at the second death is then itself subject to the section 8D(5) taper test on the second-death E. A consolidated estate above £2,700,000 loses both the own RNRB and the transferable RNRB in full.</p>

<p>Practical procedural points the PRs should plan for. First, the first deceased's IHT200 / IHT400 records and the date of death certificate are needed to populate the IHT402 / IHT436 calculation; surviving spouses in their 80s or 90s, where the first death was 20+ years ago, sometimes struggle to locate these records. A "what we will need on second death" file built early in the second-death window is cheap insurance. Second, the 2-year filing window is from the second death, not the first; the PRs file the claims at the same time as the second-death IHT400. Third, the IHT435 (own RNRB claim) goes alongside IHT436; both forms reference the same QRI nomination and downsizing-addition position. The HMRC manual at IHTM43000 onwards walks the mechanics in detail.</p>

<h2>The 2-year deed-of-variation window</h2>

<p>Section 142 IHTA 1984 is the second 2-year clock relevant to the window. Within 2 years of the first death, a beneficiary of the first deceased's estate (in a typical mirror-will arrangement, the surviving spouse) can vary their entitlement and redirect it elsewhere, with read-back to the deceased for IHT (section 142) and for CGT (section 62(6) TCGA 1992). The variation must be in writing, signed by the beneficiary giving up the entitlement, for no consideration in exchange, and include an election that the read-back applies. The personal representatives of the first deceased must concur if the variation increases the IHT charge on the first-death estate.</p>

<p>For a landlord couple, the most common use is redirecting part of the first-death estate from the surviving spouse to the adult children. Effect: the redirected portion is treated as having passed direct from the first deceased to the children, uses the first deceased's NRB and RNRB at the date of first death (which otherwise would have transferred 100% to the second death), and reduces the consolidated estate the survivor inherits. The trade-off is the lost TNRB / TRNRB on second death (because the first-death allowances are now used, not unused), set against the lower second-death E and the preserved RNRB at second death (because the consolidated estate is now below the £2,000,000 wall).</p>

<p>The DoV calculation is fact-specific and often net-positive for estates in the £2,200,000 to £2,800,000 band, where moving £400,000 to £700,000 of value out of the survivor's hands drops second-death E below £2,000,000 and preserves a full £350,000 of combined RNRB. Above £2,800,000 and below £2,200,000 the calculation typically reverses (no taper saving to capture, or the lost TNRB / TRNRB at second death is worth more than the taper saving). Detailed DoV mechanics for landlord estates are covered separately in this Wave 4 series at the slug <code>deed-of-variation-property-estate-redirecting-inheritance-iht-saving</code>.</p>

<h2>The surviving spouse's decision tree</h2>

<p>Once the first death has settled and the consolidated estate is in the survivor's hands, four routes are available for the planning interval before second death. Each addresses the consolidation problem from a different angle.</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Lifetime gifting under the 7-year clock.</strong> The survivor gifts properties (or shares in a Family Investment Company holding the portfolio) to adult children or other intended beneficiaries as Potentially Exempt Transfers under section 3A IHTA 1984. Full IHT exemption if the survivor lives 7 years from the gift, with taper relief on the IHT due if death falls between years 3 and 7. The CGT cost (section 17 TCGA 1992 deemed disposal at market value, no section 165 holdover available for non-business BTL) is the trade-off. Best for survivors in their late 60s or early 70s with good health and material gains in the portfolio. Detailed mechanics at our <a href="/blog/landlord-tax-essentials/iht-lifetime-gifts-7-year-rule-property-taper">7-year-rule for lifetime property gifts page</a>.</li>
<li><strong>Deed of variation in the first 2 years.</strong> Redirect part of the first-death estate to children directly, reading back to the first deceased and saving the survivor's RNRB at second death. Most effective in the £2,200,000 to £2,800,000 estate band. Time-limited: the 2-year clock starts on the first death, not on probate grant.</li>
<li><strong>Charitable legacy on second death.</strong> Where at least 10% of the relevant component of the estate passes to qualifying charity on second death, the IHT rate on the chargeable estate drops from 40% to 36% under Schedule 1A IHTA 1984. The mathematics often makes the gift self-funding in net family terms for estates above £2,000,000 where the RNRB has been lost to the taper. The charitable-legacy mechanics are covered in this Wave 4 series at the slug <code>iht-charitable-legacy-property-portfolio-36-percent-reduced-rate</code>.</li>
<li><strong>Accept second-death exposure, plan liquidity.</strong> Where lifetime gifting and DoV are unappealing or unavailable (survivor's age, portfolio liquidity, family circumstances), the conservative plan is to size the projected second-death IHT exposure and arrange whole-of-life cover written in trust to fund the IHT bill. The trust receives the policy proceeds outside the IHT estate; the trustees pay the IHT bill on second death; the descendants inherit the portfolio intact. Underwriting tightens with age; the policy is usually arranged during the first spouse's life rather than after first death.</li>
</ul>

<p>The four routes are not mutually exclusive. A practical plan for a £2,500,000 consolidated estate often combines a modest DoV in the first 2 years (£300,000 redirected to children), a programme of lifetime gifting in years 3 to 10 of the window (£500,000 of BTL units gifted as PETs), and a backstop life-cover policy sized to the residual second-death exposure. The strategic-framework counterpart to this page is our <a href="/blog/landlord-tax-essentials/iht-property-investors-decision-framework-2026-onwards">IHT decision framework for landlords</a>.</p>

<h2>The April 2027 pension overlay</h2>

<p>From 6 April 2027, unused defined-contribution pension funds and unused defined-benefit lump-sum death benefits enter the deceased's estate for IHT (announced at Autumn Budget 2024 and enacted by Finance Act 2026, which received Royal Assent on 18 March 2026). For a couple where the first death is post-April-2027, the surviving spouse's consolidated estate now includes any pension funds nominated to the survivor under beneficiary nomination, in addition to the property portfolio. The £2,000,000 wall is reached more quickly; the planning window between deaths is materially shorter in effective terms.</p>

<p>A couple at £1,800,000 of property + £600,000 of combined pensions sits comfortably below the wall on a current-rules death. On a post-April-2027 death the £2,400,000 gross E crosses the threshold by £400,000, withdraws £200,000 of RNRB on the survivor's death (assuming everything passed survivor-exempt on first death), and adds £80,000 of IHT. The second-death window planning conversation post-2027 must include the pension drawdown decision: pre-2027 wisdom was to leave pensions untouched and draw down the taxable BTL portfolio first; from 2027 the pension is also in IHT scope, and the case for drawing it down (or making lifetime gifts out of it where DC pension rules permit) is materially stronger. Our <a href="/blog/landlord-tax-essentials/pension-iht-april-2027-landlord-estate-planning">pension-IHT April 2027 page</a> walks the decumulation sequencing implications.</p>

<h2>Two worked windows</h2>

<h3>Smaller window: the Patels (consolidated £1,500,000)</h3>

<p>Mr Patel dies 2026. Joint family home in Slough £550,000 (50/50), three BTL flats £600,000 net total (50/50), savings £100,000 in joint names. Mr Patel's standalone estate at first death: £625,000 (£275,000 home + £300,000 BTLs + £50,000 cash). The first-death estate passes wholly to Mrs Patel under section 18, no IHT, Mr Patel's £325,000 NRB and £175,000 RNRB both unused.</p>

<p>Mrs Patel's consolidated estate on first death: £1,250,000 (excluding any growth and any of her own subsequent assets). Even on a 2-3% per annum appreciation, the projected second-death E is £1,550,000 to £1,700,000 within 10 years. Below the £2,000,000 taper threshold throughout.</p>

<p>Second-death window assessment: the Patel estate is comfortably below the wall and likely to remain so. The transferable NRB and TRNRB claim on Mrs Patel's death stacks £325,000 + £325,000 + £175,000 + £175,000 = £1,000,000 of allowances against an estate that will probably be in the £1,500,000 to £1,700,000 band. Chargeable estate £500,000 to £700,000; IHT £200,000 to £280,000. Modest planning room (annual exemptions £3,000 each year, small-gifts £250 per recipient, perhaps modest charitable legacy on Mrs Patel's death) is enough. No DoV needed; no major lifetime gifts needed. The cheapest plan is the right plan: don't over-engineer where the taper does not bite.</p>

<h3>Larger window: the Mawell-Smiths (consolidated £2,800,000)</h3>

<p>Mr Mawell-Smith dies 2026. Joint family home in Reading £900,000 (50/50), seven BTL properties net portfolio £1,800,000 (50/50 via tenants-in-common), ISAs and cash £100,000. Mr Mawell-Smith's standalone first-death estate: £1,400,000. Wholly spouse-exempt; £325,000 NRB and £175,000 RNRB both unused.</p>

<p>Mrs Mawell-Smith's consolidated estate after first death: £2,800,000. On second death without any planning, the gross E of £2,800,000 exceeds the £2,000,000 threshold by £800,000, withdraws £400,000 of RNRB (which on the £350,000 of combined allowance fully extinguishes the RNRB), and produces an IHT charge of £820,000 (£2,800,000 minus £650,000 combined NRB = £2,150,000 chargeable at 40%).</p>

<p>Three planning moves available in the window. (1) DoV within 2 years of Mr Mawell-Smith's death redirecting £600,000 of the BTL portfolio to their two adult children. Effect: £325,000 of Mr Mawell-Smith's NRB used (no IHT on first £325,000 to children, £275,000 of further chargeable transfer reading back to Mr Mawell-Smith uses some of the surviving allowance, around £110,000 of IHT on the first-death IHT400). Mrs Mawell-Smith's E drops to £2,200,000; second-death E above £2,000,000 by £200,000, withdrawing £100,000 of RNRB (£250,000 RNRB preserved out of the £350,000 combined available, £40,000 less IHT than no DoV). (2) Lifetime gifting by Mrs Mawell-Smith of a further £400,000 in BTL value over 3 years post-first-death as PETs; if she survives 7 years from the gifts, second-death E drops to £1,800,000, the RNRB is fully restored, and combined IHT falls dramatically. CGT cost on the £400,000 of gifts at her marginal 24% rate is approximately £60,000 to £80,000 depending on inherent gain. (3) Whole-of-life cover in trust written for £200,000 to £400,000 to backstop the residual second-death exposure regardless of which other planning routes land.</p>

<p>The combined plan (DoV + lifetime PETs + backstop life cover) takes the worst-case second-death IHT charge from £820,000 to a range of £150,000 to £300,000 depending on Mrs Mawell-Smith's survival and the PET clock. The DoV must be executed within 2 years of Mr Mawell-Smith's death; the lifetime gifting must start as early as possible after first death (years on the clock matter more than year-on-year amounts); the life cover must be arranged early enough that Mrs Mawell-Smith's age and health do not make the premium prohibitive.</p>

<aside><p>Window planning for a £2m+ consolidated landlord estate is rarely a single-move decision.</p><p>The right combination of DoV, lifetime gifting, and life cover depends on the survivor's age, the CGT position of the portfolio, and family circumstances. We work alongside estate solicitors on the IHT calculation and the decision sequence; the conversation is most productive in the first 18 months after first death while the DoV window is still open.</p></aside>

<h2>Closing pointers</h2>

<p>The second-death window is the planning moment landlord couples should be active in. The first-death spouse exemption defers the IHT, it does not avoid it; the procedural mechanics on second death (TNRB / TRNRB claims on IHT402 / IHT436 within 2 years) are settled but the substantive decisions (DoV in the first 2 years, lifetime gifting under the 7-year clock, charity legacy, life cover) all have time-sensitive components and the routes that need the most lead time are the routes most consequential at scale.</p>

<p>Three priorities for a surviving spouse in a landlord couple in 2026. First, size the consolidated estate against the £2,000,000 RNRB taper threshold using the section 8D(5) gross-E methodology (full mechanics at the <a href="/blog/landlord-tax-essentials/iht-residence-nil-rate-band-2m-taper-property-portfolios">RNRB taper page</a>). Second, decide whether a DoV in the first 2 years is worth executing and act before the window closes. Third, choose between lifetime gifting, charitable legacy and life cover for the residual exposure, on a basis that integrates CGT (section 17 TCGA disposal at MV on gifts), liquidity (the property is illiquid; the IHT charge is payable within 6 months of death), and family circumstances. The <a href="/blog/landlord-tax-essentials/iht-property-investors-decision-framework-2026-onwards">IHT decision framework for landlords</a> page is the planning-lens companion to the procedural mechanics here.</p>
