- question: How do I calculate VAT for a commercial property business?
  answer: First decide whether the supply is standard-rated, reduced-rated, zero-rated or exempt, because that determines whether VAT applies at all. For a taxable supply, add VAT to a net figure by multiplying by the rate (net x 1.20 for the 20% standard rate), or strip VAT from a gross figure by dividing by 1.20 and treating the difference as the VAT. The output VAT you charge on rents and sales, less the input VAT you can reclaim on costs, is what you pay to or reclaim from HMRC each period. The arithmetic is simple. The judgement is in the rate and in how much input VAT you are actually entitled to recover.
- question: Do I have to charge VAT on commercial property rent?
  answer: Not automatically. Commercial rent is exempt from VAT by default, so you neither charge VAT to tenants nor reclaim VAT on related costs. If you make a valid option to tax on the property, you must then charge VAT at 20% on the rent, and in return you can recover input VAT on repairs, professional fees and agent fees relating to that property. The option is a deliberate choice with long-term consequences, so it should follow advice, not precede it.
- question: What VAT rate applies to building work on a property?
  answer: It depends on the work and the property. Most repairs and maintenance are standard-rated at 20%. The reduced 5% rate can apply to qualifying conversions, such as changing the number of dwellings in a building, and to renovating a home that has been empty for two years or more. Construction of new-build residential property is zero-rated. Commercial construction is standard-rated. Always check the detailed conditions in VAT Notice 708 before applying anything below 20%.
- question: What is the option to tax and why does it matter for VAT recovery?
  answer: An option to tax (sometimes called electing to waive exemption) turns an otherwise exempt commercial property supply into a taxable one. Once you have opted, you charge VAT on rent and on a future sale, and you can reclaim the input VAT on costs connected to that property. It matters because without it you cannot recover VAT on refurbishment, professional fees or agent commission for that building. The option lasts 20 years and can usually only be revoked in a six-month cooling-off window or after 20 years, so it is close to irrevocable in practice.
- question: How long does an option to tax last?
  answer: An option to tax generally lasts 20 years from the date it takes effect. You can revoke it in a six-month cooling-off period shortly after it starts (subject to conditions), automatically if you have held no interest in the property for more than six years, or after the 20-year point. Because it binds you and affects buyers and tenants for two decades, treat it as a long-term commitment rather than a quick recovery trick.
- question: What is the de minimis limit for VAT partial exemption?
  answer: You can recover all your input VAT, including the part relating to exempt supplies, if your exempt input tax is below the de minimis limit. The test is that exempt input tax is not more than 625 pounds per month on average and is no more than half of your total input tax in the period. If you breach either part of the test, you must restrict your recovery to the taxable proportion under the partial exemption rules and carry out an annual adjustment.
- question: Can I reclaim VAT on costs for a property I let exempt?
  answer: Generally no. If you let a property and the rent is exempt (residential rent, or commercial rent where you have not opted to tax), the input VAT on costs relating to that letting is exempt input tax and is normally blocked. This is the trap that catches landlords who own a small commercial unit alongside residential property and assume all their VAT is recoverable. It is not, unless an option to tax or the de minimis rules apply.
- question: When does a property business have to register for VAT?
  answer: You must register when your taxable turnover exceeds the VAT registration threshold of 90,000 pounds in any rolling 12-month period, or if you expect to exceed it in the next 30 days alone. Exempt income, such as residential rent and un-opted commercial rent, does not count towards the threshold. Property developers selling zero-rated new-build homes and landlords who have opted to tax make taxable supplies, so they may need to register, and registering can let a developer recover input VAT on a project.
- question: Should a property developer register for VAT before starting a project?
  answer: Often yes. Selling new-build residential property is a zero-rated taxable supply, which means a developer can register for VAT and reclaim input VAT on construction and professional costs while charging 0% on the eventual sale. There are rules for recovering input VAT incurred before registration, so the timing of registration matters. Getting the VAT position set up correctly at the outset of a development usually recovers far more than tidying it up later.
- question: How do I work out the VAT-inclusive rent if I have opted to tax?
  answer: If you want to receive a net rent and have opted to tax, add 20% to arrive at the VAT-inclusive figure. For example, on a net quarterly rent of 10,000 pounds, the VAT is 2,000 pounds and the VAT-inclusive rent invoiced to the tenant is 12,000 pounds. You pay the 2,000 pounds of output VAT to HMRC, offset by input VAT you can reclaim on the property's costs. Always state clearly in the lease and invoices whether figures are inclusive or exclusive of VAT.
- question: Does VAT apply to converting commercial property into residential?
  answer: A conversion that changes the number of dwellings, or converts a non-residential building into dwellings, can qualify for the reduced 5% rate on the construction services, and a sale of the converted dwellings by the developer may be zero-rated. The rules are detailed and condition-heavy, so the route through VAT Notice 708 needs checking case by case. The recovery position for a developer doing this work is often very different from a buy-to-let landlord doing a simple refurbishment.
- question: Do I still need to file VAT returns digitally?
  answer: Yes. Making Tax Digital for VAT applies to all VAT-registered businesses. You must keep digital records and submit your VAT returns using MTD-compatible software, with a digital link from your records through to the figures on the return. Spreadsheets are allowed only where bridging software provides that digital link. This is separate from MTD for Income Tax, which is being phased in for landlords by income level.
- question: Is a free online VAT calculator enough for a property business?
  answer: A free calculator handles the net-to-gross arithmetic, and that part is genuinely easy. What it cannot do is tell you which rate applies, whether you have validly opted to tax, whether a supply is exempt, or how partial exemption restricts your recovery. Those decisions drive the real numbers and the real risk, which is why VAT on property is one of the areas where specialist advice consistently pays for itself.

<p>A VAT calculator does one thing well: it turns a net figure into a gross figure and back again. For most purchases that is all you need. For a commercial property business it is the easy 10% of the problem. The other 90% is deciding which rate applies in the first place, whether you have opted to tax, and how much of the VAT you pay on costs you are actually allowed to reclaim. Get those wrong and the cleanest arithmetic in the world still produces the wrong number on your return.</p>

<p>This guide works through how to calculate VAT for a commercial property business from first principles. We cover the net-to-gross sums, the rate that applies to each kind of supply, the option to tax that unlocks recovery on commercial buildings, the partial-exemption test that catches mixed portfolios, and what registration and Making Tax Digital mean for landlords and developers. The worked figures use round numbers so you can follow the method, not memorise a single answer.</p>

<h2>The arithmetic: how to calculate VAT on a figure</h2>
<p>There are only two calculations, and both come down to the rate you are working with.</p>

<p><strong>Adding VAT to a net (VAT-exclusive) amount.</strong> Multiply the net figure by 1 plus the rate. At the 20% standard rate, net x 1.20 gives the gross. So a net cost of 5,000 pounds becomes 6,000 pounds gross, and the VAT element is 1,000 pounds.</p>

<p><strong>Removing VAT from a gross (VAT-inclusive) amount.</strong> Divide the gross figure by 1 plus the rate. At 20%, gross / 1.20 gives the net, and the difference is the VAT. So a gross invoice of 6,000 pounds is 5,000 pounds net with 1,000 pounds of VAT inside it. A common mistake is taking 20% off the gross figure, which understates the VAT: 20% of 6,000 is 1,200, but the VAT actually inside that 6,000 is 1,000.</p>

<p>For the reduced 5% rate, the same logic uses 1.05 instead of 1.20. That is the whole of what an online VAT calculator does. The question that decides whether you use 1.20, 1.05, 1.00 or nothing at all is the rate, and on property that is anything but automatic.</p>

<h2>Output VAT, input VAT, and what you actually pay HMRC</h2>
<p>For a commercial property business the headline calculation each VAT period is straightforward in shape: the VAT you charge on your taxable supplies (output VAT) minus the VAT you can reclaim on your costs (input VAT) is what you pay to, or reclaim from, HMRC.</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Output VAT</strong> is the VAT you add to rents and sales, but only on supplies that are taxable. Exempt rent carries no output VAT.</li>
<li><strong>Input VAT</strong> is the VAT on your costs (repairs, refurbishment, professional fees, agent commission). You can only reclaim input VAT to the extent it relates to taxable supplies you make.</li>
</ul>

<p>That second condition is the whole game. If your supplies are exempt, the input VAT on the related costs is generally blocked, no matter how large the invoice. So before any arithmetic, classify the supply.</p>

<p>The pattern is consistent. Taxable supplies (standard, reduced or zero-rated) carry recovery; exempt supplies do not. That is why the option to tax matters so much for commercial landlords, and why developers building zero-rated new homes are usually keen to register.</p>

<h2>The option to tax: how commercial landlords unlock recovery</h2>
<p>Commercial rent is exempt by default. That sounds harmless until you spend heavily refurbishing a unit and find you cannot reclaim a penny of the VAT, because the rent you charge is exempt and the input VAT on the refurbishment is exempt input tax.</p>

<p>The fix is the option to tax (formally, electing to waive exemption under Schedule 10 of the Value Added Tax Act 1994). Once you have made a valid option on a property, the rent and any future sale become standard-rated, and the input VAT on costs for that property becomes recoverable. In effect you trade charging your tenant 20% for the right to reclaim VAT on your spending.</p>

<p>It is not a decision to make lightly. An option to tax generally lasts 20 years. You can usually only revoke it in a short cooling-off period at the start, automatically once you have held no interest in the property for more than six years, or after the 20 years are up. It also affects buyers and tenants, some of whom (banks, insurers, charities, residential converters) cannot recover the VAT you charge, which can make your building harder to let or sell. We set out the full mechanics, and the routes back out, in our guides on the <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/vat-option-to-tax-commercial-property-mechanics-election-revocation">option to tax mechanics, election and revocation</a> and on <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/option-to-tax-revocation-routes-6-month-cooling-off-20-year-exit-landlords">revocation routes including the six-month cooling-off and 20-year exit</a>.</p>

<h3>Worked example: opted commercial rent</h3>
<p>Suppose you have opted to tax a commercial unit and want to receive a net quarterly rent of 10,000 pounds. You add 20% VAT:</p>
<ul>
<li>Net rent: 10,000 pounds</li>
<li>Output VAT at 20%: 2,000 pounds</li>
<li>VAT-inclusive rent invoiced to the tenant: 12,000 pounds</li>
</ul>
<p>You pay the 2,000 pounds of output VAT to HMRC. Against that, suppose you spent 18,000 pounds plus 3,600 pounds VAT refurbishing the unit in the same period. Because you have opted to tax, that 3,600 pounds is recoverable input VAT. Your net position for the period is output VAT of 2,000 pounds minus input VAT of 3,600 pounds, a reclaim of 1,600 pounds. Without the option, the rent would be exempt, you would charge no VAT, and the 3,600 pounds of input VAT would be lost.</p>

<h2>Partial exemption: the test mixed portfolios get wrong</h2>
<p>If your business makes both taxable and exempt supplies, you are partially exempt and you cannot reclaim all your input VAT. This is extremely common in property: a landlord with an opted commercial unit (taxable) and residential flats (exempt), or a developer with a mix of zero-rated new homes and exempt lettings.</p>

<p>Input VAT splits into three pots:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Directly attributable to taxable supplies</strong>: fully recoverable.</li>
<li><strong>Directly attributable to exempt supplies</strong>: not recoverable.</li>
<li><strong>Residual (overheads relating to both)</strong>: recoverable only in the taxable proportion, calculated under the standard method (usually by reference to the value of taxable versus total supplies).</li>
</ul>

<p>There is a relief that can rescue small amounts of exempt input tax. Under the <strong>de minimis</strong> test you can recover all your input VAT, including the exempt part, if your exempt input tax is not more than 625 pounds per month on average and is no more than half of your total input tax in the period. Breach either limb and you must restrict recovery to the taxable proportion. Partial exemption is also subject to an annual adjustment, so the position is finalised across the VAT year, not period by period. Our <a href="/blog/landlord-tax-essentials/vat-on-rental-income-residential-vs-commercial-landlord-vat-status-decision-framework">decision framework for VAT on residential versus commercial rental income</a> walks through where each pot lands.</p>

<h2>Registration: when a property business must register for VAT</h2>
<p>You must register for VAT once your taxable turnover exceeds the registration threshold of 90,000 pounds in any rolling 12-month period, or if you expect to breach it in the next 30 days on its own. The key word is taxable. Exempt income (residential rent, and commercial rent where you have not opted to tax) does not count towards the threshold, which is why a landlord with a large but wholly exempt rent roll may never need to register.</p>

<p>The picture changes for developers and opted landlords:</p>
<ul>
<li>A <strong>developer selling new-build residential property</strong> makes zero-rated taxable supplies. Registering allows recovery of input VAT on construction and professional costs while charging 0% on the sale. There are specific rules for recovering pre-registration input VAT, so the timing of registration on a development matters.</li>
<li>A <strong>commercial landlord who has opted to tax</strong> makes standard-rated taxable supplies, so opted rent counts towards the threshold and usually points towards registration.</li>
</ul>

Developers should also read how to recover <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/vat-developer-pre-registration-input-tax-recovery-property-development-projects">pre-registration input VAT on development projects</a>.</p>

<h2>Conversions and developments: where the rate moves below 20%</h2>
<p>Property development is where the reduced and zero rates do real work, and where the calculation is most worth getting right at the outset.</p>

<p>Converting a non-residential building into dwellings, or changing the number of dwellings in a building, can bring the construction services within the reduced 5% rate, and the developer's eventual sale of the new dwellings may be zero-rated. Renovating a home that has been empty for two years or more can also qualify for 5%. These are condition-heavy reliefs governed by VAT Notice 708, and the recovery position for a developer carrying out a conversion is usually very different from a buy-to-let landlord doing a straightforward refurbishment. We cover the detail in our guide to <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/vat-commercial-to-residential-conversion-5-percent-reduced-rate-developer-recovery-mechanics">commercial-to-residential conversion at the 5% reduced rate and developer recovery</a>.</p>

<p>If you are weighing up where capital spend on a commercial building can also generate income tax or corporation tax relief, read it alongside <a href="/blog/property-types-and-specialist-tax/capital-allowances-commercial-property-what-can-claim">capital allowances on commercial property and what you can claim</a>. The VAT treatment and the allowances analysis run on separate tracks but both bite on the same refurbishment invoice.</p>

<h2>Making Tax Digital: how the figures reach your return</h2>
<p>Whatever the calculation, all VAT-registered businesses must keep digital records and file returns through Making Tax Digital compatible software, with a digital link from the underlying records to the figures on the return. Spreadsheets are permitted only where bridging software supplies that link. In practice this means building the VAT logic (the rate decisions, the option-to-tax position, the partial-exemption split) into your bookkeeping so the numbers flow through cleanly rather than being recreated each quarter.</p>

<p>This is separate from Making Tax Digital for Income Tax, which is now being phased in for landlords by income level (from 6 April 2026 for those with qualifying income over 50,000 pounds, from 6 April 2027 at over 30,000 pounds, and from 6 April 2028 at over 20,000 pounds). If you run both a VAT-registered property trade and a personal rental business, you may be inside both regimes. Our <a href="/blog/making-tax-digital-mtd/making-tax-digital-property-income-2026-complete-guide">complete guide to Making Tax Digital for property income</a> sets out the income-tax side.</p>

<h2>Common VAT mistakes property businesses make</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Assuming every building invoice is 20%.</strong> Many conversions and renovations qualify for 5%, and new-build residential construction is zero-rated. Check the supply, not the habit.</li>
<li><strong>Reclaiming input VAT on exempt lettings.</strong> If the rent is exempt, the input VAT on those costs is normally blocked. A small commercial unit does not make a residential portfolio's VAT recoverable.</li>
<li><strong>Opting to tax without thinking it through.</strong> The option lasts 20 years and can deter VAT-sensitive tenants and buyers. It should follow a recovery and exit analysis, not precede it.</li>
<li><strong>Ignoring partial exemption.</strong> Mixed taxable and exempt supplies require the standard-method split and an annual adjustment. The de minimis test can help, but it has to be checked each period.</li>
<li><strong>Stripping VAT off the gross with a flat 20%.</strong> The VAT inside a gross figure is gross / 6 at the 20% rate, not 20% of the gross. The two differ on every invoice.</li>
</ul>

<h2>When to get specialist advice</h2>
<p>The reason VAT on property attracts so much professional attention is precisely that the arithmetic is trivial and the classification is not. The number a calculator gives you is only correct if the rate, the option-to-tax position and the recovery analysis behind it are correct. On a single refurbished commercial unit those decisions can swing the result by thousands of pounds of recoverable or irrecoverable VAT.</p>
