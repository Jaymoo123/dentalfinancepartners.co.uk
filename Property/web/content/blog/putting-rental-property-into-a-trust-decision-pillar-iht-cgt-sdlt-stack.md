faqs:
  - question: "Why are there four trust-route vehicles rather than just one or two?"
    answer: "Because the underlying tax rules treat each vehicle differently on each of the three taxes (IHT, CGT, SDLT), and family circumstances vary widely enough that no single vehicle dominates all use cases. An IPDI created by will avoids the entry-side CGT problem (death-uplift under TCGA 1992 s.62(1) resets the base cost) and pays no entry IHT (the s.18 spouse exemption may apply if the IPDI is for the spouse; otherwise the property forms part of the death estate normally). A lifetime discretionary trust pays 20% entry IHT above the NRB but unlocks TCGA 1992 s.260 holdover where the trust is non-settlor-interested. A bare trust is fully transparent under TCGA 1992 s.60, with the property treated as if held directly by the beneficiary for CGT and income tax purposes. A Family Investment Company sits outside the trust regime entirely. Each vehicle has its own optimal use case; pillar pages that frame the choice as binary trust-vs-FIC miss two of the four routes and lead readers to suboptimal decisions."
  - question: "What is an IPDI and when does it win?"
    answer: "An immediate-post-death interest (IPDI) is an interest in possession trust created by a will (or by a deed of variation within two years of death, with the s.142 election) that takes effect immediately on the deceased's death. Under IHTA 1984 s.49A and s.49(1A), an IPDI is one of the three carve-outs from the post-22-March-2006 relevant property regime: the IIP holder is treated under s.49(1) as beneficially entitled to the underlying property, with the property sitting in the IIP holder's death estate for IHT but outside the trust's periodic and exit charge regime. The classic landlord use case: a will leaves a buy-to-let property to a discretionary trust on terms that the surviving spouse takes an IPDI for life, with the property passing to the children on the spouse's death. On first death, the s.18 spouse exemption applies (the IPDI is for the spouse, treated as the spouse's beneficially-held property for IHT). On second death, the property is in the spouse's death estate at MV at death (subject to the spouse's own NRB, RNRB, and any planning options). Crucially: no entry-side CGT charge (the s.62(1) death-uplift on first death resets the base cost; the IPDI takes the property at the resettled base cost). No SDLT (transfers on death are not SDLT events). The IPDI route is unbeatable on the entry-side tax stack for property already held at first death, but cannot solve the lifetime-planning problem."
  - question: "What is the lifetime discretionary trust route and when does it win?"
    answer: "A lifetime settlement of property into a discretionary trust is a chargeable lifetime transfer (CLT) under IHTA 1984, paying 20% entry IHT on the value above the settlor's available NRB. The trust then runs under the relevant property regime (s.64 10-year periodic charge up to 6%, s.65 exit charges on capital distributions). The strategic value is on the CGT side: TCGA 1992 s.260 allows the settlor and trustees to jointly elect to hold over the gain on the transfer in, deferring it into the trust's base cost. The s.260 election is conditional on the trust being non-settlor-interested (TCGA 1992 ss.169B to 169G; the settlor and the settlor's spouse must be expressly excluded from the beneficiary class). The route wins where: (a) the property has a substantial latent CGT gain (the £25,000-plus that would otherwise crystallise at 18% to 24% residential CGT under s.17 on the transfer in is deferred), (b) the family wants trustee-governance control with multi-generational distribution flexibility, (c) the family is comfortable with the £15,000-ish entry IHT cost on a typical £400,000 BTL with full unused NRB, and (d) the drafting can be done cleanly (express exclusion of settlor and spouse; covered in depth on our Wave 6 B4 page on the three-statute attribution stack). It loses where the trust is settlor-interested (CGT crystallises immediately, removing the strategic value)."
  - question: "What is a bare trust and when does it win?"
    answer: "A bare trust (also called a nominee arrangement) is one where the beneficiary has the exclusive right to direct the trustee how to deal with the property, subject only to satisfying any outstanding charge or lien. TCGA 1992 s.60 treats the bare trustee's acts in relation to the property as the acts of the beneficiary; transactions between the trustee and beneficiary are disregarded; the beneficiary is the direct owner for CGT purposes. For income tax, the same transparency applies: rental income from a bare-trust property is taxed on the beneficiary at their marginal rate. For IHT, the transfer into bare trust is a PET (potentially exempt transfer); the property is NOT in the relevant property regime (no 10-year periodic charges, no exit charges); the beneficiary is treated as the absolute owner from the gift date. Bare trusts win where: (a) the beneficiary is a minor and the family wants to start the IHT 7-year clock running immediately while retaining trustee control over actual day-to-day decisions until the child reaches 18, (b) the family wants the simplicity of full transparency (no trust tax returns at trust rates, no separate trust entity for IHT purposes), and (c) the s.629 ITTOIA 2005 income-tax attribution on minor-child distributions is acceptable (the income-tax outcome on a parent-to-minor-child bare-trust gift is the same as direct retention for income-tax purposes, but the IHT clock is running). Covered in depth on our forthcoming Wave 6 B6 page on bare trust vs nominee company vs formal trust."
  - question: "What is the FIC route and when does it win?"
    answer: "A Family Investment Company (FIC) is a corporate vehicle (typically a limited company) holding the family's investment assets, with the founder retaining director control while gifting growth shares to the next generation. The FIC is not a trust; the formation is not a CLT for IHT. The founder gifts shares (not property) to the children, with the share gift being a PET that runs the standard 7-year clock. TCGA 1992 s.17 (connected-person market-value disposal) bites on the share gift, with no s.260 holdover available (no CLT trigger), but minority discounts on the share valuation can typically reduce the CGT bill to 20% to 35% of the gross asset value. Income from the underlying property is taxed inside the FIC at the corporation tax main rate (25% for FY2025/26 onwards on profits above £250,000, marginal relief between £50,000 and £250,000, 19% small-profits rate up to £50,000). The FIC wins where: (a) the family wants corporate-governance separation between control and beneficial ownership, (b) the founder wants director-level remuneration (salary or dividend) post-gift, (c) the latent CGT gain on the underlying property is modest enough that the entry-side CGT is acceptable, and (d) the family wants to avoid the 10-year periodic charge regime entirely. Covered in depth on our existing pillar at FIC vs Discretionary Trust for Property."
  - question: "What is the SDLT-assumed-debt trap and which vehicles does it hit?"
    answer: "FA 2003 Schedule 4 paragraph 8(1) is the key rule: 'the amount of debt satisfied, released or assumed shall be taken to be the whole or, as the case may be, part of the chargeable consideration for the transaction.' Where the trustees (or the FIC) assume the outstanding mortgage on the gifted property as part of the transfer, the assumed debt counts as chargeable consideration for SDLT, regardless of whether any cash changes hands. Paragraph 8(2) caps the consideration at the property's market value where the assumed debt would otherwise exceed it. For a £400,000 BTL with £180,000 outstanding mortgage assumed by the trustees, the chargeable consideration is £180,000, SDLT approximately £8,400 on the additional-dwelling 5% surcharge basis (post-31-October-2024 rates). The trap hits all three of the property-transfer routes (discretionary trust, bare trust, FIC); only the IPDI route avoids it (the IPDI takes effect on death, which is not an SDLT-chargeable event). The standard fix: refinance the mortgage out of the settlor's personal name before the transfer, so the trustees take a clean title with no debt assumption. The refinance itself may trigger fees and the new mortgage may be at different terms, but the SDLT saving is typically substantial on a leveraged BTL."
  - question: "What does the three-tax-stack look like across the four vehicles?"
    answer: "Quick comparison on a £400,000 BTL with £180,000 outstanding mortgage and a £180,000 latent gain (acquired £200,000, current value £400,000, base cost after improvements £220,000). IPDI (created by will on first death, no lifetime transfer): IHT nil at gift point (transfer is on death; first-death spouse exemption applies if IPDI is for spouse). CGT nil (s.62(1) death-uplift resets base cost to £400,000). SDLT nil (transfers on death are not SDLT events). Discretionary trust (clean, non-settlor-interested, lifetime CLT): IHT £15,000 (20% of £75,000 chargeable above £325,000 NRB). CGT nil (s.260 election holds over the £180,000 gain into trust base cost). SDLT £8,400 (5% on £180,000 assumed mortgage, post-31-October-2024 rate; or nil if mortgage refinanced out before transfer). Bare trust (lifetime PET): IHT nil at gift (PET starts 7-year clock). CGT £42,480 (£180,000 gain at 24% residential, less £3,000 AEA, no holdover because no CLT). SDLT £8,400 (same as discretionary if mortgage assumed). FIC (lifetime share gift after property transferred in at MV): IHT nil at gift (PET starts 7-year clock). CGT varies (entry-side disposal at MV on property transfer into company plus subsequent share-gift CGT; minority-discounted to typically £20,000 to £35,000 net). SDLT £20,000 on transfer of property INTO company (5% additional dwelling on £400,000 full MV value); plus potentially £8,400 on assumed debt if mortgage moves to company; share gift to children has no SDLT consequence. The four vehicles produce materially different Year 0 cost stacks and call for different family circumstances."
  - question: "Which vehicle is best for a mid-life landlord with a single BTL and full NRB?"
    answer: "Typically the discretionary trust route with a clean (non-settlor-interested) deed, if the family is willing to accept the trustee-governance constraint and the 10-year periodic charge profile. The £15,000 entry IHT cost is offset by the £42,000-plus of CGT deferral via s.260 (assuming a moderate latent gain), with the property removed from the estate immediately rather than waiting on a 7-year PET clock. If the family prefers transparency and is willing to absorb the entry CGT, the bare trust route is cleaner and starts the 7-year PET clock on the same date. If corporate governance and director-level remuneration matter, the FIC route is structurally better. The IPDI-by-will route doesn't fit a mid-life live planning scenario; it's a death-side route only. For most mid-life single-BTL scenarios with substantial latent gains, the discretionary trust (clean drafting) is the dominant first choice; for landlords with small latent gains and a strong preference for keeping things simple, bare trust often wins."
  - question: "Which vehicle is best for an elderly landlord with multiple properties and substantial value?"
    answer: "For an elderly landlord in failing health, the 7-year PET clock on bare-trust and FIC routes may not deliver value (the donor may not survive the seven years). The discretionary trust route delivers immediate removal from the estate (no 7-year clock on the property itself, just the £15,000 entry IHT paid upfront), making it the strongest lifetime option. The IPDI-by-will route is the right complement: a will creates an IPDI for the surviving spouse on first death, the s.18 spouse exemption applies on first death, and the s.142 deed-of-variation flexibility within two years of first death allows the family to retroactively redirect part of the estate into the discretionary trust at the deceased's death-uplifted base cost (zero CGT, NRB available for the variation-trust). For substantial portfolios, a combined IPDI-by-will-plus-deed-of-variation route is often the cheapest total-cost structure across the family's two-death window. The discretionary lifetime trust handles the value above what the deed-of-variation can absorb."
  - question: "Which vehicle is best for a family wanting to gift a property to a minor child?"
    answer: "The bare trust route, in nearly all cases. The bare trust mechanism preserves the IHT-clock benefit (PET starts immediately from gift date; the property is outside the relevant property regime; the beneficiary is treated as the absolute owner from gift date) while keeping the trustees in legal control of the property until the child reaches 18 (or whatever age the deed specifies for transfer of legal title). The income-tax outcome via ITTOIA 2005 s.629 attribution (rents on the child's share of the property are taxed on the parent-settlor at the parent's marginal rate, subject to the £100 per-settlement de-minimis) is unavoidable on any parent-to-minor-child route; the bare trust is no worse on this front than direct transfer-of-title. The discretionary trust route loses heavily on parent-to-minor-child gifts because it adds the 20% entry IHT cost (the £325,000 NRB is shared across all the parent's CLTs in the seven-year cumulation window; multiple-property CLTs can quickly exhaust the NRB), the 10-year periodic charge, and the trust-rate income-tax rate. The grandparent-route variation (grandparent as settlor; s.629 attributes to grandparent rather than parent, often at a more favourable marginal rate) is a separate structural option covered on our Wave 6 B9 page on gifting property to minor children."
  - question: "How does the four-vehicle decision interact with the post-FA-2025 long-term-resident regime?"
    answer: "For UK-resident settlors with UK property, the post-FA-2025 changes do not affect the decision substantially. The long-term-resident test under the new IHTA 1984 s.48ZA (inserted by FA 2025 s.45 with effect from 6 April 2025) governs the excluded-property status of property situated OUTSIDE the UK held in offshore trusts, replacing the pre-FA-2025 domicile-at-settlement test. For UK-resident landlords settling UK property, the property is UK-situated and is not excluded property regardless of the settlor's residence status. The FA-2025 changes are most relevant where the family includes non-UK-resident or formerly-non-UK-domiciled members who hold (or might inherit) offshore assets; those situations need specialist advice and are beyond the scope of this pillar. For the UK-resident-settlor / UK-property baseline this pillar addresses, the four-vehicle framework operates without modification."
  - question: "What is the role of the s.260 holdover election in this decision?"
    answer: "Pivotal. The s.260 election is what makes the lifetime discretionary trust route competitive on the CGT entry side. Without s.260, the CGT crystallises at market value under TCGA 1992 s.17 (connected-person disposal) on the transfer in, often generating £25,000 to £75,000 of immediate CGT on a typical BTL with substantial latent gain. With s.260, the gain is deferred into the trust's base cost; the settlor pays no immediate CGT; the trustees pick up the gain on their eventual disposal. The s.260 election is conditional on three things: (a) the transfer must be a CLT (PETs to individuals do not trigger s.260; share gifts to FICs do not trigger s.260), (b) the trust must be non-settlor-interested (TCGA 1992 ss.169B to 169G; settlor and spouse must be expressly excluded from the beneficiary class), and (c) the settlor and trustees must jointly elect on the settlor's CGT return for the year of the transfer. Where the s.260 election is available, the discretionary trust route is structurally cheap; where it is not (settlor-interested deed, or because the family prefers bare trust / FIC for other reasons), the entry-side CGT bites at the full s.17 rate."

  - question: "What are the most common drafting mistakes across the four vehicles?"
    answer: "Five mistakes account for most of the value lost in the cluster. (1) Settlor-interested drafting on a discretionary trust: including the settlor or settlor's spouse in the beneficiary class breaks the s.260 holdover, triggers s.624 income-tax attribution, and crystallises CGT immediately. The fix is express exclusion in the deed (see our Wave 6 B4 page). (2) Gift-with-reservation on top of any property gift: the donor continues to occupy or take rents from the gifted property, triggering FA 1986 s.102, with the property added back to the death estate at MV at death (see our Wave 6 B7 page on the double-trap). (3) Failing to refinance the mortgage out before the transfer: leaving the mortgage in place and having the trustees assume it triggers FA 2003 Sch 4 para 8 SDLT on the assumed debt. The fix is pre-transfer refinance. (4) Misclassifying the trust type: drafting what was intended as a bare trust but with discretionary-class language, accidentally creating a discretionary trust subject to entry IHT and the relevant property regime. The fix is precision in the deed wording. (5) Not coordinating with the will: a lifetime trust plus a will that creates an IPDI for the surviving spouse on the same property can produce conflicting IHT-add-back consequences. The fix is integrated estate-planning that designs the lifetime structure and the will together as a single plan."

<p>The decision facing a property-owning reader who is exploring trust-based estate planning is rarely "trust yes or no". It is "which of four vehicles, for which estate-planning goal, paying which entry tax on which axis". Most online content frames the choice as a binary trust-versus-FIC selection, losing two of the four routes that work better for specific landlord cohorts. This pillar maps the four real options across the three-tax stack that determines the cost of each.</p>

<p>The four vehicles are: an immediate-post-death interest (IPDI) created by will, which takes the TCGA 1992 s.62(1) death-uplift on the entry-side CGT and the IHTA 1984 s.49(1A) read-through on the IHT side; a lifetime discretionary settlement, which is a chargeable lifetime transfer (CLT) into the relevant property regime and unlocks TCGA 1992 s.260 holdover only where the trust is non-settlor-interested; a bare trust, fully transparent for CGT under TCGA 1992 s.60 with the gift treated as a PET starting the seven-year clock; and a Family Investment Company (FIC), corporate vehicle outside the trust regime entirely with the founder's share gift running its own seven-year PET clock. Each vehicle has its optimal cohort and its mismatched cohort; the framework below sets out where each wins and where each loses.</p>

<p>The three-tax stack runs across all four vehicles: IHT entry charge, CGT entry charge, and SDLT entry charge. The often-overlooked rule on the SDLT side is FA 2003 Schedule 4 paragraph 8, which treats assumed mortgage debt as chargeable consideration for SDLT regardless of whether any cash changes hands; this rule catches three of the four vehicles (the IPDI route is the only one that escapes, because death is not an SDLT event). Sessions across the Wave 6 B-cluster forward-link to this pillar for the four-vehicle framing.</p>

<h2>The four vehicles at a glance</h2>

<ul>
<li><strong>Vehicle 1: IPDI (Immediate Post-Death Interest) created by will.</strong> Created on the testator's death by the will-trust mechanism. IHTA 1984 s.49A and the s.49(1A) carve-out apply: the IIP holder (typically the surviving spouse) is treated under s.49(1) as beneficially entitled to the underlying property; the trust does NOT enter the relevant property regime. The s.18 spouse exemption applies on first death if the IPDI is for the spouse. CGT base cost resets under TCGA 1992 s.62(1) on the testator's death. No SDLT (death is not a chargeable event). Entry-side tax stack: nil/nil/nil for the typical spouse-IPDI scenario. Limitation: cannot solve any lifetime-planning problem; the testator must be the property owner at death.</li>
<li><strong>Vehicle 2: Lifetime discretionary trust (CLT into relevant property regime).</strong> 20% lifetime IHT on the value above the settlor's available NRB. TCGA 1992 s.260 holdover available where the trust is non-settlor-interested (ss.169B to 169G); CGT deferred into trust base cost. SDLT on any assumed mortgage debt under FA 2003 Sch 4 para 8. Trust runs under the s.64 10-year periodic charge regime and s.65 exit charges. Trustee-governance vehicle; full discretion to distribute or accumulate. Optimal for landlords with substantial latent CGT gains who want trustee control and are willing to pay the entry IHT.</li>
<li><strong>Vehicle 3: Bare trust (PET, full transparency).</strong> Transfer is a PET starting the seven-year clock. TCGA 1992 s.60 transparency: trustee's acts are treated as beneficiary's acts; the beneficiary is the direct owner for CGT and income tax. Property NOT in relevant property regime; no 10-year periodic charges, no exit charges. SDLT on assumed mortgage debt under FA 2003 Sch 4 para 8. CGT on entry is at market value under TCGA 1992 s.17 (no holdover, because no CLT). Optimal for minor-child gifts (PET clock running, trustees in legal control until age 18) and for small-portfolio scenarios where the simplicity is worth the entry CGT.</li>
<li><strong>Vehicle 4: Family Investment Company (FIC).</strong> Corporate vehicle (limited company) holding the family's investment assets. Property transferred into the FIC at market value under TCGA 1992 s.17 (CGT entry charge; no s.260 holdover because no CLT). Founder's shares then gifted to children as PETs running the seven-year clock; share gifts are not "gifts of property" under FA 1986 s.102 (Ingram v IRC [1999] UKHL 47), so no GROB on the share gifts even where the founder remains involved as director. Income inside the FIC taxed at corporation tax main rate (25% for FY 2025/26 on profits above £250,000, marginal relief £50,000 to £250,000, 19% small-profits rate up to £50,000). Optimal for families wanting corporate-governance separation between control (director appointment) and beneficial ownership (share holding).</li>
</ul>

<h2>The three-tax stack as the decision spine</h2>

<p>Every trust-route decision is the resultant of three independent tax decisions on the same property transfer: how much IHT bites on entry, how much CGT bites on entry, and how much SDLT bites on entry. Vehicles differ on each. The decision spine is mapping each vehicle's cost on each tax to identify which vehicle minimises total Year 0 cost for the specific family scenario.</p>

<h3>IHT axis</h3>

<p>For a £400,000 BTL transferred by a settlor with full unused NRB:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>IPDI by will:</strong> nil at the lifetime gift point (no lifetime transfer happens; the IPDI is created on death). On death, the property is treated as the IIP holder's beneficially-held property under s.49(1) read-through; the s.18 spouse exemption applies if the IPDI is for the spouse. For first-death-into-IPDI scenarios, the IHT cost is effectively deferred to second death (when the surviving spouse dies and the property forms part of their estate).</li>
<li><strong>Lifetime discretionary trust:</strong> 20% × (£400,000 minus £325,000) = £15,000 entry IHT, payable within six months of the end of the month of transfer. Trust then pays s.64 10-year periodic charges (up to 6% on the chargeable value above the available NRB at the periodic-charge date, formula-adjusted; typical figure £8,000 to £15,000 at year 10 on a £400,000 property growing to £530,000). Exit charges under s.65 on capital distributions to beneficiaries.</li>
<li><strong>Bare trust:</strong> nil entry IHT (PET; the property is outside the relevant property regime). The PET starts the seven-year clock; if the settlor survives seven years, the property falls fully out of the estate. If the settlor dies within seven years, the PET fails and 40% IHT applies (with taper relief on years 3 to 7, per IHTA 1984 s.7(4): 32% at year 3 to 4, 24% at year 4 to 5, 16% at year 5 to 6, 8% at year 6 to 7).</li>
<li><strong>FIC:</strong> nil entry IHT at FIC formation (the company formation is not a chargeable transfer). The founder's subsequent share gift to children is a PET running the seven-year clock; same taper schedule as bare trust on early death.</li>
</ul>

<h3>CGT axis</h3>

<p>For the same £400,000 BTL with a £180,000 latent gain (base cost £220,000 after improvements):</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>IPDI by will:</strong> nil entry CGT. TCGA 1992 s.62(1) resets the base cost to MV at death (£400,000), eliminating the £180,000 latent gain entirely. This is the IPDI route's structural superpower on the CGT side; no other route delivers this on entry.</li>
<li><strong>Lifetime discretionary trust:</strong> nil entry CGT IF the s.260 election is made and the trust is non-settlor-interested. The £180,000 gain is held over into the trust's base cost (trust takes the property at £220,000); settlor pays no immediate CGT. If the trust is settlor-interested, s.260 is blocked under s.169B(1), and the £180,000 gain crystallises at the 24% residential rate (less £3,000 AEA): £42,480 dry CGT charge, payable within 60 days via the residential property return.</li>
<li><strong>Bare trust:</strong> £42,480 entry CGT (same calculation as the settlor-interested-trust scenario; no holdover available because no CLT trigger). Dry charge, payable within 60 days.</li>
<li><strong>FIC:</strong> entry CGT depends on the property-into-company step. Transfer of the £400,000 BTL into the FIC at MV under s.17 generates the £42,480 charge in the founder's hands on the property transfer. The subsequent share gift is at the share value (often minority-discounted to £20,000 to £35,000 net of any CGT on the share-gift step, depending on the share class architecture). Combined CGT on the two-step entry can be £25,000 to £50,000.</li>
</ul>

<h3>SDLT axis (the assumed-debt trap)</h3>

<p>FA 2003 Schedule 4 paragraph 8(1) is the load-bearing rule: "the amount of debt satisfied, released or assumed shall be taken to be the whole or, as the case may be, part of the chargeable consideration for the transaction." Where the trustees (or the FIC, in the case of the property-into-company step) assume the outstanding mortgage on the gifted property as part of the transfer, the assumed debt counts as chargeable consideration for SDLT, regardless of whether any cash changes hands. Paragraph 8(2) caps the consideration at market value if the assumed debt would otherwise exceed it.</p>

<p>For a £400,000 BTL with a £180,000 outstanding mortgage assumed by the trustees:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>IPDI by will:</strong> nil SDLT. Transfers on death are not SDLT-chargeable events under FA 2003 s.42(2)(a). The mortgage is typically discharged from the estate or assumed by the beneficiary on death; neither is an SDLT trigger.</li>
<li><strong>Lifetime discretionary trust:</strong> SDLT on the £180,000 assumed debt. Post-31-October-2024 additional-dwelling-surcharge rate at 5% applies (because the trustees are typically treated as a non-natural person purchaser of a residential dwelling): £180,000 × 5% = £9,000 approximate (band-adjusted; £125k at 5%, £55k at 7%, totalling roughly £9,000). If the mortgage is refinanced out of the settlor's name before the transfer, the SDLT bill is nil; this is the standard pre-transfer fix.</li>
<li><strong>Bare trust:</strong> same as discretionary trust; £9,000 approximate if mortgage assumed; nil if refinanced out first.</li>
<li><strong>FIC:</strong> SDLT on the entire £400,000 MV transfer into the company (5% additional dwelling rate; band-adjusted: £125k at 5%, £125k at 7%, £150k at 10%, totalling approximately £37,500), plus the £9,000 on assumed debt if mortgage moves. The combined SDLT cost on a property-into-FIC step is often the deciding factor against the FIC route for leveraged BTL portfolios; partnership-incorporation routes under FA 2003 Sch 15 para 10 can reduce this where the portfolio is genuinely held in a pre-existing letting partnership (but the conditions are strict; covered on our partnership-incorporation cluster).</li>
</ul>

<aside>
<p>The SDLT-assumed-debt rule under FA 2003 Sch 4 para 8 is the single most-overlooked cost in property-trust planning.</p>
<p>Family-friend solicitor drafting routinely fails to surface the £9,000-plus SDLT cost on a typical leveraged BTL transferred without pre-refinance. The fix is mechanically simple (refinance the mortgage out of the settlor's personal name before the transfer; the trustees then take a clean unmortgaged title) but requires lead time, mortgage arrangement fees, and willingness to refinance. If you are evaluating any of the three lifetime routes (discretionary, bare, FIC) for a mortgaged property, the SDLT analysis must be done at the same time as the IHT and CGT analyses, not as an afterthought. Get in touch via the form at the foot of the page for an integrated three-tax review.</p>
</aside>

<h2>Vehicle 1: IPDI created by will, the death-side route</h2>

<p>The IPDI route uses the will as the trust-creation instrument. The testator's will leaves the property to a trust under terms that create an immediate-post-death interest (typically for the surviving spouse), with the underlying property passing to the children on the IPDI holder's later death. IHTA 1984 s.49A defines the IPDI as an interest in possession arising on the death of the settlor (testator). s.49(1A) carves the IPDI out of the post-22-March-2006 relevant property regime, applying the s.49(1) read-through that treats the IIP holder as beneficially entitled to the underlying property for IHT.</p>

<p>The structural advantages are three. First, no entry-side CGT (s.62(1) death-uplift resets the base cost). Second, no SDLT (transfers on death are not SDLT events). Third, s.18 spouse exemption on first death if the IPDI is for the spouse. The IPDI route delivers the cheapest entry-side tax stack of the four vehicles for property-already-in-the-estate cohorts.</p>

<p>The structural disadvantages are also three. First, it cannot solve any lifetime-planning problem; the testator must be the property owner at death (the route is a death-side route only). Second, the property is in the surviving spouse's death estate on second death (the s.49(1) read-through brings it back into estate accounting), so the estate-removal goal is deferred to second death rather than achieved on first. Third, the s.142 deed-of-variation flexibility (within two years of first death) is the standard tool for restructuring the IPDI into a discretionary trust after first death, but the variation has its own complications and election requirements (s.142 IHT election, s.62(6) CGT election).</p>

<p>For families with substantial property holdings and a planning horizon stretching into and across both spouses' lifetimes, an integrated IPDI-by-will-plus-deed-of-variation-on-first-death plan is often the cheapest total-cost structure; covered in depth on our Wave 4 C5 page on deeds of variation on landlord estates.</p>

<h2>Vehicle 2: Lifetime discretionary trust, the strategic CGT-deferral route</h2>

<p>The lifetime discretionary trust route is a CLT into the relevant property regime under IHTA 1984. The route's strategic value is the s.260 CGT holdover, which is conditional on the trust being non-settlor-interested per TCGA 1992 ss.169B to 169G. Where the trust is clean (settlor and spouse expressly excluded from the beneficiary class; covered in depth on our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/settlor-interested-trust-iht-s49-1a-cgt-s169b-property-attribution-rules">Wave 6 B4 page on the three-statute attribution stack</a>), the route delivers £42,480 of CGT deferral on a typical £400,000 BTL with £180,000 latent gain in exchange for the £15,000 entry IHT cost; net Year 0 cost saving of £27,480 versus the bare-trust route or the FIC route.</p>

<p>The route's structural ongoing cost is the 10-year periodic charge under IHTA 1984 s.64 (up to 6% on the trust's chargeable value at each anniversary, formula-adjusted; typical figure £8,000 to £15,000 at year 10) plus exit charges on capital distributions under s.65. For a 30-year planning horizon, the total ongoing-charge cost is in the order of £40,000 to £70,000; against the £42,480 entry-side CGT saved plus the IHT removal value on the underlying property, the route typically still wins for substantial portfolios.</p>

<p>For the deep treatment of the discretionary trust mechanic with the Patel £400,000 worked example showing IHT, CGT, SDLT, and projected year-10 cost, see <a href="/blog/landlord-tax-essentials/iht-clt-property-discretionary-trust-20-percent-entry-charge">our existing Wave 4 C10 pillar on the CLT discretionary trust mechanic</a>. For the settlor-interested trap that breaks the s.260 holdover, see <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/settlor-interested-trust-iht-s49-1a-cgt-s169b-property-attribution-rules">our Wave 6 B4 page on the three-statute attribution stack</a>. For the GROB-overlay that produces a double-trap, see our Wave 6 B7 page on the settlor-interest plus GROB interaction.</p>

<h2>Vehicle 3: Bare trust, the transparent PET route</h2>

<p>The bare trust mechanism is fundamentally different from the discretionary and IIP routes. TCGA 1992 s.60(1) treats the trustee's acts in relation to the property as the acts of the beneficiary; transactions between the trustee and beneficiary are disregarded. The beneficiary is the direct owner for CGT purposes from the trust's creation. For income tax, the same transparency applies: rental income is taxed on the beneficiary at their marginal rate.</p>

<p>For IHT, the transfer into bare trust is a PET. The property is NOT in the relevant property regime; no 10-year periodic charges, no exit charges; the beneficiary is treated as the absolute owner from the gift date. The PET starts the seven-year clock from gift; if the settlor survives seven years, the property falls fully out of the estate at the gift-date value (not the death-date value); if the settlor dies within seven years, the PET fails and 40% IHT applies with taper relief on years 3 to 7.</p>

<p>The route's structural strengths are simplicity (no separate trust entity for tax purposes), transparency (single tax return on the beneficiary's marginal rate), and PET-clock running from gift date. The structural weaknesses are the entry-side CGT (no holdover; £42,480 on the £180,000-latent-gain example), the dependence on the settlor's survival for the PET clock, and the loss of trustee discretion over future distributions (the beneficiary can demand the property at any time once of age).</p>

<p>For minor-child gifts the bare trust route is structurally dominant: the trustees retain legal control until the child reaches 18 (or whatever age the trust deed specifies for transfer of legal title), while the IHT PET clock runs from the gift date and the IHT-side benefits accrue. Covered in depth on our forthcoming Wave 6 B6 page on bare trust vs nominee company vs formal trust, and our Wave 6 B9 page on gifting property to minor children.</p>

<h2>Vehicle 4: Family Investment Company, the corporate alternative</h2>

<p>The FIC route uses a limited company as the holding vehicle. Property is transferred into the FIC at market value under TCGA 1992 s.17 (CGT entry charge bites; no s.260 holdover available because no CLT trigger), the founder takes management shares with full director-control rights, and growth shares are gifted to the next generation as PETs. The share gifts are not "gifts of property" for FA 1986 s.102 GROB purposes (Ingram v IRC [1999] UKHL 47), so the founder can continue to direct the company through their director role without triggering GROB on the share gift.</p>

<p>Income inside the FIC is taxed at the corporation tax main rate (25% for FY 2025/26 on profits above £250,000, marginal relief £50,000 to £250,000, 19% small-profits rate up to £50,000). The founder may receive director's salary (subject to PAYE and the wholly-and-exclusively test on the FIC's CT deduction), dividends (subject to dividend tax), or rent (where the FIC leases the property back, with full market terms and the FIC paying CT on the rent receipts).</p>

<p>The route wins where corporate governance separation and director-level remuneration are valued; loses where the latent CGT gain is substantial (the entry-side CGT bites without holdover) or where the SDLT-on-property-into-company step (5% additional-dwelling rate on the full MV) is prohibitive on leveraged BTL portfolios. For the deep tax-side comparison against the lifetime discretionary trust on six axes (entry IHT, ongoing tax rate, CGT entry, setup cost, control, distribution flexibility), see our existing pillar at <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/fic-vs-discretionary-trust-property-comparison">FIC vs Discretionary Trust for Property</a>.</p>

<h2>The decision matrix: which vehicle wins for which cohort</h2>

<p>No single vehicle dominates all use cases. The matrix below summarises which vehicle is structurally the strongest first option for each common cohort:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Property already in the estate; substantial value; family includes spouse + children:</strong> IPDI by will, with deed-of-variation flexibility on first death. Cheapest entry-side tax stack (nil/nil/nil) and lets the family retro-engineer the right structure with full information.</li>
<li><strong>Mid-life landlord; single BTL or small portfolio; substantial latent CGT gain; spouse and adult children:</strong> Lifetime discretionary trust with clean (non-settlor-interested) drafting and pre-transfer mortgage refinance. £15,000 entry IHT in exchange for £42,000-plus of CGT deferral; trustee governance for multi-generational planning.</li>
<li><strong>Parent wanting to gift property to a minor child:</strong> Bare trust. PET clock runs from gift, trustees retain legal control until age 18, no relevant property regime exposure, no entry IHT. Income-tax attribution under s.629 applies but is unavoidable on any parent-to-minor-child route.</li>
<li><strong>Family wanting corporate-governance separation between control and beneficial ownership; mid-portfolio scale; founder wanting director remuneration:</strong> FIC. Avoids the trust regime entirely; growth-share architecture preserves founder control while gifting value to next generation.</li>
<li><strong>Elderly landlord in failing health; large portfolio:</strong> Lifetime discretionary trust (immediate removal from estate; no 7-year clock on the property itself, just the £15,000 entry IHT paid upfront) plus IPDI-by-will on remaining direct-ownership assets. Combined route handles both the lifetime-removal goal and the property-already-in-estate goal.</li>
<li><strong>Family with offshore-resident members or non-UK-domiciled history:</strong> Specialist advice required. The post-FA-2025 long-term-resident regime under IHTA 1984 s.48ZA (replacing the omitted s.48(3)-(3F)) governs excluded-property status of non-UK-situated assets in offshore trusts; the four-vehicle framework above is the UK-resident-settlor baseline and may need substantial adjustment for cross-border cases.</li>
</ul>

<p>The four-vehicle framework is the planning starting point.</p>
<p>The right vehicle for any specific family depends on the family's wealth level, the latent CGT position on the underlying property, the family's appetite for trustee or corporate governance, the donor's age and health, the survival horizon, and the family's broader estate-planning goals across the two-death window. The matrix above identifies the structural first-option for each common cohort, but the second-look adjustments (taper-relief modelling on early-death scenarios, NRB-cumulation analysis on multi-property settlements, RNRB-taper interaction on £2m-plus estates) often modify the conclusion. <h2>The five most common drafting mistakes across the cluster</h2>

<p>Five mistakes account for most of the value lost in real-world property-trust planning. They cluster on the entry-side tax stack and on the drafting precision required to avoid the various anti-avoidance regimes.</p>

<ol>
<li><strong>Settlor-interested drafting on a discretionary trust.</strong> Including the settlor or settlor's spouse in the beneficiary class breaks the s.260 holdover under TCGA 1992 s.169B, triggers s.624 income-tax attribution, and (if the deed creates an IIP for the settlor or spouse) brings the s.49(1A) read-through into play. The fix is express exclusion in the deed. See our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/settlor-interested-trust-iht-s49-1a-cgt-s169b-property-attribution-rules">Wave 6 B4 page on the three-statute attribution stack</a> for the depth treatment.</li>
<li><strong>Gift-with-reservation on top of any property gift.</strong> The donor continues to occupy or take rents from the gifted property, triggering FA 1986 s.102, with the property added back to the death estate at MV at death. HMRC's IHTM42254 published view: mere membership in a discretionary beneficiary class is reservation of benefit. See our Wave 6 B7 page on the settlor-interest plus GROB double-trap.</li>
<li><strong>Failing to refinance the mortgage out before the transfer.</strong> Leaving the mortgage in place and having the trustees assume it triggers FA 2003 Sch 4 para 8 SDLT on the assumed debt; £9,000-plus on a typical £180,000-mortgaged BTL. The fix is pre-transfer refinance into the settlor's personal name; transfer of clean unmortgaged title to trustees; no SDLT bill.</li>
<li><strong>Misclassifying the trust type.</strong> Drafting what was intended as a bare trust but with discretionary-class language, accidentally creating a discretionary trust subject to entry IHT and the relevant property regime. The fix is precision in the deed wording and using model deeds from a trust-tax specialist rather than family-friend solicitor templates.</li>
<li><strong>Not coordinating lifetime structure with the will.</strong> A lifetime discretionary trust plus a will that creates an IPDI for the surviving spouse on the same property can produce conflicting IHT-add-back consequences (the s.49(1A) read-through on the IPDI may interact unhelpfully with any reservation-of-benefit analysis on the lifetime trust). The fix is integrated estate-planning where the lifetime structure and the will are designed together as a single plan.</li>
</ol>
