<p>The UK government has been exploring significant changes to <strong>CGT property 2027 rate changes</strong> that could fundamentally alter the tax landscape for property investors. While these changes remain under consultation, understanding the potential impact is crucial for landlords planning their investment strategies.</p>
<p>Current speculation suggests that <strong>capital gains tax 2027</strong> reforms could align CGT rates more closely with income tax rates, potentially increasing the tax burden on property disposals significantly. This article examines what we know so far and what landlords should be considering.</p>

<h2>Current CGT Rates and Proposed Changes from 2027</h2>
<p>For the 2025/26 tax year, CGT on residential property disposals operates at 18% for basic rate taxpayers and 24% for higher and additional rate taxpayers, with an annual exempt amount of £3,000. Commercial property gains are subject to standard CGT rates of 10% (basic rate) or 20% (higher rate).</p>
<p>Government consultation documents have suggested several potential approaches to <strong>CGT rate increase property</strong> taxation from the 2027/28 tax year:</p>
<h3>Alignment with Income Tax Rates</h3>
<p>The most significant proposal involves aligning CGT rates with income tax rates. Under this scenario, basic rate taxpayers could see CGT increase from 18% to 20%, while higher and additional rate taxpayers could see rates rise from 24% to 40% or 45% respectively.</p>
<h3>Removal of the Annual Exempt Amount</h3>
<p>Another proposal is the complete removal or further reduction of the annual exempt amount, which has already fallen from £12,300 in 2022/23 to £3,000 in 2025/26.</p>
<h3>Changes to Taper Relief</h3>
<p>There's speculation about reintroducing taper relief or similar mechanisms that would reduce CGT rates based on the length of ownership, though this remains uncertain.</p>
<p>The government has indicated it will provide at least 12 months' notice of any confirmed changes, meaning final decisions would need to be announced by April 2026 at the latest.</p>

<h2>Impact on Different Property Investors and Structures</h2>
<h3>Individual Landlords</h3>
<p>Individual landlords holding property in their personal names would be most directly affected. A landlord disposing of a property with a £100,000 gain could see their tax liability increase from £24,000 to £40,000 (assuming higher rate taxpayer status). This makes the timing of disposals crucial.</p>
<h3>Limited Company Structures</h3>
<p>Properties held within limited companies are subject to corporation tax on gains, not CGT. With corporation tax rates at 19% (small profits rate) and 25% (main rate), company structures might become more attractive if personal CGT rates increase significantly. However, extracting gains from companies creates additional tax layers. Our guide to <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/buy-to-let-limited-company-complete-guide-uk">buy-to-let limited companies</a> explores these structures in detail.</p>
<h3>Commercial vs Residential Property</h3>
<p>The proposed changes appear focused on residential property CGT rates. Commercial property gains might remain subject to standard CGT rates (currently 10%/20%), potentially making commercial property investment relatively more attractive from a tax perspective.</p>

<h2>Strategic Planning and Timing Considerations</h2>
<h3>Timing of Disposals</h3>
<p>The most immediate consideration is whether to accelerate property disposals before the 2027 changes. This might be appropriate if you were already planning to sell, have substantial gains, or are a higher rate taxpayer. However, premature disposals purely for tax reasons can be counterproductive.</p>
<h3>Incorporation Considerations</h3>
<p>If CGT rates increase substantially, <a href="/incorporation">incorporation</a> might become more attractive for some landlords. This needs to be weighed against ongoing compliance costs, loss of mortgage interest relief benefits, and potential incorporation charges if transferring existing properties.</p>
<h3>Gift and Inheritance Planning</h3>
<p>Higher CGT rates could make gift planning more complex. While gifts between spouses remain tax-neutral, gifts to adult children or trusts could trigger larger CGT liabilities. Some landlords might consider gifting properties before 2027 to take advantage of current rates.</p>

<h2>Interaction with Other Tax Changes</h2>
<p>The <strong>capital gains tax 2027</strong> changes don't exist in isolation. Landlords need to consider them alongside other reforms.</p>
<h3>Separate Property Income Tax Rates (April 2027)</h3>
<p>From April 2027, property income will be subject to separate tax rates (22%/42%/47% instead of standard income tax rates). Combined with higher CGT rates, the overall tax burden on property investment could increase significantly.</p>
<h3>Section 24 Restrictions</h3>
<p>The <a href="/blog/section-24-and-tax-relief/section-24-tax-relief-complete-guide">Section 24 mortgage interest restrictions</a> continue to limit tax relief on mortgage interest to the basic rate. Higher CGT rates could compound the impact of these restrictions.</p>
<h3>Making Tax Digital Requirements</h3>
<p><a href="/blog/making-tax-digital-mtd/making-tax-digital-landlords-april-2026-deadline">Making Tax Digital for Income Tax</a> becomes mandatory from April 2026 for landlords with gross rental income over £10,000. This adds compliance complexity just as the tax landscape becomes more challenging.</p>

<h2>Action Plan for Landlords</h2>
<h3>Review Your Portfolio</h3>
<p>Conduct a comprehensive review of your property portfolio, including estimated gains on each property, your likely tax position in 2027, properties you might consider disposing of, and the impact of different CGT rate scenarios.</p>
<h3>Consider Professional Advice</h3>
<p>The complexity of these potential changes makes professional advice increasingly valuable. A specialist <a href="/blog/property-accountant-services/what-does-a-property-accountant-do">property accountant</a> can help model different scenarios and develop appropriate strategies.</p>
<h3>Stay Informed and Keep Options Open</h3>
<p>Tax policy remains fluid. Subscribe to updates from HMRC and professional bodies. Understand the range of possible outcomes, identify which would most affect you, and develop flexible strategies that can adapt. Avoid making irreversible decisions based on speculation.</p>

<h2>Wider Market and International Implications</h2>
<p>Significant increases in property CGT rates could have broader market implications, including reduced liquidity as higher exit taxes encourage longer holding periods, potential price impacts, shifts in investment flows towards other asset classes, and greater use of corporate structures.</p>
<p>For non-resident landlords, the interaction between UK CGT changes and their home country tax systems could become more complex. Higher UK CGT rates might affect the value of double taxation treaty relief and influence decisions about UK property investment, impacting the UK's competitiveness as an international investment destination.</p>

<p>The <strong>CGT property 2027 rate changes</strong> represent potentially the most significant shift in property taxation for a generation. While the full impact remains unclear, early preparation and professional guidance will be essential for navigating these changes successfully.</p>
