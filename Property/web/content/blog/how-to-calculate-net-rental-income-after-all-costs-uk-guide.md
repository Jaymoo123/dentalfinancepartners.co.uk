  - question: "What is the difference between gross rental income, taxable rental profit, and net cash flow?"
    answer: "Gross rental income is total rent received before any expenses. Taxable rental profit is gross income minus allowable expenses, with mortgage interest added back for personally-held BTL because Section 24 disallows it as a deduction (a 20% tax reducer applies separately). Net cash flow is what actually lands in your bank account: gross rent minus all paid costs (including the full mortgage interest you actually paid) minus the tax you owe. Many higher rate landlords have a positive taxable profit but a negative net cash flow because Section 24 charges tax on income that has already left the door as mortgage interest."
  - question: "Which rental expenses are deductible against rental income?"
    answer: "Letting and management agent fees, repairs and maintenance (not improvements), buildings and contents insurance, ground rent and service charges, council tax during void periods, utilities you pay (not the tenant's), gas safety certificates and EICR, EPC certificates, accountancy and bookkeeping fees, professional fees for evictions and legal advice on tenancies, advertising and listing fees, mileage to property visits at the HMRC approved rate, mobile and broadband apportioned to the rental business, and Replacement of Domestic Items Relief on furniture and white goods. See HMRC's Property Income Manual for the full list."
  - question: "How does Section 24 actually work in the calculation?"
    answer: "For personally-held BTL since the 2020-21 tax year, mortgage interest is added back to taxable profit (so you pay tax as if it had not been incurred). Separately, a 20% basic-rate tax reducer is applied to the lower of: total finance costs, rental profit, or your income above the personal allowance. So a 40% higher rate landlord with £10,000 of mortgage interest pays £4,000 of additional tax on the £10,000 (added back to profit), then receives a £2,000 tax reducer. Net Section 24 cost: £2,000 a year more than under pre-2017 rules."
  - question: "Are capital improvements deductible against rental income?"
    answer: "No. Capital improvements (extensions, structural changes, kitchen and bathroom replacements where you upgrade rather than like-for-like replace, new central heating systems where none existed before) are not deductible against rental profit. They add to the property's base cost for capital gains tax purposes, reducing CGT when you eventually sell. Routine repairs that restore the property to its pre-deterioration state (replacing a broken boiler, repointing brickwork, repainting) are deductible revenue expenses. The line is contested in HMRC enquiries, keep contemporary evidence and detailed contractor descriptions."
  - question: "Can losses from one property offset profit on another?"
    answer: "Yes, within the same property business. Your UK rental properties are treated as one combined property business. A loss on one property offsets profit on another in the same tax year. Unrelieved losses carry forward indefinitely against future UK property income. Overseas property is a separate property business and its losses do NOT offset UK property profits, they only carry forward against future overseas profits. FHL losses prior to 6 April 2025 also worked similarly but with some different reliefs that have now lapsed."
  - question: "What is Replacement of Domestic Items Relief?"
    answer: "RDIR allows the cost of replacing furniture, furnishings, appliances, and kitchenware in a furnished or part-furnished let, calculated as: cost of the replacement, plus the cost of disposal, minus any sale proceeds for the old item, minus any improvement element. So replacing a £400 broken washing machine with a similar £400 model gives £400 of relief. Upgrading to a £700 model gives only £400 of relief (the like-for-like cost). Initial fit-out of a newly-let furnished property does not qualify, only replacement. The relief applies from the 2016-17 tax year onwards, replacing the older Wear and Tear Allowance."
  - question: "How is mortgage interest calculated where the loan covers more than one property or also funds personal spending?"
    answer: "You can only deduct the interest on the portion of the loan that funded the rental property. If you remortgaged the BTL to release equity for personal spending (new car, deposit on a holiday home, anything not rental-business-related), the interest on the equity-release portion is not deductible. HMRC accepts that interest on a remortgage up to the original purchase price of the rental property remains fully deductible (or qualifies for the Section 24 tax reducer) regardless of what the new funds are used for. Excess borrowing above original purchase price needs to be traced to a rental-business use to qualify."
  - question: "How do MTD for ITSA quarterly updates affect my net rental income calculation?"
    answer: "MTD for ITSA does not change the annual calculation. It changes when and how often you report it. Each quarterly update is a summary of income and expense categories for that three-month period. The final declaration (replacing the old annual SA100/SA105) is where Section 24, brought-forward losses, capital allowances, and other annual adjustments are applied. So you still calculate net rental profit annually, but you report the building blocks quarterly. MTD threshold went live at £50,000 gross combined income on 6 April 2026, drops to £30,000 in April 2027, and £20,000 in April 2028."
  - question: "How does the calculation differ for limited company ownership?"
    answer: "Companies are not subject to Section 24. Mortgage interest is fully deductible against company profit. There is no add-back, no separate tax reducer. Corporation tax (19% to 25%) applies to the net profit figure. If profit is later extracted as a dividend, the shareholder pays a further 8.75%, 33.75%, or 39.35% personal tax. Net cash to the shareholder after both taxes is often similar to personal ownership after Section 24, the company route mainly helps if you retain profits inside the company for portfolio growth rather than extracting everything."
  - question: "How do I treat insurance proceeds for damage to a rental property?"
    answer: "Proceeds for loss of rental income are taxable as rental income in the period to which they relate. Proceeds for repair work are not separately taxable, but they reduce the deductible repair cost. So if a £4,000 boiler replacement was funded entirely by an insurance payout of £4,000, the net repair cost is zero and you cannot claim £4,000 of expense (you cannot have your cake and eat it). Where the insurance covers only part of the repair, you deduct the unfunded balance."
  - question: "What records should I keep for the net income calculation?"
    answer: "All rent receipts and bank statements showing rent paid in (six years minimum, longer if MTD or VAT applies). All expense invoices (with VAT details), contractor receipts for repairs (with descriptions of work done so capital-versus-revenue is documented), mortgage statements showing interest charged, insurance schedules, council tax bills, utility bills, agency statements, mileage logs for property visits, certificates (gas, EICR, EPC), and any HMRC correspondence. Cloud bookkeeping software (Hammock, Landlord Studio, FreeAgent) handles this cleanly and is mandatory under MTD anyway."
  - question: "Can I claim travel costs to visit the property?"
    answer: "Yes, where the trip's primary purpose is the rental business (showing the property to prospective tenants, meeting contractors, attending inspections). Either claim actual fuel and running costs apportioned to business mileage, or claim the simplified rate of 45p per mile for the first 10,000 business miles and 25p per mile thereafter (the same rates HMRC uses for employed mileage). Mixed-purpose trips (visiting the property and then going on holiday) need apportionment. Trips from your home to a single property where you also live are not deductible."
  - question: "How is rent treated when the tenant pays in advance or in arrears?"
    answer: "Most personally-held landlords use the cash basis by default (mandatory below £150,000 gross income, optional above), meaning rent is taxed in the year actually received. Rent paid 3 months in advance straddling the tax-year boundary is taxed when received, not when earned. The accruals basis (used by larger landlords and required for limited companies) recognises rent when earned regardless of when paid. Switching between the two requires careful transitional adjustments to avoid double-counting or omission."

<p>Net rental income is the figure that drives your tax bill and your cash flow. For a UK landlord in 2026, calculating it correctly means understanding three different numbers (gross income, taxable profit, net cash flow), the Section 24 finance cost mechanism that puts a gap between them, the capital-versus-revenue distinction that decides which costs reduce income today versus which sit on the CGT base cost for later, and the rules that decide which expenses qualify. This guide walks through the full calculation with multiple worked examples, then explains the most common errors that cost landlords money on enquiry or on annual tax assessment.</p>

<h2>Three different "net income" numbers, only one of them is taxable</h2>

<table>
<thead>
<tr><th>Figure</th><th>What it measures</th><th>Used for</th></tr>
</thead>
<tbody>
<tr><td><strong>Gross rental income</strong></td><td>Total rent received in the tax year</td><td>MTD threshold test, lender stress tests</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>Taxable rental profit</strong></td><td>Gross income minus allowable expenses (NOT minus mortgage interest, for personally-held BTL)</td><td>HMRC tax assessment, self assessment SA105 box 38</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>Net cash flow</strong></td><td>Gross income minus ALL paid costs (including mortgage interest) minus tax actually owed</td><td>Real-world bank balance impact</td></tr>
</tbody>
</table>

<p>The gap between taxable profit and net cash flow is the Section 24 effect. For a basic rate landlord with no mortgage the gap is zero. For a higher rate landlord with heavy mortgage interest the gap can be very large, sometimes making cash flow negative on a property that shows a taxable profit.</p>

<h2>Step 1: Calculate gross rental income</h2>

<p>Include in gross:</p>
<ul>
<li>Monthly rent payments received</li>
<li>Parking, storage, garage, or pet supplement charges</li>
<li>Utility re-charges (if tenant pays you and you pay the supplier)</li>
<li>Retained portions of damage deposits</li>
<li>Insurance payouts for loss of rental income</li>
<li>Service charges collected and retained (not where collected and passed straight through)</li>
</ul>

<p>Exclude:</p>
<ul>
<li>Deposits held in deposit protection schemes (only retained portions count)</li>
<li>Rent received for a period outside the tax year (cash basis users only)</li>
<li>Insurance payouts that directly fund repair work (these reduce the repair cost instead, see Step 5)</li>
</ul>

<h2>Step 2: Identify allowable expenses</h2>

<p>Deductible against rental income (revenue costs):</p>

<table>
<thead>
<tr><th>Category</th><th>Examples</th><th>Notes</th></tr>
</thead>
<tbody>
<tr><td>Letting and management</td><td>Agent fees, tenant find fees, property management percentage</td><td>Fully deductible</td></tr>
<tr><td>Repairs and maintenance</td><td>Boiler servicing, plumbing repairs, repointing, repainting</td><td>Only true repairs, not upgrades</td></tr>
<tr><td>Insurance</td><td>Buildings, contents, landlord liability, rent guarantee</td><td>Fully deductible</td></tr>
<tr><td>Council tax and utilities</td><td>Voids council tax, utility bills you pay (not tenants')</td><td>Apportion for periods of personal use</td></tr>
<tr><td>Professional fees</td><td>Accountancy, bookkeeping, tenancy legal advice, tax advice</td><td>Capital tax advice (e.g., incorporation modelling) is generally capital, not deductible</td></tr>
<tr><td>Safety certificates</td><td>Annual gas safety, 5-yearly EICR, EPC</td><td>Fully deductible</td></tr>
<tr><td>Advertising</td><td>Online listings, agent advertising fees</td><td>Fully deductible</td></tr>
<tr><td>Travel</td><td>Mileage at 45p/mile (first 10,000 miles)</td><td>Primary-purpose trips only</td></tr>
<tr><td>Communications</td><td>Apportioned mobile, broadband, postage</td><td>Reasonable apportionment to rental use</td></tr>
<tr><td>Replacement of Domestic Items</td><td>Like-for-like replacement of furniture and white goods</td><td>Initial fit-out NOT eligible</td></tr>
<tr><td>Mortgage broker fees</td><td>Fees paid to arrange BTL finance</td><td>Within Section 24 for individuals (20% tax reducer)</td></tr>
</tbody>
</table>

<p>Not deductible against rental income:</p>
<ul>
<li>Capital improvements (extensions, upgrades, complete kitchen replacements with upgrade element)</li>
<li>Mortgage interest, for personally-held BTL (treated separately under Section 24, see Step 4)</li>
<li>Personal use expenses</li>
<li>The cost of buying the property (this is capital, sits on CGT base cost)</li>
<li>Your own labour</li>
<li>Penalties and fines</li>
</ul>

<h2>Step 3: Apply the capital-versus-revenue test</h2>

<p>The single most contested area in HMRC enquiries. The principle is: a repair restores the property to its pre-deterioration state, so it is revenue. An improvement adds something new or better than what was there before, so it is capital.</p>

<p>Practical examples:</p>
<ul>
<li>Replacing a worn-out 25-year-old wooden window with a similar wooden window: <strong>repair</strong></li>
<li>Replacing the same window with a uPVC double-glazed window: HMRC originally treated as capital, but post-2014 case law accepts this as repair where uPVC is now standard equivalent</li>
<li>Adding a conservatory: <strong>capital</strong></li>
<li>Replacing a kitchen worktop with a similar worktop: <strong>repair</strong></li>
<li>Full kitchen replacement with significantly higher-spec units, appliances, and a new island: <strong>capital</strong></li>
<li>Replacing a broken boiler with a similar boiler: <strong>repair</strong></li>
<li>Installing central heating where none existed before: <strong>capital</strong></li>
<li>Loft insulation top-up: <strong>repair</strong></li>
<li>Loft conversion to add bedroom: <strong>capital</strong></li>
</ul>

<p>When the work mixes repair and improvement, apportion the cost. Document the apportionment with quotes and contractor descriptions.</p>

<h2>Step 4: Apply Section 24 for personally-held BTL</h2>

<p>Section 24 of Finance (No. 2) Act 2015 (now ITA 2007 s274A) restricts mortgage interest relief on individually-held residential property. From the 2020-21 tax year onwards, the full restriction applies: mortgage interest, broker fees, and arrangement fees are NOT deductible from rental profit. Instead, a 20% basic-rate tax reducer is applied to the lower of:</p>

<ul>
<li>The total finance costs</li>
<li>The rental profit (after other allowable expenses but before Section 24 add-back)</li>
<li>Your income above the personal allowance</li>
</ul>

<p>The lowest of those three is multiplied by 20% to calculate the tax reducer. So a landlord with £20,000 of mortgage interest who only has £10,000 of rental profit gets a reducer based on £10,000 (the cap), worth £2,000. The unused £10,000 of finance costs carries forward indefinitely against future rental profit.</p>

<h2>Step 5: Adjust for insurance proceeds and other recoveries</h2>

<p>If insurance pays for repair work, the repair cost you can deduct is the net amount you actually bore. £6,000 repair funded by £4,500 insurance equals £1,500 deductible repair cost.</p>

<p>Insurance proceeds for loss of rental income are themselves taxable as rental income.</p>

<h2>MTD for ITSA and quarterly reporting</h2>

<p>MTD for Income Tax Self Assessment went live on 6 April 2026 for sole-trader landlords with gross combined property and self-employment income above £50,000. Threshold drops to £30,000 from 6 April 2027 and £20,000 from 6 April 2028. Quarterly updates report cumulative income and expenses against HMRC standard categories. The final declaration (replacing the old annual SA100/SA105) is where Section 24 reducer, brought-forward losses, capital allowances, and other annual adjustments are applied. So the calculation logic above is unchanged, you just report the building blocks four times a year instead of once.</p>

<h2>Common calculation errors</h2>

<ul>
<li><strong>Treating mortgage interest as a normal expense.</strong> Personally-held BTL must add interest back to profit; the tax reducer is separate. Many DIY filers get this wrong.</li>
<li><strong>Claiming capital improvements against income.</strong> Boiler upgrade where central heating did not exist before is capital. Adding a conservatory is capital. These costs add to CGT base cost, not income deduction.</li>
<li><strong>Claiming the initial fit-out as RDIR.</strong> RDIR only applies to replacements, not first installation.</li>
<li><strong>Forgetting void council tax is deductible.</strong> Many landlords pay it without claiming.</li>
<li><strong>Including the full mortgage payment, not just interest.</strong> Capital repayment is not deductible (it builds equity). Only interest qualifies for the Section 24 tax reducer.</li>
<li><strong>Mixing property and personal expenses.</strong> Apportion mileage, mobile, broadband, home office time properly.</li>
<li><strong>Missing RDIR opportunities.</strong> Many landlords forget to claim replacement washing machines, fridges, sofas.</li>
<li><strong>Failing to track Section 24 carry-forward.</strong> Where finance costs exceed rental profit (often when newly-acquired heavily-mortgaged properties have void periods), the unused tax reducer carries forward indefinitely.</li>
</ul>
