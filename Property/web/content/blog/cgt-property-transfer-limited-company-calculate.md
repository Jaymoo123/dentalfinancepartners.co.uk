<p>Transferring property from personal ownership to a limited company is treated as a disposal for CGT purposes. HMRC views this as selling the property to your company at market value, even if no money actually changes hands.</p>
<p>This means you'll face a CGT bill on any gain made since you originally bought the property. Here's how to calculate exactly what you'll owe.</p>

<h2>Understanding the CGT Calculation</h2>
<p>The CGT calculation follows this formula:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Market value at transfer</strong> (what the property is worth now)</li>
<li><strong>Minus:</strong> Original purchase price</li>
<li><strong>Minus:</strong> Allowable costs (legal fees, <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/incorporation-cost-calculator-cgt-sdlt-implications">stamp duty</a>, improvement costs)</li>
<li><strong>Minus:</strong> Annual CGT allowance (£3,000 for 2025/26)</li>
<li><strong>Equals:</strong> Chargeable gain</li>
</ul>
<p>The chargeable gain is then taxed at either 18% or 24% depending on your total income for the tax year.</p>

<h3>Step-by-Step Calculation Example</h3>
<p>Let's work through a real example. Sarah bought a BTL property in Manchester for £180,000 in 2018. She paid £2,500 in legal fees and stamp duty. The property is now worth £280,000.</p>
<p><strong>Step 1: Calculate the gross gain</strong><br>
Market value: £280,000<br>
Original cost: £180,000 + £2,500 = £182,500<br>
Gross gain: £280,000 - £182,500 = £97,500</p>
<p><strong>Step 2: Deduct annual allowance</strong><br>
Chargeable gain: £97,500 - £3,000 = £94,500</p>
<p><strong>Step 3: Apply CGT rate</strong><br>
If Sarah's total income keeps her in basic rate: £94,500 × 18% = £17,010<br>
If she's a higher rate taxpayer: £94,500 × 24% = £22,680</p>

<h3>Which CGT Rate Applies?</h3>
<p>The CGT rate depends on your total taxable income for the year, including the capital gain:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>18% rate:</strong> If your income plus the gain stays within the basic rate band (£50,270 for 2025/26)</li>
<li><strong>24% rate:</strong> If your income plus the gain pushes you into higher rate territory</li>
</ul>
<p>This can create a cliff edge effect where part of the gain is taxed at 18% and part at 24%.</p>

<h3>Allowable Costs You Can Deduct</h3>
<p>You can reduce your chargeable gain by deducting these allowable costs:</p>
<ul>
<li>Original purchase price and associated legal fees</li>
<li>Stamp duty paid on purchase</li>
<li>Capital improvements (not repairs) - extensions, new kitchens, structural work</li>
<li>Legal and professional fees for the transfer to your company</li>
<li>Valuation fees for establishing market value</li>
</ul>
<p>Keep detailed records of all costs. Repair and maintenance expenses don't count - only costs that permanently improve the property's value.</p>

<h3>Market Valuation Requirements</h3>
<p>HMRC requires you to use the property's market value at the time of transfer, not what you originally paid. You'll typically need:</p>
<ul>
<li>A formal RICS valuation for properties worth over £250,000</li>
<li>Estate agent valuations for lower value properties</li>
<li>Recent comparable sales in the area</li>
</ul>
<p>The valuation cost is itself an allowable expense that reduces your CGT bill.</p>

<h2>Planning for Multiple Properties and Transfers</h2>
<p>If you're transferring multiple properties to a company, each property is treated as a separate disposal. However, you only get one £3,000 annual allowance to set against all gains in the tax year.</p>
<p>Consider the timing carefully. Transferring properties across two tax years gives you two annual allowances (£6,000 total) but may delay the benefits of <a href="/incorporation">incorporation</a>.</p>

<h3>Strategies to Reduce the CGT Bill</h3>
<p>Several strategies can help minimise your CGT liability:</p>
<p><strong>Timing the transfer:</strong> If you're close to the basic rate threshold, consider timing the transfer for a year when your income is lower.</p>
<p><strong>Spouse transfers:</strong> Transfer property to your spouse first (no CGT between spouses) then have them transfer to the company using their annual allowance.</p>
<p><strong>Phased incorporation:</strong> Transfer properties over multiple years to use annual allowances effectively, though this may not suit all situations.</p>

<h3>Company Purchase vs. Transfer</h3>
<p>Remember that you don't have to transfer existing properties to benefit from company ownership. Having your company purchase new properties <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/incorporate-rental-property-without-cgt">avoids</a> CGT entirely on future acquisitions.</p>
<p>Many landlords keep existing properties personally and use companies for new purchases, especially when the CGT bill would be substantial.</p>

<h2>Getting Professional Help</h2>
<p>CGT calculations can become complex, particularly with multiple properties or when planning timing strategies. The interaction between income tax, CGT, and company tax rules requires careful analysis.</p>
<p>Consider speaking to a specialist property accountant who can model different scenarios and help you understand the full tax implications before making any transfers.</p>
