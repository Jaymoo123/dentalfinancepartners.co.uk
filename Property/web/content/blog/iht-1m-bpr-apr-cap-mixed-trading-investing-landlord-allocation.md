  - question: "Is the allowance combined for BPR and APR, or is it £2.5m for each?"
    answer: "Combined. The enacted s.124D sets a single £2,500,000 allowance applying jointly across both reliefs. The estate cannot stack £2.5m of BPR on top of £2.5m of APR. Note: the GOV.UK announcement-stage summary still cites the £1m headline; the operative quantum under IHTA 1984 s.124D (as inserted by FA 2026 Sch 12 para 4) is £2.5m."
  - question: "What is the rate above the cap?"
    answer: "50% relief, giving an effective IHT charge of 20% on the qualifying value above the £2.5m allowance. The mechanic: above-cap qualifying value is reduced to 50% of market value for IHT purposes, and the resulting chargeable value is then taxed at the standard 40% rate. Worked example: a £4m unquoted-company share interest receives 100% relief on the first £2.5m and 50% relief on the remaining £1.5m, producing chargeable value £750,000 and IHT of £300,000 (20% of the £1.5m above-cap portion). Above-cap relief is therefore not zero relief; it is partial relief that still reduces the IHT rate from 40% to 20% on the excess."
  - question: "Are AIM-listed shares included in the combined allowance or separate?"
    answer: "Separate. Under the FA 2026 enacted architecture, shares designated as 'not listed' on the markets of a recognised stock exchange (including AIM) see the BPR rate reduced from 100% to 50% from 6 April 2026 in all circumstances, and these shares do not consume any part of the £2.5m s.124D allowance. The AIM 50% rate is a separate sub-tier. The strategic implication: a landlord with £400,000 of AIM-listed shares and £2,500,000 of qualifying trading-business property gets 100% relief on the £2.5m business property (using the full allowance) AND 50% relief on the £400k AIM portfolio (under the separate sub-tier), not a split of allowance between them."
  - question: "How does the cap apply to trusts holding BPR or APR property?"
    answer: "Each individual trust qualifying for 100% BPR or APR receives its own £2,500,000 s.124D allowance for chargeable events (10-yearly periodic charges and exit charges). The FA 2026 anti-fragmentation rules (operating through the s.124G to s.124K range alongside s.124D) ensure that the allowance is divided between trusts where a settlor sets up multiple trusts on or after 30 October 2024. Trusts settled before 30 October 2024 each retain their own £2.5m allowance. Trusts settled by the same settlor on or after that date share a single allowance divided across them. The 30 October 2024 date is the Autumn Budget 2024 announcement date, indicating government foreclosed the multi-trust avoidance opportunity from the announcement onwards."
  - question: "Are lifetime gifts before 6 April 2026 caught by the cap?"
    answer: "Caught by an anti-forestalling rule. Lifetime transfers (PETs and CLTs) made on or after 30 October 2024 fall within the cap regime if the donor dies after 6 April 2026. The mechanic: the gift is valued by reference to its date of gift in the normal way, but the relief at death is computed under the post-cap regime if the donor dies on or after 6 April 2026 within 7 years of the gift. The anti-forestalling rule prevents donors making large lifetime gifts of qualifying property in the window between the Autumn Budget 2024 announcement (30 October 2024) and the 6 April 2026 commencement date as a way of locking in pre-cap 100% relief. Lifetime transfers made before 30 October 2024 are outside the anti-forestalling rule and retain pre-cap treatment regardless of when the donor dies."
  - question: "Is there a formally prescribed order of application across different categories of qualifying property?"
    answer: "Not in the published gov.uk summary. The reform documents establish the £2.5m combined allowance under IHTA 1984 s.124D as inserted by FA 2026 Sch 12 para 4 (the GOV.UK announcement-stage summary still cites the stale £1m headline figure from 30 October 2024) and the 50% rate above, but do not prescribe a fixed hierarchy for allocating the allowance across different categories of qualifying property within an estate (for example: APR farmland first, BPR trading-business second; or BPR shares first, APR farmland second). The default position, until formal HMRC guidance is published, is that the allowance is applied across the qualifying property in the manner that minimises the estate's IHT charge. This is a practitioner-allocation question: the personal representatives, supported by the family's adviser, computes the IHT under several alternative orderings and selects the order producing the lowest total liability. Where all qualifying property is at the same relief rate (all 100% pre-cap), the order of application within the allowance does not affect the result; the £2.5m is fully used either way, and the remainder is above-cap at 50%. The allocation question matters more where the family has a mix of full-rate-eligible and 50%-rate-eligible property at different sub-tiers and wants to optimise around the AIM sub-tier."

  - question: "If I have both APR farmland and BPR business interests, which do I allocate the allowance to first?"
    answer: "At the relief-rate level (both at 100% pre-cap), the order of allocation does not affect total IHT, because the allowance is fully consumed either way and the above-cap portion is at 50% regardless of whether the above-cap portion is APR or BPR. The order matters operationally for which assets the personal representatives recommend the family retain (the within-cap APR farmland passes IHT-free; the within-cap BPR business passes IHT-free) versus accept the cap on (the above-cap APR or BPR property attracts 20% effective IHT under s.124D). Most family allocations favour allocating the within-cap allowance to the more illiquid or more legacy-significant asset (typically the working farm), accepting the above-cap charge on the more financially flexible asset (typically the BPR business interest), but this is a family decision rather than a tax decision. The total IHT is the same either way."
  - question: "Does the £2.5m allowance refresh between spouses?"
    answer: "Yes, each spouse has their own £2.5m s.124D allowance applied to their own estate at death. A married couple where both spouses each hold qualifying BPR or APR property has £5m of total allowance in aggregate, but only by structuring ownership so that each spouse's estate genuinely holds £2.5m of qualifying property. The unused-allowance transfer mechanism that operates for the NRB and RNRB (transferable NRB s.8A IHTA 1984 and transferable RNRB) does NOT apply to the £2.5m s.124D BPR / APR allowance. The first-to-die spouse's allowance is fixed to their own estate; any unused portion does not transfer to the surviving spouse. Couples with significant BPR or APR exposure should review ownership pre-6-April-2026 to make sure both allowances are usable. A spouse leaving qualifying property entirely to the survivor under the s.18 spouse exemption wastes the first-death allowance, which is recoverable only via a deed of variation within 2 years of first death (see Wave 4 C5)."

The corporate wrapper does not change the underlying business character; section 105(3) IHTA 1984 closes the BPR door for any company wholly or mainly investment. The FIC growth-share value transferred to next-generation shareholders therefore does not attract BPR pre-reform and is unaffected by the £2.5m s.124D cap (because there was no relief to cap in the first place). The April 2026 reform affects only those FICs that hold a genuine trading business as their underlying activity; pure property FICs are outside scope. The reform mechanics apply: 100% on the first £2.5m of qualifying SA value, 50% above. For an SA business genuinely above the £2.5m threshold, the post-reform 20% effective IHT charge on the excess is material. The reform pinches genuine SA businesses much more than light-touch short-let operations, because the genuine SA businesses are the ones that were getting BPR pre-reform."

    answer: "Sometimes, depending on the donor's age and survival probability. The CGT analysis on the gift is structurally different from a non-business gift (s.165 TCGA 1992 business-asset holdover may apply on a transfer of qualifying business assets, allowing the gain to be rolled into the donee's base cost without immediate CGT). The trade-off: dry CGT cost today (if s.165 holdover is not available, for example because the asset is APR farmland rather than s.165 trading-business property) against the 20% IHT saving above-cap if the donor survives 7 years. For a fit donor in their late 50s or early 60s with sizeable BPR or APR exposure above the £2.5m s.124D allowance, the lifetime gifting route is often the lowest-net-cost route to neutralise the cap impact."

Three operational implications. One: existing pre-October-2024 trusts each retain their own £2.5m s.124D allowance under the headline rule (the anti-fragmentation rule applies prospectively to trusts settled on or after the announcement date). Two: trusts settled on or after 30 October 2024 by the same settlor share a single £2.5m allowance divided across them under the anti-fragmentation rule. Three: trusts settled by different settlors each retain their own £2.5m allowance regardless of date; the rule operates on a per-settlor basis. The implication for landlord families is that any multi-trust structure created during the planning window since the Autumn Budget 2024 announcement may not have achieved the multi-allowance objective the structure was designed for, and the trust deeds may need to be reviewed before 6 April 2026 to understand the post-reform position."

    answer: "Yes. CLTs of qualifying BPR or APR property into discretionary trusts attract relief at the same combined £2.5m s.124D allowance plus 50%-above rate as transfers on death. From 6 April 2026 the previously unlimited 100% rate of Business Property Relief and Agricultural Property Relief is capped at £2,500,000 combined per estate under <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1984/51/section/124D" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">IHTA 1984 s.124D</a> (as inserted by FA 2026 Sch 12 para 4), with qualifying value above the cap attracting 50% relief instead of 100%. Our Wave 2 walkthrough at <a href="/blog/landlord-tax-essentials/iht-april-2026-bpr-apr-cap-property-impact">April 2026 BPR/APR Cap: Property Investor Impact</a> sets out the rule, names the segments of UK landlords actually affected, and walks the planning responses. This page goes one layer deeper on the allocation question that mixed-estate families face.</p>

<p>The cap is straightforward on the headline. The allocation across an actual mixed estate (working farm, trading business, investment property, AIM portfolio) is not. The single £2.5m s.124D allowance applies jointly to BPR and APR, with the AIM 50% rate as a separate sub-tier that does not consume the allowance, and the trust anti-fragmentation rule pulling in any multi-trust structure created since the Autumn Budget 2024 announcement on 30 October 2024. The lifetime anti-forestalling rule catches large pre-commencement gifts where the donor dies post-6-April-2026. Below: the four-tier relief framework, the order-of-application question that the published guidance does not formally prescribe, a full worked allocation on the Aldridge £3.5m mixed estate, and the planning responses that move IHT meaningfully on each tier.</p>

<h2>The four-tier relief framework after April 2026</h2>

<p>A mixed-estate landlord may hold property across four distinct relief tiers from 6 April 2026 onwards:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Tier 1: APR-eligible property (within £2.5m s.124D combined allowance).</strong> Owner-occupied farmland (and farmhouse, subject to character-appropriate use), tenanted farmland qualifying for APR at the 100% rate after the 2 or 7-year ownership conditions are met, woodland in an APR-qualifying business. 100% relief on the within-cap portion.</li>
<li><strong>Tier 2: BPR-eligible business property (within £2.5m s.124D combined allowance).</strong> Unquoted shares in a personal trading company, interests in a trading partnership, sole-trader trading business assets, land or buildings used in the deceased's trading business, controlling interests in a qualifying quoted-on-recognised-exchange trading company. 100% relief on the within-cap portion. The £2.5m allowance is shared with Tier 1 jointly; an estate with £1.5m of APR plus £1.5m of BPR fills the allowance to £2.5m and has £500k above-cap at 50% relief.</li>
<li><strong>Tier 3: AIM-listed shares (separate 50% sub-tier).</strong> Ordinary shares listed only on AIM (or shares 'designated as not listed on the markets of a recognised stock exchange') held for at least 2 years. 50% relief from 6 April 2026, regardless of the s.124D allowance position. AIM shares do not consume any part of the £2.5m allowance and are not eligible for the 100% rate even where the £2.5m allowance has not been used.</li>
<li><strong>Tier 4: Non-qualifying property (no relief).</strong> Residential BTL portfolio (Pawson v HMRC [2013] UKUT 050 (TCC) closes BPR for residential letting), light-touch short-let arrangements that do not clear the Pawson trading bar, the family home (RNRB applies but BPR does not), cash, listed shares on the main market, investment portfolios. Full 40% IHT after standard NRB and RNRB allowances.</li>
</ul>

<p>The £2.5m s.124D allowance is the shared pool for Tiers 1 and 2. AIM in Tier 3 is structurally separate. Tier 4 has no relief and never had relief; the cap is silent on Tier 4.</p>

<h2>The order of application question</h2>

<p>The enacted s.124D establishes the £2.5m combined allowance and the 50%-above-cap rate but does not formally prescribe an order of application across the qualifying property within an estate. Practitioner practice fills that gap on a minimise-the-tax basis: the personal representatives compute the total IHT under alternative allocations and select the lowest.</p>

<p>For most mixed estates, the order does not change the total IHT, because all Tier 1 and Tier 2 property is at the same relief rate within the allowance (100%) and the same relief rate above (50%). The total £2.5m of relieved value is the same regardless of which qualifying assets are allocated within and which are above. Where the order matters operationally is in the practical allocation of which specific asset attracts the cap charge: families typically prefer to allocate the within-cap allowance to the most legacy-significant or most illiquid asset (the working farm) and accept the above-cap charge on the more financially flexible asset (the BPR trading business interest), so that the inheritor can sell the financially flexible asset to fund the IHT if needed.</p>

<p>The order does matter in two specific scenarios. First, where the estate includes both 100%-eligible property and AIM property: the AIM is on the separate 50% sub-tier regardless, so the £2.5m allowance is best allocated entirely to the Tier 1 and Tier 2 100%-eligible property to maximise the rate differential. Second, where there is APR-qualifying property at a 50% pre-cap rate (some tenanted farmland under FA 1995 transitional provisions can be at 50% pre-cap rather than 100%); the 50%-pre-cap APR competes with the 100%-pre-cap BPR for the cap allowance, and allocating the 100%-pre-cap property within the allowance first produces a better outcome.</p>

<h2>Worked allocation: the Aldridge mixed estate</h2>

<p>The Aldridge persona. A single farming-and-property estate of £3.5m at the testator's death in 2027 (i.e. post-6-April-2026). Component breakdown:</p>

<ul>
<li>Working farm (owner-occupied, character-appropriate farmhouse, 200 acres): £1,200,000. APR-eligible at 100% pre-cap (Tier 1).</li>
<li>Property-developer special-purpose vehicle holding work-in-progress (residential development site with planning consent, £400,000 latent value): £400,000. BPR-eligible at 100% pre-cap (Tier 2).</li>
<li>BTL portfolio of four residential flats: £1,600,000. No relief (Tier 4).</li>
<li>AIM-listed share portfolio held for 5 years: £300,000. BPR-eligible at 100% pre-cap, dropping to 50% from 6 April 2026 (Tier 3, separate sub-tier).</li>
</ul>

<p>Pre-cap IHT (computed as if the testator had died on 5 April 2026):</p>

<ul>
<li>Working farm £1,200,000 at 100% APR: £0 chargeable.</li>
<li>Developer SPV £400,000 at 100% BPR: £0 chargeable.</li>
<li>BTL portfolio £1,600,000: fully chargeable, £1,600,000.</li>
<li>AIM portfolio £300,000 at 100% BPR: £0 chargeable.</li>
<li>Chargeable estate before allowances: £1,600,000.</li>
<li>NRB £325,000 plus RNRB £175,000 (single estate, simplified): £500,000.</li>
<li>Net chargeable: £1,100,000 at 40% = <strong>£440,000 IHT</strong>.</li>
</ul>

<p>Post-cap IHT (computed under the 6 April 2026 regime at the enacted £2.5m s.124D allowance):</p>

<ul>
<li>Combined APR plus BPR pool: £1,200,000 farm plus £400,000 developer SPV = £1,600,000. The £1,600,000 sits entirely within the £2,500,000 s.124D allowance: 100% relief applies, £0 chargeable.</li>
<li>AIM portfolio £300,000 at 50% separate sub-tier: £150,000 chargeable.</li>
<li>BTL portfolio £1,600,000 fully chargeable.</li>
<li>Chargeable estate before allowances: £0 plus £150,000 plus £1,600,000 = £1,750,000.</li>
<li>NRB £325,000 plus RNRB £175,000: £500,000.</li>
<li>Net chargeable: £1,250,000 at 40% = <strong>£500,000 IHT</strong>.</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Cap impact: £500,000 minus £440,000 = £60,000 of additional IHT under the post-cap regime.</strong> The £60k comes from one component: 20% effective IHT on £300,000 AIM portfolio under the new 50% rate. The £1,600,000 BPR/APR-eligible pool sits entirely within the £2.5m s.124D allowance and attracts no above-cap IHT (at the £1m headline figure it would have produced £120k of above-cap IHT on £600k of excess; the enacted £2.5m threshold removes that exposure entirely for this estate). The £1,600,000 BTL portfolio attracts the same IHT either way (no relief either pre-cap or post-cap, so the cap is irrelevant for the BTL leg). The pre-existing BTL exposure dwarfs the cap impact on this estate; the cap adds 14% to the IHT bill at the enacted £2.5m threshold but the underlying BTL exposure is the main driver.</p>

<aside>
<p>The cap impact on a mixed estate is rarely the largest piece of the IHT picture. The relief-eligible property is often a fraction of the total estate; the residential BTL portfolio frequently carries the dominant IHT exposure.</p>
<p>If your estate includes both qualifying BPR or APR property and a substantial BTL portfolio, the right planning sequence is usually to address the BTL leg first (lifetime gifting, FIC restructure, charity legacy) and then the cap leg second. <h2>The anti-forestalling rule for lifetime gifts</h2>

<p>The enacted FA 2026 confirms an anti-forestalling rule applying to lifetime transfers made between the Autumn Budget 2024 announcement (30 October 2024) and the 6 April 2026 commencement date. The mechanic: a lifetime gift of BPR or APR property made on or after 30 October 2024 falls into the post-cap regime if the donor dies on or after 6 April 2026 within 7 years of the gift. The relief at death-time is computed under the new £2.5m s.124D combined allowance plus 50%-above rates, not under the pre-cap unlimited 100% rate.</p>

<p>Three operational consequences:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Lifetime gifts made before 30 October 2024</strong> retain pre-cap treatment regardless of when the donor dies. The 7-year PET clock runs from the gift date in the normal way; full 100% BPR / APR applies at death if the donor dies within 7 years and the property was qualifying at the gift date and continued to qualify.</li>
<li><strong>Lifetime gifts made between 30 October 2024 and 5 April 2026</strong> are caught by the anti-forestalling rule. Donors who gifted in this window expecting to lock in pre-cap treatment have been told by HMRC that they will not get it if death falls on or after 6 April 2026 within 7 years. The 7-year clock runs as normal, but the relief at death is post-cap. Donors and donees in this window should re-model the IHT exposure on these transfers.</li>
<li><strong>Lifetime gifts made on or after 6 April 2026</strong> are within the post-cap regime by default. The forestalling rule is no longer relevant from that date onwards; the cap regime is the regime.</li>
</ul>

<p>The forestalling rule reduces the value of a particular planning move that some landlord families made in the window since the Budget. Rolling forward to today (mid-2026), landlords considering a lifetime gift in the run-up to 6 April 2026 should compute the IHT under both regimes and confirm the gift makes sense under the post-cap regime, not just under the pre-cap regime that the gift might have been targeting.</p>

<h2>The trust anti-fragmentation rule</h2>

<p>The default rule is that each trust holding qualifying BPR or APR property gets its own £2.5m s.124D allowance for chargeable events (the 10-yearly periodic charges under s.64 IHTA 1984 and the exit charges on distributions). The FA 2026 anti-fragmentation rules (s.124G to s.124K) ensure that the allowance is divided between trusts where a settlor sets up multiple trusts on or after 30 October 2024.</p>

<p>Three operational consequences:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Trusts settled before 30 October 2024:</strong> each trust retains its own £2.5m allowance. The grandfathered position protects estates that had legitimately set up multi-trust structures before the Budget announcement.</li>
<li><strong>Trusts settled on or after 30 October 2024 by the same settlor:</strong> share a single £2.5m allowance divided across them under the anti-fragmentation rule.</li>
<li><strong>Trusts settled by different settlors:</strong> each retain their own £2.5m allowance regardless of date. The rule operates on a per-settlor basis. A landlord whose adult children each settle their own trust each gets a full £2.5m allowance for the trust they settled, even where the trusts are economically interrelated through family ownership.</li>
</ul>

<p>The implication: any multi-trust structure created in the planning window between the Autumn Budget 2024 and 6 April 2026 should be reviewed against the anti-fragmentation rule before it commences. Trusts that were created to multiply the £1m allowance across the same settlor may not achieve that objective; trustees should seek specialist advice and may need to consider partial decant or unwind before 6 April 2026 to clarify the post-reform position.</p>

<h2>Planning responses by relief tier</h2>

<p>The cap is not a single planning problem; it is four problems matching the four relief tiers, each with distinct responses:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Tier 1 / Tier 2 above-cap (working farm or BPR business above £2.5m):</strong> the 20% effective IHT charge on the above-cap portion is the new exposure. Responses: lifetime gifting under s.165 TCGA 1992 holdover where available (true trading businesses qualifying for s.165 can be gifted with no immediate CGT, removing them from the estate via the 7-year PET clock); restructuring ownership across spouses to use both £2.5m s.124D allowances; reviewing whether multi-generational ownership structures (e.g. partial gift now plus retention of share) reduce the cap exposure at death.</li>
<li><strong>Tier 3 (AIM portfolio dropping to 50%):</strong> the AIM-as-IHT-shelter strategy is materially less effective from 6 April 2026 onwards. Existing AIM portfolios held primarily for IHT shelter should be reviewed: the after-tax position at the new 50% rate may be similar to or worse than alternative allocations (life cover in trust, charity nomination, direct gift). Selling out of AIM into other investment classes is a CGT event; the trade-off needs to be modelled on the specific portfolio.</li>
<li><strong>Tier 4 (BTL portfolio):</strong> the cap is irrelevant. The BTL is, and was, on the no-relief side of the Pawson investment line. Planning runs on the routes that have always applied: lifetime gifting (see Wave 4 C4), FIC value-freeze (Wave 4 C7), spousal exemption and second-death-window planning (Wave 4 C2), deed of variation after first death (Wave 4 C5), charitable legacy at 36% (Wave 4 C9), CLT into discretionary trust (Wave 4 C10).</li>
<li><strong>Cross-tier coordination:</strong> the single £2.5m s.124D allowance is shared. Couples with substantial Tier 1 plus Tier 2 exposure should consider pre-2026 ownership rebalancing so that each spouse's estate has £2.5m of qualifying property to use against their own allowance. The transferable mechanism that recovers wasted NRB and RNRB does not apply to the £2.5m s.124D BPR / APR allowance, so a wasted first-death allowance is only recoverable via deed of variation within 2 years of first death.</li>
</ul>
