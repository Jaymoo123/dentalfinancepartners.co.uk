<p>12 to 15 months. That is the realistic answer for a property-holding non-excepted estate with no disputes. The trade-press shorthand of "9 to 12 months" applies to smaller and excepted estates without rental property or business assets. Complicated estates with multiple SPVs, foreign assets, or APR / BPR valuation contests run 18 to 24 months. Contentious estates with a caveat or IPFDA 1975 claim run 3 to 5 years or longer.</p>

<p>The honest answer is not "it depends"; it is a specific range with named causes for the variance. <h2>The published service standards: the calibration anchors</h2>

<p>Two published service targets anchor the realistic timeline.</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>HMRC IHT421 target: 12 weeks from receipt of IHT400 to issuance of IHT421.</strong> The IHT421 is the probate summary / payment receipt that HMRC issues once the personal representatives have submitted the full IHT account and paid (or arranged instalment payment for) the IHT due. The PR cannot proceed with the probate application until HMRC has issued IHT421 for non-excepted estates. Realistic slippage on this target has been common in recent years; cross-check the current published target at submission.</li>
<li><strong>HMCTS Probate Service target: 16 weeks from receipt of a valid application to issuance of grant.</strong> The HMCTS Probate Service is the single online portal post-2019. Realistic slippage on this target has also been common; the PR application that arrives complete and clean tends to fall closer to the target than applications that trigger requisitions.</li>
</ul>

<p>The two targets in sequence (12 weeks IHT421 then 16 weeks grant) anchor a baseline of around 28 weeks (around 7 months) from IHT400 submission to grant. Preparation time pre-IHT400 typically runs 12-15 weeks for a property-holding estate. Post-grant administration (asset collection, debt payment, rental management, final distribution) typically runs 12-16 weeks. The IPFDA 1975 6-month claim window from grant runs concurrent with the post-grant administration phase but holds final distribution to roughly 6 months from grant.</p>

<p>Total realistic timeline: 12 to 15 months for the modal property-holding non-excepted estate.</p>

<h2>The IHT side: IHT400 and the s.227 instalment option</h2>

<p>For non-excepted estates, the personal representatives submit IHT400 with supporting schedules (IHT403 gifts, IHT404 jointly-owned, IHT405 houses, IHT411 listed shares, IHT413 business and partnership interests, IHT414 agricultural property, IHT418 settled property, IHT419 debts owed to the deceased, and others as relevant).</p>

<p>The IHT due is calculated on the chargeable estate (gross estate less reliefs less exemptions less liabilities less the available nil-rate band stack). For property-holding estates the IHT bill commonly sits in the £50k-£500k range, with larger estates running into the millions.</p>

<p>Critical operational option: <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1984/51/section/227" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">IHTA 1984 s.227</a> allows the PRs to elect to pay IHT attributable to qualifying property (land, buildings, certain business assets) in 10 equal annual instalments. The first instalment is due 6 months from the end of the month of death; subsequent instalments at annual intervals thereafter. Interest accrues on unpaid instalments at the HMRC published rate.</p>

<p>The instalment option is operationally critical for property-heavy estates. Without it, the PRs would be forced to sell property pre-grant to pay the IHT bill in full upfront, often at adverse cycle prices. With the instalment option, the PRs can pay the first instalment from estate cash (often the deceased's bank balances plus a small property sale where required) and roll the remainder over the 10-year window. Once a property is sold during administration, the outstanding instalment IHT becomes payable on the sold property's apportioned share at the next instalment date.</p>

<h2>The excepted estates regime</h2>

<p>Per <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2021/1167" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">SI 2021/1167</a> in force from 1 January 2022, three main routes qualify an estate as "excepted" and remove the need to file a full IHT400 account:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Low-value excepted estates:</strong> net estate within the IHT nil-rate band (£325k single, up to £650k with transferable NRB).</li>
<li><strong>Wholly-spouse-exempt estates:</strong> estate passes entirely to a UK long-term resident spouse under IHTA 1984 s.18 (with the post-FA-2025 LTR test).</li>
<li><strong>Nil-rate excepted estates:</strong> estate at or below the NRB after gifts in the seven years pre-death.</li>
</ul>

<p>For excepted estates, the PRs self-certify in the probate application; no separate IHT account is filed. HMRC has 60 days after grant to require additional information under IHTA 1984 s.256(1A); the right is rarely exercised.</p>

<p>For most landlord estates with one or more rental properties (typical net value £300k+), the excepted-estate routes do not fit unless the entire estate passes to the spouse. The IHT400 + IHT421 path is the relevant calendar.</p>

<h2>The probate-application side</h2>

<p>The probate application via the HMCTS Probate Service online portal requires:</p>

<ul>
<li>Original will (where probate sought) or evidence of intestacy (where letters of administration sought) under <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1981/54/part/V" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">Senior Courts Act 1981 Part V</a>.</li>
<li>Executor or administrator identification (passport or driving licence).</li>
<li>IHT421 for non-excepted estates (the probate summary issued by HMRC after IHT400 processing).</li>
<li>Inventory of assets and liabilities (largely derived from the IHT400 schedules).</li>
<li>Application fee (£273 for estates above £5,000; nil for estates at or below £5,000; checked at submission).</li>
</ul>

<p>HMCTS Probate Service's target is 16 weeks from receipt of a valid application to issuance of grant. Common requisition triggers that extend the timeline include:</p>

<ul>
<li>Missing or unclear executor identification.</li>
<li>Missing or out-of-date IHT421.</li>
<li>Ambiguity over domicile or jurisdiction.</li>
<li>Original will not provided (where copy provided).</li>
<li>Mismatched name spellings between will, death certificate and identification.</li>
</ul>

<p>Each requisition typically adds 4 to 8 weeks. Caveats lodged under <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/1987/2024" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">NCPR 1987</a> rule 44 remove the grant from the timeline entirely until resolved.</p>

<h2>The rental-property administration-period tax compliance</h2>

<p>The rental property continues to generate income from the death date until vesting in the beneficiary. The income is taxable on the personal representatives at PR-level rates during the administration period.</p>

<p>Per <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2010/4/section/34" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">CTA 2010 ss.34-35</a>, the PR income tax rate on rental income is the basic rate (20%) with no personal allowance available. PRs are not individuals for personal-allowance purposes per HMRC's <a href="https://www.gov.uk/hmrc-internal-manuals/trusts-settlements-and-estates-manual/tsem7320" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">Trusts, Settlements and Estates Manual TSEM7320</a>.</p>

<p>The PRs file an SA900 (trust and estate return) for the administration period. The SA900 captures all rental income, dividends and other estate income; the resulting tax is paid by the estate. Once the property vests in a beneficiary, rental income from then on is taxed on the beneficiary at their personal marginal rates.</p>

<p>The PR-level basic-rate compliance is a meaningful detail that catches families unaware. A modest BTL flat generating £15,000 per year of net rental income during a 12-month administration period produces a PR tax bill of £3,000 (£15,000 × 20%, no PA). For the modal property-holding estate the figures are bigger; budget for the PR-level tax in the administration cash flow.</p>

<h2>The CGT side during administration</h2>

<p>Two-stage analysis on the CGT side.</p>

<p><strong>During administration.</strong> PRs hold each property at the uplifted death market value base cost per <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12/section/62" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">TCGA 1992 s.62</a>. PR disposals attract PR CGT rates (24% residential / 20% non-residential from 6 April 2024 per FA 2024) with a PR annual exempt amount of £3,000 in the tax year of death plus the following two tax years per <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12/section/3" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">TCGA 1992 s.3</a>.</p>

<p><strong>After vesting in beneficiary.</strong> The beneficiary acquires at the same probate value (the death market value) as their base cost. Future disposals from then on attract the beneficiary's personal CGT rates with the beneficiary's personal annual exempt amount (£3,000 per individual from 6 April 2024).</p>

<p>The base-cost uplift under s.62 is the structural reason why PR disposals typically attract limited CGT exposure compared to lifetime gifts (where TCGA 1992 s.62(4) does not apply and the gain crystallises against the original acquisition cost). For property-holding estates the s.62 uplift is often the single most valuable feature of the IHT regime for the next-generation inheritor.</p>

<h2>The IPFDA 1975 six-month claim window</h2>

<p>The <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1975/63/contents" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">Inheritance (Provision for Family and Dependants) Act 1975</a> gives a six-month window from the issuance of the grant in which certain family members and dependants (spouse, former spouse not remarried, civil partner, child, stepchild, person treated as child of the family, person maintained by the deceased) can apply to the court for reasonable financial provision from the estate.</p>

<p>Responsible PRs typically hold final distribution until 6 months past grant plus a reasonable additional margin to ensure no late-arriving claims will catch them with assets already distributed. This is non-statutory practice rather than statutory delay, but it commonly adds 6-8 months to the modal timeline.</p>

<p>The risk of distributing within the 6-month window: a successful IPFDA claim against an already-distributed estate can result in personal liability for the PRs. PRs are not statutorily protected for early distribution; the protection of <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/Geo5/15-16/19/section/27" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">Trustee Act 1925 s.27</a> notices to creditors does not extend to IPFDA claims.</p>

<li>Stage 4 (week 16): IHT400 submitted; £30,000 IHT paid (or 10-year instalment option for property elected; in this case the cash position is sufficient to pay outright).</li>

<h2>Compressing the timeline</h2>

<p>Five steps meaningfully compress the timeline. None of them can compress below around 8-9 months for a non-excepted estate because the HMRC and HMCTS service-level standards themselves set the floor.</p>

<ol>
<li><strong>Early appointment of advisers.</strong> Solicitor, accountant and estate valuer engaged in the same week (week 1-2), not sequentially. Saves 2-4 weeks of front-loaded preparation time.</li>
<li><strong>IHT400 preparation in parallel with valuations.</strong> Start the IHT400 framework immediately with placeholder valuations; slot in the verified figures as they arrive. Saves 2-3 weeks vs the sequential approach.</li>
<li><strong>Clear executor communication channels.</strong> Many delays come from internal executor disagreements rather than HMRC or HMCTS. A documented executor decision-making process plus shared visibility of the timeline saves 2-6 weeks across the lifecycle.</li>
<li><strong>Trustee Act 1925 s.27 statutory notices early.</strong> Place the notices to creditors and claimants early in the process; the 2-month statutory window runs concurrent with the grant application rather than after, saving 6-8 weeks on the post-grant distribution side.</li>
<li><strong>Honest disclosure on the IHT400.</strong> Late-arriving HMRC requisitions on incomplete returns add 4-8 weeks per round. A complete IHT400 with all schedules and clear narrative on any judgment calls (valuation methodology; deeds-of-variation pre-planning; APR / BPR claims) avoids the requisition cycle.</li>
</ol>

<h2>The bigger picture</h2>

<p>Probate timelines for property-holding estates are predictable. The HMRC IHT421 target plus the HMCTS Probate Service target plus the IPFDA 1975 6-month claim window plus the rental-property administration-period tax compliance combine into a realistic 12-15 month modal range. The trade-press shorthand of 9-12 months reflects smaller and excepted estates; it understates the realistic duration for the property-holding cohort.</p>

<p>The compression strategies above can move the modal case to 9-11 months. The complications above can extend the case to 18-24 months (multi-SPV or foreign asset) or 3-5 years (contentious). Knowing which range your estate sits in is the first step in planning the administration timeline honestly with the family.</p>
