  - question: "What is the blended-family inheritance problem that a property FIC addresses?"
    answer: "A founder who has remarried after a first marriage with adult children typically wants three things at once: continuing income for the second spouse during their lifetime, eventual inheritance of the underlying property to the first-marriage children, and operational control of the property while the founder is alive. The default position without bespoke planning is that property left to the second spouse on the founder's death becomes the second spouse's property absolutely; the second spouse can then leave it to anyone they choose (their own children from a prior relationship, a new partner, a charity, a friend). The first-marriage children's inheritance can be unintentionally disinherited by the second spouse's later will or intestacy. A property FIC with bespoke share-class drafting can address this by separating the income strand (preference shares for the spouse during their life) from the capital strand (growth shares vested in the first-marriage children from issue), with the articles preventing re-routing on the second spouse's later death."
  - question: "What is an IPDI will-trust and how does it compare to a property FIC for blended families?"
    answer: "An immediate-post-death-interest (IPDI) trust under IHTA 1984 sections 49 to 49E is a will-trust where the surviving spouse has a right to income from the trust property for life, with the capital passing on the spouse's later death to named remainder beneficiaries (typically the first-marriage children). The IPDI is treated for IHT as if the surviving spouse owns the trust property absolutely, so the spouse exemption under IHTA 1984 section 18 applies on the founder's death (no IHT entry charge), and the capital is taxed on the spouse's death as part of the spouse's estate. The IPDI is the will-trust analogue of the FIC bespoke share-class settlement; the FIC operates during the founder's life as well as after death, while the IPDI only operates after death. The FIC carries CT and dividend tax during life; the IPDI carries trust-tax friction (45% trust rate on undistributed income above £500 standard band) but the IPDI's spouse-life-interest treatment can soften this. The right answer depends on the founder's life expectancy, the size of the portfolio, and the founder's preference for during-life vs after-death structuring."
  - question: "Can a property FIC's articles prevent my second spouse from re-routing the inheritance on their later death?"
    answer: "Yes, because the second spouse's interest in the FIC is in the preference share class (income coupon for life), not in the growth share class (capital growth). The growth shares are held by the first-marriage children from issue and continue to be held by them regardless of what the second spouse does with their preference shares. The articles can also include a contingent-vesting provision on the preference class: the spouse holds them for life with the shares automatically cancelling or transferring on the spouse's death. The articles can additionally restrict transfer of the preference shares away from the spouse during life (so the spouse cannot gift, sell, or otherwise dispose of them to a third party). The companion articles-drafting walkthrough is in our <a href=\"/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/fic-articles-of-association-property-control-mechanics\">FIC articles of association page</a>; the contingent-vesting clause is one of the bespoke clauses described there."
  - question: "Does the FIC route avoid the spouse exemption issue on the founder's death?"
    answer: "Not directly; the FIC interacts with the spouse exemption in a different way. On the founder's death, the founder's FIC shares (A-class voting plus preference, typically) are part of the founder's estate. If left to the second spouse, the IHTA 1984 section 18 spouse exemption applies and no IHT arises on the founder's death. The shares then form part of the second spouse's estate on their later death (assuming the surviving spouse holds them, which can be the case where the will leaves the founder's FIC shares to the spouse rather than back to the first-marriage children). The growth shares were already held by the first-marriage children from issue (or by a trust for them), so the growth-share value transferred when the founder gifted them as PETs during life is already outside the founder's estate. The spouse exemption on the founder's death therefore covers only the founder's residual A-class and preference holdings; the bulk of the property value has already moved to the growth-share class held by the first-marriage children. The FIC mechanic is less about avoiding the spouse exemption and more about controlling where the capital sits before the founder's death."
  - question: "What happens to the second spouse's preference share coupon on the founder's death?"
    answer: "Depends on how the articles are drafted. Three common designs. First, the founder holds the preference class for life and the shares pass to the spouse on the founder's death by will (spouse exemption applies, no IHT on the transfer). The spouse then receives the coupon for life. On the spouse's later death, the preference shares pass per the spouse's will, which can be controlled either by a binding mutual will or by the articles' contingent-vesting clause that cancels the shares on the spouse's death. Second, the spouse holds a separate preference class from the start, alongside the founder's class; on the founder's death the spouse's class continues unchanged. Third, the founder retains the preference class, and on the founder's death the shares are redeemed by the company and the cash equivalent is paid to the spouse over a defined period via dividends on a contingent class. Each design has different drafting and tax implications; choose the design at FIC incorporation rather than retrofitting on the founder's later death."
  - question: "Can I disinherit my second spouse's children from a prior relationship using the FIC route?"
    answer: "The FIC route is designed to channel the founder's property to the founder's biological or adopted children (first-marriage children in this typical case), not to the second spouse's children from a prior relationship. The mechanism is the growth-share allocation: the founder allocates growth shares to the founder's chosen beneficiaries (typically the first-marriage children) at the time of issue, with no growth-share allocation to the second spouse or to the second spouse's children. The second spouse receives income via the preference class but no growth-share allocation, so the underlying property capital does not flow through the second spouse to the second spouse's children. This is the structural feature, not a planning workaround. Whether this is the right outcome depends on the family's intentions and the legitimate-expectation discussion with the second spouse before incorporation; surprising a second spouse with the discovery that their own children have no claim on the property portfolio can produce relationship difficulty even where the structure is legally valid."
  - question: "What is the role of a deed of variation in blended-family FIC planning?"
    answer: "A deed of variation under IHTA 1984 section 142 lets a beneficiary of the deceased's estate redirect their inheritance within 2 years of death, with the variation read back to the deceased for IHT and CGT purposes. For a property founder who dies without having implemented a FIC, the surviving spouse can use a deed of variation to redirect the property inheritance to a FIC structure or to the first-marriage children directly. The deed of variation is therefore a remedial tool, not a planning tool, but it gives the family a 2-year window after the founder's death to retroactively achieve some of the FIC settlement benefit. The Wave 4 deed-of-variation page in the IHT bucket covers the mechanic; the deed-of-variation route is materially less elegant than incorporating the FIC during life but can be the right answer where the founder's death has already happened."
  - question: "How does the FIC handle a second-spouse incapacity or relationship breakdown?"
    answer: "Two scenarios warrant the articles' attention. First, second-spouse incapacity: the founder may want the spouse to continue receiving the preference coupon during incapacity (via a deputy appointment under the Mental Capacity Act 2005 or a registered enduring/lasting power of attorney) but the spouse's incapacity should not affect the founder's continuing control of the company. The articles can address this by keeping voting rights in the founder's A-class only, with the spouse's preference class being non-voting throughout. Second, relationship breakdown (divorce or separation): the spouse's preference shares can be subject to divorce-court orders if the marriage breaks down, with the value of the preference holding being a matrimonial asset. The contingent-vesting clause can include a divorce-trigger that cancels or transfers the preference shares on a decree absolute, but enforceability against a divorce court order is not certain; the family-court jurisdiction can sometimes override company-law structures."
  - question: "What is the section 177 declaration issue if the second spouse becomes a director?"
    answer: "If the second spouse is appointed as a director of the FIC (which is sometimes done to give them visibility on company affairs and a role in governance), the spouse's section 177 declarations of interest under CA 2006 become a recurring discipline. Every related-party transaction (every preference dividend declaration on the spouse's class, every loan between the spouse and the company, every property transaction involving the spouse) requires a section 177 declaration. The mechanism is the same as for the founder's own declarations (covered in our <a href=\"/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/fic-property-corporate-governance-board-meetings-resolutions-discipline\">FIC governance discipline page</a>), but the operational rhythm doubles where there are two directors. A more common arrangement in blended-family FICs is to keep the founder as sole director throughout and give the spouse company-secretary-style visibility (access to accounts, attendance at board meetings as observer) without making them a director."
  - question: "Does the second spouse appear on the Companies House public record?"
    answer: "Depends on the structure. The register of members is held at the company's registered office and is open to public inspection on payment of a small fee, but it is not directly available on the Companies House public record. The PSC register at Companies House publishes the names and dates of birth (year and month) of anyone holding 25% or more of voting rights or shares. If the second spouse holds 25% or more of either, the spouse's name appears on the PSC register; if the spouse holds less than 25% (typically the case in a blended-family FIC where the spouse only holds preference shares), the spouse does not appear on the PSC register. The articles themselves are filed at Companies House and visible publicly, so any contingent-vesting clause, share-class definition, or reserved-matters list visible in the articles is on the public record. For families who want the blended-family arrangement private, the heavy clauses can be put in the shareholders' agreement rather than the articles, although this loses the section 22 entrenchment protection (covered in our articles drafting page)."
  - question: "What is the typical timeline for setting up a property FIC for the blended-family scenario?"
    answer: "Three months from instruction to incorporation is the standard timeline. Month 1 is the discovery and decision phase: family circumstances analysis, second-spouse discussion (this is often the longest single conversation in the process), first-marriage children consultation if applicable, share-class architecture decided, articles outline drafted. Month 2 is the drafting phase: specialist lawyer drafts the bespoke articles, shareholders' agreement, valuation work commissioned, tax structuring confirmed (incorporation route from existing portfolio if applicable, section 162 incorporation relief eligibility checked). The first-year operational cycle then follows (quarterly board meetings, year-end accounts, statutory registers maintenance). For founders with an existing personally-held property portfolio that needs to be incorporated into the new FIC, add 1 to 3 months for the property transfer mechanics (s.162 incorporation relief, SDLT analysis, mortgage refinancing where needed)."
  - question: "How does the FIC route compare to a discretionary will-trust for the blended-family case?"
    answer: "Discretionary will-trust: testamentary trust under the founder's will, settled on death, with trustees having discretion to distribute income and capital among a class of beneficiaries (typically the spouse, first-marriage children, and sometimes other family). On founder's death the spouse exemption section 18 IHTA 1984 does NOT apply (the trust is not the spouse), so the trust pays entry IHT of 20% on excess over NRB. The 10-year periodic charge regime applies (up to 6% on chargeable value at each anniversary). The trustees have flexibility to distribute as circumstances change (a feature that the FIC's share-class settlement lacks). FIC route: structured during life, no entry IHT on incorporation, no periodic charges, less flexibility on distributions (driven by share-class rights rather than trustee discretion), corporate tax on rental profits during life rather than trust-rate income tax. For most blended-family scenarios, the FIC is preferred where the founder is willing to set up the structure during life and where the share-class allocation matches the family's intentions; the discretionary trust is preferred where the founder dies without an active FIC structure and the family wants flexibility on distributions."
  - question: "Can I run the blended-family FIC alongside a will trust for additional protection?"
    answer: "Yes, and this is the most robust structure for substantial blended-family wealth. The FIC handles the property portfolio during life with the share-class settlement described above. The founder's will includes an IPDI trust over residual non-FIC property assets (the founder's home, cash, and any non-FIC investments) with the spouse as life-tenant and the first-marriage children as remainder beneficiaries. The two structures reinforce each other: the FIC handles the active property portfolio with operational control during life; the IPDI handles the residual estate with the will-trust mechanism on death. The combined administrative cost is higher but the structural protection is comprehensive. For larger portfolios (£3 million or more), the combined approach is becoming the standard recommendation."
  - question: "What if the second marriage ends before the founder's death?"
    answer: "The FIC's preference share arrangement for the second spouse continues to exist regardless of the marriage's status unless the articles include an express divorce-trigger or the shares are transferred under a divorce settlement. Three operational considerations follow. First, the preference dividend stream continues to the former spouse during their life unless the articles cancel or redirect it on divorce. Second, the divorce court has jurisdiction to make orders affecting the parties' assets, which can include the preference share value; the court can order transfer of the shares back to the founder or to a trust, with the company effectively bound by the court order. Third, the founder's will (which may have made provision for the spouse's IPDI or other will-trust interest in residual estate) needs urgent review on divorce because divorce typically does not automatically revoke gifts in a will to the former spouse (changes under the Wills Act 1837 and the Inheritance and Trustees' Powers Act 2014 affect this; legal advice on the specific facts is essential). The blended-family FIC should be re-reviewed at any change in marital status."

<p>A founder remarrying after a first marriage with adult children typically wants three things from a property portfolio that pull against each other. The second spouse should receive income from the rental portfolio for the rest of their life. The first-marriage children should inherit the underlying property capital, with no risk that the second spouse later redirects that capital to a third party (the second spouse's own children from a prior relationship, a future partner, a charity, a friend). The founder should retain operational control of the property holding while alive and competent. The default UK inheritance position handles none of these three goals together. A property FIC with bespoke share-class drafting can.</p>

<p>This page walks the blended-family scenario via two anonymised personas, sets out the share-class settlement in the articles that addresses each, and compares the FIC route to the two main alternatives: an immediate-post-death-interest (IPDI) will-trust under IHTA 1984 sections 49 to 49E, and a discretionary will-trust. Out of scope: the abstract clause-by-clause articles drafting walkthrough (see our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/fic-articles-of-association-property-control-mechanics">FIC articles of association page</a>), the share-gift PET mechanic in detail (see our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/fic-gifting-shares-children-property-7-year-iht-mechanics">FIC share-gift mechanics page</a>), and the in-life retirement income mechanics (see our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/fic-property-retirement-decumulation-mechanics-uk">FIC retirement income page</a>). This page is the blended-family persona-led use case; the structural detail it relies on lives in the sibling pages.</p>

<h2>The three goals that pull against each other</h2>

<p>The blended-family founder's three goals are individually straightforward and collectively in tension.</p>

<p><strong>Income for the second spouse for life.</strong> The second spouse needs a continuing income stream from the property portfolio (or equivalent assets) to maintain their standard of living after the founder's death. A founder who has supported the spouse during life will generally want that support to continue. The IHTA 1984 section 18 spouse exemption is the standard mechanism: assets left to the spouse on death pass IHT-free, the spouse receives the income, and the assets are taxed on the spouse's later death.</p>

<p><strong>Inheritance of the capital to the first-marriage children.</strong> The founder's first-marriage children typically have an expectation of inheriting the property portfolio that the founder built before the second marriage. This expectation can be informal (verbal during the founder's life) or formal (an existing will or a side-letter to the founder's accountants). The default UK position is that property left absolutely to the second spouse becomes the spouse's property; the spouse can then dispose of it freely under their own will or by lifetime gift. The first-marriage children's expectation has no legal protection unless the structure prevents the re-routing.</p>

<p><strong>Operational control during the founder's life.</strong> While the founder is alive and competent, the founder typically wants to make the property decisions (acquisitions, disposals, refinancing, lettings strategy) without needing consent from the spouse or the first-marriage children. Operational control is a separate question from beneficial ownership; a founder can retain control via voting rights on a separate share class while beneficial ownership flows to other classes.</p>

<p>The three goals together require: an income stream to the spouse that does not depend on the spouse holding the underlying capital; a capital allocation to the first-marriage children that the spouse cannot redirect; and a voting class held by the founder that gives operational control independent of either of the other two strands. The FIC share-class architecture is built to deliver exactly this three-strand split.</p>

<h2>Persona one: the widow and the property portfolio</h2>

<p>An anonymised illustrative persona to ground the mechanics. A founder, in her early 60s, was widowed in her 40s. She has three adult children from her first marriage, now in their 30s, all financially independent. She built a property portfolio of seven residential rental properties over 20 years, with a current market value around £3.8 million and net rental income after costs around £140,000 per year. She has now remarried; her second husband has no children of his own. She is in good health and expects a 20 to 30 year horizon. She wants her second husband to have income for the rest of his life if she predeceases him, the three first-marriage children to inherit the property capital, and operational control of the portfolio during her own life.</p>

<p>The FIC settlement for this persona: incorporate the portfolio into a FIC (using section 162 TCGA incorporation relief subject to the active-business test, which the size and active management of the portfolio supports). Issue four share classes. The founder holds 100% of the A-class voting shares (lifetime operational control). The founder also holds 100% of the preference class issued at par value with a 5% cumulative coupon (the lifetime income strand). The growth class is issued at the same time to a discretionary trust for the three first-marriage children, with the founder and a professional trustee as trustees (the capital strand transferred immediately, with 7-year PET clock starting from issue). Articles include a contingent-vesting clause on the preference class: on the founder's death, the preference shares pass to the second husband for the rest of his life (spouse exemption section 18 applies, no IHT on founder's death), with automatic cancellation of the preference shares on the husband's later death (so the shares cannot pass to anyone the husband chooses).</p>

<p>The outcome for the persona: during the founder's life, she draws the preference coupon (around £25,000 per year on a £500,000 preference allocation at 5%) plus DLA repayment from the incorporation transfer plus any A-class dividend at her discretion. On her death, the spouse exemption applies to her A-class and preference holdings passing to the husband. The husband receives the preference coupon for the rest of his life. The growth class held by the children's trust continues unchanged through both deaths. On the husband's later death, the preference shares cancel (per the articles), and the trust holding the growth class continues to hold the capital. The three first-marriage children's inheritance is structurally protected.</p>

<h2>Persona two: the divorced founder with two adult children and a new spouse with three children</h2>

<p>Different persona, different settlement. A founder, in his mid 50s, divorced 10 years ago, has two first-marriage adult children in their late 20s. He has now remarried; his second wife has three children from her first marriage, all in their teens. He owns a property portfolio of 12 rental properties worth around £6 million, generating £280,000 per year net rental income. He is at peak earning years (also runs a separate consultancy business) and expects to continue working for 10 to 15 years. He wants his second wife to have a meaningful income stream during her life if he predeceases her, his two first-marriage adult children to inherit the property portfolio, and his second wife's three children to receive specific named gifts but no claim on the property portfolio.</p>

<p>The FIC settlement for this persona: incorporate the portfolio into a FIC. Issue four share classes plus a contingent fifth. The founder holds A-class voting (control during life). The growth class is split between two equal trusts, one for each first-marriage adult child, with the founder as settlor and named professional trustees alongside the founder. The preference class is issued to the founder initially with a 6% cumulative coupon on a £1 million paid-up value; the articles include contingent vesting on the founder's death passing the preference class to the second wife for life. A separate non-voting C-class is issued in small allocation to the second wife from the outset (small dividend allowance against her own basic-rate band during the founder's life as a quality-of-life income strand). Articles explicitly do not provide for any class allocation to the second wife's three children from her prior relationship; the founder's will provides for specific cash bequests to those three children separately.</p>

<p>The outcome: during the founder's life, the C-class provides the second wife with modest dividend income at her own marginal rate (typically £5,000 to £15,000 per year subject to discretion); the founder takes the preference coupon and dividend on the A-class as needed. On the founder's death, A-class voting passes to a successor (typically the older first-marriage child, or a corporate trustee); preference class passes to the second wife per the contingent-vesting clause; growth-share trusts for the first-marriage children continue. The two first-marriage children's inheritance flows through the growth-share trusts. The second wife's three children's interests are limited to the cash bequests under the founder's will and do not extend to the property portfolio.</p>

<h2>The articles' contingent-vesting clause: the key drafting move</h2>

<p>The contingent-vesting clause on the preference class is the legal mechanism that gives the blended-family FIC its structural protection. The clause has three elements drafted explicitly into the articles:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>The trigger event:</strong> typically the founder's death, with a back-up trigger for the founder's loss of capacity (registered with the Office of the Public Guardian).</li>
  <li><strong>The vesting destination:</strong> the second spouse becomes the holder of the preference shares with the same coupon entitlement (5% to 7% cumulative) for the rest of the spouse's life.</li>
  <li><strong>The termination event:</strong> on the second spouse's later death, the preference shares automatically cancel under the articles, with the capital previously allocated to the preference class being released to the company's distributable reserves and ultimately to the growth-share class (held by the first-marriage children's trusts) on a subsequent disposition. The cancellation prevents the spouse leaving the preference shares to anyone by will, because at the moment of the spouse's death the shares cease to exist.</li>
</ul>

<p>The clause is bespoke drafting that the Companies House model articles do not provide. The articles are entrenched under section 22 CA 2006 so that the clause cannot be removed by a 75% shareholder vote in subsequent generations (covered in our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/fic-articles-of-association-property-control-mechanics">articles drafting page</a>). The shareholders' agreement carries the procedural detail (notice timing, valuation mechanics on cancellation, distribution of the released capital).</p>

<h2>The IPDI will-trust alternative</h2>

<p>The immediate-post-death-interest (IPDI) trust under IHTA 1984 sections 49 to 49E is the standard will-trust analogue of the FIC bespoke share-class settlement. The mechanism: the founder's will leaves the property portfolio (or relevant share interests) on trust for the second spouse for life, with remainder to the first-marriage children. The IHT treatment under section 49: the surviving spouse is treated as if they own the trust property absolutely, so the section 18 spouse exemption applies on the founder's death (no IHT entry charge). The capital is taxed on the spouse's later death as part of the spouse's estate.</p>

<p>Comparison with the FIC route on four axes:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>When the structure takes effect.</strong> IPDI on the founder's death only; FIC during the founder's life and continues after death. A founder in their early 60s with a 20 to 30 year expected horizon has different planning needs from one in their late 70s; the FIC gives during-life benefit, the IPDI gives only after-death benefit.</li>
  <li><strong>Operational control during life.</strong> IPDI gives no during-life control mechanism (the trust does not exist until the will takes effect on death); FIC gives the founder voting control through the A-class for the duration of life.</li>
  <li><strong>Tax friction.</strong> IPDI: spouse exemption on founder's death (no entry IHT); the trust pays trust-rate income tax on undistributed income (45% above £500 standard band) which is paid out to the spouse with a tax credit. FIC: no IHT entry charge on incorporation (it is a company, not a trust); corporation tax on rental profits during life; dividend tax on extracted income.</li>
  <li><strong>Spouse's control of capital.</strong> IPDI: the spouse has a life interest only (income for life); the spouse cannot dispose of the underlying capital because the capital is held by the trustees subject to the remainder to the first-marriage children. The structural protection is similar to the FIC's contingent-vesting clause. FIC: the spouse holds the preference class which cancels on the spouse's death (per the articles); the spouse cannot dispose of the preference shares away from this arrangement. Both routes deliver the structural protection through different legal mechanics.</li>
</ul>

<p>The right route is fact-specific. For founders with active property portfolios who want operational benefit during life, the FIC route generally wins. For founders without a need for during-life operational structuring (a smaller portfolio held passively, a founder closer to natural retirement, a simpler family situation), the IPDI route is often the right answer.</p>

<h2>The discretionary will-trust alternative</h2>

<p>A discretionary will-trust gives more flexibility than an IPDI but more tax friction. The mechanism: the founder's will leaves the property on a discretionary trust with a defined class of beneficiaries (typically including the spouse, first-marriage children, and sometimes other family members), with the trustees deciding distributions over the trust's life. The IHT treatment: on the founder's death the trust is a "relevant property" trust; the spouse exemption under section 18 does NOT apply (the trust is not the spouse) so the trust pays entry IHT of 20% on excess over NRB. Ongoing 10-year periodic charges apply (up to 6% on chargeable value at each anniversary). Exit charges apply on distributions of capital.</p>

<p>The discretionary trust's advantage is flexibility: trustees can shift distributions over time as circumstances change (a beneficiary's financial difficulties, a divorce, a child's education needs). The disadvantages are tax friction (entry IHT, periodic charges, exit charges) and the absence of operational structuring during the founder's life.</p>

<p>The discretionary trust is often the right answer where the founder dies suddenly without having implemented a FIC or an IPDI, and the family uses a deed of variation under IHTA 1984 section 142 (covered in the wave 4 deed-of-variation page) to redirect the inheritance into a trust structure within 2 years of death. The discretionary trust as a primary lifetime plan is less common for blended-family scenarios because the entry IHT cost on a property portfolio above the NRB is significant.</p>

<h2>Operational pitfalls specific to blended-family FICs</h2>

<p>Five operational considerations are particular to the blended-family structure and deserve attention at incorporation or in early operational years.</p>

<p><strong>The second spouse's PSC threshold.</strong> Where the second spouse holds 25% or more of voting rights or shares in the FIC, they appear on the public PSC register at Companies House (name and birth month and year). Most blended-family FIC settlements keep the spouse below the 25% threshold (the preference class is the spouse's interest, typically a minority of the total share capital) so the spouse does not appear publicly. The PSC threshold is a binary visibility question; design for the side the family wants.</p>

<p><strong>The second spouse's section 177 declarations if a director.</strong> Appointing the spouse as a director makes every related-party transaction a section 177 declaration moment. Most blended-family FICs keep the founder as sole director with the spouse as a non-director participant (attending board meetings as observer, with visibility on accounts and decisions but no formal role). The operational discipline (covered in our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/fic-property-corporate-governance-board-meetings-resolutions-discipline">governance page</a>) is materially simpler with one director than with two.</p>

<p><strong>Founder's incapacity continuity.</strong> The founder's voting control via the A-class is meaningless during an incapacity if the articles do not address the question. Common drafting: the founder's lasting power of attorney holder (typically one of the first-marriage adult children, or a professional attorney) exercises the A-class voting during the founder's incapacity. The articles record the mechanism; the LPA is registered separately with the Office of the Public Guardian.</p>

<p><strong>The disclosure question with the second spouse.</strong> The bespoke share-class settlement, while legally valid, can produce relationship difficulty if the second spouse discovers it post-hoc rather than discussing it openly during incorporation. Most experienced advisers insist on a candid second-spouse conversation as the first step in the process, before the structure is committed to paper. The conversation is uncomfortable but materially reduces the risk of later dispute.</p>

<p><strong>Mutual wills as a non-FIC alternative.</strong> Spouses can execute mutual wills under the doctrine in Re Cleaver [1981] that create a contractual obligation not to vary their wills after the first death. A founder and second spouse can agree mutual wills under which the founder leaves property to the spouse for life and the spouse promises not to vary their own will to redirect the capital. The mutual-wills route avoids the FIC's structural cost but has its own enforceability questions (the contract is enforceable but only on the survivor's estate, and may not survive a divorce or a new marriage). For most blended-family scenarios the FIC's structural protection is more robust.</p>

<h2>Which route fits which family</h2>

<p>A defensible decision framework, with the usual caveat that real cases will not match any template exactly.</p>

<p>FIC route is the right answer where the founder has at least a 10-year expected horizon during which operational control matters, the property portfolio is substantial enough (typically £2 million or more) to justify the £15,000 to £25,000 incorporation cost and £4,000 to £8,000 ongoing annual cost, the first-marriage children's interests need affirmative protection through structural means rather than goodwill, and the family is willing to have the candid second-spouse conversation at incorporation.</p>

<p>IPDI will-trust is the right answer where the founder does not need during-life operational structuring (smaller portfolio, passive management, or a founder closer to natural retirement), the spouse exemption on the founder's death is the principal tax saving needed, and the family accepts that the structure only takes effect on the founder's death.</p>

<p>Discretionary will-trust is the right answer where the family wants flexibility on distributions across multiple potential beneficiaries (the spouse, the first-marriage children, the spouse's children from prior relationships, future grandchildren) and is willing to pay the entry IHT and periodic-charge cost for that flexibility. It is often the right answer in retrospect (via a deed of variation under IHTA 1984 section 142) where the founder died without an active FIC or IPDI structure.</p>

<p>Combined FIC and IPDI is the right answer for larger blended-family wealth (typically £3 million or more across property and non-property assets) where the property is FIC-incorporated and the residual non-property estate (the founder's home, cash, non-FIC investments) sits in an IPDI under the founder's will. The combined structure is administratively heavier but delivers structural protection across both the active portfolio and the residual estate.</p>

<p>The blended-family FIC is one of the more bespoke applications of the FIC architecture and benefits materially from advice that engages with the family's specific circumstances rather than templates. The clause text examples and persona descriptions on this page are illustrative starting points; the actual settlement for any specific family needs structural design, legal drafting, and ideally the second-spouse conversation before paper is committed.</p>
