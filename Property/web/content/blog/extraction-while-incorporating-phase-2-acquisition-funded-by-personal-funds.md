  - question: "Who is the mid-incorporation cohort?"
    answer: "A landlord who has personally-held BTLs from years before the SPV was set up plus a partially-built SPV with one or two properties already incorporated. Typically the s.162 incorporation has been phased rather than done in one event, the personal-side mortgages are still in place on some properties, and the SPV is buying further properties out of its own retained earnings or new SPV-side borrowings. The cohort is distinct from the fully-incorporated landlord (everything inside the SPV, no personal-side route available) and from the pre-incorporation landlord (everything personal, no SPV yet). The phase-2-acquisition-via-personal-funds route is a route that only exists during the mid-build period."
  - question: "What is the five-step sequence at a glance?"
    answer: "Step 1: re-mortgage one of the existing personal-held properties to release equity. Step 2: use that equity to buy a new property in personal name at arm's length on the open market. Step 3: hold the new property in personal name for at least one or two rent cycles to establish the rental record. Step 4: sell the property to the SPV at market value under TCGA 1992 s.17 and s.18 (connected-persons MV deeming applies in any event). The SPV does not pay cash; the purchase price sits on the SPV's balance sheet as a director's loan owed to the landlord. Step 5: extract the DLA credit over the next 3 to 5 years tax-free via repayment of the loan. The sequence converts a personal property purchase into a tax-free extraction route, but at the cost of the SDLT charge on the SPV's acquisition leg."
  - question: "Why does SDLT s.53 set the binding constraint?"
    answer: "Because section 53 of Finance Act 2003 deems the chargeable consideration on a transaction between connected persons (or where the purchaser is a company and the vendor is connected) to be 'not less than the market value of the subject-matter of the transaction as at the effective date'. The landlord cannot sell to the SPV at a low stated price to avoid SDLT; the SPV pays SDLT on market value regardless. Layer on top the 5% additional-dwellings surcharge in Schedule 4ZA Finance Act 2003 (applicable to all SPV residential acquisitions per house position section 1, in force from 31 October 2024), and the SDLT charge on a £350,000 property becomes 5% + standard residential bands = approximately £35,000 on a typical mid-portfolio acquisition. The £35,000 is the binding constraint on whether the DLA credit strategy beats simply buying directly in the SPV in the first place."
  - question: "Doesn't buying directly in the SPV avoid the SDLT problem?"
    answer: "Not exactly. SDLT applies on any SPV-side residential acquisition with the 5% surcharge regardless of how the purchase is funded. What changes is whether the purchase generates a DLA credit (personal funds funnel through the personal-then-sell route) or not (SPV-side cash funds the purchase directly, no DLA credit created). The trade is: pay roughly £35,000 SDLT either way; if you fund through personal then sell to SPV, you also get a £350,000 tax-free DLA credit you can extract over 3 to 5 years; if you fund directly in the SPV, no DLA credit but you save the additional CGT on the personal disposal leg (typically small if you held the property briefly). Whether the trade favours the personal-then-sell route depends on the founder's marginal extraction rate on the dividend alternative."
  - question: "What CGT applies to the personal disposal?"
    answer: "TCGA 1992 sections 17 and 18 deem the disposal at market value because the landlord and the SPV are connected persons (the landlord controls the SPV). If the property was held in personal name for a short period (a few months to a couple of years), the gain is typically small and the CGT cost is contained. CGT rates from 30 October 2024 are 18% (basic-rate) and 24% (higher-rate) on residential property gains; the £3,000 annual exempt amount applies. For a property bought at £350,000 and sold to the SPV at £370,000 nine months later, the gain is £20,000 less buying costs and selling costs; net CGT is roughly £2,000 to £4,000 depending on rate band. The 60-day CGT return on UK residential disposals applies because there is a tax to pay."
  - question: "How does the DLA credit get created mechanically?"
    answer: "Through the conveyancing record itself. At completion of the sale from the landlord to the SPV, the SPV's solicitor records the £370,000 purchase price as a liability owed to the landlord on the SPV's completion statement. The SPV's accounting entry is: debit property (£370,000); credit director's loan account (£370,000). No cash leaves the SPV at completion. The landlord acquires the right to demand repayment of £370,000 from the SPV at any future date. The director's loan is a real legal debt (not a tax fiction); HMRC, the SPV's auditors (if any), the bank, and the landlord's personal creditors will all treat it as such. The credit balance shows on the SPV's balance sheet as 'creditors due within one year' or 'creditors due after one year' depending on the agreed repayment terms."
  - question: "How fast can the DLA credit be extracted?"
    answer: "As fast as the SPV has cash to repay. A £370,000 DLA credit can be repaid in one lump sum if the SPV has the cash on hand, but typically the SPV's cash is locked in property and rent flows, so the practical extraction rate is the SPV's net operating cash flow after corporation tax. For a 4-property SPV netting £40,000 a year of CT-paid rental profit, the practical extraction rate is £40,000 a year, exhausting the £370,000 credit in just over nine years. Larger SPVs or SPVs that re-mortgage the new property post-acquisition can extract faster (the re-mortgage equity is available to repay the DLA balance). Smaller SPVs cap the extraction at the cash available."
  - question: "What is the Ramsay / s.75A risk?"
    answer: "FA 2003 section 75A ('Anti-avoidance', inserted by Finance Act 2007) is the SDLT scheme-transaction rule. It applies where the sum of SDLT payable across scheme transactions is less than the SDLT on a notional direct transaction between the original vendor and the final purchaser. The mechanism: HMRC disregards the actual scheme transactions and creates a notional direct transaction between V (the original seller) and P (the SPV), then taxes on the largest consideration given or received across the scheme. For our sequence (V sells to landlord; landlord sells to SPV), section 75A could apply to recharacterise the two transactions as one direct V-to-SPV transaction. The defence is that the landlord held the property genuinely (rent collected, mortgage serviced, registered title in landlord's name for a meaningful period); the sequence has commercial substance beyond SDLT reduction. HMRC's framework for the scheme-transaction analysis is at SDLTM09050 onwards. Acquisitions where the property is bought in personal name on Monday and sold to the SPV on Friday are the classic trigger pattern."
  - question: "Does the SPV need shareholder approval to buy the property from a director?"
    answer: "Yes, under Companies Act 2006 sections 190 to 196 (substantial property transactions involving directors). Section 190 prohibits the company from acquiring a substantial non-cash asset from a director (or a person connected with a director) without the approval of the members by ordinary resolution. Section 191 sets the 'substantial' threshold: a non-cash asset is substantial if (a) its value exceeds 10% of the company's asset value AND is more than £5,000, OR (b) it exceeds £100,000. A property worth £350,000 will almost always exceed the £100,000 ceiling, so members' approval is required regardless of the SPV's asset value. The practical mechanic is a written ordinary resolution signed by the shareholders (often just the landlord, sometimes the spouse); the resolution sits on the company's books with the conveyancing file. Failure to get the approval makes the contract voidable at the company's option under section 195 (and triggers personal liability of the director under section 195(2))."
  - question: "What documentation supports the sequence at HMRC enquiry?"
    answer: "Five documents anchor the position. First, the conveyancing file for the personal-side acquisition (showing the property was bought at arm's length on the open market). Second, a rent-roll evidence pack for the personal-holding period (lease, AST, rent receipts, repairs invoices). Third, an independent valuation for the personal-to-SPV sale dated within 3 months of completion (RICS or comparable Chartered Surveyor); SDLT applies on market value under FA 2003 s.53, so the valuation is the evidence of MV. Fourth, the CA 2006 s.190 members' resolution signed before completion (not after). Fifth, the SPV's board minutes recording the acquisition and the creation of the director's loan balance. Without contemporaneous documentation, the s.75A scheme-transaction analysis sharpens and the personal-side holding period is treated as transparent."

    answer: "It depends on the founder's alternative extraction route from existing retained earnings. Route B (this page's route, personal-then-sell-to-SPV): SDLT £35,000 (same); CGT on personal disposal £3,000; DLA credit of £370,000 extractable tax-free over years. The differential is roughly £40,000+ to £100,000+ of dividend tax saved on extraction depending on marginal rates, less the £3,000 CGT cost. The strategy is attractive where the founder has substantial retained earnings or future earnings inside the SPV that would otherwise extract at higher-rate dividend cost; less attractive where the founder's overall extraction profile is at basic rate."
  - question: "What happens to the DLA credit if I sell the SPV later?"
    answer: "The DLA credit is a debt owed by the SPV to the landlord; it transfers with the sale of the SPV's shares only if the buyer agrees to take on the liability (and prices accordingly). The cleaner pattern is to extract the DLA credit fully before any share sale of the SPV, leaving the buyer with a clean balance sheet. Where the DLA credit cannot be extracted before sale (insufficient cash), the buyer typically deducts the DLA balance from the share consideration on a pound-for-pound basis, then settles the DLA from the company's cash post-completion. The DLA credit also interacts with the founder's pre-sale extraction strategy (covered in our <a href=\"/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/pre-sale-extraction-strip-cash-before-spv-share-sale-vs-buyer-discount\">pre-sale extraction page</a> for the strip-or-discount analysis)."

    answer: "An over-aggressive timeline. The landlord buys the property in personal name on day 1, signs the personal-to-SPV sale contract on day 30, and completes the transfer on day 60. <p>The mid-incorporation cohort, half-personal portfolio plus half-SPV, has an extraction route that does not exist for the fully-incorporated landlord, but the SDLT connected-company rule sets the binding constraint on how aggressive the sequence can be. Re-mortgage a personal property, buy the next property in personal name, sell it to the SPV at market value, take the SPV's payment as a director's loan account credit, and extract that credit over the next 3 to 5 years tax-free. On paper the route turns a phase-2 acquisition into a tax-free extraction vehicle. In practice, the SDLT charge under FA 2003 section 53 lands on the SPV regardless of the price stated on the conveyance, the 5% additional-dwellings surcharge stacks on top, and FA 2003 section 75A scheme-transaction recharacterisation watches the sequence if it looks contrived.</p>

<p>This page walks the five-step sequence end to end, with anonymised persona Mike (4 personally-held BTLs from 2018 to 2022 worth £1.4m gross, mortgage £750k; SPV Holdings Ltd formed January 2026 with one property already moved via s.162; intends to acquire two further properties through the personal-then-sell-to-SPV route across 2027). It sits inside the <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/extracting-cash-from-property-spv-extraction-sequence-pillar-2026-27">multi-year extraction sequence pillar</a> as the mid-build extraction lever; it forward-links the <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/btl-spv-directors-loan-repayment-strategy-tax-efficient-extraction">post-incorporation DLA repayment strategy page</a> for what happens once the DLA credit lands on the SPV's balance sheet; and it leans on the <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/directors-loan-repayment-bed-and-breakfast-trap-s464c-s464d">DLA bed-and-breakfast trap page</a> for the post-FA-2025 anti-avoidance architecture that governs DLA extraction during the credit-balance years.</p>

<h2>The five-step sequence at a glance</h2>

<p>The sequence is:</p>

<ol>
<li><strong>Re-mortgage an existing personal property</strong> to release equity. Lender consent, valuation, and product fees apply; expect 6 to 12 weeks. For Mike, re-mortgaging the Manchester two-bed (current value £280,000, current mortgage £160,000) at 65% LTV releases £22,000 of cash to bridge the deposit on the next acquisition.</li>
<li><strong>Buy the next property in personal name</strong> at arm's length on the open market. For Mike, target acquisition is a £350,000 Leeds three-bed, deposit £105,000 (30%), mortgage £245,000.</li>
<li><strong>Hold the property in personal name</strong> for a meaningful period (typically 6 to 12 months at minimum) with a real tenancy in place, rent collected through the landlord's personal account, and personal-side mortgage payments on the regular schedule. This is the commercial-substance window; cutting it short is the single most common failure mode.</li>
<li><strong>Sell the property to the SPV at market value</strong> at the end of the holding window. SDLT applies on market value under FA 2003 s.53; CGT applies on the personal disposal at MV under TCGA 1992 s.17 and s.18 (connected persons); the SPV's purchase price (let's call it £370,000 after the holding-period uplift) sits as a director's loan owed to the landlord on the SPV's balance sheet.</li>
<li><strong>Extract the DLA credit</strong> over 3 to 5 years tax-free as the SPV's operating cash flow allows. House position section 21.1 governs the mechanics; the post-FA-2025 anti-avoidance architecture for DLA extraction (covered on our DLA bed-and-breakfast trap page) governs the discipline.</li>
</ol>

<h2>The SDLT binding constraint: FA 2003 s.53 and the 5% surcharge</h2>

<p>The SDLT cost on Step 4 is the binding constraint on whether the strategy is worth running at all. Two statutory provisions stack.</p>

<p>First, <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2003/14/section/53">FA 2003 section 53 ("Deemed market value where transaction involves connected company")</a> deems the chargeable consideration on a transaction between connected persons (or where the purchaser is a company and the vendor is connected with the purchaser) to be "not less than the market value of the subject-matter of the transaction as at the effective date". The landlord cannot sell the property to the SPV at a token consideration; SDLT applies on full market value as evidenced by independent valuation.</p>

<p>Second, the 5% additional dwellings surcharge under <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2003/14/schedule/4ZA">FA 2003 Schedule 4ZA</a> (in force at 5% from 31 October 2024 per house position section 1) applies because the purchaser is a company acquiring a residential dwelling. The 5% surcharge stacks on the standard residential band rates.</p>

<p>For Mike's Leeds £370,000 purchase by the SPV, the SDLT computation is:</p>

<ul>
<li>£0 to £125,000 at 5% (standard 0% plus 5% surcharge) = £6,250</li>
<li>£125,001 to £250,000 at 7% (standard 2% plus 5% surcharge) = £8,750</li>
<li>£250,001 to £370,000 at 10% (standard 5% plus 5% surcharge) = £12,000</li>
<li><strong>Total SDLT: £27,000</strong> (7.3% effective rate on £370,000)</li>
</ul>

<p>Compare against the alternative routes: buying directly in the SPV from personal cash injection at the start = same £27,000 SDLT, no DLA credit; not buying at all = no SDLT, no DLA credit, no portfolio growth. The £27,000 SDLT is the price of building a £370,000 DLA credit; the question is whether the DLA credit's tax-free extraction value beats the SDLT cost plus the personal-side CGT cost on the disposal.</p>

<aside><p>Get the SDLT computation done at the planning stage.</p><p>The 5% surcharge stack varies sharply by property value band. A £190,000 acquisition incurs roughly £14,000 SDLT; a £550,000 acquisition incurs roughly £45,000. The DLA-credit strategy crosses break-even at different points depending on the founder's marginal extraction rate. Property Tax Partners runs the break-even computation against the founder's actual rate profile as part of mid-incorporation planning; if you would like one, the form below routes to the right team.</p></aside>

<h2>CGT on the personal disposal: s.17 and s.18 MV-deeming</h2>

<p>The personal-side CGT is normally a small cost because the holding period is short and the gain is contained. TCGA 1992 sections 17 and 18 deem the disposal at market value (rather than at the consideration recorded on the conveyance) because the landlord and the SPV are connected persons; the landlord cannot sell at cost to avoid CGT. CGT rates from 30 October 2024 are 18% (basic-rate band) and 24% (higher-rate band) on residential property gains; the £3,000 annual exempt amount applies for 2026/27.</p>

<p>For Mike, the Leeds property was bought for £350,000 in March 2027 and sold to the SPV at £370,000 in December 2027. Selling costs (legal, agent, valuation) are roughly £4,500. The gain computation:</p>

<ul>
<li>Disposal proceeds (MV per s.17/18): £370,000</li>
<li>Less acquisition cost: £350,000</li>
<li>Less buying costs (SDLT, legal): £14,000 (approximate)</li>
<li>Less selling costs: £4,500</li>
<li>Gross gain: £1,500</li>
<li>Less annual exempt amount: £3,000 (capped at the gain)</li>
<li><strong>Net gain: £0; CGT cost £0</strong></li>
</ul>

<p>If the property had appreciated significantly (say a £40,000 gain on a £350,000 acquisition during a strong market), the gain after costs and AEA would be roughly £30,000; CGT at 24% higher rate is £7,200. The 60-day CGT return on UK residential disposals applies because there is a tax to pay (HMRC's residential property capital gains tax service).</p>

<h2>DLA credit creation and downstream extraction</h2>

<p>At completion of the personal-to-SPV sale, the SPV's accounting entry is:</p>

<ul>
<li>Debit: Property (fixed assets) £370,000</li>
<li>Credit: Director's loan account (creditor) £370,000</li>
</ul>

<p>No cash leaves the SPV at completion. The £370,000 sits as a real legal debt owed by Holdings Ltd to Mike. The landlord acquires the right to demand repayment at any future date; in practice, the repayment schedule is informal, governed by the SPV's cash position and Mike's extraction needs.</p>

<p>Per house position section 21.1, DLA credit-balance repayment is tax-free (it is repayment of debt, not income). Mike can draw cash from the SPV up to the credit balance without triggering any personal tax charge; the SPV records each drawdown as a debit to the loan account. The credit balance falls to zero over the extraction window.</p>

<p>The downstream extraction mechanics are covered on our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/btl-spv-directors-loan-repayment-strategy-tax-efficient-extraction">DLA repayment strategy page</a> (which walks the post-incorporation extraction at depth) and on our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/directors-loan-repayment-bed-and-breakfast-trap-s464c-s464d">DLA bed-and-breakfast trap page</a> (which covers the post-FA-2025 anti-avoidance architecture once the credit balance is drawn down to zero and the founder transitions to debit-balance discipline).</p>

<h2>The SPV's CGT base-cost step-up at acquisition</h2>

<p>An understated benefit of the personal-then-sell-to-SPV route is the SPV's CGT base-cost on the acquired property. Because FA 2003 s.53 and TCGA 1992 s.17 / s.18 deem the transfer at market value, the SPV picks up the property at the £370,000 MV transfer price as its CGT base cost, not at Mike's original £350,000 cost. Any future SPV-level disposal (a sale to a third party, an inter-group transfer, an MVL distribution in specie) computes the SPV's gain from the £370,000 base, not from Mike's earlier purchase price.</p>

<p>The base-cost step-up is small for a short-hold acquisition (Mike's £20,000 holding-period uplift adds only £20,000 of SPV base cost, saving roughly £5,000 of future SPV-level CGT at 25% main rate). It is more significant where the personal-holding period is longer, or where the property is held through a period of strong market appreciation: a £350,000 acquisition held personally for 3 years and sold to the SPV at £450,000 gives the SPV a £100,000 base-cost step-up, saving £25,000 of future SPV-level CGT on disposal.</p>

<p>The trade-off is the personal-side CGT cost on the larger gain at the transfer point. For the £100,000 uplift held over 3 years, the personal-side gain is £100,000 less buying / selling costs, less annual exempt amounts; CGT at 24% higher rate is roughly £22,000. Personal-side CGT (£22,000) versus SPV-side CGT saved later (£25,000) is a wash for many founders, but the timing differential matters: personal CGT is paid now, SPV CGT is deferred to disposal date. Cash-flow planning often favours the deferred position.</p>

<h2>Companies Act approval: ss.190-196 substantial property transactions</h2>

<p>The personal-to-SPV sale is a "substantial property transaction" under Companies Act 2006 Part 10 Chapter 4. Section 190 prohibits a company from acquiring a substantial non-cash asset from a director (or a person connected with a director) without members' approval by ordinary resolution. Section 191 sets the "substantial" threshold: a non-cash asset is substantial if either (a) its value exceeds 10% of the company's asset value AND exceeds £5,000, OR (b) its value exceeds £100,000.</p>

<p>For Mike's Leeds £370,000 acquisition, the value exceeds £100,000, so members' approval is required regardless of the SPV's asset value. The practical mechanic is a written ordinary resolution signed by the shareholders before completion (often just the landlord, sometimes spouse or family co-shareholders); the resolution sits on the company's statutory books with a copy on the conveyancing file.</p>

<p>The price of getting this wrong is in section 195: the contract is voidable at the company's option, and the director is personally liable for any loss the company suffers and accountable for any gain made. Practical risk: a future SPV sale where the new shareholders discover the s.190 resolution was missing or post-dated, and use the s.195 voidability as leverage in the share sale negotiation.</p>

<h2>The s.75A scheme-transaction risk</h2>

<p>The contrived-sequence guard sits at <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2003/14/section/75A">FA 2003 section 75A ("Anti-avoidance")</a>, inserted by Finance Act 2007. Section 75A applies where multiple transactions are arranged such that the sum of SDLT payable across the scheme transactions is less than the SDLT on a notional direct transaction between the original vendor (V) and the final purchaser (P). When triggered, HMRC disregards the actual transactions and creates a notional V-to-P direct transaction, taxing on the largest consideration given or received across the scheme.</p>

<p>For our sequence (V sells to landlord; landlord sells to SPV), section 75A could in principle apply to recharacterise the two transactions as one direct V-to-SPV transaction. The SDLT charge on the recharacterised V-to-SPV transaction is the same as the SDLT actually paid by the SPV in Step 4, so on the SDLT analysis nothing is gained or lost. But the CGT consequences shift: if the personal-side holding is recharacterised as transparent, the personal-side CGT relief on the small disposal disappears, and the personal-side mortgage interest deduction during the holding period is disallowed.</p>

<p>The defence requires genuine commercial substance during the personal-holding period:</p>

<ul>
<li>A meaningful holding period (6 to 12 months minimum; longer is stronger).</li>
<li>A real tenant under a real lease; rent collected in the landlord's personal account.</li>
<li>Personal-side mortgage payments made on the normal schedule, not bridge-style.</li>
<li>A documented reason for the original personal-name purchase (the SPV lacked credit history, the buying window was tight, the lender required personal-name borrowing).</li>
<li>An RICS-qualified independent valuation supporting the personal-to-SPV transfer price.</li>
</ul>

<p>HMRC's framework for the s.75A scheme-transaction analysis is at SDLTM09050 onwards in the SDLT Manual. The classic trigger pattern is acquisitions where the property is bought in personal name on Monday and sold to the SPV on Friday: there is no commercial substance to the personal-name hold, the entire sequence is SDLT-driven (and in fact does not reduce SDLT because of s.53), but the CGT and DLA-credit consequences are heavily contrived.</p>

<h2>Worked sequence for Mike: full P&L</h2>

<p>Mike's complete economic position from the Leeds £370,000 phase-2 acquisition:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Personal-side outflows:</strong> deposit £105,000 (from re-mortgage equity + savings), buying costs £14,000 (SDLT, legal, survey), holding-period net rental contribution £1,500 (rent £14,000 less mortgage interest £9,000 less letting agent £1,400 less repairs £2,100), selling costs £4,500.</li>
<li><strong>Personal-side inflow at SPV sale:</strong> £370,000 (recorded as DLA credit, not cash).</li>
<li><strong>SDLT cost (paid by SPV):</strong> £27,000.</li>
<li><strong>CGT on personal disposal:</strong> £0 (small gain absorbed by AEA).</li>
<li><strong>DLA credit created on SPV's balance sheet:</strong> £370,000.</li>
<li><strong>Tax-free extraction value of DLA credit over 5 years (if SPV cash flow supports):</strong> £370,000.</li>
</ul>

<p>Compare against the alternative (Mike injects £370,000 personal cash into the SPV as share capital, SPV buys the property directly):</p>

<ul>
<li>SDLT cost: £27,000 (same).</li>
<li>CGT on personal disposal: not applicable (no personal disposal).</li>
<li>DLA credit created: zero.</li>
<li>Tax-free extraction value: zero.</li>
<li>Future extraction of the £370,000 share capital: requires dividend extraction at 10.75% / 35.75% rates from post-CT profits, marginal cost £40,000 to £130,000+ depending on Mike's rate profile.</li>
</ul>

<p>The DLA-credit route is structurally better by the amount of dividend tax saved over the extraction window, less the £14,000 personal-side buying costs and the personal-side holding-period operational complexity. For Mike (higher-rate, expecting £40,000 a year of dividend extraction otherwise), the saving is roughly £80,000 to £100,000 of dividend tax across the 5-year DLA window.</p>

<h2>Failure modes</h2>

<p>The recurring failure patterns:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Personal-holding period too short.</strong> Property bought and sold within 60 to 90 days, no rent collected, no commercial substance. HMRC's s.75A analysis collapses the two transactions into one; personal-side CGT relief and mortgage interest deduction are disallowed.</li>
<li><strong>Members' resolution post-dated or missing.</strong> The CA 2006 s.190 resolution is signed weeks after completion or never. Contract voidable at the SPV's option under s.195; director personally liable.</li>
<li><strong>No independent valuation.</strong> Personal-to-SPV transfer price set by the founder; HMRC challenges market value under FA 2003 s.53 and assesses higher SDLT plus penalties.</li>
<li><strong>DLA credit balance treated as personal cash by the SPV's bank.</strong> A future SPV refinance is rejected because the lender treats the DLA balance as a demand-due liability; the SPV's gearing computation includes the DLA balance and breaches loan-to-value covenants.</li>
<li><strong>Sequence repeated too aggressively.</strong> Three or four properties run through the personal-then-sell route in quick succession; HMRC's s.75A enquiry framing sharpens because the pattern looks contrived.</li>
<li><strong>SDLT computation done on stated consideration.</strong> Conveyancer assumes the £370,000 stated price is the SDLT base; in fact FA 2003 s.53 applies on market value, which can be higher. SDLT under-paid; HMRC discovery assessment lands later with interest and penalties.</li>
</ul>

<p>The discipline that prevents each pattern is the same: 6+ month personal-holding period with real rent and real evidence; CA 2006 s.190 resolution signed pre-completion; RICS independent valuation; spaced acquisitions rather than rapid sequence; SDLT computed on MV per s.53, not on stated price.</p>

<h2>When the strategy does and does not pay</h2>

<p>The personal-then-sell-to-SPV route is structurally most attractive in three configurations:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Founder is consistently higher-rate or additional-rate.</strong> Dividend extraction from the SPV otherwise costs 35.75% (higher) or 39.35% (additional) per house position section 21.4. A DLA credit of £370,000 extracted tax-free over 5 years saves roughly £130,000 of dividend tax against the higher-rate alternative; the £14,000 of personal buying costs and small CGT cost are easily justified.</li>
<li><strong>SPV has reliable operating cash flow to fund the extraction.</strong> A 4-to-6 property SPV netting £40,000 to £60,000 of CT-paid rental profit a year can absorb £40,000 to £50,000 of DLA repayment without straining liquidity. SPVs whose cash is locked in fresh acquisitions or value-add refurbishments are weak candidates because the extraction rate is capped at the cash available.</li>
<li><strong>Founder has financing capacity to bridge the personal-side acquisition.</strong> The strategy requires Mike to be the borrower of record on the personal mortgage at acquisition; lenders apply their own affordability tests on rental yield, employment income, and existing personal debt. Founders already over-leveraged personally cannot reach the personal-name purchase step; the strategy never starts.</li>
</ul>

<p>Conversely, the strategy underperforms in three configurations:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Founder is consistently basic-rate.</strong> Dividend extraction at 10.75% basic rate is cheap; the DLA credit's tax-free advantage is worth only £40,000 over 5 years, possibly less than the £14,000 of personal-side buying costs after accounting for personal-side operational complexity (separate tax returns for the holding period, personal mortgage administration, void-period risk).</li>
<li><strong>SPV is cash-constrained.</strong> A young SPV with capital tied up in deposits and refurbishments cannot repay the DLA credit; the credit sits unused for years, eroding its present value to the founder.</li>
<li><strong>Personal-side property market is appreciating fast.</strong> A £100,000 holding-period uplift triggers £22,000 of personal-side CGT now in exchange for £25,000 of deferred SPV-side CGT later; the cash-flow cost can outweigh the deferral benefit if the founder needs liquidity in the personal-tax year of the transfer.</li>
</ul>
