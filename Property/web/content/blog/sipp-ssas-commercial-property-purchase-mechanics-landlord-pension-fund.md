  - question: "Why are SIPPs and SSASs the principal pension vehicles for commercial property?"
    answer: "Because both are investment-regulated pension schemes where the member can direct, influence, or advise on the investment decisions. FA 2004 Schedule 29A applies the taxable-property regime to investment-regulated schemes specifically; non-investment-regulated schemes (typical occupational defined-benefit or defined-contribution schemes where investment is at trustee discretion) are NOT within the Sch 29A regime. The structural implication is that SIPPs and SSASs uniquely give the member operational control over property selection while operating within a tax-favoured pension wrapper. The trade-off is that the same direct-control feature triggers Sch 29A; if the member directs the scheme to acquire residential property, the unauthorised payment charges bite at approximately 55% to 70% effective rate. The regime is designed to channel pension-fund property investment into commercial property only."
  - question: "What is the FA 2004 Sch 29A taxable-property regime?"
    answer: "Schedule 29A FA 2004 (inserted by Finance Act 2006 effective 6 April 2006) lists categories of property that, if held by an investment-regulated pension scheme, trigger unauthorised payment charges on the member. The principal categories: residential property (buildings suitable for use as a dwelling, including parts of buildings, gardens, attached land, hotel accommodation, beach huts; UK AND overseas residential property both within scope); tangible movable property subject to Treasury restrictions (art, classic cars, vintage wine, antiques, jewellery). Where an investment-regulated scheme acquires taxable property, the member is treated as having received an unauthorised payment equal to the property's market value. The unauthorised payment triggers: 40% income tax under FA 2004 s.208; potential 15% surcharge if total unauthorised payments in a tax year exceed 25% of the member's pension fund; 15% scheme sanction charge under FA 2004 s.209. The combined effective rate of 55% to 70% makes residential property investment via SIPP/SSAS structurally prohibitive."
  - question: "Why is commercial property excluded from taxable property?"
    answer: "Sch 29A para 7 explicitly excludes business premises (offices, warehouses, retail units, factories, mixed-use buildings where the commercial element dominates), agricultural land, and forestry from the taxable-property definition. The policy rationale: pension-fund investment in productive commercial assets is supported (encourages business investment, productive use of pension capital); pension-fund investment in residential property is not (would distort the residential market and remove housing stock from owner-occupier supply). The exclusion is statutorily explicit, not a matter of HMRC discretion. Commercial property purchases by SIPP/SSAS are therefore free of the Sch 29A unauthorised payment charge regardless of the property's value, location, or other characteristics, provided the building is genuinely commercial."
  - question: "What is an in-specie contribution and how does it work?"
    answer: "An in-specie contribution is the transfer of an existing asset (typically commercial property) from a member to their pension scheme by way of contribution, rather than the more common cash contribution. The mechanics: the member legally transfers the commercial property to the SIPP/SSAS trustees; the transfer is recorded at market value at the date of contribution; the market value counts as a contribution for tax purposes, attracting income tax relief at the member's marginal rate under FA 2004 ss.188 to 189 (subject to annual allowance and other limits). CGT treatment: TCGA 1992 ss.62 to 188 provide no-gain-no-loss treatment on the transfer (the pension scheme takes the member's base cost; no CGT crystallises on the contribution). The route is useful for business owners who already hold business premises personally and want to move the property into their pension fund without selling first. The member can then lease the property back to their trading company at full market rent, generating rental income inside the pension fund (tax-exempt) and creating a deductible rent expense in the trading company."
  - question: "What is the SSAS member-loan facility and what are its conditions?"
    answer: "An SSAS (Small Self-Administered Scheme) can lend up to 50% of its net asset value to the sponsoring employer (typically the member's trading company) subject to four mandatory conditions. First: maximum loan amount of 50% of the SSAS's net asset value at the date of the loan. Second: maximum loan period of 5 years. Third: market-rate interest (at least 1% above Bank of England base rate, with HMRC monitoring against commercial benchmarks). Fourth: first-charge security over an asset of equal value to the loan (the security can be over property, equipment, or other tangible assets but must rank first ahead of other creditors). Plus: equal instalment repayment (each loan repayment must equal each of the others, no balloon payments, no deferred-interest-only periods). Breach of any condition treats the loan as an unauthorised payment, triggering the 55% to 70% effective penalty. SIPPs do NOT have an equivalent member-loan facility; SIPP cannot lend to the member, the member's connected persons, or the member's trading company under any circumstances (FA 2004 s.179)."

  - question: "Does the FA 2024 lump-sum-allowance framework affect SIPP property purchases?"
    answer: "Yes, in two specific ways. The Finance Act 2024 abolished the Lifetime Allowance (LTA, previously £1,073,100) with effect from 6 April 2024 and replaced it with two new allowances: the lump-sum allowance (LSA) of £268,275 (capping tax-free lump sums on retirement) and the lump-sum-and-death-benefit allowance (LSDBA) of £1,073,100 (capping tax-favoured lump-sum death benefits).   - question: "What rent must a connected tenant pay on a commercial property held by their SIPP?"
    answer: "Full market rent. Where a SIPP holds commercial property and leases it to a connected party (typically the member's trading company), HMRC requires the rent to be set at full market value with a formal lease document, regular review provisions, and arm's-length terms. Below-market rent triggers a connected-party benefit that may be treated as an unauthorised payment under FA 2004; depending on the size of the under-rent, the penalty can be material. Above-market rent risks HMRC challenge as a routing of additional contributions through the rent mechanism (Sch 36 FA 2008 anti-avoidance and the standard ITTOIA 2005 transfer-pricing principles). Sessions advising on connected-party leases should commission an independent rent valuation at the start of the lease and at each rent review (typically every 5 years on commercial leases). The valuation provides the evidence basis for HMRC enquiry. The trading company on the tenant side gets a deductible rent expense at the market figure; the SIPP receives the rent tax-free; the net family-economic outcome moves rental cashflow from the company's chargeable profit into the tax-favoured pension wrapper."
  - question: "Can a SIPP or SSAS borrow to acquire property?"
    answer: "Yes, up to 50% of the scheme's net asset value at the time of borrowing. FA 2004 imposes the 50% gearing limit on registered pension schemes. The scheme can take out a commercial mortgage to acquire commercial property where the loan does not exceed 50% of the scheme's net asset value at the date of the loan. Lenders specialising in SIPP/SSAS commercial mortgages (typically smaller commercial banks and specialist lenders) understand the structure. The interest on the borrowing is a deductible expense for the scheme (reducing the scheme's tax-exempt rental income calculation, though as the income is tax-exempt the deduction does not save tax directly); the principal repayments come from the rental income and any member contributions. Breach of the 50% gearing limit triggers the unauthorised payment charge regime. Sessions advising on SIPP/SSAS commercial property acquisitions with borrowing should run the 50% test at the date of borrowing and document the calculation; subsequent fluctuations in the scheme's net asset value (caused by other investments) do not re-trigger the test."
  - question: "How does the FA 2024 pension-IHT reform affect SIPP property planning?"
    answer: "Finance Act 2024 brought pensions into the IHT estate from 6 April 2027, reversing the previous position where most pension funds passed to beneficiaries free of IHT. For SIPP commercial property held within the wrapper, the structural implications are material. Pre-6-April-2027 deaths: the SIPP's value (including the commercial property) typically passes to nominated beneficiaries outside the deceased member's IHT estate, often with favourable income-tax treatment if the death is before age 75. Post-6-April-2027 deaths: the SIPP's value is brought into the deceased member's IHT estate; if the combined estate exceeds NRB plus available transferable / residence allowances, IHT at 40% applies to the SIPP value too. The reform substantially changes the SIPP commercial property planning calculus: previously, holding commercial property in SIPP delivered both income-tax-side benefits (tax-exempt rental income) AND IHT-side benefits (outside the estate); from April 2027, only the income-tax-side benefit remains, with the IHT-side benefit lost. Sessions advising landlord families on SIPP commercial property should integrate the FA 2024 reform into the planning conversation, particularly for older members where the post-April-2027 timeline is more directly relevant."
  - question: "What is the difference between SIPP and SSAS for commercial property purposes?"
    answer: "Three structural differences. First, ownership and trustees. A SIPP is a personal pension scheme with one or more administrators (typically a specialist SIPP provider) and one or more members; the member is the beneficial owner of their share. A SSAS is an occupational scheme typically established by a small business to provide pensions for its directors; the scheme has multiple members (the directors) plus trustees drawn from the membership; the SSAS structure is more bespoke. Second, member-loan facility. SIPP cannot make member loans or loans to connected persons; SSAS can lend to the sponsoring employer up to 50% of net asset value subject to the four conditions. Third, governance and administration cost. SIPP administration is typically £500 to £1,500 per year via specialist providers; SSAS administration is £2,000 to £5,000 per year due to the bespoke trustee structure but supports multi-member arrangements within a single scheme. For commercial property purposes, SIPPs are more common (simpler administration; suit single-owner businesses); SSASs work better for multi-director companies (multiple members share a single scheme; member-loan facility provides corporate-finance flexibility). The choice typically follows the broader business structure."
  - question: "What happens if the property changes use from commercial to residential during scheme ownership?"
    answer: "The Sch 29A regime applies at the time of acquisition AND on a continuing basis. Where commercial property held by a SIPP/SSAS subsequently changes use to residential (a change of planning consent followed by physical conversion, or a redesignation of mixed-use property where the residential element becomes dominant), the property becomes taxable property within Sch 29A and the unauthorised payment charge regime activates. The scheme administrator must monitor the property's use status throughout the holding period; major refurbishment, planning-application changes, or operational use shifts can trigger Sch 29A. The defensive operational approach: include change-of-use restrictions in the lease (tenant must obtain trustee consent for any change affecting the property's commercial classification); the trustees can refuse consent where the change would trigger Sch 29A. Where the change is unavoidable (e.g. property in an area being progressively rezoned to residential), the trustees may need to dispose of the property before the change crystallises, accepting commercial-grade sale proceeds rather than the residential-conversion uplift the broader market might suggest."
  - question: "Can a SIPP / SSAS hold a mixed-use property where part is residential and part commercial?"
    answer: "Subject to careful analysis under Sch 29A. The mixed-use case turns on which element dominates. Where the commercial element clearly dominates (a freehold property with ground-floor retail unit plus single residential flat above where the retail represents 70%+ of value and rental income), the property may fall within the commercial-property exclusion at Sch 29A para 7. Where the residential element dominates (a freehold where the residential represents 60%+ of value), the entire property may be taxable. The mid-range (50/50 split) is uncertain and HMRC enquiry risk is high. Sessions advising on mixed-use SIPP/SSAS acquisitions need to commission an independent valuation showing the split, plus a tax analysis confirming the commercial-property classification. The defensive approach is to structure the acquisition so that the residential element is separated (sub-divided title, separate freeholds) and only the commercial element is acquired by the pension scheme; the residential element can be acquired separately by the member personally or by a residential-focused vehicle. The structural complexity is significant but worthwhile for genuine mixed-use opportunities."
  - question: "What records should a SIPP / SSAS retain on commercial property holdings?"
    answer: "Five categories of contemporaneous records. First, acquisition documentation: purchase contract, completion statement, SDLT Land Transaction Return, scheme trustee resolutions approving the acquisition, valuation report at acquisition date. Second, lease documentation: full lease document with rent review provisions, market-rent valuation at lease start, market-rent valuations at each rent review, evidence of rent payments received. Third, property classification evidence: planning consents demonstrating commercial use, business rates assessments showing commercial classification, any change-of-use notifications and trustee responses. Fourth, ongoing operational records: maintenance and repair invoices (treated as deductible expenses for the scheme); insurance policies; any tenant correspondence regarding use of the property. Fifth, valuation history: periodic valuations (typically every 3 to 5 years) showing the property's value progression; this supports both the scheme's annual reporting and any subsequent in-specie or sale transactions. Where all five categories are well-maintained, the scheme is well-positioned for any HMRC enquiry into Sch 29A compliance; gaps in documentation create exposure that becomes apparent only on enquiry years after the original transactions."

<p>Self-Invested Personal Pensions and Small Self-Administered Schemes give pension-fund members operational control over property selection within a tax-favoured wrapper. The constraint is FA 2004 Schedule 29A: residential property held by an investment-regulated pension scheme triggers unauthorised payment charges at approximately 55% to 70% effective rate, making residential investment effectively prohibitive within these structures. Commercial property is excluded from the taxable-property regime under <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2004/12/schedule/29A">Sch 29A para 7</a> and remains the workhorse use case for SIPP / SSAS property investment.</p>

<p>This page walks the taxable-property regime mechanics, the in-specie contribution route, the SSAS member-loan structure (with its four mandatory conditions), the connected-party rent requirements for lease-to-own-business arrangements, and the post-FA-2024 lump-sum-allowance architecture replacing the abolished Lifetime Allowance. The reference scenario is the <strong>Patel-business SIPP</strong>, an anonymised composite of an engineering business owner moving a personally-held commercial unit into his SIPP via in-specie contribution and leasing it back to his trading company at market rent. The broader pension-IHT context is shaped by the Finance Act 2024 reforms bringing pensions into the IHT estate from 6 April 2027 (see our <a href="/blog/landlord-tax-essentials/iht-clt-property-discretionary-trust-20-percent-entry-charge">IHT-side companion pages</a> for the wider estate-planning architecture).</p>

<h2>The Sch 29A taxable-property regime and why residential triggers the 55% to 70% rate</h2>

<p>Schedule 29A FA 2004 was inserted by Finance Act 2006 effective 6 April 2006 to channel pension-fund property investment away from residential property. The regime applies only to investment-regulated pension schemes (where the member can direct, influence, or advise on investment decisions); typical occupational defined-benefit or defined-contribution schemes where investment is at trustee discretion are NOT within the regime.</p>

<p>Where an investment-regulated scheme acquires taxable property, the member is treated as having received an unauthorised payment equal to the property's market value. Three charges stack:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>40% unauthorised payment charge under FA 2004 s.208.</strong> The member pays income tax at 40% of the unauthorised payment value.</li>
<li><strong>15% unauthorised payment surcharge under FA 2004 s.208.</strong> Applies where the total unauthorised payments in a tax year exceed 25% of the member's pension fund.</li>
<li><strong>15% scheme sanction charge under FA 2004 s.209.</strong> Imposed on the scheme administrator, recoverable from the member in practice via reduction in the member's pension benefits.</li>
</ul>

<p>The combined effective rate of 55% (where the surcharge does not apply) to 70% (where the surcharge does apply) makes residential property investment via SIPP / SSAS structurally prohibitive. Sessions advising on pension-fund property investment should rule out residential acquisitions immediately as a starting point.</p>

<p>Commercial property exclusion. <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2004/12/schedule/29A">Sch 29A para 7</a> explicitly excludes business premises (offices, warehouses, retail units, factories), agricultural land, and forestry from the taxable-property definition. SIPP / SSAS can acquire commercial property freely subject to the standard pension-scheme rules; no Sch 29A charge applies. The exclusion is statutorily explicit, not a matter of HMRC discretion.</p>

<aside><p>The residential-property prohibition is absolute in practice.</p><p>SIPPs and SSASs holding residential property face the 55% to 70% combined penalty rate; the structure is fundamentally incompatible with residential investment. Landlord families exploring pension-funded property acquisition should anchor on commercial property only. Property Tax Partners runs SIPP / SSAS commercial property scoping calls for business owners considering the route. The form below routes to the pension-tax team.</p></aside>

<h2>The in-specie contribution route: moving an existing commercial property into your pension</h2>

<p>An in-specie contribution is the transfer of an existing commercial asset from a member to their pension scheme by way of contribution. The mechanics are well-established but the tax-side detail is operationally important.</p>

<p><strong>Transfer mechanic.</strong> The member legally transfers the commercial property to the SIPP / SSAS trustees. The transfer is recorded at market value at the date of contribution. The market value counts as a contribution for tax purposes.</p>

<p><strong>Income tax relief.</strong> The member receives income tax relief on the market value contribution at their marginal rate under FA 2004 ss.188 to 189, subject to the annual allowance (currently £60,000 for the relevant tax year) plus the 3-year carry-back rule (the member can carry back unused annual allowance from the three preceding tax years).</p>

<p><strong>CGT treatment.</strong> TCGA 1992 ss.62 to 188 provide no-gain-no-loss treatment on the transfer. The pension scheme takes the member's base cost; no CGT crystallises on the contribution. The latent gain is rolled into the pension wrapper where it can subsequently grow free of CGT.</p>

<p><strong>SDLT treatment.</strong> The contribution is a connected-party transfer under FA 2003 s.53; SDLT applies at market value on the non-residential rates table. For commercial property the non-residential rates cap is 5% above £250,000; the SDLT cost on a £450,000 commercial unit (the Patel-business scenario) is approximately £12,000.</p>

<h2>The Patel-business worked example</h2>

<p>Mr Patel runs Patel-Engineering Ltd, a trading company operating from a freehold commercial unit in Birmingham. The unit was acquired personally by Mr Patel in 2018 for £280,000 plus £8,000 SDLT plus £4,000 fees (total base cost £292,000). Current market value 2026 is £450,000 (latent gain £158,000). Mr Patel has a SIPP with £380,000 of accumulated fund. He wants to move the commercial unit into the SIPP via in-specie contribution.</p>

<p><strong>Transaction architecture.</strong> In-specie contribution of the unit at market value £450,000. The transfer is documented with a contribution agreement; trustees take legal title to the freehold; the contribution is recorded against Mr Patel's member account.</p>

<p><strong>Income tax relief.</strong> The £450,000 deemed contribution attracts income tax relief at Mr Patel's marginal rate (40%) for the portion within the annual allowance. Annual allowance for the current tax year: £60,000. Carry-back: assume £80,000 of unused annual allowance from prior years available. Total relief available: £140,000 at 40% = £56,000 of tax relief. The £310,000 excess contribution is not relievable in the current cycle but does build pension fund value; future years' annual allowances can absorb part of the excess.</p>

<p><strong>CGT.</strong> No-gain-no-loss treatment under TCGA 1992 ss.62 / 188. SIPP takes the £292,000 base cost. Mr Patel pays no CGT on the £158,000 latent gain. The gain is preserved inside the pension wrapper and can be realised tax-free on future SIPP sale of the property.</p>

<p><strong>SDLT.</strong> Connected-party transfer at market value £450,000 on non-residential rates: £0 to £150,000 at 0% = £0; £150,000 to £250,000 at 2% = £2,000; £250,000 to £450,000 at 5% = £10,000; total SDLT £12,000. Payable by the SIPP (typically from Mr Patel's member contribution or other scheme funds).</p>

<p><strong>Ongoing operation.</strong> Patel-Engineering Ltd leases the unit from the SIPP at market rent (independently valued at £40,000 per year). The lease is a formal commercial lease with 5-yearly rent reviews. The rent is a deductible expense for Patel-Engineering (saving £10,000 of corporation tax at 25%) and is received by the SIPP tax-free. Over a 15-year holding period, the SIPP receives £600,000 of cumulative tax-free rent; Patel-Engineering saves £150,000 of corporation tax; the unit appreciates from £450,000 to (say) £675,000 with the £225,000 capital growth occurring tax-free inside the wrapper.</p>

<h2>The SSAS member-loan facility: 50% of net asset value, 5 years, first-charge security</h2>

<p>An SSAS (Small Self-Administered Scheme) can lend to the sponsoring employer subject to four mandatory conditions under FA 2004. The facility does NOT exist for SIPPs; SIPP cannot lend to the member, the member's connected persons, or the member's trading company under any circumstances (FA 2004 s.179).</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Loan amount cap: 50% of net asset value.</strong> The loan cannot exceed 50% of the SSAS's net asset value at the date of the loan. The net asset value is calculated against the scheme's total assets less liabilities at that date; valuations of property and other illiquid assets should be supported by independent reports.</li>
<li><strong>Maximum 5-year loan period.</strong> The loan must be repayable within 5 years; longer-term loans are not permitted. The 5-year clock runs from the date the loan is advanced.</li>
<li><strong>Market-rate interest.</strong> Interest rate at least 1% above Bank of England base rate. HMRC monitors against commercial benchmarks; rates that are clearly below market invite challenge as a connected-party benefit.</li>
<li><strong>First-charge security.</strong> The SSAS must take first-charge security over an asset of equal value to the loan. The security can be over property, equipment, or other tangible assets but must rank first ahead of other creditors. Floating charges or unsecured loans are not permitted.</li>
</ul>

<p>Additional condition: equal instalment repayment. Each loan repayment must equal each of the others; no balloon payments, no deferred-interest-only periods. Breach of any condition treats the loan as an unauthorised payment, triggering the 55% to 70% effective penalty.</p>

<p>Practical use case. SSAS member-loans are widely used by family-business SSASs to fund corporate-finance needs (working capital, equipment purchase, expansion plans) without involving external lenders. The structure delivers tax-favoured returns to the pension scheme (the interest is taxable on the scheme but the scheme is broadly tax-exempt on most income) while providing the trading company with internal financing.</p>

<h2>Connected-party leases and the market-rent requirement</h2>

<p>Where a SIPP or SSAS holds commercial property and leases it to a connected party (typically the member's trading company), HMRC requires the rent to be set at full market value. Three conditions support the connected-party lease.</p>

<p>First, a formal lease document. The lease should be drafted by a commercial property solicitor with standard market terms including rent review clauses, repair obligations, insurance arrangements, and assignment / sub-letting provisions. The lease should not include unusual concessions to the tenant (rent-free periods, capped service charges, sub-market initial rent) without specific commercial justification.</p>

<p>Second, market-rent valuation evidence. An independent rent valuation at the start of the lease establishes the market figure. Subsequent valuations at each rent review (typically every 5 years on commercial leases) document the rent progression. Without independent valuations, HMRC can substitute its own view on enquiry, typically setting a higher rent and treating the differential as a connected-party benefit.</p>

<p>Third, arm's-length operational practice. Rent payments should run through commercial banking channels with appropriate documentation; the tenant should treat the rent as a deductible expense in the standard way; the trustees should treat receipts as rental income in the standard way. Informal arrangements (rent waived, deferred, or set off against other amounts) invite HMRC challenge.</p>

<h2>FA 2024 lump-sum-allowance architecture and its impact on SIPP property planning</h2>

<p>Finance Act 2024 abolished the Lifetime Allowance (LTA, previously £1,073,100) with effect from 6 April 2024 and replaced it with two new allowances:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Lump-sum allowance (LSA): £268,275.</strong> Caps the total tax-free lump sums a member can take on retirement. The pension commencement lump sum (PCLS, traditionally 25% of the fund up to the LTA) is now capped at the LSA value.</li>
<li><strong>Lump-sum-and-death-benefit allowance (LSDBA): £1,073,100.</strong> Caps the total tax-favoured lump-sum death benefits payable from a member's pension funds.</li>
</ul>

<p>For SIPP commercial property purposes, two implications follow. First, the in-specie contribution mechanic continues to operate under the annual allowance framework (£60,000 per year plus 3-year carry-back). Large contributions exceeding the annual allowance can still be made but trigger the annual allowance charge on the excess. Sessions writing on in-specie contributions of high-value commercial property need to model the annual allowance interaction carefully; for property values exceeding £200,000 to £300,000, the contribution may exceed available annual allowance and the excess sits outside the relief.</p>

<p>Second, the LSDBA caps the tax-favoured death-benefit treatment of large pension funds. SIPPs with substantial commercial property value at member death may exceed the £1,073,100 LSDBA, triggering income tax on the excess for the beneficiaries. The structural implication is that very large SIPP funds (often built up over decades of business owner contributions plus property appreciation) lose some of their post-death tax efficiency under the FA 2024 architecture; the previous LTA-driven framework had different (and in some ways more generous) outcomes.</p>

<p>Sessions writing on SIPP commercial property planning must use the FA 2024 architecture, not the pre-2024 LTA framework which is no longer in force.</p>

<h2>The Finance Act 2024 pension-into-IHT-from-April-2027 reform</h2>

<p>Finance Act 2024 also brought pensions into the IHT estate from 6 April 2027, reversing the previous position where most pension funds passed to beneficiaries free of IHT. For SIPP commercial property held within the wrapper, the structural implications are material.</p>

<p>Pre-6 April 2027 deaths. The SIPP's value (including the commercial property) typically passes to nominated beneficiaries outside the deceased member's IHT estate. Income tax treatment on the death benefits depends on the member's age at death: deaths before age 75 typically pass tax-free; deaths from 75 onwards pass with income tax at the beneficiary's marginal rate.</p>

<p>Post-6 April 2027 deaths. The SIPP's value is brought into the deceased member's IHT estate. Where the combined estate exceeds the available NRB plus residence allowance plus transferable allowances, IHT at 40% applies to the SIPP value too. Combined with the income tax treatment on the underlying death benefits, the post-2027 tax cost on transferring a substantial SIPP to beneficiaries can reach 50% to 60% of the fund value.</p>

<p>The reform substantially changes the SIPP commercial property planning calculus. Previously, holding commercial property in SIPP delivered both income-tax-side benefits (tax-exempt rental income during ownership) AND IHT-side benefits (outside the estate on death). From April 2027 only the income-tax-side benefit during ownership remains; the IHT-side benefit on death is lost. The structure remains attractive for the in-life tax efficiency but the death-planning calculus needs revisiting. Sessions advising landlord families on SIPP commercial property should integrate the FA 2024 reform into the planning conversation, particularly for older members where the post-April-2027 timeline is directly relevant.</p>

<h2>SIPP versus SSAS: choosing the right vehicle</h2>

<p>Three structural differences determine the choice between SIPP and SSAS for commercial property purposes.</p>

<p>First, governance. SIPP is a personal pension administered by a specialist provider with one member; SSAS is an occupational scheme with up to typically 11 members (the directors of the sponsoring company) and bespoke trustee arrangements.</p>

<p>Second, member-loan facility. SIPP cannot make member loans or loans to connected persons; SSAS can lend to the sponsoring employer up to 50% of net asset value subject to the four conditions discussed above.</p>

<p>Third, administration cost and complexity. SIPP administration typically £500 to £1,500 per year via specialist providers; SSAS administration £2,000 to £5,000 per year due to the bespoke trustee structure.</p>

<p>For commercial property purposes, SIPPs work well for single-owner businesses (one director, simple structure, no need for member-loan facility). SSASs work better for multi-director companies (multiple members share a single scheme; member-loan facility provides internal financing flexibility for the sponsoring trading company). The choice typically follows the broader business structure; multi-director family-businesses with corporate finance needs default to SSAS, single-owner-operator businesses default to SIPP.</p>

<h2>Borrowing within the scheme and the 50% gearing limit</h2>

<p>FA 2004 imposes a 50% gearing limit on registered pension schemes. The scheme can take out a commercial mortgage to acquire commercial property where the loan does not exceed 50% of the scheme's net asset value at the date of the loan. Lenders specialising in SIPP / SSAS commercial mortgages (typically smaller commercial banks and specialist lenders) understand the structure and will lend on appropriate terms.</p>

<p>The interest on the borrowing is a deductible expense for the scheme. As the scheme's underlying income (rental from the commercial property) is tax-exempt, the deduction does not save tax directly; the principal repayments come from the rental income and any further member contributions. Breach of the 50% gearing limit triggers the unauthorised payment charge regime, so the scheme must monitor the loan-to-net-asset-value ratio at the date of any further borrowing.</p>

<p>Practical use case: a £400,000 commercial unit acquired with £200,000 cash from the SIPP and £200,000 mortgage from a commercial lender. The 50% gearing test is met at acquisition. The unit generates £30,000 per year of rental income; the rent covers mortgage interest plus principal repayment plus a margin for scheme expenses. Over 10 to 15 years the mortgage is repaid from the rental stream; at the end of the term the SIPP owns the unit outright and the rental income flows entirely to the scheme.</p>

<h2>Mixed-use property and the change-of-use risk</h2>

<p>Mixed-use property (ground-floor retail with residential above; office block with caretaker flat; light-industrial site with on-site dwelling) requires careful Sch 29A analysis at acquisition. The property is examined as a whole: where the commercial element clearly dominates by value and rental income (typically 70% or more), the property may fall within the commercial-property exclusion at Sch 29A para 7 even with some residential component. Where the residential element dominates (60% or more), the entire property may be taxable. The mid-range (50/50 split) is uncertain and HMRC enquiry risk is high.</p>

<p>The defensive operational approach for genuinely mixed-use opportunities: structure the acquisition so that the residential element is separated by title (sub-divided freehold, separate leases) and only the commercial element is acquired by the pension scheme; the residential element is acquired separately by the member personally or by a residential-focused vehicle. The structural complexity is significant but supports a clean Sch 29A position.</p>

<p>Change-of-use during ownership creates an ongoing monitoring requirement. Where commercial property held by a SIPP / SSAS subsequently changes use to residential (a change of planning consent followed by physical conversion, or a redesignation where the residential element becomes dominant), the property becomes taxable property and the unauthorised payment charge regime activates. The defensive approach: include change-of-use restrictions in the lease (tenant must obtain trustee consent for any change affecting the property's commercial classification); the trustees can refuse consent where the change would trigger Sch 29A. Where the change is unavoidable, the trustees may need to dispose of the property before the change crystallises.</p>
