<p>Principal Private Residence Relief (PRR) under sections 222 to 226 of the Taxation of Chargeable Gains Act 1992 exempts the proportion of a property's chargeable gain attributable to periods when the property was the owner's only or main residence, plus the final 9 months of ownership. For landlords, the typical PRR case is the 'accidental landlord' fact pattern: the property was the owner's main home for some period, then was let to tenants, then is sold.</p>

<p>The relief framework rewards the actual-residence proportion of ownership and adds two structural extensions: the final-9-months rule (which always applies once the property has at some point been a main residence) and the deemed-occupation rules under s.223(3) (which preserve PRR over absences for work or other reasons). Lettings Relief under section 223B, inserted by Finance Act 2020 with effect from 6 April 2020, can further extend the cover in narrow shared-occupation cases. This guide sets out the framework, the load-bearing rules and the common scenarios.</p>

For the rates picture see the <a href="/blog/capital-gains-tax/cgt-rates-property-2026-27-current-rates-explained">2026/27 CGT rates page</a>. For 60-day reporting after the disposal see the <a href="/blog/capital-gains-tax/cgt-payment-deadlines-property-sales-2026">CGT payment deadlines guide</a>.</p>

<h2>The section 222 framework</h2>

<p><a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12/section/222">Section 222 TCGA 1992</a> sets out the core relief. The exempt gain is the proportion attributable to periods when the property (or part of it) was the owner's only or main residence, computed under the time-apportionment formula. The framework is supplemented by sections 223 (final period and deemed occupation), 223B (Lettings Relief), 224 (apportionment for mixed use), 225 to 226 (specific scenarios including trust property, dependent relatives, job-related accommodation).</p>

<p>The key statutory tests run together: was the property actually the owner's main residence at some point (s.222(1)); is the gain attributable to a qualifying period (s.222 + s.223); does Lettings Relief extend the cover (s.223B); does the half-hectare permitted area apply or does a larger area qualify on a 'reasonable enjoyment' basis (s.222(2) + (3)); is part of the property excluded by exclusive business use (s.224); does the couple's election or the s.225B post-separation provision apply.</p>

<h2>Eligibility and the only-or-main-residence test</h2>

<p>The property must have been the owner's only or main residence at some point during ownership. A property that was always a BTL from the date of acquisition does not attract PRR at any percentage. The qualifying threshold is binary, not graduated: the property either was a main residence at some point or it was not.</p>

<p>HMRC's working position on the quality-of-occupation test (set out at CG64427 onwards) follows Goodwin v Curtis [1998] 73 TC 478: the question is whether the occupation has 'some degree of permanence, some degree of continuity or some expectation of continuity'. Brief stays without the intention to settle, even where the owner has slept in the property and received post, may not qualify. The factors HMRC weighs include:</p>

<ul>
<li>Actual moving-in (furniture, belongings, household effects)</li>
<li>Council tax registration in the owner's name at the address</li>
<li>Utility accounts (gas, electricity, water) in the owner's name</li>
<li>Electoral roll registration at the address</li>
<li>Personal correspondence directed to the address (bank, employer, GP, dentist)</li>
<li>Duration of the move (weeks vs months vs years)</li>
<li>Whether the owner has another residence available</li>
</ul>

<p>The property must be in the UK or, where outside the UK, the owner must satisfy specific s.222B residence-conditions for the relief to apply. Most landlord-facing PRR work is on UK-situate property.</p>

<h2>The time-apportionment formula</h2>

<p>PRR is computed on a time-apportionment basis:</p>

<p><strong>PRR exempt amount = (qualifying period / total ownership period) × total chargeable gain</strong></p>

<p>Where:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Qualifying period</strong> = actual occupation as main residence + deemed-occupation periods (s.223(3)) + the final 9 months (s.223(1))</li>
<li><strong>Total ownership period</strong> = the gap between acquisition and disposal, measured in months</li>
</ul>

<p>The formula operates on the total chargeable gain (sale proceeds minus base cost minus allowable costs). The exempt amount reduces the chargeable gain. Any remaining gain after PRR and Lettings Relief is then reduced by the £3,000 AEA before CGT applies at 18% or 24%.</p>

<h2>The final 9 months rule (section 223(1))</h2>

<p><a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12/section/223">Section 223(1) TCGA 1992</a> deems the last 9 months of ownership to be occupation as a main residence, provided the property was at some point actually the owner's main residence. The 9-month rule applies regardless of actual occupation during those final months. The current 9-month figure has been in force since 6 April 2020.</p>

<h2>Deemed occupation: when absence still qualifies (section 223(3))</h2>

<p><a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12/section/223">Section 223(3) TCGA 1992</a> deems certain periods of actual absence to count as occupation for PRR purposes, subject to a bookend requirement: the property must have been a main residence both BEFORE and AFTER the absence (or, in some cases, the owner had to be prevented from returning by reasons covered by the same provision). The three categories:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Any-reason absence (s.223(3)(a)): up to 3 years in aggregate over ownership.</strong> Multiple shorter absences (a year here, two years there) can be combined up to the 3-year ceiling. The bookend rule applies: the owner must have actually returned to use the property as main residence after the absence.</li>
<li><strong>Work-abroad absence (s.223(3)(b)): no length limit during periods of employment or self-employment outside the UK.</strong> A landlord seconded overseas for a five-year contract who lets the property in the meantime can preserve PRR on the entire absence provided the bookend rule is met (actually returned and resumed main-residence use, or unable to return for reasons recognised by HMRC).</li>
<li><strong>Work-UK-elsewhere absence (s.223(3)(c)): up to 4 years where the owner's UK work location made it impractical to occupy the property.</strong> Typical case: a teacher posted to a school 100+ miles away with employer-provided accommodation. As with the other two categories, the bookend rule applies.</li>
</ul>

<p>The three categories are cumulative: a landlord can have all three apply across a long ownership period, building substantial deemed-occupation cover. The conditions in detail and the bookend interpretation are in HMRC manuals CG65000 to CG65040. The relevant case-law authorities include Owen v Elliot [1990] STC 469 on the genuineness of occupation for PRR.</p>

<h2>Worked time-apportionment example</h2>

<p>Mark and Sarah, jointly owning a property in Leeds that they bought in March 2010 for £200,000 plus £1,500 SDLT, £800 legal fees and £400 survey costs. They lived in the property as their main home from March 2010 until February 2016 (6 years, 72 months). They then bought a larger family home and let the Leeds property to tenants from March 2016 onwards. They had no shared occupation with the tenants (they had moved out). The property is sold in March 2026 for £450,000 with £8,000 in disposal costs.</p>

<p><strong>Step 1: total chargeable gain</strong></p>

<ul>
<li>Net sale proceeds: £442,000 (£450,000 − £8,000)</li>
<li>Base cost: £202,700 (£200,000 + £1,500 + £800 + £400)</li>
<li>Total chargeable gain: £239,300</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Step 2: PRR computation</strong></p>

<ul>
<li>Total ownership period: 16 years (192 months)</li>
<li>Actual occupation as main residence: 72 months (March 2010 to February 2016)</li>
<li>Final 9 months: already covered by the 192-month total (months 184 to 192)</li>
<li>Qualifying period: 72 + 9 = 81 months</li>
<li>PRR exempt amount: 81/192 × £239,300 = £100,949</li>
<li>Residual chargeable gain after PRR: £239,300 − £100,949 = £138,351</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Step 3: Lettings Relief check</strong></p>

<ul>
<li>The tenants were not in shared occupation with Mark and Sarah during the letting period (Mark and Sarah had moved to a different property). Lettings Relief under post-2020 s.223B does NOT apply.</li>
<li>(Pre-2020 framing would have allowed up to £40,000 of Lettings Relief here. That framework was repealed by FA 2020. The disposal in March 2026 falls under post-2020 rules even though the letting started in 2016.)</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Step 4: split between spouses and apply AEAs</strong></p>

<ul>
<li>Mark's half-share of residual gain: £69,175</li>
<li>Sarah's half-share of residual gain: £69,175</li>
<li>Less Mark's £3,000 AEA: £66,175 taxable</li>
<li>Less Sarah's £3,000 AEA: £66,175 taxable</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Step 5: rate application</strong></p>

<p>Assume Mark earns £75,000 of employment income in 2026/27 (higher-rate throughout for his gain) and Sarah earns £30,000 (basic-rate with £20,270 of unused basic-rate band).</p>

<ul>
<li>Mark: £66,175 × 24% = £15,882</li>
<li>Sarah: £20,270 × 18% + £45,905 × 24% = £3,649 + £11,017 = £14,666</li>
<li>Combined household CGT: £30,548</li>
</ul>

<p>Without PRR (hypothetical), the £239,300 chargeable gain would have produced a CGT bill in the region of £55,000-£57,000. PRR has saved £24,000-£26,000 in CGT on a single disposal. The split-ownership structure preserves both AEAs and uses Sarah's basic-rate band, saving a further £1,200 versus a single-owner higher-rate computation.</p>

<h2>The half-hectare permitted area (section 222(2))</h2>

<p><a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12/section/222">Section 222(2)(b) TCGA 1992</a> sets the default permitted area for PRR purposes at 0.5 hectares (approximately 1.24 acres), including the site of the dwelling-house itself. Within the permitted area, PRR covers the house and the garden / grounds on the same basis.</p>

<p>Where the actual garden and grounds exceed 0.5 hectares, the excess does NOT automatically attract PRR. The owner must demonstrate, under s.222(3), that the additional area is 'required for the reasonable enjoyment of the dwelling-house as a residence, having regard to the size and character of the dwelling-house'. The test is objective (what is reasonable for this house) not subjective (what the owner happened to enjoy).</p>

<p>The leading case is Longson v Baker [2001] STC 6: a country house with 7.5 acres failed the 'reasonable enjoyment' test on the basis that the additional acreage was not objectively required for a house of that size and character. Country-house owners disposing of properties with extensive grounds, paddocks, stables or amenity land typically need a fact-specific argument and often need formal valuation evidence apportioning the gain between the dwelling-and-permitted-area and the excess. HMRC's working position is in CG64815-CG64835.</p>

<h2>Mixed use and exclusive business use (section 224)</h2>

<p><a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12/section/224">Section 224 TCGA 1992</a> restricts PRR where part of the dwelling-house is used 'exclusively for the purposes of a trade, business or profession'. The exemption is apportioned to remove the exclusively-business-use portion.</p>

<p>The 'exclusively' qualifier is the load-bearing test. A dedicated home office that is never used for any personal purpose (no family use, no guest use, no overnight stays) is excluded from PRR. A study or home office that doubles as a guest bedroom, family computer room or homework space is not 'exclusively' business and remains within PRR.</p>

<p>The mechanic is a common trap for self-employed home-workers who claim 100% of a room as a business expense for income tax purposes during ownership: doing so signals to HMRC that the room is exclusively business and triggers s.224 on disposal. The income tax saving during ownership (a few hundred pounds a year) is usually small compared to the CGT cost on disposal (a percentage of the property gain). Most home-office claims should use a fair-and-reasonable apportionment that preserves dual-use status (e.g., 75% business / 25% personal) rather than 100% business.</p>

<p>HMRC's position on dual-use rooms and the s.224 boundary is at CG64690 to CG64710.</p>

<h2>Lettings Relief: shared-occupation only since April 2020 (section 223B)</h2>

<p><a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12/section/223B">Section 223B TCGA 1992</a> was inserted by Finance Act 2020 with effect from 6 April 2020 and replaced the pre-2020 Lettings Relief framework. The change was substantial: the relief that previously applied broadly to the let portion of a former main residence (without any requirement for the owner to share occupation with the tenant) is now restricted to live-in landlord arrangements.</p>

<h3>The post-2020 rule</h3>

<p>Lettings Relief under s.223B applies where, during the let period, the owner shared occupation of the dwelling-house with the tenant. The typical case is a Rent-a-Room style arrangement (owner lives in the property, lets one or more rooms to lodgers with shared common areas like kitchen, bathroom, living room). The owner-and-tenant must have shared the dwelling-house as a single household for at least part of the let period.</p>

<p>The maximum relief is the lower of three amounts under s.223B(4):</p>

<ul>
<li>(a) the PRR already given on the disposal</li>
<li>(b) £40,000</li>
<li>(c) the chargeable gain on the let portion (the portion of the gain attributable to the let period, after PRR has been applied)</li>
</ul>

<p>For a couple jointly owning, each spouse can claim up to £40,000 of Lettings Relief on their share of the gain, subject to the lower-of-three test on each spouse's computation.</p>

<h3>What does NOT qualify</h3>

<p>The typical accidental-landlord scenario (owner moves out, lets the former home to tenants, no shared occupation) does NOT attract Lettings Relief under the post-2020 rules. The cut-off applies to the disposal date, not the letting start date, per HMRC manual <a href="https://www.gov.uk/hmrc-internal-manuals/capital-gains-manual/cg64710">CG64710</a>. A 2018-to-2026 letting on a 2026 disposal gets no Lettings Relief on the entire let period unless shared occupation applied throughout.</p>

<p>This is the canonical post-2020 reframe that catches most accidental landlords by surprise. Pre-2020 advice (the "£40,000 if main residence at some point + later let" framework) is wrong for any post-2020 disposal. The specialist depth is in the <a href="/blog/capital-gains-tax/letting-relief-landlords-2026-changes">Lettings Relief specialist guide</a>.</p>

<h2>The one-main-residence rule for couples (section 222(6))</h2>

<p><a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12/section/222">Section 222(6) TCGA 1992</a> provides that spouses and civil partners living together can have only one main residence between them for PRR purposes. The s.222(5)(a) nomination election lets the couple choose which property is the main residence where they have two or more residences available. The nomination must be made within 2 years of any change in the available residences, signed by both spouses (s.222(6)(a)), and can be varied by further written notice.</p>

<p>For unmarried cohabitees, the one-residence rule does not apply: each cohabitee can have their own main residence, which can be different properties.</p>

<p>For couples with a London weekday flat and a country weekend cottage (or similar two-residence pattern), the nomination election controls which property attracts PRR on eventual disposal. Without an election, HMRC assesses the main residence on the facts (where the couple actually spends most time, where the family is based, where they are registered for services). Getting the election right and refreshing it after any change in available residences is the load-bearing planning point.</p>

<p>The full mechanics of the s.222(5) election are in the <a href="/blog/capital-gains-tax/cgt-main-residence-election-two-properties">two-properties election guide</a>. The joint-ownership PRR mechanics on a single shared residence (where both spouses have a beneficial interest and the s.222(6) rule means they can only have one main residence between them) are in the <a href="/blog/capital-gains-tax/cgt-main-residence-relief-joint-ownership-pra-election-spouses-mechanics">joint-ownership PRR mechanics guide</a>.</p>

<h2>PRR and the separating-spouse extension (section 225B)</h2>

<p><a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12/section/225B">Section 225B TCGA 1992</a> deems the absent spouse's continued occupation of a former family home where the home is transferred under a court order or separation agreement, preserving PRR for that spouse on the eventual sale. The provision is the structural complement to the s.222 main-residence framework for separating spouses: it stops the absent spouse losing PRR cover simply because they had to move out.</p>

<p>The provision was widened by Finance (No. 2) Act 2023 c.30 s.41(2)(6), in force from 6 April 2023. The widening extended the period over which inter-spouse no-gain-no-loss transfers under section 58 TCGA 1992 can be made post-separation: from the previous tax-year-of-separation limit, to the end of the third tax year after separation (or until any court order, if earlier).</p>

<p>The combined effect: a separated spouse leaving the family home can:</p>

<ul>
<li>Retain PRR cover under s.225B on the eventual sale of the family home, even though they no longer actually occupy it.</li>
<li>Transfer the home to the resident spouse under s.58 no-gain-no-loss for a meaningfully longer window (up to ~3.5 years from separation in some cases).</li>
<li>Use the combined cover to manage the CGT position on the eventual disposal of the family home, often as part of a financial settlement.</li>
</ul>

<p>The full divorce-and-CGT scenario depth, including the financial-settlement-driven planning sequence, is in the <a href="/blog/capital-gains-tax/cgt-divorce-property-transfer-tax-implications">divorce CGT guide</a>. The s.58 spouse-transfer mechanics generally are in the <a href="/blog/capital-gains-tax/cgt-property-transfer-spouse">spouse transfer guide</a>.</p>

<h2>Other reliefs interaction</h2>

<p>PRR sits alongside other CGT reliefs and allowances. The interaction points landlords most often face:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>£3,000 annual exempt amount:</strong> applies to the residual chargeable gain after PRR and Lettings Relief. Each spouse has their own AEA. See the <a href="/blog/capital-gains-tax/cgt-annual-exempt-amount-3000-allowance-2026-27">AEA depth guide</a>.</li>
<li><strong>Capital losses:</strong> brought-forward losses (claimed within four years per TMA 1970 s.43) can be set against the residual taxable gain after PRR and AEA. See the <a href="/blog/capital-gains-tax/cgt-property-sold-loss-claim-capital-losses">capital losses guide</a>.</li>
<li><strong>Former Furnished Holiday Let property:</strong> FHL was abolished from 6 April 2025 by Finance Act 2025 Sch 5. Former FHL property is now treated as standard residential rental for CGT purposes. PRR can still apply where the former FHL was at some point the owner's main residence; the FHL status itself does not change the PRR analysis. Business Asset Disposal Relief at the former 10% rate is closed for post-5-April-2025 FHL disposals. The grandfathered position is in the Wave 6 <a href="/blog/capital-gains-tax/fhl-capital-allowances-post-april-2025-grandfathered-claims-mechanics">FHL grandfathered claims guide</a>.</li>
<li><strong>Reduce-CGT planning levers generally:</strong> PRR is one of the ten reduction levers covered in the <a href="/blog/capital-gains-tax/reduce-cgt-property-disposal-uk">reduce CGT survey</a>.</li>
<li><strong>Rollover relief (s.152):</strong> not generally available on a PRR-eligible property because residential occupation is not 'trade use' under s.155 Class 1 Head A. See the <a href="/blog/capital-gains-tax/rollover-relief-property-landlords">rollover relief guide</a>.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Reporting after the disposal and records to retain</h2>

<p>Where PRR fully covers the chargeable gain, no CGT is due and (for UK residents) no 60-day CGT on UK property return is required. The disposal still appears on the SA108 capital gains pages of the Self Assessment return if the seller is otherwise within Self Assessment, with the PRR figure shown.</p>

<p>Where PRR partially covers the gain and CGT is still payable, the 60-day return is required (UK residents) within 60 days of completion. The PRR figure is shown on the 60-day return, and the residual taxable gain attracts CGT at the relevant rate. Non-UK residents must file the 60-day return on any UK land disposal regardless of whether tax is due. Full 60-day mechanics are in the <a href="/blog/capital-gains-tax/cgt-payment-deadlines-property-sales-2026">60-day CGT deadlines guide</a>.</p>

<p>Records to retain for a PRR claim:</p>

<ul>
<li>Original purchase contract, completion statement, SDLT return and receipt, purchase legal invoices</li>
<li>Sale contract, completion statement, estate agent invoice, legal invoice</li>
<li>Evidence of the residence period: council tax records, utility bills, electoral roll entries, GP / dentist registration changes, employer correspondence to the address</li>
<li>Evidence of any deemed-occupation period: employer letters confirming overseas posting or UK work location, written confirmation of intention to return</li>
<li>For Lettings Relief claims (post-2020 shared occupation): tenancy agreements showing shared common areas, utility bills covering the whole property in the owner's name, council tax for the whole property in the owner's name (HMO licensing typically not required at the shared-occupation scale)</li>
<li>For mixed-use s.224 claims (where part is exclusively business): clear documentation of which space is business and the time period</li>
</ul>

<p>HMRC standard retention is 22 months after the end of the tax year for non-business taxpayers and five years and 10 months for business taxpayers. In practice retain for at least six years after disposal, and longer where there is any unusual feature in the PRR computation.</p>
