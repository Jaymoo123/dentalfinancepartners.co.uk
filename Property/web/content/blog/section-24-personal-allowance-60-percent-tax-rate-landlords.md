<p>Section 24 doesn't just restrict mortgage interest relief — it can trigger a hidden tax trap that pushes landlords into a brutal 60% marginal tax rate. When <strong>section 24 personal allowance</strong> interactions push your total income above £100,000, you start losing your personal allowance pound-for-pound, creating one of the UK's harshest tax bands.</p>
<p>This isn't a minor technical issue. For landlords with significant mortgage borrowing, Section 24 can artificially inflate your taxable income by tens of thousands, pushing you well into the personal allowance taper zone and creating an effective tax rate of 60% on income between £100,000 and £125,140.</p>

<h2>How Section 24 Inflates Your Taxable Income</h2>
<p>Before Section 24, landlords could deduct mortgage interest as a business expense, reducing their taxable rental income. Now, mortgage interest is added back to your income, then given as a basic rate (20%) tax credit.</p>
<p>Take a landlord with £60,000 rental income and £30,000 mortgage interest:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Pre-Section 24:</strong> Taxable rental income = £30,000 (£60k - £30k)</li>
<li><strong>Post-Section 24:</strong> Taxable rental income = £60,000, with £6,000 tax credit (20% of £30k)</li>
</ul>
<p>Section 24 has increased this landlord's reported income by £30,000. If they have other income sources, this artificial inflation can easily push them over the £100,000 personal allowance threshold.</p>

<h2>The Personal Allowance Trap and the 60% Marginal Rate</h2>
<p>Once your total income exceeds £100,000, you lose £1 of personal allowance for every £2 of income above this threshold. The personal allowance completely disappears at £125,140 (2026/27 figures). This creates an effective marginal tax rate of 60% on income between £100,000 and £125,140:</p>
<ul>
<li>40% higher rate tax on the income itself</li>
<li>Plus 20% tax on the income that would have been covered by the lost personal allowance</li>
<li>Total: 60% marginal rate</li>
</ul>
<p>For landlords, the problem is that Section 24 can push you into this band even when your actual cash profit doesn't justify such a high tax rate. The cruel irony is that Section 24 can push landlords into the 60% bracket without improving their actual cash position. The mortgage interest you're paying hasn't changed — but your taxable income has been artificially inflated.</p>

<h2>Real-World Example: The 60% Marginal Rate Landlord</h2>
<p>Sarah owns three BTL properties and works as a consultant. Here's how Section 24 creates her tax nightmare:</p>
<p><strong>Sarah's Income (2026/27):</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Employment income: £55,000</li>
<li>Rental income: £48,000</li>
<li>Mortgage interest: £20,000</li>
<li>Other rental expenses: £8,000</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Initial Tax Calculation:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Total taxable income: £95,000 (£55k + £48k - £8k)</li>
<li>Section 24 tax credit: £4,000 (20% of £20k mortgage interest)</li>
<li>Personal allowance: Full £12,570 (income under £100k)</li>
</ul>
<p>Sarah pays higher rate tax but keeps her personal allowance. Her effective tax rate on the rental profit (£20,000 after all expenses) is manageable.</p>
<p>Now imagine Sarah's rental income increases to £68,000 due to rent increases:</p>
<ul>
<li>Total taxable income: £115,000 (£55k + £68k - £8k)</li>
<li>Personal allowance: £5,070 (£12,570 - [(£115k - £100k) ÷ 2])</li>
<li>Section 24 tax credit: £4,000</li>
</ul>
<p>Sarah now faces the <strong>60% marginal rate</strong> on £15,000 of her income. The extra £20,000 rental income has pushed her into one of the UK's harshest tax brackets, largely due to Section 24's artificial income inflation.</p>
<p><strong>Pre-Section 24 (same landlord as above):</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Taxable rental income: £40,000 (£68k - £20k interest - £8k expenses)</li>
<li>Total taxable income: £95,000</li>
<li>Personal allowance: Full £12,570</li>
<li>No 60% marginal rate</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Post-Section 24:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Taxable rental income: £60,000 (£68k - £8k expenses)</li>
<li>Total taxable income: £115,000</li>
<li>Personal allowance: Reduced to £5,070</li>
<li>60% marginal rate on £15,000</li>
</ul>
<p>The landlord's actual rental profit is identical, but <a href="/blog/section-24-and-tax-relief/section-24-tax-relief-complete-guide">Section 24</a> has created a £20,000 artificial income increase that triggers the personal allowance taper.</p>

<h2>Planning Strategies for the 60% Marginal Rate</h2>
<h3>Pension Contributions</h3>
<p>Annual allowance pension contributions can reduce your adjusted income for personal allowance purposes. Contributing £15,000 to a pension in the example above would bring the landlord back under £100,000, restoring the full personal allowance. This effectively gives relief at 60% (the marginal rate saved) rather than the normal 40% higher rate relief.</p>
<h3>Incorporation Planning</h3>
<p>Moving properties into a limited company structure eliminates Section 24 restrictions entirely. <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/buy-to-let-limited-company-complete-guide-uk">Limited companies</a> can still deduct mortgage interest as a business expense, avoiding the artificial income inflation that triggers personal allowance loss. However, incorporation has other costs and complexities, including Stamp Duty Land Tax on the transfer and potential restriction of certain reliefs.</p>
<h3>Income Timing and Spreading</h3>
<p>Where possible, consider timing strategies:</p>
<ul>
<li>Delaying rental increases to spread income across tax years</li>
<li>Timing major repairs and maintenance to maximise deductions</li>
<li>Consider whether joint ownership with a spouse in a lower tax band might help</li>
</ul>

<h2>Other Hidden Costs of High Income</h2>
<p>Crossing £100,000 doesn't just trigger personal allowance loss. Other thresholds to consider:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Child Benefit:</strong> High Income Child Benefit Charge applies from £60,000+</li>
<li><strong>Tax-Free Childcare:</strong> Eligibility ends at £100,000</li>
<li><strong>Student Loan:</strong> Plan 2 repayments at 9% above £27,295</li>
<li><strong>Marriage Allowance:</strong> Lost if either spouse earns over £50,270</li>
</ul>
<p>Combined, these can create effective marginal rates well above 60% for affected landlords.</p>

<h2>The 2027 Property Income Tax Changes</h2>
<p>From April 2027, property income will be taxed at separate rates (22%/42%/47% instead of 20%/40%/45%). However, the personal allowance taper rules remain unchanged, so the 60% marginal rate trap will still exist for landlords pushed over £100,000 by Section 24. The interaction may become even more complex, as the specific calculation of adjusted income for personal allowance purposes may need clarification under the new property tax regime.</p>
