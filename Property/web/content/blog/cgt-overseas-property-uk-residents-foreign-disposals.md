<p>This page is for UK tax residents who own property located outside the UK and are considering selling it, or who have recently sold. The question this page answers is: how does UK capital gains tax work on an overseas property disposal? This is the <strong>opposite</strong> situation from a non-UK resident selling UK property. If you are a non-UK resident selling a UK property, <a href="/blog/non-resident-landlord-tax/non-resident-cgt-selling-uk-property-overseas-guide">see our non-resident CGT guide instead</a> as completely different rules apply.</p>

<p>The technical content most readers miss: the FX conversion rule that requires two different spot rates (one for purchase, one for sale), the fact that the 60-day UK property return does <strong>not</strong> apply to overseas disposals, and how Foreign Tax Credit Relief under TIOPA 2010 interacts with a UK CGT bill. This guide covers all three, with a worked example based on a Spanish apartment disposal, and a clear reporting roadmap via SA108 and the foreign supplementary pages.</p>

<p>For the broader CGT framework, see our <a href="/blog/capital-gains-tax/capital-gains-tax-property-complete-guide-uk">CGT on property: complete guide</a>.</p>

<h2>When UK CGT applies to overseas property</h2>

<p>UK tax residents are subject to capital gains tax on <em>worldwide</em> gains. This is the basic rule under TCGA 1992 s.1(1). If you are UK tax resident in the year you sell an overseas property, the gain falls within the UK CGT net regardless of where the property is located, what currency the transaction is in, or whether the overseas country has also charged its own CGT on the same gain.</p>

<p>The UK residence test that determines whether you are within scope is the Statutory Residence Test (SRT) introduced by Finance Act 2013. If you pass the SRT as UK resident in the tax year of disposal, your worldwide gains are taxable in the UK.</p>

<p>Common examples that catch people out:</p>

<ul>
  <li>A Spanish or French holiday home owned for many years and sold during a period of UK residence</li>
  <li>An inherited apartment in Portugal, Dubai, or the USA sold after probate is complete</li>
  <li>A buy-to-let property purchased overseas and later sold at a profit</li>
  <li>A main home in another country sold after the owner returned to the UK</li>
</ul>

<p>In all these cases, UK CGT applies. The fact that the overseas country has also taxed the gain does not eliminate the UK charge. Instead, Foreign Tax Credit Relief (covered below) prevents genuine double taxation by giving a credit for the overseas tax paid.</p>

<h2>Calculating the gain: allowable expenditure</h2>

<p>The gain is calculated using the same framework as for UK property under TCGA 1992 s.38 and s.41, which apply equally to overseas assets.</p>

<p>The gain is broadly:</p>

<p><strong>Disposal proceeds minus acquisition cost minus allowable expenditure</strong></p>

<p>Allowable expenditure includes:</p>

<ul>
  <li>The original purchase price (including any purchase taxes such as transfer tax or stamp duty paid in the overseas country)</li>
  <li>Legal and professional fees on acquisition</li>
  <li>Enhancement expenditure (capital improvements to the property, not repairs)</li>
  <li>Incidental costs of disposal (estate agent or notary fees, legal fees on the sale)</li>
</ul>

<p>Revenue repairs and maintenance are not allowable for CGT purposes (though they may be deductible against rental income if the property was let). Enhancement expenditure must be capital in nature and still reflected in the state of the property at disposal.</p>

<h2>FX conversion: the rule that trips up most computations</h2>

<p>Because the transaction is in a foreign currency, every figure in the CGT computation must be converted to sterling before the gain can be computed. The rule on how to do this conversion is one of the most misunderstood points in overseas property CGT.</p>

<p><strong>The correct rule:</strong> each amount converts to sterling at the exchange rate on the date that amount was incurred or received. This is established by two cases confirmed by HMRC at CG78310:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Bentley v Pike [1981] 53 TC 590</strong>: each foreign-currency amount converts at the spot rate on the transaction date.</li>
  <li><strong>Capcount Trading v Evans [1992] 65 TC 545</strong>: confirmed Bentley v Pike; the gain must not be computed in foreign currency and then converted at a single rate.</li>
</ul>

<p>In practice this means:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Acquisition cost: convert at the spot rate on the date of completion of the purchase</li>
  <li>Enhancement costs: convert each item at the spot rate on the date it was paid</li>
  <li>Disposal proceeds: convert at the spot rate on the date of completion of the sale</li>
  <li>Incidental disposal costs: convert at the spot rate on the date each cost was paid</li>
</ul>

<p>The critical consequence: the sterling gain is almost always different from the foreign-currency gain expressed at a single rate. If sterling weakened against the foreign currency between your purchase and sale, the sterling gain will be <em>higher</em> than the gain measured purely in the overseas currency. This embedded FX component is fully taxable. There is no FX exemption.</p>

<aside>
<p><strong>Common error to avoid:</strong> computing the entire gain in euros (or dollars, or dirhams) and then converting the net gain to sterling at a single rate is wrong in law per Bentley v Pike and HMRC CG78310. The figure it produces can be significantly lower than the correct sterling computation, particularly where the pound has weakened since you bought. HMRC will recompute using the correct two-rate method. Always use transaction-date spot rates for each individual amount.</p>
</aside>

<h2>Worked example: Spanish apartment disposal with FTCR</h2>

<p>Marcus is a UK tax resident. He bought a Spanish apartment in June 2015 for EUR 200,000. He sold it in September 2026 for EUR 320,000. He paid estate agent fees in Spain of EUR 9,600.</p>

<p>The disposal is reported on SA108 and the foreign supplementary pages (SA106) in the annual Self Assessment return. The 60-day UK property return does <strong>not</strong> apply (it applies only to UK land). The deadline for Marcus is 31 January 2028 (31 January following the end of the 2026/27 tax year).</p>

<h2>Annual Exempt Amount</h2>

<p>For 2024/25 onwards, the Annual Exempt Amount (AEA) is GBP 3,000 per individual per tax year. It is deducted from the total chargeable gains before tax is calculated. Where overseas disposals are combined with UK disposals in the same tax year, all gains and losses are pooled together first, then the AEA is applied once to the net result.</p>

<p>Spouses and civil partners each have their own AEA. Transfers between spouses are at no gain/no loss for CGT, so restructuring ownership between spouses before a disposal can, in some cases, use both AEAs and reduce the combined liability.</p>

<h2>Foreign Tax Credit Relief under TIOPA 2010</h2>

<p>Where the overseas country has charged its own CGT (or equivalent) on the gain, the UK gives credit for that foreign tax under the Foreign Tax Credit Relief (FTCR) regime. The statutory basis is TIOPA 2010 Part 2.</p>

<p>Key rules:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Credit method, not exemption:</strong> almost all UK double taxation agreements use the credit method for property gains (following OECD Model Article 13). The UK does not simply exempt the gain. It taxes the gain at full UK CGT rates and gives a credit for the foreign tax paid. Agreements that use the exemption method for property are extremely rare.</li>
  <li><strong>The cap:</strong> the credit is capped at the UK CGT attributable to the same gain. If the overseas tax rate is higher than the UK rate, the excess foreign tax above the UK liability cannot be credited, deducted, or carried forward. The net UK liability is reduced to nil, but no refund or excess arises.</li>
  <li><strong>Pound-for-pound offset:</strong> FTCR reduces the UK CGT bill directly, not the gain. It is not a deduction from the taxable gain.</li>
  <li><strong>Same gain requirement:</strong> the credit applies to foreign tax charged on the same gain as the one giving rise to the UK CGT liability. Where the overseas country uses a different base (for example, a different base date or rebased value), care is needed in matching the UK and overseas computations.</li>
</ul>

<p>FTCR is claimed on SA108 and SA106. HMRC Helpsheet HS261 (Foreign Tax Credit Relief) sets out the mechanics and the claim form.</p>

<aside>
<p><strong>FTCR versus a double tax exemption:</strong> some property owners assume that if their country of residence and the country where the property is located have a double taxation agreement, the gain is simply "exempt" from UK CGT. This is almost never correct for property gains. Most agreements allow both states to tax property gains, with FTCR resolving the double charge. Do not rely on a DTA exemption without specific legal advice on the treaty wording.</p>
</aside>

<h2>How to report: SA108 and foreign pages (not the 60-day return)</h2>

<p>This is one of the most common errors made by UK residents selling overseas property: assuming the 60-day UK property return applies.</p>

<p>The 60-day return (introduced by Finance Act 2019 Schedule 2) applies <strong>only to disposals of UK land</strong>. It does not apply to overseas property, even where the seller is UK resident and UK CGT is due. There is no separate reporting obligation for overseas property disposals within 60 days.</p>

<h2>Private Residence Relief on overseas property</h2>

<p>Private Residence Relief (PRR) under TCGA 1992 s.222 is available on overseas property where the property was your only or main residence during the ownership period. There is no statutory restriction to UK property. An overseas property qualifies if it was in fact your main home.</p>

<p>The standard PRR rules apply:</p>

<ul>
  <li>The gain attributable to the period of actual occupation as main residence is fully relieved.</li>
  <li>The final 9 months of ownership are treated as main residence regardless of actual occupation (the final-period allowance).</li>
  <li>Where you own more than one residence, you must nominate which one is to be treated as the main residence. The nomination is made to HMRC within 2 years of acquiring the second property.</li>
  <li>Periods of letting (where the property was let after a period of main residence) may attract letting relief in limited circumstances under s.223(4), though the availability of letting relief was significantly restricted by Finance Act 2020.</li>
</ul>

<p>Practical note: if you bought an overseas property as a holiday home or investment, PPR does not apply for those periods, even if you spent time there. It requires occupation as your <em>main</em> residence. Occasional use of a holiday home does not qualify.</p>

<p>Importantly, PPR only eliminates UK CGT. The overseas country may still charge its own tax on the gain even where PPR reduces the UK liability to nil. FTCR cannot be claimed against UK CGT that has already been reduced to nil by PPR.</p>

<h2>Common scenarios</h2>

<h3>Holiday homes</h3>

<p>A holiday apartment in Spain, France, or Portugal owned as a second home qualifies for no PPR during the holiday-home period (unless it was at some point nominated and used as your main residence). The full gain is chargeable. FTCR is available for any Spanish, French, or Portuguese capital gains tax paid on the same disposal.</p>

<h3>Inherited overseas property</h3>

<p>The CGT base cost for inherited overseas property is the market value at the date of death (the same probate-value rule applies as for UK property, per TCGA 1992 s.62(1)). The estate agent or valuer's figure used for the overseas probate process will typically be the starting point. On sale, the gain is measured from that probate value, converted to sterling at the exchange rate on the date of death. Enhancement costs from the legatee's ownership period are also allowable.</p>

<h3>Foreign investment property previously let</h3>

<p>Where the overseas property was let, rental income should already have been declared on the foreign supplementary pages of the Self Assessment return each year. On disposal, any capital improvement costs claimed as revenue repairs (and thus already deducted against rental income) cannot be re-claimed as enhancement expenditure for CGT. Only capital expenditure not already deducted against income qualifies.</p>

<h2>Frequently asked questions</h2>

<h3>Do UK residents pay CGT on overseas property?</h3>

<p>Yes. UK tax residents are subject to CGT on worldwide gains under TCGA 1992 s.1(1), including gains on property located outside the UK. It does not matter whether the overseas country also taxes the same gain. The UK charges CGT on the full gain, subject to a credit for foreign tax already paid. There is no blanket exemption for overseas property, even where you have lived in or owned the property for many years.</p>

<h3>How do I convert foreign currency amounts to sterling for CGT?</h3>

<p>Each amount converts to sterling at the spot rate on the date that amount was incurred or received (Bentley v Pike [1981] 53 TC 590; Capcount Trading v Evans [1992] 65 TC 545; HMRC CG78310). Acquisition cost uses the acquisition-date rate. Disposal proceeds use the disposal-date rate. You must not compute the gain in foreign currency and then convert the result at a single rate. That approach is wrong in law.</p>

<h3>Do I need to file a 60-day return for an overseas property disposal?</h3>

<p>No. The 60-day UK property return applies only to disposals of UK land. Overseas property disposals are reported via Self Assessment only (SA108 and SA106), with a deadline of 31 January following the end of the tax year.</p>

<h3>Can I claim credit for overseas CGT I have already paid?</h3>

<p>Yes, subject to a cap under TIOPA 2010. The Foreign Tax Credit Relief (FTCR) is the lower of (a) the foreign tax paid on the overseas gain and (b) the UK CGT on the same gain. It is a pound-for-pound offset against UK CGT, not a deduction from the gain. Excess foreign tax cannot be refunded.</p>

<h3>Which Self Assessment pages do I need?</h3>

<p>SA108 (Capital Gains Summary) to compute and report the gain, and SA106 (foreign supplementary pages) to record the foreign tax and claim FTCR. Both are submitted as part of the annual Self Assessment return.</p>

<h3>What if the overseas country taxed the full gain at a higher rate than the UK?</h3>

<p>The FTCR cap means the UK CGT is reduced to nil, but the excess foreign tax above the UK liability cannot be refunded, credited, or carried forward. In this scenario, you pay no net UK CGT, but you receive no UK benefit for the foreign tax in excess of the UK charge.</p>

<h3>Can I claim Private Residence Relief on my overseas home?</h3>

<p>Yes, if the overseas property was your only or main residence during the ownership period. TCGA 1992 s.222 applies equally to overseas properties. The normal PPR rules apply: actual occupation as main home, nomination where more than one residence is owned, and the 9-month final-period allowance. PPR only eliminates the UK CGT; the overseas country may still charge its own tax.</p>

<h3>What exchange rate source should I use?</h3>

<p>HMRC accepts any reasonable, consistently applied published rate. Bank of England spot rates and HMRC's published customs and VAT exchange rates are both commonly used. Retain a record of the source and rate used for each transaction date.</p>

<h3>How does this page differ from the non-resident CGT page?</h3>

<p>This page covers UK residents selling foreign property. The <a href="/blog/non-resident-landlord-tax/non-resident-cgt-selling-uk-property-overseas-guide">non-resident CGT page</a> covers non-UK residents selling UK property. The rules are completely different: different reporting requirements, different forms, and different relief frameworks. If you are a non-UK resident selling UK property, the 60-day return is mandatory and the non-resident CGT rules apply instead of the standard worldwide-gains framework.</p>

<h3>Are there special rules for properties owned before I became UK resident?</h3>

<p>The temporary non-residence rules (TCGA 1992 s.10A) can catch gains made during a period abroad if you return to UK residence within 5 years. Simply becoming UK resident does not rebase overseas property to current market value. If you owned an overseas property before becoming UK resident and are now continuously resident, the full gain from original acquisition cost (converted to sterling at acquisition-date rate) is chargeable. Timing disposals around residency status changes is a specialist planning area.</p>

<h3>Does a double taxation agreement exempt the gain?</h3>

<p>Almost never for property. Most UK DTAs follow OECD Model Article 13, giving both states taxing rights on property gains. The credit mechanism (FTCR) resolves the double charge. DTAs that use an exemption method for property are extremely rare. Do not assume exemption without specific legal advice on the wording of the treaty with the relevant country.</p>

<h3>What records do I need for the overseas CGT computation?</h3>

<p>You need: the purchase contract (showing foreign currency price and completion date), evidence of the exchange rate on the acquisition date, receipts for enhancement costs with dates paid, the sale contract (price and completion date), evidence of disposal costs (agent fees, legal fees), and the foreign tax assessment or receipt for FTCR. HMRC may require proof the foreign tax has actually been paid, not merely assessed.</p>
