<p>Yes, individual UK landlords can still claim relief for mortgage interest on rental property. The mechanism changed under Section 24 of the Finance (No.2) Act 2015: instead of a full deduction from rental income, individual landlords now receive a <strong>20% basic-rate tax credit</strong> against their overall income tax bill. The change was phased in between 6 April 2017 and 5 April 2020 and has been fully in force for six tax years.</p>

<p>This page focuses on the <em>practical claim mechanics</em> for 2025/26: which SA105 box, what costs qualify, the three-way cap that limits the credit, the carry-forward mechanism when the cap bites, and worked examples for three landlord profiles. The comprehensive policy explainer (history, intent, abolition campaigns, political background) is in our <a href="/blog/section-24-and-tax-relief/section-24-tax-relief-complete-guide">Section 24 complete guide</a>; this page is for landlords who already understand the policy and need to know exactly how to claim on the return.</p>

<h2>Which SA105 Box to Use</h2>

<p>On the SA105 UK Property pages of the 2024-25 form, the dedicated box for residential finance costs is <strong>box 44</strong>, named "Residential finance costs not included in 'Loan interest and other financial costs'". The 2025-26 form retains the same structure although box numbers may shift slightly.</p>

<p>Process:</p>

<ol>
<li>Enter the <strong>full</strong> finance cost figure in box 44 (or its 2025-26 equivalent). Do not include this amount in the general loan-interest box.</li>
<li>Do <strong>not</strong> deduct the figure from rental profit. The whole point of Section 24 is that the deduction does not happen; the credit happens later.</li>
<li>HMRC's automatic calculation engine then applies the 20% basic-rate credit against your total income tax liability, subject to the three-way cap below.</li>
<li>The credit appears on the tax calculation summary as "Relief for residential finance costs" or similar wording.</li>
</ol>

<p>The most common claim error is double-counting: entering interest in box 26 (Loan interest and other financial costs) <em>and</em> box 44. This produces a full deduction against rental profit AND a 20% credit on top, which HMRC's compliance engine flags. The penalty for careless error under FA 2007 Sch 24 is 0%-30% of the additional tax due.</p>

<h2>What Counts as a Residential Finance Cost</h2>

<p>The legislation (ITTOIA 2005 s.272A-272B) and HMRC's <a href="https://www.gov.uk/hmrc-internal-manuals/property-income-manual/pim2056" rel="external nofollow" target="_blank">PIM2056</a> include:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Mortgage interest</strong> on loans used to buy residential rental property.</li>
<li><strong>Interest on remortgages</strong> of property already in the lettings business, capped at the value of the property when it entered the lettings business (the HMRC "withdrawal rule"). Any additional interest above that cap, if the released equity went to personal use, is not allowable.</li>
<li><strong>Interest on bridging loans</strong> used to acquire residential rental property.</li>
<li><strong>Arrangement fees</strong> on residential BTL mortgages, typically spread straight-line over the term of the loan or the fixed-rate period.</li>
<li><strong>Broker fees</strong> charged by mortgage brokers, treated the same as arrangement fees.</li>
<li><strong>Interest on personal loans</strong> demonstrably used for the rental business (renovations, deposit-stretching, working capital). The use of funds is the key evidence.</li>
<li><strong>Interest on overdrafts</strong> on a dedicated rental-business bank account, in proportion to the business use.</li>
</ul>

<p>And what does <em>not</em> count:</p>

<ul>
<li>Capital repayments (only interest counts, never the principal).</li>
<li>Mortgage protection insurance premiums (allowable as an insurance expense elsewhere on SA105).</li>
<li>Valuation fees for the lender (often capital and added to base cost for CGT).</li>
<li>Solicitor and conveyancing fees on the mortgage (capital, added to base cost).</li>
<li>Interest on loans where the funds were demonstrably used for personal purposes (the holiday home, the new car).</li>
</ul>

<h2>The Three-Way Cap on the 20% Credit</h2>

<p>The 20% credit is not unlimited. Under ITTOIA 2005 s.274A, the relievable amount of finance costs in any year is the <strong>lowest of</strong>:</p>

<ol>
<li>Total finance costs incurred in the year.</li>
<li>Total property business profits for the year (before deducting any finance costs).</li>
<li>Adjusted total income above the personal allowance (excluding savings and dividend income).</li>
</ol>

<p>The credit is then 20% of the lowest of those three. Any excess finance cost over that cap is carried forward to the next tax year under s.274A(3) and added to that year's finance costs before re-testing the cap.</p>

<h3>Why the cap matters</h3>

<p>For most working-age landlords with a regular salary or self-employment income, the third leg of the cap (adjusted total income above the personal allowance) is comfortably above the first leg (finance costs), so the cap is the finance cost figure itself and the full 20% credit applies. The cap bites in three common scenarios:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>High-leverage low-yield portfolios</strong> where finance costs exceed property profits.</li>
<li><strong>Retired landlords</strong> with low adjusted total income.</li>
<li><strong>Years of major repair spend</strong> that pushed property profits to nil or below.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Practical Things That Go Wrong</h2>

<ul>
<li><strong>Double-counting</strong>: claiming interest in both the general loan-interest box and the residential finance cost box. Always one box only.</li>
<li><strong>Including capital repayments</strong>: only the interest element counts. The lender's mortgage statement separates the two; use the interest figure.</li>
<li><strong>Mixing FHL interest with standard residential interest</strong>: since 6 April 2025, FHL is no longer a separate regime, so all interest goes through the Section 24 box regardless of whether the property is a long-term let or a short-let.</li>
<li><strong>Forgetting arrangement fees</strong>: spread arrangement fees over the loan term or fixed-rate period and include the annual portion as a finance cost.</li>
<li><strong>Missing the cap calculation</strong>: HMRC's online return handles the cap automatically, but DIY filers using offline spreadsheets must remember to test the three legs and carry forward the excess.</li>
<li><strong>Not tracking the carry-forward</strong>: the unrelieved finance cost from prior years adds to the current year's finance cost in the next year's cap test. Maintain a running cumulative figure year by year.</li>
<li><strong>Withdrawal-rule trap on remortgage</strong>: remortgaging at more than the value of the property when it entered the lettings business and using the released equity for personal purposes disallows relief on the additional interest. Document use of funds.</li>
</ul>

<h2>The Incorporation Trade-Off</h2>

<p>Section 24 does not apply to limited companies. A company deducts mortgage interest in full against rental profit before paying corporation tax at 19% to 25%. For a higher-rate landlord with material mortgage interest where the Section 24 cap is biting, the question of whether to move the portfolio into a limited company becomes the central tax-planning decision. The mechanics, including the SDLT cost of getting in (5% additional-dwellings surcharge applied at market value under FA 2003 s.53), are covered in our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/sdlt-incorporation-stamp-duty-twice">SDLT on incorporation</a> guide and the wider decision matrix sits in our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/tax-efficient-property-investment-structure-guide">tax-efficient property investment structure</a> guide.</p>

<h2>MTD for ITSA: How the Finance Cost Box Lives in MTD</h2>

<p>MTD for ITSA went live on 6 April 2026 for sole-trader landlords above £50,000 of qualifying income (threshold drops to £30,000 from 6 April 2027 and £20,000 from 6 April 2028). For landlords in MTD, the year-end SA105 is replaced by quarterly digital updates plus a Final Declaration. The Section 24 finance cost is reported within the quarterly updates and the Final Declaration in a dedicated finance cost field, with the same three-way cap mechanics applied at year-end.</p>

<p>MTD-compatible software handles the Section 24 calculation automatically provided the finance cost is correctly categorised at the time of entry. The common error is the same one as on the paper return: categorising mortgage interest as a general expense rather than a finance cost. Compliant software flags this if set up correctly.</p>

<h2>Carry-Forward When the Cap Bites: Step by Step</h2>

<ol>
<li>Calculate the year's finance costs in full (including any brought-forward unrelieved amount from prior years).</li>
<li>Calculate property profits before finance costs.</li>
<li>Calculate adjusted total income above the personal allowance.</li>
<li>Take the lowest of (1), (2), (3). This is the "relievable finance cost" for the year.</li>
<li>Credit = 20% of relievable finance cost. Applied against your income tax liability.</li>
<li>Carry forward = (total finance costs including b/f) minus (relievable finance cost). Track this figure year by year.</li>
<li>In the next year, add the b/f to that year's actual finance costs at step 1 and repeat.</li>
</ol>

<p>There is no time limit on the carry-forward. It is a permanent ratchet held against future years. The accumulated balance is lost only on the landlord's death (it does not transfer to the estate or surviving spouse).</p>
