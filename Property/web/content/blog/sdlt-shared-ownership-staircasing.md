  - question: "What is the market value election in shared ownership SDLT?"
    answer: "The market value election under paragraphs 2 and 4 of Schedule 9 FA 2003 is a one-off choice made on the first SDLT return: the buyer elects to pay SDLT on the full market value of the dwelling at the date of the initial shared-ownership grant, as if buying it outright. The election is irrevocable. The upside is that no further SDLT is ever due on subsequent staircasing or on the rent payable to the qualifying body. The downside is a much larger upfront SDLT bill at a point when the buyer typically has the least cash."
  - question: "What if I do not make the market value election?"
    answer: "Without an election, the default route applies: SDLT is paid in stages. The initial SDLT charge is on the premium for the first share only (often below the £125,000 nil-rate band, so often nil). SDLT also becomes chargeable on the net present value of the rent over the lease term where the NPV exceeds the relevant threshold. Subsequent staircasing transactions trigger further SDLT once cumulative ownership crosses 80%, calculated by reference to the linked-transactions rules in section 108 FA 2003."
  - question: "What is the 80% rule on staircasing?"
    answer: "Where the buyer is paying in stages, staircasing transactions up to and including a cumulative 80% holding are individually free of SDLT under paragraph 4A of Schedule 9 FA 2003. Once the buyer's cumulative ownership exceeds 80% on a staircasing transaction, that transaction and every subsequent staircasing increment become chargeable to SDLT. The chargeable amount is computed using the linked-transactions formula so the rate band reflects the buyer's cumulative purchases, not just the latest increment."
  - question: "Can I switch from pay-in-stages to a market value election later?"
    answer: "The election is made on the original SDLT return for the initial shared-ownership grant. An amendment to elect can be filed within 12 months of that original return's filing date. After that 12-month window, the election is no longer available. Buyers who have already started staircasing and now want to switch must accept that future increments above 80% remain chargeable; there is no retrospective route to convert the original return into an election after the amendment window closes."
  - question: "Does the 5% additional dwellings surcharge apply on shared ownership?"
    answer: "It can. Whether the surcharge applies depends on what other property the buyer (and any spouse or civil partner) owns at completion of the shared-ownership purchase, and on whether the shared-ownership unit is the buyer's main residence. A first-time buyer of a shared-ownership main residence is not subject to the surcharge. A buyer who already owns a buy-to-let and is acquiring a shared-ownership main residence may be subject to the surcharge unless they replace their previous main residence within 3 years and reclaim under the standard refund process."
  - question: "Can a shared-ownership buyer claim first-time buyer relief?"
    answer: "Yes, where the buyer is an individual who has never previously owned a freehold or leasehold interest in any dwelling worldwide and intends to occupy the shared-ownership unit as their only or main residence. The relief applies in the same form as on a non-shared-ownership purchase: 0% on the first £300,000 and 5% on the portion £300,001 to £500,000, with no relief above £500,000. Under a market value election, the relief applies to the full market value; under pay-in-stages, it applies to the initial premium."
  - question: "How is SDLT on the rent calculated under pay-in-stages?"
    answer: "SDLT on the rent is calculated on the net present value (NPV) of the total rent payable over the term of the lease, discounted at the statutory rate (3.5% per annum), as set out in paragraph 3 of Schedule 5 FA 2003. SDLT is then 1% on the slice of NPV between £125,001 and £250,000, and 2% on any NPV above £250,000. On a typical 25-year shared-ownership lease at moderate rent, the NPV often falls under or only slightly above the threshold, so the rent-SDLT charge is frequently small or nil. It is a recurring calculation that needs to be done; not skipped."
  - question: "What counts as a staircasing transaction for SDLT?"
    answer: "Staircasing is the buyer's acquisition of additional shares in the shared-ownership lease from the qualifying body (housing association or registered provider). Each acquisition of an additional share is a separate land transaction with its own effective date. Under pay-in-stages, each transaction is potentially chargeable; the 80% rule then exempts the early increments. Sub-letting, lease assignment, or transfer of the lease to a connected party is not staircasing but a separate disposal / acquisition with its own SDLT treatment."
  - question: "Does the non-UK resident 2% surcharge apply on a shared-ownership grant?"
    answer: "Yes, where the buyer meets the non-UK resident test in Schedule 9A FA 2003 (broadly, present in the UK for fewer than 183 days in the 365 days preceding the effective date). The 2% applies on top of any other rates and any 5% additional dwellings surcharge. The buyer can later reclaim the 2% if they become UK-resident within the 365-day window following completion. The reclaim runs through the standard gov.uk online refund service."

    answer: "Under pay-in-stages, the buyer files an SDLT return only on the initial shared-ownership grant and on staircasing transactions that are chargeable (i.e. once cumulative ownership exceeds 80%). Non-chargeable staircasing transactions below 80% do not require a return. Under a market value election, the buyer files the SDLT return on the initial grant and no further SDLT returns are required on any staircasing transaction or on the rent."
  - question: "Should I make the market value election or pay in stages?"
    answer: "The election usually wins where the buyer expects to staircase to 100% relatively quickly, the property is expected to appreciate materially, and the buyer can fund the upfront SDLT on the full market value. Pay-in-stages usually wins where the buyer is uncertain whether they will staircase past 80%, where the initial share is small relative to the dwelling value, where cash flow is tight at completion, or where the property is unlikely to appreciate dramatically. We model both routes on the buyer's expected staircasing pattern before completion; the election is irrevocable, so the modelling has to be done up front, not after."

<p>Anyone buying a shared-ownership home in England or Northern Ireland faces a one-off, irrevocable SDLT choice at completion: pay SDLT on the full market value of the dwelling now (the market value election), or pay only on the share you are buying and let further SDLT charges land later as you staircase and as the rent accrues. The choice is binary, made on the first SDLT return, and cannot be undone after the 12-month amendment window closes.</p>

<p>This page works through both routes, the 80% staircasing rule, the interaction with the 5% additional dwellings surcharge and the 2% non-resident surcharge, and a full worked example following a buyer from a 25% initial share through to 80% ownership under each route. The objective is to make the decision at completion legible, because by the time you have started staircasing it is too late to revisit.</p>

<h2>The two routes at a glance</h2>

<table>
<thead>
<tr><th></th><th>Market value election</th><th>Pay in stages</th></tr>
</thead>
<tbody>
<tr><td>Statutory basis</td><td>Paragraphs 2 and 4 of Schedule 9 FA 2003</td><td>Default treatment; paragraph 4A of Schedule 9 FA 2003 disapplies SDLT on intermediate staircasing</td></tr>
<tr><td>SDLT on initial transaction</td><td>On full market value of the dwelling</td><td>On the premium for the share purchased only</td></tr>
<tr><td>SDLT on rent</td><td>None (rent is outside the SDLT charge)</td><td>On NPV of total rent over the lease term, at 1% / 2% bands</td></tr>
<tr><td>SDLT on staircasing increments</td><td>None (entire lifetime SDLT exposure fixed on initial transaction)</td><td>Exempt up to cumulative 80%; chargeable on the first increment that crosses 80% and on all subsequent increments</td></tr>
<tr><td>Revocability</td><td>Irrevocable after 12-month amendment window</td><td>Cannot retrospectively elect after 12 months</td></tr>

<p>The wider SDLT framework for residential rates (covered in our <a href="/blog/landlord-tax-essentials/sdlt-buy-to-let-rates-surcharge-guide-2025">SDLT rates pillar</a>) sits behind both routes; what differs is the tax base on which those rates bite.</p>

<h2>Route 1: market value election</h2>

<p>Under paragraphs 2 and 4 of Schedule 9 to the <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2003/14/contents">Finance Act 2003</a>, the buyer elects on the first SDLT return to pay SDLT on the full market value of the dwelling, as if they were acquiring 100% of it outright at the date of the initial shared-ownership grant. Standard residential SDLT rates apply to that market value: 0% on the first £125,000, 2% on £125,001 to £250,000, 5% on £250,001 to £925,000, and so on.</p>

<p>The price paid for that lifetime certainty is real. A first-time buyer acquiring a 25% share of a £450,000 new-build pays £56,250 on completion for the share itself; the SDLT bill on the same purchase under the election would be calculated on £450,000 (£12,500 in 2026/27 without first-time buyer relief; reduced by FTB relief if eligible). That £12,500 is payable at completion alongside the share premium, often when the buyer is least able to find the cash.</p>

<p>The upside is that the buyer never files another shared-ownership SDLT return. No SDLT on rent. No SDLT on any staircasing transaction. No SDLT when they reach 100%. The lifetime SDLT exposure for the whole journey is fixed on day one.</p>

<h2>Route 2: pay in stages</h2>

<p>If no election is made on the first SDLT return, the default applies. The initial SDLT charge is on the premium paid for the first share only, run through the standard residential bands. On a 25% share of a £450,000 property the premium is £112,500; that falls under the £125,000 nil-rate band, so the initial SDLT charge is £0.</p>

<p>Three further SDLT events can arise under pay-in-stages.</p>

<p><strong>SDLT on rent</strong>, calculated on the net present value of the total rent payable to the qualifying body over the term of the shared-ownership lease, discounted at 3.5% per annum under paragraph 3 of Schedule 5 FA 2003. The NPV is then taxed at 1% on the slice between £125,001 and £250,000 and 2% above £250,000. On a typical 99-year shared-ownership lease at, say, £4,800 per year rising annually, the NPV often falls around or modestly above the threshold and the rent-SDLT charge is in the low hundreds of pounds. The charge needs computing on the first return; it is not optional.</p>

<p><strong>SDLT on staircasing increments below 80%</strong>: under paragraph 4A of Schedule 9 FA 2003, transactions that move the buyer's cumulative holding to 80% or less are not chargeable. A buyer who moves from 25% to 50% to 75% in three steps pays no SDLT on any of those steps.</p>

<p><strong>SDLT on staircasing increments above 80%</strong>: the first transaction that crosses 80% is chargeable, and every subsequent staircasing transaction is chargeable. The chargeable amount is computed using the linked-transactions rule in section 108 FA 2003, which aggregates all chargeable staircasing transactions to determine the band.</p>

<h2>The 80% rule: where the pay-in-stages route bites</h2>

<p>The 80% threshold under paragraph 4A is the single most important number in shared-ownership SDLT under pay-in-stages. The mechanics:</p>

<ul>
<li>Each staircasing transaction is a separate land transaction with its own effective date.</li>
<li>Staircasing transactions are linked to the original shared-ownership grant and to each other under section 108 FA 2003.</li>
<li>Where, immediately after a staircasing transaction, the buyer's cumulative share is 80% or less, paragraph 4A treats the transaction as exempt from SDLT.</li>
<li>Where, immediately after a staircasing transaction, the buyer's cumulative share exceeds 80%, the exemption is unavailable. The transaction is chargeable and SDLT is computed on the consideration for that increment, but using rate bands determined by reference to the buyer's cumulative purchases (so the rates can be higher than the increment alone would suggest).</li>
<li>Every staircasing transaction after the first one that crossed 80% is similarly chargeable.</li>
</ul>

<p>The practical consequence: pay-in-stages buyers who staircase in big jumps that cross 80% in a single step often face a larger SDLT bill on that step than the rate-band on the increment alone would suggest, because the linked-transactions rule includes the earlier (originally exempt) staircasing transactions in the rate-band calculation.</p>

<h2>Worked example: 25% to 50% to 80% under each route</h2>

<p>Consider Aisha, a first-time buyer in 2026 who acquires a 25% share of a £450,000 new-build flat in Reading at completion on 1 June 2026. The lease provides for staircasing at market value at each step. She plans to staircase to 50% in 2029 and to 80% in 2033, when the property is expected to be worth £510,000 and £580,000 respectively.</p>

<p>The numerical contrast is large. Under the election, Aisha pays £12,500 (or £7,500 with FTB relief) at completion and never sees another shared-ownership SDLT bill. Under pay-in-stages she pays £400 at completion and £0 on staircasing up to 80%. The break-even depends on whether she ever crosses 80%, on the rate of property appreciation between completion and the 80%-crossing step, and on whether she remains a first-time buyer for relief purposes (a buyer who has already owned property cannot reclaim that status).</p>

<p>For a buyer who is uncertain whether they will reach 80%, who has no urgent reason to lock in the SDLT exposure, and who needs the cash at completion, pay-in-stages is usually the better answer. For a buyer who is reasonably certain they will pass 80% within five to ten years, in a market expected to appreciate, the election can win on a present-value basis once the future linked-transaction SDLT is brought into the comparison.</p>

<h2>Interaction with the 5% additional dwellings surcharge</h2>

<p>The 5% additional dwellings surcharge (HRAD) applies to a shared-ownership grant on the same tests as any other residential purchase: does the buyer (or their spouse / civil partner) own any other residential property, valued at £40,000 or more, that is not being replaced as a main residence?</p>

<p>Three common shared-ownership scenarios produce different outcomes.</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>First-time buyer, no other property:</strong> the HRAD surcharge does not apply. First-time buyer relief is available if the property qualifies.</li>
<li><strong>Existing landlord adding a shared-ownership main home:</strong> HRAD usually applies because the buyer owns another residential property and is not selling it as a main residence simultaneously. If the buyer is replacing their previous main residence and selling it within 3 years, the surcharge can be reclaimed under the <a href="/blog/landlord-tax-essentials/sdlt-5-percent-surcharge-refund-claim-process">5% SDLT surcharge refund process</a>.</li>
<li><strong>Investor buying a shared-ownership unit as a buy-to-let (rare; most schemes prohibit this):</strong> HRAD applies and the buyer is generally not eligible for first-time buyer relief.</li>
</ul>

<p>Under a market value election, HRAD applies to the full market value. Under pay-in-stages, HRAD applies only to the initial premium and to any chargeable staircasing increment above 80%. The election can therefore look materially less attractive to buyers who fall into the HRAD net.</p>

<h2>Non-resident surcharge and devolved equivalents</h2>

<p>The 2% non-UK resident surcharge under Schedule 9A FA 2003 applies on the same tests, with the buyer eligible to reclaim it if they become UK-resident within the 365-day window following completion. The reclaim runs through the standard online refund service on gov.uk.</p>

<p>Land and Buildings Transaction Tax in Scotland and Land Transaction Tax in Wales have their own shared-ownership treatments. The architecture broadly resembles SDLT (election / pay-in-stages with a similar staircasing threshold) but the rates, bands and statutory references are different. Cross-border buyers need to apply the devolved provision rather than reading across from SDLT.</p>

<h2>How the SDLT return is completed</h2>

<p>The first SDLT return is filed by the buyer's conveyancer within 14 days of completion of the initial shared-ownership grant. The election (if made) is recorded in the dedicated election fields on the SDLT1; the NPV rent calculation (if no election) is computed and the rent SDLT entered on the return. Subsequent chargeable staircasing transactions (above 80%) are reported on a fresh SDLT return within 14 days of each effective date. HMRC's guidance on the return is at <a href="https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/stamp-duty-land-tax-online-and-paper-returns">gov.uk's SDLT return guidance</a>, and the underlying SDLT Manual at <a href="https://www.gov.uk/hmrc-internal-manuals/stamp-duty-land-tax-manual">SDLTM</a>.</p>
