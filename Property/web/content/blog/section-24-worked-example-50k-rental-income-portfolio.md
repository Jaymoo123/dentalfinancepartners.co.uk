<p>Section 24 mortgage interest restrictions hit landlords with significant portfolios hardest. This <strong>section 24 worked example 50k</strong> shows exactly how much tax a landlord with £50,000 annual rental income pays, comparing pre and post-Section 24 scenarios.</p>

<p>We'll walk through a realistic example with mortgage costs, allowable expenses, and the 20% basic rate tax credit that replaced full mortgage interest deductions. This practical <strong>section 24 calculation 50000</strong> demonstrates why many landlords consider incorporation or portfolio restructuring.</p>

<h2>The Example Portfolio: £50k Rental Income Landlord</h2>

<p>Our example landlord owns three buy-to-let properties generating £50,000 gross rental income annually. Here's the portfolio breakdown:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Property 1:</strong> Two-bed flat in Manchester - £18,000 annual rent, £140,000 mortgage at 5% interest</li>
<li><strong>Property 2:</strong> Three-bed house in Birmingham - £20,000 annual rent, £180,000 mortgage at 5.5% interest</li>
<li><strong>Property 3:</strong> Two-bed house in Leicester - £12,000 annual rent, £120,000 mortgage at 5.2% interest</li>
</ul>

<p>Total annual mortgage interest: £22,340 (Property 1: £7,000 + Property 2: £9,900 + Property 3: £6,240). The landlord also pays £3,200 in other allowable expenses (insurance, repairs, letting agent fees, accountancy).</p>

<h2>Pre-Section 24 Tax Calculation (Historical)</h2>

<p>Before Section 24 restrictions, landlords could deduct full mortgage interest as a business expense. Here's how the tax calculation worked:</p>

<p><strong>Rental Income:</strong> £50,000<br>
<strong>Less: Mortgage Interest:</strong> £22,340<br>
<strong>Less: Other Expenses:</strong> £3,200<br>
<strong>Net Rental Profit:</strong> £24,460</p>

<p>Assuming this landlord has £35,000 employment income, their total taxable income would be £59,460. With the personal allowance of £12,570, they'd pay:</p>

<ul>
<li>Basic rate (20%) on £46,890: £9,378</li>
<li>Higher rate (40%) on £9,190: £3,676</li>
<li><strong>Total tax on rental income portion:</strong> Approximately £4,892</li>
</ul>

<h2>Post-Section 24 Tax Calculation (Current Rules)</h2>

<p>Under current Section 24 rules, this <strong>realistic section 24 example</strong> shows a dramatically different calculation:</p>

<p><strong>Step 1: Calculate Rental Profit (No Mortgage Interest Deduction)</strong><br>
Rental Income: £50,000<br>
Less: Other Expenses Only: £3,200<br>
Rental Profit Before Interest: £46,800</p>

<p><strong>Step 2: Add to Other Income</strong><br>
Employment Income: £35,000<br>
Rental Profit: £46,800<br>
Total Income: £81,800<br>
Less Personal Allowance: £12,570<br>
Taxable Income: £69,230</p>

<p><strong>Step 3: Calculate Income Tax</strong><br>
Basic rate (20%) on £37,700: £7,540<br>
Higher rate (40%) on £31,530: £12,612<br>
Total Income Tax: £20,152</p>

<p><strong>Step 4: Apply 20% Tax Credit on Mortgage Interest</strong><br>
Tax Credit: £22,340 × 20% = £4,468<br>
Final Tax Liability: £20,152 - £4,468 = £15,684</p>

<h2>Section 24 Impact Analysis</h2>

<p>The tax increase from this <strong>section 24 worked example 50k</strong> is substantial:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Pre-Section 24 total tax:</strong> Approximately £13,270 (including employment income)</li>
<li><strong>Post-Section 24 total tax:</strong> £15,684</li>
<li><strong>Additional annual tax cost:</strong> £2,414</li>
<li><strong>Effective tax rate on rental income:</strong> 31.4% (up from 19.6%)</li>
</ul>

<p>This landlord now pays an extra £201 per month in tax purely due to Section 24 restrictions. The mortgage interest relief is effectively capped at basic rate (20%) instead of their marginal rate (40%).</p>

<h2>Cash Flow Impact on £50k Portfolio</h2>

<p>Section 24 doesn't just increase tax—it can create cash flow problems. Here's the monthly cash flow analysis:</p>

<p><strong>Monthly Rental Income:</strong> £4,167<br>
<strong>Less: Mortgage Payments:</strong> £2,200 (assuming capital repayment mortgages)<br>
<strong>Less: Other Expenses:</strong> £267<br>
<strong>Less: Additional Tax (Section 24):</strong> £201<br>
<strong>Net Monthly Cash Flow:</strong> £1,499</p>

<p>Before Section 24, this landlord's monthly cash flow would have been £1,700—a reduction of £201 per month or £2,414 annually.</p>

<h2>Higher Rate Tax Trap</h2>

<p>This example demonstrates the "higher rate tax trap" created by Section 24. The landlord's rental income pushes them into the 40% tax bracket, but they only receive 20% relief on mortgage interest.</p>

<p>Key impacts include:</p>

<ul>
<li>Loss of personal allowance if total income exceeds £100,000</li>
<li>Potential loss of child benefit if applicable</li>
<li>Higher tax rates on any additional income</li>
<li>Reduced pension contribution capacity</li>
</ul>

<h2>Planning Options for High-Income Landlords</h2>

<p>Landlords facing this level of Section 24 impact often consider several strategies:</p>

<h3>Limited Company Structure</h3>

<p>Moving properties to a limited company avoids Section 24 entirely. <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/buy-to-let-limited-company-complete-guide-uk">Buy-to-let companies</a> can deduct full mortgage interest and pay corporation tax at 19% or 25%.</p>

<h3>Pension Contributions</h3>

<p>Maximizing pension contributions can reduce taxable income and push the landlord back into basic rate tax. Annual allowance of £60,000 (2026/27) provides substantial tax relief.</p>

<h3>Portfolio Restructuring</h3>

<p>Some landlords reduce their mortgage exposure by selling highly leveraged properties and buying others with cash or lower loan-to-value ratios.</p>

<h2>Section 24 and Making Tax Digital</h2>

<p>From April 2026, landlords with gross rental income over £10,000 must comply with <a href="/blog/making-tax-digital-mtd/making-tax-digital-landlords-april-2026-deadline">Making Tax Digital requirements</a>. This £50k portfolio will need quarterly digital submissions, making accurate Section 24 calculations even more important.</p>

<h2>Looking Ahead: April 2027 Changes</h2>

<p>From April 2027, separate property income tax rates will apply—22% basic rate, 42% higher rate, and 47% additional rate on rental income specifically. This could further increase the tax burden for landlords like our example, making incorporation or other planning strategies even more attractive.</p>

<p>The calculation methodology remains the same under Section 24, but the tax rates applied to rental income will be higher across all brackets.</p>
