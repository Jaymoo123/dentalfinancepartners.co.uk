<p>Property investment runs through four tax moments, and most landlords meet them in order: you pay tax when you <strong>buy</strong>, while you <strong>hold and let</strong>, when you <strong>sell</strong>, and again when you <strong>pass property on</strong>. Wrapped around those four moments are two decisions that shape every figure: <strong>how you hold the property</strong> (personally or through a company) and <strong>how you report it</strong> as Making Tax Digital is phased in.</p>

<p>This guide is the map. Each stage below gives you the headline rules and the current 2026/27 figures, then points you to the in-depth guide that covers the mechanics. It is written for individual landlords and portfolio investors across the UK, and it reflects the law as it stands for 2026/27 plus the enacted changes that take effect from 6 April 2027.</p>

<h2>Buy: Stamp Duty Land Tax and the 5% surcharge</h2>

<p>The first tax most investors meet is on purchase. In England and Northern Ireland this is Stamp Duty Land Tax (SDLT). On top of the standard residential bands that apply from 1 April 2025, second and investment properties attract a <strong>5% additional-dwellings surcharge</strong>, which has applied to transactions on or after 31 October 2024 under the Finance (No.2) Act 2024. The surcharge is added to every band, so on a typical buy-to-let it is a significant share of the total bill.</p>

<p>Two further points matter for investors. A <strong>2% non-resident surcharge</strong> applies on top of the standard rates where the buyer is not UK resident. And Multiple Dwellings Relief was abolished from 1 June 2024, so the old portfolio-relief route is gone, although the non-residential rates can still apply to a single transaction involving six or more dwellings. First-time-buyer relief (nil to £300,000, then 5% to £500,000) is not available where the surcharge applies.</p>

<p>Purchase tax is devolved outside England and Northern Ireland. <strong>Scotland</strong> charges Land and Buildings Transaction Tax through Revenue Scotland, with an Additional Dwelling Supplement of <strong>8%</strong> on second properties. <strong>Wales</strong> charges Land Transaction Tax through the Welsh Revenue Authority, with its own higher rates for additional properties. The bands and reliefs differ in each jurisdiction, so use the figures for the country where the property sits. The statutory basis for SDLT is the <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2003/14/contents" rel="nofollow">Finance Act 2003</a>, and HMRC's current bands are on the <a href="https://www.gov.uk/stamp-duty-land-tax/residential-property-rates" rel="nofollow">gov.uk SDLT pages</a>. The full purchase mechanics, including the surcharge bands and the mitigation routes that survive, are in our <a href="/blog/landlord-tax-essentials/sdlt-buy-to-let-rates-surcharge-guide-2025">SDLT buy-to-let surcharge guide</a>.</p>

<h2>Hold and let: income tax on rental profit</h2>

<p>While you let a property, you are taxed on your <strong>rental profit</strong>, which is rental income minus allowable expenses. For an individual landlord this profit is added to your other income and taxed at your marginal rate.</p>

<p>For <strong>2026/27</strong> the rates are <strong>20% basic, 40% higher and 45% additional</strong>. From <strong>6 April 2027</strong>, property income is taxed at separate property rates of <strong>22% basic, 42% higher and 47% additional</strong> in England, Wales and Northern Ireland (Scotland is excluded). This is enacted law: the <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2026/11/contents" rel="nofollow">Finance Act 2026</a> sets the new rates at sections 6 and 7, and section 7 confirms the 22/42/47 figures for 2027/28. It is not a proposal or a draft, so it should be planned for now rather than treated as speculative.</p>

<p><strong>Worked example 1 (the April 2027 step-up).</strong> A higher-rate landlord receives £18,000 of rent and has £4,000 of allowable expenses (excluding mortgage interest, which is dealt with separately under Section 24). Their rental profit is £14,000. In 2026/27 the tax on that profit is 40%, or £5,600. In 2027/28, on the same figures, the higher property rate of 42% applies, giving £5,880. The 2 percentage point rise adds £280 of tax on this profit, and the gap scales with profit. A landlord at the top of the higher-rate band should also watch the £100,000 personal-allowance taper, where allowances are withdrawn and create an effective 60% band on income between £100,000 and £125,140.</p>

<p>For the full mechanics of taxing rental income, see <a href="/blog/landlord-tax-essentials/how-much-tax-rental-income-uk-complete-guide">how much tax you pay on rental income</a> and the year-by-year detail in our <a href="/blog/landlord-tax-essentials/income-tax-rates-landlords-2026-27-complete-guide">income tax rates for landlords 2026/27 guide</a> and the <a href="/blog/landlord-tax-essentials/2027-property-income-tax-rates-landlords-uk">2027 property income tax rates guide</a>.</p>

<h2>Section 24: the finance-cost restriction</h2>

<p>For individual landlords, mortgage and finance interest is <strong>not a deductible expense</strong>. Instead, you receive a basic-rate <strong>tax credit</strong> on it (a reducer applied after your tax is calculated). For 2026/27 the credit is <strong>20%</strong> of the lower of your finance costs, your residential rental profit, or your income above the personal allowance.</p>

<p><strong>Worked example 2 (Section 24).</strong> A higher-rate landlord pays £10,000 of mortgage interest in 2026/27. Before Section 24, deducting that interest as an expense would have saved tax at 40%, worth £4,000. Under Section 24, the landlord instead receives a 20% credit, worth £2,000. The difference of £2,000 is the extra tax burden the restriction creates on this interest. The effect falls hardest on heavily geared higher and additional-rate landlords.</p>

<p>From <strong>2027/28 the reducer rises to 22%</strong> in step with the new property basic rate, under Schedule 1 of the Finance Act 2026 (which amends the individual reducer at ITTOIA 2005 section 274AA, with the partnership equivalent at ITA 2007 section 399B). This matters: because the credit tracks the 22% property basic rate, a <strong>basic-rate landlord sees no new wedge</strong> open. Higher and additional-rate landlords see the credit rise from 20% to 22%, a small improvement, but it stays well below their 42% and 47% rates, so the finance-cost gap remains broadly the same as today rather than widening. Section 24 does not apply to companies, which is one of the drivers of the incorporation question below. The detail, including the three-part cap and carry-forward of unused credit, is in our <a href="/blog/section-24-and-tax-relief/section-24-tax-relief-complete-guide">Section 24 complete guide</a> and the applied walkthrough in <a href="/blog/section-24-and-tax-relief/claim-mortgage-interest-rental-property-uk-section-24">claiming mortgage interest relief under Section 24</a>.</p>

<h2>Allowable expenses and capital allowances</h2>

<p>Getting the expense side right is where most of the routine tax saving lives. You can deduct revenue costs incurred wholly and exclusively for the letting, including:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Property management fees</strong>, letting agent commissions and management charges</li>
<li><strong>Repairs and maintenance</strong> that restore the property, not improvements that enhance it</li>
<li><strong>Insurance</strong>, buildings and contents premiums</li>
<li><strong>Professional fees</strong>, accountancy and certain legal costs</li>
<li><strong>Utilities and council tax</strong> where you pay them between tenancies</li>
<li><strong>Advertising</strong> to find tenants, and travel for genuine management visits</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Capital improvements</strong> such as a new extension or a first central-heating system are not deductible against rental income. They add to your base cost and reduce capital gains tax when you sell, so they are not lost, just deferred to the disposal calculation. The boundary between a repair and an improvement is the single most common area of error, and HMRC's <a href="https://www.gov.uk/hmrc-internal-manuals/property-income-manual" rel="nofollow">Property Income Manual</a> sets out the tests in detail.</p>

<p>Capital allowances are mostly a footnote for residential buy-to-let. The dwelling-house bar in the Capital Allowances Act 2001 means you cannot claim plant-and-machinery allowances on items inside a let dwelling, although common parts and integral features in non-dwelling areas can qualify. Where allowances do apply, the main-pool writing-down allowance is now <strong>14%</strong> (reduced from 18% by the Finance Act 2026), and a new 40% first-year allowance is available to unincorporated landlords on qualifying new plant from 1 January 2026. The full deductions checklist is in our <a href="/blog/section-24-and-tax-relief/landlord-tax-deductions-uk-2026-complete-list">landlord tax deductions list</a>.</p>

<h2>Structure: personal ownership versus a limited company</h2>

<p>How you hold property is the decision that changes everything downstream. Held <strong>personally</strong>, rental profit is taxed at your income tax rates and Section 24 restricts your interest relief. Held through a <strong>limited company</strong>, the company deducts mortgage interest in full (no Section 24) and pays <strong>corporation tax</strong>: 19% on profits up to £50,000, 25% on profits above £250,000, and an effective 26.5% on the slice between (via marginal relief).</p>

<p>The catch is the <strong>second layer of tax</strong>. Money inside the company is not yours until you extract it, and extracting profit as dividends is taxed again in your hands (the dividend ordinary and upper rates were raised from 6 April 2026 (ordinary 10.75%, upper 35.75%), with the additional rate unchanged at 39.35%). Transferring an existing portfolio into a company can also trigger SDLT and CGT on the way in. The table below summarises the trade-off.</p>

<p><strong>Worked example 3 (personal versus company).</strong> Take a higher-rate landlord with £30,000 of rental profit and £10,000 of mortgage interest. Held personally, the £30,000 profit is taxed at 40% (£12,000) and the £10,000 interest gives only a £2,000 credit, a combined cost of £10,000. Held in a company, the interest is fully deductible, so taxable profit is £20,000, corporation tax at the small profits rate is £3,800, and the after-tax cash sits in the company. If the landlord then draws the cash as dividends, the second layer of tax applies and narrows the gap. The lesson is that incorporation can help leveraged higher-rate landlords, but the answer is specific to your numbers and your plans, not a universal yes. We do not recommend one route over the other here. <h2>Sell: capital gains tax on disposal</h2>

<p>When you sell, you pay capital gains tax on the gain (sale proceeds minus base cost, capital improvements and costs of buying and selling). For UK residential property the rates are <strong>18% for basic-rate taxpayers and 24% for higher and additional-rate taxpayers</strong>, in force from 30 October 2024. The CGT rate sits at section 1H of the <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12/section/1H" rel="nofollow">Taxation of Chargeable Gains Act 1992</a>, and the current figures are confirmed on the <a href="https://www.gov.uk/capital-gains-tax/rates" rel="nofollow">gov.uk CGT rates page</a>.</p>

<p>Each individual has a <strong>£3,000 annual exempt amount</strong> for 2026/27. Where tax is due on a UK residential disposal, you must <strong>report and pay within 60 days</strong> of completion through a UK property return, separately from your Self Assessment. Transfers between spouses and civil partners are on a no-gain, no-loss basis, which lets couples use both annual exemptions and split the gain across rate bands. Business asset disposal relief does not apply to ordinary investment property. For the full disposal mechanics, see our <a href="/blog/capital-gains-tax/capital-gains-tax-property-complete-guide-uk">CGT on property complete guide</a>, the rates detail in <a href="/blog/capital-gains-tax/cgt-rates-property-2026-27-current-rates-explained">CGT rates for property 2026/27</a>, and the deadline detail in <a href="/blog/capital-gains-tax/cgt-payment-deadlines-property-sales-2026">CGT payment deadlines</a>.</p>

<h2>Pass on: inheritance tax</h2>

<p>Rental property forms part of your estate, so it is exposed to inheritance tax. IHT is charged at <strong>40%</strong> on the value of the estate above the available <strong>nil-rate band of £325,000</strong> (frozen to 5 April 2031), with the residence nil-rate band of £175,000 potentially adding to that where a home passes to direct descendants (the residence band tapers away above a £2m estate). The statutory rate and bands are in the <a href="https://www.gov.uk/inheritance-tax" rel="nofollow">Inheritance Tax Act 1984</a>, and HMRC's overview is on the <a href="https://www.gov.uk/inheritance-tax" rel="nofollow">gov.uk inheritance tax pages</a>.</p>

<p>The key trap for landlords is that <strong>standard buy-to-let does not qualify for business property relief</strong>. Letting is treated as an investment activity, not a trade, so the property is taxed in full at 40% rather than benefiting from relief. From <strong>6 April 2027</strong>, unused defined-contribution pension funds also fall within the IHT estate, which matters for landlords whose wealth is split between property and pensions. The spouse exemption and transferable nil-rate bands remain important planning tools. The detail is in our <a href="/blog/landlord-tax-essentials/inheritance-tax-rental-property-uk-guide">inheritance tax on rental property guide</a>.</p>

<h2>Comply: Making Tax Digital for Income Tax</h2>

<p>Making Tax Digital for Income Tax changes how landlords report, and it is being phased in by qualifying income. It applies from <strong>6 April 2026</strong> where your combined self-employment and property income is above <strong>£50,000</strong>, from <strong>6 April 2027</strong> above <strong>£30,000</strong>, and from <strong>6 April 2028</strong> above <strong>£20,000</strong>. HMRC's eligibility tool is on the <a href="https://www.gov.uk/guidance/check-if-you-need-to-sign-up-for-making-tax-digital-for-income-tax" rel="nofollow">gov.uk MTD pages</a>.</p>

<p>Once you are in, you must keep <strong>digital records</strong>, send <strong>quarterly updates</strong> of income and expenses, and submit a <strong>final declaration</strong> in place of the old Self Assessment return, all through compatible software. Joint owners test their own share of gross income, so co-owners may enter MTD at different times. Companies are outside MTD for Income Tax (they have their own corporation tax regime). The practical readiness steps are in our <a href="/blog/making-tax-digital-mtd/making-tax-digital-property-income-2026-complete-guide">Making Tax Digital for property income guide</a>.</p>

<h2>Planning across the lifecycle</h2>

<p>The value in tax planning comes from looking across the whole lifecycle rather than at one stage in isolation. A few themes recur:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Timing disposals.</strong> Spreading a sale, or completing in different tax years, can use more than one annual exempt amount and keep part of a gain in the basic-rate CGT band.</li>
<li><strong>Spousal ownership splits.</strong> Placing income with the lower-rate partner and using both annual exemptions on a future sale can be efficient, supported by a Form 17 declaration where shares are unequal.</li>
<li><strong>Sequencing structure changes.</strong> The cost of moving a portfolio into a company (SDLT and CGT on entry) has to be weighed against the ongoing saving, which depends on leverage and extraction plans.</li>
<li><strong>Pensions and IHT.</strong> With unused pensions entering the estate from 2027, the interaction between property wealth and pension wealth becomes a live planning point.</li>
</ul>
