<p>Many UK dental practices now offer facial aesthetics alongside traditional dentistry. But the VAT treatment of these services differs significantly from standard dental care, and getting it wrong can be costly.</p>

<p>This guide covers everything you need to know about facial aesthetics VAT, from basic compliance through to complex mixed-practice scenarios.</p>

<h2>The Basic Rule: Most Facial Aesthetics are Standard Rated</h2>

<p>Unlike most dental treatments, facial aesthetics typically attract standard rate VAT at 20%. This includes:</p>

<ul>
<li>Botox injections for cosmetic purposes</li>
<li>Dermal fillers</li>
<li>Chemical peels</li>
<li>Laser treatments for aesthetic improvement</li>
<li>Non-surgical facelifts</li>
</ul>

<p>The key distinction is whether the treatment is therapeutic or cosmetic. If it's primarily for appearance rather than treating a medical condition, it's standard rated.</p>

<h2>When Facial Aesthetics Might Be Exempt</h2>

<p>Some facial aesthetic treatments can qualify for VAT exemption if they meet strict medical criteria. These typically include:</p>

<p><strong>Reconstructive treatments:</strong> Following surgery, trauma, or congenital conditions. For example, facial reconstruction after cancer surgery or treating facial palsy.</p>

<p><strong>Medical botox:</strong> When used to treat conditions like excessive sweating (hyperhidrosis), chronic migraines, or muscle spasticity. The treatment must be prescribed for a medical condition, not cosmetic enhancement.</p>

<p>The test is whether the primary purpose is therapeutic. If a patient seeks botox purely to reduce wrinkles, it's standard rated regardless of any incidental medical benefit.</p>

<h2>Registration Thresholds and Mixed Practices</h2>

<p>Adding facial aesthetics VAT to your income affects your VAT registration threshold. Currently set at £85,000, this includes all standard-rated services.</p>

<p>Many dental practices operate with mixed income streams - exempt dental services plus standard-rated aesthetics. This creates a partial exemption situation where you can only recover VAT on expenses that relate to taxable supplies.</p>

<p>For example, if 30% of your income comes from facial aesthetics, you can typically recover 30% of the VAT on general practice expenses like rent, utilities, and equipment.</p>

<h2>Practical VAT Compliance for Aesthetic Services</h2>

<p><strong>Separate recording:</strong> Keep detailed records distinguishing between exempt dental work and standard-rated aesthetics. Your practice management software should categorise treatments correctly.</p>

<p><strong>Invoice requirements:</strong> Standard-rated treatments need proper VAT invoices showing the 20% charge separately. Patients paying privately need to see this breakdown.</p>

<p><strong>Input VAT recovery:</strong> Track which expenses relate to which income streams. Equipment used solely for aesthetics allows full VAT recovery, while shared costs need apportionment.</p>

<h2>Common Mistakes to Avoid</h2>

<p>The biggest error is assuming all treatments within a dental practice follow the same VAT rules. We regularly see practices that have:</p>

<ul>
<li>Applied dental VAT exemption to cosmetic botox</li>
<li>Failed to register for VAT when aesthetic income pushed them over the threshold</li>
<li>Incorrectly calculated partial exemption percentages</li>
<li>Mixed up input VAT recovery on shared expenses</li>
</ul>

<p>Another common issue is the boundary between therapeutic and cosmetic treatment. When in doubt, the safer approach is to treat as standard-rated and seek specific advice.</p>

<h2>Impact on Practice Finances</h2>

<p>Adding facial aesthetics VAT changes your financial position in several ways. Your prices need to include the 20% VAT element, which affects competitiveness and patient acceptance.</p>

<p>However, you gain the ability to recover input VAT on related expenses. New aesthetic equipment, dedicated treatment rooms, and relevant training courses become 20% cheaper in VAT terms.</p>

<p>Cash flow also changes. Unlike exempt dental services where you simply receive payment, standard-rated aesthetics require you to collect VAT and pay it to HMRC quarterly.</p>

<h2>Documentation and Record Keeping</h2>

<p>HMRC expects clear evidence supporting your VAT treatment of aesthetic services. This means:</p>

<p><strong>Treatment records:</strong> Note the primary purpose of each treatment. Is it medical necessity or cosmetic enhancement? Document the patient's presenting concerns and your clinical reasoning.</p>

<p><strong>Financial separation:</strong> Keep aesthetic income clearly separate in your accounting system. This supports partial exemption calculations and makes compliance reviews straightforward.</p>

<p><strong>Professional advice records:</strong> When you've sought advice on borderline cases, keep the documentation. It demonstrates you've tried to get the VAT treatment right.</p>

<h2>Planning for Growth</h2>

<p>As your aesthetic services expand, VAT compliance becomes more complex. Consider whether a separate company structure might simplify matters, particularly if aesthetics become a major revenue stream.</p>

<p>Some practices find it cleaner to operate aesthetics through a separate VAT-registered entity, avoiding partial exemption complications in the main dental practice. However, this needs careful planning around premises, equipment sharing, and employment arrangements.</p>

<p>The decision depends on your specific circumstances and growth plans. What works for a practice earning £20k annually from botox differs from one generating £200k in aesthetic revenue.</p>

<p>For complex situations involving multiple income streams, getting advice from specialists familiar with both dental and aesthetic practice structures makes sense. Understanding these rules properly protects your practice from compliance issues while ensuring you're not overpaying VAT.</p>
