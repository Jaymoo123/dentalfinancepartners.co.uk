faqs:
  - question: "What does multi-year sequencing actually mean in extraction planning?"
    answer: "It means treating extraction as a five to ten year programme rather than a single tax-year decision. Each year's mix of salary, dividend, employer pension contribution and DLA repayment changes the start position for next year (DLA balance moves down, retained earnings move up or down, the founder's pension annual-allowance carry-forward shrinks). Optimising one year in isolation, against the 2026/27 marginal stack only, often leaves the founder paying more tax over the cycle than a deliberate multi-year plan would have. The plan does not need to be precise to the pound; it needs to surface the two or three forward decision points (DLA exhaustion, dividend-band cliff, retirement transition) and let the founder adjust the mix as those points approach."
  - question: "How do I figure out the right starting order for extraction?"
    answer: "Start with the routes that have no personal-side tax cost and work outward. The standard ordering for a single-director property SPV in 2026/27 is: salary at the secondary national insurance threshold (£5,000, no NI, no income tax, corporation-tax-deductible); DLA repayment from any credit balance left over from incorporation (no income tax, repayment of debt); dividend up to the dividend allowance and basic-rate threshold (10.75% above the £500 allowance, within the £50,270 total income band); employer pension contribution up to the annual allowance (£60,000 in 2026/27, corporation-tax-deductible, no income tax at contribution); further dividend at higher rate (35.75% in the £50,270 to £125,140 band); additional dividend (39.35% above £125,140). The mix at the top end is constrained by the founder's other personal income, the company's distributable reserves, and any pension-annual-allowance taper above £260,000 of adjusted income."
  - question: "How long does a typical incorporation-credit DLA balance last?"
    answer: "Most s.162 incorporation transfers create a credit DLA balance equal to the property's incorporation-date market value less any mortgage outstanding at that date. A typical mid-sized portfolio (three or four properties, £1.5m gross value, £900,000 borrowings) creates a £600,000 credit DLA. At extraction of £40,000 a year through DLA repayment plus other strands, the credit DLA exhausts in about 15 years. Larger incorporations of £200,000 a year extracted through DLA can exhaust a £500,000 credit balance in four or five years. The exhaustion point is the single most important forward-looking variable in the extraction sequence: once the DLA credit hits zero, the founder loses the tax-free repayment route entirely and the marginal cost of extraction jumps from zero (DLA repayment) to the basic-rate dividend rate of 10.75% plus the underlying corporation tax."
  - question: "What changes once the DLA credit runs out?"
    answer: "Three things shift. First, the tax-free repayment route closes; further cash extraction is dividend, salary, or pension. Second, the founder's annual extraction may need to step down (or the corporation tax retained-earnings load may need to step up) to avoid pushing dividend income into the 35.75% higher band. Third, the planning horizon shortens: instead of running the extraction-and-pension mix indefinitely, the founder is now choosing between dividend extraction at higher rates and either leaving cash in the company (for re-investment or eventual MVL exit) or accelerating pension contributions to use the £60,000 annual allowance more aggressively. The DLA-exhaustion event is also a natural moment to consider whether the SPV should be wound down (see our <a href=\"/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/mvl-members-voluntary-liquidation-property-company-cgt-vs-income-treatment\">MVL exit page</a>) or restructured into a HoldCo group (see our <a href=\"/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/multi-company-group-extraction-spv-holding-co-dividend-conduit-mechanics\">HoldCo extraction page</a>)."
  - question: "Should I take a salary even though the SPV has only one director?"
    answer: "Usually yes, at the £5,000 secondary-threshold level. A salary at exactly the secondary threshold attracts no employer national insurance (£5,000 from the Reeves Autumn Budget 2024 reform, in force from 6 April 2025), no employee national insurance (below the £12,570 primary threshold), and no income tax (within the personal allowance). It is corporation-tax-deductible to the company, saving 19%, 25%, or 26.5% depending on the profit band. The £5,000 also counts toward a national insurance qualifying year for the new state pension. Going above £5,000 in a sole-director SPV triggers employer NI at 15% on every pound above the threshold (with no Employment Allowance offset for sole-director companies), which usually destroys the marginal benefit. Multi-director SPVs that genuinely employ a second director can claim Employment Allowance (£10,500 in 2026/27) and run salaries up to the personal allowance of £12,570 more efficiently."
  - question: "When does employer pension contribution start to dominate the mix?"
    answer: "Pension contributions become structurally dominant once the founder's other extraction strands push them into the higher-rate band on dividend income and the company has spare distributable cash. At the £50,270 to £125,140 income band, the dividend rate is 35.75%; the marginal cost of a higher-rate dividend pound out of post-CT cash is 35.75%. An employer pension contribution of the same gross amount avoids both the dividend tax and (with carry-forward of unused annual allowance from the three prior tax years) avoids the personal-tax leg entirely at the contribution moment. The constraint is access timing: pension contributions cannot be drawn until age 55, rising to 57 from April 2028. For founders in their 40s with a long pension-lock-in tolerance, layering pension above the basic-rate band is often the single highest-leverage tax move of the year. For founders within 5 years of retirement, the pension-vs-dividend trade-off shifts back toward dividend because access timing matters more."
  - question: "Does the SPV being a close investment-holding company change the sequence?"
    answer: "Yes on the corporation-tax side; not on the personal side. A CIHC under CTA 2010 s.18N is denied the small-profits rate and pays the 25% main rate regardless of profit level. The marginal-relief 26.5% band does not apply to a CIHC. In sequencing terms this means the deductible extraction strands (salary, employer pension contributions) are more valuable inside a CIHC than inside a non-CIHC at sub-£250,000 profit levels, because the corporation-tax saving is locked at 25% rather than tapering down to 19% as profits fall. Most BTL SPVs that let to unconnected third-party tenants are NOT CIHCs under the s.18N qualifying-purpose carve-out for land investment. The CIHC question typically arises only for SPVs whose tenant base is dominated by connected parties (founder's family, related companies) or whose activity has shifted to passive cash or share investment."
  - question: "How does the founder's age shift the answer?"
    answer: "Three age zones: pre-50, 50-60, post-retirement. Pre-50 (long pension lock-in, high annual-allowance carry-forward room), the optimal mix tilts heavily toward employer pension contributions and retained earnings inside the company; dividend extraction is restrained to current-cash needs. 50-60 (pension access nearing, retirement runway visible), the mix shifts toward maximising the pension annual allowance in the run-up to age 55 (rising to 57 from April 2028) and toward stepping up dividend extraction to balance liquidity. Post-retirement (income from other sources may be lower, basic-rate band re-opens), dividend extraction at the basic rate of 10.75% can become very efficient; an MVL exit may be on the table if the founder is winding down property activity. The age-zone framing is approximate and depends on the founder's wider financial position; it is a planning anchor, not a rule."
  - question: "Can I switch the extraction sequence mid-tax-year?"
    answer: "Yes, with care. The dividend strand requires a board minute and distributable reserves at the date of declaration (Companies Act 2006 s.830); switching from monthly dividend extraction to lump-sum pension contribution mid-year is mechanically straightforward. The salary strand requires PAYE registration and monthly RTI submission; stopping or changing a salary mid-year needs a P46 / P45-style event on the payroll. The DLA repayment strand is informal and can flex. The pension strand has annual-allowance constraints and the contribution must be received by the scheme before the end of the SPV's accounting period to count for that period's corporation tax deduction. The single most common mid-year switch we see is the founder ramping employer pension contributions in February and March to use surplus distributable cash before year-end; the discipline is to confirm distributable reserves are in place and that the contribution is genuinely employer-side (not a director recycling personal cash through the company)."
  - question: "What is the dividend-band cliff?"
    answer: "It is the jump from the basic-rate dividend rate of 10.75% (on dividend income within the £50,270 total income band) to the higher-rate dividend rate of 35.75% (on dividend income in the £50,270 to £125,140 band) to the additional-rate dividend rate of 39.35% (above £125,140). Each cliff represents a step-change in the marginal cost of taking an additional dividend pound. A founder whose total income is at £49,000 paying themselves an additional £2,000 of dividend will see the first £1,270 of that dividend taxed at 10.75% (basic) and the remaining £730 at 35.75% (higher). The cliff is the planning anchor for deciding when to stop dividend extraction and switch to pension or to leaving cash in the company. The cliff also moves year by year as the basic and higher-rate thresholds shift; verify against gov.uk before any client decision."
  - question: "How does a HoldCo structure change the sequencing?"
    answer: "A HoldCo (a parent company sitting above one or more property SPVs) opens dividend extraction routes that are unavailable to a single-SPV founder. Dividends flow up from the SPVs to the HoldCo as inter-company dividends, exempt from corporation tax under CTA 2009 s.931A (dividends received by UK companies are generally exempt). The HoldCo can then declare a dividend to the founder, and the founder pays personal dividend tax on that declared amount. The cash inside the HoldCo can also fund further property acquisitions through a sister SPV, recycle into pension contributions for the founder, or sit as retained earnings. The HoldCo structure is also the natural staging ground for an SSE-eligible share sale (see our <a href=\"/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/substantial-shareholding-exemption-property-companies\">SSE for property companies page</a>) where the underlying activity is development rather than passive let. The detailed mechanics are on our <a href=\"/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/multi-company-group-extraction-spv-holding-co-dividend-conduit-mechanics\">HoldCo extraction page</a>."
  - question: "What is the difference between this page and the salary-vs-dividends page?"
    answer: "The salary-vs-dividends page is a single-year point-in-time analysis. It works the 2026/27 marginal-rate stack at four profit bands (£30,000, £50,000, £100,000, £125,000) and identifies the right one-year mix. This page is the multi-year sequencer: it walks the sequence across five years (or longer), incorporates DLA exhaustion, the dividend-band cliff and the founder's age into the decision tree, and surfaces the moments when the optimal one-year mix is the wrong multi-year choice.   - question: "What happens to the extraction sequence if I'm planning an MVL exit in 18 months?"
    answer: "An MVL exit changes the optimal sequence sharply. In the 18 months before MVL, the goal is usually to extract any DLA credit balance fully (otherwise it sits as a debt the liquidator has to settle, with no further tax benefit), to wind down dividend extraction (because the residual reserves will be capital-distributed under TCGA 1992 s.122 at 18% or 24%, often more efficient than higher-rate dividend extraction), and to consider front-loading employer pension contributions if there is surplus distributable cash. The 18-month run-up to MVL is also the period when any cross-bucket considerations (timing of property sales, redemption of any debt to founder, settlement of overdrawn DLA) need to land. The s.396B targeted anti-avoidance rule then runs for 2 years from the date of the final distribution, so the sequence does not end at MVL completion; the founder's forward activity within those 2 years still affects the MVL tax outcome. See our <a href=\"/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/mvl-members-voluntary-liquidation-property-company-cgt-vs-income-treatment\">MVL page</a> for the full mechanics."
  - question: "What documentation supports the sequence I choose?"
    answer: "Five documents anchor the sequence year by year. First, a written extraction plan signed off by the director(s) at the start of each accounting period, recording the planned mix of salary, dividend, employer pension and DLA repayment for that year. Second, board minutes for each dividend declaration with distributable-reserves evidence attached. Third, payroll records and P60s for the salary strand. Fourth, pension contribution receipts and a record of annual-allowance and carry-forward usage. Fifth, a running DLA ledger showing the credit balance opening, repayments through the year, and closing balance. The discipline is contemporaneous: the documentation has to be created at the time of the decision, not retrospectively at Self Assessment time. HMRC's standard challenge pattern at enquiry is to look for retrospectively-dated minutes or bank movements that don't line up with the minute dates; the contemporaneous file is the defence."

<p>The question is rarely which extraction route from a property SPV, but in what order, and how the order shifts year by year as the director's loan account credit exhausts, the dividend band cliffs from basic to higher to additional rate, and the founder ages toward retirement. Six routes are in scope (DLA repayment, dividends, salary at the secondary national insurance threshold, employer pension contributions, share buyback, members' voluntary liquidation), each with its own tax profile and its own depth page on this site. This pillar is the umbrella decision tree: how the six routes layer into a coherent multi-year sequence against the 2026/27 corporation tax stack, the post-6-April-2026 dividend rates, the post-6-April-2025 employer national insurance shift, and the founder's age and post-retirement runway.</p>

<p>The page sits alongside two existing decision-frame pages that it does not duplicate. Our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/extracting-money-from-property-limited-company">extracting money from a property limited company</a> page lists the per-route mechanics: how each lever works in isolation. Our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/salary-vs-dividends-property-spv-2026-27-marginal-rate-analysis">salary vs dividends 2026/27 page</a> walks the single-year marginal-rate stack at four profit bands. This page is the multi-year sequencer above both: it answers how to combine the levers over a five to ten year programme rather than which lever to pull this year.</p>

<h2>The six extraction routes at a glance</h2>

<p>This section is one paragraph per route. Each route has its own depth page on this site; the paragraphs here cover only what is needed to position the route in the multi-year sequence.</p>

<p><strong>1. Director's loan account repayment.</strong> A credit balance on the DLA, typically built up from an <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/section-162-incorporation-relief-property-landlords">s.162 incorporation transfer</a> or from undrawn historic dividends, can be repaid to the director tax-free. Repayment is settlement of a debt the company owes; no income tax, no national insurance, no dividend tax. The route only works while the credit balance lasts. Each pound of DLA repayment reduces the company's cash but does not affect the SPV's profit and loss, so there is no corporation tax leg. An overdrawn DLA at year-end triggers the 33.75% s.455 charge under <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2010/4/section/455">CTA 2010 s.455</a>, refundable on later repayment; the DLA-as-extraction discipline assumes the balance is in credit throughout. The bed-and-breakfasting anti-avoidance interaction is covered separately on our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/directors-loan-repayment-bed-and-breakfast-trap-s464c-s464d">DLA bed-and-breakfast trap page</a>.</p>

<p><strong>2. Dividends.</strong> Paid out of post-corporation-tax profits, taxed at 0% within the £500 dividend allowance and then at 10.75% (basic rate), 35.75% (higher rate) or 39.35% (additional rate) depending on the recipient's total income band for 2026/27. The corporation tax paid by the company before the dividend is declared is not credited to the recipient; the dividend tax is on the gross dividend received. Dividends require sufficient distributable reserves at the date of declaration under <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2006/46/section/830">CA 2006 s.830</a>. The single-year band cliff (basic, higher, additional) is one of the two structural cliffs that shape the multi-year sequence.</p>

<p><strong>3. Salary at the secondary national insurance threshold.</strong> A salary of £5,000 a year (the secondary threshold from the Reeves Autumn Budget 2024 reform, in force from 6 April 2025) attracts no employer NI, no employee NI, no income tax, and is corporation-tax-deductible. It counts toward a national insurance qualifying year for the new state pension. Going above £5,000 for a sole-director SPV triggers employer NI at 15% on every pound, with no Employment Allowance offset under the sole-director exclusion. The £5,000 salary is the floor extraction strand for most property SPVs; the next decision points are when (if ever) to step it up to the personal allowance level of £12,570.</p>

<p><strong>4. Employer pension contributions.</strong> Made by the company directly into the director's pension scheme, deductible against corporation tax under <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2009/4/section/54">CTA 2009 s.54</a> (subject to the wholly-and-exclusively test), with no income tax at the contribution moment. The annual allowance is £60,000 in 2026/27 (tapered down to £10,000 for adjusted incomes above £260,000). Unused annual allowance from the three previous tax years can be carried forward, supporting one-off contributions of £180,000-plus where the company has the post-CT cash. Pension funds are locked until age 55 (rising to 57 from April 2028). The route is structurally dominant once the founder's other extraction strands push them into the higher-rate dividend band. The wholly-and-exclusively analysis is covered in detail on our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/property-spv-employer-pension-contributions-wholly-and-exclusively-test-mechanics">employer pension contributions page</a>.</p>

<p><strong>5. Share buyback.</strong> The company purchases its own shares from a shareholder under sections 690 to 708 of the Companies Act 2006, financed from distributable reserves. The default tax treatment is as a distribution (dividend rates); capital treatment under CTA 2010 s.1033 requires the buyback to be for the benefit of the trade of the company, a gate that fails by default for a property investment SPV (which carries on a business, not a trade). Buyback is a specialist route rather than a routine extraction lever, and is generally NOT a workable substitute for an MVL for a property investment SPV. Our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/property-spv-share-buyback-out-of-distributable-reserves-mechanics">share buyback mechanics page</a> walks the s.1033(2) trade-benefit failure mode in detail.</p>

<p><strong>6. Members' voluntary liquidation.</strong> The company-exit endpoint, not a recurring extraction route. The liquidator distributes residual reserves to shareholders as capital under <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12/section/122">TCGA 1992 s.122</a>; the shareholder is taxed at CGT rates of 18% or 24% on the deemed disposal of their shares (no BADR for pure investment SPVs because the Pawson trading-company test in TCGA 1992 s.169I fails). The ITTOIA 2005 s.396B targeted anti-avoidance rule catches phoenix arrangements where the founder restarts similar activity within 2 years. Full mechanics on our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/mvl-members-voluntary-liquidation-property-company-cgt-vs-income-treatment">MVL page</a>.</p>

<h2>The 2026/27 marginal-rate spine</h2>

<p>Three stacks frame every extraction decision: the corporation tax stack at the company level, the income tax band stack at the personal level, and the national insurance stack on salary income. The figures here are the framework anchors; verify each against gov.uk before any client decision.</p>

<p><strong>Corporation tax 2026/27.</strong> 19% on profits up to £50,000 (small profits rate under <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2010/4/section/18A">CTA 2010 s.18A</a>); 26.5% effective marginal rate in the £50,000 to £250,000 marginal-relief band; 25% main rate above £250,000. Associated-company aggregation under <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2010/4/section/18E">CTA 2010 s.18E</a> divides the £50,000 and £250,000 thresholds by the number of associated companies. The deeper mechanics are on our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/corporation-tax-marginal-relief-property-companies">corporation tax marginal relief page</a>; for sequencing purposes the key inputs are the effective rate at the SPV's actual profit level and the rate's interaction with the deductible extraction strands.</p>

<p><strong>Income tax bands 2026/27.</strong> Personal allowance £12,570 (taper above £100,000), basic-rate band to £50,270 of total income, higher-rate band to £125,140, additional rate above. The bands are critical to the dividend strand because each band has its own dividend rate (10.75%, 35.75%, 39.35%). The band cliffs are the second of the two structural cliffs that shape multi-year sequencing.</p>

<p><strong>National insurance 2026/27.</strong> Primary threshold (employee NI starts) £12,570 a year; secondary threshold (employer NI starts) £5,000 a year; employee NI 8% in the basic-rate band, 2% above; employer NI 15% above the secondary threshold; Employment Allowance £10,500 (sole-director SPVs excluded). The standard property-SPV salary floor of £5,000 is set by the secondary threshold under the Reeves Autumn Budget 2024 reform.</p>

<p><strong>Dividend rates 2026/27.</strong> £500 allowance, then 10.75% / 35.75% / 39.35% above the allowance, by income band. The basic and higher rates rose by 2 percentage points from 6 April 2026 (Autumn Budget 2024 reform); the additional rate is unchanged at 39.35%. Worked examples in this pillar use the post-6-April-2026 rates.</p>

<h2>The founder-age dimension</h2>

<p>The optimal extraction mix depends on the founder's age more than most generic salary-vs-dividends content acknowledges. Three age zones shape the sequence in materially different ways.</p>

<p><strong>Pre-50.</strong> Long pension lock-in (10-plus years before access at 55, rising to 57 from April 2028); high annual-allowance carry-forward room from previous years; long compounding window inside the pension wrapper. The mix tilts heavily toward employer pension contributions and toward leaving retained earnings inside the company for the next property purchase. Dividend extraction is restrained to current-cash needs (mortgage, school fees, lifestyle baseline). Salary is at the £5,000 secondary-threshold floor.</p>

<p><strong>50 to 60.</strong> Pension access nearing, retirement runway visible. The mix shifts toward maximising the pension annual allowance in the run-up to age 55 (rising to 57 from April 2028) and toward stepping up dividend extraction to balance liquidity. The DLA-exhaustion question becomes important: a founder with a £400,000 DLA credit balance at age 50 needs to plan whether to extract it before retirement (using the tax-free repayment route) or to let it sit and be cleared on a future MVL or HoldCo restructure.</p>

<p><strong>Post-retirement.</strong> Income from other sources may be lower, basic-rate band re-opens, dividend extraction at the basic rate of 10.75% can become very efficient. An MVL exit may be on the table if the founder is winding down property activity. The s.396B 2-year similar-activity window then runs and constrains any post-MVL restart. The full mechanics on the MVL exit are on the depth page; sequencing here is the question of whether the SPV stays alive (continued dividend / pension extraction) or winds down (MVL distribution at CGT rates).</p>

<h2>The five-year extraction sequence: a worked persona</h2>

<p>Persona: Sarah, age 48 at the start of the period. Single-director property SPV holding a portfolio of six BTL flats. Annual SPV profit before extraction approximately £85,000 (rental net of mortgage interest, repairs, agent fees, with no associated companies). DLA credit balance at start of period £180,000 (built from a 2022 s.162 incorporation transfer). Personal income outside the SPV: £8,000 of casual freelance income. Sarah has £50,000 of unused pension annual allowance carry-forward from the three previous tax years.</p>

<p><strong>Year 1.</strong> Salary £5,000 (secondary-threshold floor, NI-free, IT-free). DLA repayment £30,000 (tax-free repayment of debt; DLA balance moves from £180,000 to £150,000). Dividend £35,000 within Sarah's basic-rate band given total income of (£5,000 salary + £8,000 freelance + £35,000 dividend = £48,000, just under £50,270 higher-rate cliff). Dividend tax: (£35,000 - £500 allowance) × 10.75% = £3,709. Employer pension contribution £20,000 (deductible against CT; current-year annual allowance still has £40,000 unused). SPV profit after £25,000 of deductible extraction (£5,000 salary + £20,000 pension) is £60,000; CT at marginal-relief band: approximately £60,000 × 26.5% effective = £15,900. Distributable post-CT cash £44,100; £35,000 dividend declared. £9,100 retained. Sarah's net cash extraction year 1: £5,000 + £30,000 + £35,000 = £70,000 with personal tax cost of £3,709.</p>

<p><strong>Year 2.</strong> Same shape; DLA balance moves from £150,000 to £120,000. Pension contribution stepped up to £30,000 using current-year and prior-year unused allowance. Profit after deductible extraction £50,000; CT at small-profits rate 19% = £9,500. Distributable post-CT cash £40,500. Dividend extraction reduced to £30,000 to stay below the higher-rate cliff (total income £5,000 salary + £8,000 freelance + £30,000 dividend = £43,000, comfortable headroom). The mix shift recognises the carry-forward pension room and the moderate freelance income.</p>

<p><strong>Year 3.</strong> DLA balance at start of year £90,000. Salary £5,000. DLA repayment £30,000 (balance to £60,000). Pension contribution £40,000 (using the rest of the carry-forward room; current-year allowance still has £20,000 unused for year 4). Dividend reduced to £20,000. Total extraction £55,000 with very low personal tax cost (£2,096 on the dividend). The retained-earnings approach is starting to bite; the SPV's balance sheet is accumulating cash that will need a forward home (next property purchase, further pension contributions, or eventually an MVL distribution).</p>

<p><strong>Year 4.</strong> DLA balance at start of year £30,000. Sarah extracts the last £30,000 of DLA credit. Pension contribution £60,000 (current-year annual allowance fully used; no more carry-forward). Salary £5,000. Dividend £25,000 (within basic-rate band). Sarah's DLA-exhaustion event is now visible on the horizon.</p>

<p><strong>Year 5.</strong> DLA balance £0. The mix shifts: no more tax-free DLA repayment. Salary £5,000. Pension contribution £60,000 (annual allowance maxed). Dividend £35,000 within basic-rate band (total income £5,000 + £8,000 + £35,000 = £48,000). Total extraction £100,000 with personal tax cost of £3,709, but the structure has now lost the tax-free repayment route. Future-year extraction at this level requires either crossing into the higher-rate dividend band (35.75% on the next pound) or accepting that retained earnings stay in the SPV.</p>

<p>The cumulative pattern: across 5 years, Sarah has extracted £325,000 in cash to herself, contributed £210,000 to her pension, paid approximately £15,500 of personal dividend tax, and exhausted her DLA credit. The post-5-year sequence then enters phase 2 (covered in part on our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/extraction-while-incorporating-phase-2-acquisition-funded-by-personal-funds">phase 2 acquisition page</a>): higher-rate dividend extraction, continued employer pension, and eventual decision on MVL exit or HoldCo restructure.</p>

<aside><p>Your DLA exhaustion year is the single most important forward-looking variable.</p><p>The sequencing you choose in years 1-3 directly determines when the tax-free route closes. A founder with a £600,000 credit DLA can extend the runway by sequencing pension contributions earlier and DLA repayment later; a founder with a £120,000 credit DLA has very little planning room. The dialog with us starts with the current DLA balance and the founder's intended forward cash needs.</p></aside>

<h2>The DLA-exhaustion cliff</h2>

<p>The DLA credit balance is the single most powerful tax-free extraction strand a property SPV founder can have, and its exhaustion is a one-way event. Once the balance hits zero, future extraction at the same total annual level requires the founder to step up dividend extraction (with the personal tax cost that goes with it) or step up pension contributions (with the access-timing constraint that goes with that) or accept reduced annual cash extraction with surplus cash sitting in the SPV.</p>

<p>The planning question is not "how do I avoid DLA exhaustion" (it cannot be avoided indefinitely; the credit balance is finite) but "when should DLA exhaustion happen". Pushing the exhaustion point forward (extracting DLA early in the cycle) front-loads the tax-free runway but leaves the founder facing higher post-exhaustion personal tax for longer. Pushing the exhaustion point back (running DLA repayment lightly while ramping pension contributions) extends the tax-free runway but ties up cash in the pension wrapper for longer.</p>

<p>For founders within 5 years of intended retirement and MVL exit, the working answer is usually to clear the DLA credit before the MVL because any residual credit balance becomes a debt the liquidator settles (cash out the door but with no further tax benefit beyond what was already in scope). For founders 10-plus years from intended exit, the answer is more open-ended and depends on the trade-off between current-year cash extraction and pension-allowance utilisation.</p>

<h2>The dividend-band cliff</h2>

<p>The second structural cliff is the dividend rate transition at the higher-rate threshold (£50,270 of total income in 2026/27). Crossing from 10.75% (basic) to 35.75% (higher) more than triples the marginal cost of a dividend pound. The cliff is the reason why most SPV founders sequence dividends to stop at the higher-rate threshold and switch to employer pension contributions for any further extraction.</p>

<p>Two patterns reshape the cliff. First, where the founder has substantial other personal income (freelance, employment elsewhere, rental income from personally-held property), the dividend headroom below the higher-rate cliff shrinks accordingly. A founder with £30,000 of other income has only £20,270 of dividend headroom at the basic rate; above that, every additional dividend pound is at 35.75%. Second, where the SPV has multiple shareholders (spouse on B-class shares, adult children on C-class), each shareholder has their own dividend allowance and their own basic-rate band; the household total dividend extraction at the basic rate is multiplied accordingly. The alphabet-share design discipline is on our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/alphabet-shares-property-spv-dividend-splitting-spouse-children">alphabet shares page</a>.</p>

<h2>The HoldCo and trust-owned-SPV overlay</h2>

<p>Two structural overlays change the sequence materially. The HoldCo overlay (a parent company sitting above one or more property SPVs) opens inter-company dividend flows under <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2009/4/section/931A">CTA 2009 s.931A</a> (UK-company dividends are generally exempt from corporation tax in the receiving company), allowing cash to accumulate inside the HoldCo and be recycled into further property acquisitions, pension contributions, or eventual founder dividend. The detailed mechanics including the AIA-across-associated-SPVs implication are on our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/multi-company-group-extraction-spv-holding-co-dividend-conduit-mechanics">HoldCo extraction page</a>.</p>

<p>The trust-owned-SPV overlay (where the SPV's shares are held by a settlor-interested or non-settlor-interested trust) constrains the sequence on the personal side because the founder's extraction can be re-characterised under the settlements legislation (ITTOIA 2005 s.624 and s.629) or the IHT settlor-interest rules. The depth treatment is on our <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/directors-of-trust-owned-spv-extraction-rules-settlor-interested-trap">trust-owned SPV extraction page</a>.</p>

<h2>Common sequencing failure modes</h2>

<p>Five patterns we see repeatedly when reviewing existing property-SPV extraction plans.</p>

<p><strong>Dividend-first, pension-last.</strong> A founder takes dividends to fund current cash needs without sequencing employer pension contributions first. The result is dividend tax paid (10.75% basic or 35.75% higher) when the same gross extraction could have been routed through employer pension contributions at zero personal tax cost. The defensive move is to model pension first, dividend second, in any year where the founder's age and access-timing constraints permit it.</p>

<p><strong>DLA repayment delayed too long.</strong> A founder leaves a £400,000 DLA credit balance sitting on the books for 10 years while extracting via dividend. The tax-free repayment route was available throughout but went unused; the cumulative cost is the dividend tax paid on amounts that could have been DLA repayments. The defensive move is to track DLA balance year by year and ensure repayment runs alongside the other strands at a tempo that uses the credit before any planned MVL exit.</p>

<p><strong>Salary set above the secondary threshold without Employment Allowance.</strong> A sole-director SPV runs a salary at £12,570 (the personal allowance) without recognising that Employment Allowance does not apply to sole-director companies. Employer NI at 15% on every pound above the £5,000 secondary threshold then attaches; the marginal cost is high relative to a £5,000 salary supplemented by dividends. The defensive move is to confirm Employment Allowance eligibility before running any salary above the secondary threshold.</p>

<p><strong>Pension contributions exceeding the wholly-and-exclusively test.</strong> A founder pushes employer pension contributions to a level that HMRC challenges as not being commensurate with the director's work for the company under <a href="https://www.gov.uk/hmrc-internal-manuals/business-income-manual/bim46035">BIM46035</a> guidance. Contributions disproportionate to the director's role can be denied as a corporation tax deduction. The defensive move is to align the contribution with documented director duties and to keep the contribution within a defensible band relative to the director's salary and role.</p>

<p><strong>MVL exit planned without 2-year forward planning for s.396B.</strong> A founder MVLs the SPV intending to restart property investment through a fresh structure shortly afterwards. The ITTOIA 2005 s.396B TAAR catches the restart and re-characterises the MVL distribution as income at dividend rates. The defensive move is to confirm the founder's forward intent before triggering the MVL.</p>

<h2>Decision-tree quick reference</h2>

<p>For a single-director property SPV in 2026/27, the working sequence each year is:</p>

<ol>
<li>Salary £5,000 at the secondary national insurance threshold (no NI, no IT, CT-deductible). Always, unless the founder already has 35-plus NI qualifying years and explicitly does not want the deduction.</li>
<li>DLA credit repayment up to the founder's cash needs (no tax). Until the credit balance hits zero.</li>
<li>Dividend within the basic-rate band (10.75% above £500 allowance). Capped by other personal income approaching the £50,270 higher-rate threshold.</li>
<li>Employer pension contribution up to current-year annual allowance plus carry-forward (deductible, no income tax at contribution moment). For founders with lock-in tolerance.</li>
<li>Higher-rate dividend (35.75% above £50,270 to £125,140) only where current cash need exceeds the above strands. Often a sign the founder should be extracting differently.</li>
<li>Additional-rate dividend (39.35% above £125,140) only in exceptional cases. Usually a signal that the structure (single SPV, single shareholder) is constraining a position that should be re-structured (HoldCo, alphabet shares, FIC).</li>
</ol>
