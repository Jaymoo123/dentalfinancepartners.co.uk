faqs:
  - question: "What is a chargeable lifetime transfer and why does settling property into a discretionary trust trigger it?"
    answer: "A chargeable lifetime transfer (CLT) under IHTA 1984 is a transfer of value made during the donor's lifetime that does not qualify as a Potentially Exempt Transfer (PET). A transfer into a relevant property trust, including most discretionary trusts, is a CLT because the gift is not made to a specific individual (the PET requirement at s.3A IHTA 1984) but into the trust as a non-personal entity. The 20% lifetime IHT rate at s.7(2) IHTA 1984 then applies to the value of the transfer above the settlor's available NRB, with the donor or trustees liable to pay. The CLT differs from a PET in three key ways: it pays IHT at the time of the gift (PETs pay nothing at the time); the IHT is at 20% lifetime rate (not the 40% death rate); the trust is itself a separate tax-paying entity subject to periodic and exit charges under the relevant property regime."
  - question: "How much is the 20% entry charge on a property settlement?"
    answer: "20% IHT on the value of the property settled, above the settlor's available NRB. Available NRB is computed as £325,000 less any 7-year cumulative gifts the settlor has already made (including any earlier CLTs in the 7 years before this one). Worked: settlor with £325,000 unused NRB transfers a £400,000 BTL into a discretionary trust. CLT value £400,000, less NRB £325,000 = chargeable element £75,000. Lifetime IHT at 20% = £15,000 payable to HMRC within 6 months of the end of the month of transfer. The settlor or the trustees pay (typically the trustees from trust funds, with the trust then bearing the IHT economic cost). If the settlor pays from their own funds, the payment itself is not a further transfer (the IHT is treated as part of the same chargeable transfer for grossing-up purposes; the gross gift becomes £375,000 plus the £15,000 IHT paid by the settlor, with the gross-up rate at 1/(1-0.20) = 1.25 making the chargeable element £93,750 and the IHT £18,750, with the donor's payment being grossed up because they bear the tax)."
  - question: "What happens if the settlor dies within 7 years of the CLT?"
    answer: "An additional IHT charge may arise, computed as the full IHT rate (40%) less the 20% already paid, less any taper relief under s.7(4) IHTA 1984 between years 3 and 7. Worked: settlor transfers £400,000 BTL into trust in year 0, pays £15,000 lifetime IHT on the £75,000 chargeable element. Settlor dies in year 5. The £75,000 chargeable element is now subject to the 40% death rate, taper relief 40% (year 5 to 6 band) = effective rate 16%. Tax: 16% × £75,000 = £12,000. Less the £15,000 already paid = £3,000 REFUND to the donor's estate, because the tapered death rate is less than the lifetime rate already paid. The mechanic gives donors who survive between 3 and 7 years a partial reclaim of the lifetime IHT. Donors who die within 3 years pay no taper relief: 40% on £75,000 = £30,000, less £15,000 already paid = £15,000 additional. Donors surviving 7 years: the £15,000 lifetime IHT is final; no further charge."
  - question: "What is the 10-year periodic charge?"
    answer: "Section 64 IHTA 1984 imposes a periodic charge on the value of a relevant property trust every 10 years from the trust's creation date. The maximum rate is 6% of the trust assets above the available NRB at the periodic-charge date, less any 7-year cumulative CLTs the settlor had made before settling the trust. The rate is computed by reference to a hypothetical chargeable transfer formula that adjusts for the relevant property regime's grossing-up and time-apportionment. The actual rate is typically below 6% on most trusts because the formula incorporates the NRB available at the periodic-charge date and the time since the trust was created. Worked: £400,000 BTL grew to £530,000 by year 10. Available NRB at year 10 (assuming no further settlor gifts and frozen NRB at £325,000 to April 2030): £325,000. Chargeable trust value at year 10: £530,000 minus £325,000 = £205,000. Periodic charge at maximum 6%: £12,300. Actual rate may be lower depending on formula adjustments; the trustees compute on form IHT100d at the 10-year anniversary and pay within 6 months."
  - question: "What is the exit charge?"
    answer: "Section 65 IHTA 1984 imposes a proportionate charge on capital distributions made out of a relevant property trust between 10-year anniversaries. The rate is based on the rate of the most recent periodic charge, time-apportioned to the date of the exit. A £100,000 capital distribution from a trust that paid a 4% periodic charge at year 10 and is making the distribution at year 14 (40% of the way through the next 10-year period) would attract an exit charge at approximately 4% × 40% = 1.6% × £100,000 = £1,600. The full mechanic is at IHTM42100 onwards. Exit charges apply to all capital distributions; income distributions are taxed in the recipient's hands at their marginal income tax rate but do not trigger exit charges. The exit charge is computed and paid by the trustees on form IHT100c at the time of the distribution."
  - question: "When is the s.260 CGT holdover available?"
    answer: "Where the property settlement triggers an immediate IHT charge (i.e., is a CLT into a relevant property trust), the settlor and trustees can jointly elect under s.260 TCGA 1992 to hold over the CGT gain on the transfer in. The election rolls the gain into the trust's base cost: the settlor pays no immediate CGT on the gift; the trust takes the property at the settlor's base cost; any subsequent gain on the trust's disposal of the property is computed against that historic base cost. The election is on the donor's CGT return for the year of the transfer. The s.260 election is what makes the CLT route viable for landlords with large latent gains on BTL properties: without the holdover, a £400,000 BTL with a £250,000 gain would trigger £50,000 to £60,000 of immediate CGT on top of the IHT cost, undermining the planning rationale."
  - question: "What is the settlor-interested trust exclusion and why does it matter?"
    answer: "Sections 169B to 169G TCGA 1992 disapply s.260 holdover where the trust is settlor-interested. A settlor-interested trust is one in which the settlor, the settlor's spouse, the settlor's minor unmarried children, or a corporate body controlled by the settlor or those persons is or may become a beneficiary. The exclusion is the most common landlord misstep on this route: a parent settles a BTL into a discretionary trust intending to benefit the children but draws the beneficiary class wide enough to include the parent themselves as a discretionary beneficiary (in case of need, or for tax-flexibility reasons), and the s.260 holdover is denied. The CGT then crystallises immediately on the transfer in at market value under s.17 TCGA 1992. The fix: draft the beneficiary class to exclude the settlor and settlor's spouse expressly, and document the exclusion in the trust deed. The trustees retain discretion over all other family members within the class (children, grandchildren, future descendants), which is usually wide enough for the family's planning purposes without triggering the exclusion."
  - question: "What is the worked total cost on a £400,000 BTL settled into a clean (non-settlor-interested) trust?"
    answer: "The Patel-settlement worked example. Mr Patel, settlor, age 60, transfers a £400,000 BTL property into a discretionary trust for the benefit of his two adult children (settlor and spouse expressly excluded from beneficiary class). The BTL was acquired in 2010 for £160,000 plus £4,800 SDLT plus £1,600 legal fees plus £20,000 enhancement = £186,400 base cost. Latent gain: £213,600. Year 0 costs. IHT (CLT): £400,000 value, less £325,000 NRB (no prior gifts) = £75,000 chargeable at 20% = £15,000 paid by trustees within 6 months. CGT: s.260 election made, gain £213,600 held over into trust's base cost (trust now has base cost £186,400, settlor pays no CGT). SDLT: gift between connected persons with no consideration, so the standard 0% SDLT applies (no debt assumption; if debt had been assumed by the trustees, SDLT would apply on the debt-assumed amount). Year 0 total tax: £15,000. By contrast, a direct PET gift to the children would have triggered no entry IHT but £40,000 to £50,000 CGT immediately under s.17 (no s.260, no s.165 for non-business BTL), so the CLT route is cheaper at the entry point by roughly £25,000 to £35,000. Year 10 onwards. 10-year periodic charge as computed above (up to 6% on trust value above NRB at year 10, formula-adjusted, in the order of £8,000 to £12,000). Exit charges on capital distributions at proportionate rates."
  - question: "How does the CLT route compare to FIC value-freezing?"
    answer: "Different trade-offs at the entry point and during the running period. CLT into discretionary trust: 20% entry IHT above NRB (£15,000 on a £400,000 BTL); s.260 CGT holdover available if non-settlor-interested; 10-year periodic charges and exit charges; trust runs as a separate tax-paying entity. FIC value-freeze: zero entry IHT (the FIC formation itself is not a chargeable transfer; the growth-share gift to children is a PET starting the 7-year clock); no s.260 holdover (s.260 requires a CLT trigger which doesn't arise on FIC formation), so CGT on the share gift is at market value under s.17 (potentially minority-discounted, reducing the bill); the FIC runs as a CT-paying corporate entity at the 25% main rate on rental income net of finance costs. The two routes are not equivalent: the trust delivers IHT certainty up front (the CLT enters the trust at a known IHT cost) and CGT deferral; the FIC delivers no entry IHT but immediate CGT and the rental income is then inside the corporate wrapper. For most landlord families with substantial latent CGT gains, the s.260 holdover swings the balance toward the trust route. For families with low CGT gains and a strong preference for keeping the income inside a corporate wrapper, the FIC route may dominate. Our forthcoming Wave 4 page on FIC value-freeze (C7) covers the FIC side in detail."
  - question: "How does the CLT route compare to a direct PET gift?"
    answer: "Direct PET gift to an individual: no entry IHT (the gift becomes IHT-free if the donor survives 7 years); CGT at market value immediately under s.17 (no holdover for non-business BTL); donor must survive 7 years for the gift to fully escape the estate. CLT into trust: 20% entry IHT above NRB (immediate cost); s.260 CGT holdover (no immediate CGT); 10-year periodic charges (ongoing cost). The PET route is cheaper at the entry point if the donor has no significant latent CGT gain or is willing to pay the CGT (and the donor expects to survive 7 years). The CLT route is cheaper at the entry point if the donor has a significant latent CGT gain and wants to defer it (typical for long-held BTL properties acquired in the early 2000s). Both routes leave the property outside the donor's estate, but the trust then has its own 10-year periodic charges that the direct PET does not. The decision usually turns on the donor's CGT latent gain plus the donor's age and survival probability over 7 years."
  - question: "Does SDLT apply on a settlement into trust?"
    answer: "SDLT applies where there is chargeable consideration. A pure settlement of property into trust with no debt assumed by the trustees and no other consideration is outside SDLT (the gift is treated as a transfer for no consideration). Where the trustees assume the outstanding mortgage on the gifted property as part of the settlement, the assumed debt is chargeable consideration in HMRC's view, and SDLT is charged on the assumed-debt figure. For a £400,000 BTL with a £180,000 outstanding mortgage assumed by the trustees, the SDLT chargeable consideration is £180,000, SDLT approximately £8,400 (with 3% additional-dwelling surcharge assuming the trustees would be treated as a non-natural person purchasing residential property). Most landlord-trust settlements first refinance or repay the mortgage out of the settlor's name before the settlement, transferring a clean title to the trust to avoid the SDLT cost."
  - question: "Who is liable for the 20% lifetime IHT, settlor or trustees?"
    answer: "Joint and several liability between the settlor and the trustees, with the settlor as primary debtor under s.199 IHTA 1984. Practically, the trustees often pay the IHT from trust funds (where the trust has liquid assets at settlement time, such as a portion of cash settled alongside the property). Where the settlor pays from personal funds, the payment itself is not a further chargeable transfer but it does trigger the grossing-up rule at s.5(2) IHTA 1984: the gross-up factor 1/(1-0.20) = 1.25 increases the chargeable element to incorporate the IHT itself. The trustees should be authorised in the trust deed to settle the IHT liability from trust funds; if they are not, the settlor's payment is the default route. The lifetime IHT is due within 6 months of the end of the month of transfer; HMRC charges interest on late payment from the 7th month onwards."
  - question: "Can a CLT into trust be combined with the deed-of-variation route?"
    answer: "Yes, in a useful pattern for couples. The first-to-die spouse's will leaves everything to the surviving spouse under the s.18 spouse exemption. Within 2 years of first death, the surviving spouse executes a deed of variation (covered on Wave 4 C5) redirecting £325,000 of property (matching the deceased's NRB) into a discretionary trust for the adult children and grandchildren. The variation reads back to the deceased for IHT under s.142, with the deceased's NRB applied to the settlement, so no IHT is payable on the variation. The deceased's settlement is then a CLT-equivalent within the NRB, with no entry charge. The CGT side (with the s.62(6) election) treats the disposal as having been made by the deceased; the trust takes the property at the deceased's date-of-death uplifted base cost. This combined deed-of-variation-plus-trust route achieves a clean trust settlement within the deceased's NRB without any immediate IHT or CGT cost, removing £325,000 of property from the surviving spouse's second-death estate. The trust then runs under the standard 10-year and exit charge regime. The route is one of the most powerful planning moves available to surviving spouses in the deed-of-variation window."
  - question: "When is a CLT into trust definitely the wrong route?"
    answer: "Three scenarios. One: the settlor draws the beneficiary class to include themselves or their spouse (settlor-interested trust), losing the s.260 holdover. Without the holdover the CGT crystallises immediately at market value, often making the route more expensive than a PET. Two: the settlor expects to live more than 7 years and has no large latent CGT gain, in which case the direct PET route delivers the same end result (property outside the estate) without any entry IHT charge. Three: the settlement is wanted purely to defer the IHT exposure to second death, in which case the s.18 spouse exemption plus the deed-of-variation flexibility within 2 years of first death is the cleaner route. The CLT-into-trust route is right when the family wants: defined control through trustees (rather than direct ownership by individuals), CGT deferral via s.260, and the structural separation of the property from any family member's personal estate. For all other scenarios, one of the alternatives (PET, FIC, DoV) is usually cheaper or simpler."

<p>Settling property into a discretionary trust during life is the third route in the landlord IHT planning trio alongside direct lifetime gifts (the seven-year clock, covered on our <a href="/blog/landlord-tax-essentials/iht-7-year-clock-property-gifting-mid-life-landlord-strategy">Wave 4 C4 page</a>) and Family Investment Company structures (Wave 4 C7, forthcoming). The chargeable-lifetime-transfer (CLT) mechanic triggers a 20% IHT charge at the point of settlement on the value above the settlor's available nil-rate band, with the trust then running under the relevant property regime's 10-year periodic charges and exit charges. The CGT side hinges on s.260 TCGA 1992 holdover, which is the route's strategic value (it defers the CGT into the trust's base cost rather than crystallising it on the transfer in), and the settlor-interested trust exclusion under ss.169B to 169G TCGA 1992 is the most common landlord misstep.</p>

<p>For a side-by-side tax comparison of FIC versus discretionary trust on the same axes (IHT, IT / CT, CGT, setup cost, control), see our existing pillar at <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-company-structures/fic-vs-discretionary-trust-property-comparison">FIC vs Discretionary Trust for Property</a>. This page goes deeper on the trust side specifically: the mechanic, a worked Patel £400,000 BTL settlement showing IHT plus CGT plus SDLT plus the year-10 projection, the settlor-interested trap that breaks the s.260 holdover, the deed-of-variation route that turns a first-death NRB into a no-cost settlement, and when the CLT route is definitely the wrong choice.</p>

<h2>The CLT mechanic in one paragraph</h2>

<p>A transfer of property into a relevant property trust (most discretionary trusts and many interest-in-possession trusts settled after 22 March 2006) is a chargeable lifetime transfer under IHTA 1984. The CLT triggers an immediate 20% IHT charge on the value of the property above the settlor's available nil-rate band, with the settlor (or the trustees) paying the IHT to HMRC within 6 months of the end of the month of transfer. The available NRB is £325,000 less any 7-year cumulative gifts the settlor has already made. If the settlor dies within 7 years of the CLT, an additional charge is computed at the full 40% death rate less the 20% lifetime rate already paid, tapered by s.7(4) between years 3 and 7. The CGT on the transfer in is held over under s.260 TCGA 1992 if the trust is non-settlor-interested; if it is settlor-interested (ss.169B to 169G TCGA 1992), the holdover is denied and the CGT crystallises at market value under s.17.</p>

<p>Inside the trust, the relevant property regime applies. Section 64 IHTA 1984 imposes a periodic charge of up to 6% on the trust's chargeable value at each 10-year anniversary. Section 65 imposes a proportionate exit charge on capital distributions made between anniversaries. Income distributions are taxed in the recipient's hands at their marginal income tax rate but do not trigger exit charges.</p>

<h2>The 20% entry charge: computing the cost</h2>

<p>The 20% lifetime IHT applies to the value of the transfer above the settlor's available NRB at the date of the transfer. The arithmetic:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Step 1.</strong> Value the property at market value at the transfer date. For a residential BTL this is typically a formal RICS valuation; for a less liquid asset it is the agreed open-market figure.</li>
<li><strong>Step 2.</strong> Compute the settlor's available NRB at the transfer date. Starting figure £325,000 (frozen until 5 April 2031 per the current gov.uk IHT thresholds publication). Less any 7-year cumulative chargeable transfers already made by the settlor (any earlier CLTs or any PETs that would have failed if the settlor had died at the date of the new transfer, which for a living settlor reduces to the cumulative CLTs in the 7 years before the new transfer).</li>
<li><strong>Step 3.</strong> Compute the chargeable element: value at Step 1 minus available NRB at Step 2.</li>
<li><strong>Step 4.</strong> Apply the 20% lifetime rate to the chargeable element. The trustees pay (typically from trust funds where the trust has cash at settlement time), or the settlor pays from personal funds. If the settlor pays from personal funds, the gross-up rule at s.5(2) IHTA 1984 increases the chargeable element to incorporate the IHT itself: gross factor 1/(1-0.20) = 1.25.</li>
</ul>

<p>Worked: settlor with full unused NRB of £325,000 transfers a £400,000 BTL into a discretionary trust. Chargeable element £75,000. Lifetime IHT 20% × £75,000 = £15,000. If trustees pay from trust funds (the trust having been settled with the property plus a small cash buffer), the gross-up does not apply and the chargeable element remains £75,000.</p>

<h2>The 7-year top-up if the settlor dies</h2>

<p>If the settlor dies within 7 years of the CLT, an additional charge is computed:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Total IHT at death rate:</strong> 40% × chargeable element (less any retrospective NRB adjustment if other PETs in the 7-year window have failed, requiring a recomputation of cumulative gifts).</li>
<li><strong>Less the 20% lifetime IHT already paid.</strong></li>
<li><strong>Less taper relief under s.7(4) between years 3 and 7:</strong> 80% of the top-up at 3-4 years, 60% at 4-5 years, 40% at 5-6 years, 20% at 6-7 years.</li>
</ul>

<p>Worked on the same £75,000 chargeable element. Settlor dies in year 5. Death IHT 40% × £75,000 = £30,000. Less lifetime IHT £15,000 = £15,000 top-up before taper. Year 5 to 6 taper band: 40% of full rate, so 40% × £15,000 = £6,000 effective top-up. Less the £15,000 already paid: a £9,000 REFUND would actually arise where the tapered death rate is less than the lifetime rate paid. The taper mechanic operates in favour of the donor's estate in some bands. A donor surviving 7 years: no further charge; the £15,000 lifetime IHT is final. A donor dying within 3 years: full 40% × £75,000 = £30,000 less the £15,000 already paid = £15,000 additional, no taper relief.</p>

<h2>The 10-year periodic charge</h2>

<p>Section 64 IHTA 1984 imposes a charge on the value of the trust's relevant property at each 10-year anniversary. The mechanic is mathematically intricate but the headline figure is a maximum 6% of the chargeable trust value at the periodic charge date, computed by reference to the hypothetical chargeable transfer formula at IHTM42100 onwards.</p>

<p>The rate formula in outline: the trustees compute a hypothetical lifetime transfer at the 10-year date by reference to the trust's value plus the settlor's pre-settlement 7-year cumulative gifts, less the available NRB at the periodic charge date. The effective rate is then 30% of the lifetime rate that would apply to that hypothetical transfer, time-apportioned for any periods within the 10 years where the trust held non-relevant property. The maximum effective rate is 6% (30% of the 20% lifetime rate, on the assumption the hypothetical transfer is fully above NRB).</p>

<p>Worked at year 10 on the Patel example. £400,000 BTL has grown to £530,000 by year 10. Available NRB at year 10 assumed at £325,000 (NRB frozen until 5 April 2031 per the extended Autumn Budget 2024 settlement, may be uprated thereafter). Trust value above NRB: £530,000 minus £325,000 = £205,000. Hypothetical transfer at this value would pay 20% IHT = £41,000. Effective rate 30% × £41,000 / £530,000 = 2.32%. Periodic charge: 2.32% × £530,000 = approximately £12,300. The actual figure depends on the formula's full application; the trustees compute and report on form IHT100d at the 10-year anniversary.</p>

<aside>
<p>The 10-year periodic charge is the ongoing cost that distinguishes the trust route from the FIC route or the direct PET route.</p>
<p>For trusts holding a single property, a £10,000 to £15,000 periodic charge every 10 years is material against the underlying IHT saving. The modelling should run the full 30-to-40-year cost (typical trust lifetime) before the route is committed. Get in touch via the form at the foot of the page for a specialist review.</p>
</aside>

<h2>The s.260 CGT holdover</h2>

<p>The CLT trigger unlocks s.260 TCGA 1992. The settlor and trustees jointly elect on the settlor's CGT return for the year of the transfer; the gain on the gift is rolled into the trust's base cost rather than being assessed on the settlor as a deemed disposal at market value.</p>

<p>The election is the strategic value of the trust route for landlords with large latent CGT gains on long-held BTL properties. Without the holdover, a £400,000 BTL with a £213,600 latent gain (the Patel base cost in our worked example) would trigger £40,000 to £50,000 of immediate CGT at the s.17 deemed market value, taxed at 18% or 24% residential rate. With the s.260 election, the settlor pays no immediate CGT; the trust takes the property at the settlor's base cost (£186,400 in the Patel example); the gain is realised by the trustees only on the trust's eventual disposal of the property.</p>

<h2>The settlor-interested trust exclusion</h2>

<p>The s.260 holdover is the route's strategic value. The settlor-interested exclusion at TCGA 1992 ss.169B to 169G disapplies the holdover where the trust is settlor-interested. A settlor-interested trust is one where the settlor, the settlor's spouse, the settlor's minor unmarried children, or a corporate body controlled by any of those persons is or may at any time become a beneficiary.</p>

<p>The exclusion is the most common landlord misstep on the CLT route. A parent settles a BTL into a discretionary trust intending to benefit the adult children, but the trust deed draws the beneficiary class as "the settlor's family" or names the settlor (or settlor's spouse) as a discretionary beneficiary "in case of unforeseen need". The trust is then settlor-interested. The s.260 holdover is denied. The CGT crystallises immediately on the transfer in at market value under s.17. On the Patel £400,000 BTL with a £213,600 latent gain, the immediate CGT is approximately £45,000 (£213,600 × 24% effective rate, less any unused annual exempt amount, with the settlor paying via the 60-day residential property return). The trust route has lost its strategic value.</p>

<p>The fix is drafting. The trust deed expressly excludes the settlor and the settlor's spouse from the beneficiary class. The exclusion is documented in the deed itself, not in a side letter or guidance to the trustees. The exclusion applies to all current and any future spouses (so a remarriage by the settlor does not bring the new spouse into the class). The trustees retain discretion over all other family members within the class (children, grandchildren, future descendants, and where appropriate the families they marry into), which is wide enough for the family's planning purposes without triggering the s.169B exclusion.</p>

<h2>The Patel worked settlement: full cost summary</h2>

<p>The Patel persona, clean (non-settlor-interested) trust. Mr Patel, settlor, age 60, in good health, transfers a £400,000 BTL property into a discretionary trust for the benefit of his two adult children (settlor and spouse expressly excluded from beneficiary class). The BTL was acquired in 2010 for £160,000 base cost plus £4,800 SDLT plus £1,600 legal fees plus £20,000 of enhancement expenditure across the years = total base cost £186,400. Latent gain £213,600.</p>

<p><strong>Year 0 costs.</strong></p>

<ul>
<li><strong>IHT (CLT):</strong> £400,000 value less £325,000 NRB = £75,000 chargeable at 20% = £15,000. Paid by trustees within 6 months of the end of the month of transfer.</li>
<li><strong>CGT:</strong> s.260 election made jointly by settlor and trustees. Gain £213,600 held over into trust's base cost. Trust's base cost becomes £186,400. Settlor pays zero CGT.</li>
<li><strong>SDLT:</strong> gift transfer with no chargeable consideration (no debt assumed by trustees, no cash payment). Zero SDLT.</li>
<li><strong>TRS registration:</strong> within 90 days of trust creation. Annual updates thereafter. Compliance cost only.</li>
<li><strong>Total Year 0 cost: £15,000.</strong></li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Year 0 comparison to alternatives.</strong></p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Direct PET gift to children:</strong> zero entry IHT, but s.17 deemed market value disposal, gain £213,600 at 24% residential rate (Mr Patel higher rate, age 60), £20,000 unused annual exempt amount, net CGT approximately £45,000 to £50,000. Plus 7-year clock starting from gift date.</li>
<li><strong>FIC growth-share gift:</strong> zero entry IHT, but CGT on minority-discounted share value at 24%, typically £25,000 to £35,000 on equivalent latent gain. Plus 7-year clock on the share gift.</li>
<li><strong>CLT into clean trust (this route):</strong> £15,000 entry IHT, zero immediate CGT. No 7-year clock on the trust (the trust holds the property; the children become beneficiaries with discretionary entitlements).</li>
</ul>

<p>For the Patel facts, the CLT-into-trust route is cheaper by £30,000 to £35,000 at Year 0 than the direct PET, and cheaper by £10,000 to £20,000 than the FIC route. The trust route's offsetting cost is the ongoing 10-year periodic charge plus exit charges on distributions.</p>

<p><strong>Year 10 cost.</strong> 10-year periodic charge as computed above, approximately £8,000 to £12,000 depending on the actual rate formula at the date and the trust's value at year 10.</p>

<p><strong>Year 0 to 30 total.</strong> Year 0 £15,000 + Year 10 periodic charge approximately £10,000 + Year 20 periodic charge approximately £12,000 + Year 30 periodic charge approximately £15,000 + exit charges on capital distributions to children (proportionate to the rate at the most recent anniversary) totalling perhaps £20,000 to £40,000 across the period. Total trust route cost: in the order of £70,000 to £100,000 across a 30-year window for a £400,000-rising-to-£800,000 trust holding.</p>

<p><strong>The counterfactual:</strong> Mr Patel holds the property to death at age 90 (30 years from now). Estate value at death (with growth): £800,000 to £1,000,000. IHT at 40% above his unused NRB on the property: £320,000 to £400,000, less any RNRB available. Even with full £350,000 of combined RNRB (which requires the rest of his estate to be below the £2m taper threshold, often not the case for portfolio landlords), the trust route saves £200,000+ over the 30-year window. The trust route wins comfortably for landlords with substantial portfolios and a 30-year-plus planning horizon.</p>

<h2>The deed-of-variation-plus-trust route on first death</h2>

<p>One of the most useful combined routes on the trust side. The first-to-die spouse's will leaves the entire estate to the surviving spouse under the s.18 spouse exemption. Within 2 years of the first death, the surviving spouse executes a deed of variation under s.142 IHTA 1984 (covered on Wave 4 C5) redirecting £325,000 of property (matching the deceased's unused NRB) into a discretionary trust for the adult children and grandchildren, with the s.142 IHT election and the s.62(6) CGT election. The variation reads back to the deceased: the trust receives the £325,000 settlement at the deceased's death-uplifted base cost (s.62(1) CGT uplift on death applied before variation); the deceased's NRB is applied to the settlement, so no IHT is payable on the variation; the £325,000 is then outside the surviving spouse's second-death estate.</p>

<p>The combined route achieves a clean trust settlement within the deceased's NRB at zero immediate IHT and zero immediate CGT cost. The trust then runs under the standard 10-year and exit charge regime. The route is one of the most powerful planning moves available to surviving spouses in the deed-of-variation window, and it depends on coordinating the variation drafting with the trust drafting so the s.142 election, the s.62(6) election, and the trust beneficiary class are all aligned. For the deed-of-variation mechanic in full, see <a href="/blog/landlord-tax-essentials/deed-of-variation-property-estate-redirecting-inheritance-iht-saving">Deeds of Variation on Landlord Estates</a>.</p>

<h2>When the CLT route is definitely the wrong choice</h2>

<p>Three scenarios where the CLT-into-trust route is the wrong tool, despite its strategic value in the right hands:</p>

<ol>
<li><strong>Settlor-interested trust drafting.</strong> If the trust deed includes the settlor or settlor's spouse in the beneficiary class, the s.260 holdover is denied, the CGT crystallises immediately, and the route loses most of its tax-efficiency rationale. The fix is express exclusion in the deed; if that is not possible (because the family wants flexibility for the settlor to benefit in case of need), the trust route is not the right tool.</li>
<li><strong>Donor expects to live more than 7 years with no large latent CGT gain.</strong> A direct PET gift to the children achieves the same end result (property outside the estate) with no entry IHT charge, no s.260 election required, and no ongoing 10-year periodic charges. The trust route's entry IHT (£15,000 on a £400,000 BTL) and ongoing periodic charges are an unnecessary cost.</li>
<li><strong>Settlement wanted purely to defer IHT to second death.</strong> The s.18 spouse exemption defers all first-death IHT; the deed-of-variation flexibility within 2 years of first death lets the family retro-engineer the right structure with full information. The trust route on first death is locking-in a structure that the deed-of-variation route could have built more flexibly.</li>
</ol>

<p>The CLT route is right when the family wants defined control through trustees (rather than direct ownership by individuals), CGT deferral via s.260 on a property with substantial latent gain, and structural separation of the property from any family member's personal estate (so the property is not vulnerable to the children's divorces, business failures, or other personal-estate events). For families where these conditions all apply, the route is one of the most useful tools in landlord IHT planning. For all other scenarios, one of the alternatives is usually cheaper or simpler.</p>
