## 3. Angle (2 to 3 sentences) + differentiation

**Angle.** Basis period reform quietly changed how every GP partnership is taxed: partnerships now pay tax on the profit arising in the **tax year itself** rather than on the profit of the accounting year ending in it. This page is the dedicated deep-dive on what that means for a GP practice: the move to the tax-year basis from 2024/25, the one-off 2023/24 transition year, how overlap relief was used up, the option to spread the transition profit over five years, and the ongoing apportionment a practice faces if its accounting date is not 31 March or 5 April. It is the full explainer the complete guide only summarises in an FAQ.

## 4. Section-by-section outline (target 2,800 to 3,500 words)

- **H1: Basis Period Reform and Your GP Partnership: The Tax-Year Basis, the Transition and Overlap Relief**
- **H2: What basis period reform is, in one paragraph**
  - State it plainly: for 2024/25 onwards a partner is taxed on the profit **arising in the tax year (6 April to 5 April)**, not on the profit of the accounting period ending in the tax year (the old "current year basis"). The partnership still files one SA800 and each partner's share flows to SA104; only the **measure** of the year's profit changed (anchor §1; link `gp-partnership-tax-complete-guide`).
- **H2: The old current-year basis (brief, for context)**
  - Explain how it used to work: a practice with, say, a 30 June year-end was taxed in a tax year on the profit of the accounting year ending in it, which created a lag between earning and being taxed, plus "overlap profit" on commencement that was relieved only on cessation or change of accounting date. Keep this short, it is background.
- **H2: The tax-year basis from 2024/25**
  - The new rule: profit is taxed in the tax year it arises. Where the practice accounting date is **not** 31 March or 5 April, profit from two accounting periods is **apportioned** (by days, or another reasonable and consistent time basis) to build the tax-year figure. A practice with a 31 March (or 5 April) year-end needs no apportionment, which is why many practices considered aligning their year-end. (Cite BIM81201.)
  - H3: The estimation problem. If the second accounting period's accounts are not finalised by the filing deadline, the practice may need provisional figures and a later amendment. Note this as a practical consequence; link D2 for the reserving angle and D1 for the accounts.
- **H2: 2023/24: the transition year**
  - Explain that **2023/24 was the one-off transition year** that moved practices with a non-tax-year accounting date onto the new basis. The taxable amount for a transition-year practice was the **"standard part"** (profit to the normal accounting date) plus the **"transition part"** (profit from the end of that accounting period up to 5 April 2024), so some practices were taxed on **more than 12 months of profit** in that single year. (Cite BIM81200 contents and BIM81240/BIM81260.)
  - State clearly that 2023/24 is now behind us, but the **five-year spreading of the transition profit continues to 2027/28**, so it still affects current and near-future tax bills (bridge to the spreading section).
- **H2: Overlap relief in the transition**
  - Explain overlap relief: the relief for profit that was taxed twice on commencement under the old rules. In the transition year, a partner **deducted their overlap relief from the transition profit**, and overlap relief **cannot be used after 2023/24**. Note that some long-standing partners had little or no recorded overlap relief, and tracking down the correct figure was a common transition headache. (Cite the gov.uk transition-profit guidance and BIM81200 family.)
- **H2: Spreading the transition profit over five years**
  - The reform let the **transition profit (after overlap relief) be spread over five tax years, 2023/24 to 2027/28**, by default in equal fifths (20% a year), to soften the extra charge. A partner could **elect to accelerate** more of it into an earlier year (for example to use spare basic-rate band or avoid a future-year cliff), with the election made by the first anniversary of the normal filing date for the year it relates to. State that **at least 20% falls in 2023/24** under the default, and that the balance otherwise falls in 2027/28. (Cite BIM81310 and the gov.uk transition-profit guidance.)
  - H3: Why spreading still matters in 2026 and 2027. Because the default spreads to 2027/28, a partner is **still picking up a slice of transition profit in current bills**, and the final 2027/28 slice can coincide with other changes, so it remains a live planning point, not history. Link D2 for reserving for it.
  - H3: The interaction with thresholds and the pension. A note that the extra transition profit can push a partner over income-tax thresholds, the personal-allowance taper or into the pension annual-allowance taper in a given year, which is exactly why the spread/accelerate decision is modelled per partner. Keep light; link `nhs-pension-annual-allowance-complete-guide` and the complete guide. Do NOT re-teach the annual allowance.
- **H2: Does basis period reform mean my practice should change its year-end?**
  - Lay out the trade-off neutrally: a 31 March / 5 April year-end removes apportionment and provisional figures; changing the date has its own admin and one-off effects. Frame as a practice-specific decision for the accountant, not a blanket recommendation. Link D1 (accounts) and the complete guide.
- **H2: Basis period reform and MTD: two separate things**
  - Clear up a common confusion: basis period reform (which profit is taxed when) is **separate** from Making Tax Digital for Income Tax (how you keep records and report). State the §9 position: **general partnerships are deferred from MTD for Income Tax with no confirmed start date**, so a GP partnership is not yet mandated at partnership level, but a partner's **own** Self Assessment can be caught where their personal qualifying income (for example sole-trader private or locum work) exceeds £50,000 from 6 April 2026 (then £30,000 from April 2027, £20,000 from April 2028). (Anchor §9; link the complete guide; do NOT say MTD applies to partnerships from April 2026.)
- **H2: What GP partners should do now**
  - Short checklist: confirm the practice's accounting date and whether apportionment applies; check that overlap relief was correctly identified and used in 2023/24; know how much transition profit is still being spread and which years it falls in; reserve for the remaining slices (link D2); and review whether aligning the year-end is worth it. Signpost `gp-tax-return` and `gp-accounting-guide`.

## 5. Exact HP positions, dates and verified anchors

**HP positions to use:**
- A GP partner is **self-employed**; partnership files **SA800**, share flows to **SA104**; taxed as trading income. (§1)
- Taxed on **profit share, not drawings** (one reinforcing sentence). (§1)
- **General partnerships are deferred from MTD for Income Tax with no confirmed date.** Personal thresholds: **£50k from 6 April 2026, £30k from 6 April 2027, £20k from 6 April 2028**; tested on gross qualifying income on the prior year. (§9)
