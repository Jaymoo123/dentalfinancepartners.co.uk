## 3. Angle (2 to 3 sentences) + differentiation

**Angle.** Two GPs can work side by side under very different legal arrangements, and the labels matter for tax. In an expense-sharing arrangement each GP is effectively a separate practitioner who shares premises and staff costs but keeps their own income; in a full partnership the GPs carry on a business in common and pool and share the profit. This page explains the difference clearly, why it turns on whether there is a business in common with a view to profit (the legal test for a partnership), and the practical tax, accounting and liability consequences of each, so a reader can tell which one they are actually in.

## 4. Section-by-section outline (target 2,800 to 3,500 words)

- **H1: Expense Sharing Versus a Full GP Partnership: The Difference and the Tax**
- **H2: The core distinction in one paragraph**
  - State it plainly: in a full partnership the GPs carry on one business together and share the profit of that single business; in an expense-sharing arrangement each GP runs their own practice and they share only the costs of premises and staff, while keeping their own income separately. Anchor §1: status and structure follow the substance of the arrangement, not the label on the door. Preview that the legal dividing line is whether there is a business carried on in common with a view to profit.
- **H2: What an expense-sharing arrangement is**
  - H3: Each GP is effectively a separate practitioner. Explain that in an expense-sharing arrangement each doctor holds their own income (their own NHS contract position or their own share of receipts) and is, in substance, a separate sole practitioner; they come together only to share the running costs (premises, reception and nursing staff, equipment, utilities) under an agreed cost-sharing formula. There is no pooling of profit. Anchor §1: each is self-employed and taxed on their own income less their own share of the shared costs.
  - H3: How the costs are shared. Explain that the arrangement sets out how the shared overheads are split (equally, by sessions, by list size, or by another agreed measure), and that each GP claims their own share of those costs as a deduction against their own income. Note that there can still be a services company or a property arrangement holding the premises and staff, but that does not make the doctors profit-sharing partners. Do not fabricate figures or formulas; the agreement sets them.
  - H3: When it is used. Note (without over-claiming) the typical reasons GPs use expense sharing: doctors who want to keep their income and clinical autonomy separate while sharing the cost of a building and a team, transitional arrangements, or where the parties do not want the mutual financial exposure of a full partnership. Frame as common reasons, not a recommendation.
- **H2: What a full GP partnership is**
  - H3: A business carried on in common, with profit pooled and shared. Explain that in a full partnership the GPs carry on a single practice together and share its profit per the partnership deed (anchor §1: partner is self-employed; partnership files an SA800; each partner's share flows to the SA104; partners are taxed on their profit share, not their drawings). Contrast directly with expense sharing: here income is pooled and shared, not just costs.
  - H3: The legal test. This is the page's technical core. Explain, verified at the Partnership Act 1890 s.1(1) and HMRC PM133000, that a partnership in England and Wales is "the relation which subsists between persons carrying on a business in common with a view of profit", and that whether an arrangement is a partnership is judged on the substance: HMRC's view (PM133000) is that the receipt of a share of net profits is prima facie but not conclusive evidence of a partnership, that an obligation to share net losses is an even stronger indicator, that no single factor is conclusive, and that the parties' stated intention is relevant but not decisive on its own. Quote the s.1(1) definition exactly. Make the practical point: an arrangement labelled "expense sharing" that in substance pools and shares profit, or shares losses, may actually be a partnership, with the partnership tax consequences that follow, and the reverse is also true.
- **H2: The tax differences**
  - H3: How income is returned. Expense sharing: each GP files their own self-employment return (their own income, their own share of shared costs); there is no partnership return. Full partnership: the partnership files an SA800 and each partner's share flows to the partnership pages (SA104) of their personal return (anchor §1). State this contrast clearly.
  - H3: What you are taxed on. Both routes are self-employed (Class 4 NIC, payments on account, MTD for Income Tax where qualifying income crosses the threshold); the difference is the unit of measurement: your own income in expense sharing, your allocated share of the partnership profit in a partnership (taxed on profit share, not drawings). Keep the NIC/MTD/payments-on-account detail to a sentence each and link `gp-partnership-tax-complete-guide` and `gp-tax-deductions-complete-list-2026` rather than re-teaching.
  - H3: Expenses. Note that in both cases costs must be wholly and exclusively for the business; in expense sharing each GP deducts their own share of the shared costs against their own income, while in a partnership the shared costs are partnership expenses deducted before the profit is split. Brief; link the deductions page.
- **H2: The accounting differences**
  - Explain that a full partnership prepares one set of partnership accounts with capital and current accounts for each partner and a single divisible profit, whereas an expense-sharing arrangement does not have pooled partnership accounts: each GP keeps their own accounts, and there is usually a separate record or simple set of accounts for the shared-cost pool (and for any services company or property entity holding the building and staff). Link D1 (`reading-gp-partnership-accounts-current-capital-accounts`) for what partnership accounts look like, by contrast.
- **H2: The liability and risk differences**
  - Explain that in a full (general) partnership the partners share the profits and also the liabilities and the mutual exposure of the business, including the standing premises liability and the last-man-standing risk where premises are owned, whereas in an expense-sharing arrangement each GP carries their own income and, broadly, their own liabilities, with shared exposure usually limited to the shared-cost commitments (the lease and the staff contracts they have jointly signed up to). State carefully that the actual exposure depends on what each party has signed (a jointly guaranteed lease creates shared exposure even in an expense-sharing arrangement), so the documents matter more than the label. Link `gp-partnership-last-man-standing-premises-risk` for the premises-liability detail; do not re-teach.
- **H2: NHS contract and pension consequences (brief, signpost)**
  - Keep light and tax-relevant. Note that the NHS contract and pension position can differ: in a full partnership the partners are typically Type 1 medical practitioners pensioning the profit share through the Annual Certificate; an expense-sharing GP pensions their own income on the basis that fits their own contract/role. Do not lock pension figures or teach the certification machinery; one or two sentences and a link to `gp-pension-contributions-tax-relief`. State plainly that the right NHS contract and pension treatment depends on the actual arrangement and should be confirmed.
- **H2: Which one are you actually in? A short test**
  - A focused checklist a reader can apply (anchored to the PM133000 indicators): Do you pool and share profit, or only share costs? Is there a single divisible profit, or does each of you keep your own income? Is there a partnership deed and a partnership return (SA800), or separate returns and a cost-sharing agreement? Do you share losses? What do the documents (deed, lease, contract) actually say? State the headline: the label does not decide it, the substance does (anchor §1), and an arrangement can be a partnership in law even if it is not called one.
