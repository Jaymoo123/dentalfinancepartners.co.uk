## 3. Angle (2 to 3 sentences) + differentiation

**Angle.** When two GP practices merge, the partners' first question is "are we one practice now, and what does that do to our accounts and our tax?" This page works through the mechanics: what happens to the two partnerships and their capital accounts, the GMS/PMS contracts and patient lists, the premises, and the pivotal tax question of whether the merger is treated as a cessation of the old businesses and the commencement of a new one, or as a continuation of an existing business. It explains why that cessation-versus-continuation distinction matters (for overlap, losses, accounting dates and the partners' final or continuing tax position) and how the test is applied.

## 4. Section-by-section outline (target 2,800 to 3,500 words)

- **H1: GP Practice Mergers: What Happens to the Accounts, the Partners and the Tax**
- **H2: What a GP practice merger actually involves**
  - Set the scene: a merger means two (or more) existing GP partnerships combining into one practice, which usually involves combining the partnerships, the patient lists, the premises and the staff. State the medical-specific point up front, anchored to §4: there is no purchase of NHS goodwill in any of this, because NHS GP goodwill cannot be sold (prohibited since 1 April 2004; current instrument SI 2019/251). A merger is about combining contracts, lists, tangible assets, capital accounts and premises, not buying goodwill. Distinguish a true merger (two partnerships becoming one) from a takeover, a single partner moving, or a federation/PCN collaboration that leaves each practice separate; keep the focus on the merger of partnerships.
- **H2: What happens to the two partnerships and their capital accounts**
  - H3: The legal partnership. Explain that a merger usually means the partners of both practices entering a single new partnership deed, with one combined partnership going forward (anchor §1, partnership taxation; partners self-employed, SA800/SA104). Note the deed must address profit-sharing across the combined partner group, decision-making, and the treatment of each side's pre-merger reserves.
  - H3: The capital accounts. Explain that each practice's capital and current accounts have to be brought together: the two sets of accounts are reconciled, net assets valued, and each incoming partner's capital position established in the combined partnership (anchor §4). Where one practice brings more net assets or premises than the other, the deed sets how that imbalance is handled (capital adjustments, a parity timetable, or separate property arrangements). Do not lock figures; this is practice-specific. Link D1 (`reading-gp-partnership-accounts-current-capital-accounts`) for how capital accounts are presented and E1 (`buying-into-gp-partnership-capital-parity-explained`) for the parity concept where capital is equalised.
  - H3: Pre-merger profits and reserves. Note that each practice's profits up to the merger date, and any tax reserves, need to be settled and allocated to the pre-merger partners before the combined accounts run; the deed and the accountants set the cut-off.
- **H2: The contracts and the patient lists**
  - Keep this tax-relevant and signposting (anchor §3, light). Explain that the GMS/PMS contracts and the registered patient lists are central to a merger: typically the contracts and lists are combined under the surviving or a new contract, which requires the agreement of the commissioner (the integrated care board / NHS England), and the contract type (GMS, PMS, APMS) and any list-size funding (Global Sum weighted by Carr-Hill, QOF, enhanced services, PCN/ARRS) carry through to the merged practice. State that this is a regulatory/commissioner process running alongside the tax and accounting work, and that there is no single national per-patient value (figures are weighted and uplifted annually). Do not lock contract values. Link `gp-partnership-tax-complete-guide` for the contract/income context.
- **H2: The premises**
  - Explain that premises are often the most complex strand: each practice may own or lease its surgery, often through a separate property partnership or LLP (anchor §4). A merger has to decide which premises survive, whether one site closes, how the property partnerships combine or whether they stay separate, and how owners are bought out or brought in. Note notional rent (owner-occupiers, current-market basis, District-Valuer assessed) and legacy cost rent affect the property economics and that valuations are property-specific, so no standard figure. Flag the last-man-standing risk where a merger leaves fewer owners on a larger property liability. Link `gp-surgery-premises-own-vs-rent-tax-guide` and `gp-partnership-last-man-standing-premises-risk`; do not re-teach.
- **H2: The pivotal tax question: cessation and recommencement, or continuation?**
  - H3: Why it matters. Explain that the single most important tax question on a merger is whether, for income tax, the old businesses cease and a new one commences, or whether the merged business continues an existing trade. This affects the partners' final or continuing basis position, the treatment of brought-forward trading losses, and the accounting-date handling. Frame it as the question to settle with the accountant early.
  - H3: When it is a cessation and a commencement. Explain, anchored to §1 and verified at PM135200, that where the facts show the merged business is different in nature from the two original businesses, the normal cessation rules apply to each original business and the normal commencement rules to the new merged business. Quote the PM135200 wording. Note the practical effect: a cessation crystallises the closing-year position for the ceasing businesses (and, post-reform, accelerates any untaxed 2023/24 transition profit still being spread, link D3 for that point).
  - H3: When it is a continuation. Explain, verified at PM135300, that where the activities of the two practices are similar in nature and the merged business has the same essential characteristics as both, the merged business can be a continuation (an enlarged version) rather than a new business, so the partners continue to be taxed on the trading profits of their notional trade and brought-forward losses are unaffected, particularly where the merged business keeps the same accounting date. Quote the PM135300 wording. Make the GP-specific observation (carefully, not as advice): two NHS general practices delivering the same primary medical services are often similar in nature, which points towards continuation, but the treatment turns on the actual facts and the accounting-date handling, so it must be confirmed case by case.
  - H3: The notional-trade lens. Tie it back, anchored to §1 and PM163090: because each partner has their own notional trade, the cessation-versus-continuation question is applied through whether each partner's notional trade continues or ceases and recommences on the merger. Keep this concise; link D3 for the basis period mechanics.
- **H2: Capital gains tax on a merger**
  - Explain, anchored to §4 and verified at CG27000+/CG27700, that a partnership is transparent for CGT, so where partners' fractional interests in chargeable assets (such as premises) change on a merger, that can be a CGT event for the partners whose interests reduce. HMRC's Statement of Practice D12 (and specifically the partnership-merger guidance at CG27700) governs how merger-driven changes in fractional interests are treated. State plainly that this is a specialist computation, that whether a gain arises depends on revaluation and consideration, and that no figures should be assumed. Link the complete guide for the CGT/BADR detail; do not reproduce SP D12.
- **H2: VAT, payroll and the practical integration (brief)**
  - One short section: note that the merged practice has to consolidate payroll for combined staff, may inherit different software and bookkeeping systems, and should review VAT only if either practice has taxable (non-exempt) income such as a dispensary or significant private/medico-legal work (most core NHS GP income is exempt or outside scope, so a merger of two purely NHS practices rarely changes the VAT position). Keep VAT to a sentence or two and link `gp-partnership-tax-complete-guide`; do not teach the VAT rules here.
- **H2: A practical checklist for a GP practice merger** (short)
  - An ordered list of the strands to settle: agree the new partnership deed and profit-sharing; reconcile and combine the two sets of accounts and capital accounts; agree the contract and list combination with the commissioner; settle the premises (which sites, how property partnerships combine, buy-outs); get the cessation-versus-continuation treatment confirmed by the accountants early; check CGT on any premises interest changes; and consolidate payroll, systems and tax reserves. Signpost the linked pages rather than re-teaching.

## 5. Exact HP positions, dates and verified anchors

**HP positions to use:**
- GP partners are **self-employed**; the partnership files an **SA800** and shares flow to the **SA104**; each partner has a **notional trade** (per §1 and PM163090). Use for the partnership-combination and notional-trade framing.
- On a merger what is combined is **tangible assets, capital accounts and premises** (plus contracts and lists), never **NHS goodwill**, which **cannot be sold** (prohibited since 1 April 2004; current instrument SI 2019/251). (§4)
- **Premises** are often held in a **separate property partnership or LLP**; valuations are property-specific (notional/cost rent context), so do NOT lock a figure; a merger can create or relieve the **last-man-standing** risk. (§4)
- NHS contract context (light, §3): GMS/PMS/APMS contracts, registered lists, Global Sum weighted by Carr-Hill, QOF, enhanced services, PCN/ARRS; **no single national per-patient value** (weighted and uplifted annually). Do NOT lock contract values.
- **CGT**: a partnership is **transparent**; merger-driven changes in partners' **fractional interests** in chargeable assets can be CGT events; governed by **SP D12** (merger guidance at CG27700). (§4 plus CG27000+ verification.)
