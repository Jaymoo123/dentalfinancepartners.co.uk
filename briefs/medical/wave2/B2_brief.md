## 3. Angle (2 to 3 sentences) + differentiation

**Angle.** The Additional Roles Reimbursement Scheme is widely misread as free money for extra staff, when it is a reimbursement of defined employment costs up to a capped maximum, with real tax and VAT consequences for whoever does the employing. This page takes the practical employer view: which roles are reimbursable, that ARRS reimburses actual salary plus employer on-costs up to a per-role ceiling (so anything above the cap falls on the network), who can legally employ the staff (the PCN where it has a legal entity, a lead or single practice, a GP federation or other provider, or a shared/joint employment arrangement), how each model changes the payroll, the NHS pension position, the Employment Allowance and, most importantly, the VAT supply-of-staff trap that can strip irrecoverable VAT out of general practice. It closes with how the reimbursement should sit in the accounts so it nets against the cost rather than inflating profit.

## 4. Section-by-section outline (target 2,800 to 3,500 words)

- **H1: ARRS Explained: Reimbursement, Employing PCN Staff and the Tax Consequences**
- **H2: What ARRS is, and the one thing practices get wrong**
  - Define ARRS: a Network Contract DES funding stream that reimburses the employment cost of defined additional roles, introduced to expand the general-practice workforce. State the core correction up front: it is a reimbursement up to a maximum, not a grant and not free money. The network must actually incur the cost (employ or engage the person and pay them) before it can claim, and anything above the per-role cap is the network's own cost.
  - Signpost B1 for where ARRS sits in the wider DES (link `pcn-funding-network-contract-des-explained`).
- **H2: Which roles are reimbursable**
  - List the recognised role families qualitatively (clinical pharmacists and pharmacy technicians, first-contact physiotherapists, paramedics, social prescribing link workers, care coordinators, health and wellbeing coaches, mental-health practitioners, nursing associates, and others), and note the scheme has broadened over time. Frame the exact current list as set in the Network Contract DES guidance and confirm at source.
  - Flag the 2025/26 change: the previously separate GP ARRS pot was merged into the main ARRS as a short-term measure, practice nurses were added, and there is no cap on the number of GPs who can be engaged (hedge, tag 2025/26, confirm current; signpost B1 for the funding context).
  - Do NOT lock the role list or the per-role reimbursement maxima as permanent; these are set in guidance and uplifted.
- **H2: Reimbursement up to a maximum, not free money**
  - Explain the mechanic: ARRS reimburses the actual salary plus defined employer on-costs (employer National Insurance and employer pension) up to a maximum per whole-time-equivalent for each role. If the post is paid above the cap, the excess is funded by the network from its own resources.
  - State the salaried-GP figure as an illustration only: for 2025/26 the reimbursable amount for a GP was around £82,418 plus on-costs (increased by £9,305 in-year), tagged "2025/26, confirm current at source", never fixed.
  - Cash-flow and timing point: the network pays the staff first and claims reimbursement, so there is a working-capital and reconciliation discipline (the claim must match the actual cost). Signpost the practice-income reconciliation theme by topic only; do not link a different Wave 2 cluster.
- **H2: Who employs ARRS staff (the model decides everything that follows)**
  - Frame the central question: ARRS staff need a legal employer, and the choice of employer drives the payroll, the pension, the Employment Allowance and the VAT.
  - H3: Single lead (host) practice employs. One member practice is the legal employer and the staff work across the network. Simplest to run; but recharging that staff resource to the other practices is the classic VAT trigger (see the VAT section).
  - H3: The PCN as a separate legal entity (a federation, a company limited by guarantee, an LLP, or a flat PCN company). The entity employs and deploys the staff. This concentrates the employment but raises the supply-of-staff and Cost Sharing Group questions (VAT section).
  - H3: Joint / shared employment. All member practices are co-employers under one contract, or a concurrent-employment / shared-workforce model is used. Described by NHS England guidance as avoiding the supply-of-staff VAT problem because there is no supply between separate organisations; the trade-off is legal complexity and joint-and-several employment liability.
  - H3: Direct practice employment and other-provider models. Note that the DES has, over successive years, broadened who may employ (a Core Network Practice, the PCN, or another provider with commissioner approval); confirm the current-year position at source. Some roles are deployed in from a trust or pharmacy under a separate arrangement.
  - Tie-back: there is no single right model; it is a tax, VAT, HR and risk decision per network, and this is specialist territory.
- **H2: The payroll, pension and employer-cost view**
  - PAYE and RTI: ARRS staff are employees of whoever employs them, run through PAYE under the usual real-time-information rules (link `gp-payroll-services` for the operation).
  - NHS pension: ARRS staff can generally access the NHS Pension Scheme where employed by an NHS-pension-employing body within the network; the employer pension cost is one of the on-costs ARRS reimburses. Keep this factual and confirm scheme-access rules at source; do not over-assert for every model.
  - Employer NIC: the employer pays secondary Class 1 NIC at 15% on pay above the £5,000 secondary threshold (from 6 April 2025) (anchor §8). This employer NIC on ARRS posts is itself part of the reimbursable on-costs up to the cap.
  - H3: The Employment Allowance question. The Employment Allowance is £10,500 (2025/26) and is not available where the only employee is a single director (not the issue here). The harder point is the public-sector restriction: an employer that does more than half its work in the public sector cannot claim, BUT HMRC treats providers of NHS primary medical services (GP practices) as falling outside that restriction so they can generally claim. **VERIFY:** the writer must confirm, at HMRC's Employment Allowance guidance, the current treatment of GP practices and of a PCN company or federation employing ARRS staff (the carve-out is fact-sensitive and the position can differ for a non-practice legal entity). Frame as "a GP practice can generally claim the Employment Allowance; a separate PCN company or federation should take advice on its eligibility", not as a guarantee. Anchor §8.
- **H2: The VAT trap on shared ARRS staff (the expensive one)**
  - Lead with the risk: when one entity (a lead practice, federation or PCN company) employs staff and recharges or supplies them to other member practices for consideration, HMRC is likely to treat that as a standard-rated supply of staff at 20%, not exempt medical care. Because GP practices make mostly exempt and outside-the-scope supplies, that VAT is largely irrecoverable, so it is a real cost leak.
  - H3: The control test. If the employing entity merely provides personnel who then work under the direction and control of the recipient practices, that points to a (taxable) supply of staff; if the entity retains control and is itself delivering a healthcare service to the patient using its staff, that can be an exempt supply of medical care. Frame as the distinction HMRC applies, and note NHS England has suggested ARRS services might be exempt regardless of structure but that this has not been formally agreed by HMRC, so reliance carries risk.
  - H3: Why the £90,000 threshold matters. If the employing entity's taxable turnover (including the value of any taxable staff supply) exceeds £90,000 in a 12-month period, VAT registration becomes compulsory (anchor §6, link `gp-vat-registration`).
  - H3: The models that reduce the risk. Joint / concurrent employment (no supply between separate organisations, so no VAT on the salary movement) and a properly constituted Cost Sharing Group (a separate entity supplying members at cost, for their exempt activities, with no profit and no competition distortion, can be VAT-exempt). State both as options that need specialist VAT advice, with strict conditions, not as a guaranteed fix.
  - H3: The on-trust misconception. Note neutrally that labelling fund movements as held "on trust" addresses the movement of money but does not by itself change the VAT liability of the underlying supply. Do not give a definitive ruling; flag it as a point on which specialist advice is needed.
- **H2: How the reimbursement should sit in the accounts**
  - The discipline: ARRS income should be recognised against the matching staff cost so it largely nets off, rather than being booked as profit with the cost hidden elsewhere. Any above-cap excess is a net cost to the network. Misposting overstates profit and distorts the partners' tax (link `gp-accounting-guide`, `gp-bookkeeping-guide-uk`).
  - State the §3 rule plainly: whatever lands in the practice is trading income, and a partner is taxed on profit share, not drawings (link `gp-partnership-tax-complete-guide`).

## 5. Exact HP positions, dates and verified anchors

**HP positions to use:**
- ARRS is the Additional Roles Reimbursement Scheme, a Network Contract DES funding stream that reimburses defined additional-role employment costs up to a maximum; it is a reimbursement, not a grant. (§3, verified below.)
- Employer secondary Class 1 NIC is 15% above the £5,000 secondary threshold (from 6 April 2025); the Employment Allowance is £10,500 and is not available to single-director-only companies. (§8.)
- VAT: medical care by a registered practitioner is exempt; registration threshold £90,000 of taxable turnover; practices with mixed supplies operate partial exemption. A supply of staff is standard-rated. (§6.)
- PCN/ARRS income reaching the practice is trading income; partner taxed on profit share not drawings. (§3.)
