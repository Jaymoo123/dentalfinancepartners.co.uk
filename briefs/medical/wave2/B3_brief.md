## 3. Angle (2 to 3 sentences) + differentiation

**Angle.** A PCN clinical director can be paid in several quite different ways, and the route chosen decides everything that follows: the income tax and National Insurance, whether the pay is NHS pensionable, and even whether VAT bites. This page sets out the funding (clinical director and leadership pay now sits inside core PCN funding), then walks the common payment routes (paid as a responsibility allowance or additional sessions through a member practice, invoiced from the director's own GP partnership, paid via a personal service company, or taken as self-employed income direct), and for each one explains the tax treatment and, carefully and with hedging, the pension position. The honest headline is that pensionability is not automatic and depends on the route, so the page treats it as a "check before you agree the arrangement" decision rather than asserting a single answer.

**How this differs from existing pages and the two sibling picks.**
- Differs from sibling B1 (`pcn-funding-network-contract-des-explained`): B1 names clinical-director and leadership funding only as a component of core PCN funding. This page is how that pay reaches and is taxed in the hands of the individual doctor.
- Differs from sibling B2 (`arrs-reimbursement-employing-pcn-staff-tax`): B2 is the reimbursed additional-role workforce. This page is leadership pay to a doctor (a fundamentally different question: it is the director's own income, not reimbursed staff).
- Differs from `gp-partnership-tax-complete-guide`, `gp-partner-vs-salaried-gp-tax-comparison` and `becoming-gp-partner-financial-implications`: those are core partnership and salaried-GP taxation. This page is the specific, often-overlooked clinical-director payment and how it interacts with the partner's profit share or salary, and with the NHS pension.
- Differs from `gp-pension-contributions-tax-relief` and `nhs-pension-annual-allowance-complete-guide`: those are the pension and annual-allowance mechanics in general. This page is the narrower question of whether one specific income stream (clinical-director pay) is pensionable at all, by route, and links to those pages for the wider pension and annual-allowance detail.

- **H2: Route 1: paid through a member practice (responsibility allowance or additional pay)**
  - Mechanics: the network funds the director's practice, and the practice pays the director. For a salaried GP this is additional pay through PAYE with Class 1 NIC; for a partner it is usually reflected in the partnership profit allocation or drawings rather than a separate salary.
  - Tax: PAYE/Class 1 for a salaried director; profit-share/Class 4 treatment for a partner (link `gp-partner-vs-salaried-gp-tax-comparison`, `gp-partnership-tax-complete-guide`).
  - Pension (HEDGE): where the director is an employee or partner of an NHS-pension-employing member practice and the pay is delivered as a recognised pensionable element (for example, a salaried GP's pay, or a partner's NHS-derived practitioner profit), it can be pensionable; but pensionability is not automatic and depends on how the pay is characterised. State this carefully and point to the verification step. Anchor §2.C.
- **H2: Route 2: invoiced from the director's GP partnership**
  - Mechanics: the director's practice invoices the network for the director's leadership work; the income sits in the partnership and is shared (often with a drawings adjustment so the director carries the benefit).
  - Tax: partnership trading income, taxed as profit share with Class 4 NIC (link `gp-partnership-tax-complete-guide`, `gp-partnership-profit-sharing-tax-planning`).
  - VAT watch-item (light): management/leadership work supplied by a practice to the network is not patient medical care, so it can fall outside the medical exemption and be a taxable supply; flag as a point to check (anchor §6, link `gp-vat-registration`). Do not over-assert; frame as "could be subject to VAT, take advice".
  - Pension (HEDGE): whether this flows into the partner's pensionable practitioner income depends on whether it is treated as NHS-derived profit for the Type 1 certificate; confirm, do not assert. Anchor §2.C.
- **H2: Route 3: paid via a personal service company (PSC)**
  - Mechanics: the director invoices through their own limited company.
  - Tax: corporation tax in the company, then salary/dividend extraction; and the off-payroll/IR35 question arises because the network (or fee-paying entity) may have to determine status. Link `locum-doctor-ir35-what-you-need-to-know` and anchor §1.A; do not re-teach IR35.
  - Pension (HEDGE, important): income routed through a company is not NHS pensionable. State this plainly per §2.C: company/dividend income loses NHS accrual. So a PSC route, whatever its tax merits, generally costs the NHS pension on that income. This is the one route where the pension answer is clear and negative.
- **H2: Route 4: paid direct as self-employed income**
  - Mechanics: the director is paid directly and declares it as self-employed income on self assessment.
  - Tax: trading/other income with Class 4 NIC; record-keeping and self-assessment implications (link `gp-tax-return` only if it exists; if not, link `gp-accounting-guide`). NOTE TO WRITER: do not link `gp-tax-return` unless confirmed to exist; the safe links are in section 7.
  - Pension (HEDGE): self-employed income paid direct (not as NHS-employer/practitioner pay) is generally not pensionable in the NHS scheme; confirm and do not over-assert. Anchor §2.C.
- **H2: So is clinical director pay pensionable? The honest answer**
  - The reasoning-first synthesis: pensionability turns on whether the pay reaches the doctor as NHS-pension-eligible pay through an NHS-pension-employing body (as practitioner NHS-derived profit or as officer/employee pay delivered in a pensionable form), versus being routed outside that (PSC, or self-employed income paid direct), in which case it is generally not pensionable. The single clear rule from house position §2.C: company/dividend income is never NHS pensionable.
  - Carry across the NHS-leadership-pay principle (from the BMA medical-director guidance): even within employment, leadership pay is pensionable only when delivered in a recognised pensionable form (for example a responsibility allowance or pensionable sessions), not automatically; and contracts cannot be retrospectively re-engineered just to boost pension.
  - **State the page's hedge explicitly to the reader:** the pension treatment of clinical-director pay depends on the specific arrangement, it is a point to confirm with the practice, the network and the pension agency (PCSE) before agreeing the route, and this page does not assert a single answer. Link `gp-pension-contributions-tax-relief` and `nhs-pension-annual-allowance-complete-guide` for the wider pension picture.
- **H2: The annual allowance angle for high-earning directors**
  - Short: extra pensionable income can push a high-earning partner or consultant toward the annual-allowance taper, so even where the pay is pensionable, the marginal value should be weighed (link `nhs-pension-annual-allowance-complete-guide`). Keep brief; do not re-teach the taper.

## 5. Exact HP positions, dates and verified anchors

**HP positions to use:**
- Clinical director and leadership/management funding is now combined into core PCN funding within the Network Contract DES. (§3, verified below.)
- Employment status and tax follow the substance of the arrangement, not the label; a partner is taxed on profit share (Class 4), a salaried GP under PAYE (Class 1). (§1.)
- A PSC route brings the off-payroll/IR35 question; the network or fee-payer may determine status. (§1.A.)
- Company/dividend income is NOT NHS pensionable; NHS accrual is lost on income routed through a company. (§2.C, the one clear pension rule on this page.)
- Pensionable practitioner pay flows via the Type 1/Type 2 machinery; whether a given payment is pensionable depends on its characterisation. (§2.C.)
- Management/leadership work that is not patient medical care can fall outside the VAT exemption (light touch). (§6.)
