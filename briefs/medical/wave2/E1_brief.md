## 3. Angle (2 to 3 sentences) + differentiation

**Angle.** When a salaried or sessional GP is offered a partnership, the practical question is "what do I actually pay to come in, and what does that money buy?" This page answers exactly that: it explains the **capital buy-in** as a contribution into the partnership capital accounts for the incoming partner's share of net assets, working capital and any premises share, the concept of **parity** (working up from a reduced share to a full, equal profit share over an agreed parity or incremental period), and how the buy-in figure is **set and valued** (net assets per the accounts, premises on a District-Valuer or surveyor basis, never an NHS goodwill multiple). It is the money-and-mechanics page for joining, not a broad pros-and-cons of partnership.

## 4. Section-by-section outline (target 2,800 to 3,500 words)

- **H1: Buying Into a GP Partnership: The Capital Buy-In, Parity and How the Cost Is Set**
- **H2: What a buy-in actually is (and what it is not)**
  - Define the buy-in: a payment (or a credit built up over time) into the **partnership capital accounts** representing the incoming partner's share of the practice's **net assets**: equipment and fixtures, working capital, and any **share of owned premises** (anchor §4). State plainly that it is **not** a payment for NHS goodwill, because the sale of NHS GP goodwill has been prohibited since 1 April 2004 (current instrument SI 2019/251); the only goodwill that can ever be paid for is goodwill on genuinely private, non-NHS work (anchor §4, one sentence; link the complete guide for the goodwill detail, do NOT re-teach it).
  - Contrast with how buy-ins work in some other professions (a multiple of profits / goodwill). Make the GP-specific point: there is **no goodwill multiple** in an NHS GP buy-in; the number is driven by net assets and premises valuation. Cross-link `becoming-gp-partner-financial-implications` for the broad picture in one sentence.
- **H2: The two things you might be buying into: net assets and premises**
  - H3: The net-assets share (the capital account). What the partnership owns less what it owes, divided into capital accounts. Explain that an incoming partner usually contributes capital to bring their capital account up to the level of an existing partner's (so all partners hold a comparable stake), funding their share of working capital and tangible assets. Do not lock any figure. Link D1 (`reading-gp-partnership-accounts-current-capital-accounts`) for how the capital account is presented.
  - H3: The premises share (often a separate decision). Explain that surgery premises are frequently held in a **separate property partnership or LLP** alongside the medical partnership, so buying into the practice and buying into the premises can be **two separate transactions** (anchor §4). A new partner may join the medical partnership but not (yet, or ever) the property; or may buy a premises share funded separately. Note premises valuation is property-specific and surveyor/District-Valuer assessed, so there is no standard figure, and that the income support (notional rent for owner-occupiers, the legacy cost rent scheme) affects the property economics. Link `gp-surgery-premises-own-vs-rent-tax-guide` for the own-versus-rent decision and `gp-partnership-last-man-standing-premises-risk` for the standing-liability risk; do NOT re-teach either.
- **H2: Parity: working up to a full profit share**
  - H3: What parity means. Define parity as the point at which a partner holds a **full, equal share** of profits (subject to the deed's allocation method). Explain the common practice of an incoming partner starting on a **reduced share** and stepping up to parity over an agreed **parity period** (the incremental period), reflecting that they may initially do fewer sessions, carry less capital, or be on a trial footing. State clearly that **parity periods vary widely by practice and are set in the deed**, so do NOT state a standard length or a standard starting percentage (no fabricated figures).
  - H3: How parity interacts with the buy-in. Explain that the share you work up to often tracks the capital you contribute and the sessions you commit: a partner building to a full share may build their capital account up in parallel. Note that "parity" can refer both to the **profit share** and to **equal capital**; the deed should make clear which it means. Anchor §1 (taxed on profit share, not drawings): during the parity period you are taxed on your **allocated** share for each year, whatever drawings you take. Link `gp-partnership-profit-sharing-tax-planning` for how shares are allocated and `gp-partner-drawings-vs-profit-tax-reserving` for the drawings-versus-profit point.
  - H3: Mutual assessment versus parity (signpost only). Note in one or two sentences that many practices run a **mutual assessment period** before offering parity or permanency, and that the financial due diligence to do before committing is covered separately. Link sibling E2 (`gp-partnership-mutual-assessment-period-what-to-check`); do NOT teach the DD checklist here.
- **H2: How the buy-in cost is set and valued**
  - H3: The starting point: the partnership accounts. Explain that the buy-in is anchored to the **net assets per the most recent accounts** (or a valuation drawn up for the transaction), divided to give the incoming partner's share. Link D1 for reading those accounts. Note tangible assets may be at book value or revalued; the deed and the negotiation decide.
  - H3: Premises valuation. Where premises are bought, the property share is valued on a **current-market basis** (surveyor / District-Valuer assessed), separately from the medical-partnership net assets. Again, no standard figure; this is property-specific (anchor §4).
  - H3: Working capital. Explain that part of the buy-in is the incoming partner's share of **working capital** (the cash the practice needs to fund the gap between paying staff and receiving NHS income). A partner who joins with a low or nil capital account may build it up from undrawn profit over time instead of paying cash up front; the deed sets the mechanism.
  - H3: What is explicitly excluded. Reiterate once: **no NHS goodwill** (cannot be sold), and that the figure is therefore net-assets-and-premises driven, not a profit multiple (anchor §4; link the complete guide). State that any **private goodwill** (genuinely private, non-NHS work) is the only goodwill that could legitimately feature, and is usually small or nil in a typical NHS-led practice.
- **H2: Paying the buy-in: cash, or building it from profit**
  - Explain the two common routes without fabricating figures: paying capital in (often funded by a **partnership-capital loan**, covered in E3), or **leaving in undrawn profit** so the capital account builds over the parity period. Note the cash-flow implication of each, and that the choice interacts with how parity is structured. Link sibling E3 (`financing-gp-partnership-buy-in-tax-relief`) for the loan and the interest relief; do NOT teach interest relief here. One sentence on the §1 point that drawings are set against anticipated profit and trued up at year-end, linking the broad page and `gp-partner-drawings-vs-profit-tax-reserving`.
- **H2: The capital guarantees and liabilities that come with the stake**
  - Note that buying in can bring **capital guarantees** for practice borrowings or lease commitments, and (where you buy premises) exposure to the premises liability and the **last-man-standing** risk. Keep this short: state it is part of the risk picture and link `gp-partnership-last-man-standing-premises-risk` and the broad `becoming-gp-partner-financial-implications` page; do NOT re-teach last-man-standing here.
- **H2: Questions to ask about the buy-in before you agree it**
  - A focused checklist (buy-in mechanics only, not the general deed checklist on the broad page): What exactly does the buy-in represent in the capital accounts (net assets, working capital, premises share)? Is the premises a separate transaction, and am I buying in to it? What is the parity timetable and the starting share, and what triggers each step up? How is the figure valued (book value or revaluation; who values the premises)? Do I pay cash, build capital from undrawn profit, or borrow? What capital guarantees or liabilities attach? ## 5. Exact HP positions, dates and verified anchors

**HP positions to use:**
- On a partner's **entry**, what changes hands is a share of the partnership's **tangible assets** (equipment, fixtures, working capital) and any **owned premises**, recorded through the **partnership capital accounts**; a buy-in pays for the incoming partner's share of net assets. (§4)
- **NHS GP goodwill cannot be sold** (prohibited since 1 April 2004; current instrument SI 2019/251); a GP, or anyone on their behalf, cannot sell the goodwill of an NHS medical practice, nor company shares whose value includes it. The only saleable goodwill is **private, non-NHS** goodwill. So a buy-in is driven by **capital-account and premises valuation, not a goodwill multiple**. (§4)
- **Premises** are often held in a **separate property partnership or LLP** outside the medical partnership; income support via **notional rent** (owner-occupiers, current-market-rent basis, District-Valuer assessed) or the legacy **cost rent** scheme; amounts are property-specific, so do NOT lock a figure. **Last-man-standing** is a real planning point. (§4, light; link the two premises pages.)
- A GP partner is **self-employed** and **taxed on their profit share, not their drawings**; drawings are set against anticipated profit and trued up at year-end. (§1; relevant to the parity-period share.)

**DO NOT** invent buy-in figures or parity periods (they vary by practice; §4 premises and parity are practice-specific). Do NOT state a standard buy-in amount, a standard parity length, or a standard starting share. Do NOT re-teach the NHS goodwill rules, the broad financial implications, the pension/annual-allowance taper, last-man-standing, or basis-period reform: link out. Do NOT use a goodwill multiple framing for an NHS practice.
