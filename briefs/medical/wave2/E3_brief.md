## 3. Angle (2 to 3 sentences) + differentiation

**Angle.** Most GPs do not have the buy-in capital sitting in cash, so they borrow it, and the good news is that the **interest on a loan taken out to buy into or contribute capital to a partnership qualifies for income tax relief** under the qualifying loan interest rules. This page explains how a GP **finances** a buy-in (a partnership-capital loan, building capital from undrawn profit, or a mix), the **tax relief on the loan interest** (how it works, the conditions, the cap on income tax reliefs, and how it is claimed), and the **cash flow** of servicing the loan against partnership drawings. It is the funding-and-relief page that sits underneath the buy-in (E1) and the diligence (E2).

- **H2: Why most GPs borrow to buy in**
  - Set the scene: a buy-in funds the incoming partner's **capital-account contribution** for their share of net assets, working capital and any premises share (light §4; link E1 for the detail, do NOT re-teach valuation). Few GPs hold that in cash, so borrowing is normal. Introduce the headline that the **interest on the loan is tax-relievable** if the loan is used to buy into or contribute capital to the partnership, which materially changes the after-tax cost. One sentence that NHS goodwill is not part of the buy-in (§4; link E1 / the complete guide).
- **H2: The ways to fund a buy-in**
  - H3: A partnership-capital loan. The common route: a personal loan (often a specialist medical or partnership-capital facility) to fund the capital contribution. Note arrangement and the security position vary; keep general, no product names or rates, no pricing.
  - H3: Building capital from undrawn profit. The alternative: joining with a low or nil capital account and leaving in undrawn profit so the capital account builds over the parity period (link E1 and `gp-partner-drawings-vs-profit-tax-reserving`). Trade-off: lower borrowing, but lower drawings while the capital builds.
  - H3: A mix, and the premises loan as a separate facility. Note that where you also buy a premises share (often a **separate property partnership**, §4), that may be a **separate borrowing** (a commercial property loan), and that the interest treatment can differ (a property loan funds a property interest, not a contribution to the trading partnership's capital). Keep this a flag, and steer the property-loan detail to `gp-surgery-premises-own-vs-rent-tax-guide`.
- **H2: Tax relief on the loan interest: the qualifying loan interest rules**
  - H3: The core rule. State plainly: interest on a loan **to buy a share in a partnership, or to contribute capital or advance money to a partnership** (where the money is used wholly for the partnership's trade or profession), is **eligible for income tax relief**. This is "qualifying loan interest relief". Cite ITA 2007 s.398 (loan to invest in partnership) and s.383 (relief for interest payments). Verified, see §5.
  - H3: How the relief is given. The relief is given by **deducting the interest in calculating the individual's net income** for the tax year (a relief at Step 2 of the income-tax calculation), i.e. it reduces the income on which you pay tax across the board, not just trading profit. State that it is claimed on the **Self Assessment return** in the "other tax reliefs" section (HS340), **not** as a partnership business expense in the accounts. This is the key distinction from a trading deduction. Verified, see §5.
  - H3: The conditions. You must be a **genuine partner** in a trading or professional partnership and remain so; the relief is **not available to a limited partner** in a limited partnership, nor to a **member of an investment LLP** (a partnership whose business is wholly or mainly making investments). State that a GP partnership is a trading/professional partnership, so a GP buying in normally qualifies, but the loan must actually be **used for the partnership** (to buy the share, or contribute/advance capital used wholly for its trade). Cite ITA 2007 s.399 (the limited-partner / investment-LLP exclusions). Verified, see §5.
  - H3: The recovery-of-capital rule (keep brief, flag for the writer). Note that the relief can be **reduced or withdrawn if the partner later recovers capital** from the partnership (an anti-avoidance mechanism), so the relief assumes the capital stays invested. Frame as "if you draw your capital back out, the relief can be clawed back, take advice". **VERIFY:** writer to confirm the recovery-of-capital provision reference (ITA 2007 around ss.406 to 412) at draft time before citing a precise section; if unconfirmed, state the principle without a section number.
- **H2: The cap on income tax reliefs**
  - State the cap clearly: qualifying loan interest relief is one of the reliefs caught by the **limit on income tax reliefs**, which restricts the total of these reliefs in a tax year to the **greater of £50,000 and 25% of "adjusted total income"** (ITA 2007 s.24A; confirmed in HMRC HS340 and HS204). For almost every GP buying into a partnership the interest is far below this cap, so it is a ceiling to be aware of rather than a live constraint, but it should be stated. Verified, see §5. Do NOT imply the cap bites at normal buy-in interest levels.
- **H2: The cash flow of servicing the buy-in loan**
  - H3: Drawings, profit and the loan. Anchor §1 (a partner is taxed on **profit share, not drawings**; drawings are set against anticipated profit and trued up at year-end). Explain that the loan is serviced from **drawings**, so the buy-in is funded out of post-personal-tax income, and the interest relief softens, but does not remove, that cost. No fabricated figures; any worked illustration must be labelled "illustrative" and use round, clearly hypothetical numbers and a hypothetical interest rate.
  - H3: Budgeting for tax and the loan together. Make the planning point: a new partner is simultaneously (1) servicing the buy-in loan, (2) reserving for Self Assessment with payments on account, and (3) possibly building a current account. Link `gp-partner-drawings-vs-profit-tax-reserving` for the reserving discipline and `gp-financial-planning` for the wider plan. Note the relief is realised through the tax return (a reduction in the tax bill), not as cash in hand from the lender, so the cash-flow timing matters.
  - H3: A worked illustration (clearly hypothetical). Optional: show, with explicitly illustrative figures and a hypothetical rate, how relief at the partner's marginal rate reduces the net cost of the interest (for example, interest at a marginal rate of 40% costs 60% after relief). Label every number "illustrative" and the rate "hypothetical"; do NOT present it as a typical or expected figure.
- **H2: Practical points and pitfalls**
  - Keep the loan **clearly identifiable as used for the partnership** (mixed-use or refinanced loans can lose relief; the use of the money is what matters); do not route the loan through a structure that breaks the "used wholly for the partnership" condition; remember a **property** loan to buy a premises share is treated under its own rules (link the premises page); the **recovery-of-capital** rule can reduce relief if you draw capital back out; and the relief must be **claimed** on the return (it is not automatic). State that a medical accountant should set the loan up and claim correctly from year one.

**VERIFIED qualifying loan interest relief anchors (primary source fetched 2026-06-03):**
- **ITA 2007 s.398 (loan to invest in partnership)**, https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2007/3/section/398
  - *Verified 2026-06-03 (legislation.gov.uk):* subsection (2) lists the qualifying uses, "(a) purchasing a share in a partnership; (b) contributing money to a partnership, by way of capital or premium, that is used wholly for the purposes of the trade or profession carried on by the partnership; (c) advancing money to a partnership that is so used; (d) repaying another loan to which this section applies." This is the statutory hook for "a loan to buy into or contribute capital to a partnership qualifies".
- **ITA 2007 s.383 (relief for interest payments)**, https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2007/3/section/383
  - *Verified 2026-06-03 (legislation.gov.uk):* s.383(2) lists s.398 among the loans to which Chapter 1 of Part 8 applies; s.383(4): "The relief is given by deducting that amount in calculating the person's net income for the tax year." Use for "relief is given against income (at Step 2 of the income-tax calculation), claimed on the return, not as a partnership business expense." NOTE for writer: the brief direction "relief against total income" is correct in substance (it reduces income generally, beyond just trading profit), but the precise statutory mechanism is a deduction in computing **net income** at Step 2; phrase it as "set against your income for the year" / "reduces your overall income-tax bill", and avoid asserting the literal phrase "total income" as a quote.
- **ITA 2007 s.399 (loan to invest in partnership: exclusions)**, https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2007/3/section/399
  - *Verified 2026-06-03 (legislation.gov.uk):* Condition A requires the individual to have been a partner "otherwise than (a) as a limited partner in a limited partnership registered under the Limited Partnerships Act 1907, or (b) as a member of an investment LLP"; an "investment LLP" is an LLP whose business consists wholly or mainly of making investments and which derives its principal income from investments. Use for "must be a genuine partner; not a limited partner, not an investment-LLP member."
- **ITA 2007 s.24A (cap on income tax reliefs)**, https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2007/3/section/24A
  - *Verified 2026-06-03 (legislation.gov.uk):* the cap (Amount B) is "(a) £50,000, or (b) if more, 25% of the taxpayer's adjusted total income." Qualifying loan interest relief (relief under Chapter 1 of Part 8) is within the capped reliefs.
- **HMRC HS340 (Interest and alternative finance payments eligible for relief on qualifying loans), 2026**, https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/interest-and-alternative-finance-payments-eligible-for-relief-on-qualifying-loans-and-alternative-finance-arrangements-hs340-self-assessment-helpshee/hs340-interest-and-alternative-finance-payments-eligible-for-relief-on-qualifying-loans-and-alternative-finance-arrangements-2026

Use as the plain-English HMRC anchor and the claim-mechanics anchor (cite HS340 by name).
