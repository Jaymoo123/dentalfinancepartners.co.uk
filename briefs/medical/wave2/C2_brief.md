## 3. Angle (2 to 3 sentences) + differentiation

**Angle.** Alongside its NHS contract a typical GP practice earns a long tail of private and non-NHS income: private medicals and forms, insurance and medico-legal reports, travel clinics and travel vaccinations, occupational health, minor surgery done privately, and various signing and certification fees. These streams are easy to under-record and they carry the practice's only real VAT exposure, because while genuine clinical care is VAT-exempt, the reports and certificates that enable a third party to make a decision (and any purely cosmetic work) are standard-rated. This page maps the streams, explains how each is taxed as practice trading income, and draws the exempt-vs-standard-rated VAT line clearly, with the £90,000 threshold and partial exemption that follow from it.

  - Open with the picture, then take each stream and give (a) what it is, (b) how it is taxed, (c) the VAT line. Keep the tax line short each time (it is the same answer: practice trading income, profit share, not pensionable) and let the VAT line carry the detail.
  - H3: Private medicals and forms. Examples: insurance medicals, pre-employment and fitness medicals, HGV/LGV and taxi-driver medicals, sports and diving medicals, fitness-to-fly, adoption and fostering medicals, private sick notes and forms. VAT: most of these are STANDARD-RATED because their primary purpose is to inform a third party's decision (employer, insurer, licensing body), not to treat the patient. Flag pre-employment medicals and driving-fitness medicals as standard-rated explicitly.
  - H3: Insurance and medico-legal reports. Examples: insurance reports (for example DVLA/insurer reports on a patient), GP factual reports for solicitors, medico-legal and expert-witness reports, court reports, capacity assessments for legal purposes. VAT: STANDARD-RATED, because the principal purpose is to enable a third party (insurer, court, solicitor) to make a decision (the d'Ambrumenil position). Note the narrow exception type (a report that is genuinely part of the patient's care can be exempt) but lead with standard-rated for third-party reports.
  - H3: Travel clinics and travel vaccinations. VAT: the vaccination itself is medical care and is EXEMPT (administering a travel vaccine protects the patient's health); the international certificate of vaccination is part of that exempt supply. Flag the planning nuance: travel-clinic income is largely exempt, which is the opposite of reports, and note that any goods sold (for example anti-malarials on a private prescription) follow the C1 private-prescription rule (link C1).
  - H3: Minor surgery and minor procedures done privately. Where minor surgery is done for a clinical (therapeutic) reason it is EXEMPT medical care; where it is purely cosmetic with no therapeutic purpose it is STANDARD-RATED. Use this as the clean illustration of the purpose test: the same procedure can be exempt or taxable depending on why it is done. (Note that NHS-commissioned minor surgery is an enhanced service, not private income; link `enhanced-services-gp-practice-income-tax`.)
  - H3: Occupational health services. Practices sometimes provide occupational health to local employers. VAT: mixed, and often STANDARD-RATED where the service is for the employer's benefit (fitness assessments, management reports) rather than treating the worker; flag it as a watch-item and point to professional advice on the specific service mix (§6 lists occupational health as a watch-item).
  - H3: Signing fees, certification and administrative work. Examples: passport countersignature, certifying documents, firearms certificates, some cremation work. VAT: largely STANDARD-RATED where there is no care element (administrative/third-party-decision work), though some statutory certificates (for example cremation certificates) are treated as exempt; flag that the liability is item-specific and point to the HMRC table.
  - H3: Other private clinical work. Private GP consultations and self-pay clinics where the purpose is genuinely the patient's health are EXEMPT medical care. Use to reinforce that the default for genuine private clinical care is exempt, not taxable (the §6 "do not say private medical services are standard-rated by default" rule).
- **H2: How private and non-NHS income is taxed**
  - It is all practice trading income. Whatever the practice invoices and collects for private work is part of the partnership's trading profit, taxed on the partners as part of their profit share through the SA800 and their personal SA104, exactly like NHS income (§1, §3). There is no separate regime; it simply increases trading profit. Link `gp-partnership-tax-complete-guide`.
  - It is not NHS-pensionable. Private and non-NHS income is not NHS-derived, so it is not pensionable under the NHS Pension Scheme (the Type 1 certificate captures NHS-derived profit only). State this plainly. Anchor §2.C; link `gp-pension-contributions-tax-relief`.
  - Recording and under-recording. Private income is invoiced and collected by the practice directly (it does not come through PCSE), so it is the income stream most often under-recorded or banked inconsistently; clean recording matters for both tax and the VAT threshold. Signpost `gp-bookkeeping-guide-uk` and `gp-accounting-guide`.
  - Expenses against private work. Costs incurred to earn private income are deductible against practice profit on normal wholly-and-exclusively principles; signpost `gp-tax-deductions-complete-list-2026` rather than teaching it.
- **H2: VAT: the exempt-vs-standard-rated line and what it means for the practice**
  - H3: The principle (the purpose test). Restate: a service is exempt only where its principal purpose is the protection, maintenance or restoration of the patient's health and it is within the doctor's registered field (VATA 1994 Sch 9 Group 7, Item 1). Where the primary purpose is to enable a third party to make a decision, or the work is purely cosmetic, it is standard-rated. This is the d'Ambrumenil purpose test, in force since 1 May 2007.
  - H3: A practical exempt-vs-standard-rated list. Give a clear two-column-style list (in prose or a list, not invented figures) drawn from HMRC's doctors' liability table: STANDARD-RATED includes pre-employment medicals, HGV/driving-fitness medicals, medico-legal and expert-witness reports, coroner's post-mortem work, paternity/DNA blood tests, passport countersignature, purely cosmetic procedures; EXEMPT includes genuine clinical consultations and treatment, therapeutic minor surgery, travel vaccinations and the international vaccination certificate, cremation certificates, and reports that are a genuine part of the patient's care. Flag that the table is the reference and the liability is decided service by service on the purpose test.
  - H3: The £90,000 threshold (taxable turnover only). Only the standard-rated (and any zero-rated dispensing) turnover counts towards the £90,000 VAT registration threshold; exempt clinical income and outside-the-scope NHS income do not. So a practice doing mostly exempt clinical work with a modest amount of reports and medicals usually stays under the threshold, but a busy reports/medicals/occupational-health operation can cross it. Link `gp-vat-registration` for the registration mechanics and the £88,000 deregistration threshold.
  - H3: Partial exemption (light). A registered practice with both exempt and taxable supplies operates partial exemption: it can recover input VAT on costs relating to its taxable (standard-rated) work but not on costs relating to exempt care, subject to the de minimis test. Keep this to a short paragraph and hand the detail to `gp-vat-registration`.
  - Anchor §6 throughout.

  - Assuming all medical work is exempt and so missing a VAT registration obligation once reports/medicals cross the threshold.

## 5. Exact HP positions, dates and verified anchors

**HP positions to use:**
- Private and non-NHS income is practice trading income; a partner is taxed on profit share, not drawings (§3, §1).
- Private/non-NHS income is not NHS-pensionable (only NHS-derived profit is, via the Type 1 certificate) (§2.C).
- VAT: genuine medical care is exempt under VATA 1994 Sch 9 Group 7 on the principal-purpose test (protection, maintenance or restoration of health); cosmetic-only work and medico-legal / expert-witness / third-party-decision reports are STANDARD-RATED; the default for genuine private clinical care is EXEMPT, not standard-rated (§6).
- The £90,000 registration threshold counts taxable (non-exempt) turnover only; £88,000 deregistration; partial exemption for a practice with both exempt and taxable supplies (§6).

2. How is private and non-NHS income taxed? (Answer: practice trading income, taxed on the partners' profit share; SA800/SA104.)
3. Is private income NHS-pensionable? (Answer: no, only NHS-derived profit is pensionable.)
4. Does private income come through PCSE? (Answer: no, it is invoiced and collected by the practice directly; reconciliation via C3 is for NHS payments only.)

7. Is a pre-employment or HGV medical exempt? (Answer: no, standard-rated.)

10. Is occupational health work standard-rated? (Answer: often yes where it is for the employer's benefit; it is a watch-item, take advice on the specific service.)
11. Does private income count towards the VAT registration threshold? (Answer: only the standard-rated part counts towards the £90,000 threshold; exempt clinical income does not.)
12. If we register for VAT, can we recover VAT on costs? (Answer: partly, under partial exemption, on costs relating to taxable work; link gp-vat-registration.)
13. How is this different from a consultant's or GP's personal private practice? (Answer: this is the practice's income streams; the individual doctor's personal NHS-plus-private position is a separate topic; link private-practice-tax-nhs-and-private-income.)
