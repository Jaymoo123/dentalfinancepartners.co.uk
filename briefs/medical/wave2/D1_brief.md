## 3. Angle (2 to 3 sentences) + differentiation

**Angle.** Every year a GP partner is handed a bound set of partnership accounts, signs them, and often understands very little of what the numbers actually mean. This page is a plain-English reading guide to that document: what the profit and loss account shows, what the balance sheet shows, and, the part that confuses partners most, the difference between each partner's **capital account** (their stake in the practice's net assets) and their **current account** (the running tally of profit allocated to them, less what they have drawn). The aim is comprehension of the accounts themselves, so a partner can open the file and know what they are looking at, not a tax tutorial.

## 4. Section-by-section outline (target 2,800 to 3,500 words)

- **H1: How to Read GP Partnership Accounts: Current Accounts, Capital Accounts and Drawings**
- **H2: What a set of GP partnership accounts actually is**
  - Define the document: a set of accounts prepared by the practice accountant for the partnership as a whole, normally comprising a profit and loss account (income and expenditure), a balance sheet, and a set of partners' capital and current accounts, plus notes. State that the partnership is tax-transparent: it files one partnership return (SA800) and each partner's allocated share flows to the partnership pages (SA104) of their own return (anchor §1; link `gp-partnership-tax-complete-guide` for the tax).
  - Note these are usually unaudited accounts prepared to an agreed accounting date, and signed by the partners. Distinguish from the tax computation (the accounts profit is adjusted for tax, briefly; link the complete guide).
- **H2: The profit and loss account: how the practice did this year**
  - H3: Income lines. Map the practice income to the accounts: core NHS funding (Global Sum and other GMS/PMS income), QOF, enhanced services, PCN/Network Contract DES, premises reimbursement (notional/cost rent), dispensing income where relevant, and private/non-NHS income. Keep this a reading guide, not a funding tutorial; do not lock any figure.
  - H3: Expenditure lines. Staff costs (the biggest line in most practices), premises costs, locum cover, drugs/clinical supplies, IT and equipment, professional fees, finance costs. Note depreciation appears here but is added back for tax (one sentence; link the complete guide).
  - H3: Net profit. The bottom line: income less expenditure equals the profit available to share between partners. State plainly that this profit, allocated under the deed, is what each partner is taxed on, not their drawings (anchor §1).
- **H2: The balance sheet: what the practice owns and owes**
  - Explain assets (fixtures and equipment, any owned premises, debtors such as money owed by the commissioner, cash) and liabilities (creditors, loans, accruals). The net assets figure is what the partners collectively own.
  - H3: How the net assets are financed. Bridge to the key idea: net assets are represented by the partners' capital and current accounts. This is the line that connects the balance sheet to each partner's personal stake.
- **H2: The capital account: your long-term stake in the practice**
  - Define the capital account as each partner's share of the **fixed, long-term capital** of the partnership: their share of net assets (equipment, fixtures, working capital and any owned premises share). Explain that a buy-in pays into it and a buy-out returns it (anchor §4; link `becoming-gp-partner-financial-implications`).
  - State the GP-specific point clearly: the capital account reflects **tangible assets and premises, never NHS goodwill**, because NHS GP goodwill cannot be sold (anchor §4; one sentence, link the complete guide for the goodwill detail; do NOT re-teach goodwill here).
  - Note capital accounts are often "fixed" amounts revalued only on events (a property revaluation, a partner change), in contrast to the current account which moves every year.
- **H2: The current account: profit in, drawings out**
  - Define the current account as the **running, year-to-year** account: profit allocated to the partner is credited, drawings and any partner-specific costs are debited, and the balance carried forward is what the partnership owes the partner (a credit balance) or the partner owes the partnership (a debit/overdrawn balance).
  - H3: A worked illustration (clearly illustrative, no fabricated practice figures). Opening current account balance, plus profit share for the year, less drawings taken, less any tax paid on the partner's behalf or personal expenses charged through, equals the closing balance. Keep numbers round and labelled "illustrative".
  - H3: A credit vs an overdrawn current account. A healthy credit balance means the partner has left profit in the practice (often funding working capital); an overdrawn current account means the partner has drawn more than their share of profit and may need to repay it. Tie to drawings discipline and link D2 for the reserving and cash-flow detail.
- **H2: Capital account vs current account: the distinction that confuses partners**
  - A short, explicit side-by-side: capital account = long-term stake in net assets (changes on buy-in/buy-out and revaluations); current account = annual profit-and-drawings tally (changes every year). Spell out that money sitting in your current account is not the same as your capital stake, and that the two are presented separately for a reason.
  - Optional H3: why some practices keep them combined or split them; keep brief and neutral.
- **H2: Drawings, reserves and undrawn profit in the accounts**
  - Explain how drawings appear (a debit to the current account), what "undrawn profit" or a profit reserve is (profit retained in the practice rather than drawn, shown as a credit current-account balance or a reserve), and why a practice holds working-capital reserves. Make the §1 point again: tax follows allocated profit, not drawings, so a partner can owe tax on profit still sitting in the practice. Link D2 for how to reserve for that.
- **H2: Notes, the accountant's report and the accounting date**
  - What the notes add (accounting policies, the basis of the accounts, the partners' shares), what an accountant's report on unaudited accounts means, and why the accounting date matters (it frames the period the accounts cover). One sentence on the move to the tax-year basis and link D3 for basis-period reform; do NOT teach it here.
- **H2: How reading your accounts helps you (and what to ask your accountant)**
  - A short checklist of questions a partner should be able to answer from their accounts: what is my profit share, what is my current account balance, what is my capital account, am I overdrawn, how much undrawn profit is in the practice. Signpost `gp-accounting-guide` and `gp-bookkeeping-guide-uk` for the practice-side bookkeeping.

## 5. Exact HP positions, dates and verified anchors

**HP positions to use:**
- A GP partner is **self-employed**; the partnership files an **SA800** and each partner's share flows to the **partnership pages (SA104)** of their personal return; taxed as trading income with Class 4 NIC. (§1)
- **Partners are taxed on their profit share, not their drawings.** (§1) Reinforce in the current-account and drawings sections.
- Partnership taxation under the **Income Tax (Trading and Other Income) Act 2005**; partnership-return machinery under the **Taxes Management Act 1970**. (§1)
- On entry/exit, what changes hands is a share of **tangible assets and any owned premises through the capital accounts**, plus any private goodwill; **NHS goodwill cannot be sold**. (§4, one sentence, link the complete guide.)
- Allowable expenses are deducted before profit is shared (light touch, §8; link the complete guide / deductions page).
