## 3. Angle (2 to 3 sentences) + differentiation

**Angle.** When a GP partner retires or otherwise leaves, two separate things happen: they receive what is owed to them (their capital account balance and, where they own one, their premises share), and their share of the partnership trade ceases for tax. This page joins those two threads into one practical guide: what the leaving partner actually gets back, how the cessation of their notional trade works under the post-reform tax-year basis (including why there is usually no overlap relief left to give on a later cessation), whether any capital gains tax arises on a premises share, and the deed mechanics that make an exit orderly. It is the leaving-partner's money-and-tax page, not a premises-liability page.

**Differentiation.**
- **Differs from the Wave 1 page `gp-partnership-last-man-standing-premises-risk`.** That page is about the PREMISES liability of a shrinking partnership, the risk that a single remaining partner is left holding the whole lease and property obligation. This page is the opposite vantage point: the partner who is LEAVING, what they get back (capital account and any premises share) and the cessation tax on their share. It links last-man-standing once, as the flip-side risk the continuing partners carry, and does not re-teach it.
- **Differs from sibling F2 (`gp-practice-merger-accounts-tax-explained`).** F2 is about two practices combining and whether that is a cessation/recommencement or a continuation. This page is one individual partner exiting a continuing practice. Cross-link F2 for the merger scenario; do not cover mergers here.
- **Differs from Cluster D (`reading-gp-partnership-accounts-current-capital-accounts` and `gp-partnership-basis-period-reform-explained`).** D1 teaches how to read a capital account; this page uses the capital account as the thing that is RETURNED on exit. D3 teaches basis period reform in general; this page applies the post-reform tax-year basis to the specific event of a partner leaving (and the overlap-relief consequence on cessation). ## 4. Section-by-section outline (target 2,800 to 3,500 words)

- **H1: Retiring or Leaving a GP Partnership: Your Capital Account, Cessation and the Tax**
- **H2: Two separate things happen when you leave**
  - Frame the whole page: (1) you receive what the partnership owes you, your capital account balance and any premises share; (2) your share of the trade ceases for tax. Make clear these are governed by different rules (the partnership deed and accounts for what you get back; the cessation tax rules for your final tax position) and that the page covers both. State up front, in one sentence anchored to §4, that there is no NHS goodwill payment on the way out, because NHS GP goodwill cannot be sold (prohibited since 1 April 2004; current instrument SI 2019/251), so an exit is about capital, premises and the cessation of your trade share, not a goodwill sale. Link the complete guide for the goodwill detail; do not re-teach it.
- **H2: What the leaving partner gets back**
  - H3: Your capital account. Explain that the capital account records the partner's long-term stake (capital introduced, accumulated undrawn profits, their share of net assets) and that on leaving this balance is repaid under the terms of the deed, often over a period rather than as a single lump sum (anchor §4). Note the current account (short-term drawings versus profit share) is settled too. Do not lock any figure or repayment period; the deed sets them. Link D1 (`reading-gp-partnership-accounts-current-capital-accounts`) for how the capital and current accounts are presented.
  - H3: Your premises share (if you own one). Explain that surgery premises are frequently held in a separate property partnership or LLP, so a partner can leave the medical partnership but still hold (or have to sell) a premises share, which can be a separate transaction on its own timetable (anchor §4). The continuing partners or an incoming partner may buy out the share; valuation is property-specific and surveyor/District-Valuer assessed, so no standard figure. Note the income support (notional rent for owner-occupiers, legacy cost rent) affects the property economics. Cross-link `gp-surgery-premises-own-vs-rent-tax-guide` for the ownership picture and `gp-partnership-last-man-standing-premises-risk` for the standing-liability risk the remaining partners carry.
  - H3: What you do NOT get: NHS goodwill. One short reiteration anchored to §4: there is no NHS goodwill to sell on exit; the only goodwill that can ever feature is genuinely private, non-NHS goodwill, usually small or nil in an NHS-led practice. Link the complete guide.
- **H2: The cessation of your share for tax**
  - H3: You have a notional trade, and it ceases. Explain the core mechanic anchored to §1 and verified at PM163090: for tax, each partner is treated as carrying on their own notional trade (their share of the partnership trade), which commences when they join and ceases when they leave. When you leave, the cessation (closing year) rules apply to your notional trade, even though the practice itself carries on. Quote the PM163090 wording that the notional trade ceases when the partner leaves.
  - H3: Your final tax year under the post-reform tax-year basis. Explain that since basis period reform, profits are taxed on a tax-year basis (the share allocated for the tax year), anchored to §9. So your final assessment is your allocated profit share for the part of the final tax year up to your leaving date (plus any earlier untaxed period if your dates were unusual). Link D3 (`gp-partnership-basis-period-reform-explained`) for the basis period mechanics; do not re-teach reform here.
  - H3: Why there is usually no overlap relief left on a later cessation. This is the key technical point and the page's differentiator. Explain, anchored to §9 and verified at gov.uk "Work out your transition profit", that under the old rules a partner leaving used to deduct their accumulated overlap relief in the final year. Basis period reform changed this: in the 2023/24 transition year every continuing partner deducted all of their overlap relief against the transition part, and no new overlap relief can be created afterwards. The reasoned consequence (state it as the reasoned position, not a single quoted line): a partner who leaves in 2024/25 or later will typically have no overlap relief left to set against their final-year profit, because it was already given in 2023/24. So the old "overlap relief cushions your last year" planning point no longer applies for most retiring partners. Flag clearly: a partner who joined after the transition, or whose overlap was not fully relieved in 2023/24, is a fact-specific exception to confirm with an accountant.
  - H3: Transition profit and an early cessation (brief). Note, anchored to §9 / the gov.uk guidance, that where a partner is still spreading 2023/24 transition profit over the five-year window, ceasing before the spreading ends accelerates any untaxed transition profit into the year of cessation. Keep this to two or three sentences and link D3.
- **H2: Capital gains tax on a premises share**
  - H3: When CGT can arise. Explain, anchored to §4 and verified at CG27000+/CG27500, that a partnership is transparent for CGT (each partner owns a fractional share of the underlying chargeable assets), so when a partner leaves and gives up their entire interest in an asset such as the premises, that is a disposal of their share for CGT. Whether a gain arises depends on the consideration and whether the asset has been revalued; on a no-revaluation, no-consideration exit at balance-sheet value the result can be no gain/no loss, but a gain can arise where the premises has appreciated and consideration passes. State plainly that this is a specialist computation and the numbers are practice-specific; do not fabricate any figure.
  - H3: Statement of Practice D12. Note that HMRC's long-standing practice for partnership CGT is set out in Statement of Practice D12 (full text at CG27170), which governs how a partner's disposal of a fractional interest is computed. One sentence; link out for detail rather than reproducing SP D12.
  - H3: Business Asset Disposal Relief, only on a private disposal. One careful paragraph anchored to §4: BADR can apply to a partner's disposal of an interest in a trading partnership's chargeable assets, but the relief and its date-banded rate (10% to 5 Apr 2025; 14% 6 Apr 2025 to 5 Apr 2026; 18% from 6 Apr 2026; £1m lifetime limit; 2-year qualifying period) are the relevant frame only where there is an actual chargeable disposal of a private/trading interest. Make the medical-specific point: there is no NHS goodwill disposal to relieve. Date-tag the BADR rate exactly. Link the complete guide for the BADR detail.
- **H2: NHS pension on retirement (cross-reference, light)**
  - Keep this short and signpost only, anchored loosely to §2. State that retiring from the partnership and drawing the NHS pension are separate decisions on separate timetables, that the scheme has its own normal pension ages (60 for 1995, 65 for 2008, State Pension Age for 2015) and partial-retirement and early-retirement routes, and that drawing the pension does not depend on selling anything (there is nothing to sell). Do NOT lock pension figures or actuarial reduction percentages. Link `gp-pension-contributions-tax-relief` and `gp-financial-planning`; do not teach the pension mechanics here.
- **H2: The deed mechanics for an orderly exit**
  - Explain the practical points a well-drafted partnership deed should cover for a leaver: the notice period; how and over what period the capital account is repaid; how the final profit share to the leaving date is calculated and trued up; whether and how a premises share is bought out; release from practice borrowings, leases and capital guarantees; restrictive covenants; and the handling of any tax reserves. Anchor §1 (taxed on allocated profit share, not drawings) for the final-year truing up. State plainly that an exit handled without a clear deed is where disputes and unexpected tax arise. Do not turn this into legal advice; frame as the checklist of items to settle.
- **H2: A practical sequence for a planned retirement** (short)
  - A brief ordered list: agree the leaving date and notice; confirm the capital account and current account balances and the repayment basis; settle the premises share separately if you own one; calculate the final-year profit share and reserve for the tax on it; check whether any CGT arises on the premises and whether BADR is in point; confirm there is no overlap relief left to rely on (post-reform); and coordinate the NHS pension claim separately. Signpost the relevant linked pages rather than re-teaching.

## 5. Exact HP positions, dates and verified anchors

**HP positions to use:**
- A GP partner is **self-employed**; the partnership files an **SA800** and each partner's share flows to the **SA104** partnership pages; the partner is taxed on their **allocated profit share, not drawings**. (§1) Use for the final-year assessment and the deed truing-up point.
- On a partner's **exit**, what is returned is the **capital account** balance and any **owned premises** share, recorded through the capital accounts; **premises** are often held in a **separate property partnership or LLP**; valuations are property-specific (notional/cost rent context), so do NOT lock a figure; **last-man-standing** is the risk the remaining partners carry. (§4)
- **NHS GP goodwill cannot be sold** (prohibited since 1 April 2004; current instrument SI 2019/251); there is no NHS goodwill payment on exit; the only saleable goodwill is **private, non-NHS** goodwill. (§4)
- **Basis period reform**: profits are taxed on a **tax-year basis**; in the **2023/24 transition year** all overlap relief was given against the transition profit and **no new overlap relief can be created**, so on a later cessation there is usually **no overlap relief left**; ceasing before the five-year transition-profit spreading ends accelerates untaxed transition profit. (§9)
- **CGT**: a partnership is **transparent** for CGT; a leaving partner gives up their **entire fractional interest** in chargeable assets (such as premises), which is a **disposal**; governed by **SP D12**. (§4 plus the CG27000+ verification.)
- **BADR** date-banded rate: **10%** to 5 Apr 2025; **14%** 6 Apr 2025 to 5 Apr 2026; **18%** from 6 Apr 2026; **£1m** lifetime limit; **2-year** qualifying period; confined to an actual private/trading disposal, never NHS goodwill. (§4)

**DO NOT** invent capital-account figures, repayment periods, premises valuations, pension figures or actuarial reductions. Do NOT state that a retiring GP can sell goodwill. Do NOT assert that overlap relief is available on a later cessation as a general rule (post-reform it usually is not; flag the fact-specific exceptions). Do NOT re-teach basis period reform, last-man-standing, the NHS pension mechanics or the BADR detail; link out.
