## 3. Angle (2 to 3 sentences) + differentiation

**Angle.** QOF is the quality-and-incentive layer of GP funding, and it confuses partners because the cash arrives in two parts and across two financial years: monthly aspiration payments through the year, then an achievement reconciliation after year-end. This page explains the points-based mechanics (how points convert to money, how list size and prevalence adjust the value), then does the bit competitors skip: how QOF income should be recognised in the practice accounts (accruing the expected achievement, not just the cash received) and how it is taxed as ordinary practice trading profit. The aim is a partner who can read the QOF line in their accounts and knows why the cash and the profit do not match in any one year.

## 4. Section-by-section outline (target 2,800 to 3,500 words)

- **H1: QOF Income Explained: How the Quality and Outcomes Framework Pays Your Practice**
- **H2: What QOF is (and where it sits in GP funding)**
  - QOF is the **voluntary** annual quality-and-incentive scheme that rewards practices for measured quality of care. It sits alongside the core Global Sum (link A1), not inside it.
  - One paragraph framing: it is points-based, the practice scores points against clinical and other indicators, and points convert to money. It is income to the practice, ultimately part of partners' profit (link `gp-partnership-tax-complete-guide`).
- **H2: How QOF points work**
  - H3: Indicators and the points total. The scheme is a set of indicators, each worth points, summing to an annual maximum that is set nationally and changes year to year (state that the indicator set and the maximum are reviewed annually; do NOT lock the exact maximum as permanent, see section 5).
  - H3: The value of a point. Each point has a national base value that is uplifted annually and then adjusted at practice level. Do NOT lock the £-per-point as a permanent figure (section 5 VERIFY).
  - H3: The two practice adjustments. The point value is adjusted for (1) practice list size relative to the national average list, and (2) disease prevalence (the adjusted prevalence factor), so a larger or higher-prevalence practice earns more per point. Keep the mechanism qualitative; do not invent multipliers.
- **H2: Aspiration payments and achievement payments (the two-part cash flow)**
  - H3: Aspiration payments. Paid through the year as part-payment in advance of expected achievement (broadly based on a percentage of the prior year's achievement, or an agreed aspiration), paid monthly via the commissioner/PCSE route.
  - H3: Achievement payments. After year-end, actual achievement is measured; the practice is paid the balance (achievement value minus aspiration already paid). If achievement is below aspiration, there can be a clawback/adjustment.
  - H3: Why the cash crosses two years. Aspiration for the current year plus the achievement balance for the prior year both land in a given year, which is exactly why the cash received in a year is not the same as the QOF the practice actually earned for that year. This is the key accounting hook.
- **H2: How QOF income is recognised in the practice accounts**
  - H3: Accrual, not just cash. The correct treatment is to recognise the QOF the practice has earned for the accounting period (the expected achievement), with a debtor/accrual for the achievement balance not yet received and the aspiration already received offset against it. State this clearly as the reason QOF in the accounts differs from QOF in the bank.
  - H3: Estimating year-end achievement. Practices (with their accountant) estimate likely achievement to accrue the right figure; an over- or under-estimate is corrected the following year.
  - H3: Why it matters for partners. Drawings and profit-share decisions should track earned profit, not the cash timing; a partner leaving or joining mid-year is affected by how QOF is accrued (signpost the Wave 2 partnership-accounts work only by theme, do NOT link to it).
  - Signpost `gp-accounting-guide` and `gp-bookkeeping-guide-uk` for the broader accounts; this section is the QOF-specific treatment.
- **H2: How QOF income is taxed**
  - QOF is ordinary practice **trading income**: it is part of the partnership's taxable trading profit, allocated to partners and taxed on each partner's profit share with Class 4 NIC, via SA800 to SA104 (link `gp-partnership-tax-complete-guide`; anchor §1, §8).
  - State plainly: there is no special QOF tax regime; it is taxed when earned (accruals basis in the accounts), not when the cash arrives. Tag NIC/rate references to the current year and keep to the §8 figures by link, do not re-teach Class 4.
  - Pension note (one short paragraph): because QOF feeds NHS-derived profit, it forms part of pensionable profit for a Type 1 partner; income routed through a company is not pensionable (anchor §2.C; link `gp-pension-contributions-tax-relief`, `gp-corporation-tax`).
- **H2: Common QOF accounting and tax mistakes**
  - Treating QOF as cash-in (missing the accrual). Forgetting the prior-year achievement balance lands this year. Double-counting aspiration and achievement. Not reconciling the QOF actually paid against what was expected (signpost reconciliation by theme only, no link). Keep practical and neutral.

## 5. Exact HP positions, dates and verified anchors

**HP positions to use (anchor §3 unless noted):**
- QOF is **the Quality and Outcomes Framework, a voluntary, points-based quality scheme**, sitting alongside the Global Sum, enhanced services and PCN/Network Contract DES. (§3)
- QOF income is **practice trading income**, part of partnership profit, taxed on the partner's profit share. (§3, §1, §8)
- **No single fixed QOF point value; it is uplifted annually.** (§3, and the §3 VERIFY flag.)
