## 3. Angle (2 to 3 sentences) + differentiation

**Angle.** A GP partner draws a steady monthly amount but is taxed on something different: their full allocated profit share, whether or not they drew it as cash. This page is the practical, cash-flow-first treatment of that gap: why the two figures diverge, the timing mismatch it creates (drawings now, tax bill later), and a workable method for reserving for income tax, Class 4 National Insurance, payments on account and superannuation so the January and July bills never come as a shock. It is a "how to keep money back" page, not a tax-law explainer.

## 4. Section-by-section outline (target 2,800 to 3,500 words)

- **H1: Drawings Versus Profit for a GP Partner: Why They Differ and How to Reserve for Tax**
- **H2: Drawings and profit share are two different things**
  - Define drawings: the regular (usually monthly) cash a partner takes from the practice, set against an anticipated profit share and trued up at year-end. Define profit share: the partner's allocated slice of the practice's taxable profit under the deed. State the core §1 rule up front: **you are taxed on your profit share, not on your drawings** (link `gp-partnership-tax-complete-guide` and D1).
  - Make the mechanics concrete: the partnership files one SA800; your allocated share flows to the partnership pages (SA104) of your own return and is taxed as self-employed trading income (§1).
- **H2: Why the two figures diverge**
  - H3: Drawings are deliberately conservative. Practices usually set drawings below expected profit to protect working capital, so undrawn profit accumulates in the current account. You are still taxed on the full share.
  - H3: A strong (or weak) practice year. If profit comes in above the drawings rate, you owe tax on profit you have not taken as cash; if drawings ran ahead of profit, you may have to repay the excess. Link D1 for how the current account records this.
  - H3: Superannuation and other deductions taken at source. NHS pension (superannuation) contributions and sometimes tax are deducted from the practice before drawings, widening the gap between the headline profit and the cash a partner sees.
- **H2: The cash-flow gap and the Self Assessment timetable**
  - Walk the timeline: profit is earned across the year, the accounts are finalised after year-end, the tax return is filed, and the bill is due on **31 January following the tax year**, with a second **payment on account on 31 July**. Explain payments on account: two interim payments each broadly 50% of the prior year's liability, due 31 January and 31 July, where the prior bill exceeded £1,000 and less than 80% was collected at source (§8).
  - H3: The "double hit" first full year. A new partner's first January can carry the balancing payment for the year plus the first payment on account, so roughly 150% of a year's tax can fall due at once. This is the single biggest cash-flow shock in partnership; flag it prominently. Link `becoming-gp-partner-financial-implications`.
- **H2: What you are reserving for**
  - List the liabilities a partner must reserve against from their profit share, not just income tax:
    - **Income tax** at the partner's marginal rate (bands referenced lightly, link the complete guide; do not reproduce the full band table).
    - **Class 4 National Insurance**: 6% on profits between £12,570 and £50,270, then 2% above £50,270 (§8). Note **Class 2 is no longer a required payment from 6 April 2024** for profits at/above the Small Profits Threshold, so there is no weekly Class 2 to budget for (§8).
    - **Superannuation (NHS pension) contributions**: tiered by pensionable pay; explain that the correct tier must be paid and that, for a Type 1 partner, contributions are reconciled through the Annual Certificate of Pensionable Profits, so an under-deduction in-year can mean a balancing pension payment later (anchor §2.C, light; link `gp-pension-contributions-tax-relief`). **DO NOT state specific superannuation tier percentages or figures** (per the brief constraint and the §2.C / §3 hedge); describe tiers qualitatively and point to PCSE / the pension page for current rates.
    - **The annual allowance risk** for higher earners: a big profit year can trigger a pension annual allowance charge, settled via Scheme Pays, which is a further reserving consideration (one or two sentences; link `nhs-pension-annual-allowance-complete-guide`; do not re-teach, anchor §2.B).
- **H2: A practical method for reserving**
  - H3: Reserve a percentage of every drawing. The simplest discipline: set aside a sensible proportion of each drawing into a separate account so the tax and pension liabilities are funded as profit is taken. Frame the proportion as a planning decision modelled on the partner's marginal position, NOT a fixed recommended percentage (do not assert "set aside X%"; say it is modelled on projected profit, marginal rate and pension tier).
  - H3: Use a separate tax reserve account. Keep the reserve out of the current account/personal account so it is not accidentally spent; top it up monthly.
  - H3: Reconcile to the accounts and the tax computation. Once the accounts and computation are done, compare the reserve to the actual liability and adjust drawings or the reserve rate for the next year. Link D1 for reading the figures and `gp-tax-return` for filing.
  - H3: Build a working-capital buffer too. Partners also need a buffer for practice cash-flow dips (delayed reimbursements, locum costs), separate from the tax reserve.
- **H2: Worked illustration (clearly illustrative, no fabricated practice figures)**
  - A single round-number walk-through: allocated profit share for the year, drawings taken, the resulting undrawn profit, an illustrative income tax + Class 4 estimate, and the reserve needed, ending with the January/July split. Label every number "illustrative" and state the partner's own figures will differ. Do NOT present any percentage as a recommended reserve rate; show it as worked-through for that illustration only.
- **H2: Common drawings-and-reserving mistakes**
  - Bulleted, practical: treating drawings as the taxable figure and under-reserving; spending undrawn profit that is really earmarked for tax; forgetting the first-year payments-on-account double hit; ignoring the superannuation balancing payment; not adjusting drawings when profit or the tax bill changes; no separate reserve account. ## 5. Exact HP positions, dates and verified anchors

**HP positions to use:**
- **A GP partner is taxed on their profit share, not their drawings** (the central position of this page). (§1)
- Partnership files **SA800**; partner's share flows to **SA104**; taxed as self-employed trading income. (§1)
- **Class 4 NIC: 6%** on profits £12,570 to £50,270, **2%** above £50,270. (§8)
- **Class 2 is no longer a required payment from 6 April 2024** for profits at/above the Small Profits Threshold. (§8)
- **Payments on account**: two interim payments, 31 January and 31 July, each 50% of the prior year's liability, where the prior bill exceeded £1,000 and less than 80% was collected at source. (§8)
- Balancing payment / return due **31 January following the tax year** (e.g. 2026/27 due 31 January 2028). (§8, complete guide.)
- Superannuation: a Type 1 partner reconciles pension contributions via the **Annual Certificate of Pensionable Profits**; correct tier must be paid. (§2.C) **Do NOT state tier percentages.**
- Higher earners may face a pension **annual allowance** charge settled by **Scheme Pays** (light; anchor §2.B). Standard annual allowance **£60,000**, tapering where threshold income > £200,000 and adjusted income > £260,000, to a £10,000 floor (state only if the writer goes into the annual-allowance reserving point; tag 2025/26).
