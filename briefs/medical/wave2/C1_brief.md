## 3. Angle (2 to 3 sentences) + differentiation

**Angle.** A dispensing practice (a rural GP practice that supplies medicines directly to patients who live far from a pharmacy) has an income stream most GP practices never see, and it behaves very differently from core contract funding: it is largely a reimbursement of drug cost plus a fee, it turns the practice into a buyer and seller of stock, and the VAT treatment is genuinely unusual (it is the one part of a GP practice that produces zero-rated taxable supplies). This page explains how dispensing income is generated (drug reimbursement at Drug Tariff prices, dispensing fees, the buying margin), how it sits in the practice accounts (turnover and matching drug-purchase cost, stock, and the effect on profit and partners' profit share), and how it is taxed (trading income, and the specific VAT position where NHS dispensed drugs are zero-rated, personally administered drugs are exempt, and private-prescription drugs are standard-rated). It stays at the dispensing-income altitude and hands the wider VAT-registration mechanics to `gp-vat-registration`.

**How this differs from existing pages and the two sibling picks.**
- Differs from `gp-vat-registration`: that page is the general VAT-registration decision for a GP practice (the £90,000 threshold, exempt vs taxable supplies, partial exemption). ## 4. Section-by-section outline (target 2,800 to 3,500 words)

- **H1: Dispensing Practice Income: How It Works in the Accounts and How It Is Taxed**
- **H2: What a dispensing practice is, and who can dispense**
  - Define a dispensing practice: a GP practice with contractual rights to supply medicines directly to certain patients, so the patient leaves the surgery with the medicine itself rather than a prescription to take to a pharmacy.
  - The eligibility rule (verify at source): dispensing is generally allowed for patients in a "controlled area" who live more than one mile (1.6 km) from a pharmacy and would have serious difficulty getting medicines otherwise. Dispensing practices are therefore predominantly rural. Frame as the principle, not an exhaustive legal test (the Pharmaceutical and Local Pharmaceutical Services Regulations govern the detail).
  - State plainly that prescribing and dispensing are different acts: every GP prescribes; only a dispensing practice both prescribes and then dispenses (supplies) the medicine and is reimbursed for it.
- **H2: How dispensing income is actually generated**
  - Open with the shape: dispensing income is not one payment, it is several components that together cover the cost of the drug plus a payment for the work of dispensing. Take them one at a time.
  - H3: Drug reimbursement (the Drug Tariff). The practice is reimbursed for the cost of the medicines it dispenses, at prices set in the NHS Drug Tariff (published monthly by the NHSBSA). Explain that the Tariff is the national price list that determines reimbursement; do NOT quote individual drug prices (they change monthly). Note that claims are submitted using the GP prescriber number and processed via the NHSBSA/PCSE route.
  - H3: Dispensing fees. On top of reimbursement, the practice earns a dispensing fee per item dispensed, on a fee scale that is uplifted in line with the contract. Frame the fee as set on a national scale and uplifted annually; do NOT lock a per-item figure.
  - H3: The buying margin (and clawback). Because the practice buys drugs at trade prices but is reimbursed at Tariff prices, there can be a margin on the purchase. The contract is designed to deliver a planned level of margin nationally, and a margin survey and adjustment mechanism operates so that excess margin is recovered (a "clawback" or discount deduction). Frame neutrally: the headline reimbursement is not all profit, because purchase cost and the margin adjustment both bite.
  - H3: Dispensing quality and other components. Note that additional payments (for example a quality scheme for dispensing services) may apply; frame as additional components, uplifted/varied by the contract, confirm current-year detail at source.
  - Tie-back: every figure here (fee scale, margin) is set and uplifted in the contract and the Tariff, so the page must frame them as current-year and point the reader to the source, never assert a fixed number (§3 hedging rule).
- **H2: How dispensing income sits in the practice accounts**
  - The key accounting point: dispensing turns the practice into a business that buys and sells stock. Drug reimbursement is income (turnover); the cost of buying those drugs is a matching cost of sale; the difference (after the margin adjustment) is what actually contributes to profit. The big gross numbers can dwarf core GMS income without adding much to the bottom line, so reading gross dispensing turnover as profit is a classic error.
  - Drug stock: the practice holds drug stock, which must be counted and valued at the year-end (stock on hand is an asset; only drugs actually dispensed in the year are a cost of that year). A poor stock count distorts the dispensing profit.
  - Dispensary staff and overheads: the cost of running the dispensary (dispensary staff, software, storage) is a practice cost that should be set against dispensing income to see the true contribution.
  - The §1 rule plainly: whatever dispensing contributes to practice profit is trading income, and a partner is taxed on their share of practice profit, not on drawings (link `gp-partnership-tax-complete-guide`, `gp-partnership-profit-sharing-tax-planning`). Note that profit-sharing arrangements sometimes treat dispensing profit differently between partners (for example where only some partners carry the dispensing risk), which is a partnership-agreement point, not a tax rule.
  - Signpost `gp-accounting-guide` and `gp-bookkeeping-guide-uk` for the bookkeeping mechanics; do not re-teach them.
- **H2: How dispensing income is taxed**
  - Income tax: dispensing income is part of the practice's trading profit, taxed on the partners as part of their profit share through the partnership return (SA800) and their personal returns (SA104). No separate regime; it simply increases trading profit. Link `gp-partnership-tax-complete-guide`.
  - Capital allowances (light): dispensary fit-out and equipment (shelving, fridges, dispensing robots, software) can qualify for capital allowances / the Annual Investment Allowance; signpost `gp-tax-deductions-complete-list-2026` rather than teaching §7 here.
  - Pension (one short paragraph): dispensing income is part of NHS-derived profit and is pensionable for a partner via the Type 1 Annual Certificate of Pensionable Profits; a company cannot hold the GMS/PMS contract and company income is not pensionable. Anchor §2.C; link `gp-pension-contributions-tax-relief`.
- **H2: The VAT position on dispensed drugs (the part that is genuinely different)**
  - Open with the headline: a dispensing practice is unusual among GP practices because it makes zero-rated taxable supplies. That changes the VAT picture in two ways: it can take the practice over the registration threshold, and once registered it can recover input VAT.
  - H3: NHS prescription drugs the patient takes away are zero-rated. Where the practice dispenses drugs to a patient against an NHS prescription, for the patient's personal use, to take away, the NHS payment for those drugs is zero-rated (a taxable supply at 0%), not exempt. State the statutory basis: VATA 1994 Schedule 8 Group 12. This is the crucial distinction from ordinary medical care.
  - H3: Personally administered drugs are exempt. Drugs and items the doctor administers, injects or applies to the patient in the course of treatment (for example vaccines and injections given at the surgery) are part of the single supply of medical care and are exempt under VATA 1994 Schedule 9 Group 7, not zero-rated. (This is the §6 exempt line.)
  - H3: Drugs against a private prescription are standard-rated. Where the practice dispenses against a private prescription, VAT may be due at the standard rate. Flag this and cross-link C2 for the practice's other standard-rated private work.
  - H3: Why the zero-rate matters for registration and recovery. Zero-rated dispensing income is taxable turnover, so it counts towards the £90,000 VAT registration threshold (unlike exempt NHS care, which does not). Many dispensing practices are therefore VAT-registered. Once registered, the practice can recover input VAT on the drugs it buys and on dispensary costs (because they relate to zero-rated supplies), while its exempt clinical income brings partial exemption into play. State plainly that this is a real, recoverable benefit but that it makes the VAT return more complex (taxable, exempt and outside-scope income side by side). Hand the registration and partial-exemption mechanics to `gp-vat-registration`; do not re-teach them.
  - Anchor §6, extended at primary source (the zero-rate for dispensed drugs sits in Schedule 8, alongside the Schedule 9 exemption for care).
- **H2: Common mistakes and planning points (neutral, not salesy)**
  - Reading gross dispensing turnover as profit (the margin and drug cost are the point).
  - Weak year-end stock counts distorting profit.
  - Missing VAT registration when zero-rated dispensing income crosses the threshold, and so missing recoverable input VAT.
  - Treating personally administered drugs the same as dispensed take-away drugs (different VAT liability).
  - Not reconciling the dispensing income on the PCSE statement against what was actually dispensed (link C3).

## 5. Exact HP positions, dates and verified anchors

**HP positions to use:**
- Dispensing practices earn dispensing income on top of core contract funding (§3).
- There is no single national fixed figure; the Drug Tariff prices and the dispensing fee scale are uplifted annually, confirm the current figure at source (§3 VERIFY framing applied to the Tariff and fee scale).
- Partner income is profit share, not salary; a partner is taxed on profit share, not drawings (§3, §1).
- NHS-derived profit (including dispensing) is pensionable via the Type 1 certificate; a company cannot hold the contract and company income is not pensionable (§2.C).
- VAT: genuine medical care by a registered doctor is exempt under VATA 1994 Sch 9 Group 7; here extended at primary source: NHS dispensed take-away drugs are ZERO-RATED under Sch 8 Group 12, personally administered drugs are EXEMPT, private-prescription drugs are STANDARD-RATED; only taxable (zero-rated + standard-rated) turnover counts towards the £90,000 threshold; partial exemption applies to a practice with both exempt and taxable supplies (§6).

Do NOT state a single national per-item or per-patient dispensing value.

## 6. FAQ questions (writer drafts answers; target 12 to 14)

1. What is a dispensing practice?
2. Who is allowed to dispense, and which patients qualify? (Answer: controlled-area patients more than one mile from a pharmacy; principle not full legal test.)
3. How does a dispensing practice make money from dispensing? (Answer: drug reimbursement at Drug Tariff prices plus dispensing fees, less the cost of buying the drugs and the margin adjustment.)
4. What is the Drug Tariff? (Answer: the national reimbursement price list, published monthly by the NHSBSA; do not quote prices.)
5. Is dispensing income just profit? (Answer: no, the reimbursement covers drug cost; the contribution to profit is the fees plus the net margin.)
6. How does dispensing income show up in the practice accounts? (Answer: gross turnover with a matching drug-purchase cost and stock; only the net contributes to profit.)
7. Do I need to count drug stock at the year-end? (Answer: yes, it materially affects the dispensing profit.)
8. How is dispensing income taxed? (Answer: trading income, taxed on the partners' profit share; SA800/SA104.)
9. Is dispensing income NHS-pensionable? (Answer: yes, it is NHS-derived profit, pensionable via the Type 1 certificate.)
10. Is VAT charged on the drugs a dispensing practice dispenses? (Answer: NHS take-away drugs are zero-rated; personally administered drugs are exempt; private-prescription drugs are standard-rated.)
11. Does dispensing income count towards the VAT registration threshold? (Answer: yes, zero-rated dispensing income is taxable turnover and counts towards £90,000; exempt clinical income does not.)
12. Can a dispensing practice recover VAT on the drugs it buys? (Answer: yes, once registered, because the drugs relate to zero-rated supplies; partial exemption applies to the exempt clinical side. Link gp-vat-registration.)
13. What is the difference between a personally administered drug and a dispensed drug for VAT? (Answer: administered = exempt medical care; dispensed take-away = zero-rated.)
14. How do I check the dispensing income I have actually been paid? (Answer: reconcile the dispensing line on the PCSE statement; link C3.)
