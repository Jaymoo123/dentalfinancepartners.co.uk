## 3. Angle (2 to 3 sentences) + differentiation

**Angle.** When a GP partnership owns or builds its own surgery, the NHS does not pay nothing towards the building, it reimburses the premises cost through one of three routes: notional rent for owner-occupiers (a current-market-rent figure assessed by the District Valuer), the older cost rent scheme (based on the actual borrowing and build cost, now closed to new schemes but still running for some practices), or improvement grants. This page explains exactly how each route works, who sets the figure, and the tax point that practices most often get wrong: that the reimbursement is taxable income to whoever owns the premises, set against the loan interest, repairs and other premises costs they incur. It is written for the partners who actually receive that money and report it.

## 4. Section-by-section outline (target 2,800 to 3,500 words)

- **H1: Notional Rent vs Cost Rent: How NHS Funds (and Taxes) Your GP Surgery Premises**
- **H2: Why GP premises funding is its own subject**
  - Most NHS work is funded through the Global Sum and related streams (cross-ref §3 territory, but keep light). Premises are funded separately, through the Premises Costs Directions. Set the scene: an owner-occupier partnership has a real building, a real loan, and a separate NHS income stream that reimburses the cost of providing those premises.
  - State up front this is a specialist area (the figures are property-specific and assessed case by case); frame the page as the framework plus the tax treatment, not a promise of a specific rent figure.
- **H2: The legal framework: the NHS Premises Costs Directions 2024**
  - The current instrument is the National Health Service (General Medical Services - Premises Costs) Directions 2024, in force from 10 May 2024, which replaced the 2013 Directions (revoked 9 May 2024). Earlier framework was the 2004 and then 2013 Directions.
  - The Directions set out the reimbursement routes: notional rent (owner-occupiers), the legacy cost rent scheme, leasehold rent reimbursement (covered in B3), and improvement grants.
- **H2: Notional rent (the owner-occupier route)**
  - Definition: where the partnership (or its property partnership) owns the surgery, the NHS pays a notional rent, a current-market-rent (CMR) assessment of what the premises would let for, as if the practice were a tenant.
  - H3: Who sets it. The District Valuer (the VOA District Valuer Services), or an appointed/independent valuer acting for the commissioner (ICB), assesses the figure. Note the 2024 Directions widened who can act ("appointed valuer"). Do NOT state a specific rent figure (property-specific, DV-assessed).
  - H3: The assessment basis. Assessed on notional lease terms (commonly a 15-year term, tenant internal-repairing, landlord responsible for external/structural and insurance). It is the rent a tenant "might reasonably be expected to pay" at the valuation date. Reviewed periodically (typically every three years).
  - H3: Owner-occupier choice. Under the 2024 Directions an owner-occupier chooses either borrowing-cost reimbursement OR notional rent, not both (Direction 42 framework).
  - H3: Abatement. Notional rent is abated where a grant or public capital funded the development or improvement (so the NHS does not reimburse the same cost twice).
- **H2: Cost rent (the legacy borrowing-based scheme)**
  - Definition: the older scheme, where reimbursement is based on the actual cost of acquiring the site and building or improving the premises (the borrowing/build cost), rather than open-market rental value.
  - H3: Closed to new schemes. State plainly: cost rent is no longer available for new schemes; it survives only for some existing practice premises that were set up under it. Frame as legacy.
  - H3: How it behaves. Cost rent tends to sit above notional rent and is generally not subject to the same periodic review; there is a requirement to convert to notional rent once the underlying mortgage is repaid. Practices on cost rent often review whether converting to notional rent is now better.
- **H2: Improvement grants**
  - Definition: capital grants from the commissioner towards developing or improving premises. The 2024 Directions expanded grants to up to 100% of project value (previously capped lower), and broadened the eligible purposes (including fit-out works on new builds and land for extensions).
  - H3: The abatement / clawback period. Grants come with rent-abatement periods scaled to grant size (illustratively: up to roughly six years for smaller grants, rising to around 18 years for the largest, per the 2024 Directions thresholds). State these as the framework, flag that the exact bands are in the Directions, and frame the grant as carrying ongoing conditions, not free money.
  - H3: Negative-equity protection (signpost to B2). The Directions protect an owner-occupier from being required to repay more than the actual (or best reasonably obtainable) sale price, so a grant clawback cannot itself force negative equity. Mention briefly and link B2.
- **H2: How each route is taxed (the part most guides skip)**
  - Core position: notional rent and cost rent are TAXABLE INCOME of whoever owns the premises (the property-owning partners, or the separate property partnership/LLP). They are received because the owner provides premises, so they are property/trading income in the owners' hands.
  - H3: Set against premises costs. Against that reimbursement the owners set the deductible costs of holding the premises: loan/mortgage INTEREST (interest, not capital repayment), repairs and maintenance, buildings insurance, and other running costs. The net is what is taxed. (Cross-ref §8 on wholly-and-exclusively and business-loan interest.)
  - H3: Where the income sits. If premises are held inside the medical partnership, the reimbursement flows through the partnership accounts to the partners' profit shares (taxed as self-employment, §1). If held in a SEPARATE property partnership or LLP (common, and covered structurally in B3), it is taxed in that vehicle and allocated to its members. Flag that the structure changes where the income and the interest deduction land, and link B3.
  - H3: Capital repayments are not deductible. Make the standard point: only the interest element of loan repayments is allowable, not the capital. A grant is a capital receipt against the asset, not taxable rent.
  - H3: Improvement grant treatment. Treat as a capital contribution toward the asset (reducing the base cost / interacting with capital allowances and future CGT, see B3), not as taxable rental income. Hedge and signpost B3 for the disposal-side effect.
- **H2: A worked illustration (anonymised, no client names or invented figures)**
  - Illustrative owner-occupier partnership that holds its surgery in a property partnership, receives notional rent assessed by the District Valuer, and has a development loan. Walk the flow: notional rent received, minus loan interest, repairs and insurance, equals the net taxable amount allocated to the property partners. Keep all numbers illustrative and clearly labelled as such; do NOT assert a specific notional-rent figure.
- **H2: Notional rent vs cost rent: which leaves a practice better off**
  - General, non-advisory: cost rent can be higher but is fixed and converts on mortgage redemption; notional rent tracks market value and is reviewed. The right route depends on the loan position, the building and local market rents. Frame as a District-Valuer-and-adviser question, not a universal answer.
- **H2: Common mistakes practices make**
  - Forgetting the reimbursement is taxable; deducting capital repayments instead of just interest; not abating where a grant was taken; staying on cost rent past mortgage redemption without reviewing; not aligning who owns the premises with who can use the interest deduction (link B3).
