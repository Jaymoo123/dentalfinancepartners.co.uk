## 3. Angle (2 to 3 sentences) + differentiation

**Angle.** When partners leave a GP partnership faster than new ones join, liability for the surgery does not shrink to match: a leasehold practice can leave the remaining partners (in the worst case, one partner) holding the whole lease, and an owner-occupier practice can leave departing partners unable to find a buyer for their premises share. This is the "last man standing" problem, and it is the single biggest reason incoming partners now hesitate to buy in to premises. This page explains how the risk arises, why it has worsened, the protections that have been built in (the NHS/BMA standard lease break right, the negative-equity protection in the Premises Costs Directions), and how the partnership deed and the ownership structure are used to manage it. It is written for partners weighing a buy-in and for practices trying to make their premises liability survivable.

## 4. Section-by-section outline (target 2,800 to 3,500 words)

- **H1: The "Last Man Standing" Problem: GP Partnership Premises Liability and How to Manage It**
- **H2: What "last man standing" actually means**
  - Plain definition: when partners leave and are not replaced, the liabilities attached to the surgery (a lease, or an owned building and its loan) do not disappear; they concentrate on the remaining partner(s), potentially leaving one partner exposed to the whole liability.
  - Two flavours: the LEASEHOLD version (one partner left on the hook for the full lease and dilapidations) and the OWNER-OCCUPIER version (a departing partner cannot sell their share of the building; or surplus/over-valued premises sit on the books).
  - Set scene: this is why "premises risk" now sits near the top of any buy-in conversation.
- **H2: Why the risk has grown**
  - Partnership numbers under pressure (partners leaving for salaried/locum roles, fewer partners willing to take on liability), longer leases and historic over-development of premises, and the difficulty of recruiting incoming partners willing to buy in. Keep factual and non-alarmist; no fabricated statistics.
- **H2: The leasehold version of the risk**
  - Joint and several liability: partners are usually jointly and severally liable on the lease, so the landlord can pursue any one partner for the whole rent and for dilapidations at the end.
  - H3: The "last man standing" exposure. If partners leave without the lease being reassigned, the remaining partner can face the full ongoing rent and repairing obligations on a building far larger than one GP can use.
  - H3: The NHS/BMA standard lease and the break right. The NHS Property Services / BMA standard lease for health-centre occupiers includes a tenant break right tied to the core contract: the tenant can break the lease if the core NHS contract is terminated or not renewed, which is more favourable than a typical commercial lease. Explain this as a genuine protection but not a complete fix.
  - H3: Reverse / surplus premises and dilapidations. Flag end-of-lease dilapidations liability and the risk of being tied to space the practice no longer needs.
- **H2: The owner-occupier version of the risk**
  - Departing partners want their capital out (their share of the building), but incoming partners increasingly will not buy in, so there is no buyer for the outgoing share. The remaining partners may have to buy it, or the share sits unsold.
  - H3: Negative equity and surplus premises. Where a building is worth less than the loan, or is over-specified for current needs, departing partners can be reluctant to release and incoming partners reluctant to take on. State the protection: under the Premises Costs Directions, an owner-occupier cannot be required to repay more than the actual sale price (or best price reasonably obtainable on the open market), which is designed to stop a grant clawback from creating negative equity. Frame as mitigation, not elimination.
  - H3: Why incoming partners resist buying in. They see the liability, the illiquidity of the asset, and the recruitment trend, and choose salaried or non-property-owning partner routes instead. This is the core dynamic.
- **H2: The two-tier partnership: property-owning vs non-property-owning partners**
  - A common response: hold the premises in a SEPARATE property partnership or LLP, so that only some partners (the property owners) carry the premises, while clinical partners can join the medical partnership without buying in to the building. Explain the structure at a high level and link B3 for the SDLT/CGT/capital-allowance detail.
  - H3: Pros. New partners can join without a premises buy-in; property risk is ring-fenced from the clinical partnership; clearer separation of the rent stream.
  - H3: Cons / cautions. The property partners still carry the liability; the structure must be documented carefully; it does not by itself solve the last-man-standing problem if all property partners want out. Hedge.
- **H2: How the partnership deed manages the risk**
  - The deed is the primary control. Cover the levers, framed as "ensure your deed addresses these", not legal drafting advice:
  - H3: Limiting simultaneous exits. A well-drafted deed can cap how many partners may retire in a single accounting period, preventing a domino collapse of partner numbers.
  - H3: Notice periods and buy-out terms. Long notice periods, clear premises buy-out valuation and timing, and what happens to an outgoing partner's premises share.
  - H3: Compulsory provisions and continuity. Provisions that keep the partnership going and avoid an unintended dissolution (which can crystallise liabilities). Note that a partnership at will (no deed, or a lapsed deed) is especially dangerous here.
  - H3: Aligning the deed with the lease and the property structure. The deed, the lease and any property-partnership agreement must be consistent.
- **H2: What an incoming partner should check before buying in**
  - The lease term and break rights, the dilapidations exposure, the premises valuation and how the buy-in/buy-out is priced, whether premises are in a separate property partnership, and the deed's exit and retirement-limit provisions. A practical due-diligence checklist (no fabricated figures).
- **H2: What a "last man standing" partner can actually do**
  - Practical options if you are the remaining partner: talk to the ICB early (they may know practices looking to expand or take over), explore lease assignment to another practice, surrender (if the landlord agrees), or, for grant-funded premises, the routes in the Directions (assign to an NHS nominee, or apply for waiver of residual grant repayment). Frame as options to take with professional advice, not guarantees.
- **H2: The tax and accounts angle (kept proportionate, links out)**
  - Briefly: how the premises sit in the accounts (capital accounts, the property partnership), the loan-interest deduction, and the CGT position on an eventual disposal all interact with these decisions. Do NOT re-teach; link B1 (rent/tax) and B3 (structure/SDLT/CGT).
- **H2: Common misconceptions**
  - "The NHS will always bail out the lease" (no, the break right is contract-linked, not automatic); "buying in to premises is always a bad idea" (no, owner-occupiers benefit from notional rent and asset growth, link B1/B3); "a handshake partnership is fine" (a partnership without a current deed is the most exposed).

## 5. Exact HP figures, dates and statutory anchors to cite (verified 2026-06-03)

Frame these (premises is a hedged area, follow the HP):
- The "last man standing" risk = a single remaining partner left holding the whole premises liability and lease (HP §4). State it as a real planning point, not a certainty for any given practice.
