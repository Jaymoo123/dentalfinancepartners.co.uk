## 3. Angle (2 to 3 sentences) + differentiation

**Angle.** A GP partnership choosing between renting its surgery from a third party and owning it (usually through a separate property partnership or LLP) is making a tax decision as much as a clinical one: ownership unlocks notional rent, asset growth and capital allowances, but brings SDLT on purchase, illiquidity and a CGT bill on the way out. This page walks the whole own-vs-rent tax picture for a GP surgery, from the SDLT on a freehold purchase, to the capital allowances on the integral features (and the CAA 2001 fixtures election that is so often missed and lost), to CGT and Business Asset Disposal Relief on an eventual disposal. It is the structural and lifetime-tax companion to the rent-scheme and liability pages, written for partners actually deciding whether to buy.

## 4. Section-by-section outline (target 2,800 to 3,500 words)

- **H1: Owning vs Renting Your GP Surgery: The Property Partnership, SDLT and the Tax Trade-Offs**
- **H2: The decision in one paragraph**
  - Rent from a third party (or NHS Property Services / a developer): no capital outlay, no asset risk, but no asset and no notional rent in your own pocket. Own (usually via a property partnership/LLP): notional rent reimburses you, you build an asset, you get capital allowances, but you take on SDLT, a loan, illiquidity and a future CGT bill. State up front this is specialist and practice-specific (HP hedge).
- **H2: The two ways to "rent"**
  - Third-party commercial lease (private landlord or developer): the practice is a tenant; the NHS reimburses rent through the leasehold reimbursement route (link B1). No ownership, no asset growth, but flexibility and no last-man-standing-style ownership exposure (link B2).
  - NHS Property Services / NHS-owned estate: the practice occupies NHS-owned premises. Keep brief and factual.
- **H2: Owning the surgery: the property partnership or LLP**
  - The common structure: premises held in a SEPARATE property partnership or LLP outside the medical partnership, so property ownership and clinical partnership are not locked together (link B2 for why this matters for risk).
  - H3: Who owns and who occupies. The property vehicle owns; the medical partnership occupies and the NHS notional rent flows to the owners (link B1 for the rent and its tax).
  - H3: Why separate the vehicle. Ring-fences premises risk, lets clinical partners join without a premises buy-in, and cleanly separates the rent stream and its interest deduction (link B2).
  - H3: Property partnership vs LLP. High-level: a general property partnership vs an LLP (limited liability, separate legal personality, members taxed personally). Note SDLT and CGT consequences differ on transfers in/out; flag this as needing specific advice. Do NOT over-promise; hedge.
- **H2: SDLT on buying the surgery (England and Northern Ireland)**
  - Buying the freehold is a non-residential / commercial purchase. State the rates: 0% up to £150,000, 2% on £150,001 to £250,000, 5% above £250,000 (slice/banded). Worked illustration on a realistic but clearly illustrative surgery price.
  - H3: SDLT on a new lease. For a new non-residential lease there is also SDLT on the net present value (NPV) of the rent: nil up to £150,000 NPV, 1% on the slice from £150,001 to £5m, 2% above £5m, separate from any premium.
  - H3: Devolved note. These are the England and Northern Ireland figures (SDLT). Scotland uses LBTT and Wales uses LTT, which differ; flag that a surgery in Scotland or Wales follows the devolved tax (do NOT state SDLT figures for those). (Consistent with the HP convention of tagging devolved scope.)
  - H3: Partnership SDLT rules. Transfers of property into/out of a partnership have special SDLT rules (sum of lower proportions etc.); flag as a specialist area and signpost professional advice. Hedge, do not attempt the calculation.
- **H2: Capital allowances on the building's fixtures**
  - You cannot claim allowances on the bricks-and-mortar structure, but you CAN claim on the integral features and fixtures within the surgery: electrical systems, heating, ventilation and air-conditioning, cold/hot water systems, lifts, and the like (special-rate pool, 6% WDA), plus general plant within a fit-out (main-rate pool).
  - H3: AIA and the rates. AIA £1,000,000 a year gives 100% relief on qualifying plant and machinery; direct it at the special-rate (6%) integral features first. Main-rate WDA is 18% reducing to 14% from 1 April 2026 (CT) / 6 April 2026 (IT) per FA 2026 s.28; special rate stays 6%. (Cross-ref §7, link a capital-allowances page if helpful.)
  - H3: The fixtures election on a purchase (the big one). When you BUY a surgery that already contains fixtures, a CAA 2001 s.198 election fixes, jointly with the seller, the value attributed to those fixtures. Without it, the buyer can lose the fixtures allowances entirely. There is a strict 2-year time limit (CAA 2001 s.201): the election must be made by notice to HMRC no later than 2 years after the buyer acquires the interest. State this as a common, preventable loss and a reason to involve an accountant before completion.
  - H3: New vs existing build. On a new build or fit-out, the 40% first-year allowance on new (unused, not second-hand) main-rate plant from 1 January 2026 (FA 2026 s.29) and AIA both apply; on a second-hand purchase the s.198 election route is the lever. Keep aligned with §7.
- **H2: How notional rent makes owning pay (link, do not re-teach)**
  - Brief: owner-occupiers receive notional rent (District-Valuer-assessed current market rent), taxable but set against loan interest and premises costs. This is the income that supports the loan and rewards ownership. Full detail in B1; link.
- **H2: CGT and Business Asset Disposal Relief when you eventually sell**
  - When the premises (or a partner's share) is sold, CGT applies to the gain. For a building used in the partnership trade, the gain can qualify for relief, but the rules are specific.
  - H3: BADR. Business Asset Disposal Relief can apply on a qualifying disposal of (a share in) the business; the rate is 10% to 5 April 2025, 14% from 6 April 2025 to 5 April 2026, and 18% from 6 April 2026, with a £1m lifetime limit and a 2-year qualifying period. Flag that BADR on a premises held in a property vehicle, vs an "associated disposal" alongside a withdrawal from the partnership, has specific conditions; hedge and signpost advice. Date-tag the rate every time.
  - H3: Associated disposals and personally-held premises. Where premises are held personally or in the property partnership but used by the trading partnership, an associated-disposal claim may be possible but is restricted (especially where rent was charged). Flag as a specialist point, do not over-claim.
  - H3: Timing. The 6 April 2026 step to 18% BADR makes disposal timing relevant; the date of disposal is the contract date if unconditional (cross-ref §4 territory). Keep light and link C2 territory conceptually only via the category, not a forbidden cross-cluster link (do NOT link Cluster C slugs).
- **H2: Own vs rent: a side-by-side**
  - Table-style comparison (in prose or a simple list): capital outlay, SDLT, notional rent vs reimbursed lease rent, capital allowances, asset growth, illiquidity/exit, CGT on disposal, last-man-standing exposure. Balanced, non-advisory; the answer is practice-specific.
- **H2: Common mistakes**
  - Missing the s.198 fixtures election on a purchase (and the 2-year deadline); assuming SDLT figures apply in Scotland/Wales; ignoring partnership SDLT rules on transfers; not separating the property vehicle; and assuming BADR is automatic on a premises disposal.

## 5. Exact HP figures, dates and statutory anchors to cite (verified 2026-06-03)

Quote/frame these exactly (hedge the premises-specific points per HP §4):
- Premises often held in a **separate property partnership or LLP** (HP §4).
- **SDLT non-residential freehold:** 0% up to **£150,000**, 2% on **£150,001 to £250,000**, 5% above **£250,000** (England and NI; Scotland LBTT / Wales LTT differ).
- **SDLT on new non-residential lease (NPV of rent):** nil up to **£150,000**, 1% on **£150,001 to £5,000,000**, 2% above **£5,000,000**.
- **Capital allowances (HP §7):** AIA **£1,000,000**; integral features special-rate **6%** WDA; main-rate WDA **18% reducing to 14%** from 1 Apr 2026 (CT) / 6 Apr 2026 (IT) per **FA 2026 s.28**; **40% FYA** on new main-rate plant from 1 Jan 2026 (**FA 2026 s.29**).
- **CAA 2001 s.198 fixtures election** on a premises purchase, **2-year time limit** under **s.201** (election by notice to HMRC no later than 2 years after the buyer acquires the qualifying interest).
- **BADR (HP §4):** **10%** to 5 Apr 2025; **14%** 6 Apr 2025 to 5 Apr 2026; **18%** from 6 Apr 2026; **£1m lifetime limit**; **2-year** qualifying period. Date-tag every time. Hedge associated-disposal / property-vehicle conditions.
