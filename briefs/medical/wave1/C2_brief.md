## 3. Angle (2 to 3 sentences) + differentiation

**Angle.** Where a doctor runs a genuinely private business (a self-pay clinic, an aesthetics arm, a medico-legal book, a private consultant company) and comes to sell it, this is real saleable value and the capital gains tax rules apply in full, unlike the NHS practice where goodwill cannot be sold at all. This page walks through the CGT computation on a private-practice disposal, how Business Asset Disposal Relief (BADR) cuts the rate, the conditions and limits that have to be met, and why the BADR rate stepping from 14% to 18% on 6 April 2026 turns disposal timing into a planning decision. It states clearly that NHS goodwill is outside scope and links to the page that explains why.

## 4. Section-by-section outline (target 2,800 to 3,500 words)

- **H1: Selling a Private Medical Practice: CGT, Business Asset Disposal Relief and the 2026 Rate Rise**
- **H2: First, what can actually be sold (and what cannot)**
  - Open by drawing the line: NHS GP goodwill cannot be sold (prohibited since 1 April 2004, SI 2019/251), so this page is about genuinely PRIVATE medical businesses. Link C1 for the NHS position in the first 120 words so the reader self-selects.
  - Examples of saleable private medical businesses: a self-pay or cosmetic clinic, an aesthetics company, a private consultant limited company, an occupational-health business, a medico-legal book of work run as a distinct business.
  - Two disposal shapes: selling the assets and goodwill of an unincorporated private practice (asset sale), or selling the shares in a private company (share sale). The CGT and BADR conditions differ slightly; cover both.
- **H2: How capital gains tax works on the sale**
  - Core mechanic: CGT is charged on the gain (proceeds less base cost less allowable costs of acquisition and disposal), not on the whole price.
  - Annual exempt amount applies first (state it is modest now and date-tag rather than over-anchoring; signpost gov.uk for the current figure as it has been reduced).
  - Standard CGT rates without relief (state the main-rate position for the relevant year, date-tagged) versus the BADR rate. Make the saving from BADR concrete.
  - Asset sale vs share sale: on an asset sale the individual disposes of goodwill and chargeable assets; on a share sale the individual disposes of the shares. Note that a company selling its own assets gets no BADR (BADR is a personal relief on the individual's disposal).
- **H2: Business Asset Disposal Relief: the headline rate cut**
  - What BADR does: charges qualifying gains at a reduced CGT rate instead of the main rate.
  - H3: The rate bands (state every time with dates). **10%** on disposals to 5 April 2025; **14%** on disposals 6 April 2025 to 5 April 2026; **18%** on disposals from 6 April 2026. This is a rising rate; the relief is getting less generous.
  - H3: The £1 million lifetime limit. BADR is capped at **£1,000,000 of qualifying gains per individual over their lifetime** (not per disposal). Gains above the limit fall to the normal CGT rate. Note prior disposals use up the limit.
  - H3: The 2-year qualifying period. The qualifying conditions must be met throughout the **2 years ending on the date of disposal** (the business must have been owned, or the shares/role held, for at least 2 years).
- **H2: Do you actually qualify? The conditions**
  - H3: For an unincorporated private practice (asset/business disposal). It must be a disposal of all or part of a trading business you have owned for at least 2 years, or of assets used in the business at cessation (with the associated time limits). Investment activity does not qualify.
  - H3: For a share sale of a private company. For at least 2 years to the date of disposal you must hold at least **5% of the ordinary share capital AND 5% of the voting rights**, be entitled to at least 5% of the economic interest (distributable profits and assets on a winding up), be an **officer or employee** of the company (or a group company), and the company must be a **trading company** (or holding company of a trading group). State the 5% + 5% + economic + officer/employee + trading test together.
  - H3: The trading requirement and medical companies. A consultant's private company is normally trading; flag that a company holding large cash reserves or investments can jeopardise trading status. Keep practical, not alarmist.
- **H2: The 6 April 2026 rate step as a timing lever**
  - The rate moves from 14% to 18% on 6 April 2026, so on a £1m gain the BADR cost rises from £140,000 to £180,000, a £40,000 difference purely on timing. Use that arithmetic (illustrative, clearly labelled).
  - H3: When is the disposal treated as made? CGT timing follows the contract, not completion. Under **TCGA 1992 s.28(1)**, an unconditional contract is treated as made (the date of disposal) when the contract is entered into, not when the asset is conveyed or money changes hands. So a binding unconditional contract signed on or before 5 April 2026 falls in the 14% band even if completion is later.
  - H3: Conditional contracts. Under **s.28(2)**, where the contract is conditional (including on a third party's consent or an option), the disposal date is when the condition is satisfied. So a contract conditional on a consent that is only met after 5 April 2026 is dated in the 18% band. The conditional-vs-unconditional distinction is the technical heart of the timing point.
  - H3: Do not let the tail wag the dog. Timing a sale to beat the rate step only makes sense if the deal is genuinely ready; an artificially rushed or contrived contract carries its own risks. Frame as a planning point to raise early with an adviser, not a trick.

Then the same disposal as a share sale meeting the 5% + officer/employee + 2-year tests. Label every number as illustrative; no fabricated client names or real figures.
- **H2: What is NOT in scope (and where it goes instead)**
  - NHS GP goodwill: cannot be sold, link C1.
  - Deferring instead of selling: if you are incorporating rather than selling out, s.162 incorporation relief defers the gain into the shares, link C3.
  - The income-tax treatment of private income while you run the business: link `private-practice-tax-nhs-and-private-income`.
  - Pension note: private income (and any company dividends) is not NHS-pensionable (§2.C); a sale is a capital event and does not change that, but it is worth the one-line reminder for consultants weighing the private route.
- **H2: Practical steps before you sell**
  - Check the 2-year clock and the 5% / officer-or-employee tests early. Confirm trading status. Keep records of base cost and improvement spend. Take advice on contract structure (conditional vs unconditional) with the 6 April 2026 step in mind. Consider how the lifetime £1m limit has already been used.

## 5. Exact HP figures, dates and statutory anchors to cite (verified 2026-06-03)

State these exactly:
- **BADR rate bands:** **10%** to 5 Apr 2025; **14%** 6 Apr 2025 to 5 Apr 2026; **18%** from 6 Apr 2026. (HP §4 / §2.B verification log.)
- **BADR lifetime limit:** **£1,000,000** of qualifying gains per individual.
- **Qualifying period:** conditions met throughout the **2 years to the date of disposal**.
- **Share-sale conditions:** **5% ordinary share capital + 5% voting rights**, 5% economic entitlement, **officer or employee**, **trading** company/group, for the 2-year period.
- **Disposal timing:** **TCGA 1992 s.28(1)** unconditional contract dated when made; **s.28(2)** conditional contract dated when the condition is satisfied. This is the lever for the 6 Apr 2026 step.
- **Out of scope:** NHS GP goodwill cannot be sold (SI 2019/251), so BADR never applies to it (HP §4); private income / company dividends are not NHS-pensionable (§2.C).
