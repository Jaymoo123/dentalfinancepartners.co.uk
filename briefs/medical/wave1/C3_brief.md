## 3. Angle (2 to 3 sentences) + differentiation

**Angle.** When a consultant or GP moves a genuinely private practice into a limited company, transferring the business (including its goodwill) is a disposal that can trigger capital gains tax, and TCGA 1992 section 162 incorporation relief is the mechanism that defers that gain by rolling it into the base cost of the new shares. This page explains how the deferral works, the going-concern and wholly-or-partly-for-shares conditions, and the Finance Act 2026 change that, from 6 April 2026, makes the relief something you must actively CLAIM (with a deadline) rather than something that applies automatically, and that removes the old election to disapply it. It then pairs the CGT saving with the medical reality that company income is not NHS-pensionable, so deferring tax on incorporation is never the whole decision.

## 4. Section-by-section outline (target 2,800 to 3,500 words)

- **H1: Incorporation Relief (Section 162) for a Private Medical Practice: Deferring CGT**
- **H2: When this applies (private practice only)**
  - Frame the scope in the first 120 words: incorporation is a private-work decision. A limited company CANNOT hold an NHS GMS/PMS contract and company income is NOT NHS-pensionable (§2.C). So s.162 incorporation relief is relevant to a consultant's or GP's PRIVATE practice (self-pay clinics, aesthetics, medico-legal, occupational health, private consultant work), not the NHS practice. Link C1 for the NHS position.
  - The CGT problem incorporation creates: transferring a business with goodwill to a company is a disposal of that goodwill at market value (connected-party transfer), which can crystallise a gain even though no cash leaves your pocket. s.162 is the relief that defers it.
- **H2: How section 162 incorporation relief works**
  - The core mechanic: when you transfer the business as a going concern to a company wholly or partly in exchange for shares, the gain on the transferred assets (mainly goodwill) is not taxed now; instead it is rolled over and DEDUCTED from the base cost of the new shares.
  - H3: The deferral, not exemption. The gain is deferred, not wiped out. When you later sell the shares, the reduced base cost means a larger gain then (unless further relief applies). Make this explicit: it is a timing benefit.
  - H3: The wholly-or-partly-for-shares apportionment. If consideration is wholly shares, the whole gain can be deferred. If consideration is partly shares and partly something else (cash, a loan account), only the share-proportion of the gain is deferred; the rest is chargeable now. Explain the A/B fraction in plain terms (the part of the gain deferred is broadly the proportion that the value of the shares received bears to the total consideration).
  - H3: Interaction with the director's loan account. Note that taking part of the consideration as a credit to a loan account (to draw down tax-free later) reduces the share consideration and so reduces the deferred amount; this is a common structuring trade-off. Keep it factual, signpost the step-by-step / pros-cons pages for the extraction mechanics.
- **H2: The conditions you have to meet**
  - H3: A business transferred as a going concern. It must be a genuine business (not merely the holding of investments) transferred as a going concern.
  - H3: All the assets of the business (cash may be excluded). Either all the business assets, or all except cash, must be transferred to the company.
  - H3: Consideration wholly or partly in shares. The transfer must be wholly or partly in exchange for shares issued by the company to the transferor.
  - H3: Goodwill valuation matters. The transferred private goodwill must be valued properly (HMRC can challenge); a robust valuation underpins both the deferred gain and any later base cost. Frame as needing specialist input, do not invent a multiple.
- **H2: The Finance Act 2026 change (this is the new bit)**
  - State the headline: for transfers of a business made **on or after 6 April 2026**, FA 2026 s.39 changes how s.162 works.
  - H3: It is no longer automatic, you must CLAIM it. Before 6 April 2026, s.162 applied automatically where the conditions were met (you had to elect OUT if you did not want it). From 6 April 2026, the relief only applies if the person makes a claim in respect of the transfer.
  - H3: The claim deadline. The claim must be made on or before the **first anniversary of the 31 January following the tax year in which the transfer took place**. Give a worked date: a transfer in 2026/27 must be claimed by 31 January 2029. Missing the deadline means the gain is chargeable now with no deferral. This is the practical trap to flag.
  - H3: The old s.162A election is gone. FA 2026 s.39 OMITS s.162A (the election to disapply s.162). Under the old regime you could elect to disapply automatic relief (sometimes deliberately, to crystallise a gain and use BADR or the annual exempt amount). Now the logic is inverted: relief is opt-IN by claim, so to NOT defer you simply do not claim. Explain why someone might choose not to claim (for example to crystallise a gain within the BADR £1m limit at the current rate, link C2).
  - H3: Before vs after 6 April 2026. For transfers before 6 April 2026 the old automatic basis and s.162A election still apply; for transfers on or after, the claim regime applies. The 6 April 2026 date is the dividing line.
- **H2: The medical pension trade-off (do not skip this)**
  - The single most important medical caveat: company income is NOT NHS-pensionable (§2.C). For a consultant, only the NHS post is pensionable; private work routed through a company earns no NHS accrual. So deferring CGT on incorporation can be the right move for asset protection, retained earnings, family-shareholder planning or managing the annual-allowance taper (routing private income outside pensionable pay), but it permanently forgoes NHS pension accrual on that income.
  - State plainly: dividends are not pensionable. Always weigh the CGT deferral and ongoing corporation-tax/dividend position against the pension-accrual loss; never present the tax saving alone. Link `gp-limited-company-tax-benefits-drawbacks` and `private-practice-tax-nhs-and-private-income`.
  - Note also that incorporation does not let you sell NHS goodwill via shares (reg 4(3), §4); s.162 is about private goodwill only. Link C1.
- **H2: s.162 vs the alternatives**
  - H3: s.162 (this page): automatic deferral of the whole gain (if wholly for shares), but base cost rolls into the shares; no choice of how much to defer (it is all or the share-proportion).
  - H3: Selling outright with BADR (C2): crystallise now at the reduced BADR rate; relevant if you are exiting rather than continuing through a company.
  - H3: Holdover/gift relief (s.165) and partial approaches: mention briefly that other reliefs exist for different facts; keep s.162 as the focus and signpost an adviser.
  - The decision turns on whether you are continuing the business through a company (defer with s.162) or selling out (BADR), and on the pension and extraction picture.
- **H2: Disposal timing and the wider 2026 context**
  - The CGT date of disposal still follows the contract: unconditional contract dated when made (TCGA 1992 s.28(1)), conditional contract dated when the condition is met (s.28(2)). Relevant where the incorporation date straddles 6 April 2026 (which regime applies) or where you are choosing between deferring and crystallising under the rising BADR rate. Keep light, link C2 for the BADR-timing detail.
- **H2: Practical steps to get s.162 right**
  - Confirm it is genuinely a private business transferred as a going concern. Value the private goodwill robustly. Decide consideration mix (shares vs loan account) with the deferral consequence in mind. From 6 April 2026, diarise the claim and its deadline (the relief is lost if unclaimed). Model the pension-accrual loss alongside the tax position. Take advice (this is a high-value, technical area).

## 5. Exact HP figures, dates and statutory anchors to cite (verified 2026-06-03)

State these exactly:
- **s.162 mechanic:** transfer of a business as a going concern, all assets (cash may be excluded), wholly or partly for shares; the gain is deferred by deducting it from the base cost of the new shares.
- **FA 2026 s.39 change (transfers on or after 6 April 2026):** s.162 relief is **no longer automatic, it must be claimed**; the claim deadline is **the first anniversary of the 31 January following the tax year of the transfer** (a 2026/27 transfer must be claimed by 31 January 2029); **s.162A (election to disapply) is omitted**. For transfers before 6 April 2026 the old automatic-with-opt-out basis applies.
- **Disposal timing:** TCGA 1992 **s.28(1)** unconditional contract dated when made; **s.28(2)** conditional dated when condition satisfied.
- **Pension trade-off (§2.C):** a company cannot hold a GMS/PMS contract; company income / dividends are NOT NHS-pensionable; for a consultant only the NHS post is pensionable. Always pair the tax saving with the pension-accrual loss.
- **NHS goodwill out of scope (§4):** s.162 applies to PRIVATE goodwill only; reg 4(3) of SI 2019/251 blocks routing NHS goodwill through shares.
