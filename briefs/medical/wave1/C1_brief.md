## 3. Angle (2 to 3 sentences) + differentiation

**Angle.** A GP who has heard a dentist friend talk about selling practice goodwill, or who reads generic business-sale advice, will often assume the same applies to them. It does not: the sale of NHS GP practice goodwill has been prohibited since 1 April 2004, and a GP (or anyone acting on their behalf) cannot sell the goodwill of an NHS medical practice or shares whose value includes it. This page gives the headline answer plainly, explains why the ban exists, sets out what actually changes hands on a partnership buy-in or buy-out (tangible assets, premises and capital accounts, not goodwill), names the one narrow exception (genuinely private, non-NHS goodwill), and contrasts the position with dentists and ordinary businesses where goodwill is freely saleable.

## 4. Section-by-section outline (target 2,800 to 3,500 words)

- **H1: Can You Sell GP Practice Goodwill? The NHS Goodwill-Sale Ban Explained**
- **H2: The short answer: no, NHS GP goodwill cannot be sold**
  - State it plainly in the first 80 words for the featured-snippet position: the sale of goodwill of an NHS GP practice is prohibited and has been since 1 April 2004. A GP partner, or anyone on their behalf, cannot sell that goodwill, and cannot sell company shares whose value includes it.
  - Define goodwill in plain terms (the value of the practice over and above its tangible assets, reflecting the patient list, reputation and earning capacity), then say: for an NHS practice, you are not allowed to put a price on it and sell it.
- **H2: What the law actually says (SI 2019/251 and the 2004 origin)**
  - The current instrument is The Primary Medical Services (Prohibition on the Sale of Goodwill) Regulations 2019 (SI 2019/251), in force from 1 April 2019.
  - Regulation 4(1): performers or providers of primary medical services (or any other person on their behalf) may not sell the goodwill of a medical practice. Quote the operative phrase.
  - Regulation 4(3): a member or shareholder in a relevant company may not sell a share whose value includes an element in respect of the goodwill. Make the point that you cannot route around the ban by incorporating.
  - Regulation 6(1): the 2019 Regulations revoked the original 2004 Regulations (SI 2004/906) and re-enacted the prohibition. So the ban is not new; it dates from 1 April 2004 under SI 2004/906, the 2019 instrument is the current re-enactment.
  - Scope note: applies to GMS, PMS and APMS providers with registered patient lists in England. Devolved nations have their own equivalent arrangements; keep England-focused and flag that point briefly.
- **H2: Why NHS goodwill cannot be sold (the 2004 reform and fairness to incoming partners)**
  - History in brief: before 2004 there were long-standing restrictions, and the 2004 reform put a clear statutory prohibition in place. The policy reason: NHS patient lists and the public funding behind them are a public resource, not a private asset to be capitalised and sold. An incoming GP should not have to buy their way in by paying a departing partner for the value of an NHS list that the NHS funds.
  - Fairness to incoming partners is the core rationale: the ban keeps the cost of becoming a partner down and stops the patient list itself being treated as a tradeable asset.
- **H2: So what actually changes hands on a GP partnership transaction?**
  - Reframe: a GP partnership buy-in or buy-out is about net assets and premises, not goodwill. Three things move:
  - H3: A share of the partnership's tangible assets. Equipment, fixtures, fittings and working capital. An incoming partner buys into their share of these (or the partnership values them on exit). Cross-ref §7 capital allowances on the tangibles.
  - H3: Partnership capital accounts. A buy-in pays for the incoming partner's share of net assets; a buy-out returns the outgoing partner's capital account balance. This is the number that actually gets negotiated, not a goodwill multiple. Link to `becoming-gp-partner-financial-implications` and `gp-partnership-tax-complete-guide`.
  - H3: Premises (often the biggest item). Owned surgery premises, frequently held in a separate property partnership or LLP, with NHS notional or cost rent attached. Premises share valuation, not goodwill, drives the money. Signpost the Cluster B premises pages rather than covering premises tax here.
  - Key line: because there is no goodwill payment, a GP "buy-in" is usually far cheaper than buying into a comparable private business, and is essentially funding your share of the assets and any premises.
- **H2: The one exception: private (non-NHS) goodwill**
  - The prohibition is on NHS goodwill. Genuinely private, non-NHS work can have transferable goodwill that CAN be sold (for example a self-pay clinic, an aesthetics arm, occupational-health contracts or a medico-legal book run as a distinct private business).
  - Be careful: the value must be genuinely attributable to the private activity, not a disguised sale of the NHS list. Reg 4(3) blocks dressing it up through a company.
  - This is where CGT and BADR can come into play, but only on the private element. Hand off cleanly to C2 (`selling-private-medical-practice-cgt-badr`).
- **H2: The contrast: dentists and ordinary businesses can sell goodwill**
  - Dentists: an NHS dental practice (GDS contract) is NOT caught by this prohibition, so dental goodwill IS routinely sold and the "sell goodwill, claim BADR" playbook is real for dentists. State this as the explicit contrast, it is the most common source of confusion for GPs reading generic or dental-oriented advice.
  - Ordinary businesses: a normal trading business sells its goodwill on a sale and the seller claims BADR on the gain. GPs cannot do this for the NHS practice.
  - The takeaway: do not transcribe a dental or general-business goodwill-sale plan onto a GP practice; the headline asset (NHS goodwill) is simply not saleable.
- **H2: Common misconceptions to clear up**
  - "I'll incorporate and sell the company shares." Reg 4(3) blocks selling shares whose value includes NHS goodwill, and a company cannot hold a GMS/PMS contract anyway (§2.C). Cross-ref C3 and §5 pages.
  - "I'll just call it something other than goodwill." Substance over label; the prohibition catches the goodwill element however described.
  - "It changed recently." No, the ban dates from 1 April 2004; SI 2019/251 is a re-enactment, not a new restriction.
- **H2: What this means for your planning**
  - If you are joining: budget for your capital-account buy-in and any premises share, not a goodwill payment. Get the partnership accounts and the deed reviewed.
  - If you are leaving or retiring: your exit value is your capital account plus any premises interest, plus the value of any genuinely private goodwill you separately own. Plan the timing of any private disposal with CGT in mind (link C2).
  - Frame the whole area as needing practice-specific advice (the partnership deed, the premises structure and any private activity all matter).

## 5. Exact HP figures, dates and statutory anchors to cite (verified 2026-06-03)

State these exactly:
- **NHS GP goodwill sale prohibited since 1 April 2004.** Current instrument: **The Primary Medical Services (Prohibition on the Sale of Goodwill) Regulations 2019 (SI 2019/251)**, in force **1 April 2019**.
- **Reg 4(1):** a performer or provider of primary medical services (or any person on their behalf) may not sell the goodwill of a medical practice.
- **Reg 4(3):** cannot sell company shares whose value includes the goodwill element.
- **Reg 6(1):** the 2019 Regulations revoked the original **2004 Regulations (SI 2004/906)**, which first imposed the ban from 1 April 2004.
- Scope: GMS / PMS / APMS providers with registered patient lists in England.
- What transfers instead: tangible assets, partnership capital accounts, premises (HP §4); the only saleable goodwill is genuinely private/non-NHS goodwill.
- A limited company cannot hold a GMS/PMS contract and company income is not NHS-pensionable (HP §2.C), so you cannot incorporate your way to a saleable NHS goodwill.
