## 2. Key legal positions (BINDING — do not drift)

### 2.1 Limited company: separate account is effectively required

A limited company is a **separate legal person** under the Companies Act 2006. The company owns its own assets; the director-shareholder's personal assets are separate. This has two practical consequences that make a separate business account not merely advisable but effectively unavoidable:

1. **The company's money is not the director's money.** Mixing funds in a personal account treats the company's assets as if they were personal property. That blurs the corporate veil, creates an unquantified director's loan account (credits in from the company are drawings; credits out to the company are repayments), and makes it impossible to produce reliable statutory accounts.

2. **Banking terms.** Most retail personal current accounts include terms prohibiting or restricting their use for business purposes. A director running a company through a personal account is likely breaching those terms (state this generically; do NOT name any bank or describe its terms negatively).

A limited company has no legal rule in any Act of Parliament that says "thou shalt have a business bank account." The obligation arises from: (a) the legal-person status of the company under CA 2006; (b) the practical impossibility of accurate bookkeeping without separation; and (c) the personal-account T&C restriction (generic). Frame it as "effectively required" or "you have no workable alternative" rather than citing a statutory duty that does not exist.

### 2.2 Sole trader: not legally required, but practically essential

A sole trader and their business are **the same legal person**. There is no statutory rule requiring a separate account. HMRC does not mandate one.

However, two arguments make separation the only sensible practical choice:

- **HMRC record-keeping.** HMRC expects a sole trader to maintain adequate records to support a correct Self Assessment return (TMA 1970 s.12B). A mixed account produces mixed bank statements. Identifying business receipts and payments in a mixed statement is time-consuming and error-prone, and HMRC can challenge any computation that looks inconsistent.

- **Personal-account T&Cs.** As with limited companies, most retail personal account terms restrict business use. A sole trader running significant business turnover through a personal account may breach those terms (state generically; no bank named negatively).

The legal answer (FAQ 1 must state this plainly): a sole trader is not legally required by statute to hold a separate business bank account. The practical case is overwhelming.

## 3. HMRC-records angle

HMRC's record-keeping requirements for businesses (TMA 1970 s.12B for Self Assessment; VATA 1994 for VAT records; Companies Act 2006 s.386 for company accounting records) do not specify a "business bank account." They specify adequate records to support accurate returns and accounts. A mixed personal/business account makes those records materially harder to produce and more likely to contain errors. Frame the HMRC angle as: separation makes compliance easier and reduces enquiry risk, not as a direct statutory duty.

## 7. FAQ drafts (full text — 10 FAQs)

**FAQ 1 — The legal answer for sole traders (must be FAQ 1 per brief instructions)**

Q: Does a sole trader legally have to have a separate business bank account?

A: No. There is no UK statute that requires a sole trader to hold a separate business bank account. HMRC does not mandate one, and Companies Act 2006 does not apply to sole traders (who are not incorporated). However, HMRC does require sole traders to keep adequate records to support a correct Self Assessment return (TMA 1970 s.12B). Running business and personal transactions through the same account makes those records significantly harder to produce accurately. Most personal current account terms also prohibit or restrict regular business use. The legal answer is no requirement; the practical answer is that separation is strongly advisable.

---

**FAQ 2 — The legal answer for limited companies**

Q: Does a limited company have to have a separate business bank account?

A: There is no single Act of Parliament that says a company must hold a business bank account by name. However, a limited company is a separate legal person under the Companies Act 2006, which means the company's money legally belongs to the company, not its directors. Running company transactions through a director's personal account creates an unquantified director's loan account, makes statutory accounts almost impossible to prepare accurately, and is likely to breach the terms of the personal account. For a limited company, a separate account is not optional in any practical sense.

---

**FAQ 3 — What happens if a sole trader uses a personal account?**

Q: Can I use my personal bank account for my sole trader business?

A: You can, technically, because there is no law stopping you. But most personal accounts prohibit business use in their terms and conditions, and running all transactions through one account makes your Self Assessment bookkeeping significantly harder. Identifying which payments were business expenses and which were personal costs means reviewing every line of every statement, which is time-consuming and error-prone. A missed expense means higher tax; an incorrectly claimed personal expense means an error on your return. Separation is the simplest and most reliable way to maintain accurate records.

---

**FAQ 4 — VAT and bank accounts**

Q: Do I need a separate business account if I'm VAT registered?

A: VAT registration does not legally require a separate bank account either. But VAT returns require you to account accurately for input tax (VAT you have paid on business purchases) and output tax (VAT you have charged customers). HMRC can challenge input tax claims on purchases that appear mixed with personal spending. A dedicated account makes the VAT audit trail cleaner and reduces the risk of a compliance check. Under Making Tax Digital for VAT (which applies to all VAT-registered businesses since April 2022), digital records of transactions must be maintained; a dedicated account feeding bookkeeping software is the cleanest way to meet that requirement.

---

**FAQ 5 — Director's loan account risk**

Q: What is the risk of a company director using a personal account?

A: When a company's money passes through a director's personal account, every deposit becomes a company receipt and every payment made by the director on the company's behalf becomes a loan from the director to the company, or a drawing from the company. This creates a director's loan account (DLA) that must be tracked and, if overdrawn at the company's accounting year end, triggers a CTA 2010 s.455 charge: the company must pay an amount equal to the dividend upper rate (35.75% from 6 April 2026) on the overdrawn balance, 9 months and 1 day after the period end. That charge is repayable when the loan is repaid, but the admin cost of reconstructing an unrecorded DLA at year-end can be substantial.

---

**FAQ 6 — When should I open a business account?**

Q: When should I open a business bank account?

A: The answer is: before the first business transaction. For a limited company, that means before the company receives its first payment, pays its first invoice, or transfers any funds. For a sole trader, it means at the point of starting to trade, or at the point of registering for Self Assessment. Opening an account retrospectively and reconstructing transactions from a mixed personal statement is possible but time-consuming and risky. Starting clean is always easier.

---

**FAQ 7 — What do I need to open a business account?**

Q: What do I need to open a business bank account in the UK?

A: Requirements vary by provider, but you will typically need: proof of identity (passport or driving licence), proof of address, the business name and trading address, Companies House registration number and incorporation certificate for a limited company, your Unique Taxpayer Reference (UTR) for a sole trader or partnership, an estimate of expected turnover, and (for a company) details of directors and significant shareholders. Some providers complete the process entirely online in under an hour; others require a branch visit or a document submission period.

---

**FAQ 8 — Partnerships**

Q: Does a business partnership need a separate bank account?

A: A general partnership does not have a separate legal personality in the same way a limited company does, but it is a distinct entity under the Partnership Act 1890 with its own debts and obligations. In practice, running a partnership through partners' personal accounts creates the same record-keeping problems as for a sole trader, multiplied by the number of partners: it is almost impossible to produce an accurate partnership return (SA800) without a single account that records the firm's receipts and payments. Opening a business account in the firm's name is the standard approach.

---

**FAQ 9 — Does the type of account matter?**

Q: Does it matter what kind of business account I open?

A: Any dedicated business current account will meet the core need of separating business and personal finances. The practical considerations are: whether the account integrates with your bookkeeping software (which reduces manual data entry), the monthly fee and transaction charges relative to your transaction volume, whether you need to deposit cash (not all accounts support cash deposits), and whether you need multiple users (useful if a bookkeeper or accountant needs access). The category of account — high-street bank, digital-first provider, or accountancy-integrated account — affects these features more than the account type itself.

---

**FAQ 10 — HMRC enquiries and bank accounts**

Q: Will HMRC check my bank account?

A: HMRC has statutory powers to request bank statements and transaction records as part of a compliance check or investigation (Finance Act 2011 Sch 23 — data-gathering powers). If you are self-employed or a company director, HMRC can ask your bank directly for records in certain circumstances. Running business and personal transactions through the same account does not protect you from an enquiry; it makes an enquiry more complicated because every personal transaction has to be explained. A dedicated business account means any HMRC request produces only business records, which is a much cleaner position.
