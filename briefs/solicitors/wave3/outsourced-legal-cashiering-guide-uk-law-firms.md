## 2. The TPMA distinction (CRITICAL — must appear early)

**What outsourced cashiering is NOT.** A third-party managed account (TPMA) under SRA Accounts Rules **Rule 11** is a structure in which the firm does NOT receive or hold client money at all; an FCA-authorised payment institution holds the funds. Rule 11.1 permits a TPMA only if "use of the account does not result in you receiving or holding the client's money." The regulatory obligations that flow from client money — the five-weekly reconciliation (Rule 8.3), the accountant's report trigger (Rule 12.1) and the COFA's oversight — do not apply where the firm never holds client money.

**Outsourced cashiering is entirely different.** The firm still operates its own client account, still receives and holds client money, still owes all the obligations under the SRA Accounts Rules 2019, and still carries full COFA accountability. What it outsources is the day-to-day execution: ledger posting, reconciliations, payment processing, reporting. The provider performs the work; the firm and its COFA remain legally responsible for compliance.

This distinction matters when evaluating cost: a firm on the outsourced-cashiering model carries the Rule 12 accountant's-report cost and the underlying client-account bank costs alongside the provider fee, whereas a firm that moves to a TPMA may remove those costs entirely (but changes the disbursement-funding model and the client experience).

## 3. Regulatory framework: what the SRA requires when you outsource

### 3.1 COFA accountability does not transfer

The COFA (Compliance Officer for Finance and Administration) is appointed under the SRA Authorisation of Firms Rules and carries primary responsibility for compliance with the SRA Accounts Rules 2019. Outsourcing the cashiering function to a third party does not transfer COFA accountability. **SRA Code of Conduct for Firms paragraph 2.3** (verified at sra.org.uk) provides: "You remain accountable for compliance with the SRA's regulatory arrangements where your work is carried out through others, including your managers and those you employ or contract with."

The COFA must therefore:

- Set and enforce the service-level framework for the provider.
- Sign off reconciliations (Rule 8.3) — the COFA's sign-off obligation does not delegate to the cashiering provider.
- Retain oversight of the accountant's-report cycle (Rule 12).
- Take responsibility for any SRA notification obligation that arises from a breach the provider caused.

### 3.2 SRA Accounts Rules obligations that remain with the firm

The following obligations sit with the regulated firm regardless of who performs the day-to-day cashiering:

- **Client account (Rule 3):** client money must be held in a properly designated client account at a bank or building society branch in England and Wales. The provider does not hold the account; the firm does.
- **Banking-facility prohibition (Rule 3.3):** every payment in or out must relate to the delivery of regulated services. The provider executes these payments, but the obligation — and any breach — is the firm's.
- **Interest (Rule 7):** the firm must account to clients for a fair sum of interest. The provider may calculate and administer this, but the obligation and any client-facing liability sit with the firm.
- **Five-weekly reconciliation (Rule 8.3):** ledger totals must be reconciled to the cash book and the bank statement at least every five weeks, signed off by the COFA or a manager. A provider delivering a reconciliation for COFA sign-off satisfies the mechanics; the COFA's scrutiny and sign-off are not delegable.
- **Accountant's report (Rule 12.1):** if the firm has held client money at any point in the accounting period, a report by a qualified accountant is required within six months of the period end. The Rule 12.2 exemption (average balance not exceeding £10,000 AND maximum not exceeding £250,000) applies on the usual terms regardless of whether a cashiering provider is used.

### 3.3 Records and data

Records of client-account transactions must be kept for at least six years. An outsourced arrangement must preserve the firm's ability to produce those records at any time, including if the provider relationship ends. Contracts with cashiering providers should address: data ownership, data export in a usable format, continuity of access during a transition, and deletion obligations at contract end.

## 4. Provider selection: what to check

This section is structured as criteria the firm should apply, not as a recommendation of any provider.

**SRA registration and professional indemnity.** The provider is not itself regulated by the SRA (unless it is a licensed body). The firm should confirm what professional indemnity or errors-and-omissions cover the provider carries and whether that cover would respond to a claim arising from a cashiering error that exposed the firm to SRA sanction.

**Understanding of SRA Accounts Rules 2019.** The provider's team must work within the current Rules (in force from 25 November 2019). Key operational competencies to test: correct treatment of mixed payments under Rule 4.2 (allocate promptly); the prohibition on moving the firm's costs out of client account without a prior bill (Rule 4.3); the Rule 3.3 banking-facility prohibition; and the residual-balance procedure under Rule 5.1(c) (£500 threshold for charity payment without SRA authority; SRA prior written authority required above £500).

**Reconciliation output.** Rule 8.3 requires a three-way reconciliation: client ledger balances, cash book and bank statement. The provider's output must be in a form the COFA can review, understand and sign off. Firms should ask to see a sample reconciliation before contracting.

**Practice-management software compatibility.** Most cashiering providers work within or alongside the firm's existing practice-management system (PMS). Compatibility, data-feed method and the cost of any integration are material selection factors.

**SLAs and breach-response.** Because SRA breaches can be self-reported obligations (a material breach must be reported to the SRA promptly), the contract should require the provider to notify the firm immediately of any error or breach it identifies, with enough detail for the COFA to assess SRA-reportability.

**Confidentiality and GDPR.** The provider will process personal data as a data processor under UK GDPR. A data-processing agreement (DPA) is required. The firm remains the data controller. Check whether the provider uses sub-processors (e.g. offshore bookkeeping teams) and whether the relevant international-transfer safeguards are in place.

## 5. Cost versus in-house: the comparison framework

A genuine cost comparison involves more than the provider's monthly fee. The full cost of in-house cashiering includes: salary and employer NIC (15% above the £5,000 secondary threshold from 6 April 2025), pension auto-enrolment contributions, recruitment and training, cover during absence, and PMS licences. Against this, the provider fee (and any integration cost) plus the retention of ancillary costs (bank charges, accountant's-report fee if applicable) should be weighed.

The comparison also has a risk dimension. An in-house cashier has employment-law protections and may be difficult to exit if performance is poor. A provider contract can be exited on notice, but transition risk (moving ledgers, access, reconciliation history) must be managed.

This page does not model specific figures: the economics depend entirely on the firm's size, jurisdiction and PMS. The comparison framework is the tool.

## 6. Tax and cost treatment of cashiering provider fees

### 6.1 Deductibility: the "wholly and exclusively" test

Fees paid to an outsourced cashiering provider are a revenue expense incurred for the purposes of the firm's trade. For an **unincorporated firm** (sole practitioner, partnership, LLP), the deduction is governed by **ITTOIA 2005 s.34**: expenditure is deductible if incurred "wholly and exclusively for the purposes of the trade, profession or vocation". For a **company or ABS**, the equivalent is **CTA 2009 s.54**. A cashiering fee is an ordinary recurring management expense of running the practice and satisfies the wholly-and-exclusively test without difficulty: there is no private or dual purpose. The deduction reduces taxable profit in the period the expense is incurred (accruals basis, which applies to LLPs and partnerships with a corporate member; see HP §4).

### 6.2 VAT on the provider's fee

An outsourced cashiering provider supplies a professional management service, which is a **standard-rated supply at 20% VAT**. The firm pays 20% VAT on the provider's fee. If the firm is VAT-registered (registration threshold £90,000 from 1 April 2024) and makes taxable legal supplies, it recovers that input VAT in the normal way on its VAT return. A firm that is not VAT-registered bears the VAT as an irrecoverable cost, which increases the effective fee.

For a partially exempt firm (see HP §6.C), input VAT on overheads attributable to exempt income may be restricted, but for a firm whose income is predominantly standard-rated legal services the cashiering fee VAT will typically be fully recoverable. If there is any doubt, the firm should apply its partial-exemption method to the cashiering fee.

### 6.3 Treatment in the accounts

The cashiering fee is a practice-overhead expense and sits in the profit-and-loss account, not on the balance sheet. It does not attract capital allowances (it is not expenditure on plant or machinery). For LLPs, this reduces the trading profit allocated to members and so reduces each member's income-tax and Class 4 NIC liability in proportion to their profit share.

## 7. When outsourcing may not be the right model

Not every firm should outsource. Factors that weigh against it:

- **High-volume, complex client-account activity** where the latency of an external provider creates operational risk (e.g. conveyancing practices with multiple completions per day and tight payment deadlines).
- **Regulatory history.** A firm with a recent SRA investigation or client-account breach may find that close COFA oversight of an in-house cashier is a stronger control environment than an outsourced model, at least until the position is restored.
- **Size.** For a sole practitioner or very small firm with minimal client-account activity, the Rule 12.2 exemption (average balance not exceeding £10,000 / maximum not exceeding £250,000) may already apply, reducing the compliance burden that an outsourced cashier is hired to manage. In that case, the TPMA model (cross-ref: `third-party-managed-accounts-tpma-for-law-firms`) may eliminate the compliance layer entirely rather than just managing it.
