## 2. The TPMA distinction (CRITICAL -- must appear early)

**What outsourced cashiering is NOT.** A third-party managed account (TPMA) under SRA Accounts Rules **Rule 11** is a structure in which the firm does NOT receive or hold client money at all; an FCA-authorised payment institution (per the SRA Glossary definition of "third party managed account") holds the funds. Rule 11.1 permits a TPMA only if "use of the account does not result in you receiving or holding the client's money." The regulatory obligations that flow from client money -- the five-weekly reconciliation (Rule 8.3), the accountant's report trigger (Rule 12.1) and the COFA's oversight -- do not apply where the firm never holds client money.

**Outsourced cashiering is entirely different.** The firm still operates its own client account, still receives and holds client money, still owes all the obligations under the SRA Accounts Rules 2019, and still carries full COFA accountability. What it outsources is the day-to-day execution: ledger posting, reconciliations, payment processing, reporting. The provider performs the work; the firm and its COFA remain legally responsible for compliance.

This distinction matters when evaluating cost: a firm on the outsourced-cashiering model carries the Rule 12 accountant's-report cost and the underlying client-account bank costs alongside the provider fee, whereas a firm that moves to a TPMA may remove those costs entirely (but changes the disbursement-funding model and the client experience).

## 3. Regulatory framework: what the SRA requires when you outsource

### 3.1 COFA accountability does not transfer

The COFA (Compliance Officer for Finance and Administration) is appointed under the SRA Authorisation of Firms Rules and carries primary responsibility for compliance with the SRA Accounts Rules 2019. Outsourcing the cashiering function to a third party does not transfer COFA accountability. **SRA Code of Conduct for Firms paragraph 2.3** (verified live at sra.org.uk, version in effect from 11 April 2025) provides: "You remain accountable for compliance with the SRA's regulatory arrangements where your work is carried out through others, including your managers and those you employ or contract with."

The COFA must therefore:

- Set and enforce the service-level framework for the provider.
- Sign off reconciliations (Rule 8.3) -- the COFA's sign-off obligation does not delegate to the cashiering provider.
- Retain oversight of the accountant's-report cycle (Rule 12).
- Take responsibility for any SRA notification obligation that arises from a breach the provider caused.

### 3.2 SRA Accounts Rules obligations that remain with the firm

The following obligations sit with the regulated firm regardless of who performs the day-to-day cashiering:

- **Client account (Rule 3):** client money must be held in a properly designated client account at a bank or building society branch in England and Wales. The provider does not hold the account; the firm does.
- **Banking-facility prohibition (Rule 3.3):** every payment in or out must relate to the delivery of regulated services. The provider executes these payments, but the obligation -- and any breach -- is the firm's.
- **Interest (Rule 7):** the firm must account to clients for a fair sum of interest. The provider may calculate and administer this, but the obligation and any client-facing liability sit with the firm.
- **Five-weekly reconciliation (Rule 8.3):** ledger totals must be reconciled to the cash book and the bank statement at least every five weeks, signed off by the COFA or a manager. A provider delivering a reconciliation for COFA sign-off satisfies the mechanics; the COFA's scrutiny and sign-off are not delegable.
- **Accountant's report (Rule 12.1):** if the firm has held client money at any point in the accounting period, a report by a qualified accountant is required within six months of the period end. The Rule 12.2 exemption (average balance not exceeding £10,000 AND maximum not exceeding £250,000) applies on the usual terms regardless of whether a cashiering provider is used.

### 3.3 Records and data

Records of client-account transactions must be kept for at least six years. An outsourced arrangement must preserve the firm's ability to produce those records at any time, including if the provider relationship ends. Contracts with cashiering providers should address: data ownership, data export in a usable format, continuity of access during a transition, and deletion obligations at contract end. Because the provider accesses the firm's existing practice-management system (PMS) remotely rather than running a separate ledger, the data typically remains in the firm's own PMS; confirm this contractually nonetheless.

This section is structured as criteria the firm should apply, mapped to the COFA's para 2.3 accountability. It is not a recommendation of any provider.

### 4.1 Due-diligence checklist (mapped to para 2.3)

The checklist below is the provider-selection tool the COFA should complete before contracting. Each item maps to a specific regulatory risk the firm retains.

| # | Check | Regulatory hook |
|---|-------|----------------|
| 1 | Professional indemnity or errors-and-omissions cover: confirm the level, whether it responds to cashiering errors that expose the firm to SRA sanction, and whether the firm is a named beneficiary. | Para 2.3 accountability; the firm bears the SRA sanction even if the provider caused the error. |
| 2 | Working knowledge of SRA Accounts Rules 2019 (in force 25 November 2019): ask the provider to walk through how they handle mixed payments (Rule 4.2 -- allocate promptly), the prohibition on moving firm costs out of client account without a prior bill (Rule 4.3), and residual balances (Rule 5.1(c) -- £500 charity threshold; SRA prior written authority above £500). | Rules 4.2, 4.3, 5.1(c) -- mechanical errors here are the firm's breach. |

### 5.2 Risk dimension

The comparison also has a risk dimension that the cost template does not capture directly.

**In-house risk:** an employee has employment-law protections; if performance falls short, exit is slow and potentially costly. A sole cashier creates a single point of failure during absence or departure.

**Outsourced risk:** the contract can typically be exited on notice, removing the employment-law constraint. But transition risk must be managed: moving ledger history, access credentials and reconciliation records takes time and creates an operational gap. The due-diligence checklist in §4 mitigates this by requiring clear exit and data-portability terms before signing.

**Calibration note.** Some providers claim 20-30% savings on in-house salary costs, and up to 50% in some cases. These figures are not independently verified and will vary materially by firm size, location and existing staffing structure. The template in §5.1 is the tool; apply it to the firm's own numbers.

## 6. Tax and cost treatment of cashiering provider fees

### 6.1 Deductibility: the "wholly and exclusively" test

Fees paid to an outsourced cashiering provider are a revenue expense incurred for the purposes of the firm's trade. For an **unincorporated firm** (sole practitioner, partnership, LLP), the deduction is governed by **ITTOIA 2005 s.34**: expenditure is deductible if incurred "wholly and exclusively for the purposes of the trade, profession or vocation". For a **company or ABS**, the equivalent is **CTA 2009 s.54**. A cashiering fee is an ordinary recurring management expense of running the practice and satisfies the wholly-and-exclusively test without difficulty: there is no private or dual purpose. The deduction reduces taxable profit in the period the expense is incurred (accruals basis -- which applies to LLPs and partnerships with a corporate member; see HP §4).

### 6.2 VAT on the provider's fee

An outsourced cashiering provider supplies a professional management service. Management and bookkeeping services do not appear in any of the 16 exempt-supply groups in **VATA 1994 Schedule 9** (verified at legislation.gov.uk, 2026-07-09): they are a **standard-rated supply at 20% VAT** by default. The firm pays 20% VAT on the provider's fee.

**Input VAT recovery.** If the firm is VAT-registered (registration threshold £90,000 from 1 April 2024) and makes predominantly taxable legal supplies, it recovers that input VAT in the normal way on its VAT return. The cashiering fee is an overhead attributable to the firm's taxable activities. For a typical law firm whose income is predominantly standard-rated legal services, the cashiering fee VAT will be fully recoverable, making the VAT cost-neutral. A firm that is not VAT-registered bears the VAT as an irrecoverable cost, which increases the effective fee by 20%.

**Partial exemption.** For a partially exempt firm (HP §6.C), input VAT on overheads attributable to exempt income may be restricted. But for a firm whose income is predominantly standard-rated legal services, the cashiering fee VAT will typically be fully recoverable. If there is any doubt, the firm should apply its partial-exemption method to the cashiering fee as an overhead.

### 6.3 Treatment in the accounts

The cashiering fee is a practice-overhead expense and sits in the profit-and-loss account, not on the balance sheet. It does not attract capital allowances (it is not expenditure on plant or machinery). For LLPs, this reduces the trading profit allocated to members and so reduces each member's income-tax and Class 4 NIC liability in proportion to their profit share.

## 7. When outsourcing may not be the right model

Not every firm should outsource. Factors that weigh against it:

- **High-volume, complex client-account activity** where the latency of an external provider creates operational risk (e.g. conveyancing practices with multiple completions per day and tight payment deadlines).
- **Regulatory history.** A firm with a recent SRA investigation or client-account breach may find that close COFA oversight of an in-house cashier is a stronger control environment than an outsourced model, at least until the position is restored.
- **Size.** For a sole practitioner or very small firm with minimal client-account activity, the Rule 12.2 exemption (average balance not exceeding £10,000 / maximum not exceeding £250,000) may already apply, reducing the compliance burden that an outsourced cashier is hired to manage. In that case, the TPMA model (cross-ref: `third-party-managed-accounts-tpma-for-law-firms`) may eliminate the compliance layer entirely rather than just managing it.

## 11. FAQs (full draft answers -- 12 FAQs)

### FAQ 1: Is outsourced cashiering the same as a TPMA?

No. These are fundamentally different models. A third-party managed account (TPMA) under SRA Accounts Rules Rule 11 is a structure where the firm does NOT receive or hold client money at all -- an FCA-authorised payment institution holds the funds under Rule 11.1. The firm avoids the five-weekly reconciliation requirement (Rule 8.3) and the accountant's-report trigger (Rule 12.1) because it never holds client money.

Outsourced cashiering is the opposite premise: the firm continues to operate its own client account, hold client money, and bear all the obligations under the SRA Accounts Rules 2019. What is outsourced is the day-to-day execution -- ledger posting, reconciliations, payment processing. The firm's COFA retains full accountability. For firms wanting to explore removing client-account obligations entirely, see our guide to [third-party managed accounts for law firms].

### FAQ 2: Can a law firm outsource its legal cashiering?

Yes. Nothing in the SRA Accounts Rules 2019 prohibits a firm from using an external provider to carry out the day-to-day cashiering function. What the Rules make clear -- particularly through SRA Code of Conduct for Firms para 2.3 -- is that the firm remains accountable for compliance regardless of who performs the work. The COFA's obligations (Rule 8.3 sign-off, Rule 12 oversight) do not delegate to the provider.

### FAQ 3: Does outsourcing cashiering remove the need for a COFA?

No. The COFA is appointed under the SRA Authorisation of Firms Rules and is a mandatory officer of every authorised firm. Outsourcing the cashiering function does not change that. The COFA must still: set the service-level framework for the provider, sign off Rule 8.3 reconciliations, oversee the Rule 12 accountant's-report cycle, and report any material breaches to the SRA. The provider performs the work; the COFA carries the regulatory accountability.

### FAQ 4: Is a cashiering provider required to be SRA-regulated?

No. An outsourced cashiering provider is not itself conducting regulated legal activities and is not required to be authorised by the SRA (unless it is itself a licensed body providing other regulated legal services). The provider's obligation to comply with the SRA Accounts Rules exists through its contract with the firm; regulatory responsibility remains with the firm. The firm should assess the provider's professional-indemnity cover, relevant expertise and data-handling practices during due diligence (see §4).

### FAQ 5: Does the firm still need an accountant's report if it uses an outsourced cashier?

Yes, if it has held client money during the accounting period. The Rule 12.1 trigger -- holding or receiving client money at any point in the period -- is not affected by whether an external provider does the cashiering. The Rule 12.2 exemption (average client-account balance not exceeding £10,000 AND maximum not exceeding £250,000) applies on exactly the same terms, regardless of the cashiering model. If the exemption conditions are met, no report is required; if the firm holds larger balances, the report is required and must be filed within six months of the period end.

### FAQ 6: Are cashiering provider fees deductible for tax?

Yes. Fees paid to an outsourced cashiering provider are a revenue expense incurred wholly and exclusively for the purposes of the firm's trade. For an unincorporated firm (sole practitioner, partnership or LLP) the deduction falls under ITTOIA 2005 s.34; for a company or ABS, under CTA 2009 s.54. There is no private or dual-purpose element; the expense reduces the firm's taxable profits in the period it is incurred.

### FAQ 7: Is VAT charged on cashiering provider fees?

Yes. An outsourced cashiering provider supplies a professional management service, which is a standard-rated supply at 20% VAT under VATA 1994. Management and bookkeeping services do not fall within any of the 16 exempt-supply groups in VATA 1994 Schedule 9. If the firm is VAT-registered and makes predominantly taxable legal supplies, it recovers the input VAT through its VAT return, making the VAT cost-neutral. A firm that is not VAT-registered bears the 20% VAT as an irrecoverable cost.

### FAQ 8: What happens to client-account records if I switch provider?

The six-year records-retention obligation in the SRA Accounts Rules sits with the firm, not the provider. Before contracting with any provider, the COFA should confirm: (a) who owns the data -- in most outsourced models the data remains in the firm's own practice-management system, since the provider accesses the PMS remotely; (b) what format data can be exported in and whether it is usable by a successor; and (c) what the provider's obligations are on contract termination, including deletion of any copies it holds. A well-drafted contract will specify that the firm has continuous access to all records during the relationship and the right to a full data export on exit, without additional charge.

### FAQ 9: How often must reconciliations be produced under an outsourced model?

At least every five weeks, per SRA Accounts Rules Rule 8.3. The outsourced model does not change this frequency. The provider's job is to produce the three-way reconciliation (client ledger totals, cash book, bank statement) and deliver it to the COFA in a form the COFA can review and sign. The COFA's sign-off is mandatory; it cannot be delegated to the provider.

### FAQ 10: What should a law firm include in its contract with a cashiering provider?

At minimum: (a) scope of services (which Rules the provider covers, which remain with the COFA); (b) service levels (response times, reconciliation deadlines, payment turnaround); (c) breach-notification obligation (provider must notify the firm immediately of any error or potential SRA breach, with sufficient detail for the COFA to assess reportability); (d) professional-indemnity and liability provisions; (e) data-processing agreement (UK GDPR Art 28 -- the provider is a data processor); (f) sub-processor list and international-transfer safeguards; (g) data ownership and export on termination; (h) notice period and transition-support obligations; and (i) audit rights (the COFA must be able to inspect the provider's work on request).

### FAQ 11: Can a sole practitioner use an outsourced cashiering provider?

Yes. There is no firm-size restriction. For a sole practitioner with limited client-account activity, outsourcing may remove the management overhead of employing a cashier. However, a sole practitioner with an average client-account balance not exceeding £10,000 and a maximum not exceeding £250,000 may already qualify for the Rule 12.2 accountant's-report exemption; in that case, the compliance driver for outsourcing is reduced and the TPMA model (removing client-account obligations entirely) may be worth considering.

### FAQ 12: Does the SRA require any notification when a firm starts using an outsourced cashiering provider?

There is no specific SRA notification requirement triggered solely by appointing an outsourced cashiering provider. The obligation to notify the SRA arises where a material breach of the SRA Accounts Rules occurs -- caused by the provider or otherwise -- and where a notifiable event under the SRA Authorisation of Firms Rules takes place (e.g. a change in the firm's COFA). Using an outsourced provider is a management decision, not itself a notifiable change, provided the COFA remains in post and the firm's authorisation structure is unchanged.
