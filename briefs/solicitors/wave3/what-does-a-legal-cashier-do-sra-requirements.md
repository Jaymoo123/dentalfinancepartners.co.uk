## 3. House positions to anchor (§5 and sub-sections)

All positions below are **locked in house_positions.md §5** (verified at sra.org.uk 2026-06-03; reconciliation re-verified 2026-07-09). Use these and only these; raise BRIEF_DRIFT if a competitor source conflicts.

- **Client money (Rule 2):** money held or received relating to regulated services, on behalf of a third party, as trustee or office-holder, or for fees/unpaid disbursements before billing.
- **Client account (Rule 3):** must be held at a bank or building society branch in England and Wales; account name must include the firm name and the word "client"; kept separate from the firm's own money (Rule 4 separates the two streams).
- **Authorised withdrawals (Rule 5):** client money may only be withdrawn (a) for the purpose for which it is held, (b) following client/third-party instructions, or (c) on SRA's prior written authorisation or in prescribed circumstances. The cashier executes withdrawals only within these grounds; they do not have a freestanding authorisation to move money for other purposes.
- **Reconciliation (Rule 8 / Rule 8.3):** "at least every five weeks, for all client accounts held or operated by you, a reconciliation of the bank or building society statement balance with the cash book balance and the client ledger total, a record of which must be signed off by the COFA or a manager of the firm." (Verified verbatim at sra.org.uk 2026-07-09.) The **cashier performs** the reconciliation; the **COFA or a manager signs it off**. This is the canonical cashier-vs-COFA split on this task.
- **Interest (Rule 7):** the firm must account to clients for a fair sum of interest on client money held; the cashier maintains the data (ledger balances, duration) the firm needs to calculate and pay this.
- **Banking-facility prohibition (Rule 3.3):** payments in, transfers, and withdrawals must all relate to the delivery of regulated services; the cashier is the operational gatekeeper for this prohibition.
- **Mixed payments (Rule 4.2):** a single receipt part-client / part-office must be allocated promptly; the cashier allocates on receipt.
- **Bill before transfer (Rule 4.3):** a bill of costs (or written notification of costs) must precede any transfer of the firm's costs out of client account; the cashier cannot make this transfer until the bill is issued.
- **Residual balances (Rule 5.1(c)):** unclaimed balances of £500 or less per matter may be paid to charity (no SRA authority needed, if all conditions met); over £500 needs SRA prior written authorisation. The cashier identifies these; the COFA or manager authorises disposal.
- **Accountant's report (Rule 12.1 / 12.2):** a report is required if the firm held client money in the period; exempt only if average balance did not exceed £10,000 AND maximum did not exceed £250,000. The cashier's ledger accuracy is the foundation for this report.
- **Records (Rule 8):** client account records must be kept for at least six years.

## 5. Key content points (per section)

**§1 The cashier's place in an SRA-regulated firm**
The SRA Accounts Rules 2019 place the primary accountability for client-money compliance on the COFA and the firm's managers. The legal cashier is the **operational executor**: the person who maintains the day-to-day records, performs the transactions, and produces the data the COFA relies on to sign off. In a very small firm the roles can overlap (a fee earner may perform cashiering tasks), but the accountability architecture stays the same: someone executes, the COFA (or a manager) reviews and signs off.

**§2 Day-to-day ledger and client account duties**
Maintain a separate ledger for each client matter (Rule 8); post receipts and payments accurately on the day of receipt/payment; ensure the client account is held at a bank or building society branch in England and Wales in the correct name (Rule 3); keep client money entirely separate from office money (Rule 4); apply the banking-facility prohibition (Rule 3.3 - never use the client account for non-regulated-service transactions).

**§3 Reconciliation: the five-weekly cycle (Rule 8)**
The SRA Accounts Rules require a reconciliation "at least every five weeks" across all client accounts: bank statement balance to cash book to client ledger total (Rule 8, verified verbatim at sra.org.uk 2026-07-09). The cashier **performs** the reconciliation and produces the reconciliation statement; the COFA or a manager **signs it off**. Records are kept for at least six years. A cashier who lets reconciliations slip, or who produces a reconciliation the COFA cannot verify, is the most common trigger for SRA compliance concerns.

**§4 Authorised withdrawals (Rule 5)**
Client money may only be withdrawn: (a) for the purpose for which it was received, (b) on client or third-party instructions, or (c) on SRA's prior written authorisation or in prescribed circumstances. The cashier must be able to point to a clear ground for every withdrawal; any withdrawal without a matching ground is a potential Rule 5 breach. The cashier spots patterns (e.g., withdrawals that do not match a corresponding instruction or a matter ledger) and escalates.

**§5 Mixed receipts and bill-before-transfer (Rule 4)**
When a single payment contains both client money and office money (e.g., a cheque covering fees already billed plus money on account), the cashier allocates promptly to the correct accounts (Rule 4.2). Before transferring the firm's costs out of client account, the cashier checks that a bill or written notification has been issued (Rule 4.3); the transfer cannot precede the bill.

**§6 Interest tracking (Rule 7)**
The firm must account to each client for a fair sum of interest on client money held; the cashier maintains the ledger data (balance held, duration, interest earned on designated accounts) that the firm uses to calculate and credit fair interest. The cashier flags accounts where a large balance has been held for an extended period, so that the firm can review its interest obligation.

**§7 Residual and dormant balances**
The cashier identifies old, unclaimed matter balances and flags them for management attention. Balances of £500 or less per matter may be donated to charity without SRA authorisation (if all conditions under Rule 5.1(c) are met); over £500 requires SRA prior written authorisation. The cashier maintains the required central register. Clearing these before the accountant's report period is good practice (a long-standing uncleared suspense or residual balance is a classic report-qualification trigger).

**§8 Breach recognition and escalation**
The cashier is frequently the first person to notice a potential breach: a withdrawal without a matching client instruction, a receipt that does not correspond to any known matter, a reconciliation difference that cannot be resolved, a prohibited banking-facility transaction. The cashier's duty is to escalate immediately to the COFA; they do not self-authorise a resolution. Document the escalation. The COFA then decides whether to report to the SRA.

**§9 Cashier vs COFA: the accountability split**
The cashier **executes**: posts, reconciles, pays, flags. The COFA **reviews and is accountable**: signs off reconciliations, decides on breach reports, holds the SRA licence risk. This distinction matters for firm owners structuring oversight: a cashier cannot substitute for a properly appointed COFA, and a COFA who rubber-stamps a cashier's work without reviewing it has not discharged their duty.

**§10 Hiring a legal cashier**
What to look for: direct experience of SRA Accounts Rules 2019 (not just legacy rules pre-2019); familiarity with the firm's practice management software (e.g., Clio, LEAP, Osprey, SOS); a track record of clean reconciliations (ask to see the frequency and sign-off discipline); understanding of Rule 5 withdrawal grounds and Rule 4.2/4.3 mixed-payment and bill-before-transfer discipline. Qualifications: the Institute of Legal Finance and Management (ILFM) offers the Diploma in Legal Practice Management and the Certificate in Legal Cashiering; these are the recognised specialist credentials in England and Wales.

**§11 Outsourced cashiering (brief pointer)**
Some firms outsource the cashiering function to a specialist provider. The COFA's accountability does not transfer with the function: the COFA remains responsible for ensuring the outsourced cashier performs to SRA standard and signs off reconciliations. Full treatment: [outsourced-legal-cashiering-guide-uk-law-firms] (A6).

### 12.3 Worked example: five-weekly three-way reconciliation walk-through

**Context for writer:** This is a concrete, illustrative example. Figures are round numbers for clarity. All numbers are fictional. Place this in §3 (Reconciliation: the five-weekly cycle).

---

**Scenario: Greenfield Family Law LLP, reconciliation at 4 November 2024**

The cashier is reconciling two client accounts (current account and a designated deposit account) across all open matters.

**Step 1 — Client ledger total**
The cashier runs a report from the practice management system showing the balance of every client matter ledger with a credit balance:

| Matter | Client | Ledger balance |
|--------|--------|---------------|
| GFL-001 | Smith / conveyancing | £12,400.00 |
| GFL-002 | Jones / matrimonial | £3,500.00 |
| GFL-003 | Patel / probate | £28,750.00 |
| GFL-004 | Davis / litigation | £6,200.00 |
| GFL-005 | Ahmed / conveyancing | £950.00 |
| **Total** | | **£51,800.00** |

**Step 2 — Cash book balance**
The cash book (internal record of all receipts and payments through the client account) shows a closing balance of £51,800.00.

Result: client ledger total = cash book balance. First check passes.

**Step 3 — Bank statement reconciliation**
The bank statement for the same period shows:

- Opening balance: £49,200.00
- Add receipts in period: £18,600.00
- Less payments in period: £(16,000.00)
- **Closing statement balance: £51,800.00**

Result: bank statement balance = cash book balance = client ledger total.

**All three agree. Reconciliation is clean.** The cashier prepares the reconciliation statement, noting the date of the bank statement used, the date of the reconciliation, and the total reconciled. The COFA (or a manager) reviews and signs.

---

**Discrepancy example: what a difference looks like and what the cashier does**

Same scenario, but the bank statement shows £51,550.00, not £51,800.00.

Difference: £250.00 unexplained.

The cashier's sequence:
1. Re-cast the cash book for posting errors (transpositions, double-posts, missed entries).
2. Check the bank statement for entries not yet posted to the cash book (e.g. bank charges, interest credits, a late-arriving CHAPS the firm did not yet post).
3. Check whether any receipts were posted to the cash book but have not yet cleared the bank (timing differences).
4. If the difference cannot be resolved, it is an **unreconciled item** and must be investigated before sign-off. The cashier escalates to the COFA and documents the escalation.

A £250 unexplained shortfall on the bank statement side could mean: (a) a payment was made from the bank that was not posted to the cash book (Rule 5 risk: was it authorised?); (b) a bank charge was applied (check whether Rule 3.3 / banking-facility prohibition applies); (c) a timing difference not yet resolved. The cashier does not sign off a reconciliation with an unresolved difference. The COFA cannot sign off a reconciliation the cashier has not resolved.

**Six-year retention reminder:** The signed reconciliation statement and supporting workings are kept for at least six years (Rule 8).

### 12.4 Day / week / month cashier task calendar

**For writer:** Place a summary version of this in §2 (Day-to-day ledger and client account duties) or as a standalone callout box. This is the operational cadence the firm owner should expect.

**Daily tasks**
- Post all client-money receipts on the day of receipt; allocate to the correct matter ledger.
- Post all payments out of client account on the day of payment; verify the withdrawal ground (Rule 5) before processing.
- Review any incoming receipts that cannot be immediately identified: open a suspense entry, investigate, and allocate as soon as possible (Rule 8; §5.F of house positions).
- Check for any mixed receipts (part client money, part office money) and allocate promptly to the correct accounts (Rule 4.2).
- Verify that any transfer of the firm's costs out of client account is preceded by a bill or written notification (Rule 4.3).

**Weekly tasks**
- Reconcile bank statement to cash book for any active accounts where there is material movement (pre-cursor to the formal five-weekly reconciliation, good practice).
- Review open matters for interest accumulation: flag to fee earner or COFA any matter where a large balance has been held and interest has not been reviewed (Rule 7).
- Check for residual or dormant balances approaching action thresholds; update the residual balance register.

**Every five weeks (at latest)**
- Prepare the formal three-way reconciliation (bank statement / cash book / client ledger total) for all client accounts (Rule 8.3).
- Present the reconciliation statement to the COFA or a manager for sign-off.
- File the signed reconciliation with a note of the bank statement date used and the reconciliation date.

**Monthly / periodic tasks**
- Review the residual balance register; identify any balance that has become dormant and initiate the return-to-client process (or, for balances under £500 with no returnable owner, follow the §5.B process).
- Review the client interest position: produce a report of balances and duration for the COFA's review (Rule 7).
- Assist with management accounts by providing aged-matter balance reports, client-account balance summaries, and any compliance queries from fee earners.
- Ahead of the accountant's report period end: clear suspense, resolve unidentified receipts, and prepare a clean ledger for the reporting accountant (Rule 12).

**Q: Does a small law firm need a dedicated legal cashier?**

Not necessarily a dedicated one, but someone must perform the cashiering function. The SRA Accounts Rules 2019 require the work to be done: ledgers maintained, receipts posted on the day, withdrawals authorised against Rule 5 grounds, five-weekly reconciliations prepared and signed off. In a small firm (say, fewer than five fee earners), a practice manager or office manager with SRA Accounts Rules knowledge can carry out the cashiering role. The COFA is accountable for ensuring the work is done correctly regardless of who does it. The risk of not having a dedicated cashier is that compliance slips when the practice manager is also handling front-office duties: the reconciliation drifts past five weeks, unidentified receipts sit in suspense, and the accountant's report is qualified. Most firms above five or six fee earners find it cost-effective to have at least part-time specialist cashiering resource.

---

**Q: What qualifications does a legal cashier need in England and Wales?**

There is no statutory minimum qualification for the legal cashiering role. In practice, the recognised specialist body is the **Institute of Legal Finance and Management (ILFM)**. The ILFM offers a Diploma (the Dip ILFM, described by the institute as the gold standard for legal finance professionals) and qualifications in Legal Finance Compliance and Accounts Rules, which are directly aligned with SRA Accounts Rules practice. The ILFM also endorses legal accounts courses through CILEx Regulation. When hiring, a firm owner should look for: direct experience with the SRA Accounts Rules 2019 (not only legacy pre-2019 rules); familiarity with the firm's practice management software (Clio, LEAP, Osprey, SOS Connect and similar); a demonstrable track record of producing clean, on-time reconciliations; and an understanding of Rule 5 withdrawal grounds and Rule 4.2/4.3 discipline. An ILFM qualification signals that the candidate has been assessed on exactly this framework.

**Q: What is a client ledger and why does it matter?**

A client ledger is the individual financial record the firm maintains for each client matter, showing every receipt and payment of client money related to that matter, with a running balance. The SRA Accounts Rules 2019 (Rule 8) require a separate ledger for each client or controlled trust. The ledger is the cashier's primary working document: it is how the firm can demonstrate at any time what it holds for each client, support the five-weekly reconciliation (the sum of all ledger balances must equal the cash book balance and the bank statement balance), and produce the data for the accountant's report. A ledger error (a wrong posting, a credit to the wrong matter, an unposted receipt) corrupts the reconciliation and creates a compliance risk. The COFA's sign-off on the reconciliation is only as reliable as the cashier's ledger accuracy.

---

**Q: How does a legal cashier handle a mixed payment?**

A mixed payment is a single receipt that contains both client money (for example, a payment on account for future work) and the firm's own money (for example, payment of an outstanding bill). Rule 4.2 of the SRA Accounts Rules 2019 requires the cashier to allocate the funds promptly: the client-money portion goes to the client account and the office-money portion goes to the business account. There is no fixed day-count for "promptly" (this is not the VAT 14-day rule, which is a different concept): the standard is that the allocation should happen as soon as possible after receipt. The cashier should not hold the whole amount in client account or the whole amount in office account pending an instruction. If the split is unclear (for example, a payment that does not match any outstanding bill), the cashier should treat the whole amount as client money until the split is confirmed, then allocate when the information is available. The cashier documents the split and the basis for the allocation.

---

**Q: What does a legal cashier do about a residual client balance?**

When a matter closes but a balance remains in the client ledger (often because the client has been overpaid, a third-party payment did not clear, or a small balance was not swept at completion), the cashier's first duty is to identify it and flag it. The cashier then initiates the return process: contacting the client (or the relevant third party) and attempting to return the money. If the money cannot be returned (the client is unreachable, the amount is too small to transfer cost-effectively), the cashier maintains a central register of the balance and escalates to the COFA for a disposal decision. Under Rule 5.1(c) and SRA guidance, a balance of £500 or less per matter may be paid to a charity of the firm's choice without SRA authorisation (provided all conditions are met, including evidence of reasonable steps taken to return the money). For balances over £500, the cashier prepares the matter file for an SRA prior-written-authorisation application. The cashier does not make the disposal decision: the COFA or a manager authorises it.

---

**Q: When must a legal cashier escalate a potential breach?**

Immediately, and in writing. The cashier's duty is to recognise and escalate, not to investigate and resolve unilaterally. The trigger for escalation is a reasonable belief that a potential breach of the SRA Accounts Rules has occurred or is occurring: a withdrawal without a clear Rule 5 ground, an unresolved reconciliation difference, a prohibited banking-facility transaction (Rule 3.3), a transfer out of client account without a bill (Rule 4.3), or a receipt that cannot be identified and allocated. The cashier does not self-authorise a correction (for example, posting a contra-entry to close a difference). They flag the issue to the COFA with a written note of what they observed and when. The COFA then decides whether to investigate, correct, and whether to make a self-report to the SRA. A cashier who delays escalation or attempts to fix a breach without COFA involvement is compounding the risk to the firm's authorisation.
