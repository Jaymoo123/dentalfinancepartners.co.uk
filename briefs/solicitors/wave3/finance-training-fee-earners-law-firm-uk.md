# Finance Training for Fee Earners in UK Law Firms: Curriculum, Delivery and Measuring Behaviour Change

## Statutory anchor

This is a management and operational topic. Statutory anchors are deliberately light.

- **Training cost deductibility (ITTOIA 2005 s.34 / CTA 2009 s.54):** The "wholly and exclusively" test governs whether training expenditure is deductible in computing trading profits. ITTOIA 2005 s.34 (unincorporated firms) and CTA 2009 s.54 (companies) each carry the same heading: "Expenses not wholly and exclusively for trade and unconnected losses." Both are confirmed live at legislation.gov.uk. HMRC's position at BIM42526 ("Specific deductions: administration: own training courses", confirmed live) is that training which updates or enhances skills within the individual's existing business field is deductible revenue expenditure. BIM35660 ("Capital/revenue divide: intangible assets: proprietor's training courses", confirmed live) draws the capital/revenue line: training that refreshes existing competencies is revenue; training that creates a new qualification enabling an entirely new trade or business is capital and disallowed. Finance literacy training for fee earners who are already practising lawyers (understanding WIP, utilisation and lock-up to do their existing job better) sits comfortably within the deductible category. Do NOT state a blanket rule that all staff training is deductible without that nuance.
- **Apprenticeship Levy funding (HP §3.A):** A firm with a pay bill over £3,000,000 pays the levy at 0.5% and holds funds in its apprenticeship service account. The Level 7 solicitor apprenticeship (which incorporates the SQE) is fundable from that account. Finance literacy modules embedded within a formal apprenticeship programme can be funded via the levy. Government funding for Level 7 was restricted from January 2026; verify current eligibility before relying. Non-levy firms use government co-investment at 5% contribution. The levy is a deductible business cost (HP §3.A). This is an angle to raise when scoping training budgets: the apprenticeship funding line can offset external provider costs.
- **No further statutory pegs are needed.** Do not fabricate a regulatory mandate for fee-earner finance training. There is no SRA rule requiring it. The authority comes from operational best practice and the demonstrable link to lock-up reduction and profitability, not from a statute.

### Behaviour-change measurement examples (lock-up days before/after)

The writer must include worked examples of what measurement looks like in practice. Use these as the template; they are illustrative, not claimed as real case data. Frame them explicitly as illustrative.

**Example A: Time-recording completion rate**
Baseline (month 0): 62% of chargeable time entered within 24 hours across the litigation team (8 fee earners). After 3 months of embedded monthly sessions: 81%. Impact: average WIP age for the team fell from 34 days to 22 days. Lock-up contribution from that team (WIP days portion) reduced by 12 days.

**Example B: Days from time entry to bill**
Baseline: average 41 days from last time entry to bill raised on a completed matter (commercial property team, 5 associates). After the billing-narrative module and a billing-review protocol aligned to the month-end cycle: average 26 days. Direct lock-up reduction: 15 days on the debtor side of that team's position.

**Example C: WIP write-down rate**
Baseline: 18% of recorded WIP written off at billing across the personal injury team. After a billing-narrative quality session for all associates (not how to do PI, but how to record time in a way the billing partner can defend): write-down rate fell to 11%. At a blended hourly rate of £200, on 6,000 hours recorded per year, that 7-point improvement is worth approximately £84,000 in recovered billing value.

Writers: present these as "what good measurement looks like" rather than claimed client outcomes. Do not describe them as the firm's own data. They illustrate the measurement methodology, not a service guarantee.
