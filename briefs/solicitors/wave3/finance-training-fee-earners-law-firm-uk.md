# Finance Training for Fee Earners in UK Law Firms: Curriculum, Delivery and Measuring Behaviour Change

## Statutory anchor

This is a management and operational topic. Statutory anchors are deliberately light.

- **Training cost deductibility (ITTOIA 2005 s.34 / CTA 2009 s.54):** The "wholly and exclusively" test governs whether training expenditure is deductible in computing trading profits. HMRC's position (BIM42526 and BIM35660) is that training that updates or improves skills used in the existing trade is deductible; training that creates a new qualification enabling a new trade or business is capital and disallowed. Finance literacy training for fee earners who are already practising lawyers (understanding WIP, utilisation and lock-up to do their existing job better) sits comfortably within the deductible category. Do NOT state a blanket rule that all staff training is deductible without that nuance.
- **Apprenticeship Levy funding (HP §3.A):** A firm with a pay bill over £3,000,000 pays the levy at 0.5% and holds funds in its apprenticeship service account. The Level 7 solicitor apprenticeship (which incorporates the SQE) is fundable from that account. Finance literacy modules embedded within a formal apprenticeship programme can be funded via the levy. Government funding for Level 7 was restricted from January 2026; verify current eligibility before relying. Non-levy firms use government co-investment at 5% contribution. The levy is a deductible business cost (HP §3.A). This is an angle to raise when scoping training budgets: the apprenticeship funding line can offset external provider costs.
- **No further statutory pegs are needed.** Do not fabricate a regulatory mandate for fee-earner finance training. There is no SRA rule requiring it. The authority comes from operational best practice and the demonstrable link to lock-up reduction and profitability, not from a statute.

## Key questions this page must answer (8)

1. **Why do fee earners resist finance training, and how do you frame it so they engage?** (Personal benefit angle: a fee earner who understands their utilisation target and lock-up position is better placed at appraisal and partnership consideration; it is not firm surveillance, it is career intelligence.)

2. **What should trainees and NQs learn, versus associates, versus senior associates and salaried partners, versus equity partners?** (Role-segmented curriculum: the trainee needs to understand time-recording accuracy and what a WIP write-off means; the equity partner needs to read a P&L, interpret lock-up days and understand basis-period cash-flow implications. One-size training fails because the stakes and the levers differ.)

3. **What are the practical session formats and what does evidence say about what works for lawyers?** (Live workshops, lunch-and-learns, short asynchronous modules, 1:1 finance reviews with the COFA or external accountant, peer cohort sessions. Lawyers respond to case-based problem-solving; didactic lectures on accounting principles score poorly. Cadence: an annual one-day event does not change behaviour; monthly micro-sessions embedded in team meetings do.)

4. **How do you structure a 12-month curriculum from scratch?** (Phased rollout: months 1-3 foundations for each grade, months 4-9 application sessions tied to the billing cycle, month 10-12 consolidation and measurement. The page should provide a table or worked skeleton the reader can adapt.)

5. **How does the training programme connect to the billing and matter-review cycle?** (Training isolated from operations fails. Finance literacy sessions should be timed to the end-of-month billing review, the quarterly management accounts cycle, and the partner appraisal calendar. The link to billing-discipline-end-of-quarter-uk-law-firms is mandatory here.)

6. **How do you measure behaviour change rather than training attendance?** (Attendance and satisfaction scores are vanity metrics. Leading indicators: time-recording completion rates, average days between time entry and billing, WIP age profile by fee earner, lock-up days by team. The management accounts page has the monthly reporting framework; this page says what to track pre- and post-training and how to attribute change.)

7. **Can the firm use the Apprenticeship Levy to fund formal finance modules?** (Yes, where levy funds are available and the programme is structured as an accredited apprenticeship component. HP §3.A applies. Mention the January 2026 Level 7 funding restriction and the need to verify current eligibility with the ESFA / apprenticeship service.)

8. **What is the typical build-vs-buy decision, and what does external finance training for law firms actually cost in the UK?** (Faceless: no pricing on this site. The page should describe the decision framework: internal COFA-led sessions vs external specialist (law firm accountancy firms, the Law Management Section, LexisNexis training, Big 4 advisory) vs blended. Cost is a private matter; frame around what the firm gets for each model, not quotes. The apprenticeship funding line can offset external cost significantly for levy-paying firms.)
