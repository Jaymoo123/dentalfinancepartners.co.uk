## Statutory anchors (verified at primary source)

### 1. Matrimonial Causes Act 1973 s.25 (VERIFIED legislation.gov.uk 2026-07-09)

Section 25(1): the court must "have regard to all the circumstances of the case", first consideration to children under 18. Section 25(2)(a): the court shall have regard to "the income, earning capacity, property and other financial resources which each of the parties to the marriage has or is likely to have in the foreseeable future." A business or shareholding is a "financial resource" and falls within s.25(2)(a); it must be disclosed on Form E (Part 2.4 for business interests / Part 2.5 for shareholdings). s.25A (clean-break duty) is engaged where a capital settlement replaces periodical payments: a business-heavy asset pool raises the liquidity question of whether a lump sum can be paid without a forced sale.

**HP anchor:** MCA 1973 s.25 / s.25A (verified legislation.gov.uk 2026-07-09).

### 2. FPR 2010 Part 25: Single Joint Expert (VERIFIED justice.gov.uk 2026-07-09)

Part 25 (applying to applications issued on or after 6 April 2022) governs expert evidence in family proceedings:
- Rule 25.11(1): the court may direct a **single joint expert (SJE)** where multiple parties wish to put expert evidence before the court on an issue.
- Rule 25.12(1): instructions to an SJE must be in a **jointly agreed letter** unless the court directs otherwise.
- Rule 25.10(2): written questions to an expert may be put **once only**, within **10 days** of report service, for clarification only.
- Parties are **jointly and severally liable** for the SJE's fees and expenses.
- **Practice Direction 25B** governs the form of the expert's written report and the expert's duties to the court.

In financial remedy proceedings, an SJE (typically a chartered accountant or chartered business valuer) is the norm. Dual-expert appointments require court permission and are rare outside highly contested proceedings.

**HP anchor:** FPR 2010 Part 25 / PD 25B (verified justice.gov.uk 2026-07-09).

### 3. TCGA 1992 s.58: post-separation no-gain/no-loss window (VERIFIED legislation.gov.uk 2026-07-09)

Finance (No. 2) Act 2023 substituted TCGA 1992 s.58 with subsections (1A)-(1D), effective 6 April 2023:
- **(1A)-(1C):** no-gain/no-loss treatment for transfers between separated spouses/civil partners runs to the **earlier of the end of the third tax year after the tax year of separation, or the date of the divorce/dissolution order**.
- **(1D):** transfers made **in accordance with a formal divorce agreement or court order** qualify for no-gain/no-loss treatment **without the three-year time limit**.
- In both cases the transferee takes the transferor's **base cost**: the latent gain travels with the asset.

**HP anchor:** TCGA 1992 s.58(1A)-(1D) (F(No.2)A 2023, from 6 Apr 2023) verified legislation.gov.uk 2026-07-09. Cross-ref house_positions §9.A.

## Case-law anchors (Stage 2 verification status)

### Versteegh v Versteegh [2018] EWCA Civ 1050 — VERIFIED caselaw.nationalarchives.gov.uk 2026-07-09

The Court of Appeal confirmed key propositions about business valuation in matrimonial proceedings:
1. Where a business contains speculative development projects with "extremely subjective and variable" variables, a judge may conclude that a precise valuation is unreliable rather than adopting a conservative estimate.
2. "Valuations are often a matter of opinion on which experts differ"; pursuing precision through exhaustive investigation can become disproportionately expensive without providing reliable guidance.
3. Small adjustments to timing assumptions can create dramatic value swings (in the case, deferring projected sales by one year reduced one asset's valuation from £45.7m to £33.8m), demonstrating the fragility of earnings-forecast-dependent approaches.
4. Where historical management projections have proved chronically inaccurate, relying on future projections for valuation becomes unprincipled.
5. Where valuation is genuinely unreliable, transferring shares to the receiving spouse (so both parties participate in future outcomes) can be a principled alternative to fixing a number.

**Use in brief:** Versteegh is the verified authority for the "fragility" proposition: valuations of businesses with speculative or forecast-dependent value are inherently uncertain, and courts may reject a precise figure where the underlying assumptions are too variable.

### White v White [2000] UKHL 54 — VERIFIED per house_positions §9.A (verified cite)

The House of Lords established the **sharing principle**: matrimonial assets built up during the marriage should in principle be divided equally absent good reason to depart. The yardstick of equality does not mandate a 50:50 split, but a departure from it requires justification. This principle directly constrains how a minority discount is applied in financial remedy proceedings: a court may resist applying a full commercial minority discount to a matrimonial-asset shareholding because the sharing principle pushes against a mechanical deduction on assets built up during the marriage.

In Clarke v Clarke the court rejected a 20% minority shareholding discount, finding that the shareholder would likely sell together with the majority shareholders rather than suffer a discounted price on a standalone minority sale. ## Latent CGT worked example (binding on writer — include in "Tax on business assets" section)

**Scenario:** shares in a private trading company have a current market value of **£800,000**. The spouse who owns the shares acquired them at a base cost (including subscription price and any further contributions) of **£100,000**. The latent gain is £700,000.

**Option A: Transfer within the s.58 window (no-gain/no-loss)**

The shares are transferred to the receiving spouse within three tax years of separation (or under a formal divorce order). TCGA 1992 s.58(1A)-(1D) applies: the transfer is treated as producing no gain and no loss. The receiving spouse takes the transferor's base cost of £100,000. If the receiving spouse later sells the shares for £800,000, the gain is £700,000.

Tax on that gain (assuming no BADR; standard CGT rates per house_positions §9):
- Annual exempt amount: £3,000 (2026/27)
- Chargeable gain: £697,000
- CGT at 18% (basic-rate band element) + 24% (above): the exact split depends on the receiving spouse's other income. In a worst-case scenario (all above basic rate): £697,000 x 24% = **£167,280 CGT payable on a later sale**.

**Option B: Transfer outside the s.58 window (or after divorce order where formal-order route not used)**

The transfer is treated as a disposal at market value (£800,000) by the transferring spouse. CGT is immediately in point on the latent gain of £700,000. The transferring spouse faces:
- Chargeable gain: £697,000 (after annual exempt amount)
- CGT (worst case, no BADR): £697,000 x 24% = **£167,280 CGT due on the settlement itself**, before the receiving spouse has received anything.

**Advisory point for the brief:** the headline settlement value of £800,000 and the net-of-tax value diverge materially. The solicitor must model both options. Where the divorce order can be timed to fall within the s.58 window, the latent CGT is deferred to the receiving spouse's future disposal rather than crystallising immediately. The receiving spouse, however, should understand they are receiving a £800,000 asset carrying an embedded £167,280 future tax liability: the net receipt is effectively £632,720 if the shares are sold at current value.

**BADR note:** BADR is unlikely to be available to a receiving spouse on a later disposal unless they also work in the business and independently satisfy the qualifying conditions (5% ordinary shares, 5% voting rights, officer/employee status, 2-year holding). Do not assume BADR applies.

## Minority discount illustration (binding on writer — include in "Minority interests" section)

**Scenario:** the matrimonial asset is a 25% shareholding in a private trading company valued on an enterprise-value basis at £2,000,000 (100% value). The husband's 25% pro-rata share would be £500,000.

**Commercial minority discount approach:** a 25% minority shareholder cannot force a sale, declare a dividend unilaterally, or block strategic decisions (assuming no pre-emption or drag-along rights). A commercial buyer of a 25% minority stake would apply a discount reflecting illiquidity and lack of control, typically 20% to 40% depending on the company's constitution. At 30%: **£500,000 x (1 - 0.30) = £350,000**.

**Court's approach in financial remedy proceedings:** the court is not bound to apply a full commercial minority discount. Under the sharing principle (White v White [2000] UKHL 54), matrimonial assets built up during the marriage should in principle be divided equally. A mechanical minority discount on an asset the couple built together may be resisted. In Clarke v Clarke [2022] EWHC 2698 (Fam), the court rejected a 20% discount because the shareholder would likely sell alongside majority shareholders, reducing the practical illiquidity.

**Practical range for the brief:** the expert will produce a range; the realistic outcome in financial remedy proceedings may be closer to a 10%-20% discount rather than the 30%-40% a commercial buyer would apply. The solicitor's role is to understand the range and challenge a discount that is not supported by the company's constitution and shareholder dynamics.

**Q8. Can the court override an expert's valuation?**

The court is not bound by an expert's valuation in financial remedy proceedings. The expert's report is evidence, not a determination. The court will scrutinise the methodology, the assumptions and the range of values in the context of the s.25 factors. Where the expert has applied assumptions the court finds unrealistic (for example, a DCF based on management projections that have historically proved unreliable), the court may depart from the expert's figure or decline to value the business at all, instead transferring shares to the receiving spouse so both parties share in the future outcome. Versteegh v Versteegh [2018] EWCA Civ 1050 is authority for the proposition that a judge is not required to adopt an expert's figure where the underlying assumptions are too speculative to produce a reliable result.

**Q9. What happens if the parties disagree with the single joint expert's report?**

Under FPR 2010 Rule 25.10(2), each party may put written questions to the SJE, but only once and within 10 days of the report being served, and only for the purpose of clarifying the report. The answers form part of the expert's evidence. Parties cannot commission a shadow expert without court permission. If a party wishes to challenge the SJE's methodology or assumptions, the appropriate route is written questions (and, at the FDR or final hearing, submissions on the weight to be given to the report) rather than a separate expert report. A court may give permission for a party to instruct their own expert where the SJE's report is genuinely deficient, but this is expensive and courts are reluctant to permit it.

**Q10. What is the s.25 test for financial resources in a financial remedy order?**

Section 25(2)(a) of the Matrimonial Causes Act 1973 requires the court to have regard to "the income, earning capacity, property and other financial resources which each of the parties to the marriage has or is likely to have in the foreseeable future." A business interest is a "financial resource" within s.25(2)(a) and must be included in the matrimonial pot. The court assesses the net value of the business interest (after any deductions for tax, minority discount or other adjustments) in the context of the other assets, the parties' needs and the clean-break obligation under s.25A. A business valuation is the mechanism by which the court quantifies the financial resource: without a figure, the s.25 exercise cannot be completed.

**Q11. How long does a business valuation take in financial remedy proceedings?**

A business valuation in financial remedy proceedings typically takes four to eight weeks from the date the SJE has access to all necessary documents. The timeline depends on the complexity of the business (a simple sole-trader enterprise may take less time; a multi-entity group with several years of accounts, management information and pension assets may take longer), the responsiveness of the business owner in providing documents, and whether the expert requires access to management for interview. The solicitor should build the valuation timeline into the directions timetable from the First Appointment: a valuation that is not ready before the FDR appointment adds cost and delay. The jointly agreed letter of instruction should specify a deadline for the report.

**Q12. Does the receiving spouse pay CGT when they later sell shares transferred in a divorce settlement?**

Yes, in most cases. Where shares are transferred within the TCGA 1992 s.58 no-gain/no-loss window (under Finance (No. 2) Act 2023: up to the end of the third tax year after the year of separation, or under a formal court order or divorce agreement without time limit), the receiving spouse takes the transferring spouse's base cost. When the receiving spouse later sells the shares, CGT is charged on the gain from that base cost to the sale proceeds. At current rates (18% within the basic-rate band, 24% above for individuals per house_positions §9, 2026/27), a large latent gain can significantly reduce the net value of the asset received. Business Asset Disposal Relief (BADR at 18% from 6 April 2026) is unlikely to be available to a receiving spouse unless they independently satisfy the qualifying conditions, including officer or employee status and a 5% ordinary-share holding held for at least two years. The solicitor should always model the net-of-latent-CGT value when advising on a settlement that includes a share transfer.

When a client owns or co-owns a business, the family solicitor faces a valuation question before the financial remedy order can be negotiated or made. Section 25(2)(a) of the Matrimonial Causes Act 1973 requires the court to assess financial resources, and a privately-held business is a resource the court cannot price without expert evidence. This guide explains how business valuations work in financial remedy proceedings, what drives the number, and what to scrutinise when the expert's report arrives.

### Why business valuation matters in financial remedy proceedings

- The s.25(2)(a) financial resources question: the court cannot assess the matrimonial pot without a value for any privately-held business interest. Form E (Parts 2.4 and 2.5) requires disclosure of business interests and shareholdings, supported by three years' accounts, any existing valuations and any shareholder agreements.
- The clean-break pressure (s.25A MCA 1973): if the business is the main asset, the respondent must either transfer other assets of equivalent value, pay a lump sum (requiring funding without selling the business) or agree a structured/deferred payment. The illiquidity problem is central to every business-asset case.

### The single joint expert: appointment and instructions (FPR Part 25)

- When is an SJE directed? Typically at the First Appointment or a subsequent directions hearing.
- The jointly agreed letter of instruction: specify the valuation basis (going-concern? break-up? minority?), the date of valuation, access required (management accounts, board minutes, management interview), specific issues (minority interest, deferred consideration, pension in business, key-person dependency).
- The SJE's overriding duty is to the court, not to either party (PD 25B).
- Written questions: one round only, within 10 days of report service, for clarification only (FPR Rule 25.10(2)).
- Joint and several fee liability: both parties are liable for the SJE's costs; the party less able to fund their share may seek a costs direction at the First Appointment.

### Business valuation methodologies

Include the methods table (see above). Then prose expanding each method:

#### Earnings-based approach

The most common method for trading SMEs. The expert identifies **normalised maintainable earnings** (stripping out owner's remuneration above or below market rate, one-off costs, related-party transactions) and applies an **earnings multiple** derived from sector comparable transactions and the company's specific risk profile (size, growth, customer concentration, management depth). The resulting value represents what a willing buyer would pay for the business as a going concern.

The Versteegh point: where the business's earnings are forecast-dependent and management projections have proved unreliable historically, even an earnings-multiple approach becomes fragile (Versteegh v Versteegh [2018] EWCA Civ 1050). A court may give less weight to a valuation built on contested assumptions.

#### Net assets approach

Used where the business is asset-heavy (property portfolios, investment companies) or where the earnings approach produces an anomalous result. The expert values the underlying assets independently (a surveyor's report may be needed for property) and deducts liabilities. For a property-holding company, the net asset value and the latent CGT on an eventual sale of the underlying properties are both material to the settlement.

#### Discounted cash flow (DCF)

Converts future projected free cash flows into a current capital value using a discount rate that reflects the riskiness of those cash flows. Used for high-growth or project-stage businesses where a track-record earnings multiple is unavailable. Versteegh [2018] EWCA Civ 1050 specifically addressed DCF fragility: deferring projected sales by one year reduced one asset's valuation from £45.7m to £33.8m, illustrating that small timing assumptions drive large value swings.

#### Dividend yield approach

Values a minority shareholding by reference to the historical dividend stream and a comparable yield. Limited to established businesses with a consistent dividend history and a quoted peer-group yield to benchmark against. Rarely the primary method for closely-held SMEs.

### Minority interests and discounts

- Where the matrimonial asset is a **minority shareholding** (less than 50%, especially below 25%), the expert will typically apply a **minority discount**: a deduction reflecting the shareholder's inability to force a sale, declare dividends unilaterally or control strategy.
- Commercial minority discounts typically range from 20% to 40%, depending on the size of the interest, the company's constitution, drag-along and tag-along rights, and the realistic exit options.
- Include the minority discount illustration (see above).
- The court's approach: White v White [2000] UKHL 54 established the sharing principle; a court may resist a full commercial minority discount on a matrimonial-asset shareholding built up during the marriage. In Clarke v Clarke [2022] EWHC 2698 (Fam), the court rejected a 20% discount because the shareholder was likely to sell alongside the majority. The discount is a judgment call, not a formula.
- Shareholder agreement provisions (pre-emption rights, drag, put options) are material and should be attached to the Form E disclosure.

### Liquidity versus value: the implementation gap

- A valuation figure does not equate to cash in hand. The business-asset case turns on how the value can be realised.
- Options for the respondent: (a) pay a lump sum (requires external funding or extraction from the business); (b) transfer other assets of equivalent value; (c) a deferred lump sum order with phased payment; (d) dividend extraction over time (subject to income tax at dividend rates; see house_positions §3; the receiving spouse who is not a shareholder cannot extract dividends directly).
- The solicitor's role: model whether the settlement number can be realised, on what timescale, and at what tax cost.

### Tax on business assets in financial remedy settlements

#### CGT: the s.58 no-gain/no-loss window (FA 2023 change, effective 6 April 2023)

Include the latent CGT worked example (see above). Key points:
- The extended window: up to end of third tax year after separation, or under a formal court order or divorce agreement without time limit (TCGA 1992 s.58(1A)-(1D), F(No.2)A 2023).
- The transferee takes the base cost: latent CGT deferred, not forgiven.

- Dividend extraction from a company: income tax at dividend rates (10.75% / 35.75% / 39.35% from 6 April 2026, £500 dividend allowance; house_positions §3). Only a shareholder can extract dividends.

### Preparing the client for Form E disclosure

- Three years' accounts, management accounts, shareholders' agreements, any existing valuations.
- Common failure: a "director's estimate" on Form E followed by a materially different expert figure. Advise clients against understating or guessing values on Form E.
- Privilege: jointly agreed SJE instructions are not privileged. A party's own expert report (if permitted) may retain privilege unless relied upon, but courts will generally require disclosure of any expert evidence before relying on it.

## FAQ (all 12, for writer)

### Q1. What is a single joint expert in financial remedy proceedings?

A single joint expert (SJE) is an expert witness appointed by both parties together, on the court's direction or by consent, to provide evidence on a particular issue to the court. In financial remedy proceedings, an SJE is typically a chartered accountant or chartered business valuer instructed to value a business or shareholding. The SJE's overriding duty is to the court, not to either party, and their instructions must be contained in a jointly agreed letter under FPR 2010 Rule 25.12(1). Both parties are jointly and severally liable for the SJE's fees.

### Q2. How is a private company valued in divorce proceedings?

A private company in divorce proceedings is typically valued by a single joint expert using one or more of the following methods: an earnings multiple (applying a sector multiple to normalised maintainable earnings), a net asset approach (valuing the underlying assets and deducting liabilities), or a discounted cash flow where no earnings track record exists. The expert produces a range of values; the court then applies the s.25(2)(a) Matrimonial Causes Act 1973 framework to determine what weight to give to the valuation as part of the financial resources assessment.

### Q3. What is a minority discount and how does it apply to shares in a divorce settlement?

A minority discount is a reduction applied to the pro-rata value of a minority shareholding to reflect the shareholder's lack of control, inability to force a sale or declare dividends, and the reduced pool of buyers for a minority stake. Commercial minority discounts typically run from 20% to 40%. In financial remedy proceedings, courts may resist applying a full commercial discount to shares built up during the marriage, because the sharing principle (White v White [2000] UKHL 54) favours equal division of matrimonial assets. The discount is a matter of expert judgment and judicial discretion, not a formula.

### Q4. Can my client transfer business assets to their spouse without paying CGT?

Transfers of business assets or shares between spouses or civil partners within the TCGA 1992 s.58 no-gain/no-loss window (as extended by Finance (No. 2) Act 2023 from 6 April 2023) do not trigger CGT at the time of transfer. The window runs to the earlier of the end of the third tax year after the tax year of separation, or the date of the divorce order; transfers under a formal court order or divorce agreement qualify without time limit. The receiving spouse takes the transferor's base cost, so latent CGT on any future disposal remains a real cost. Timing the consent order within the window matters.

### Q5. What is the difference between a valuation for settlement and a valuation for sale?

A valuation for settlement in financial remedy proceedings is a going-concern opinion produced by an expert for the court, reflecting what a willing buyer would pay in an orderly transaction. A valuation for sale is a live market exercise reflecting actual buyer appetite, due diligence findings and negotiation. A settlement valuation is inherently more uncertain: it is a point-in-time opinion, the business is not actually being marketed, and there is no transaction to test the assumptions. Courts recognise this gap (Versteegh v Versteegh [2018] EWCA Civ 1050) and may allow a wider range of values than a vendor would accept in a real sale process.

### Q6. How does illiquidity affect a business valuation in financial remedy proceedings?

Illiquidity affects both the value of the asset and the implementation of any settlement based on that value. On valuation, a minority discount partly reflects illiquidity (a minority stake in a private company is hard to sell at full value). On implementation, even a 100%-owned business may not be liquid: the respondent who must pay a lump sum based on the business value has to find cash without selling the business (by refinancing, extraction over time, or transferring other assets). Courts can make deferred lump sum orders to give the business owner time to extract value; the solicitor must model the cash-flow and tax cost of each extraction route.

### Q7. What documents does the expert need to value a business in divorce proceedings?

Typically: three years' statutory accounts (or five for larger businesses); recent management accounts; profit-and-loss budgets or forecasts; details of the director/owner's total remuneration package (salary, dividends, pension contributions, benefits) over the period; any shareholder agreements, articles of association, or share option schemes; details of any related-party transactions; information about key customers, contracts and key-person risk; and any existing valuations (for sale, for insurance or for internal purposes). The jointly agreed letter of instruction should specify the documents required and a deadline for the business owner to provide them.
