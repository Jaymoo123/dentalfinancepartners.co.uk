## Statutory anchors (verified at primary source by Stage 1)

### 1. Matrimonial Causes Act 1973 s.25 (VERIFIED at legislation.gov.uk)

Section 25(1) requires the court to "have regard to all the circumstances of the case", with first consideration given to the welfare of any child of the family under 18. Section 25(2) lists the specific factors the court must consider when exercising powers relating to the parties, including:

- s.25(2)(a): income, earning capacity, property and other **financial resources** which each party has or is likely to have in the foreseeable future. A business or shareholding is a "financial resource" and must appear on Form E (Part 2.4 or 2.5 for business interests/shares).
- s.25(2)(b): financial needs, obligations and responsibilities of each party.
- s.25(2)(h): the value to each party of any benefit (such as a pension) which would be lost on dissolution.

The business valuation feeds directly into s.25(2)(a): the court cannot assess the matrimonial pot without a figure for any privately-held business interest. Section 25A (clean-break duty) also matters: a capital settlement based on a business value requires the respondent to either make a cash transfer (raising the liquidity question) or transfer other assets of equivalent value.

**HP anchor:** MCA 1973 s.25 (verified at legislation.gov.uk 2026-07-09).

### 2. FPR 2010 Part 25: Single Joint Expert (VERIFIED at justice.gov.uk)

The Family Procedure Rules 2010 Part 25 governs expert evidence in family proceedings. Key points:

- Where two or more parties wish to put expert evidence before the court, the court may direct that evidence comes from a **single joint expert (SJE)**.
- Instructions to an SJE must be contained in a **jointly agreed letter** unless the court directs otherwise. If parties cannot agree instructions, the court determines them.
- Each party sending separate instructions must simultaneously copy all other parties.
- The parties are **jointly and severally liable** for the SJE's fees and expenses.
- The SJE's **overriding duty is to the court**, not to the instructing parties.
- Practice Direction 25B governs the form of the expert's written report.

In financial remedy proceedings, a business valuation is almost always conducted by an SJE (a chartered accountant or chartered business valuer) appointed by consent or on the court's direction at a First Appointment or subsequent directions hearing. Each party may appoint their own expert only with court permission; in practice the SJE model dominates because a contested dual-expert approach is expensive and the court tends to prefer a single figure with written questions under Part 25.

**HP anchor:** FPR 2010 Part 25 (verified at justice.gov.uk 2026-07-09). Note: the one-round written-questions rule (10 days from report service) and the court's power to direct expert discussions are confirmed.

### 3. TCGA 1992 s.58 post-separation CGT window (VERIFIED at legislation.gov.uk)

Where the settlement involves transferring a business asset or shares from one spouse to the other, CGT is a live issue. TCGA 1992 s.58 provides that transfers between spouses / civil partners who are still living together are treated as producing no gain and no loss (the transferee takes the transferor's base cost).

**Crucially, and this is a NEW HP LOCK NEEDED item:** Finance (No. 2) Act 2023 amended s.58 to extend the no-gain no-loss window to post-separation transfers. Under the amended s.58(1C), the no-gain no-loss treatment applies to transfers made on or before **the earlier of: (i) the last day of the third tax year after the tax year in which A and B ceased to live together, OR (ii) the day on which a court grants a divorce order**. This is a significant change from the pre-FA 2023 position where the window closed at the end of the tax year of separation. The change applies to disposals made on or after 6 April 2023.

This has direct relevance to financial remedy advice: a transfer of business assets or shares as part of the settlement order may qualify for no-gain no-loss treatment under the extended s.58 window, deferring any CGT until the transferee later disposes. However, the transferee takes the transferor's base cost, so the deferred gain may crystallise on a later disposal at the standard CGT rates (18% / 24% for individuals, per house_positions §9). The practical question is whether the transferee can afford the latent CGT on a future sale.

**NEW HP LOCK NEEDED:** TCGA 1992 s.58 as amended by FA 2023 (Finance (No. 2) Act 2023). The extension to three tax years post-separation for spousal asset transfers in divorce settlements. ### Wells v Wells and the sharing principle

The "sharing" principle in financial remedies (the proposition that matrimonial assets built up during the marriage should in principle be divided equally absent good reason to depart) derives from the House of Lords in White v White [2000] UKHL 54. "Wells v Wells" in the context of business valuation is sometimes cited loosely to mean the application of sharing to illiquid business assets; however the major Wells v Wells [2002] EWCA Civ 476 is more properly a case on the correct approach to the net effect of a Duxbury fund. Stage 2 should verify which Wells v Wells authority is intended (there are multiple cases of that name) before citing. If uncertain, the sharing principle can be anchored to White v White [2000] UKHL 54 which is unambiguous and widely recognised.

## Proposed page structure (H2/H3 outline)

### Introduction (no H2)
Brief orienting paragraph: when a client owns or co-owns a business, the family solicitor faces a valuation question before the financial remedy order can be negotiated or made. The section 25(1) exercise and the Form E both require a figure. This guide explains how valuations work, what drives them, and what to watch for.

### Why business valuation matters in financial remedy proceedings
- The s.25(2)(a) financial resources question: the court cannot assess the pot without a value.
- Form E requirements: where to disclose and what documents to attach (last 3 years' accounts, management accounts, shareholders' agreements, any existing valuations).
- The clean-break pressure (s.25A): if the business is the main asset, the respondent must either transfer other assets or borrow to pay a lump sum. The illiquidity problem.

### The single joint expert: appointment and instructions (FPR Part 25)
- When is an SJE directed? Typically at the First Appointment or on a subsequent directions order.
- Jointly agreed instructions: the letter of instruction should specify the valuation basis, the date of valuation, access to management, any specific issues (minority interest, deferred consideration, pension in the business).
- The SJE's duty to the court (not to either party): written questions allowed (one round, 10 days); no direct communication with only one party.
- Cost and liability: jointly and severally liable; who funds the valuation at the outset (important for clients who cannot fund their share).
- When dual experts are permitted (rare; usually contested litigation rather than FDR track).

### Business valuation methodologies

#### Earnings-based approaches
- Maintainable earnings / EBITDA multiple: the most common approach for trading businesses. Identify normalised maintainable earnings (strip out owner's remuneration above or below market rate, one-off items, related-party transactions). Apply an appropriate earnings multiple (driven by sector, size, growth, risk profile, listed comparable transactions). The expert will typically use a range of multiples and produce a range of values.
- The "fragility" point (derived from the Versteegh line of authority, subject to Stage 2 verification): valuations of owner-managed businesses are inherently uncertain because the business's value may depend entirely on the individual continuing to run it. A court-directed value is a point-in-time expert opinion, not a market price.

#### Net assets approach
- Relevant where the business is asset-heavy (property-holding, investment) or where the earnings approach produces an anomalous result.
- For a property business, the underlying property values drive the net asset figure; a surveyor's report is needed alongside the accountant's valuation.

#### Dividend yield / market comparables
- Less common in owner-managed SME context; more relevant where the business has a market-comparable quoted peer group.

### Minority interests and discounts
- Where the matrimonial-asset is a **minority shareholding** (less than 50%, and especially less than 25%), the expert will typically apply a **minority discount** to reflect the lack of control and the inability to force a sale or declare a dividend.
- Minority discounts range widely (typically 20-40% depending on the size of the interest, the company's constitution and any drag/tag rights). The discount is a valuation judgment, not a formula.
- The counterpoint: the court in financial remedy proceedings sometimes resists applying a full minority discount if the shares are matrimonial assets built up during the marriage (the sharing principle may push against a mechanical discount). This is a live area of judicial discretion.
- Shareholder agreement provisions (pre-emption rights, drag-along, put options) can affect both marketability and the appropriate discount.

### Liquidity versus value: the implementation gap
- The central tension in business-asset cases: a valuation figure does not equate to cash in hand.
- The respondent who must pay a lump sum from a business interest faces: (i) finding funding without selling the business; (ii) selling shares where a minority discount applies to any external buyer; (iii) a forced sale at an undervalue.
- Practical options: deferred lump sum order (Mesher-style in the business context), phased payment, transfer of other assets, use of business loan facilities, dividend extraction over time (subject to tax).
- Solicitor's role: advise the client on whether the number on paper can be realised and on what timescale. The CGT and income tax costs of extracting value must also be modelled (see below).

### Tax on business assets in financial remedy settlements

#### CGT and the s.58 no-gain no-loss window (FA 2023 change)
- Transfers of business assets or shares between spouses / civil partners within the extended s.58 window (up to the end of the third tax year after the year of separation, or the divorce order if earlier) are treated as no-gain no-loss: the transferee takes the transferor's base cost.
- The latent CGT point: the receiving spouse takes the base cost. A future disposal at market value will crystallise a gain. At current rates (18% / 24% for individuals, or 10%-18% under BADR if the transferee later qualifies), the latent CGT may substantially reduce the net receipt.
- Where the divorce order is made after the three-year window, the transfer is a disposal at market value and CGT is immediately in point. Timing the consent order to fall within the window matters.
- BADR is unlikely to be available to a receiving spouse on a later disposal unless they also work in the business and meet the conditions independently.
- Note: CGT rates cited here are per house_positions §9 (18% / 24% standard; BADR 18% from 6 April 2026). Always date-tag.

#### Income tax on extracting value
- Dividend extraction from a company: subject to income tax at dividend rates (10.75% / 35.75% / 39.35% from 6 April 2026, house_positions §3). A £500 dividend allowance.
- Salary from the business: PAYE; subject to employer secondary Class 1 NIC at 15% above the £5,000 secondary threshold (house_positions §3).
- The receiving spouse who is not a shareholder cannot extract dividends; the settlement must give them shares or a cash sum first.

### Preparing the client for Form E disclosure
- What the business valuation section of Form E requires: three years' accounts, explanation of the basis of any existing valuation, any shareholder agreement.
- Common failure: a rough "director's estimate" on Form E followed by a very different expert figure. Advise clients to disclose all accounts promptly and not to understate.
- Privilege and expert instructions: instructions to a single joint expert are not privileged (they are jointly produced); instructions to a party's own expert (if permitted) may retain privilege but must usually be disclosed if relied upon.

### Conclusion / lead call to action
Frame around the solicitor's need for specialist accounting support alongside legal advice; the business valuation is an accounting question, the solicitor's role is to instruct, scrutinise and translate the figure into a settlement framework.
