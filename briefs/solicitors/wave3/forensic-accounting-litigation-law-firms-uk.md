## 3. Proposed structure (H1 / H2 / H3 outline)

**H1:** Forensic Accounting in Litigation: A Guide for Instructing Solicitors

**H2: When to instruct a forensic accountant**
- Quantum claims (loss of earnings, business interruption, share/asset valuations)
- Fraud and asset-tracing matters
- Shareholder and partnership disputes
- Family proceedings: valuing the matrimonial asset pool (cross-ref A8)
- Estate and probate disputes
- The timing question: early instruction narrows scope creep; late instruction risks a narrow permission

**H2: CPR Part 35 — the expert's overriding duty**
- Rule 35.3(1): experts help the court, not the party
- Rule 35.3(2): that duty overrides any obligation to the instructing party or the paying party
- Rule 35.4(1): no expert evidence without court permission — apply in good time and include a cost estimate and the issues to be addressed (r35.4(2))
- Rule 35.4(4): the court may cap the recoverable amount of expert fees; instruct the expert to scope to the permitted issues
- Practical consequence: a forensic accountant who shades an opinion in the client's favour risks losing standing; the instructing solicitor's job is to select an expert who will hold their ground

**H2: Single joint experts — when the court directs one**
- Rule 35.7(1): the court may direct a single joint expert (SJE) where multiple parties seek evidence on the same issue
- Rule 35.8: any instructing party may send instructions; copies go simultaneously to other parties
- Rule 35.8(5): parties are jointly and severally liable for the SJE's fees and expenses unless the court directs otherwise
- Tactical considerations: an SJE narrows the parties' ability to build competing narratives; in a complex quantum dispute the parties may prefer party-appointed experts and argue the cost
- If the SJE's report is adverse, Part 35 written questions (below) are the primary lever before seeking permission to adduce party evidence

**H2: Part 35 written questions**
- Rule 35.6: a party may send written questions to any expert (party-appointed or SJE), limited to clarification, submitted within 28 days of the report, and proportionate in scope
- The answers form part of the expert's report (r35.6(3))
- Rule 35.6(4): failure to answer may trigger sanctions — the court can direct that the party may not rely on the evidence or that the relevant fees are not recoverable
- Practice point: draft Part 35 questions precisely; unfocused questions waste the expert's time (cost) and can antagonise the court

**H2: Scope of instruction — what a forensic accountant does in litigation**
- Quantum of loss: lost profits, diminution in value, wasted expenditure
- Business and share valuations (earnings/asset/market approach; minority discounts; see A8 for matrimonial context)
- Fraud and asset-tracing: cash-flow reconstructions, invoice analysis, lifestyle assessments
- Expert report: must comply with PD 35 (Practice Direction 35) requirements — statement of truth, overriding duty declaration, sources relied on, facts vs assumptions
- Joint statements: when experts are directed to meet without the parties' solicitors and produce a statement of agreed and disputed issues; the solicitor's role before and after

**H2: Fee and VAT treatment of expert fees — the instructing firm's position**

### Disbursement or firm cost?
Apply the eight conditions in VAT Notice 700 §25.1.1 (HMRC VTAXPER39000) — all eight must be met:

1. The firm acted as the client's agent when paying the third party (the expert)
2. The client received and used the services
3. The client was responsible for paying the expert
4. The client authorised the payment
5. The client knew the expert would be provided by a third party
6. The outlay is separately itemised on the firm's invoice
7. The firm recovers only the exact amount paid to the expert
8. The expert's services are clearly additional to the firm's own legal supply

Where all eight are met, the expert's fee is a **disbursement**: the firm passes it through at cost, outside the scope of VAT (no VAT on the recharge). Where any condition fails — for example, where the firm negotiates a volume rate or adds a handling margin — it becomes a component of the firm's standard-rated supply and VAT applies on the recharge.

**Alignment with §6.B counsel analysis:** The same principal question applies as for counsel's fees (HP §6.B). In the usual litigation model, where the firm selects the expert, shapes the instruction, and uses the report in building its case, the expert is likely a supply to the firm rather than a direct agent of the client. In practice, whether the fee is a disbursement turns on the eight conditions, applied to the specific instruction. Contrast: where the client contracts directly with the expert (some SJE arrangements) the agency condition is more readily met.

**VAT on the expert's own invoice:** If the forensic accountant is VAT-registered, their fee note to the firm will carry 20% VAT. The firm recovers that as input tax (provided it makes taxable supplies — standard-rated legal services, §6). On the recharge to the client, if treated as a disbursement, the exact gross amount (inclusive of the expert's VAT) is passed through; if treated as a cost component of the firm's supply, output VAT is charged on the total, and the firm recovers the expert's VAT as input tax.

**H2: Recoverability of expert fees as costs**
- CPR Rule 44.1(1): "costs" includes fees, charges, disbursements and expenses — expert fees are within the costs regime
- Rule 44.2(2): the general rule is that costs follow the event (unsuccessful party pays)
- Rule 44.3(2): on the standard basis only proportionate costs are allowed; expert fees must bear a reasonable relationship to the matters in issue (r44.3(5))
- Rule 35.4(4): the court may cap the recoverable amount of expert fees — keep the expert's scope tight and obtain costs-budget approval in advance (Costs Management Order)
- Practice point: expert fees not included in an approved costs budget (where costs management applies) are at risk of disallowance. The solicitor should update the Precedent H whenever the scope of expert instruction changes
- Inter-partes recovery and VAT: where the receiving party's client can recover the input VAT, the inter-partes bill claims the expert's fee NET of VAT; where the client cannot recover VAT, the bill claims the VAT-inclusive amount. The VAT position on recovered costs follows HP §6.G

**H2: Selecting and managing the expert — practical checklist**
- Independence first: the expert must be able to perform the overriding duty without restriction
- Narrow the issues: agree a written letter of instruction that maps to the court's permission; wider instructions inflate cost and risk costs-capping
- Fee estimate at instruction: obtain a written estimate for court-budget purposes; a forensic scope can expand rapidly once disclosure arrives
- Privilege in draft reports: draft expert reports prepared for the dominant purpose of litigation are protected by litigation privilege (Three Rivers No. 6); the solicitor should not volunteer drafts in disclosure
- Joint meetings: agree the agenda with opposing solicitors in advance; under PD 35 the expert must not be given instructions that restrict what they can agree with the opposing expert

## 8. Stage 2 task resolutions

### PD 35 mandatory report contents (WebFetch confirmed — justice.gov.uk/courts/procedure-rules/civil/rules/part35/pd_part35, 2026-07-09)

Practice Direction 35 paragraph 3.2 requires an expert's report to:

1. Give details of the expert's qualifications (3.2(1))
2. Give details of any literature or other material relied on (3.2(2))
3. Contain a statement setting out the substance of all facts and instructions material to the opinions expressed (3.2(3))
4. Make clear which facts stated are within the expert's own knowledge (3.2(4))
5. State who carried out any examination, measurement, test or experiment, and their qualifications (3.2(5))
6. Summarise the range of opinion on the matters dealt with and give reasons for the expert's own opinion (3.2(6))
7. Contain a summary of conclusions (3.2(7))
8. Contain a statement of any qualifications to the expert's opinion (3.2(8))
9. Confirm that the expert understands their duty to the court and has complied with it (3.2(9)(a))

Paragraph 3.3 requires a statement of truth in the prescribed form.

**Facts vs assumptions:** Condition 3.2(4) ("make clear which facts are within the expert's own knowledge") and 3.2(3) (the substance of all material facts and instructions) together create the facts-vs-instructions-vs-assumptions structure. A forensic accountant must flag which figures are taken from the client's records, which from agreed instructions, and which from assumptions (e.g. a growth-rate assumption for lost-profits quantification).

## 12. Worked examples (Stage 2)

### 12A. Quantum-of-loss expert: fee and VAT walk-through under HP §6.K (party-appointed route)

**Scenario:** A solicitor's firm (VAT-registered, standard-rated supplies) instructs a forensic accountant as party-appointed expert on a business-interruption quantum claim. The expert invoices the firm £10,000 + VAT (£2,000), total £12,000. The firm then recharges the client.

**Step 1 — who received the supply?**
The firm contracted with the expert, selected them, shaped the instruction letter, and will use the report in its own case strategy. Under HP §6.K the default is a supply to the firm, not a disbursement. Condition 1 (firm acted as client's agent) is not met because the firm selected and instructed the expert independently.

**Step 2 — VAT on the expert's invoice**
The expert's £2,000 VAT is input tax for the firm. Firm recovers £2,000 as input tax (fully taxable firm, no partial exemption issue).

**Step 3 — recharging the client (party-appointed: supply-to-firm route)**
Because it is a component of the firm's supply (not a disbursement), the firm adds output VAT to the recharge. Firm recharges £12,000 to the client and charges output VAT on that £12,000 = £2,400 output VAT. Total to client: £14,400.

**Net VAT position for firm:** output VAT £2,400 minus input VAT £2,000 = £400 net VAT paid to HMRC (the firm effectively VATs only its handling/margin element).

**Step 4 — if all eight conditions ARE met (disbursement route)**
If, exceptionally, the client contracted directly with the expert and authorised the firm to pay on their behalf, all eight VTAXPER39000 conditions may be met. Then: firm passes through £12,000 (including the expert's £2,000 VAT) at cost. No output VAT on the recharge. Firm claims £2,000 input VAT separately. Client is billed £12,000 with no VAT added by the firm on the recharge line.

**Practical note:** in most party-appointed expert instructions the supply-to-firm route applies. The disbursement route is unusual and requires the eight conditions to be satisfied on the specific facts.

### 12B. SJE fee-splitting example under CPR r35.8(5)

**Scenario:** Court directs a single joint expert on a share-valuation issue. Two parties (Claimant and Defendant). SJE invoices £8,000 + VAT (£1,600), total £9,600.

**Who pays?**
Under CPR r35.8(5) both parties are jointly and severally liable unless the court directs otherwise. If no costs direction, each party is liable for the full £9,600 (but in practice the instruction letter usually allocates the split: 50:50 = each £4,800 inclusive).

**VAT treatment under HP §6.K (SJE route)**
Because the court directed the SJE and both parties are directly liable (r35.8(5)), the "client was responsible for paying the third party" condition (VTAXPER39000 condition 3) is materially easier to satisfy. If the firm's client contracted directly as part of the court order and the firm merely paid on their behalf, disbursement treatment is a stronger (but still facts-dependent) case.

**Inter-partes recovery (HP §6.G):**
- If the receiving party's client is VAT-registered and can recover input VAT: the inter-partes bill claims the SJE fee NET of VAT (e.g. £4,000).
- If the receiving party's client cannot recover VAT (e.g. an individual): the bill claims the VAT-inclusive amount (e.g. £4,800) and the paying party bears the VAT.

**Costs cap risk (r35.4(4)):** if the court capped recoverable expert fees at permission stage (e.g. limited to £6,000 total), each party recovers only up to their proportionate share of the cap, regardless of what the SJE actually charged. Instruction letters should reference any costs cap and require the SJE to notify parties before exceeding the permitted amount.

## 13. FAQ full answers (questions 8–12)

### FAQ 8: Does an expert's report need to be included in the costs budget?

Yes, where costs management applies (Costs Management Order under CPR Part 3), the expert's fees must be included in the Precedent H budget and approved before they are incurred. Expert fees not included in an approved costs budget are at risk of disallowance on assessment, because the court's approval of a phase budget is the trigger for the costs being "in budget" and therefore recoverable on the standard basis without re-justification. The solicitor should update the Precedent H whenever the scope of instruction changes (for example, when disclosure reveals additional issues requiring expanded forensic analysis). A failure to update the budget before incurring additional expert costs is a common and preventable recovery risk. Where a costs management order is not in place (cases below the CMO threshold), expert fees still need to be proportionate under r44.3(2) but are not subject to budget approval.

### FAQ 9: What must a CPR-compliant expert report contain (PD 35 requirements)?

Under Practice Direction 35 paragraph 3.2 (verified at justice.gov.uk 2026-07-09), a compliant report must:

- State the expert's qualifications (3.2(1)).
- List all literature and materials relied on (3.2(2)).
- Set out the substance of all material facts and instructions (3.2(3)).
- Make clear which facts are within the expert's own knowledge and which are taken from instructions or records (3.2(4)).
- State who carried out any test or examination and their qualifications (3.2(5)).
- Summarise the range of opinion on each issue and give reasons for the expert's own view (3.2(6)).
- Contain a summary of conclusions (3.2(7)).
- State any qualifications or caveats on the opinions (3.2(8)).
- Confirm the expert understands their duty to the court and has complied with it (3.2(9)(a)).

Paragraph 3.3 requires a statement of truth in a prescribed form. A forensic-accounting report that simply presents a single figure without addressing the range of opinion, or without distinguishing facts from assumptions, does not comply with PD 35 and is vulnerable to challenge.

### FAQ 10: Is litigation privilege lost if solicitors comment on a draft expert report?

Litigation privilege protects draft expert reports prepared for the dominant purpose of actual or reasonably contemplated litigation. The key issue with solicitor involvement in drafts is not privilege but the expert's duty of independence: under r35.3(2) the expert's duty to the court overrides any obligation to the instructing party. Solicitors may properly correct factual errors in a draft (the expert misidentified a date; a figure was misquoted from the financial statements), but instructing the expert to shade an opinion in the client's favour, or to omit an adverse range of opinion, risks compromising the expert's standing and, if revealed, is likely to result in costs sanctions or adverse commentary from the court.

On privilege: provided the drafts were prepared for litigation purposes, passing drafts to the solicitor to review factual accuracy does not destroy privilege. What can damage the expert's credibility, and should be avoided, is the solicitor effectively becoming the author of the expert's conclusions. Courts have criticised experts whose final reports tracked solicitor redlines closely. The safe practice is to limit written comments to factual corrections, confirm any input was disclosed in the instructions section of the final report (per PD 35 para 3.2(3)), and never suggest amendments to the expert's conclusions.

### FAQ 11: What happens if the opposing expert does not answer Part 35 written questions?

Under CPR r35.6(4), the court may make one or both of the following orders against the party whose expert has failed to answer: (a) direct that the party may not rely on the expert's evidence; (b) direct that the party may not recover the expert's fees from any other party. These are the primary sanctions, but the court retains its general case-management powers. In practice, before issuing an application, the instructing solicitor should write to the opposing party's solicitor setting a reasonable deadline for the answers (the rule requires answers within a reasonable time after the 28-day question period); if answers remain outstanding, the correct route is an unless order application. Draft Part 35 questions tightly: unfocused or disproportionate questions may be refused by the court and reflect poorly on the asking party.

### FAQ 12: In a family law case, does the forensic accountant have the same CPR Part 35 duties as in civil litigation?

In financial remedy proceedings (Family Court) the expert's duties are governed by the Family Procedure Rules 2010 (FPR 2010) Part 25 and Practice Direction 25B, not CPR Part 35 directly. However, the overriding duty to the court (r35.3 equivalent) is replicated in FPR Part 25: the expert's duty is to the court and overrides any obligation to the instructing party. FPR 2010 Part 25 also defaults to a single joint expert regime (joint instructions, simultaneously sent to both parties, joint and several fee liability). The practical difference for the forensic accountant is procedural: the report format, the PD 25B statement requirements, and the method of instruction differ, but the substantive independence obligation is the same. See A8 (business-valuation-for-family-lawyers-uk) for the full matrimonial-proceedings context, including the s.25 MCA 1973 factors, FDR dynamics, and minority-discount treatment.

### FAQ bonus: When do I need a forensic accountant, not my client's own accountant?

A client's own accountant is a witness of fact about what the business records show. A forensic accountant appointed as expert under CPR Part 35 gives opinion evidence: they apply professional judgment to quantify disputed losses, value assets, or trace diverted funds, and their duty is to the court, not the client. A client's accountant cannot perform this function in litigation: they are a partisan witness, their evidence is factual (subject to witness-statement rules, not expert-report rules), and they cannot give the independent range-of-opinion analysis that PD 35 requires. The practical triggers for forensic instruction are: a quantum dispute above a proportionate expert-cost threshold (often above £50,000 in dispute value); a fraud or asset-tracing allegation where reconstruction is needed; a share or business valuation that the client's own accountant prepared but which opposing counsel will argue is not independent; or a court-directed SJE where the parties need a neutral to perform the analysis.
