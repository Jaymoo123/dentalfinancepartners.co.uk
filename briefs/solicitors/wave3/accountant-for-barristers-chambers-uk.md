## 2. Proposed structure (H1 to H3 level)

**H1:** Accountant for Barristers and Chambers: UK Tax and VAT Guide

**H2: The self-employment baseline**
Barristers in independent practice (the Bar of England and Wales) are self-employed: they pay income tax on trading profits through self-assessment and Class 4 NIC. They are not employees of chambers. The Bar Standards Board (BSB) governs practice; HM Revenue and Customs governs tax. The two regimes are independent.

**H2: Income tax on barrister profits**
- Trading profits = brief fees and other professional receipts, less allowable expenses.
- Self-assessment: income tax at 20%/40%/45% on profit above the personal allowance (£12,570, tapered above £100,000 and lost at £125,140); payments on account 31 January and 31 July.
- Class 4 NIC: 6% on profits £12,570 to £50,270, 2% above (2026/27). Class 2 NIC: no longer payable from 6 April 2024 (National Insurance Contributions Act 2024); profits at or above the Small Profits Threshold are treated as having paid, protecting the state-pension record.

**H2: Cash basis or accruals?**
- **HP LOCK NEEDED (see §8):** The old ITTOIA 2005 s.160 barrister-specific "early years" cash basis was repealed by Finance Act 2013 Schedule 4 para 51 (effective 2013-14 onwards, with transitional protection for those already on the old basis for 2012-13 and earlier). Since repeal, no barrister-specific cash basis provision exists.
- From 6 April 2024, Finance Act 2024 made the **general cash basis the default** for unincorporated businesses (ITTOIA 2005 s.25 as amended; accruals now requires an election via ITTOIA 2005 s.25C). Barristers are unincorporated sole traders and are **not** in the excluded categories (the exclusions are: companies, LLPs, partnerships with corporate partners, Lloyd's underwriters, herd-basis elections, profit-averaging elections, business premises renovation allowance, mineral extraction, R&D allowances). A barrister **can therefore use the general cash basis by default** from 2024/25, subject to the normal cash-basis restrictions (no loss relief carry-back under the cash basis, interest deduction cap). Most established barristers with significant WIP will want to consider electing to accruals for better matching of income and expenses. **Flag for HP lock review.**
- Chambers (if they were an LLP or partnership with a corporate partner) would be excluded from cash basis; but barristers practice as sole traders, not as LLP members.

**H2: What expenses can a barrister deduct?**
- **Chambers contributions:** A barrister pays chambers for accommodation, clerks' fees, administrative services, library, IT and other overheads. These are **allowable trading expenses** deducted in computing taxable profits (ITTOIA 2005 s.34, wholly and exclusively for the purposes of the trade). Chambers itself is not a VAT-registered business making a supply to its members in the normal sense; the treatment of chambers contributions for VAT purposes is addressed in the VAT section below.
- **Pupillage / tenancy costs:** Pupillage fees paid by a pupil barrister are a cost of acquiring the qualification to practise, not an ongoing trade expense; they sit in the capital/pre-trading period. Once called to the Bar and in tenancy, ongoing expenses are deductible.
- **BMIF (Bar Mutual Indemnity Fund):** The Bar Mutual Indemnity Fund provides compulsory professional indemnity cover for practising barristers. The BMIF premium is an **allowable trading expense** (wholly and exclusively for the purpose of the profession, ITTOIA 2005 s.34). It is an insurance premium; the HMRC treatment follows standard PII deductibility. **Note:** BMIF is structured as a mutual (members' club), not a conventional insurer; the premium is still a deductible expense. A specific HP lock is not needed here but the writer should not overstate certainty about the VAT status of BMIF contributions without further verification.
- **Home office and chambers rooms:** Apportioned use-of-home costs (heat, light, broadband, a proportion of rent or mortgage interest) or costs of a separate home study are deductible on a just and reasonable basis.
- **Books, reports and subscriptions:** Practitioner texts, legal databases, and BSB/Bar Council subscriptions are fully allowable.
- **Barrister's wig and gown:** Allowable; specific protective/occupational clothing at the profession's requirement.
- **CPD, training and conferences:** Allowable if the training maintains or updates existing professional competence (not retraining into a new trade).
- **Travel:** Business travel (to court, conferences, client meetings) allowable at AMAP rates for car use (55p per mile from 6 April 2026, FA 2026; 45p previously). Ordinary commuting (chambers to home) is not allowable.
- **Equipment and technology:** Laptops, printers, robes bags, phone; AIA (£1,000,000 per year) and the 40% first-year allowance on new main-rate plant (Finance Act 2026 s.29, from 1 January 2026) are available to a self-employed barrister as an unincorporated business.
- **Spouse/partner wages:** Deductible if genuine work is performed at a market rate and actually paid (BIM37700+).
- **Disallowable:** Personal clothing, personal travel, costs of a failed application for silk (Queen's/King's Counsel), fines, interest on a personal tax loan (BIM45690 applies, same as in house_positions §3.A).

**H2: Pupillage, tenancy transition and the early-years cash-flow trap**
- A pupil barrister earns income (minimum pupillage award from 6-month second six) but may have minimal deductible expenses until in tenancy. Tax falls due on 31 January following the tax year end; the payments-on-account regime (50% on 31 January, 50% on 31 July) can create a first-year cash-flow shock.
- Once in tenancy, the barrister registers for VAT if taxable turnover exceeds £90,000 (registration threshold from 1 April 2024). Many busy junior barristers pass this threshold quickly.
- Basis period reform (Finance Act 2022): the tax-year basis from 2024/25 simplifies matters for barristers using a 5 April year-end (the norm for sole traders), but note any non-April-5 year-end caused a transitional year in 2023/24.

**H2: VAT on barrister's fees**
- A barrister whose taxable turnover exceeds **£90,000** (VATA 1994 Schedule 1) must register for VAT and charge 20% on professional fees. Brief fees and advisory fees are standard-rated taxable supplies. VAT on disallowable personal items cannot be reclaimed as input tax.
- **Tax point for barristers (VERIFIED):** The general VAT time-of-supply rules (VATA 1994 s.6) are **modified for barristers and advocates** by **VAT Regulations 1995 (SI 1995/2518) regulation 92**. Under reg 92, the tax point for a barrister's supply is the **earlier of: (a) receipt of the fee; (b) issue of a VAT invoice; or (c) the day the barrister ceases to practise.** This differs from the standard s.6 rule where the basic tax point is completion of the service. The practical effect: a barrister who has completed a case but has not been paid and has issued no VAT invoice has no tax point yet; the tax point arises on payment or invoice, whichever comes first. This is important for cash-flow planning and has long historical roots in barristers' practice of not suing for fees. **This is a locked verified fact; cite reg 92 exactly.**
- **Chambers cost-sharing and VAT:** Chambers typically makes no supply to its members for VAT purposes when collecting pooled contributions for shared costs (accommodation, clerks' wages, overheads). HMRC's longstanding position (reflected in VBARRISTERS guidance) is that the cost-sharing arrangements within chambers do not constitute a supply by chambers to its individual members where they are truly pooling and sharing costs rather than purchasing services from chambers as a separate entity. However, this is an area of complexity; if chambers is constituted as a separate entity (a management company, for example) making supplies to members, VAT may be chargeable. **This position requires a Stage 2 primary-source verification against the current VBARRISTERS manual or Notice 700A; flagged as NEW HP LOCK NEEDED.**
- **Overseas brief fees and place of supply:** Where a barrister advises an overseas client (whether an overseas solicitor, an overseas company, or an overseas government), the place-of-supply rules determine whether UK VAT is chargeable. Under VATA 1994 s.7A and the VAT (Place of Supply of Services) Order 1992, legal services supplied to a **business customer outside the UK** are generally **outside the scope of UK VAT** (the "B2B general rule": the place of supply is where the customer belongs). Where the overseas client is a private individual (B2C), the supply is made where the supplier belongs (the UK), making it UK-standard-rated at 20%. A barrister regularly advising overseas business clients should confirm the client's business status and keep records. **Flag for Stage 2 place-of-supply verification against the current VATA 1994 s.7A / SI 1992 enacted position post-Brexit.**

**H2: Making Tax Digital for Income Tax (MTD ITSA)**
- Barristers with gross income above **£50,000** from 6 April 2026, and above **£30,000** from 6 April 2027, must comply with MTD for ITSA (quarterly digital reporting to HMRC). Most busy barristers will be in scope from 2026. The old annual self-assessment return is replaced by quarterly updates and a final declaration.

**H2: Chambers as an entity: does it need an accountant?**
- Most chambers are unincorporated associations or loose cost-sharing arrangements, not partnerships; they have no separate corporation-tax liability.
- Where chambers is constituted as a management company, it will pay corporation tax on any retained profit and must file accounts. An accountant is engaged to manage the company's tax affairs separately from each barrister's personal self-assessment.
- The practice manager or chambers director typically oversees the pooling of costs; an accountant familiar with the Bar advises on the VAT position of the cost-sharing arrangement (see above) and ensures individual barristers are correctly registered and filing.

**H2: King's Counsel (silk) and the tax position on appointment**
- Appointment as KC brings no immediate tax event. The increased fee levels that follow will push taxable profits higher; planning for the payments-on-account step-change is essential (the January after a high-earnings year will carry a large balancing payment plus the first payment on account for the new, higher level).
- KC application fees and the cost of silk ceremony robes are likely allowable as professional costs (the application is to advance an existing practice, not to acquire a new trade).

**H2: Pensions for self-employed barristers**
- A self-employed barrister can contribute to a personal pension (SIPP or similar). Annual allowance £60,000 (or 100% of relevant UK earnings if lower). Tax relief at the marginal rate (up to 45%). Barristers often have irregular income and should plan contributions around earnings.
- The Bar's own schemes (the Bar Mutual and Bar Council do not run a pension scheme as such); the individual barrister selects their own pension provider.
