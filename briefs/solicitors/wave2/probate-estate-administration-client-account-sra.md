## The honest thesis (lead and hold)

Estate-administration money the firm holds during a probate matter is **client money** under the SRA Accounts Rules 2019, but probate raises questions the everyday conveyancing/litigation guidance does not answer cleanly. Whose money is it, the deceased, the personal representatives (PRs), or the beneficiaries? When the firm is **instructed by the PRs** (the common case), it holds the money on behalf of those PRs and it is client money under Rule 2.1; the analysis differs again where the firm is itself appointed executor or trustee (that is the sibling page `solicitor-acting-as-executor-or-trustee-fees-tax`). The page must give precise client-account mechanics: one ledger per estate, every receipt promptly to client account, the Rule 3.3 banking-facility prohibition (you administer an estate, you do not run a bank account for the PRs), interest under Rule 7, and clean distribution mechanics that close the matter without a residual balance. Technical, reassuring, SRA-cited.

## H2 outline (aim 9-12 sections, ~2,900-3,500 words)

1. **Why probate money is the SRA's highest-risk client money** · set the scene: estate administration runs large balances (property proceeds, investments, bank accounts realised) through the client account, and the SRA's own thematic review found probate has generated some of the largest Compensation Fund payouts. So the Accounts Rules bite hardest here. Signpost the structure: status, the account, ledgering, interest, distribution, closing.
2. **Is estate money client money? The Rule 2.1 analysis** · the legal anchor. Where the firm is **instructed by the PRs** to administer the estate, money it holds (assets realised, sale proceeds, cash to distribute) is client money under Rule 2.1: limb (a) money held relating to regulated services delivered to the client (the PRs) and/or limb (b) money held on behalf of a third party in relation to regulated services. State plainly: it is the PRs' money in their capacity as PRs, held by the firm for the estate. Distinguish the separate case where the **firm is the executor/trustee** (limb (c), money held "as a trustee or as the holder of a specified office or appointment"), which is the sibling page. Cross-link `what-counts-as-client-money-uk-solicitors`.
3. **The client account and the banking-facility trap (Rule 3.3)** · estate money goes into the general client account (or a separate designated deposit account for a large/long-held estate, see interest). The Rule 3.3 prohibition is acute in probate: a PR may be tempted to route the estate's cash flow through the firm's client account for convenience (paying the deceased's standing bills, running a "PR bank account"). Every payment in, transfer or withdrawal must relate to the **delivery of regulated services** (administering the estate). Holding estate funds long after the regulated work is done, or moving money with no underlying legal service, breaches Rule 3.3. Cross-link `solicitor-trust-accounting-guide`.
4. **One ledger per estate: the ledgering discipline** · open a dedicated client ledger for each estate, in the name of the estate (e.g. "Estate of [deceased], deceased"), recording every receipt and payment with date, amount, source/payee and purpose. Sub-analysis where useful (asset realisations vs liabilities paid vs legacies vs residue). The estate ledger feeds the five-weekly three-way reconciliation (Rule 8.3) like any other client ledger. Never let an estate ledger go overdrawn (a debit balance on client account is a serious breach).
5. **The estate cash cycle through client account** · the practical flow: (i) money in, as assets are realised (bank/building society closures, investment redemptions, the property sale proceeds, debts collected); (ii) money out, paying the deceased's debts and liabilities, IHT (to HMRC), probate fees, the firm's billed costs (transferred to office account only after a bill is delivered, §6), interim and final distributions to beneficiaries. Stress: IHT and the probate application fee are paid from estate funds; the firm's fee is a separate VAT supply (cross-link `vat-on-probate-and-estate-administration-fees`).
6. **Property sale proceeds within an estate** · a common high-value receipt: where the firm also acts on the sale of the deceased's property, completion proceeds land in client account and feed the estate ledger. Note the overlap with conveyancing client-money rules (the same Rule 3.3 and reconciliation discipline) but the receipt belongs to the estate, not a living seller. Watch for an estate that holds the property unsold for a long period (long-held balances raise interest and Rule 3.3 questions).
7. **Interest on estate money (Rule 7)** · estates can hold material sums for months. Under Rule 7 the firm must account for a **fair sum of interest** on client money held (a fairness test by amount and duration), and a large/long-held estate balance is a clear candidate. Options: pay a fair sum of interest from office account, or hold the funds in a **separate designated deposit account** so the account's interest reaches the estate. Cross-link `handling-client-money-interest-sra-rules` for the policy mechanics. Note interest the firm retains is its own (exempt) income for VAT (cross-link `law-firm-partial-exemption-vat-client-account-interest`).
8. **Distribution mechanics: interim and final** · how to distribute cleanly. Interim distributions to beneficiaries during administration; the final distribution of residue once liabilities, tax and costs are settled and the estate accounts are approved. Best practice: obtain beneficiary identity/bank verification (anti-fraud), get receipts, time distributions against the s.27 Trustee Act 1925 advertisement period (so the PRs are protected against unknown claimants, cross-link the VAT/disbursements page where s.27 notices feature). The aim is a NIL estate ledger balance at close.
9. **Closing the estate ledger and avoiding a residual balance** · how to bring the ledger to zero: account for every penny, distribute the residue, transfer the firm's billed costs out, and reconcile to nil. Where a small unclaimed amount is left (a missing beneficiary, an uncashed cheque, a rounding remnant), that becomes a **residual client balance** with its own SRA disposal route (do NOT just leave it on client account, and do NOT sweep it to office account). Cross-link the dedicated sibling `residual-client-balances-clearing-unclaimed-money-sra`.
10. **Records, retention and the accountant's report** · keep the estate ledger and all supporting records for at least six years (Rule 13/Rule 12 context). A firm that has held client money in the period needs an accountant's report unless it qualifies for the Rule 12.2 exemption (all client money within an **average not exceeding £10,000 AND a maximum not exceeding £250,000** in the period). Most probate-active firms will exceed this, so plan for the report. Cross-link `sra-accountants-report-exemption-thresholds`.

## VERIFIED ANCHOR BLOCK (quote/paraphrase only from this; verified at primary source 2026-06-03)

- **Rule 2.1 client money definition** · SRA Accounts Rules 2019 (https://www.sra.org.uk/solicitors/standards-regulations/accounts-rules/), in force 25 November 2019. Money is "client money" if held or received: **(a)** relating to regulated services delivered to a client; **(b)** "on behalf of a third party in relation to regulated services delivered by you (such as money held as agent, stakeholder or held to the sender's order)"; **(c)** "as a trustee or as the holder of a specified office or appointment, such as donee of a power of attorney, Court of Protection deputy or trustee of an occupational pension scheme"; **(d)** in respect of the firm's fees and any unpaid disbursements held before delivery of a bill. PR/estate money administered for the PRs falls within (a) and/or (b); a firm appointed as executor/trustee falls within (c). VERIFIED VERBATIM (limbs (b) and (c) quoted above).
- **Rule 3 / 3.3 (client account + banking-facility prohibition)** · client money in a separate client account at a bank/building society branch in England and Wales (Rule 3.1), named to include the firm name and "client"; a firm must NOT use a client account to provide banking facilities to clients or third parties, and payments into, transfers and withdrawals must be in respect of the delivery of regulated services (Rule 3.3). §5.
- **Rule 5.1 (withdrawal conditions)** · client money may be withdrawn from client account only: **(a)** "for the purpose for which it is being held"; **(b)** "following receipt of instructions from the client, or the third party for whom the money is held"; **(c)** "on the SRA's prior written authorisation or in prescribed circumstances". VERIFIED VERBATIM. (Distribution to beneficiaries is withdrawal for the purpose for which it is held; a true unclaimed remnant uses limb (c), the residual-balance route, sibling page.)
- **Rule 7 (interest)** · account to clients/third parties for a "fair sum of interest" on client money held (fairness by amount and duration; a contrary written agreement is permitted). §5.
- **Rule 8.3 (reconciliation)** · reconcile client account (ledgers to cash book to bank statement) at least every five weeks, signed off by the COFA or a manager. §5.
- **Rule 12 / 12.2 (accountant's report + exemption)** · report needed if client money held in the period, unless all client money was within an **average not exceeding £10,000 AND a maximum not exceeding £250,000** in the period (or all from the Legal Aid Agency). DO NOT write "£250" (that is the corpus-wide stale error, see EXISTING_PAGE_STALE in §5). §5.
- **s.27 Trustee Act 1925 (PR protection by advertisement)** · trustees/PRs may "give notice by advertisement in the Gazette" (and, for land, in a newspaper) of intention to distribute, requiring claimants to send particulars "within the time, not being less than two months, fixed in the notice"; after the deadline the PRs may distribute "having regard only to the claims ... of which the trustees or personal representatives then had notice" and are not liable to a claimant of whom they had no notice. legislation.gov.uk Trustee Act 1925 s.27 (https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/Geo5/15-16/19/section/27). VERIFIED VERBATIM (operative wording). Relevance here: time distributions against this period.
- **Probate application fee (estate disbursement, paid from estate funds)** · gov.uk: application fee **£300** for estates over £5,000; no fee if the estate is £5,000 or less; extra/office copies of the grant **£16 each** (https://www.gov.uk/applying-for-probate/fees). ## Worked-example sketches (illustrative only; label as not advice; no real figures presented as a quote)

1. **The standard PR-instructed estate.** The firm is instructed by two PRs to administer an estate comprising a property, two bank accounts and an investment portfolio. Walk the cash cycle: open an "Estate of [deceased], deceased" client ledger; receipts as each asset is realised (bank closures, investment redemption, property completion proceeds); payments out for the deceased's debts, IHT to HMRC, the £300 probate fee, the firm's billed costs (only after a bill is delivered); interim distribution to the residuary beneficiaries; final distribution to nil after the estate accounts are approved. Caption: "one ledger per estate, reconciled five-weekly, closed to nil."
2. **The Rule 3.3 banking-facility trap.** A PR asks the firm to keep the estate's cash in client account and pay the deceased's ongoing household bills and the PR's expenses from it over many months, well after the substantive legal work is done. Show why this is a Rule 3.3 breach: the payments stop relating to the delivery of regulated services and the client account is being used as a bank account. The fix: the PRs open a dedicated executor's bank account for ongoing cash flow; the firm holds only what it needs for the regulated work. Caption: "you administer the estate, you do not bank for the PRs."
3. **The long-held estate and interest.** A property within an estate takes 14 months to sell and a six-figure balance sits in client account meanwhile. Under Rule 7 a fair sum of interest is plainly due. Show the choice: pay a fair sum from office account, or place the funds in a separate designated deposit account so the account's interest accrues to the estate. Caption: "long-held estate balances are exactly where the Rule 7 fairness test bites."
