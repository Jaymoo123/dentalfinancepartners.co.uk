## The honest thesis (lead and hold)

A purchase by a company or other non-natural person is the most complex SDLT case a conveyancer meets, because two charging regimes can apply and they have to be told apart. Most corporate residential purchases are charged at the **standard residential rates plus the 5% additional-dwelling surcharge, plus the 2% non-resident surcharge** where the buyer is non-UK-resident. But where a **company or other non-natural person buys a single dwelling for more than £500,000** and no relief applies, a **flat higher rate of 17%** (raised from 15% on 31 October 2024) bites on the whole price. The 17% charge is heavily relieved (property rental businesses, property developers and traders, dwellings made available to the public, and others), and where a relief applies the purchase reverts to the ordinary rates plus surcharges. The same "enveloped dwelling" then usually falls within **ATED**, an annual charge. A conveyancer who can place the transaction in the right regime, check the reliefs, and flag the ATED follow-on protects a high-value client. This page maps the rate stack, the 17%-vs-relief decision, the ATED interaction and the firm's own fee/VAT role (§6, §7). CRITICAL: the flat rate is 17% now, not 15%.

## H2 outline (aim 9-12 sections, ~3,200-3,800 words)

1. **Non-resident and corporate buyers: why SDLT gets complicated** · framing. SDLT applies in **England and Northern Ireland** only (Scotland LBTT, Wales LTT). Set out the two regimes: (a) standard rates + surcharges; (b) the flat 17% over £500,000 for non-natural persons absent relief. Signpost the non-resident 2% surcharge and the ATED follow-on. State up front that the flat rate is **17% from 31 October 2024** (was 15%).
2. **The non-resident surcharge (2%)** · §7. A **2%** surcharge (in force from **1 April 2021**) applies to purchases of residential property in England and Northern Ireland by non-UK-resident buyers, on top of the standard rates and any HRAD surcharge. The residence test is day-count based (broadly, present in the UK on fewer than 183 days in the relevant 12 months); it can apply to individuals, companies and certain trusts. Conveyancers must establish residence status before completion.
3. **Companies and the 5% additional-dwelling surcharge** · §7. A company (or other non-natural person) buying residential property is generally treated as buying an additional dwelling, so the **5% HRAD surcharge** (from 31 October 2024) applies from the first property, not just the second. So a typical corporate purchase under £500,000 (or over £500,000 with a relief) is charged at standard rates + 5% + (if non-resident) 2%.
4. **The flat 17% higher rate for non-natural persons over £500,000** · THE key corporate point. Where a **company, a partnership with a corporate member, or a collective investment scheme** buys a **single dwelling for more than £500,000** and **no relief applies**, SDLT is charged at a **flat 17%** on the whole consideration (raised from **15% on 31 October 2024**). This is the anti-enveloping charge. State the 17% figure and its 31-October-2024 effective date unmistakably, and warn explicitly against the superseded 15% rate.
5. **The reliefs from the 17% charge** · gov.uk. The 17% flat rate does NOT apply (the purchase reverts to ordinary rates + surcharges) where the dwelling is acquired for a qualifying purpose, including: a **property rental business**; a **property developer or trader**; a trade making the property **available to the public**; purchase by a **financial institution** in the course of lending; a dwelling **occupied by employees** of the purchaser; a **farmhouse**; or a **qualifying housing co-operative**. The relief can be withdrawn (clawed back) if the qualifying use ceases within a control period (commonly 3 years). Conveyancers must evidence the qualifying purpose on the return.
6. **The ATED interaction** · §6 (the firm's role), gov.uk ATED. A dwelling worth **more than £500,000** held by a **company, a partnership with a company partner, or a collective investment scheme** is within **ATED** (Annual Tax on Enveloped Dwellings), an **annual** charge by value band, with an **ATED return** due even where a relief reduces the charge to nil. ATED has parallel reliefs (property rental business, developers, etc) that broadly track the SDLT reliefs. The conveyancer should flag the ATED follow-on at purchase: the SDLT is a one-off at completion; ATED is annual and the client must register and file. Keep the ATED detail proportionate (signpost, do not turn this into an ATED-rates page).
7. **Putting the rate stack together** · a clear decision path: is the buyer a non-natural person buying a single dwelling over £500,000? If yes and no relief, **flat 17%**. If a relief applies (or price is £500,000 or under, or it is not a single-dwelling case), use **standard rates + 5% HRAD + 2% non-resident (if applicable)**. Use a short ordered list, not a table dump. Cross-link `sdlt-calculation-uk-conveyancing-solicitors` for the standard bands.
8. **The firm's role, fee and VAT** · §6, §7. The SDLT paid for the client is a **disbursement** (the firm as conduit, §7). The firm's conveyancing and SDLT-advisory work is a **standard-rated supply at 20%** (§6). Where the firm advises on the regime, the reliefs and the ATED follow-on, that is part of its standard-rated supply. Note the high negligence stakes on a mis-applied 17% charge (a six-figure error on a high-value purchase) and the PII relevance. Cross-link `conveyancing-vat-rules-uk-2025-26`.
9. **Devolved equivalents** · §7. Scotland (LBTT, Revenue Scotland) and Wales (LTT, WRA) have their own treatment of corporate and non-resident buyers and do NOT use the SDLT 17%/£500,000 flat charge or the 2% non-resident surcharge. Do not apply SDLT rules across the border. Brief signpost.
10. **Worked sketches** · see below.
11. **Practical checklist for a corporate/non-resident purchase** · (i) establish buyer status (company/non-natural person? UK-resident?); (ii) test the flat 17% over £500,000 and screen the reliefs; (iii) apply the 5% HRAD and 2% non-resident surcharge where the standard regime applies; (iv) evidence any relief on the SDLT return and note the clawback control period; (v) flag the ATED registration/return follow-on; (vi) recharge the SDLT as a disbursement and charge VAT on the firm's fee. Inline CTA to lead form (segment: firm handling corporate/offshore/high-value purchases).

## VERIFIED ANCHOR BLOCK (quote/paraphrase only from this; verified at PRIMARY SOURCE 2026-06-03)

- **Flat higher rate for non-natural persons (gov.uk "SDLT: corporate bodies", https://www.gov.uk/guidance/stamp-duty-land-tax-corporate-bodies, verified 2026-06-03):**
  - VERBATIM: *"SDLT is charged at 17% on residential properties costing more than £500,000 bought by certain corporate bodies or 'non-natural persons'."*
  - EFFECTIVE DATE: the page states *"There was a different rate for corporate bodies before 31 October 2024"* and the 30 October 2024 update confirms *"The higher rate of Stamp Duty Land Tax has been updated to 17%."* So the rate is **17% from 31 October 2024** (it was **15%** before; do NOT use 15% for a current transaction).
  - SCOPE: applies to *"companies, partnerships with company partners, and collective investment schemes"* buying a single dwelling over £500,000.
  - RELIEFS (VERBATIM list, relief may apply where the property is used in/for): *"a property rental business"*; *"a property developer or trader"*; *"a trade involving making the property available to the public"*; *"bought by a financial institution"* (lending); *"occupied by employees of the purchaser"*; *"a farmhouse"*; *"bought by a qualifying housing co-operative"*.
  - SURCHARGES ON CORPORATE BUYERS (VERBATIM): *"There is a 5% surcharge on residential properties bought by companies"* (updated October 2024); and *"a 2% surcharge on residential properties in England and Northern Ireland bought by non-UK residents on or after 1 April 2021."*
  - ATED LINK (VERBATIM): *"These bodies may also need to pay Annual Tax on Enveloped Dwellings."*
- **ATED basics (gov.uk ATED guidance, verified 2026-06-03):** VERBATIM: *"ATED is an annual tax payable mainly by companies that own UK residential property valued at more than £500,000."* Applies to *"companies"*, a *"partnership where any of the partners is a company"*, and a *"collective investment scheme"*. Value threshold: dwellings valued at **more than £500,000**. ATED has reliefs (broadly tracking the SDLT corporate reliefs); a relief declaration ATED return is still required even where the charge is reduced to nil.
- **SDLT surcharge/rate context (§7, primary-source-locked):** HRAD surcharge **5%** for effective dates on or after **31 October 2024** (raised from 3%); non-resident surcharge **2%** from **1 April 2021**; from **1 April 2025** standard nil-rate band **£125,000**, FTB relief nil to **£300,000** (5% to £500,000, none above). Residential SDLT return + payment within **14 days** of the effective date.
- **Firm's fee and the disbursement (§6, §7):** SDLT paid for the client is a clean disbursement (no VAT); the firm's conveyancing/SDLT-advisory work is standard-rated at **20%** (registration threshold £90,000).
- **Devolved (§7):** LBTT (Scotland, Revenue Scotland) and LTT (Wales, WRA) have their own corporate/non-resident treatment and do NOT use the SDLT 17%/£500,000 flat charge or the 2% non-resident surcharge.

## Worked-example sketches (illustrative only; label as not advice)

1. **Company buys a £900,000 home, no relief: flat 17%.** A company with no qualifying use buys a single dwelling for £900,000 in England. The flat **17%** charge applies to the whole £900,000 (not the banded rates). Caption: "a non-natural person buying a dwelling over £500,000 with no relief pays a flat 17% (the rate rose from 15% on 31 October 2024)." Note explicitly that using the old 15% would understate the SDLT.
2. **Company buys to let, relief applies: standard rates plus 5%.** A property-rental company buys a £900,000 dwelling for its lettings business and claims the property-rental-business relief. The flat 17% does not apply; SDLT is charged at standard residential rates plus the 5% HRAD surcharge (plus 2% if the company is non-UK-resident). The relief can be clawed back if the qualifying use ceases within the control period. ATED relief is claimed via an ATED return. Caption: "a relief takes the purchase out of the 17% charge and back to standard rates plus surcharges, but watch the clawback and the ATED return."
3. **Non-resident individual buyer.** A non-UK-resident individual buys a £600,000 main home. Standard rates apply, plus the 2% non-resident surcharge (no HRAD if it is their only dwelling); the flat 17% does not apply (an individual is not a non-natural person). Caption: "the 2% non-resident surcharge applies to individuals too; the 17% flat charge is for non-natural persons."
