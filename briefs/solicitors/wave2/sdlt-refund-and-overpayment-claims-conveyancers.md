## The honest thesis (lead and hold)

When a buyer has paid SDLT they did not ultimately owe, the money is recoverable, but only inside the right window and via the right route, and the three routes have **different clocks**. The most common is the **higher-rate (additional-dwelling) surcharge refund** where the buyer has now sold their previous main residence: that must be claimed within the **later of 12 months of selling the old home OR 12 months of the SDLT return's filing date**, and the old home must have been **sold within 3 years** of the new purchase (subject to exceptional-circumstances extension). Separately, a straightforward **error on the return** can be fixed by **amending within 12 months of the filing date**; and beyond that, **overpayment relief** reclaims overpaid SDLT up to **4 years after the effective date of the transaction**. A conveyancer who flags these windows protects the client and the firm. This page sets out each route, its deadline, and the firm's role and fee/VAT position (the refund work is a standard-rated legal service; the SDLT itself was a disbursement when first paid, §6, §7).

## H2 outline (aim 9-12 sections, ~3,000-3,600 words)

1. **When SDLT comes back: the three routes and three clocks** · framing. SDLT applies in **England and Northern Ireland** only (Scotland LBTT, Wales LTT, with their own refund rules). Set out the three routes up front: (1) higher-rate surcharge refund on replacing a main residence; (2) amend the return (12 months); (3) overpayment relief (4 years). Stress that the deadline depends on which route applies.
2. **The higher-rate surcharge: why a refund arises** · §7. The HRAD surcharge is **5%** for effective dates on or after **31 October 2024** (raised from 3%; contracts exchanged on/before 30 October 2024 keep 3%). A buyer who completes on a new main home before selling the old one pays the surcharge up front because, on the day, they own two dwellings. When they then sell the old main residence, the transaction is recharacterised as a replacement and the surcharge becomes refundable.
3. **The refund deadline: the LATER of two dates (the key window)** · VERBATIM-anchored. HMRC must receive the refund request by **whichever is the later of: 12 months after the date of sale of the previous main home, OR 12 months after the filing date of the SDLT return** for the new purchase. The previous main home must have been **sold within 3 years (36 months)** of the new purchase, unless exceptional circumstances apply. Spell out the filing date (14 days after the effective date). This is THE section; make the "later of" unmistakable.
4. **How the firm makes the higher-rate refund claim** · the practical steps: identify eligibility (replacement of only/main residence, both within the time test), gather the completion details and the UTRN of the original return, submit the HMRC repayment request (online or by post), and diarise the deadline. The claim can be made by the purchaser or by an agent on their behalf. Keep it process-led, not a step-by-step HMRC form walkthrough.
5. **Amending an SDLT return (the 12-month route)** · gov.uk: you have **12 months from the filing date** to amend the return. Use this where the original return contained an error (wrong rate, wrong relief, miscalculated bands) and the amendment window is still open. Distinguish from the surcharge refund: amendment corrects the return itself.
6. **Overpayment relief (the 4-year route)** · gov.uk: if it is **more than a year since the filing date but no more than 4 years since the effective date of the transaction**, you can claim **overpayment relief**. This is the catch-all for SDLT paid that was not due where the amendment window has closed. Note the strict conditions and that it is the route of last resort after amendment.
7. **Common SDLT overpayment scenarios conveyancers see** · (i) surcharge paid then old home sold (route 1); (ii) first-time-buyer relief or multiple-dwellings/mixed-use position missed on the original return (amend or overpayment relief); (iii) wrong nil-rate band used (note the 1 April 2025 reversion to **£125,000** nil-rate and FTB **£300,000**, §7, a live source of mis-filed returns straddling the change); (iv) non-resident surcharge wrongly applied where the buyer met the residence test. Tag each with its route/clock.
8. **The firm's role, fee and VAT** · §6, §7. The SDLT paid for the client was a **disbursement** when first paid (the firm as conduit, §7). The firm's work in making the refund/overpayment claim is a **standard-rated legal/advisory service at 20%** (§6); the refund itself is the client's money. Warn against "SDLT refund" cold-call reclaim firms that take a contingent cut and file weak claims (a known HMRC concern); position the conveyancing firm as the trusted route. Cross-link `conveyancing-vat-rules-uk-2025-26`.
9. **Devolved equivalents: LBTT and LTT refunds** · §7. Scotland's LBTT **ADS** (8% from 5 December 2024) has its own repayment route and clock via **Revenue Scotland**; Wales's LTT higher-rates refund runs via the **Welsh Revenue Authority**. Do NOT apply the SDLT windows to LBTT/LTT. Keep brief; signpost the jurisdiction split.
10. **Negligence and diary risk for the firm** · short. A missed refund window can crystallise a client loss and a PII claim; the firm should have a system to flag replacement-of-main-residence cases and diarise the surcharge-refund deadline. Cross-link `client-due-diligence-for-uk-conveyancers` and a PII page. Keep proportionate, not alarmist.

## VERIFIED ANCHOR BLOCK (quote/paraphrase only from this; verified at PRIMARY SOURCE 2026-06-03)

- **Higher-rate surcharge refund deadline** (gov.uk "Apply for a refund of the higher rates of Stamp Duty Land Tax", https://www.gov.uk/guidance/apply-for-a-refund-of-the-higher-rates-of-stamp-duty-land-tax, verified 2026-06-03):
  - VERBATIM: *"HMRC must receive your request for a refund by whichever date is the later of: 12 months after the date of sale [or] 12 months after the filing date of the SDLT return"* (for sales on or after 29 October 2018).
  - VERBATIM: *"You must have sold your previous main home within 3 years of buying the new property, unless exceptional circumstances apply."*
- **Amend the return (12-month route)** (gov.uk SDLT returns guidance, verified 2026-06-03): VERBATIM: *"You've 12 months from the filing date to amend your return."* The filing date is **14 days after the effective date** of the transaction.
- **Overpayment relief (4-year route)** (gov.uk SDLT returns guidance, verified 2026-06-03): VERBATIM: *"If it's been more than a year since the filing date of your SDLT return, but no more than 4 years since the effective date of transaction, you can make a claim for overpayment relief."*
- **HRAD surcharge rate (§7, primary-source-locked):** higher rates for additional dwellings (HRAD) surcharge is **5%** for effective dates on or after **31 October 2024** (raised from 3%; contracts exchanged on/before 30 October 2024 keep 3%). Non-resident surcharge **2%** from 1 April 2021. From **1 April 2025** the standard nil-rate band is **£125,000** and first-time-buyer relief runs nil to **£300,000** (5% £300,001 to £500,000, none above). Residential SDLT return + payment due within **14 days** of the effective date.
- **The SDLT itself is a disbursement; the firm's claim work is standard-rated (§6, §7):** the property transaction tax paid for the client is a clean disbursement (no VAT); the firm's fee for advising on/making the refund is a standard-rated supply at 20%.
- **Devolved (§7):** LBTT (Scotland, Revenue Scotland), ADS **8%** from **5 December 2024**, 30-day return; LTT (Wales, WRA), higher residential rate tables opening **5%** from **11 December 2024**, 30-day return. Each has its own refund route and time limit; do NOT apply the SDLT windows to them.

## Worked-example sketches (illustrative only; label as not advice)

1. **Surcharge refund, "later of" deadline.** A buyer completes a new main home in England in January while still owning their old home, paying the 5% HRAD surcharge. They sell the old home 14 months later. The refund deadline is the later of (a) 12 months after that sale, or (b) 12 months after the new purchase's SDLT filing date; here (a) is later, so the clock runs from the sale. Both the 3-year sale test and the refund window are met. Caption: "the refund clock is the LATER of two dates; here it runs from the sale of the old home."
2. **Wrong band on a straddle return, amend within 12 months.** A return filed in April 2025 mistakenly used the temporary £250,000 nil-rate band that ended 31 March 2025. Spotted within the year, the firm amends the return within 12 months of the filing date and recovers the overpayment. Caption: "an error on the face of the return is an amendment, not an overpayment-relief claim, if you are inside 12 months."
3. **Closed amendment window, overpayment relief.** A relief was missed and discovered 2 years after completion. The 12-month amendment window has closed, but the firm is within 4 years of the effective date, so it claims overpayment relief. Caption: "after 12 months, the route is overpayment relief, available up to 4 years from the effective date."
