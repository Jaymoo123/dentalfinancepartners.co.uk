- Intent: a managing/finance partner or practice manager at an LLP or partnership considering (or already running) a **separate service company** that owns or employs the firm's staff, premises or IT and **recharges those costs to the trading LLP**. They need to understand: the VAT on the recharge (standard-rated unless the entities are VAT-grouped), the arm's-length / mark-up requirement, how a service company differs from a profit-sharing **corporate member** (and why the mixed-membership rules do not catch a genuine cost-recharge entity but can catch profit parked in it), and why firms use the structure. This is a **cost-recharge entity**, not a profit-share member.

## The honest thesis (lead and hold)

A service company is a separate company that owns the "back office" (it employs the support staff, or holds the premises lease, or owns the IT and equipment) and **recharges those costs to the trading LLP at arm's length**. It is a legitimate, common structure, used for limited-liability ring-fencing of staff and assets, for pension and benefit planning, and for cleaner cost allocation across multiple partnerships. But it has two pressure points the page must nail. First, **VAT**: a recharge from the service company to the LLP is a **supply of services, standard-rated at 20%**, unless the two entities are in a **VAT group**, in which case the recharge is **disregarded for VAT** (VATA 1994 s.43). Because a law firm makes mostly taxable supplies it can usually recover that VAT, but the cash-flow and the partial-exemption position (§6.C) still matter, and a VAT group removes the recharge VAT entirely. Second, the recharge must be **at arm's length** (a commercial cost-plus charge), and the structure must be a genuine cost-recharge, not a way to **park profit** in the company: a service company earning only an arm's-length recharge is **not** a profit-sharing corporate member and is not caught by the mixed-membership rules (§2.B), but profit diverted into it can be. Set up the recharge correctly, decide on VAT grouping, and keep it a cost-recharge entity, not a disguised profit-share.

## H2 outline (aim 9-12 sections, ~3,200-3,600 words)

1. **What a law-firm service company is (and is not)** · define it precisely. A **service company** is a separate company that holds the firm's overheads (commonly: employs the support/administrative staff and seconds or supplies them to the LLP; or owns/leases the premises and sub-lets to the LLP; or owns the IT, equipment and library) and **recharges the cost to the trading LLP**. Distinguish it sharply from a **corporate member** of the LLP (Wave 1 page): a corporate member takes a **profit share** at the corporation-tax rate; a service company takes a **cost recharge** for services/assets it provides. Signpost the three threads: VAT on the recharge, the arm's-length requirement, and the mixed-membership-rule boundary.
2. **Why firms use a service company** · the honest rationale. (a) **Limited liability / ring-fencing** of staff employment and of premises/asset ownership away from the trading entity; (b) **pension and benefit planning** (the company employs staff and can run occupational/benefit arrangements); (c) **cleaner cost allocation** where one back office serves more than one partnership or office; (d) historically, employment and NIC structuring (now far more constrained). Be candid that the modern driver is operational and risk-management, not a tax saving (anti-avoidance has closed most of the old profit/NIC angles). No pricing.
3. **The VAT headline: a recharge is a standard-rated supply** · the core point (§6). When the service company recharges the LLP for staff, premises or IT, it is making a **supply of services**, which is **standard-rated at 20%** (legal-sector overheads carry no VAT exemption). So **without a VAT group**, the service company charges 20% VAT on its recharge to the LLP, and the LLP recovers it as input tax to the extent it makes taxable supplies. State plainly: a service company's recharge to the LLP is a **standard-rated supply unless the two are VAT-grouped**. Note the staff-supply nuance and cross-link `secondment-of-solicitors-vat-and-tax-treatment` (E3) for the supply-of-staff detail.
4. **The VAT group solution: intra-group supplies are disregarded (VATA 1994 s.43)** · the key mechanism, verified verbatim. Where the service company and the LLP form a **VAT group**, supplies between them are **disregarded for VAT**: VATA 1994 **s.43(1)** provides that "any business carried on by a member of the group shall be treated as carried on by the representative member" and "any supply of goods or services by a member of the group to another member of the group shall be disregarded". So the recharge carries **no VAT** and no VAT invoice is issued for it (VAT Notice 700/2: such supplies "are normally disregarded for VAT", "VAT need not be accounted for on these supplies and no VAT invoices must be issued in respect of them"). The group files one VAT return through a representative member. This is the clean answer to the recharge-VAT problem.
5. **Who can form a VAT group, and the 2019 extension to LLPs/partnerships (VATA 1994 s.43A)** · the eligibility point that makes this work for law firms. Originally a VAT group was for **bodies corporate** under common control. **From 1 November 2019** (amended by Finance Act 2019), **non-corporate entities, individuals and partnerships (including an LLP), can join a VAT group** where they control the body corporate. VATA 1994 **s.43A(1)** (verbatim): two or more UK bodies corporate are eligible if "(a) one of them controls each of the others, (b) one person (whether a body corporate or an individual) controls all of them, or (c) two or more individuals carrying on a business in partnership control all of them"; and a UK body corporate is one "established or has a fixed establishment in the United Kingdom". gov.uk Notice 700/2 confirms "individuals and partnerships can join a VAT group if they: control the UK body corporate ... and are established, or have a fixed establishment in the UK". State the practical upshot: **an LLP that controls its service company can VAT-group with it**, so the recharge is disregarded. This is the post-2019 extension that lets the LLP-plus-service-company structure VAT-group, whereas pre-2019 only bodies corporate could group.
6. **The arm's-length / commercial-recharge requirement** · the pricing discipline. The recharge must be **at arm's length** (a commercial charge, typically **cost plus a mark-up**), for two reasons: (a) the company needs a commercial return on the function it performs, and (b) the **transfer-pricing** rules (TIOPA 2010 Part 4) and the **wholly-and-exclusively** test (ITTOIA 2005 s.34 / CTA 2009 for the company) require connected-party charges to be commercial for the deduction to stand at the LLP and the income to be properly taxed at the company. A cost-plus mark-up is the typical model. Warn that a non-commercial recharge (too high to shift profit into the company, or too low to strip it out) is challengeable. Note that within a VAT group the VAT falls away, but the **direct-tax** arm's-length requirement does not (the deduction/income still has to be commercial).
7. **Service company vs corporate member: the mixed-membership boundary (§2.B)** · the critical differentiation from the Wave 1 page. A **corporate member** has a **profit share** and is policed by the mixed-membership rules (ITTOIA 2005 ss.850C to 850E, from 6 April 2014): any profit share above the company's **appropriate notional profit** that an individual partner has the power to enjoy is reallocated back to the individuals. A **service company** earning only a **genuine arm's-length recharge** for services/assets it really provides is **not** a profit-sharing member, so it is **not caught by s.850C** in the ordinary case. The risk reappears only if the firm **parks profit** in the service company (over-charging the recharge so the company holds what is really partner profit): then the substance looks like profit diversion and the mixed-membership rules (plus the general anti-abuse rule) can bite. State the line clearly: arm's-length cost recharge = outside; parked profit = at risk.
8. **Corporation tax in the service company and getting money out** · the company-level tax (§3). The service company pays **corporation tax** on its profit (the mark-up): **19%** small-profits rate on profits up to £50,000, **25%** main rate above £250,000, marginal relief between (FY2025 and FY2026). Extracting value to the partners still costs tax: a **dividend** at 8.75% / 33.75% / 39.35% (2025/26), and from **6 April 2026** ordinary **10.75%** / upper **35.75%** (additional 39.35% unchanged), £500 allowance; a **salary** triggers **employer (secondary Class 1) NIC at 15% above the £5,000 secondary threshold** (from 6 April 2025); a **loan to a participator** triggers **s.455** (33.75% before 6 April 2026, 35.75% on/after, repayable under s.458). So a service company is rarely a net tax saving; it is an operational/risk structure whose profit (the mark-up) should be modest.
9. **Premises, the option to tax and capital allowances** · where the service company owns assets (§6.E, §8). If the service company owns the **premises** and charges rent/recharge to the LLP, supplies of land are normally **VAT-exempt**, which blocks input-VAT recovery on buying/refurbishing; an **option to tax** (VATA 1994 Schedule 10, notified to HMRC within 30 days) makes that supply standard-rated and recovers the VAT (note: if VAT-grouped, the intra-group rent is disregarded, but the OTT still governs recovery on the spend). The **Capital Goods Scheme** can apply to £250,000+ (VAT-exclusive) spend on land/buildings (10 intervals). On fit-out and IT the company can claim **AIA (£1m)**, the special-rate pool (6%) and main-rate WDA (18%, reducing to 14% from 1 April 2026 corporation tax per FA 2026 s.28), and **full expensing** (companies only). Keep this section tight and cross-link the capital-allowances and option-to-tax pages.
10. **SRA and practical governance** · the regulatory and housekeeping layer (§1, §5). The service company is **not** itself an SRA-regulated legal entity (it provides services to the firm, it does not deliver regulated legal services), but it must not blur the firm's regulatory lines: it does not hold **client money** (client money stays in the LLP's client account under the Accounts Rules), and the recharge must be a real, documented arm's-length arrangement (a written services/recharge agreement, regular invoicing or VAT-group accounting, board governance). If the structure ever gives a non-lawyer control over the **legal** practice, check ABS/licensing (§1). Inline CTA to lead form (segment: firm running or considering a service company wanting the VAT-group, recharge-pricing and structure review).

## Worked-example sketches (illustrative only; label as not advice; no real figures presented as a quote)

1. **Staff service company, no VAT group, then with one.** A service company employs the firm's support staff and recharges the cost to the LLP. **Without a VAT group**: the recharge is a **standard-rated supply (20%)**; the company adds VAT, the LLP recovers it as input tax (assuming it is fully taxable), so it is a cash-flow/administration cost, not an absolute one. **With a VAT group** (the LLP controls the company, eligible under s.43A since 1 Nov 2019): the recharge is **disregarded** under s.43(1), no VAT and no VAT invoice, one return through the representative member. Caption: "the recharge is standard-rated unless you VAT-group; an LLP can group with its service company since November 2019."
2. **Arm's-length recharge vs parked profit.** A service company supplies IT and premises and charges the LLP **cost plus a commercial mark-up**: this is a genuine arm's-length recharge, the company's modest mark-up is taxed at corporation tax, and the mixed-membership rules do **not** apply because the company is not a profit-sharing member (§2.B). Contrast a version where the recharge is inflated so the company holds what is really partner profit: now the substance is profit diversion, exposed to the mixed-membership rules (s.850C) and the general anti-abuse rule, and the LLP's deduction is challengeable on transfer-pricing / wholly-and-exclusively grounds. Caption: "a commercial cost-plus recharge is fine; parking partner profit in the company is not."
3. **Premises-owning service company and the option to tax.** The service company buys and refurbishes the firm's premises and recharges rent to the LLP. Land supplies are VAT-exempt, blocking input-VAT recovery on the refurbishment, so the company **opts to tax** (notifying HMRC within 30 days) to recover the VAT on the spend; the Capital Goods Scheme tracks the recovery over 10 intervals if the spend is £250,000+ (VAT-exclusive); the company claims AIA/special-rate allowances on the fit-out. If the company and LLP are VAT-grouped the intra-group rent is disregarded, but the OTT still governs recovery on the third-party spend. Caption: "owning premises means thinking about the option to tax and the Capital Goods Scheme, even inside a VAT group."
