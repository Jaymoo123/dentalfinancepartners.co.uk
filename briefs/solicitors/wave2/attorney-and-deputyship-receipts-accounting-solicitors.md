## The honest thesis (lead and hold)

Money a firm handles when it is acting **as attorney (donee of an LPA/EPA)** or as a **Court of Protection deputy** for someone who lacks capacity ("P") is **client money** under SRA Accounts Rule 2.1 limb (c): money held "as a trustee or as the holder of a specified office or appointment, such as donee of a power of attorney, Court of Protection deputy". The definition names attorneys and deputies expressly, so there is no doubt about status. But this money sits inside a **second supervisory regime**: the **Office of the Public Guardian (OPG)** supervises deputies (and oversees attorneys), a property-and-affairs deputy must usually put a **security bond** in place before they can act, and a deputy files an **annual report**. The page must hold two threads cleanly: the SRA client-account mechanics (the same client-account, ledgering, Rule 3.3, reconciliation discipline) and the OPG/MCA layer (supervision levels, the bond, the annual report, the best-interests duty), without straying into giving Court of Protection legal advice. The firm's fees are a standard-rated supply (VAT). Distinct from the everyday client-money page because the money is fiduciary and dual-regulated.

## H2 outline (aim 9-12 sections, ~2,900-3,400 words)

1. **Two regimes at once: the SRA and the OPG** · set the scene. When a firm (or an individual within it) acts as attorney or deputy, or assists an attorney/deputy with the financial management, it handles money that is both **client money for the SRA** and **P's money supervised by the OPG** under the Mental Capacity Act 2005. The page covers the accounting/compliance interface, not Court of Protection litigation. Signpost: status, the account, the OPG layer, the bond, the annual report, fees/VAT.
2. **The Rule 2.1 status: attorney and deputy money is client money** · the anchor. Rule 2.1(c) defines client money to include money held "as a trustee or as the holder of a specified office or appointment, **such as donee of a power of attorney, Court of Protection deputy** or trustee of an occupational pension scheme". So money the firm holds in that capacity is client money: it must go into the client account, be ledgered, reconciled and protected like any other. Distinguish the two common scenarios: (a) the firm/its member is **appointed** as attorney or deputy (limb (c) squarely); (b) the firm **acts for** an attorney/deputy client managing P's affairs (limb (a)/(b)). Cross-link `what-counts-as-client-money-uk-solicitors`.
3. **LPA, EPA and deputyship: what the firm is handling** · brief, factual framing of the instruments so the reader knows what money flows where: a **lasting power of attorney** (property and financial affairs) or older **enduring power of attorney** appoints a donee/attorney to manage P's finances; a **Court of Protection deputy** is appointed by the court where there is no valid LPA/EPA and P lacks capacity. The money: P's income (pension, benefits), realised assets, property proceeds, bills paid for P. Keep it factual, not legal advice on capacity.
4. **The client account and Rule 3.3 in a deputyship/attorney matter** · the same client-account discipline (Rule 3, separate client account, named "client"). The Rule 3.3 banking-facility prohibition is again the live trap: the firm must not turn its client account into P's general bank account. P's day-to-day finances are typically run through P's own bank account (operated by the attorney/deputy); the firm's client account holds money only in respect of the regulated services it delivers. Cross-link `solicitor-trust-accounting-guide`, `probate-estate-administration-client-account-sra`.
5. **Ledgering and reconciliation for P's money** · open a dedicated client ledger for the matter (in P's name/the deputyship), record every receipt and payment with purpose, feed the five-weekly three-way reconciliation (Rule 8.3). Note the parallel record-keeping the OPG expects (the deputy must account to the OPG, see annual report), so the firm keeps clean records that serve both regulators. Never overdraw the ledger.
6. **The OPG supervision layer** · factual. The OPG supervises deputies under the Mental Capacity Act 2005. Supervision levels (verified): **general supervision** applies to all new deputies in their first year; a property-and-affairs deputy can move to **minimal supervision** after year one where they manage **less than £21,000** and no longer need general oversight (reduced fees, shorter annual report). The OPG may arrange **visits** by Court of Protection visitors. State this is oversight of how P's money is managed, layered on top of the SRA's oversight of how the firm's client account is run. Light, factual, not advice.
7. **The deputy's security bond** · a distinctive feature worth its own section. A property-and-affairs deputy must usually put a **security bond** in place before they can act: the court order authorising the deputy is **not released until appropriate security is in place**, and the bond is **insurance solely for the protection of P** (it protects P's estate against loss caused by the deputy's failure to perform their duties; it does not protect the deputy). The OPG/court recommends an approved bond provider. Frame factually: it is a cost of acting as deputy, not the firm's client money.
8. **The annual report and accounting to the OPG** · a deputy must send the OPG an **annual deputy report** explaining the decisions made and accounting for P's money; minimal-supervision deputies file a shorter report. This sits alongside (and is informed by) the firm's own client-ledger records. Stress the discipline: clean, contemporaneous records make both the OPG annual report and the SRA reconciliation straightforward.
9. **Fees and VAT** · the firm's professional fees for acting as attorney/deputy, or for assisting one, are a **standard-rated supply of legal/professional services (20%)** (§6, no exemption). For a court-appointed deputy, fees are typically subject to assessment (fixed costs or detailed assessment by the court), but the VAT treatment is the same: standard-rated. Disbursements (court fees, the OPG application/supervision fees the deputy pays, the bond premium) follow the eight-condition test (§6); the bond premium is insurance and the OPG fees are statutory charges. Cross-link `vat-on-probate-and-estate-administration-fees`, `disbursements-vat-treatment-uk-law-firms`.
10. **Interest, residual balances and closing** · Rule 7 fair-interest applies to P's money held by the firm (§5); on death of P or discharge of the deputyship/attorney, account for and return/transmit the money cleanly (a deputyship ends on P's death and the estate then falls to be administered, cross-link `probate-estate-administration-client-account-sra`). A small unreturnable remnant uses the residual-balance route (cross-link `residual-client-balances-clearing-unclaimed-money-sra`).
11. **Common pitfalls** · (i) treating attorney/deputy money as the firm's or as office money (it is client money, Rule 2.1(c)); (ii) running P's bank account through the client account (Rule 3.3); (iii) acting before the security bond is in place; (iv) missing the OPG annual report; (v) forgetting the firm's fee is standard-rated. Inline CTA to lead form (segment: private-client/Court of Protection firm wanting client-account and fee/VAT review).
12. **FAQ** (frontmatter, 10-14 Qs).

## VERIFIED ANCHOR BLOCK (quote/paraphrase only from this; verified at primary source 2026-06-03)

- **Rule 2.1(c) names attorneys and deputies** · SRA Accounts Rules 2019 (https://www.sra.org.uk/solicitors/standards-regulations/accounts-rules/). VERIFIED VERBATIM: client money includes money held or received "as a trustee or as the holder of a specified office or appointment, **such as donee of a power of attorney, Court of Protection deputy** or trustee of an occupational pension scheme". §5. This is the squarely on-point limb for this page.
- **Rule 3 / 3.3 (client account + banking-facility prohibition)** · §5 (as above): client account separate, named "client"; no banking facilities; payments must relate to delivery of regulated services.
- **Rule 7 (interest), Rule 8.3 (five-weekly reconciliation), Rule 12.2 (report exemption average not exceeding £10,000 AND maximum not exceeding £250,000)** · §5. Do NOT write "£250".
- **OPG supervision levels** · gov.uk "Supervision, support and visits" (https://www.gov.uk/become-deputy/supervision-support-and-visits), verified 2026-06-03: **general supervision** for all new deputies in the first year; **minimal supervision** available to a property-and-affairs deputy after year one where they manage **less than £21,000** and no longer require general oversight (reduced fees, shorter annual reports); the OPG may arrange visits by Court of Protection visitors.
- **Security bond** · gov.uk (deputy guidance, verified 2026-06-03): a property-and-affairs deputy may have to pay a **security bond before the Court of Protection releases the deputy order**; "If you need to pay a security bond, you can't start acting for the person until you've paid it"; the bond is **insurance to protect the estate of the person you are acting for** if the deputy fails to perform their duties, and "the security given by the bond is solely for the protection of P ... there is no protection for the deputy". The court/OPG recommends an approved bond provider.
- **Annual deputy report** · gov.uk (verified 2026-06-03): "when you become a deputy, you must send an annual deputy report to the Office of the Public Guardian (OPG) each year explaining the decisions you've made"; minimal-supervision deputies submit shorter reports.
- **Mental Capacity Act 2005 / OPG** · the OPG is established under, and deputies/attorneys operate under, the Mental Capacity Act 2005 (best-interests duty). Context only; do NOT give MCA legal advice.
- **VAT** · the firm's attorney/deputy fees are a standard-rated supply of services (20%), no exemption (§6).

## Worked-example sketches (illustrative only; label as not advice; no real figures presented as a quote)

1. **The firm appointed as property-and-affairs deputy.** A firm is appointed deputy for P who lacks capacity. Walk the setup: the security bond must be in place before the firm can act; P's day-to-day income and bills run through P's own bank account; money the firm holds in the deputyship (e.g. proceeds it realises, a reserve for legal work) is client money under Rule 2.1(c), ledgered in P's name and reconciled five-weekly. The firm files the OPG annual report from the same records and bills its assessed fees plus 20% VAT. Caption: "deputy money is client money, but you cannot act until the bond is in place."
2. **The Rule 3.3 trap in an attorney matter.** Acting under an LPA, the firm is asked to route all of P's pension, benefits and household bills through its client account for convenience. Show why this risks a Rule 3.3 breach (banking facilities; payments not tied to the firm's regulated services) and the fix: run P's routine finances through P's own account; hold only matter-related money in client account. Caption: "you manage P's affairs, you do not bank for P."
3. **Closing on P's death.** P dies; the deputyship ends. The firm accounts for P's money, files the final OPG report, and the funds it holds become part of P's estate to be administered (the analysis then shifts to estate client money). A tiny unreturnable remnant goes through the residual-balance route. Caption: "the deputyship ends at death; the money flows into the estate."
