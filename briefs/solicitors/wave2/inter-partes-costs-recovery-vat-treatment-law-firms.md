## The honest thesis (lead and hold)

The single rule that governs VAT on recovered (inter-partes / party-and-party) costs is this: you claim VAT from the other side ONLY to the extent your own client cannot recover it. If your client is VAT-registered and using the legal services for its taxable business, your client recovers the VAT as input tax, so the costs are claimed NET of VAT (the paying party pays no VAT). If your client cannot recover the VAT (a private individual, a non-registered party, or a matter not for a taxable business purpose), the costs are claimed INCLUSIVE of VAT, and the paying party bears it. This flows from the indemnity principle (you can only recover what the receiving party is actually out of pocket for) and from a long-standing HMRC / Law Society Revenue Law Committee agreement. Critically, the firm always invoices its OWN client for VAT; it never issues a tax invoice to the paying party (who received no supply and cannot reclaim). The page lays out the rule, the agreement, and the accounting.

## H2 outline (aim 9-12 sections, ~2,900-3,500 words)

1. **The one rule that governs VAT on recovered costs** · lead with it: claim VAT against the other side only to the extent your client cannot recover it as input tax. Signpost the two cases (registered client = net; non-registered = inclusive) and the indemnity-principle root.
2. **The supply is to your client, not the paying party** · the foundation. The firm supplies legal services to its OWN client (standard-rated at 20%, §6); the paying party never receives a supply from the receiving party's solicitor. So the firm invoices its client and accounts for output VAT on that bill; the paying party simply reimburses (an indemnity), it does not buy a supply. Verbatim anchor: a tax invoice goes to the firm's own client, never to the paying party.
3. **The indemnity principle** · you can only recover from the other side costs the receiving party is liable to pay. If the receiving party can reclaim the VAT, the receiving party is not "out of pocket" on the VAT, so it cannot be recovered inter partes. Frame the indemnity principle plainly (the costs order indemnifies, it does not enrich).
4. **The HMRC / Law Society Revenue Law Committee agreement (VATSC11534)** · the authority. Where the client is a registered fully taxable person obtaining the legal services for its business, the indemnifying party need pay only the costs EXCLUSIVE of VAT; where the client cannot recover the VAT, the indemnifying party pays the costs AND the VAT (but cannot itself recover that VAT). Verbatim in anchor block. State that this is the settled, agreed position, not the firm's choice.
5. **Case 1: receiving party is VAT-registered and recovers input tax (costs claimed net)** · the bill of costs is drawn NET of VAT; the paying party pays the net costs; the receiving party's client reclaims the VAT on the firm's invoice as input tax. Worked sketch below. Note partial-exemption nuance: if the client is partially exempt and cannot fully recover, the irrecoverable proportion can be claimed inter partes (cross-link partial exemption page).
6. **Case 2: receiving party cannot recover (costs claimed VAT-inclusive)** · a private individual or non-registered party. The bill of costs includes VAT; the paying party reimburses the VAT-inclusive figure. The paying party cannot reclaim that VAT (no supply was made to it). Worked sketch below.
7. **The certificate / statement in the bill of costs** · CPR Practice Direction 44 requires a statement in the bill as to whether the receiving party can recover VAT (e.g. is registered and the input tax position). Frame as the procedural hook that operationalises the rule; the costs draftsman certifies the VAT position. (Attribute to CPR PD 44; do not over-specify a paragraph number you have not verified.)
8. **No tax invoice to the paying party** · the discipline. The firm must not issue a VAT invoice/tax invoice to the paying party, because the paying party did not receive the supply and is not entitled to input-tax credit. The firm invoices its own client; the paying party gets a note/copy of the costs, not a tax invoice. Verbatim anchor.
9. **Accounting for it in the firm's books** · output VAT is accounted on the firm's bill to its own client at the tax point (§6.D), regardless of when or whether the paying party pays. Recovered costs received from the other side are receipts against the client's account/the firm's fees, not a separate VAT event. Cross-link `vat-tax-point-time-of-supply-law-firm-billing`. Tie to client-money rules if costs are received into client account (§5).

## VERIFIED ANCHOR BLOCK (quote/paraphrase only from this; verified at primary source 2026-06-03)

- **The inter-partes costs-VAT rule, HMRC VATSC11534 (Law Society Revenue Law Committee agreement, verified 2026-06-03):**
  - Where the receiving party's client IS VAT-registered and recovers the VAT: *"the indemnifying party need only pay the costs exclusive of VAT"* (i.e. costs claimed net of VAT).
  - Where the receiving party's client is NOT registered / cannot recover: *"the indemnifying party is liable to pay the costs and VAT as well. However, the indemnifying party cannot recover the VAT"* (i.e. costs claimed VAT-inclusive, and the paying party cannot reclaim it).
  - Tax invoice: *"the solicitor whose costs are to be paid should deliver a tax invoice to his own client"*; and *"in no circumstances may a tax invoice be issued by the client's solicitor to the paying party who is not in law entitled to receive an input tax credit as the services have not been rendered to him"* (the paying party receives only a note of the costs).
  - Source: https://www.gov.uk/hmrc-internal-manuals/vat-supply-and-consideration/vatsc11534 (HMRC internal manual, "Agreement with the Law Society's Revenue Law Committee on VAT in respect of third party cost payments").
- **The legal supply is to the firm's own client, standard-rated (§6):** legal services are standard-rated at 20%; the firm accounts for output VAT on its bill to its own client. The paying party receives no supply.
- **Indemnity principle** · a party may recover from the other side only the costs it is itself liable to bear (so VAT recoverable as the client's input tax cannot be recovered inter partes). Authority: the indemnity principle as applied on assessment of costs; CPR Part 44 and PD 44 require the bill to state the receiving party's VAT-recovery position. CPR PD 44 (VAT in bills of costs). NOTE FOR WRITER: attribute the VAT-statement requirement to CPR PD 44 generally; do not cite an unverified paragraph number.
- **Tax point on the firm's own bill (§6.D):** VATA 1994 s.6 (basic tax point on performance; earlier of invoice or payment; 14-day rule). Standard rate **20%** since 4 January 2011.
- **Disbursements / counsel (§6, §6.B):** the eight-condition disbursement test applies to recovered disbursements; counsel's fee defaults to a supply to the firm.

## Worked-example sketches (illustrative only; label as not advice)

1. **VAT-registered commercial client wins (costs claimed net).** Firm acts for a registered company in a commercial dispute; the company recovers the firm's VAT as input tax. The bill of costs against the losing side is drawn NET of VAT. The loser pays the net costs; the firm's own client reclaims the VAT on the firm's invoice. Caption: "registered client recovers the VAT, so the other side pays costs net of VAT."
2. **Private individual wins (costs claimed inclusive).** Firm acts for a private claimant in a personal claim; the client cannot recover VAT. The bill of costs is drawn VAT-INCLUSIVE. The losing side reimburses the VAT-inclusive costs; it cannot reclaim that VAT (no supply was made to it). Caption: "non-recovering client claims costs inclusive of VAT; the loser bears it and cannot reclaim it."
3. **No tax invoice to the loser.** The firm invoices its own client for VAT and delivers a tax invoice to that client only; the losing party gets a note/copy of the costs, never a tax invoice. Caption: "you invoice your client, not the other side; the other side has no input-tax claim."
4. **Partially exempt client.** A partially exempt client recovers only part of the input VAT; the irrecoverable proportion can be claimed inter partes, the recoverable proportion cannot. Caption: "for a partially exempt client, claim only the VAT the client genuinely cannot recover."
