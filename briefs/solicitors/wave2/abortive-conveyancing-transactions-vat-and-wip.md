## The honest thesis (lead and hold)

A transaction falling through does not make the work tax-free. If you bill the client an abortive-cost charge, you have **supplied legal services** (you took instructions, did the title work, raised enquiries, reviewed searches), and that fee is **standard-rated for VAT at 20%** in exactly the same way as a completion fee (§6). The collapse changes the commercial outcome, not the VAT character of the supply. Three things then have to be handled cleanly: (1) the **VAT on the abortive fee itself** (standard-rated); (2) the **WIP** for the unbilled time, which under FRS 102 you carry as an asset and must **write off** to the extent you cannot or will not recover it (§4); and (3) the **disbursements already incurred** (searches, an early Land Registry priority search, a bank transfer fee), each tested on the §6 eight conditions just as on a live matter, with the Brabners search-fee point (§6.A) applying unchanged. The page also pins the **tax point** (§6.D): a "request for payment" is not a VAT invoice, so output VAT on the abortive charge crystallises only when you issue the bill/VAT invoice or take payment, whichever is first.

## H2 outline (aim 9-12 sections, ~2,900-3,500 words)

1. **When a conveyancing deal falls through: the accounting and VAT questions** · framing. Abortive transactions are common (chain breaks, gazumping, failed survey/mortgage). State up front that this page is about the firm's OWN tax and VAT position, not about advising the client on their loss. Signpost the three threads: VAT on the abortive fee, WIP write-off, disbursements already paid.
2. **Is an abortive-transaction fee subject to VAT?** · YES. Legal services are standard-rated at 20% (§6); a fee for work actually performed is consideration for that supply whether or not the deal completed. The trigger for VAT is that you made a supply of services, not that the client got the house. Contrast the lay assumption ("no completion, so no VAT"). State plainly: if your engagement letter lets you charge for abortive work, that charge carries 20% VAT.
3. **"No completion, no fee" and what it really means for VAT** · many residential firms market "no move, no fee" or "no completion, no legal fee". That is a **commercial pricing promise**, not a VAT rule: where it applies you simply do not raise a fee (no consideration, no output VAT), but you may still have disbursements to recover and WIP to write off. Where your retainer carves out an abortive-cost charge (common for survey/search costs and a contribution to time), that charge is standard-rated. Cross-link `fee-structure-for-uk-residential-conveyancing-firms`.
4. **The tax point on an abortive charge (VATA 1994 s.6)** · §6.D. The basic tax point is when the services are performed (here, effectively when the matter is closed as abortive), but an **actual tax point** arises earlier if you issue a VAT invoice or receive payment first; the **14-day rule** can move the tax point to the invoice date. A **request for payment on account is not a VAT invoice and creates no tax point**. Practical: account for output VAT on the abortive fee in the period in which you issue the bill/VAT invoice or are paid, whichever is first. Cross-link `vat-tax-point-time-of-supply-law-firm-billing`.
5. **Work in progress on an abortive matter (FRS 102)** · §4. Unbilled time is WIP, recognised under FRS 102 Section 23 as the firm obtains the right to consideration. On a failed deal, recoverability changes: to the extent you will not bill or cannot recover the time, the WIP carrying value must be **written down/written off**, reducing recognised revenue and so reducing taxable profit for the period. State that this is a recognition/measurement question, not a "loss you can simply claim": you reduce WIP to its recoverable amount. Note LLPs are on the accruals basis (excluded from the cash basis, §4), so WIP movements hit the tax computation.
6. **Disbursements already incurred when the deal collapses** · apply the §6 eight-condition disbursement test to each cost already paid out: searches, an OS1/OS2 priority search or Land Registry fee, a bankruptcy search, a CHAPS fee. SDLT/LBTT/LTT will usually not yet have been paid (it falls at completion), so that rarely features. Make clear that an abortive matter does not change WHETHER something is a disbursement; it only changes whether you will recover it from the client.
7. **Search fees on an abortive matter (Brabners)** · §6.A. The search-fee position is unchanged by the collapse: a search you read and interpreted in advising the client is part of your standard-rated supply (Brabners, R&C Brief 6 (2020)), so if you recharge it on the abortive bill you add 20% VAT; a search genuinely passed through unused that meets all eight conditions can be a VAT-free disbursement. The Brabners principle does not flip just because the deal died. Cross-link `conveyancing-vat-rules-uk-2025-26` and `disbursements-vs-recharges-conveyancing-vat`.
8. **CHAPS, bank and panel-manager fees on an abortive file** · §6, §6.A. These are standard-rated recharges (the bank/panel manager supplies the firm, not the client), abortive or not. If you recharge them, add VAT.
9. **Client account and money on account when a deal aborts** · §5. Any unused money on account held in client account is the client's money and must be returned promptly (or applied to a properly billed sum under Rule 4.3, after a bill); do not use it as a holding balance. Cross-link `client-account-handling-residential-conveyancing`. Keep this short (regulatory context, not the core tax point).
10. **Worked sketches** · see below.
11. **Practical checklist for handling an abortive conveyance** · (i) confirm whether the retainer allows an abortive-cost charge; (ii) raise the abortive bill with 20% VAT on the fee; (iii) recharge disbursements per the §6/§6.A test (VAT on interpreted searches and bank fees, no VAT on clean disbursements); (iv) account for output VAT at the correct tax point; (v) write WIP down to recoverable amount; (vi) return unused client money promptly. ## VERIFIED ANCHOR BLOCK (quote/paraphrase only from this; locked in house_positions, primary-source-verified 2026-06-03)

- **Legal services standard-rated at 20% (§6, VATA 1994):** a fee for legal services actually performed is consideration for a standard-rated supply; an abortive-transaction charge is therefore standard-rated at 20%. Registration threshold £90,000 / deregistration £88,000 (do NOT use the old £85,000). The collapse of the deal does not change the VAT character of work done.
- **Eight-condition disbursement test (§6, VAT Notice 700 §25.1.1 / HMRC VTAXPER39000):** a payment is a VAT-free disbursement only if ALL EIGHT conditions are met (agent of the client; client received/used; client responsible to pay; client authorised; client knew a third party would supply; separately itemised; recover exactly the amount paid; clearly additional to the firm's own supply). Apply unchanged to an abortive matter.
- **Brabners search-fee principle (§6.A):** *Brabners LLP v HMRC* [2017] UKFTT 0666 (TC), TC06093 · a search the firm uses/interprets in its advice is part of the firm's standard-rated supply, not a disbursement; R&C Brief 6 (2020) withdrew the postal-search concession from 1 December 2020. CHAPS/bank fees are standard-rated recharges (the bank supplies the firm).
- **Tax point (§6.D, VATA 1994 s.6):** basic tax point is when services are performed (s.6(3)); an earlier VAT invoice or payment pulls it forward (s.6(4)); the 14-day rule (s.6(5)) can move it to the invoice date. A "request for payment on account" is NOT a VAT invoice and creates NO tax point. Standard rate has been 20% since 4 January 2011.
- **WIP / FRS 102 (§4):** WIP is recognised under FRS 102 Section 23 as the firm obtains the right to consideration; carried as an asset and taxed as recognised. On an abortive matter, reduce WIP to its recoverable amount (write off unrecoverable time). LLPs and partnerships with a corporate partner are EXCLUDED from the cash basis, so most law firms are on accruals and WIP movements affect the tax computation. Do NOT tell an LLP it can use the cash basis.
- **SDLT/LBTT/LTT note (§7):** the property transaction tax usually falls at completion, so on an aborted matter it is typically not yet paid. If it had been paid early in error, that is a refund/overpayment question (see the sibling page `sdlt-refund-and-overpayment-claims-conveyancers`).

## Worked-example sketches (illustrative only; label as not advice)

1. **Abortive purchase, fee charged.** A residential purchase collapses after the buyer pulls out post-survey. The firm's retainer allows an abortive-cost charge. The firm bills a fixed abortive charge for the work done plus the cost of two interpreted searches. VAT at 20% applies to the fee AND to the recharged interpreted searches (Brabners). Caption: "abortive does not mean VAT-free; the fee and any interpreted searches are standard-rated."
2. **"No completion, no fee" file with disbursements outstanding.** The firm operates "no move, no legal fee" and so raises no fee, but has paid for searches and a priority search. It recovers the genuine pass-through disbursements (no VAT) and writes off the unbilled WIP. Caption: "no fee means no output VAT on the fee, but disbursements and WIP still need handling."
3. **WIP write-off on a dead chain.** A firm holds £X of unbilled time on a matter that has died with no recoverable charge. It writes the WIP down to nil, reducing recognised revenue and taxable profit for the period under FRS 102. Caption: "carry WIP at its recoverable amount; write off what you will not bill."
