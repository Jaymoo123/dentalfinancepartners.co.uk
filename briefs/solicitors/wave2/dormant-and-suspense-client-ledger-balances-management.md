## The honest thesis (lead and hold)

A clean client account has every penny identified to a named client or matter, with a clear reason it is still held. Suspense ledgers (a holding line for money you cannot yet allocate), unidentified receipts (money in with no obvious owner) and dormant balances (small or old sums left on closed or inactive matters) are the three ways a client account drifts away from that ideal, and they are exactly what an accountant's report (and an SRA inspection) homes in on. The SRA's expectation is that suspense use is minimal and short-lived, that unidentified money is actively investigated, and that dormant balances are returned to the client or properly disposed of, not parked indefinitely. The page must explain the SRA expectation, how each category is treated in the Rule 8.3 reconciliation, and a clean-up routine to run before the report. The actual DISPOSAL route for genuinely unreturnable money (prescribed circumstances, de minimis, charity donation) is the sibling page A3, route it there.

## H2 outline (aim 9-12 sections, ~2,800-3,300 words)

1. **Why dormant and suspense balances are a compliance flashpoint** · set the scene: every unidentified or stale balance is a question the firm cannot answer about its own client account, and the accountant's report and SRA inspections target exactly this. Define the three categories up front (suspense, unidentified receipts, dormant matter balances) and signpost that the goal is a fully-identified client account.
2. **What a suspense ledger is, and the SRA's narrow tolerance for it** · a suspense ledger is a temporary holding line for money you genuinely cannot yet allocate. The SRA expects suspense use to be minimal, justified and short-lived, with active steps to clear it, NOT a dumping ground. Frame the discipline: open a suspense entry only when you must, record why, and chase it to resolution.
3. **Unidentified receipts: the investigate-and-allocate duty** · money arrives with no clear owner (a transfer with no reference, an overpayment, a payment for a matter you cannot trace). The expectation is prompt investigation (bank narrative, sender, matching open matters), allocation to the correct client ledger as soon as identified, and documentation of the steps taken. Tie to the mixed-money sibling (a badly-split mixed receipt is a common source of an unidentified slice).
4. **Dormant matter balances: small sums on closed or inactive files** · the long tail. Money left on completed or long-inactive matters: a few pounds of unspent disbursement float, an uncashed payment, a residual after a bill. The expectation is that these are returned to the client promptly when the matter ends, and that the firm proactively reviews and clears them rather than letting them age.
5. **How these balances are treated in the Rule 8.3 reconciliation** · the control. The five-weekly reconciliation (Rule 8.3, ledger total to cash book to bank statement, signed off by the COFA or a manager) is where suspense, unidentified and dormant balances must be visible and explained. Best practice: a separate listing of suspense/unidentified items and aged dormant balances attached to each reconciliation, with an explanation and an action for each, reviewed by the COFA. Cross-link the reconciliation pages.
6. **The link to the banking-facility prohibition (Rule 3.3)** · why aged balances are not just untidy. Money sitting in the client account that is not tied to delivering regulated services drifts towards being a banking facility (Rule 3.3), a top SRA enforcement area. Holding dormant money indefinitely "for" a client who no longer needs a regulated service is exactly the risk. Cross-link the breaches page.
7. **Pre-accountant's-report clean-up: the routine** · the core practical payload. A staged clean-up before the period end and the report: (i) run an aged client-ledger listing and a suspense/unidentified listing; (ii) investigate and allocate every unidentified item; (iii) clear suspense to its proper home; (iv) return dormant balances to clients (contact the client, repay); (v) for genuinely unreturnable balances, follow the proper disposal route (route to sibling A3, do not detail it here); (vi) document every step. Explain that a clean ledger going into the report avoids qualifications and queries.
8. **What the accountant's report looks for** · brief, honest: the reporting accountant tests whether client money is identified, whether suspense/unidentified items are explained and cleared, whether dormant balances are being actioned, and whether reconciliations are timely and signed off (Rule 8.3). A report can be qualified where balances are unexplained or systemic. Cross-link the existing report pages. Keep the Rule 12 trigger/exemption LIGHT (route the exemption detail to the no-client-account D4 page and the existing report-threshold pages).
9. **Prevention: the controls that stop balances accreting** · forward-looking: a matter-closure checklist (zero the client ledger on file closure), prompt return of residual balances, prompt allocation of mixed receipts (cross-link the mixed-money sibling), strong bank-narrative discipline, and regular COFA review of aged balances between reconciliations. ## VERIFIED ANCHOR BLOCK (quote/paraphrase only from this; verified at primary source 2026-06-03)

- **Reconciliation: SRA Accounts Rules 2019, Rule 8.3** (https://www.sra.org.uk/solicitors/standards-regulations/accounts-rules/). Verbatim: *"You complete at least every five weeks, for all client accounts held or operated by you, a reconciliation of the bank or building society statement balance with the cash book balance and the client ledger total, a record of which must be signed off by the COFA or a manager of the firm."* CONFIRMED: at least every **five weeks**.
- **Client ledger records: Rule 8.1** (the firm keeps accurate, contemporaneous and chronological records, a separate client ledger for each client/matter showing all dealings with client money) - the records that must reconcile and against which suspense/unidentified items stand out. (Paraphrase; the core obligation is contemporaneous, accurate, fully identified records.)
- **Banking-facility prohibition: Rule 3.3** *"You must not use a client account to provide banking facilities to clients or third parties; payments into, and transfers or withdrawals from, a client account must be in respect of the delivery by you of regulated services."* (§5) - the rule aged/dormant balances drift towards.
- **Accountant's report trigger: Rule 12.1** verbatim: *"If you have, at any time during an accounting period, held or received client money, or operated a joint account or a client's own account as signatory, you must... obtain an accountant's report for that accounting period within six months of the end of the period."* - so a firm holding client money must obtain a report, which is why the ledgers should be clean before period end. (Keep the exemption LIGHT here, route detail to the report-threshold pages and D4.)
- **Withdrawal of client money: Rule 5.1 / 5.3** - client money may only be withdrawn for the purpose for which it is held, on the client's/third party's instructions, or on the SRA's prior written authorisation (Rule 5.1), and only where sufficient funds are held for that specific client (Rule 5.3). Returning a dormant balance to its client is a withdrawal for the purpose for which it is held. ## Worked-example sketches (illustrative only; label as not advice; no real figures presented as a quote)

1. **The aged dormant float.** A closed conveyancing matter has a small residual disbursement float left on the client ledger eighteen months after completion. Show the review (it surfaces on the aged listing), the action (contact the client, return the balance, zero the ledger), and the record. Caption: "matter closed means client ledger zeroed; a residual float is an action, not a resting place."
2. **The unidentified inbound transfer.** A receipt lands with a bank reference that matches no open matter. Show the investigation (bank narrative, sender bank, cross-check open files), the temporary suspense posting with a documented reason, and the prompt allocation once identified. Caption: "suspense is a waiting room, not a destination; investigate and clear."
3. **The pre-report clean-up sweep.** Two months before period end, the COFA runs the aged and suspense listings, allocates the unidentified items, returns the dormant balances, and documents the residue. The accountant's report comes back without a client-money qualification. Caption: "clean the ledgers before the accountant arrives, not after the report is qualified."
