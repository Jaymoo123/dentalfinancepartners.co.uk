## The honest thesis (lead and hold)

Residual (unclaimed/dormant) client-account balances are a near-universal housekeeping problem: an uncashed cheque, an over-payment, a missing beneficiary on an estate, a small remnant after a matter closed. The temptation, sweeping it to office account, is a breach. Client money can be withdrawn from a client account only on the **Rule 5.1** conditions, and the only clean route to dispose of a genuinely unreturnable balance is to **pay it to a charity** under the **prescribed circumstances** in the SRA's guidance. The hard line is **£500 per client matter**: balances of **£500 or less on any one client matter** may be paid to a charity **without prior SRA authorisation** provided all the conditions are met; for balances **over £500** the firm needs the **SRA's prior written authorisation** before removing the money. The page must state the £500 line and the conditions precisely (reasonable steps to trace, six-year records, central register, charity number, indemnity, no deduction of tracing costs) and stress this is disposal, not interest. Precise, procedural, SRA-cited.

## H2 outline (aim 9-12 sections, ~2,800-3,300 words)

1. **What a residual client balance is, and why you cannot just keep it** · define it: client money left on the client account after the matter is over that the firm has not returned to its rightful owner (uncashed cheque, small over-payment, missing beneficiary, remnant on a closed file). State the bright line: it is still client money; you cannot transfer it to office account as a windfall, and you cannot leave it indefinitely. Probate and long-running litigation are the usual sources. Signpost the Rule 5.1 route and the £500 charity line.
2. **First, return it to the rightful owner** · the primary obligation: client money is held for a purpose and belongs to someone. Before any disposal you must take **reasonable steps to return** it. State what "reasonable" turns on (per the SRA guidance): the age of the balance, the amount, whether you have the client's up-to-date contact details, and if not the cost of tracing. Document every step. Most balances should be returned, not disposed of.
3. **The Rule 5.1 withdrawal conditions** · the legal frame. Client money may be withdrawn from client account only: (a) for the purpose for which it is held; (b) on the client's (or the third party's) instructions; (c) on the **SRA's prior written authorisation or in prescribed circumstances**. Returning the balance to its owner is limb (a)/(b); disposing of a genuinely unreturnable balance uses limb (c), the prescribed-circumstances charity route. Quote Rule 5.1 verbatim. Cross-link `handling-client-money-interest-sra-rules` (a different point, disposal not interest).
4. **The £500 charity route (prescribed circumstances), no SRA authorisation** · the core anchor. Where a residual balance is **£500 or less on any one client matter**, the firm may withdraw it and **pay it to a charity of its choice** without prior SRA authorisation, provided ALL the conditions are met (next section). Stress "per client matter" (the test is applied matter by matter, not aggregated across the firm). This is the prescribed-circumstances route under Rule 5.1(c).
5. **The conditions you must satisfy (verbatim from SRA guidance)** · list each: (i) the balance is paid to a charity of your choice; (ii) you have taken **reasonable steps to return** the money to the rightful owner (reasonableness factors above); (iii) you **record the steps** taken and retain those records and all relevant documentation **for at least six years**; (iv) you keep a **central register** recording the name of the rightful owner, the amount, the name of the recipient charity **and its charity number**, and the date of payment; (v) you keep **all receipts from the charity and confirmation of any indemnity** provided against a legitimate claim later made; (vi) you do **not deduct from the residual balance any costs** incurred in tracing or communicating with the rightful owner. All six must hold.
6. **Balances over £500: SRA prior authorisation** · for any residual balance **over £500** on a client matter, the firm must obtain the **SRA's prior written authorisation** before removing the money from client account. Outline the application route (apply to the SRA for authority to withdraw, evidencing the tracing steps and the proposed destination, typically a charity with an indemnity). Cross-reference the SRA's "granting authority to withdraw residual client balances" guidance. Do NOT suggest self-certifying above £500.
7. **The charity and the indemnity** · choosing the charity (the firm's choice), obtaining the charity's number for the register, and the indemnity: where the rightful owner later surfaces with a legitimate claim, the firm remains liable to pay them, so an indemnity from the recipient charity (or otherwise being able to meet the claim) protects the firm. The Solicitors' Charity and others operate to receive such balances. Keep the receipt.
8. **Record-keeping and the central register** · the documentation discipline: the central register as a standing record (owner, amount, charity, charity number, date), the tracing-steps file, the charity receipts and indemnity, all kept at least six years. This is also what an accountant's report reviewer or SRA inspection will look for. Cross-link `sra-accounts-rules-explained-for-uk-solicitors`.
9. **How residual balances arise, and preventing them** · root causes (uncashed distribution cheques on estates, over-payments on account, unbilled disbursement remnants, failing to chase residue) and prevention: clean matter-closing to a nil ledger (cross-link `probate-estate-administration-client-account-sra`), prompt return at file closure, and a periodic dormant-balance review built into the five-weekly reconciliation cycle (Rule 8.3). Note that letting balances accumulate is itself a compliance and Rule 3.3 risk.
10. **Common mistakes** · (i) sweeping a residual balance to office account (a breach, it is still client money); (ii) treating the £500 line as a per-firm or per-year allowance rather than per matter; (iii) self-clearing a balance over £500 without SRA authorisation; (iv) deducting tracing costs from the balance; (v) failing to keep the central register or the charity indemnity; (vi) confusing this disposal route with the Rule 7 interest duty. Inline CTA to lead form (segment: firm with a backlog of dormant client balances wanting a clean-up and a compliant process).
11. **Worked sketches** · see below.

## VERIFIED ANCHOR BLOCK (quote/paraphrase only from this; verified at primary source 2026-06-03)

- **Rule 5.1 (withdrawal conditions)** · SRA Accounts Rules 2019 (https://www.sra.org.uk/solicitors/standards-regulations/accounts-rules/). VERIFIED VERBATIM: client money may be withdrawn from client account only **(a)** "for the purpose for which it is being held"; **(b)** "following receipt of instructions from the client, or the third party for whom the money is held"; **(c)** "on the SRA's prior written authorisation or in prescribed circumstances". §5.
- **The £500 prescribed-circumstances charity route** · SRA "The prescribed circumstances in which you can withdraw client money from client account to pay to a charity of your choice" (https://www.sra.org.uk/solicitors/standards-regulations/withdraw-client-money/), verified 2026-06-03. VERIFIED VERBATIM elements:
  - Threshold: applies to "residual client balances of **£500 or less on any one client matter**".
  - Higher amounts: "For amounts over £500 you will need our authority before removing this money from the client account."
  - Conditions, all required: (1) "the balance is paid to a charity of your choice"; (2) "you have taken reasonable steps to return the money to the rightful owner" (reasonableness depends on "the age of the residual balance; the amount of the residual balance; if you have access to the client's most up to date contact details; if not, the costs associated with tracing your client"); (3) "you record the steps taken to return the money to the rightful owner and retain those records, together with all relevant documentation for at least six years"; (4) "a central register which records the name of the rightful owner on whose behalf the money was held, the amount, name of the recipient charity (and their charity number) and the date of the payment"; (5) "all receipts from the charity and confirmation of any indemnity provided against any legitimate claim subsequently made for the sum they have received"; (6) "you do not deduct from the residual balance any costs incurred in attempting to trace or communicate with the rightful owner".
- **Over-£500 authorisation** · SRA "Granting authority to withdraw residual client balances" guidance (https://www.sra.org.uk/solicitors/guidance/general-granting-authority-withdraw-residual-client-balances/): the firm must obtain the SRA's prior written authorisation. (Confirmed live 2026-06-03.)
- **Rule 7 (interest) is a DIFFERENT point** · this page is disposal of capital balances, not the Rule 7 fair-interest duty (§5). Do not conflate.
- **Records / six-year retention** · ties to the Accounts Rules record-keeping duty (§5). The central register is the standing artefact.

## Worked-example sketches (illustrative only; label as not advice; no real figures presented as a quote)

1. **The small estate remnant cleared to charity (under £500).** After an estate is distributed, a sub-£500 balance remains because a residuary beneficiary's distribution cheque was never cashed and the beneficiary cannot be traced. The firm takes and documents reasonable tracing steps, pays the balance to a charity of its choice, records the owner, amount, charity, charity number and date on its central register, keeps the charity receipt and indemnity, and deducts no tracing costs. Caption: "£500 or less per matter, charity route, no SRA authorisation, but every condition must be met."
2. **The balance over £500 needing SRA authorisation.** A closed litigation matter leaves a balance above £500 that cannot be returned (the client has emigrated, no contact). The firm cannot self-clear it: it applies to the SRA for prior written authorisation, evidencing its tracing steps and the proposed payment to charity with an indemnity, and only withdraws once authority is granted. Caption: "over £500, you must have the SRA's prior authorisation first."
3. **The breach to avoid.** A firm doing a year-end tidy-up sweeps a batch of small dormant balances straight into office account as "aged credits". Show why this breaches the Accounts Rules (it is still client money; Rule 5.1 is not satisfied; no tracing, no register, no charity). The correct fix: reverse it, run each balance through the tracing-then-charity (or SRA-authorisation) route. Caption: "a dormant balance is still client money, never a windfall."
