## The honest thesis (lead and hold)

When a firm seconds a fee-earner to a client or another firm and charges for it, that is a SUPPLY OF STAFF, and a supply of staff is STANDARD-RATED for VAT at 20%. The trap that catches firms is the recharged salary: VAT is due on the FULL charge, including the wages, employer NIC and pension costs passed on, because the consideration for a supply of staff includes those charges. The concession that used to let the salary element be excluded (the "staff hire concession") was WITHDRAWN with effect from 1 April 2009, so since then VAT applies to the whole charge. The page states that plainly, then sets out the genuine exceptions where there is NO taxable supply (a real joint contract of employment, and a secondment involving a temporary suspension of the original employment contract), explains the PAYE / who-employs / recharge mechanics, and gives a clean decision frame. The honest, accuracy-led message: do not assume recharged salary is VAT-free, because that has been wrong since 1 April 2009.

## H2 outline (aim 9-12 sections, ~2,900-3,500 words)

1. **When law firms second solicitors (and why VAT is the first question)** · lead: secondments are common (to a client's in-house team, between firms on a matter, or within a group), and the firm usually recharges the cost. The first question is VAT, because the default answer surprises people. Signpost: supply of staff = standard-rated; the salary element is in scope; the concession is gone; the exceptions; PAYE/recharge mechanics. England and Wales (SRA); VAT UK-wide.
2. **A secondment is a supply of staff, and a supply of staff is standard-rated** · the core. HMRC: there is a supply of staff when one party makes its employee available to another for a consideration, and the consideration "need not necessarily be a fee but can constitute charges by the employer to the other party of wages, national insurance and similar employment costs" (VATSC03540). That supply is standard-rated at 20%. So VAT is charged on the recharge. State the 20% standard rate (in force since 4 January 2011, §6.D).
3. **VAT is due on the FULL charge, including the recharged salary (the staff hire concession is gone)** · THE load-bearing correction. Until 31 March 2009 the "staff hire concession" let employment businesses exclude the salary/NIC/pension element from the VAT value. That concession was WITHDRAWN with effect from 1 April 2009 (VATSC03540: "This concession is WITHDRAWN with effect from 1st April 2009"). Since then VAT is due on the FULL value of the supply, including the salary recharged. Writer: say this explicitly and date it; the most common firm error is treating the recharged salary as outside VAT. Do NOT state the concession still applies. Do NOT state 1 January 2009 (the correct date is 1 April 2009).
4. **The exceptions: when there is NO taxable supply of staff** · two genuine routes out:
   - **Joint contract of employment.** HMRC (VATSC03540): "If staff are jointly employed by two or more parties it is not possible for one of the employers to make a taxable supply of the staff to another." So where the secondee is genuinely jointly employed by both parties, neither makes a VATable supply to the other. Substance matters (both must genuinely be employers).
   - **Temporary suspension of the original employment.** HMRC (VATSC03540): "There also is no taxable supply where a secondment involves a temporary suspension of the original employment contract." Where the secondee's original employment is genuinely suspended and they are employed by the host for the period, there is no supply of staff for VAT.
   Writer: present these as fact-and-substance tests, not labels; getting the paperwork to match the reality is essential.
5. **Paymaster / cost-sharing recharges (handle carefully)** · in some group/cost-sharing structures one entity acts as paymaster, employing staff and recovering each other party's share of the actual cost. Where the recovery is genuinely a reimbursement of a shared cost (not consideration for a supply of staff by one party to another), it may fall outside the scope of VAT, but this is fact-sensitive and easy to get wrong; a recharge that is in substance one firm supplying staff to another is standard-rated. Writer: keep this measured, flag it as an area to take advice on, and do NOT present "paymaster" as a general way to avoid VAT on secondments. (Note: the paymaster framing is not on the VATSC03540 page; rely on the joint-employment and suspended-contract exceptions as the verified routes, and treat paymaster/cost-sharing as a "take advice" area.)
6. **Who employs the secondee, and who runs PAYE** · normally the SECONDING firm remains the employer and continues to operate PAYE and employer NIC (§3) on the secondee's pay; the host pays the seconding firm (with VAT). In a joint-employment or suspended-contract structure the PAYE/employer position changes, so the VAT analysis and the PAYE analysis must line up. Writer: link the employment/PAYE reality to the VAT outcome; they are two sides of the same structure.
7. **The recharge mechanics and the invoice** · the seconding firm invoices the host for the agreed charge (cost-plus or a day rate) PLUS VAT at 20% on the whole amount (default case). The host recovers that input VAT if it is VAT-registered and uses the secondee for taxable activities (an in-house legal team of an exempt or partly exempt business may not fully recover it, §6.C partial exemption). Note the firm's own legal services to clients remain standard-rated (§6); a secondment is a supply of STAFF, a distinct supply from supplying legal services.
8. **Secondment vs supplying legal services (do not confuse)** · if the firm is engaged to DO legal work for the client, that is a supply of legal services (standard-rated, §6) and the fee-earner stays the firm's resource. A secondment is different: the firm makes the PERSON available to work under the host's direction. Both are usually standard-rated, but the analysis (and the employment/PAYE position) differs. Writer: draw this line so the reader classifies the arrangement correctly.
9. **Decision frame and checklist** · (i) default: a secondment is a standard-rated supply of staff, VAT on the FULL charge including salary (concession gone since 1 April 2009); (ii) consider a genuine joint contract of employment or a suspended-contract structure if you want to avoid the supply, but only where the substance supports it; (iii) line up the PAYE/employer position with the VAT analysis; (iv) check the host's input-VAT recovery (partial exemption for in-house/exempt hosts); (v) classify secondment vs supply of legal services correctly; (vi) document everything. Inline CTA (segment: firm structuring a secondment). No pricing.

## VERIFIED ANCHOR BLOCK (quote/paraphrase only from this; verified at primary source 2026-06-03)

- **Supply of staff / consideration (HMRC VATSC03540, verbatim 2026-06-03):** "There is a supply of staff when one party makes available its employee to another party ... in return for a consideration. The consideration for such a supply need not necessarily be a fee but can constitute charges by the employer to the other party of wages, national insurance and similar employment costs." A supply of staff is standard-rated at 20%.
- **Staff hire concession WITHDRAWN 1 April 2009 (HMRC VATSC03540, verbatim 2026-06-03):** "This concession is WITHDRAWN with effect from 1st April 2009." The concession had let the salary/remuneration element be disregarded in valuing the supply; since 1 April 2009 VAT is due on the FULL value, including the salary recharged. (Note: announced in the March 2008 Budget; effective 1 April 2009. State 1 April 2009, NOT 1 January 2009.)
- **Joint-employment exception (HMRC VATSC03540, verbatim 2026-06-03):** "If staff are jointly employed by two or more parties it is not possible for one of the employers to make a taxable supply of the staff to another."
- **Suspended-contract exception (HMRC VATSC03540, verbatim 2026-06-03):** "There also is no taxable supply where a secondment involves a temporary suspension of the original employment contract."
- **VAT standard rate and legal services (§6, §6.D):** legal services are standard-rated at 20% (rate in force since 4 January 2011); registration threshold £90,000 / deregistration £88,000 (from 1 April 2024). A secondment is a supply of STAFF, distinct from a supply of legal services, but both are normally standard-rated.
- **PAYE / employer NIC (§3):** the seconding firm normally remains the employer, operating PAYE and employer (secondary Class 1) NIC at 15% above the £5,000 secondary threshold (from 6 April 2025); this can shift in a joint-employment or suspended-contract structure.
- **Partial exemption on the host side (§6.C):** a VAT-registered host recovers the input VAT on the secondment charge to the extent it is used for taxable supplies; an exempt or partly exempt host (e.g. an in-house team in a financial/insurance business) may not fully recover it.
- **Paymaster/cost-sharing (NOT verified on VATSC03540; treat as "take advice"):** where one entity employs staff and recovers each party's genuine share of the actual cost, the recovery may be outside the scope of VAT, but this is fact-sensitive and not the same as the verified joint-employment/suspended-contract exceptions. Do NOT present it as a general VAT-avoidance route.

## Worked-example sketches (illustrative only; label as not advice; date-tag)

1. **Standard secondment to a client (VAT on the full charge).** A firm seconds an associate to a client's in-house team and recharges salary, employer NIC and a margin. That is a supply of staff: VAT at 20% is due on the WHOLE charge, including the recharged salary (the staff hire concession ended 1 April 2009). Caption: "since 1 April 2009 you charge VAT on the full secondment charge, salary included."
2. **Joint contract of employment (no supply).** The secondee is genuinely jointly employed by both the firm and the host under a joint contract; neither party makes a taxable supply of staff to the other, so there is no VAT on the cost shared between them. Caption: "a genuine joint employment contract takes the arrangement outside a taxable supply of staff."
3. **Temporary suspension of the original employment (no supply).** The associate's original employment is genuinely suspended for the secondment and they are employed by the host for the period; there is no taxable supply of staff. Caption: "if the original employment is genuinely suspended, there is no VATable supply of staff."
4. **Host cannot fully recover the VAT.** A secondee goes to an in-house team in a partly exempt business; the host pays VAT on the charge but cannot recover all of it (partial exemption, §6.C), making the structure more expensive than it first looks. Caption: "the VAT cost bites where the host cannot recover it."
