## The honest thesis (lead and hold)

When a firm is **named as executor** in a will or **appointed as trustee** of a trust, three questions follow. First, **can it be paid?** Historically the office is gratuitous, so payment depends on either a **professional charging clause** in the will/trust instrument (now read generously under **Trustee Act 2000 s.28**) or, where there is no such clause, the **statutory default in Trustee Act 2000 s.29** (a trust corporation, or a trustee acting in a professional capacity, may receive **reasonable remuneration** for services, the latter only with the written agreement of each co-trustee, and only if there is no inconsistent provision in the instrument or any enactment). Second, the **tax/VAT** of those fees: they are the firm's professional income (taxable as trading income), and a **standard-rated supply of services for VAT (20%)**. Third, the **client-money** treatment: estate/trust money the firm controls **as** executor/trustee is client money under SRA Rule 2.1(c) (held "as a trustee or as the holder of a specified office or appointment"), so the full client-account discipline applies, and there is a sharp conflict-of-interest point when the firm pays its own fees out of money it controls. Technical, statute-cited, alert to the conflict.

## H2 outline (aim 9-12 sections, ~3,000-3,500 words)

1. **Adviser vs fiduciary: when the firm IS the executor or trustee** · set the scene and draw the line. Most probate work is the firm advising the PRs (sibling page). This page is the different situation: the firm (or a partner) is **named as executor** or **appointed as trustee** and therefore holds the office. That changes the basis of payment, the client-money analysis and the conflict position. Signpost: can it be paid (charging clause vs s.29), tax/VAT, client money, the conflict.
2. **The default rule: the office is unpaid** · the honest baseline. A trustee or executor cannot, by default, charge for their time; the office is historically gratuitous. Payment requires either an authorising provision (a charging clause) or a statutory entitlement (Trustee Act 2000). State this plainly so the reader sees why the next two sections matter.
3. **The professional charging clause (Trustee Act 2000 s.28)** · the usual route. A well-drafted will/trust includes a charging clause entitling a professional executor/trustee to be paid for services. Under s.28 Trustee Act 2000, where the instrument entitles a trustee to payment for services and the trustee is a trust corporation or is **acting in a professional capacity**, the trustee may be paid even for services that could have been provided by a lay trustee (subject to the instrument not making inconsistent provision). Quote the s.28(1) condition and the s.28(5) definition of "acting in a professional capacity". Note the case-law limit: a professional can charge under the clause only for work within the scope of their profession (i.e. work that would attract their usual professional fees).
4. **The statutory default where there is no charging clause (Trustee Act 2000 s.29)** · the key anchor. Where the instrument is silent (no charging clause), s.29 provides a default: a **trust corporation** (not a charitable trustee) "is entitled to receive reasonable remuneration out of the trust funds for any services that the trust corporation provides"; a **trustee acting in a professional capacity** who is **not a trust corporation, charitable trustee or sole trustee** is entitled to reasonable remuneration "if each other trustee has agreed in writing that he may be remunerated for the services". No entitlement under s.29 if remuneration is otherwise provided for by the trust instrument or by any enactment/subordinate legislation (s.29(5)). Quote the operative wording. Stress two practical limits: a **sole** professional trustee cannot use s.29 (needs a co-trustee's written agreement), and the entitlement is to **reasonable** remuneration.
5. **What "reasonable remuneration" and "professional capacity" mean** · unpack the statutory terms. "Acting in a professional capacity" (s.28(5)): acting in the course of a profession or business that includes providing services in connection with the management or administration of trusts (or an aspect of it). "Reasonable remuneration" is remuneration reasonable in the circumstances for the services provided. The court can assess reasonableness, time spent, hourly rate, seniority. (No pricing on the page; describe the basis, not figures.)
6. **The client-money status of the estate/trust money the firm controls** · the SRA layer. Money the firm holds **as** executor or trustee is client money under Rule 2.1(c) (held "as a trustee or as the holder of a specified office or appointment"). So the full client-account discipline applies: separate client account, one ledger per estate/trust, Rule 3.3 (no banking facilities), Rule 7 interest, Rule 8.3 five-weekly reconciliation, Rule 12 accountant's report. Cross-link `probate-estate-administration-client-account-sra` and `solicitor-trust-accounting-guide`.
7. **The conflict point: paying your own fees from money you control** · the sharp practical risk and a genuine differentiator. When the firm is both the office-holder (controlling the funds) and the service provider (billing for the work), it is paying itself out of money it controls. This demands rigour: a valid authority to charge (clause or s.29), reasonable fees, transparent estate/trust accounts, and care over conflicts (the SRA Principles and Code, and beneficiary scrutiny). Transferring the firm's fees from client money to office account still requires a delivered bill (§5, §6) and the withdrawal to be for the purpose for which the money is held (Rule 5.1(a)). Flag this is where beneficiary disputes and SRA attention concentrate.
8. **Tax of the fees: the firm's trading income** · the executor/trustee remuneration the firm earns is its **professional income**, taxed as trading profits of the firm (income tax + Class 4 NIC for a partnership/LLP via the partners, §2/§3; corporation tax for a company/ABS, §3). It is not a capital receipt. Recognise it under normal revenue-recognition/WIP rules (§4) as the work is done. Keep it short and cross-link the structure/tax pages.
9. **VAT on executor and trustee fees** · the fees are a **standard-rated supply of services (20%)**, no exemption (§6). The firm charges VAT on its executor/trustee remuneration like any other professional fee; an unregistered firm under the £90,000 threshold (from 1 April 2024) does not. Disbursements within the estate/trust follow the eight-condition test (cross-link `vat-on-probate-and-estate-administration-fees`). Note the VAT is accounted for at the tax point on the bill (§6.D), not simply when the firm draws the money.
10. **Practical governance and records** · maintaining estate/trust accounts the beneficiaries (and the SRA accountant's-report reviewer) can scrutinise; documenting the authority to charge (clause or co-trustee written agreement under s.29); keeping fee bills, the reasonableness basis, and the client-ledger reconciliation; six-year retention. Inline CTA to lead form (segment: firm acting as professional executor/trustee wanting fee, VAT and client-money governance review).

## VERIFIED ANCHOR BLOCK (quote/paraphrase only from this; verified at primary source 2026-06-03)

- **Trustee Act 2000 s.28 (charging clause)** · legislation.gov.uk (https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2000/29/section/28). VERIFIED VERBATIM:
  - s.28(1): "Except to the extent (if any) to which the trust instrument makes inconsistent provision, subsections (2) to (4) apply to a trustee if (a) there is a provision in the trust instrument entitling him to receive payment out of trust funds in respect of services provided by him to or on behalf of the trust, and (b) the trustee is a trust corporation or is acting in a professional capacity."
  - s.28(5) definition: "a trustee acts in a professional capacity if he acts in the course of a profession or business which consists of or includes the provision of services in connection with (a) the management or administration of trusts generally or a particular kind of trust, or (b) any particular aspect of the management or administration of trusts generally or a particular kind of trust, and the services he provides to or on behalf of the trust fall within that description."
- **Trustee Act 2000 s.29 (remuneration of certain trustees)** · legislation.gov.uk (https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2000/29/section/29). VERIFIED VERBATIM:
  - s.29(1): a trust corporation that is not a charitable trustee "is entitled to receive reasonable remuneration out of the trust funds for any services that the trust corporation provides to or on behalf of the trust."
  - s.29(2): a trustee acting in a professional capacity who is not a trust corporation, a charitable trustee or a sole trustee "is entitled to receive reasonable remuneration out of the trust funds for any services that he provides to or on behalf of the trust if each other trustee has agreed in writing that he may be remunerated for the services."
  - s.29(5): no entitlement to remuneration under this section "if any provision about his entitlement to remuneration has been made (a) by the trust instrument, or (b) by any enactment or any provision of subordinate legislation."
  - NOTE: s.29 applies to a personal representative as it applies to a trustee (Trustee Act 2000 applies its provisions to PRs); state the executor/PR application, but the verbatim wording above is the trustee text.
- **SRA Rule 2.1(c) (firm as office-holder = client money)** · SRA Accounts Rules 2019 (https://www.sra.org.uk/solicitors/standards-regulations/accounts-rules/). VERIFIED VERBATIM: client money includes money held "as a trustee or as the holder of a specified office or appointment, such as donee of a power of attorney, Court of Protection deputy or trustee of an occupational pension scheme". §5. (Executor/trustee money the firm controls is squarely within this limb.)
- **Rule 5.1 (withdrawal), Rule 3.3, Rule 7, Rule 8.3, Rule 12.2** · §5; Rule 5.1(a) withdrawal "for the purpose for which it is being held" governs paying the firm's fees out (after a bill, §6). Do NOT write "£250" for the report exemption (average not exceeding £10,000 AND maximum not exceeding £250,000).
- **VAT** · executor/trustee fees are a standard-rated supply of services (20%), no exemption; registration threshold £90,000 (from 1 April 2024). §6. Tax point on the bill (§6.D).
- **Tax of the fees** · the firm's professional/trading income (income tax + Class 4 NIC via partners for a partnership/LLP, §2/§3; corporation tax for a company/ABS, §3); recognised under FRS 102 revenue/WIP (§4). Not a capital receipt.

## Worked-example sketches (illustrative only; label as not advice; no real figures presented as a quote)

1. **The firm named executor with a charging clause.** A will names the firm as executor and includes a professional charging clause. Under s.28 the firm (acting in a professional capacity) may charge for its services administering the estate; it holds the estate money as client money under Rule 2.1(c), ledgers it per estate, and transfers its billed fees to office account only after delivering a bill, the fees standard-rated at 20%, the income taxed as the firm's trading profit. Caption: "the charging clause authorises the fee; the client-money rules still govern the money."
2. **No charging clause: relying on s.29.** A trust instrument is silent on remuneration and the firm is one of two professional trustees. The firm can be paid only under s.29(2): it is acting in a professional capacity, it is not a sole trustee, and the other trustee agrees in writing that the firm may be remunerated. The remuneration must be reasonable. A sole professional trustee in the same position could not use s.29 (no co-trustee to agree). Caption: "no clause, no sole trustee, get the co-trustee's written agreement under s.29."
3. **The conflict: paying yourself from money you control.** The firm, as trustee, holds the trust fund and bills the trust for its work. Show the discipline: valid authority to charge, reasonable fees, transparent trust accounts the beneficiaries can scrutinise, a delivered bill before any transfer from client to office account, and the transfer being for the purpose for which the money is held (Rule 5.1(a)). Caption: "office-holder and fee-earner in one, so the governance has to be visible."
