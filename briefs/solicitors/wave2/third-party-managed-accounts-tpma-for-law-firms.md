## The honest thesis (lead and hold)

A TPMA lets a law firm route client funds through an FCA-authorised payment institution operating an escrow payment service, so the firm never receives or holds the money itself. Because the firm does not hold or receive the money, it is NOT client money under the SRA Accounts Rules (it falls outside the Rule 2.1 definition), and the firm sidesteps a large slice of the client-account machinery (the banking-facility risk under Rule 3.3, residual balances, five-weekly reconciliations on that money). The SRA expressly permits TPMAs in **Rule 11** of the Accounts Rules 2019. But a TPMA is not a free pass: Rule 11 imposes real conditions (the provider must be FCA-authorised, the client must be properly informed, the firm must obtain and check regular statements), and the firm still has Code of Conduct duties to safeguard client assets. The page must explain precisely what a TPMA is, how the escrow flow works, the exact rule and its conditions, and where the firm's residual duties sit. Honest, technical, neither salesy nor dismissive.

## H2 outline (aim 9-12 sections, ~2,900-3,400 words)

1. **What a third-party managed account actually is** · plain definition: an account held at a bank or building society by an FCA-authorised third party (an authorised payment institution) operating an **escrow payment service**, into which the client (and other parties) pay, and from which the third party disburses on the joint instruction of the firm and the client. The money is owned beneficially by the third party while held. Contrast in one line with the firm's own client account (firm holds the money) and signpost the office-vs-client-account page.
2. **How the money actually flows** · walk the escrow mechanics: tripartite agreement (provider, firm, client); client pays into the TPMA, not the firm; the firm instructs releases (e.g. to complete a purchase, pay a third party, take its billed fee); the provider executes and reports. Make clear the firm never has the money pass through its own bank account.
3. **The rule that permits it: SRA Rule 11** · the legal anchor. Rule 11 of the SRA Accounts Rules 2019 ("Third party managed accounts"). Quote Rule 11.1 and Rule 11.2 verbatim from the anchor block. State plainly: this is Rule 11, not Rule 4 (Rule 4.2 governs splitting mixed money in your OWN client account, a different thing, cross-link the sibling).
4. **Why TPMA money is NOT client money** · the pivotal regulatory consequence. Money in a TPMA does not fall within the Rule 2.1 client-money definition because it is not "held or received by you" (the firm). Quote the SRA guidance line. Therefore the bulk of the client-account rules do not apply to it. Cross-link `what-counts-as-client-money-uk-solicitors` (the four limbs) to show why "not held or received" takes it outside limb (a) to (d).
5. **The Rule 11 conditions in detail** · the firm's obligations even when using a TPMA: (a) use of the account does not result in the firm receiving or holding the client's money (Rule 11.1(a)); (b) reasonable steps BEFORE accepting instructions to ensure the client is informed of and understands the terms, how fees for the TPMA are paid and who bears them, and the client's right to terminate and to dispute payment requests (Rule 11.1(b)); (c) the firm obtains regular statements from the provider and ensures they accurately reflect all transactions (Rule 11.2). Lay these out as a checklist.
6. **The FCA-authorised-provider condition (the glossary definition)** · the often-missed point: the FCA requirement is not written into Rule 11 itself, it lives in the SRA Glossary definition of "third party managed account". The provider must be an **authorised payment institution**, a **small payment institution that has adopted voluntary safeguarding**, or an **EEA authorised payment institution** (each as defined in the Payment Services Regulations), **regulated by the Financial Conduct Authority**, operating the account as an **escrow payment service**. Practical point: the firm must do due diligence that its provider actually holds FCA authorisation before routing client funds.
7. **What a TPMA solves (the upside, honestly framed)** · removes the Rule 3.3 banking-facility risk on that money (a top enforcement area, cross-link the breaches page), removes the residual-balance and dormant-ledger clean-up problem on TPMA matters (cross-link the dormant/suspense sibling), and, for a firm that holds NO client money at all, can remove the accountant's-report requirement and the Compensation Fund contribution. Keep the report-exemption treatment LIGHT here and route to the D4 page `running-a-law-firm-without-a-client-account-model` for the full strategic case.
8. **What a TPMA does NOT remove** · the firm still owes Code of Conduct duties to safeguard client money and assets; it must still account properly for its own fees and any office money it does receive; if it ALSO holds client money on other matters, the Accounts Rules (and a report) still apply to that money. A TPMA is a tool for specific matters or a whole-firm model, not a way to ignore the rules.
9. **When a TPMA suits a firm (and when it does not)** · suits new firms avoiding client-account set-up, firms wanting to de-risk completions, conveyancing and litigation distributions, firms cutting compliance overhead. Less suited where the firm holds long-term funds (e.g. some probate/trust money), where transaction fees on the TPMA outweigh the saving, or where clients expect a traditional client account. Frame as a structural decision, route the deep dive to D4.
10. **Notifying the SRA and getting set up** · the firm should notify the SRA of TPMA use (via the SRA's TPMA notification process), put the tripartite agreement in place, and document its provider due diligence and its Rule 11.1(b) client-information process. ## VERIFIED ANCHOR BLOCK (quote/paraphrase only from this; verified at primary source 2026-06-03)

- **The rule: SRA Accounts Rules 2019, Rule 11 "Third party managed accounts"** (https://www.sra.org.uk/solicitors/standards-regulations/accounts-rules/). CONFIRMED rule number = **Rule 11** (NOT Rule 4.2).
  - **Rule 11.1 (verbatim):** *"You may enter into arrangements with a client to use a third party managed account for the purposes of receiving payments from or on behalf of, or making payments to or on behalf of, the client in respect of regulated services you provide to that client, only if: (a) use of the account does not result in you receiving or holding the client's money; and (b) you take reasonable steps to ensure, before accepting instructions, that the client is informed of and understands: (i) the terms of the contractual arrangements relating to the use of the third party managed account, and in particular how any fees for use of the third party managed account will be paid and who will bear them; and (ii) the client's right to terminate the agreement and dispute payment requests made by you."*
  - **Rule 11.2 (verbatim):** *"Where you use a third party managed account you obtain regular statements from the provider of the account and ensure that these accurately reflect all transactions on the account."*
- **The FCA-authorised-provider condition: SRA Glossary definition of "third party managed account"** (https://www.sra.org.uk/solicitors/standards-regulations/glossary/). The provider must be **"an authorised payment institution, a small payment institution that has chosen to implement safeguarding arrangements... or an EEA authorised payment institution (as each defined in the Payment Services Regulations) regulated by the Financial Conduct Authority"**, operating the account **"as an escrow payment service"**, with the monies **owned beneficially by the third party**. NOTE FOR WRITER: this is the load-bearing point that the FCA condition is in the GLOSSARY DEFINITION, not in the wording of Rule 11 itself. Cite both.
- **TPMA money is not client money: SRA guidance "Third-party managed accounts"** (https://www.sra.org.uk/solicitors/guidance/third-party-managed-accounts/), verbatim: *"Money held in a TPMA does not fall under the definition of client money in the SRA Accounts Rules (the Accounts Rules) as it is not held or received by you."* The guidance also confirms: *"rule 11 of the Accounts Rules applies to you if you use a TPMA and you should take appropriate steps to comply with the requirements set out."*
- **Client money definition (for the "not held or received" point): Rule 2.1**, four limbs (a) regulated services, (b) on behalf of a third party, (c) as trustee/office-holder, (d) fees and unpaid disbursements held before a bill (§5). TPMA money fails the "held or received by you" gateway, so it is outside all four limbs.
- **Cross-anchor (banking-facility prohibition): Rule 3.3** *"You must not use a client account to provide banking facilities to clients or third parties..."* (§5) - a TPMA removes this risk on the routed money.

## Worked-example sketches (illustrative only; label as not advice; no real figures presented as a quote)

1. **Completion through a TPMA.** A conveyancing firm uses a TPMA for a purchase: the buyer pays the balance into the TPMA (not the firm's client account), the firm instructs release to the seller's representative on completion, and the provider executes and issues a statement. Caption: "the funds never touch the firm's bank account, so they are not client money, and there is no client-account reconciliation to run on them."
2. **The Rule 11.1(b) client-information step.** Before taking instructions, the firm gives the client a clear note of the TPMA terms, who pays the provider's fees, and the client's right to terminate and to dispute a payment request, and records that the client understood it. Caption: "Rule 11.1(b) is a before-you-start duty, not a footnote in the retainer."
3. **The provider due-diligence check.** The firm confirms the provider is an FCA-authorised payment institution operating an escrow payment service (checking the FCA register) and keeps the evidence. Caption: "the FCA condition lives in the SRA Glossary definition, verify it before you route a penny."
