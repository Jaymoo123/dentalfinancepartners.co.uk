## The thesis (lead and hold)

As a self-employed partner you are taxed on your allocated profit share, not on the cash you draw (§2), and the bill arrives in lumps through payments on account, not smoothly through PAYE. So reserving is a discipline, not an afterthought. The page does three jobs the existing pages do not pull together: (1) it explains payments on account mechanically and precisely (TMA 1970 s.59A: two payments, 31 January and 31 July, each 50% of last year's liability, including Class 4 NIC, required unless the prior bill was under £1,000 or over 80% was collected at source); (2) it gives a defensible reserving rule of thumb by marginal band and the per-pound logic behind it; (3) it explains the basis-period-reform transition-profit cash squeeze, the one-off extra charge spread over five tax years (2023/24 to 2027/28) at 20% a year unless accelerated, which is still being collected and which partners with non-March year-ends are paying through to 2027/28. It closes with the lock-up reality: you can owe tax on profit that is still tied up in WIP and debtors.

## H2 outline (aim 10-12 sections, ~3,000-3,600 words)

1. **Why reserving is different for a partner** · you are taxed on the allocated profit share, not drawings (§2; cross-link drawings-vs-profit). Tax is not deducted at source as it is under PAYE; it lands in two lumps a year through self-assessment. So you must hold it back yourself. Set up the three parts: the POA mechanism, the reserving rule of thumb, and the transition-profit squeeze on top.
2. **Payments on account: the mechanics, precisely** · THE core mechanical section. Under TMA 1970 s.59A you make TWO payments on account towards your income tax (and Class 4 NIC) for a year: the first on or before 31 January IN the tax year, the second on or before the following 31 July, each equal to 50% of "the relevant amount", broadly the prior year's self-assessment liability. A balancing payment (or repayment) on the next 31 January settles the difference once the return is filed. State the two EXCLUSIONS verbatim from gov.uk: you make the two payments unless your last self-assessment bill was less than £1,000, OR you paid more than 80% of last year's tax at source (so the self-assessment slice was under 20%). Note Class 4 NIC IS included in the POA calculation (the "high-income side" of the bill is in scope); Class 2 is not a charge from 6 April 2024 (§3). Use the anchor block for the verbatim wording.
3. **Why the first 31 January feels like 150% of a year's tax** · the timing pile-up. When POAs first kick in (a new partner, or a rising profit share), the 31 January payment can carry the balancing payment for the year just ended PLUS the first 50% payment on account for the next year, so the cash demand can approach 150% of a single year's liability, with a further 50% due on 31 July. Explain this is the system catching up to your real-time income, not a penalty. Cross-link C1 (the promotion page) where this first hits.
4. **Can you reduce a payment on account, and when should you?** · you can claim to reduce POAs if you genuinely expect a lower liability (e.g. profit share falling). State the risk plainly: reduce too far and HMRC charges interest on the shortfall back to the original due dates. So reduce on evidence, not optimism. Keep this tight and practical.
5. **How much of each profit allocation to hold back: the rule of thumb** · the reserving heuristic, by marginal band, with the per-pound logic so it is defensible not arbitrary. Build it from the 2025/26 rates (§3): for profit in the higher-rate band the marginal cost is 40% income tax plus 2% Class 4 NIC = 42% on that slice; in the basic-rate band 20% plus 6% Class 4 = 26%; in the additional-rate band 45% plus 2% = 47%; and a brutal pocket between £100,000 and £125,140 where the personal-allowance taper produces an effective ~60% plus 2% Class 4 marginal cost. Translate into a practical blended reserve (many partners land around 30% to 45% of the allocation overall, higher if much of the share sits in the upper bands or the taper zone). Stress: reserve against the ALLOCATION, not the cash drawn, and sweep it monthly into a separate named account. (Note: the drawings page already states the 30-45% range; go DEEPER here with the band-by-band per-pound build so this page adds rather than repeats.)
6. **A reserving system that actually holds** · practical mechanics: estimate the allocation early from management accounts and revise it through the year; move the reserve monthly, not in January; hold the next POA visibly so it is never spent; treat 31 January and 31 July as immovable. Brief checklist. This is the operational counterpart to the maths in section 5.
7. **The basis-period transition-profit squeeze (still being collected to 2027/28)** · THE differentiating, time-sensitive section. Basis period reform (Finance Act 2022, §4) moved partners to a tax-year basis from 2024/25, with 2023/24 the transition year. Firms with a non-March/5-April year-end generated a one-off block of "transition profit" (the catch-up from the old basis date to 5 April 2024, less overlap relief). That transition profit is SPREAD over five tax years (2023/24 to 2027/28), with 20% treated as arising in each of the first four years and the balance in the fifth, UNLESS the partner elects to accelerate (bring more, or all, into charge sooner). So a partner with a December or April-ish year-end is STILL paying the spread transition tax through to 2027/28, on top of their normal annual liability, and it feeds the payments-on-account calculation. Use the verbatim FA 2022 Sch 1 wording in the anchor block. State the separate-charge nuance: transition profit is taxed as a STANDALONE amount (relieved in net income at Step 2, then removed, with a standalone tax calculated and added back), so it does not, of itself, sit in net income for things like the personal-allowance taper, BUT it can still push you into the taper if your TOTAL income (standard profits plus transition profits) exceeds £100,000. Confirm transition profits ARE within Class 4 NIC. Tell partners to reserve for the transition slice SEPARATELY so it is not a surprise each January through 2027/28, and to consider the acceleration election where a low-income year makes it cheap to clear.
8. **When accelerating the transition charge makes sense** · the election lever: bringing more transition profit into a lower-income year (e.g. a year of reduced profit share, or before a band/rate change) can cost less overall than the default 20%-a-year spread. Frame as a planning point to model, not a default. Note it is irreversible in effect for the amount elected, and interacts with the personal-allowance taper at £100,000.
9. **The lock-up reality: taxed on profit tied up in WIP and debtors** · the cruel cash-flow point specific to law firms. You are taxed on the allocated profit share even where the firm's cash is locked up in unbilled work in progress and unpaid debtors (WIP is recognised as revenue under FRS 102, §4). So a profitable firm with heavy lock-up can leave a partner owing tax on profit that has not yet turned into cash they can draw. This is exactly why reserving against the allocation (not drawings) matters, and why drawings policy and billing discipline are part of the tax-reserving picture. Cross-link the cash-flow / drawings pages.

## VERIFIED ANCHOR BLOCK (quote/paraphrase only from this; verified at primary source 2026-06-03)

- **Payments on account · TMA 1970 s.59A** (verified at legislation.gov.uk 2026-06-03): s.59A(2) "the taxpayer shall make two payments on account of his liability to income tax for the year of assessment", the first "on or before the 31st January in that year", the second "on or before the next following 31st July"; "each of those payments on account shall be of an amount equal to 50 per cent. of the relevant amount". The de minimis (under £1,000) and the source-deduction (80%) tests are set by regulations.
- **POA exclusions (verbatim, gov.uk "Understand your Self Assessment tax bill / Payments on account", verified 2026-06-03):** you make 2 payments unless "the amount of tax you owed last year was less than £1,000" OR "last year you paid more than 80% of the tax you owed outside of Self Assessment". Each payment is "usually half of the tax you owed the previous year"; due "by midnight on 31 January and 31 July"; the balancing payment is due "by midnight on 31 January the following year".
- **Income tax / Class 4 (2025/26), §3:** personal allowance £12,570 (tapered between £100,000 and £125,140), basic 20% to £50,270, higher 40% to £125,140, additional 45% above. Class 4 NIC 6% on £12,570 to £50,270, 2% above. Class 2 not a charge from 6 April 2024. Marginal-cost slices for the reserving build: basic 26% (20%+6%), higher 42% (40%+2%), additional 47% (45%+2%), taper zone ~62% (effective 60% income tax + 2% Class 4).
- **Basis period reform · Finance Act 2022 Schedule 1** (verified at legislation.gov.uk 2026-06-03):
  - Transition year is **2023-24** (Sch 1 para 65(1)(a): the basis period for 2023-24 is the period beginning immediately after the end of the 2022-23 basis period and "ending with 5 April 2024").
  - Transition profits = the "transition part" of the basis period (the part after the standard 12 months up to 5 April 2024), with overlap relief deducted (Sch 1 para 70: Step 1 standard part, Step 2 transition part, Step 3 "Deduct from the amount given by Step 2 the amount of any deduction for overlap profit").
  - **Five-year spreading, verbatim (Sch 1 para 72(3)-(4)):** "In each of the four tax years beginning with the tax year 2023-24, an amount equal to 20% of the amount of the transition profits is treated as arising ... In the fifth tax year, the balance of the amount of the transition profits is treated as arising." So 20% per year for four years (2023/24, 2024/25, 2025/26, 2026/27) and the balance in 2027/28.
  - **Acceleration election (Sch 1 para 73(1)-(3)):** "The trader may elect for an additional amount of those profits to be treated as arising in the tax year ... The election must specify the amount of the transition profits to be treated as arising in the tax year." Para 73(4) adjusts the remaining spread.
  - **Standalone / separate treatment (Sch 1 para 75; corroborated by HMRC BIM81290 and gov.uk guidance):** transition profits are relieved in net income, then removed; a standalone amount of tax is calculated on them and added back, so they do not, of themselves, sit in net income for the personal-allowance taper, HICBC or student loans. CAVEAT (corroborated by HMRC/gov.uk guidance): the personal-allowance taper can STILL bite if TOTAL income (standard part plus transition part) exceeds £100,000. HMRC BIM81290 (verbatim): "Transition profits will usually be spread, and taxed, equally across five tax years. This will usually be 20% of the total transition profits unless an election is made." Transition profits ARE within Class 4 NIC.

## Worked-example sketches (illustrative only; anonymised, label as not advice)

1. **The 150% January.** A partner whose POAs have just begun faces, on one 31 January, the balancing payment for the year just ended plus a first payment on account (50% of the same liability) for the next year, then a further 50% on 31 July. Caption: this is the system catching up to your income in arrears, not a penalty; a reserve built monthly absorbs it.
2. **The band-by-band reserve.** A partner's allocation spans the bands. On the slice in the basic-rate band the marginal cost is ~26% (20% + 6% Class 4); on the higher-rate slice ~42% (40% + 2%); on any slice in the £100,000 to £125,140 taper zone roughly ~62% (effective 60% income tax + 2% Class 4). Blending these for a typical upper-band partner lands a working reserve in the 35% to 45% range. Caption: reserve against the allocation, sweep it monthly, and treat the taper zone as the expensive band it is.
3. **The transition slice still landing in 2026/27.** A partner in a firm with a non-March year-end carries transition profit spread at 20% a year. The 2026/27 tax year still includes a 20% transition slice (the fourth of the five), with the final balance in 2027/28, on top of the normal annual bill, all feeding the POA calculation. Caption: budget the transition slice separately each January through 2027/28; it is one-off in total but spread across the years.
4. **Accelerating into a low year.** A partner expecting a lower profit share in a particular year elects to bring more transition profit into that year (Sch 1 para 73), so it is taxed while spare basic/higher-rate band is available, potentially below the rate it would suffer if left to a higher-income later year. Caption: the acceleration election is a model-it lever, not a default; check the £100,000 taper interaction first.
5. **Taxed on locked-up profit.** A firm is profitable on paper but heavy WIP and debtor lock-up means the cash is not in the bank. A partner is still taxed on the full allocated share (WIP recognised under FRS 102, §4), so the tax falls due on profit not yet turned into drawable cash. Caption: this is the strongest argument for reserving against the allocation, not the cash you managed to draw.
