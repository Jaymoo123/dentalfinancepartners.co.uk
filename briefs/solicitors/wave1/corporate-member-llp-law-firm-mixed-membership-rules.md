## The honest thesis (lead and hold)

Admitting a corporate member to an LLP to "cap" profit at the corporation-tax rate (rather than the partners' marginal income-tax rate) was a popular plan, but the **mixed-membership partnership rules** in ITTOIA 2005 ss.850C to 850E (inserted by Finance Act 2014) were written precisely to defeat the abusive version of it. Where excess profit is allocated to a non-individual (corporate) member and either the individual members can "enjoy" that profit or it represents their deferred profit, the excess is **reallocated back to the individual members** and taxed on them at their normal rates. The page must be scrupulously non-promotional: explain the structure, then explain in detail when s.850C bites, and conclude that a corporate-member structure only works where it reflects genuine commercial substance (real capital, real risk, profit genuinely belonging to the company), not as a rate-arbitrage device. This is "understand the rules before you build the structure," not a how-to-avoid-tax page.

## H2 outline (aim 9-12 sections, ~2,800-3,400 words)

1. **What a corporate member of a law firm LLP is** · an LLP is tax-transparent (ITTOIA 2005 s.863, §2): each member is taxed on its allocated profit share. A member can be a COMPANY as well as an individual. If a company is a member, its profit share is charged to CORPORATION TAX (19% to £50k / marginal / 25%, §3), not to the individuals' income tax. That rate gap (corporation tax vs up to 45% income tax plus Class 4) is the whole appeal. Note the SRA dimension: a corporate member raises authorisation/ownership questions (recognised body vs ABS, Legal Services Act 2007 Part 5, §1); cross-link A3.
2. **Why firms reach for it: capping profit at the corporation-tax rate** · the theory. Allocate a slice of profit to the corporate member, retain it there at CT rates, reinvest in the firm. State the appeal plainly, then immediately signal the anti-avoidance counterweight.
3. **The mixed-membership partnership rules: the core anchor** · THE load-bearing section. ITTOIA 2005 **s.850C "Excess profit allocation to non-individual partners"** (inserted FA 2014 Sch 17, effect from 6 April 2014). Set up the parties: **"A" is an individual partner; "B" is a non-individual partner** (s.850C(6): a partner is an "individual partner" if the partner is an individual and "non-individual partner" is read accordingly). The rule reallocates excess profit from B (the company) back to A (the individual) where the conditions are met. See the verbatim anchor block below; the writer must paraphrase accurately, not overstate.
4. **The two trigger conditions (Condition X and Condition Y / power to enjoy)** · explain both pathways the verified section sets out:
   - **Deferred-profit pathway (Condition X):** amounts representing A's deferred profit are included in B's profit share, and in consequence A's profit share (and the relevant tax amount) are lower than they would otherwise have been. "Deferred profit" = remuneration or other benefits/returns whose provision to A has been deferred.
   - **Power-to-enjoy pathway (Condition Y):** B's profit share exceeds "the appropriate notional profit", AND A has "the power to enjoy" B's profit share, AND the whole or part of B's profit share is attributable to that power to enjoy.
   Where either bites, s.850C(4) INCREASES A's profit share by so much of B's profit share as it is reasonable to suppose is attributable to A's deferred profit or A's power to enjoy. Result: the diverted profit is taxed on the individual.
5. **The "appropriate notional profit" carve-out (genuine returns survive)** · s.850C(10) to (11): the appropriate notional profit is the appropriate notional return on capital PLUS the appropriate notional consideration for services; the capital return is what B would receive for its genuine contribution at a commercial interest rate, less returns already received. Point: a corporate member that contributes real capital and bears real risk can keep a commercial return; only the EXCESS above that notional profit is exposed to reallocation. This is the substance test in practice.
6. **The companion sections: s.850D and s.850E (state them precisely)** · **s.850D "Excess profit allocation: cases involving individuals who are not partners"** extends the reach to where the individual (A) provides services to the firm but is NOT a partner, yet a corporate partner's share carries A's deferred profit or A's power to enjoy. **s.850E "Payments by B out of the excess part of B's profit share"** is a relieving provision: where s.850C(4) or s.850D(4) has applied, a payment by B (the company) out of the reallocated excess to A is not treated as income/distribution/deductible (so the same profit is not taxed twice), provided it is not part of tax-avoidance arrangements. NOTE FOR WRITER: do NOT describe s.850D as "the loss rule" (that is wrong); ss.850C to 850E are all PROFIT-allocation provisions. The mixed-membership LOSS counterpart is a separate provision (ITA 2007 loss-relief restriction) and is out of scope here, mention only in one line if at all.
7. **When a corporate member structure genuinely works** · genuine commercial substance: the company has real capital at risk, performs a real function (e.g. owns premises, employs staff, funds growth), and the profit allocated to it reflects that, not a diversion of the individuals' earnings. Retained profit then legitimately sits at the CT rate. Frame as "substance first."
8. **The interaction with the salaried member rules and with extraction** · separate but related: the salaried member rules (ss.863A to 863G, §2.A) can recharacterise an individual MEMBER as an employee; the mixed-membership rules target profit diverted to a CORPORATE member. Also: getting profit OUT of the corporate member to the individuals later still costs dividend tax (10.75% / 35.75% from 6 April 2026, FA 2026 s.4, §3) or salary plus employer NIC (15%, §3), so the "saving" is often a deferral, not an absolute saving. s.455 may apply if value leaves as a loan to participators (33.75% pre-6-Apr-2026 / 35.75% on/after, §3).

## VERIFIED ANCHOR BLOCK (quote/paraphrase only from this; verified at primary source 2026-06-03)

- **ITTOIA 2005 s.850C** · heading verbatim: **"Excess profit allocation to non-individual partners"**. Parties: **A = individual partner, B = non-individual partner** (s.850C(6): "A partner in a firm is an 'individual partner' if the partner is an individual and 'non-individual partner' is to be read accordingly"). Trigger requires the s.849 calculation for A to produce a PROFIT for the firm.
  - **Operative trigger (verbatim fragments):** deferred-profit limb · *"amounts representing A's deferred profit ... are included in B's profit share, and in consequence, both A's profit share and the relevant tax amount are lower than they would otherwise have been."* Power-to-enjoy limb · *"B's profit share exceeds the appropriate notional profit"* AND *"A has the power to enjoy B's profit share"* AND *"the whole or any part of B's profit share is attributable to A's power to enjoy."*
  - **Deferred profit definition (verbatim):** *"any remuneration or other benefits or returns the provision of which to A has been deferred."*
  - **Reallocation mechanism (s.850C(4), verbatim):** A's profit share is increased *"by so much of the amount of B's profit share as, it is reasonable to suppose, is attributable to A's deferred profit, or A's power to enjoy."*
  - **Appropriate notional profit (s.850C(10) to (11)):** the sum of the appropriate notional return on capital and the appropriate notional consideration for services; the return component is what B would receive for the period in respect of B's contribution to the firm at a commercial interest rate, less returns already received outside the profit share.
- **ITTOIA 2005 s.850D** · heading verbatim: **"Excess profit allocation: cases involving individuals who are not partners"** · extends s.850C to an individual A who provides services to the firm but is not a partner.
- **ITTOIA 2005 s.850E** · heading verbatim: **"Payments by B out of the excess part of B's profit share"** · relieving provision; opens *"Subsection (2) applies in a case in which section 850C(4) or section 850D(4) applies if ..."*; a payment by B to A out of the reallocated excess is not treated as income/distribution/deductible (avoids double taxation) unless part of tax-avoidance arrangements.
- All three inserted by **Finance Act 2014 (Sch 17)**, effect from **6 April 2014**.
- HMRC manual anchors (for external links / further reading): PM210000+ (mixed membership partnerships), specifically PM214000 onwards for s.850C.

## Worked-example sketches (illustrative only; label as not advice)

1. **The abusive version that fails.** LLP with two individual members and a newly-formed corporate member owned by the same individuals. A large profit slice is allocated to the company "to save tax," but the company contributed negligible capital and performs no function, and the individuals can draw on the company's funds at will (power to enjoy). Walk through: B's share exceeds the appropriate notional profit (B contributed almost no capital, so the notional return is tiny); A has the power to enjoy; s.850C(4) reallocates the excess back to the individuals, taxed at their marginal income-tax rates plus Class 4. Caption: "the rate arbitrage is reversed; the structure achieves nothing but cost and risk."
2. **The genuine version that survives (within limits).** The corporate member has subscribed real capital (say it funded an office fit-out and carries the lease) and bears genuine risk. Its commercial return on that capital and function is its "appropriate notional profit", which it keeps at the CT rate; only profit allocated ABOVE that notional figure would be exposed to s.850C. Caption: "substance defines how much the company can legitimately retain."
3. **The deferral, not saving, point.** Profit retained in the corporate member at CT (19% to £50k / marginal / 25%) is later extracted to the individuals as dividend at 10.75% / 35.75% from 6 April 2026 (§3); show that the combined two-layer cost on eventual extraction is close to the income-tax cost, so the benefit is timing/reinvestment, not an absolute tax cut. Caption: "the win is reinvestment inside the firm, not a lower lifetime tax bill."
