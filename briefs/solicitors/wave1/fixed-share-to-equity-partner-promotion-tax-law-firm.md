## The thesis (lead and hold)

Being promoted from fixed-share (or salaried) partner to full equity partner is, for tax, a change of STATUS, not just a pay rise. If the old fixed-share role was caught by the salaried member rules (§2.A), the solicitor was being taxed as an employee: PAYE on the reward, Class 1 employee NIC, and employer NIC borne by the firm. As a genuine full-equity partner whose reward varies with the firm's profit, who has real influence, and who has put in capital of at least 25% of their reward, the salaried member rules stop biting and the solicitor becomes self-employed for tax: taxed on an allocated profit share through self-assessment, paying Class 4 NIC instead of Class 1, with no employer NIC. That switch brings three things the new partner has rarely met before: a capital buy-in to fund (with qualifying-loan interest relief available), a first self-assessment, and the payments-on-account timing shock. The page walks the event in order and tells the partner what to set up before the promotion date.

## H2 outline (aim 9-12 sections, ~2,800-3,400 words)

1. **What actually changes when you make equity partner** · frame it as a status change, not a pay rise. Three shifts: (a) tax status, employee to self-employed; (b) you now contribute capital; (c) you file self-assessment and meet payments on account. Set up the rest of the page. Note the page assumes the firm is a partnership or LLP (tax-transparent, §2); if the firm is incorporated the promotion is a share/role question, out of scope, one-line cross-link to the incorporation cluster.
2. **The starting point: how a fixed-share or salaried partner is usually taxed** · many fixed-share and salaried members are caught by the salaried member rules (Finance Act 2014, ITTOIA 2005 ss.863A to 863G, §2.A), which treat the member as an employee for tax. That means PAYE on the reward, Class 1 employee NIC (8% / 2%, §3), and the FIRM pays employer (secondary Class 1) NIC at 15% above the £5,000 secondary threshold (§3). Explain the three conditions briefly (Condition A 80% disguised salary; Condition B no significant influence; Condition C capital under 25% of disguised salary), all three must be met to be caught. Cross-link to the dedicated salaried-member explainer rather than re-teaching it in full.
3. **The salaried-member exit: why promotion to full equity usually breaks the rules** · THE differentiating section. A genuine full-equity partner typically FAILS the salaried member conditions on more than one limb, so once promoted the member is no longer treated as an employee: (a) reward now genuinely varies with the firm's overall profit, so under ~80% is "disguised salary" and Condition A fails; (b) the partner gains real say in how the firm is run, so Condition B fails; (c) the partner contributes capital of at least 25% of their disguised salary, genuinely at risk, so Condition C fails. Stress that this must be REAL, not a paper exercise (substance governs, not the title), and that it is the underlying economics, not the new "equity partner" label, that ends employee treatment. Note the rules are tested year by year, so the exit must be genuine from the date the new arrangement takes effect.
4. **From PAYE to self-assessment: the NIC and collection change** · the mechanics of the status switch. As a self-employed partner you pay income tax and Class 4 NIC (6% on profits £12,570 to £50,270, 2% above, 2025/26, §3) through self-assessment, not PAYE. Class 1 employee NIC stops. Class 2 NIC is NOT payable (the Class 2 liability was removed from 6 April 2024, §3), but you are still treated as having paid it for state-pension purposes if profits are at/above the small profits threshold. The firm stops paying employer NIC on you, which is part of why firms value the self-employed treatment. Important nuance: under PAYE tax was collected as you earned; under self-assessment it is collected LATER and in lumps, which is the heart of the cash-flow shock in section 7.
5. **The capital buy-in trigger: you now have to put money in** · becoming full equity almost always means contributing capital to the firm (it is also one of the levers that breaks the salaried member rules, see section 3). The capital is your investment in the firm: it is NOT taxable income to you when you put it in, and it is NOT a tax-deductible expense (cross-link C2 on capital accounts for the full treatment). What is new is that you have to FIND the money. Set up funding options at a high level (savings, partnership capital loan, firm-financed), and hand the detailed cost-by-firm-tier question to the existing buy-in cost page. Keep this page on the TAX STATUS change, not the £ amount.
6. **Funding the capital: qualifying-loan interest relief (the live deduction)** · if you borrow to fund the capital contribution, the interest is deductible against your income under the qualifying-loan-interest-relief rules, ITA 2007 ss.398 to 412 (§2). State the conditions precisely (see anchor block): the loan must be used to purchase a share in the partnership or to contribute capital to / advance money to a partnership used for its trade or profession (s.398); you must be a member/partner throughout from the use of the loan until the interest is paid (s.399); relief is denied for an investment LLP (s.399); and relief is withdrawn to the extent you later recover capital from the partnership (s.399 / ss.406 to 412). Note relief is given at your marginal rate and claimed on self-assessment, and that the general cap on certain income tax reliefs (ITA 2007 s.24A, the greater of £50,000 or 25% of adjusted total income) can in principle limit very large claims, though typical partner buy-in interest is well within it. Cross-link the buy-in cost page for the financing comparison.
7. **The first-year self-assessment and payments-on-account shock** · the cash-flow event most new equity partners are unprepared for. In your first year as a self-employed partner, tax on the profit share is not collected as you earn it; it lands as a self-assessment bill on 31 January after the tax year. Worse, on that SAME 31 January you may also have to make the first payment on account towards the NEXT year (TMA 1970 s.59A, see anchor block): two payments on account, due 31 January and 31 July, each 50% of the prior year's liability, required unless last year's bill was under £1,000 or more than 80% of your tax was collected at source. The practical sting: the first 31 January can carry a balancing payment for year one PLUS a 50% payment on account for year two, so the cash demand can look like roughly 150% of a year's tax at once. Explain that this is timing, not extra tax. Hand the detailed reserving discipline to C3 (cross-link), and keep this section on the FIRST-YEAR event.
8. **What to set up before the promotion date** · practical checklist framing: register for self-assessment in good time (the 5 October registration deadline after the tax year you start); arrange the capital and the loan, documenting the use of funds so the s.398 relief is clean; start a tax reserve immediately rather than waiting for the first bill; confirm with the firm whether your old PAYE reward is being trued up and whether there is any overlap with the new self-employed period; check whether the firm's accounting date interacts with basis period reform for your first year (cross-link C3 and the basis-period page).
9. **A note on what does NOT change** · the firm is still tax-transparent (you were always going to be taxed on the firm's results in some form); your underlying income tax bands are the same; and the salaried member analysis is the SAME framework you may already have sat just inside of. Reassure that the change is about collection and status, and that with reserving in place it is manageable.

## VERIFIED ANCHOR BLOCK (quote/paraphrase only from this; verified at primary source 2026-06-03)

- **Salaried member rules · ITTOIA 2005 ss.863A to 863G** (Finance Act 2014), §2.A: all three of Condition A (s.863B, disguised salary at least 80% of reward), Condition B (s.863C, no significant influence), Condition C (s.863D, capital less than 25% of disguised salary) must be met to treat the member as an employee. Fail one and the member is self-employed. A full-equity partner whose reward genuinely varies with profit typically fails Condition A.
- **Class 4 NIC** (SSCBA 1992 s.15), §3: 6% on profits £12,570 to £50,270, 2% above (2025/26). **Class 2 liability removed from 6 April 2024** (National Insurance Contributions Act 2024), §3. Employee Class 1 NIC 8% / 2%; employer (secondary Class 1) NIC 15% above the £5,000 secondary threshold from 6 April 2025 (§3).
- **Qualifying-loan interest relief · ITA 2007 s.398** (verified at legislation.gov.uk 2026-06-03): s.398(1) "This section applies to a loan to an individual that is used in one or more of the ways specified in subsection (2)." s.398(2) qualifying uses include: (a) purchasing a share in a partnership; (b) contributing capital to a partnership used wholly for the purposes of the trade or profession; (c) advancing money to a partnership so used; (d) repaying another loan within this section.
- **ITA 2007 s.399** (verified 2026-06-03): the individual must be a member of the partnership throughout the period from the use of the loan until the interest is paid; relief is unavailable if the individual ceases to be a member during that period; relief is denied to a member of an investment LLP (an LLP whose business consists wholly or mainly of making investments and the principal part of whose income is derived from investments); relief ceases if the individual has recovered any capital from the partnership (except amounts treated as a repayment of the loan under s.406(2)). The capital-recovery rules run across ss.406 to 412.
- **ITA 2007 s.24A** (general cap on certain income tax reliefs): qualifying-loan interest relief is within the capped reliefs; the cap is the greater of £50,000 and 25% of adjusted total income. State as a backstop, not a typical constraint.
- **Payments on account · TMA 1970 s.59A** (verified at legislation.gov.uk 2026-06-03): s.59A(2) "the taxpayer shall make two payments on account of his liability to income tax for the year of assessment", the first "on or before the 31st January in that year" and the second "on or before the next following 31st July"; "each of those payments on account shall be of an amount equal to 50 per cent. of the relevant amount". The de minimis (under £1,000) and the 80%-collected-at-source exclusions are set by regulations and stated on gov.uk: gov.uk "Understand your Self Assessment tax bill / Payments on account" confirms verbatim that you make 2 payments unless "the amount of tax you owed last year was less than £1,000" or "last year you paid more than 80% of the tax you owed outside of Self Assessment", each payment "usually half of the tax you owed the previous year", due "by midnight on 31 January and 31 July", with the balancing payment by 31 January the following year.

## Worked-example sketches (illustrative only; anonymised, label as not advice)

1. **The status switch in numbers.** A fixed-share partner caught by the salaried member rules is on PAYE: the firm deducts income tax and employee Class 1 NIC and pays employer NIC at 15% on the reward above £5,000. On promotion to full equity, the same person is allocated a profit share, taxed through self-assessment, paying Class 4 NIC (6% / 2%) instead of Class 1, with no employer NIC for the firm. Caption: the take-home difference is modest; the bigger change is that collection moves from monthly-at-source to twice-a-year-in-arrears, and the firm sheds employer NIC.
2. **Funding the buy-in with loan interest relief.** A new equity partner borrows to fund a capital contribution and pays loan interest in the year. At a 40% marginal rate the relief is 40% of the interest, claimed on self-assessment under ITA 2007 s.398, reducing the net cost of financing the buy-in. Caption: keep the loan-use documentation clean so the relief is unarguable, and remember relief is withdrawn if you later draw the capital back out (s.399 / ss.406 to 412).
3. **The first 31 January.** In the first full year as a self-employed partner, the 31 January self-assessment bill includes the balancing payment for that year PLUS a first payment on account (50% of the same liability) towards the next year, so the cash demand can approach 150% of a single year's tax in one go, with a further 50% due on 31 July. Caption: this is a timing pile-up, not extra tax; a reserve built from day one absorbs it (see our reserving guide).
