## The honest thesis (lead and hold)

Land and buildings are normally **VAT-exempt**, so a firm buying or refurbishing premises often cannot recover the input VAT, and a landlord renting to the firm normally charges no VAT (but also recovers none on its costs). The **option to tax** (VATA 1994 Schedule 10) lets the owner/landlord turn that exempt supply into a **standard-rated** one, which unlocks input VAT recovery on the purchase, refurbishment or running costs, at the price of charging VAT on rent or sale. But the option is a **long commitment (20 years)**, with a short **6-month cooling-off window** and limited later revocation. And because premises are high-value, the **Capital Goods Scheme (CGS)** then watches the firm's recovery for **10 years**, re-adjusting it each year as the firm's taxable/exempt use changes, which is where this page connects back to partial exemption (a firm that becomes partially exempt because of exempt interest income, see the sibling page, may have to repay some of the VAT it recovered on its building). The page must lay out both regimes accurately and show how they interact, without recommending the option in the abstract (it depends on the numbers and on whether the firm is fully taxable).

## H2 outline (aim 9-12 sections, ~3,000-3,600 words)

1. **The default: land and buildings are VAT-exempt** · the starting point. Selling or letting land/buildings is normally an exempt supply, so the owner charges no VAT and cannot recover input VAT on related costs (purchase, refurb, agent fees). For a law firm buying or fitting out an office, that can mean a large block of unrecoverable VAT. Signpost the option to tax as the lever, and the CGS + partial exemption as the long-tail consequences.
2. **What the option to tax does (VATA 1994 Schedule 10)** · the legal anchor. Opting to tax makes supplies of the opter's interest in the land/building **standard-rated**, so the opter can recover input VAT on costs of those supplies. Verbatim in anchor block (Notice 742A para 1.2). Make clear it is the OWNER/LANDLORD'S option over their interest; a tenant firm does not "opt to tax" its landlord's building, but a firm that OWNS its premises (or holds them in a connected property company/SIPP) can.
3. **Why a firm or landlord opts to tax** · to recover input VAT on a purchase or a substantial refurbishment/fit-out. Worked logic: VAT on a £x purchase or refurb is recoverable only if the onward supply (rent or sale) is taxable, which requires the option. Trade-off: the firm/landlord must then charge 20% VAT on rent or on a later sale, which matters if a future buyer/tenant is VAT-exempt or partly exempt (e.g. another professional firm, a bank, a charity) and cannot recover it.
4. **How to opt: the decision and the notification** · the option is made (the decision) and then **notified to HMRC**, normally within **30 days** of the decision, on **form VAT1614A** (Notice 742A para 4.2.1). State both steps: deciding to opt, then notifying. Mention HMRC's reduced acknowledgement practice only lightly.
5. **The 6-month cooling-off period** · within **less than 6 months** of the option taking effect, and if conditions are met (broadly: no tax has yet been recovered/charged under it in a way that prevents it, no relevant supplies made), the option can be **revoked without HMRC permission**, notified on **form VAT1614C** (Notice 742A section 8.1). The safety valve for a change of mind.
6. **The 20-year commitment and later revocation** · an option to tax generally lasts **20 years**; it can be revoked after **more than 20 years have elapsed** since it first had effect (Notice 742A para 8.3, form VAT1614J), subject to conditions, and there is a separate automatic revocation where the opter has not held an interest in the property for a continuous 6-year period after the option had effect (para 8.2.1). Emphasise: outside the 6-month window and the 6-year-no-interest rule, the firm is committed for two decades, so opting affects every future rent invoice and any sale.
7. **The Capital Goods Scheme: what it is and the thresholds** · THE second anchor. For high-value capital assets the CGS spreads and RE-ADJUSTS input VAT recovery over an adjustment period of fixed **intervals**, reflecting changes in taxable vs exempt use. Verified thresholds: **land, buildings, civil engineering works and refurbishment costing £250,000 or more (excluding VAT) = 10 intervals**; a **single computer or item of computer equipment costing £50,000 or more (excluding VAT) = 5 intervals**. (Aircraft/ships/boats are also 10-interval items, out of scope for a law firm.) State the legal basis: VAT Regulations 1995 (SI 1995/2518) regs 112 to 116.
8. **How the CGS re-adjusts recovery each year** · at the end of each interval the firm compares its taxable-use percentage in that interval against the baseline (first interval) recovery, and adjusts. Verified formula: **total input VAT on the capital item ÷ number of intervals × the adjustment percentage** (the difference between the interval's recovery percentage and the baseline). If taxable use FALLS in a later interval (e.g. the firm becomes partially exempt), the firm REPAYS part of the VAT it originally recovered; if it RISES, the firm recovers more. So the recovery on a building is not locked in at purchase, it is monitored for 10 years.
9. **The interaction with partial exemption (the link the reader needs)** · the crucial join to the sibling page. The CGS adjustment percentage IS the firm's partial-exemption recovery rate for the interval. A fully taxable firm recovers 100% and stays there. But if the firm later becomes partially exempt (e.g. exempt interest income breaching de minimis, see `law-firm-partial-exemption-vat-client-account-interest`), its recovery rate drops, and the CGS makes it repay a slice of the originally-recovered building VAT for each affected interval. Conversely, opting to tax can itself improve recovery. State plainly: premises VAT decisions and partial-exemption status are linked over a 10-year horizon.
10. **Worked sketches** · see below.
11. **Practical decision points and cautions** · (i) check whether the landlord has opted (it changes the rent VAT and the firm's own recovery); (ii) model the option before committing, given the 20-year lock and the effect on a future sale to an exempt buyer; (iii) notify on time (VAT1614A within 30 days); (iv) track CGS items and intervals for 10 years (or 5 for big IT spend); (v) coordinate with partial-exemption monitoring; (vi) note TOGC interaction on a property/business sale (cross-ref §9 TOGC, only a light mention). Inline CTA to lead form (segment: firm buying/refurbishing/leasing premises or reviewing premises VAT recovery).

## VERIFIED ANCHOR BLOCK (quote/paraphrase only from this; verified at primary source 2026-06-03)

- **Option to tax, effect** (gov.uk VAT Notice 742A, Opting to tax land and buildings; legal basis VATA 1994 Schedule 10 paras 23-29; verified 2026-06-03): para 1.2 (verbatim) *"Once you have opted to tax all the supplies you make of your interest in the land or buildings will normally be standard-rated, and you will normally be able to recover any VAT you incur in making those supplies."*
- **Notification** (Notice 742A para 4.2.1, verbatim): *"you, as authorised signatory ... must normally make your notification within 30 days of your decision."* Form **VAT1614A**. (Two stages: decide to opt, then notify within 30 days.)
- **6-month cooling-off** (Notice 742A section 8.1): revocation permitted where *"less than 6 months have passed since the day on which the option had effect"* and conditions are met, no prior HMRC permission needed, notified on **form VAT1614C**.
- **20-year duration / revocation** (Notice 742A): para 8.3 allows revocation where *"more than 20 years have elapsed since the option first had effect"* (subject to conditions, form **VAT1614J**); para 8.2.1 (verbatim) *"An option to tax is revoked where the opter has not held an interest in the opted building or land for a continuous period of 6 years commencing at any time after the option to tax has effect."*
- **Legal basis** · option to tax is in **Schedule 10 to the Value Added Tax Act 1994 (paragraphs 23 to 29)**.
- **Capital Goods Scheme thresholds and intervals** (gov.uk VAT Notice 706/2, Capital Goods Scheme, https://www.gov.uk/guidance/capital-goods-scheme-notice-7062; legal basis VAT Regulations 1995 (SI 1995/2518) regs 112 to 116; verified 2026-06-03):
  - **Land, a building or part of a building, civil engineering work, and refurbishment/construction costing £250,000 or more excluding VAT = 10 intervals** (HMRC: *"10 intervals for all other capital items"*). HMRC: you must use the scheme if you spend **£250,000 (excluding VAT) or more** on buying land, a building or part of a building.
  - **A single computer or item of computer equipment costing £50,000 or more excluding VAT = 5 intervals** (HMRC: *"5 intervals for computers"*; the scheme applies to individual computers/items of computer equipment costing **£50,000 (excluding VAT) or more**).
  - **Adjustment formula** (verbatim): **"Total input tax on the capital item / Number of intervals in the adjustment period × the adjustment percentage."** The adjustment percentage compares the baseline recovery (end of first interval) against the extent of taxable use in each later interval.
- NOTE FOR WRITER: state the £250,000 and £50,000 figures as **VAT-exclusive**. State 10 intervals for land/buildings/refurb and 5 intervals for the qualifying computer/IT spend. Do NOT call intervals "years" without the caveat that the first interval can be a part-period.

## Worked-example sketches (illustrative only; label as not advice)

1. **Opting to recover refurb VAT on an owned office.** A firm owns its office (or holds it through a connected property company) and spends a substantial sum, over the CGS land/buildings threshold of £250,000 excluding VAT, on refurbishment. Without an option to tax, the related supply (e.g. on a later sale or sublet) would be exempt and the input VAT irrecoverable. The owner opts to tax (notifying on VAT1614A within 30 days), recovers the input VAT, and the refurbishment becomes a 10-interval CGS item monitored over 10 years. Caption: "the option unlocks recovery; the CGS then watches it for a decade."
2. **Partial exemption clawing back building VAT.** Two years after recovering VAT on its premises, the firm becomes partially exempt (large retained client-account interest breaches the de minimis limits, see the sibling page). Its recovery percentage for the interval falls below 100%. Under the CGS formula (total VAT ÷ 10 × the fall in the adjustment percentage), the firm repays a slice of the originally-recovered VAT for that interval, and potentially for each later interval the restriction persists. Caption: "premises VAT recovery is not locked in; a later change in taxable use re-adjusts it."
3. **The landlord who has opted, and the firm's rent.** A firm leases premises from a landlord who has opted to tax. The firm now pays 20% VAT on its rent; if the firm is fully taxable it recovers that input VAT, so the option is broadly neutral to it, but if the firm is partially exempt the rent VAT becomes partly a real cost. Caption: "always check whether your landlord has opted; it changes your rent and your recovery."
