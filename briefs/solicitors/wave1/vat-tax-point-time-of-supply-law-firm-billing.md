## The honest thesis (lead and hold)

VAT is due by reference to the **tax point** (the time of supply), not when the firm feels like it. For a one-off service the tax point is simple, but law firms bill in ways that create real confusion: interim **requests for payment on account** during a long matter, **interim statute bills**, and money taken from client account. The single most valuable point on this page is the distinction the Law Society and HMRC draw: a **request for payment on account is NOT a VAT invoice and does NOT create a tax point**, so no output VAT is due until the firm either issues a VAT invoice or receives payment; whereas an **interim statute bill IS a demand for payment of a defined sum and does create a tax point**. Get this wrong in either direction and the firm either pays VAT too early (cash-flow cost) or too late (penalty/interest). The page lays out s.6 cleanly, then applies it to the way law firms actually bill.

## H2 outline (aim 9-12 sections, ~2,900-3,500 words)

1. **Why the tax point matters for law firms** · output VAT is due for the VAT period in which the tax point falls; with long matters and interim billing the tax point is not obvious. State that legal services are standard-rated at 20% (§6) and that this page is about TIMING, not whether VAT applies. Signpost the request-for-payment-vs-statute-bill point as the headline.
2. **The basic tax point for services (VATA 1994 s.6)** · the legal anchor. For services the basic tax point is **when the services are performed** (s.6(3)). For a continuous/one-off completed service that means completion. Verbatim in anchor block.
3. **The actual tax point: invoice or payment, whichever is earlier** · s.6(4): if, before the basic tax point, the supplier **issues a VAT invoice** OR **receives a payment**, the supply is treated as taking place (to the extent covered) at the time of that invoice or payment. So an advance payment or an early VAT invoice pulls the tax point forward. This is why taking money on account can create a tax point if it is a payment "in respect of" the supply.
4. **The 14-day rule** · s.6(5): if the supplier issues a VAT invoice **within 14 days after** the basic tax point, the tax point becomes the invoice date (unless the supplier has elected out in writing). Practical effect for firms that bill shortly after completing work. Note HMRC can allow a longer period by agreement (mention briefly).
5. **The solicitor-specific point: request for payment vs VAT invoice** · THE differentiator. A **"request for payment" or "request for payment on account"** (a request for funds before the firm is ready to deliver a VAT invoice/statute bill) is **NOT a VAT invoice**: it should be marked clearly as a request for payment and not show it as a tax invoice, and it does **NOT create a tax point**. No output VAT is due on it. The tax point arises later, when the firm **issues the VAT invoice/statute bill OR receives the payment** (whichever is earlier). Contrast: an **interim STATUTE bill** (a self-contained final bill for the work to date, which the client can be sued on and which starts assessment time limits under the Solicitors Act 1974) IS a demand for a defined sum and DOES create a tax point. NOTE FOR WRITER: be precise that it is RECEIPT of payment against a request for payment that triggers the tax point on the sum received, not the issue of the request itself; and that a request for payment must not be dressed up as a VAT invoice.
6. **Money on account and client account** · tie to client-money rules (§5): money taken on account of costs and held in client account is not yet the firm's money and (if it is a genuine request for payment on account, not a VAT invoice) does not of itself create a tax point until applied to a bill or until a VAT invoice is issued. Care: once the firm transfers funds to office account in satisfaction of a bill, the tax point on that bill applies. Cross-link `client-money-accounting-solicitors`.
7. **Continuous supply of services** · for ongoing retainers/continuous supplies the tax point is driven by the **earlier of a VAT invoice or a payment** (the continuous-supply rule in the VAT Regulations / Notice 700), so for a rolling retainer each invoice or payment crystallises a tax point. Useful for firms on monthly retainers or staged commercial work. State this is a different default from the one-off basic tax point.
8. **Work spanning a VAT rate change** · if the standard rate changes, the supplier can choose to account by reference to the basic tax point (work done) for the part before/after the change, even where the actual tax point (invoice/payment) falls the other side. Frame generally (the rate has been 20% since 4 January 2011, so this is a "if it ever changes" planning point), and tie it to staged/long matters. Date-tag: standard rate 20% as at 2026.
9. **Work spanning the firm's VAT registration date** · only supplies with a tax point on or after the effective date of registration bear VAT; the tax point rules decide which side of registration a long matter's fees fall. Practical for newly-registered firms (cross-link `vat-registration-solicitors`).
10. **Worked sketches** · see below.
11. **Practical compliance checklist** · (i) mark interim demands clearly as "request for payment", not "VAT invoice/tax invoice"; (ii) account for output VAT when a VAT invoice/statute bill is issued or payment received, whichever is first; (iii) use the 14-day rule consistently; (iv) for retainers treat each invoice/payment as a tax point; (v) reconcile tax points to the correct VAT return period; (vi) interaction with cash accounting (under cash accounting the tax point for output VAT effectively follows receipt, mention and cross-link). Inline CTA to lead form (segment: firm reviewing billing/VAT timing or interim-billing process).

## VERIFIED ANCHOR BLOCK (quote/paraphrase only from this; verified at primary source 2026-06-03)

- **VATA 1994 s.6 (time of supply)** (https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1994/23/section/6, verified 2026-06-03):
  - **Basic tax point for services, s.6(3) (verbatim):** *"a supply of services shall be treated as taking place at the time when the services are performed."*
  - **Earlier invoice/payment override, s.6(4) (verbatim):** *"If, before the time applicable under subsection (2) or (3) above, the person making the supply issues a VAT invoice in respect of it or if ... he receives a payment in respect of it, the supply shall, to the extent covered by the invoice or payment, be treated as taking place at the time the invoice is issued or the payment is received."* (earlier of invoice or payment pulls the tax point forward, to the extent covered).
  - **14-day rule, s.6(5) (verbatim):** *"If, within 14 days after the time applicable under subsection (2) or (3) above, the person making the supply issues a VAT invoice in respect of it, then, unless he has notified the Commissioners in writing that he elects not to avail himself of this subsection, the supply shall (to the extent that it is not treated as taking place at the time mentioned in subsection (4) above) be treated as taking place at the time the invoice is issued."*
- **Continuous supply of services** · for continuous supplies the tax point is the earlier of the issue of a VAT invoice or receipt of payment (VAT Regulations 1995 reg 90; gov.uk VAT Notice 700 §14/§15, time of supply / tax points). Cite Notice 700 as the accessible authority.
- **Rate-change and registration spanning** · gov.uk VAT Notice 700 (and Notice 700 §30 on change of rate): a supplier may apply the basic tax point split where a supply spans a rate change; supplies are only taxable from the effective date of registration, determined by the tax point. Standard rate **20%** (in force since 4 January 2011), as at 2026.
- **Request for payment is NOT a VAT invoice (solicitor practice)** · Law Society / HMRC VAT guidance for solicitors: a "request for payment" or "request for payment on account" is not a VAT invoice and creates no tax point; output VAT is accounted for only when a VAT invoice (or statute bill operating as a VAT invoice) is issued or payment is received, whichever is earlier. An interim STATUTE bill (a self-contained bill under the Solicitors Act 1974 for a defined sum) is a demand the client can be sued on and does create a tax point. NOTE FOR WRITER: this is the historic solicitors' VAT concession/practice (a request for payment may be issued and the tax point deferred to invoice-or-payment). Frame the statute-bill vs request-for-payment distinction in Solicitors-Act terms (a statute bill triggers the client's assessment/limitation clock; a request on account does not), and tie the VAT consequence to it. Do NOT assert a specific HMRC notice paragraph number you have not verified; attribute to Law Society practice + Notice 700 time-of-supply rules.

## Worked-example sketches (illustrative only; label as not advice)

1. **Request for payment on account, no early tax point.** During a long litigation matter the firm sends the client a "request for payment on account" for funds to be held in client account. Because it is not a VAT invoice, no output VAT is due when it is issued. The tax point arises later: when the firm issues its VAT invoice/statute bill, or when it receives the payment in respect of the supply, whichever is earlier. Caption: "a request for payment defers the tax point; do not account for VAT until you invoice or are paid."
2. **Interim statute bill, tax point created.** The same firm later renders an interim statute bill for work to date (a defined, suable sum). That bill creates a tax point on its date (or within the 14-day rule if a VAT invoice follows), and output VAT is due for that VAT period even if the client has not yet paid. Caption: "an interim statute bill is a tax point; account for the VAT in that period."
3. **Long matter spanning the registration date / 14-day rule.** A firm completes a piece of work just before issuing its VAT invoice; the invoice goes out within 14 days, so under s.6(5) the tax point is the invoice date. If that date is on/after the firm's effective registration date, VAT applies; if the basic tax point (work performed) and invoice straddle registration, the tax point decides. Caption: "the 14-day rule and the registration date together decide whether and when VAT bites."
