## The honest thesis (lead and hold)

Almost every law firm assumes it is fully taxable for VAT (legal services are standard-rated at 20%, §6) and recovers all its input VAT. But a firm that earns **interest** (on its own deposits, and on client-account / designated-deposit balances it is entitled to retain) is making an **exempt supply of finance** (VATA 1994 Schedule 9 Group 5). Exempt income can tip a firm into **partial exemption**, and if the input VAT linked to that exempt activity breaches the **de minimis limits**, the firm loses recovery on a slice of its overhead VAT. The saving grace is that interest is usually **"incidental"** and can be excluded from the standard-method calculation, so most firms are not actually restricted. The page must walk the reader from "you might be partially exempt without realising" to "here is the de minimis test, here is why interest is usually incidental, and here is when you should actually worry," without overstating the risk. Honest, technical, reassuring-but-alert.

## H2 outline (aim 9-12 sections, ~2,900-3,500 words)

1. **The hidden VAT risk most firms miss** · set the scene: legal services standard-rated at 20% (§6), so firms assume full input VAT recovery. But interest income is exempt, and with interest rates elevated since 2022 the numbers are now big enough to matter. State plainly this is a 2026-live issue advisers are flagging. One-line signpost to the de minimis safety net so the reader does not panic.
2. **Why interest is an exempt supply (Schedule 9 Group 5)** · the legal anchor. VATA 1994 Sch 9 Group 5 (Finance) exempts the issue/dealing with money (Item 1), the making of advances and granting of credit (Item 2), and the operation of deposit/savings accounts (Item 8). Interest a firm earns is consideration for an exempt financial supply. Distinguish: this is the firm's OWN exempt income, separate from client money the firm merely holds (client money is not the firm's income, §5). The exempt supply arises on interest the firm KEEPS, e.g. the margin/retained interest on general client account and on its own office-account deposits.
3. **The client-account interest angle (the topical hook)** · explain the SRA Rule 7 duty to account to clients for a fair sum of interest (§5), and that whatever the firm retains above what it pays the client is the firm's own income, which (being interest) is exempt for VAT. Cross-link `handling-client-money-interest-sra-rules`. Make the link explicit: the SRA-driven interest the firm keeps is the exempt supply that can trigger partial exemption.
4. **What "partially exempt" actually means** · a business that makes BOTH taxable and exempt supplies is partially exempt and cannot automatically recover all its input VAT. Three categories of input VAT: directly attributable to taxable supplies (fully recoverable), directly attributable to exempt supplies (not recoverable, subject to de minimis), and residual/overhead (rent, utilities, IT, professional fees, software) which is apportioned.
5. **The standard method** · the recovery fraction (VERIFIED, see anchor block): value of taxable supplies (excluding VAT) ÷ total value of all supplies (excluding VAT) × 100, **rounded up to the next whole percentage** (round to 2 decimal places only if residual input tax exceeds £400,000/month on average). That percentage is applied to the residual (overhead) input VAT. Worked micro-illustration in section 9.
6. **The de minimis test: the safety net** · THE reassuring section, exact figures. A firm is treated as fully taxable (recovers ALL input VAT including the exempt-related part) if its exempt input tax is BOTH (a) no more than **£625 per month on average** (so **£1,875 per quarter / £7,500 a year**) AND (b) **no more than 50% of total input tax**. Mention the two simplified tests (Test One / Test Two) that let many firms confirm de minimis without a full calculation. Point: because the input VAT actually ATTRIBUTABLE to passively earning interest is usually tiny, most firms sail under de minimis.
7. **Is interest "incidental"? The crux** · the real escape route. VAT Notice 706 directs that **incidental financial transactions, for instance interest received on a bank account, are excluded from the standard-method calculation** because they would be distortive. So if the firm's interest is genuinely incidental (it arises passively, the firm does not actively manage deposits as a business activity, little/no cost is incurred to generate it, it is not a permanent extension of the business), it is left OUT of the fraction entirely and does not restrict recovery at all. State the nuance honestly: "incidental" is not statutorily defined, so it is a facts-and-degree judgment; very large or actively-managed interest may not be incidental. This is exactly the point Armstrong Watson and HMRC's PE guidance flag.
8. **The annual adjustment** · partial-exemption recovery in each quarter is provisional; at the end of the "longer period" (normally the VAT year) the firm must do an **annual adjustment**, recalculating recovery on the full-year figures and re-testing de minimis, then correcting the difference on the next return (or by error correction). Why it matters: a firm can be de minimis quarter by quarter but breach over the year, or vice versa. Date-tag: rules current 2026.
9. **Worked sketches** · see below.
10. **Practical mitigation and record-keeping** · (i) test whether interest is incidental and document the reasoning; (ii) track exempt input tax against the de minimis limits each period and at year end; (iii) consider whether any direct cost is genuinely attributable to the interest activity; (iv) keep the standard method unless a special method is genuinely better (special method needs HMRC approval, mention only briefly); (v) do not confuse this with the Flat Rate Scheme (cross-link `flat-rate-scheme-for-uk-solicitors`, where partial exemption does not apply in the same way). ## VERIFIED ANCHOR BLOCK (quote/paraphrase only from this; verified at primary source 2026-06-03)

- **Interest is an exempt supply** · **VATA 1994 Schedule 9 Group 5 (Finance)** (https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1994/23/schedule/9). Verbatim items: **Item 1** *"The issue, transfer or receipt of, or any dealing with, money, any security for money or any note or order for the payment of money."* **Item 2** *"The making of any advance or the granting of any credit."* **Item 8** *"The operation of any current, deposit or savings account."* Interest earned falls within this exempt group.
- **De minimis limits** (gov.uk VAT Notice 706, Partial exemption, https://www.gov.uk/guidance/partial-exemption-vat-notice-706, verified 2026-06-03): exempt input tax must be **no more than £625 per month on average** (stated by HMRC as **£1,875 for a quarter** and **£7,500 for an annual calculation**) **AND no more than 50% of total input tax** in the period. A business that satisfies both is treated as de minimis and may recover all its input tax (including the exempt-related part). Two simplified tests exist (Test One: total input tax no more than £625/month average AND exempt supplies no more than 50% of all supplies; Test Two: total input tax less input tax directly attributable to taxable supplies no more than £625/month average AND exempt supplies no more than 50% of all supplies). Pass any one of the three tests and you are de minimis.
- **Standard method** (Notice 706): recoverable percentage of residual input tax = **value of taxable supplies (excluding VAT) ÷ total value of supplies (excluding VAT) × 100**; the percentage **"must be rounded up to the next whole number unless you incur more than £400,000 of residual input tax each month on average in which case you must round to 2 decimal places."**
- **Annual adjustment** (Notice 706): a partial-exemption annual adjustment must be carried out at the end of the longer period (normally the tax/VAT year); it recalculates recoverable input tax on the actual full-year figures, re-assesses de minimis status, and the difference between provisional and final figures is adjusted.
- **Incidental financial transactions excluded** (Notice 706): **"Incidental financial or real estate transactions, for instance, interest received on a bank account"** must be **excluded** from the standard-method calculation because they are **"distortive."** This is the crux of the page. NOTE FOR WRITER: "incidental" is not defined in statute; treat it as a facts-and-degree test (passive vs actively managed, cost incurred to generate it, permanence, scale). Do NOT assert that interest is ALWAYS incidental; assert that it USUALLY is for a typical firm and explain when it might not be.
- Cross-anchor (regulatory): SRA Accounts Rules 2019 Rule 7 (account to client for a fair sum of interest), §5. The relevant underlying regulations are the VAT Regulations 1995 (SI 1995/2518) regs 99 to 111 (attribution, de minimis, standard method, annual adjustment); cite Notice 706 as the accessible authority and the regs as the legal basis.

## Worked-example sketches (illustrative only; label as not advice; no real figures presented as a quote)

1. **The firm that is de minimis (the common case).** A VAT-registered firm earns a modest amount of retained interest on general client account and office deposits. The input VAT directly attributable to that interest is negligible (no dedicated staff or systems), and even if it were counted, the exempt input tax is far below £625/month on average and well under 50% of total input tax. Walk through: the firm passes the de minimis test, so it recovers ALL its input VAT, including overheads. Caption: "for most firms the de minimis safety net means no actual restriction, but you must still test it."
2. **The incidental-exclusion case.** A firm with larger interest income worries it has crossed de minimis. It applies the Notice 706 rule that incidental interest is excluded from the standard-method fraction: the interest arises passively, no material cost is incurred to earn it, and it is not a business activity in its own right. Excluding it, the firm's recovery percentage is unaffected. Caption: "incidental interest comes out of the calculation entirely, document why."
3. **The firm that should worry (the edge).** A firm holds very large designated-deposit balances, actively manages placement to maximise retained interest, and the retained interest is a significant and recurring income stream. Here "incidental" is harder to argue, exempt input tax could exceed the de minimis limits, and a slice of overhead input VAT becomes irrecoverable, confirmed at the annual adjustment. Caption: "scale and active management can move interest from incidental to restrictive; this is when to get advice."
