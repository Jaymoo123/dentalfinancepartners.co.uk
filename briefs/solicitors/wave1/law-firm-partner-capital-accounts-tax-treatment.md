## The thesis (lead and hold)

A partner's capital account and current account are two different things, and confusing them causes most of the avoidable tax errors at partner level. The CAPITAL account holds the partner's fixed long-term investment in the firm (the buy-in). The CURRENT account holds the running balance of allocated profit not yet drawn, plus interest on capital, minus drawings. The key tax points the page must nail: (1) a capital contribution is an INVESTMENT, not income, so it is not taxable when paid in and not deductible; (2) the partner is taxed on their allocated PROFIT SHARE (§2), not on movements on either account and not on drawings; (3) "interest on capital" in a partnership is NOT savings interest, it is the first slice of the profit allocation under the profit-sharing agreement, so it is taxed as trading profit share with Class 4 NIC, not as bank interest; (4) the return of capital when a partner leaves is the return of their own money, not income. Funding the capital with a qualifying loan attracts interest relief (ITA 2007 ss.398 to 412, §2). The page is a clear, technically correct explainer that removes the common misconceptions.

## H2 outline (aim 9-12 sections, ~2,800-3,400 words)

1. **Two accounts, two jobs: capital vs current** · open with the distinction. The CAPITAL account = the partner's fixed, long-term investment in the firm (the buy-in), broadly fixed unless the agreement changes it. The CURRENT account = the working balance: allocated profit share credited in, interest on capital credited in, drawings taken out; it moves every month. State that both are tracked per partner in the firm's accounts and reconciled at year-end, and that the firm is tax-transparent (partnership or LLP, ITTOIA 2005 s.863, §2), so neither account is itself a taxable event. Set up the four tax questions the page answers.
2. **The capital contribution: an investment, not income** · THE core tax point. When you put capital into the firm you are buying/funding an ownership stake. It is NOT taxable income to you on the way in, and it is NOT a tax-deductible expense (you cannot deduct your buy-in). It simply sits on your capital account as your investment. Contrast with what IS taxed: your allocated profit share (§2). Note the firm does not get a deduction for it either; it is a balance-sheet movement, not a profit-and-loss item.
3. **The current account: where undrawn profit lives** · the current account is the running tally of profit you have been allocated but not yet drawn (plus interest on capital, less drawings, less your tax if the firm operates tax reserves). The crucial tax link: you are taxed on the ALLOCATED profit share when it is allocated, regardless of whether you draw it (HP §2; cross-link the drawings-vs-profit page). Undrawn profit credited to your current account has ALREADY been taxed on you; drawing it later is not a second taxable event. This is where partners wrongly think undrawn profit "has not been taxed yet."
4. **Interest on capital: an allocation, not savings interest** · THE most misunderstood item, and a strong differentiator. Many partnership agreements credit partners with "interest on capital" (e.g. a set percentage on their capital balance) before the residual profit is shared. Despite the name, this is NOT bank/savings interest and is NOT taxed under the savings rules. It is an ALLOCATION mechanism: the first slice of the partnership's trading profit, allocated to the partner under the profit-sharing arrangement (ITTOIA 2005 s.850, §2). So it is taxed as part of the partner's TRADING profit share, at income-tax rates plus Class 4 NIC (6% / 2%, §3), not as savings income with a personal savings allowance. State clearly: do not report "interest on capital" in the savings boxes of the return; it forms part of the partnership profit share. Note the firm gets no deduction for it (it is an appropriation of profit, not an expense).
5. **Drawings against the accounts: cash, not a tax point** · drawings reduce the current account (or, if overdrawn, push it negative); they are advances against the allocated share, not income in their own right and not the measure of tax. Brief, because the drawings-vs-profit page owns this; cross-link it. Make the bridge: tax follows allocation, the accounts just record the cash plumbing.
6. **Funding the capital account with a qualifying loan** · if a partner borrows to fund the capital contribution, the loan interest is deductible under qualifying-loan interest relief, ITA 2007 ss.398 to 412 (§2). Conditions (see anchor block): loan used to purchase a share in / contribute capital to / advance money to the partnership for its trade (s.398); partner throughout from use of loan to payment of interest (s.399); not an investment LLP (s.399); relief withdrawn to the extent capital is later recovered (s.399 / ss.406 to 412). Tie back: the contribution itself is not deductible, but the cost of BORROWING to fund it is relievable. Cross-link the buy-in cost page for the financing market.
7. **Return of capital when a partner leaves: not income** · on exit the partner's capital account is settled (often phased over a period set by the agreement). Returning the partner's own capital is NOT a taxable receipt: it is the return of an investment, not income. The taxable question on exit is a separate one: whether there is a CAPITAL GAIN on the disposal of the partnership interest / goodwill (CGT, with BADR potentially available, §9), which is a different cluster; one-line cross-link to the sale/exit content. Distinguish clearly: capital back at face value = not taxed; gain on the interest above what you put in = CGT, not income tax. Also flag the qualifying-loan relief withdrawal point: recovering capital can withdraw the interest relief on any loan still outstanding (s.399 / ss.406 to 412).
8. **The tax point is the profit allocation, not the account movements** · pull the thread together. The single charge to tax at partner level is on the allocated profit share each year (§2), taxed on the share not on drawings and not on account balances. Capital in, interest on capital, undrawn profit, capital out, none of those is a separate income-tax event in its own right; they are recorded on the two accounts, while the tax sits on the allocation. State this as the page's organising principle.
9. **How this looks in the firm's accounts and your return** · practical: the partnership return (SA800) reports the firm's profit and each partner's allocated share; the partner reports that share (including any interest-on-capital element) on the partnership pages of the SA100 as trading profit, NOT in the savings section. The capital and current account balances appear on the firm's balance sheet, not on the partner's personal return as income. Keep documentation of capital contributions and any loan used to fund them.

## VERIFIED ANCHOR BLOCK (quote/paraphrase only from this; verified at primary source 2026-06-03)

- **LLP tax transparency · ITTOIA 2005 s.863** (§2): the LLP pays no corporation tax on trading profit; each member is taxed as a self-employed partner on the allocated profit share. **Profit allocation · ITTOIA 2005 s.850** (§2): profit is divided between partners by the profit-sharing arrangement, and each is taxed on the allocated share. "Interest on capital" credited under the agreement is part of that allocation, taxed as trading profit share, NOT as savings interest.
- **Class 4 NIC** (SSCBA 1992 s.15), §3: 6% £12,570 to £50,270, 2% above (2025/26). **Class 2 removed from 6 April 2024** (§3).
- **Qualifying-loan interest relief · ITA 2007 s.398** (verified 2026-06-03): s.398(1) "This section applies to a loan to an individual that is used in one or more of the ways specified in subsection (2)." s.398(2): (a) purchasing a share in a partnership; (b) contributing capital to a partnership used wholly for the purposes of the trade or profession; (c) advancing money to a partnership so used; (d) repaying a loan within this section.
- **ITA 2007 s.399** (verified 2026-06-03): individual must be a member of the partnership throughout the period from the use of the loan until the interest is paid; relief unavailable if they cease to be a member; denied for an investment LLP (business wholly or mainly making investments, principal income from investments); relief ceases on recovery of capital from the partnership (except amounts treated as repayment of the loan under s.406(2)); capital-recovery rules ss.406 to 412.
- **Capital gain on exit · TCGA 1992** (§9): disposal of a partnership interest / goodwill above what the partner put in is a chargeable gain (CGT, individual rates 18% / 24% from 30 October 2024; BADR 14% for disposals 6 April 2025 to 5 April 2026, 18% from 6 April 2026, £1m lifetime limit, §9). Return of the partner's own capital at face value is NOT income. State this only as a one-line distinction and cross-link the sale cluster; do not deep-dive CGT here.

## Worked-example sketches (illustrative only; anonymised, label as not advice)

1. **Capital in is not income.** A new partner contributes capital to the firm. Nothing is taxed on them on the way in: it is an investment, recorded on their capital account, not income and not a deduction. They are taxed only on their allocated profit share for the year (§2). Caption: the buy-in is not a tax event; the profit share is.
2. **"Interest on capital" is taxed as profit share, not savings.** A partnership agreement credits a partner with interest on their capital balance before the residual profit is split. Despite the label, this is the first slice of the profit allocation (s.850), taxed as trading profit with Class 4 NIC (6% / 2%), reported on the partnership pages, NOT in the savings boxes and NOT covered by the personal savings allowance. Caption: the name says interest; the tax says profit share.
3. **Undrawn profit and the current account.** A partner is allocated profit but draws less; the undrawn balance is credited to their current account. They were taxed on the full allocation in the year it was allocated; drawing the balance later is not taxed again. Caption: the current account holds already-taxed profit, not untaxed money.
4. **Capital back on exit.** A retiring partner's capital is returned, phased over the period set by the agreement. The return of their own capital is not income. Any value received ABOVE what they put in (a gain on the interest / goodwill) is a separate CGT question (BADR may apply, §9). Caption: capital back is not income; a gain on the interest is CGT, not income tax.
