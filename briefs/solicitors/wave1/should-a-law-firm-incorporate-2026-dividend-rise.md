## The honest thesis (lead with this, hold it to the end)

The 2026/27 dividend rise (ordinary 8.75% to **10.75%**, upper 33.75% to **35.75%** from 6 April 2026, FA 2026 c.11 s.4, §3) NARROWS the incorporation tax advantage. At typical partner profit levels, incorporation is rarely a slam-dunk tax win once you fully extract profits by salary and dividend, because the combined corporation-tax-plus-dividend cost now sits close to (and sometimes above) the self-employed income-tax-plus-Class-4 cost. The REAL drivers of incorporation are not the headline tax rate: they are (1) retaining earnings inside the company at the corporation-tax rate to fund growth, lateral hires and lock-up/working capital, (2) limited liability, and (3) sale and succession planning (a company can do a share sale; a partnership/LLP cannot, §9). Frame the whole page around "the tax case has narrowed; the structural case is what matters now."

## H2 outline (aim 9-12 sections, ~2,600-3,200 words)

1. **The four structures a law firm can use (and why the choice is tax AND regulation)** · sole practitioner, general partnership (Partnership Act 1890), LLP (LLP Act 2000), company/ABS. State tax transparency: partnership and LLP are tax-transparent (no entity-level tax; partners taxed personally), a company pays corporation tax and owners extract by salary/dividend (§1, §3). Flag that incorporation is also a regulatory event: a company needs SRA recognised-body authorisation, or ABS/licensed-body licensing under Legal Services Act 2007 Part 5 if there is non-lawyer ownership (§1). Cross-link A3 for the mechanics.
2. **How an LLP member is taxed today (the baseline you are comparing against)** · taxed on **allocated profit share, not drawings** (§2); income tax at 2025/26 bands (PA £12,570 tapered £100k to £125,140; 20% to £50,270; 40% to £125,140; 45% above) plus **Class 4 NIC 6%** (£12,570 to £50,270) and **2%** above (§3); Class 2 no longer payable from 6 April 2024 (§3). No employer NIC on a genuine member's profit share.
3. **How a company is taxed (corporation tax plus the extraction stack)** · CT **19%** small-profits to £50,000, **25%** main above £250,000, marginal relief between (FY2025 and FY2026, §3). Extraction by salary plus dividend. Dividend tax 2025/26 **8.75% / 33.75% / 39.35%**, £500 allowance; from **6 April 2026** ordinary **10.75%** / upper **35.75%** (additional 39.35% unchanged), £500 allowance (FA 2026 s.4, §3). **Employer (secondary Class 1) NIC 15%** above the £5,000 secondary threshold from 6 April 2025; Employment Allowance £10,500 NOT available to a single-director-only company (§3).
4. **Why the 2026/27 dividend rise narrows the case** · the core argument. Walk the "extract everything" maths: profit faces corporation tax, then the post-CT amount faces dividend tax on extraction, plus employer NIC on any salary. Compare with the self-employed stack (income tax plus Class 4, no second layer). Conclusion: for a partner fully drawing their profit, the two routes are now close; the higher-rate band especially (upper dividend 35.75% from 6 Apr 2026 stacked on 25% CT) erodes the old advantage.
5. **Where incorporation still wins on tax: retained earnings** · if profit is RETAINED inside the company (not extracted), it suffers only corporation tax (19% to £50k / marginal / 25%), not the second dividend layer. This is the genuine lever: funding growth, lateral hires, premises, and lock-up (WIP plus debtor days, §4). Frame as "incorporation helps the firm that reinvests, not the partner who spends everything."
6. **The salaried-member interaction (a hidden complication, not a benefit)** · if you are a fixed-share or salaried member, the salaried member rules (ITTOIA 2005 ss.863A to 863G, FA 2014, §2.A) may already treat you as an employee for tax (PAYE plus employer NIC) inside the LLP. Conditions A (80% disguised salary), B (no significant influence), C (capital under 25% of disguised salary); all three must be met (§2.A). Point: a member already caught is not enjoying full self-employed treatment, which changes the comparison and may affect BADR on a later exit (§9). Cross-link the salaried-member page.
7. **Limited liability and the regulatory reality** · LLP and company both give limited liability (subject to own negligence and personal guarantees, §1); a general partnership has unlimited joint and several liability (§1). PII minimum cover is **£3 million** per claim for a recognised/licensed body (LLP, company, ABS) and **£2 million** for sole practitioners/partnerships (§10), so incorporation can raise the PII floor. Note SRA authorisation is required (recognised body, or ABS if non-lawyer owners, §1).
8. **Sale and succession: the structural case** · only an incorporated firm can do a **share sale**; a partnership/LLP can only do an **asset/business sale** (§9). Incorporating now, holding shares for 2 years, can position a later share sale for **BADR** (rate **18% from 6 April 2026**, after 14% for 6 Apr 2025 to 5 Apr 2026, £1m lifetime limit, §9). Note s.162 incorporation relief defers CGT on the goodwill when you incorporate (§9). Cross-link A3 and the practice-sale guide.
9. **Worked comparison at two profit levels** · see worked-example sketches below. Present as a table comparing total tax under LLP vs company (full extraction) at each level, then the retained-earnings scenario.
10. **A decision checklist: when incorporation makes sense (and when it does not)** · reinvesting heavily = lean incorporate; drawing everything to live on = the tax case is thin; planning a sale in a few years = incorporate for the share-sale route; want simplicity and full transparency = stay LLP. Note the regulatory cost and ongoing compliance (annual accounts, corporation tax return, payroll for salary).
