# VAT Loan for Dental Practices: When You Actually Need One

---

## Most dental practices pay no VAT

The starting point for any dentist reading this page: the supply of dental care by a
person registered in the GDC dentists' register or the dental care professionals
register is **exempt from VAT** under VATA 1994 Schedule 9 Group 7. Exempt means
outside VAT entirely, not zero-rated. Exempt turnover does not count toward the
£90,000 registration threshold. A practice with £600,000 in NHS and private dental
income but no cosmetic or aesthetics revenue owes HMRC nothing on a VAT return and
has no VAT return to file.

This page is for the minority of practices that do have a VAT bill. If you are not
sure whether your practice is VAT-registered, check with your accountant before
reading further.

---

## Which dental practices actually pay VAT?

Three categories create a genuine VAT liability for a dental practice.

**1. Mixed or cosmetic-heavy practices above the £90,000 threshold.**
When a practice provides facial aesthetics (Botox, fillers for cosmetic purposes),
tooth whitening assessed as purely aesthetic, or other treatments that do not have
"protecting, maintaining or restoring the health of the person concerned" as their
principal purpose (VATHLT2450, VATHLT2480), those supplies are standard-rated at 20%.
Once that taxable turnover exceeds £90,000 in a rolling 12-month period (raised from
£85,000 on 1 April 2024, per VATA 1994 Schedule 1, HP §6), the practice must register
for VAT and charge 20% on the cosmetic element. The net quarterly VAT payable is the
output VAT on cosmetic invoices minus the input VAT on allowable business costs (the
partial-exemption apportionment reduces the input-VAT recovery, since most dental
expenditure relates to exempt dental supplies).

The cosmetic-VAT boundary is facts-specific. HMRC scrutinises facial aesthetics and
tooth whitening in particular (VATHLT2480 makes clear that each case turns on its
facts). The BDA notes that appearance is rarely the only factor, but a practice that
markets and delivers Botox or fillers for purely cosmetic purposes, or tooth whitening
with no therapeutic purpose, has standard-rated supplies.

**2. Partially exempt practices with a recoverable-input-VAT position.**
A practice with both exempt dental and standard-rated cosmetic supplies is partially
exempt (VAT Regulations 1995 regs 99-110, HP §6.A). It reclaims only the fraction of
input VAT attributable to taxable supplies, calculated under the standard method with
a de minimis of £625 per month or £7,500 per year and no more than 50% of total input
VAT. The annual partial-exemption adjustment, which settles the difference between
in-year provisional apportionment and the true annual fraction, can create an
unexpected year-end VAT payment if the taxable proportion rose during the year.

**3. Partially exempt practices running a major CGS refurbishment.**
Where a partially exempt practice spends £250,000 or more (VAT-exclusive) on land or
a building, the Capital Goods Scheme applies (VAT Regulations 1995 regs 112-116, HP
§6.B). Input VAT reclaimed at the time of purchase is adjusted over ten annual
intervals. A practice whose taxable fraction falls in a later interval faces a
clawback of previously reclaimed input VAT, payable on the anniversary VAT return.
That adjustment is a large, dateable, one-off payment that the practice must fund
regardless of whether it has the cash available. The CGS mechanics are covered in full
on the refurbishment page; the cash-flow event is what matters here.

---

## The payment deadline and the cash-flow gap

A quarterly VAT return must be filed and paid **one calendar month and seven days
after the end of the accounting period** (verified at gov.uk/vat-returns/pay-your-vat-bill,
2026-07-09). Quarter endings and their due dates:

| Quarter end | Payment due |
|-------------|-------------|
| 31 March    | 7 May       |
| 30 June     | 7 August    |
| 30 September | 7 November  |
| 31 December | 7 February  |

The gap between the quarter end and the due date is 37 days. In that window the
practice must collect enough from its normal income to fund the VAT bill, even though
many cosmetic patients pay on the day of treatment and the cash may already be in the
account. The problem arises when a large CGS adjustment arrives unexpectedly, when a
cosmetic-heavy quarter coincides with a thin receivables month, or when the practice
has drawn down its reserves for another purpose (equipment purchase, leasehold
improvement).

---

## What is a VAT loan?

A VAT loan is a short-term, purpose-specific facility used to pay a VAT bill on its
due date. The lender advances the VAT amount (typically £5,000 to £500,000 for
UK SME products) and the practice repays over an agreed term, usually three to twelve
months, from normal trading income. The product sits outside the practice's main
working-capital facility (overdraft, revolving credit line) so it does not consume the
headroom the practice relies on for day-to-day cash management.

For a dental practice the typical use case is a quarterly cosmetic VAT bill or a
one-off CGS adjustment. The borrowing term is matched to the repayment cycle: three
months for a quarterly bill (repaid before the next quarter's bill lands), longer for
a CGS adjustment where the refurbishment recovery period stretches repayment.

The interest on a VAT loan is **tax-deductible** as a trading expense (ITTOIA 2005
for unincorporated practices, CTA 2009 Part 5 loan-relationship rules for dental
companies, HP §5.B). Principal repayments are not deductible. The HMRC late-payment
interest and penalties that the loan is designed to avoid are themselves
**not deductible** (HP §6.D).

For a comparison of overdraft, revolving facility and other working-capital products,
see [dental-practice-working-capital-overdraft-finance-options]. This page focuses
specifically on the VAT-bill context.

## HMRC late-payment penalties and interest: the cost of not bridging the gap

Since 1 January 2023, HMRC replaced the old default surcharge with a new penalty and
interest system for late VAT (Finance Act 2021 Schedule 26). The regime has three
components:

**Late-payment interest (from day 1).**
Interest accrues on the unpaid VAT from the first day after the due date at the
current rate, which floats at Bank of England base rate plus 4%. The current rate is
**7.75% per annum from 9 January 2026** (verified at gov.uk HMRC interest rates page,
2026-07-09). This is non-deductible. At 7.75%, a £10,000 unpaid bill accrues
roughly £2.12 in interest per day.

**First late-payment penalty (days 16 to 30).**
If the bill is still unpaid at day 15, HMRC charges 3% of the VAT outstanding at
that date. No penalty applies for the first 15 days. So a practice that pays between
day 16 and day 30 owes the day-1 interest plus a 3% penalty.

**Escalation at day 31.**
If still unpaid at day 31, the first penalty increases to 6% (the initial 3% plus a
further 3% charged on the amount outstanding at day 30). A second daily penalty then
accrues at an annualised rate of 10% on the outstanding balance until the bill is
cleared.

(Source: gov.uk "How late payment penalties work if you pay VAT late", verified
2026-07-09.)

## Worked example A: £48,000 quarterly VAT bill, 45 days late

*A mixed cosmetic and dental practice has a quarterly VAT liability of £48,000 (output
VAT on cosmetic treatments, net of recoverable input VAT). The bill is due 7 May. The
practice does not pay until 21 June, 45 days late.*

**Option 1: Pay 45 days late, no arrangement.**

- Day-1 to day-45 interest at 7.75% p.a. on £48,000:
  - Daily rate: £48,000 x 7.75% / 365 = £10.19 per day
  - 45 days: £10.19 x 45 = **£458.63**
- First late-payment penalty at day 15: 3% x £48,000 = **£1,440**
- Escalation at day 31: additional 3% x £48,000 = **£1,440** (first penalty now 6% total = £2,880)
- Second penalty (daily, 10% p.a.): £48,000 x 10% / 365 x 14 days (day 31 to day 45) = **£184.11**
- **Total penalty and interest cost: approximately £3,523**

**Option 2: Three-month VAT loan at 8% per annum (illustrative rate, not a quote).**

- Interest on £48,000 over 3 months at 8% p.a.: £48,000 x 8% x 3/12 = **£960**
- No HMRC penalties. Interest is tax-deductible as a trading expense.
- After-tax cost at 25% corporation tax: £960 x 75% = **£720**

**Option 3: HMRC Time to Pay over 3 months.**

- Interest at 7.75% p.a. for 3 months: £48,000 x 7.75% x 3/12 = **£930**
- If TTP is agreed and maintained: lower or no late-payment penalties apply.
- If TTP is rejected or broken: penalties apply as if no arrangement existed.
- Interest is not deductible.
- **Net cost if TTP approved and maintained: approximately £930 (non-deductible)**

**Summary:** A commercial VAT loan at a market rate compares favourably with the
late-payment route once the penalty exposure is included. A TTP arrangement, if
approved and maintained, can cost a similar amount to a commercial loan in interest
terms, but carries the risk of full penalties if broken. The right choice depends on
the practice's credit profile and HMRC's willingness to agree TTP.

## Worked example B: CGS refurbishment, partial-exemption VAT liability

*A mixed dental and cosmetic practice completes a surgery extension costing £320,000
(VAT-exclusive), paying £64,000 in VAT. At the time of the refurbishment the
practice's taxable fraction is 35% (cosmetic supply as a proportion of total supply),
so it reclaims 35% of £64,000 = £22,400 in the first VAT period. In year 3, cosmetic
turnover falls and the taxable fraction drops to 20%. The CGS adjustment for year 3
requires repayment of: (35% - 20%) x £64,000 / 10 = 15% x £6,400 = £960. This is a
VAT repayment on the year-3 VAT return.*

In this case the adjustment is relatively modest (£960). Where the taxable fraction
swings more sharply or the refurbishment is larger (a £2m building, CGS input VAT of
£400,000), the annual adjustment can run to tens of thousands of pounds. The practice
knows the adjustment is coming when it files its annual partial-exemption calculation,
giving enough lead time to arrange a short-term facility.

For the full CGS adjustment mechanics see
[capital-goods-scheme-dental-practice-refurbishment-vat].

## HMRC Time to Pay: how it actually works for VAT

HMRC may agree a Time to Pay arrangement for an overdue or upcoming VAT bill. The
process for businesses with a VAT bill is:

- Contact HMRC's Business Payment Support Service (BPSS) before the bill becomes
  overdue if possible, or as soon as the practice knows it cannot pay.
- HMRC runs an affordability assessment: the practice should be prepared to explain
  its cashflow position, why it cannot pay in full, and what it can pay and when.
- If agreed, TTP can cover the VAT principal, plus any penalties and interest already
  accrued.
- While TTP is maintained, HMRC can apply lower or no late-payment penalties. However,
  late-payment interest continues to accrue at the current rate (7.75% p.a. from
  9 January 2026).
- **If the TTP is broken**, HMRC charges first and second late-payment penalties as
  if the arrangement had never existed. This is a material risk for a practice with
  volatile cosmetic income.

TTP is not a right: approval is discretionary. A practice with an existing HMRC
compliance record, no prior TTP history in the same period, and a credible repayment
plan is more likely to be approved. A practice that has previously defaulted on a
TTP is less likely to get a further agreement.

The practical question is whether TTP or a commercial VAT loan is cheaper and more
reliable. TTP interest (7.75% p.a.) is comparable to commercial VAT loan rates; TTP
does not involve lender due-diligence or credit checks; but TTP is not guaranteed and
carries a penalty cliff if it breaks. A practice that can demonstrate clear credit
history and repayable quarterly income may prefer the certainty of a commercial
facility. A practice that is under temporary pressure and cannot borrow commercially
may have TTP as its only viable route.

## Frequently asked questions

### 1. Does my dental practice actually pay VAT?

Almost certainly not if you do only NHS or private dental treatment. The supply of
dental care by a GDC-registered dentist is exempt from VAT under VATA 1994 Schedule 9
Group 7, so exempt turnover does not accumulate toward the £90,000 registration
threshold. The practices that do face a VAT bill are those with significant cosmetic
or facial-aesthetics income (Botox, fillers, tooth whitening assessed as purely
aesthetic) above the threshold, or partially exempt practices carrying a large CGS
refurbishment liability.

### 2. Which dental practices are VAT-registered?

A practice must register once its taxable (non-exempt) turnover exceeds £90,000 in a
rolling 12-month period (raised from £85,000 on 1 April 2024). Only taxable income
counts: a practice with £500,000 in NHS fees and £80,000 in cosmetic turnover is below
the threshold. Once cosmetic turnover crosses £90,000, registration is compulsory. A
partially exempt practice with both exempt dental and standard-rated cosmetic supplies
reclaims only the proportion of input VAT that relates to its taxable supplies.

### 3. How big is a typical dental VAT bill and when is it due?

A quarterly VAT return must be paid one calendar month and seven days after the end of
the accounting period (so a quarter ending 31 March is due 7 May). For a practice with
£200,000 per year in taxable cosmetic turnover, the output VAT is roughly £40,000 per
year (£10,000 per quarter) before deducting recoverable input VAT on business costs.
A mixed practice with a large CGS refurbishment may face a one-off VAT bill of
£50,000 or more on a single construction invoice.

### 4. What is a VAT loan and how does it work for a dental practice?

A VAT loan is a short-term facility drawn when the quarter's VAT payment is due and
repaid from the next quarter's income or an existing cash buffer over three to twelve
months. The lender pays HMRC directly or releases funds to the practice. It is a
specific product distinct from a revolving working-capital facility or an overdraft.
The interest on a VAT loan is tax-deductible as a trading expense (HP §5.B); the
principal repayment is not.

### 5. When does a VAT loan make more sense than using an overdraft?

An overdraft is flexible but usually callable and carries a facility fee on the
headroom whether drawn or not. A VAT loan is purpose-matched to the quarterly payment
cycle: drawn for a fixed term (typically three months), repaid from the next quarter's
receipts, and off the balance sheet when the next bill arrives. If the practice already
carries a high overdraft balance, a separate VAT facility avoids compounding the
overdraft utilisation.

### 6. What is HMRC Time to Pay and is it a better option than borrowing?

HMRC may agree a TTP arrangement allowing the practice to spread a VAT bill over
agreed instalments. A TTP, if maintained, can result in lower or no late-payment
penalties (HP §6.D). However, interest continues to accrue from day one at the current
late-payment rate (7.75% per annum from 9 January 2026, base rate plus 4%). TTP is
not guaranteed: HMRC runs an affordability assessment. If the arrangement is broken,
HMRC charges all first and second late-payment penalties as if the TTP had never
existed. For a practice with a good credit profile, a commercial VAT loan at a
competitive rate may cost less than the interest-plus-penalty exposure of a rejected
or broken TTP.

### 7. What are the HMRC late-payment penalties if I just pay my VAT late?

Under the regime introduced from 1 January 2023 (Finance Act 2021 Schedule 26):
late-payment interest accrues from day one at the current rate (7.75% p.a. from
9 January 2026). No penalty applies for the first 15 days. A first late-payment
penalty of 3% of the VAT owed is charged if you pay between day 16 and day 30
(calculated on the amount outstanding at day 15). If you still have not paid by day
31, the first penalty rises to 6% (3% plus an additional 3% on the amount outstanding
at day 30), and a second penalty accrues daily at an annualised rate of 10% on the
outstanding balance until the bill is cleared.

### 8. Is the interest on a VAT loan tax-deductible?

Yes. Interest (not principal) on borrowing wholly and exclusively for the trade is
deductible: for an unincorporated practice under the trading-expense rules (ITTOIA 2005,
BIM45650+), and for a dental company under the loan-relationship rules (CTA 2009
Part 5). The Corporate Interest Restriction (TIOPA 2010 Part 10) only bites above a
£2,000,000 group de minimis, so a normal single dental company is unaffected. The
HMRC interest and late-payment penalties themselves are NOT deductible (HP §6.D).

### 9. Are HMRC late-payment interest and penalties themselves tax-deductible?

No. HMRC late-payment interest and late-payment penalties on VAT are not deductible
as trading expenses. Only the interest on a commercial loan taken out to fund the VAT
payment is deductible (HP §6.D; HP §5.B).

### 10. What if my practice is partially exempt? Does that change the VAT loan picture?

A partially exempt practice reclaims only the portion of input VAT attributable to
taxable supplies (VAT Regulations 1995 regs 99-110, de minimis £625/month or
£7,500/year and no more than 50% of total input VAT). The net VAT payable is therefore
smaller than the headline output VAT on cosmetic turnover, but there is still a
quarterly cash-flow gap. Partial exemption mechanics are covered in full on the VAT
registration page. This page focuses on bridging the net payment when it falls due.

### 11. What about a large refurbishment triggering a one-off CGS VAT bill?

A partially exempt practice that spends £250,000 or more (VAT-exclusive) on land or a
building triggers the Capital Goods Scheme (VAT Regulations 1995 regs 112-116). The
annual CGS adjustment, calculated over ten intervals, can generate a one-off repayment
of previously reclaimed input VAT in a later year. A VAT loan can bridge that
adjustment between the return-filing date and the practice's next cash cycle. Full CGS
mechanics are on the refurbishment page.

### 12. What is the deregistration threshold?

£88,000 (VATA 1994 Schedule 1, HP §6.C). A practice whose taxable turnover falls
below £88,000 may apply to deregister. On deregistration a deemed supply arises on
VAT-registered stock and assets (Schedule 4 paragraph 8 of VATA 1994, de minimis
£1,000 for the resulting VAT charge), which must be settled on the final return. If
the practice has significant VAT-bearing assets, that final deemed-supply charge may
itself need bridging.
