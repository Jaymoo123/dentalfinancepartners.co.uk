# Wealth Management and Investment Planning for Dentists (UK)

## Statutory anchor

Primary education anchors (tax treatment, not product), all verified at
primary source 2026-07-09:

- **ISA:** Income Tax Act 2007 s.694 and Individual Savings Account Regulations
  1998 (SI 1998/1870, as amended); annual subscription limit **£20,000 for
  2026/27** (gov.uk/individual-savings-accounts, confirmed 2026-07-09). Income
  and gains inside an ISA are exempt from UK income tax and CGT.
- **CGT annual exempt amount:** **£3,000 per individual** for 2026/27
  (gov.uk/capital-gains-tax/allowances, confirmed 2026-07-09). Locked per
  house position §15.
- **CGT rates on non-residential assets (2026/27, LOCKED):** **18% basic rate
  / 24% higher and additional rate** on non-residential, non-BADR gains from
  6 April 2026 (gov.uk/capital-gains-tax/rates, confirmed 2026-07-09). This
  resolves the [NEW HP LOCK NEEDED] flag in the Stage 1 seed brief. Residential
  property rates are also 18% / 24% from 6 Apr 2024 (unchanged). BADR gains
  remain 18% from 6 Apr 2026 (house position §4).
- **Personal Savings Allowance:** **£1,000 basic rate / £500 higher rate / nil
  additional rate** (gov.uk/apply-tax-free-interest-on-savings, confirmed
  2026-07-09; no 2026/27 change legislated). Statutory basis: Finance Act 2016
  ss.4-5, ITA 2007 ss.12A-12B.
- **Dividend allowance + rates 2026/27:** £500 per individual; rates
  10.75% / 35.75% / 39.35% from 6 Apr 2026 (gov.uk/tax-on-dividends; FA 2026
  s.4 amending ITA 2007 s.8; house position §15).
- **SIPP / private pension:** Finance Act 2004 Part 4. Annual allowance figure
  deferred entirely to house position §2.B (£60,000 / taper / £10,000 floor,
  2025/26). This page does NOT re-state pension mechanics.
- **GIA (general investment account):** no statutory wrapper; gains subject to
  CGT at 18% / 24% (non-residential assets, from 6 Apr 2026, confirmed above).
  AEA £3,000 applies per individual.
- **CIHC / investing inside the company:** CTA 2010 s.18N defines a close
  investment-holding company (CIHC); s.18A(1)(b) excludes a CIHC from the
  small-profits rate, so a CIHC pays main-rate CT (25%) on all profits
  regardless of size. There is no undistributed passive-income surcharge under
  current law (house position §15, CTM60710 confirmed 2026-07-09). The costs
  are: main-rate CT on investment income and gains, plus an extraction layer
  (income tax and NIC on salary, or dividend tax at 35.75% / 39.35% for a
  higher-rate dentist) when the money comes out. No personal CGT AEA or PSA
  inside a company.
- **Spousal transfers:** TCGA 1992 s.58 (confirmed in force, last amended
  Finance (No. 2) Act 2023, legislation.gov.uk 2026-07-09): disposals between
  spouses / civil partners living together are no gain / no loss; the
  transferee takes the asset at the transferor's base cost. Allows transfer of
  assets to a lower-rate spouse before sale or to use a second CGT AEA.
- **LISA:** governed by HMRC regulations under ITA 2007; £4,000 annual
  contribution limit, 25% government bonus (max £1,000/year), open if under
  40, contributions stop at 50 (gov.uk/lifetime-isa, confirmed 2026-07-09).
  Property price cap and withdrawal charge details: fetched page did not return
  the specific figure; writer must fetch gov.uk/lifetime-isa/buying-property
  and gov.uk/lifetime-isa/withdrawal-charges at write time and lock the
  current figures.

## Worked Example A: Order-of-wrappers for a principal with £30,000 annual surplus (2026/27)

*For illustration only. Tax depends on individual circumstances. This is not
financial advice.*

Scenario: sole-trader or unincorporated principal, £130,000 NHS-derived profit
after practice costs; NHS pension funded at practitioner rate; £30,000 net
annual surplus available to invest.

**Step 1: Pension top-up (short link, not re-explained)**
If annual allowance headroom exists (carry-forward, taper position, or SIPP
room), pension is the first call on any surplus because income-tax relief is
immediate and compounding is tax-free. Mechanics: see
[dentist-pension-contributions-tax-relief-uk] and
[nhs-pension-annual-allowance-nhs-plus-private-sipp-aggregation].

Assume for this example the pension is fully funded (no further allowance
room). The £30,000 surplus moves to Step 2.

**Step 2: ISA (£20,000 out of the £30,000)**
£20,000 goes into a Stocks and Shares ISA. Income and growth inside the ISA
are permanently sheltered: no income tax on interest or dividends, no CGT on
gains, no reporting on the SA return. A spouse with their own £20,000 ISA
allowance can simultaneously shelter another £20,000 of household funds
(assets may be transferred to the spouse at nil gain under TCGA s.58 first if
that simplifies the source-of-funds position).

**Step 3: GIA with allowance harvest (the remaining £10,000)**
The remaining £10,000 goes into a general investment account. As this
dentist's total income is £130,000 the effective CGT rate on any gains will
be 24% (higher rate). Each tax year, up to £3,000 of realised gains can be
taken free of CGT (the annual exempt amount). A routine bed-and-ISA in
subsequent tax years (sell in the GIA, rebuy inside the ISA subscription)
gradually migrates the portfolio into the permanent shelter. The £500
dividend allowance and the £500 higher-rate PSA cover the first slice of
investment income from the GIA.

**Take-away:** At a £30,000 surplus, ISA and GIA cover the full amount. A
pension top-up would be preferred if carry-forward allowance exists, because
relief at the 40% or 45% rate (depending on adjusted income) exceeds the ISA
benefit in year one. The ISA wins on flexibility: no minimum drawdown age,
no annuity, withdrawals tax-free at any time.

## Worked Example B: Investing inside the company vs extracting and wrapping personally (2026/27)

*For illustration only. Not a personal recommendation.*

Scenario: dental limited company, £50,000 retained profit after the
principal's salary and NHS pension funding. Principal is a higher-rate
taxpayer on personal income. Two options:

**Option 1: Leave the money inside the company and invest it there**

The company invests the £50,000 in a portfolio of equities and bonds.

- Investment income (dividends from the portfolio) is subject to CT at 19%
  (small profits) initially.
- If the company's investment activity grows to the point where it exists
  "wholly or mainly" for holding investments, it becomes a close
  investment-holding company (CIHC) under CTA 2010 s.18N. At that point
  s.18A(1)(b) removes the small-profits rate and the company pays CT at
  25% on all profits, regardless of size.
- There is no undistributed-income surcharge (the old apportionment rules
  were abolished decades ago), so retained profits are not taxed again simply
  for sitting in the company. The CIHC rate consequence (25% on everything)
  and the extraction layer are the real costs.
- Eventually, to use the money personally (retire, ISA contribution, living
  costs), the principal must extract it: salary (income tax + NIC) or
  dividends (35.75% for a higher-rate taxpayer in 2026/27, per FA 2026 s.4
  and house position §15). Only £500 of dividends escapes tax via the dividend
  allowance.
- No personal CGT AEA and no PSA applies inside the company.

**Option 2: Extract and invest personally**

The principal takes a dividend of £50,000. At higher-rate dividend tax
(35.75% in 2026/27): tax of ~£17,875 (net of the £500 allowance). After-tax
proceeds: ~£32,125 available to invest personally.

- £20,000 into ISA: permanently sheltered.
- £12,125 into GIA: first £3,000 of gains sheltered by AEA; first £500 of
  interest sheltered by PSA; first £500 of dividends sheltered by dividend
  allowance.

**The crossover question:** Option 2 hurts upfront (the dividend tax is
cash out now) but shelters growth in the ISA permanently. Option 1 defers
that cash cost but risks the CIHC trap, adds an extraction layer later, and
earns no personal wrapper benefits on the money while it sits in the company.
For a dentist with a long runway to retirement and meaningful ISA capacity,
early extraction and ISA wrapping typically wins over the long term. For a
dentist within a few years of practice sale, the timing question (extract
now at 35.75% or wait for a lower personal income year post-sale) becomes
more nuanced. An FCA-regulated financial planner can model the numbers for a
specific situation.

## FAQ expansion (8 to 12 questions, full draft answers)

### 1. After maxing the NHS pension and SIPP, what tax-sheltered options are left?

Once your pension allowance is fully used, the next shelters are the ISA
(£20,000 per person per year, 2026/27) and, where relevant, a spouse's ISA.
Income and gains inside an ISA are permanently exempt from UK income tax and
CGT. There is no lock-in age and no drawdown rules: you can access the money
any time. After the ISA is filled, a general investment account (GIA) uses the
annual CGT exempt amount (£3,000, 2026/27), the personal savings allowance
(£500 for higher-rate taxpayers, 2026/27) and the dividend allowance (£500,
2026/27) to shelter the first slice of returns. None of these is as
tax-efficient as the pension or ISA, but they are flexible and accessible.

### 2. What is the "order-of-wrappers" and does it change for dentists?

The standard tax-planning order is: pension first (immediate income-tax relief,
tax-free compounding), ISA second (no relief upfront but fully sheltered
thereafter, no minimum age), GIA last (allowances only). For a dentist on
120,000 to 200,000 of NHS-derived income, the personal allowance is usually
tapered to nil (the taper operates above £100,000 of adjusted net income,
removing £1 of allowance for every £2 of excess, creating a 60% effective
rate band between £100,000 and £125,140). That makes pension contributions
especially valuable in clearing the taper band. Once the pension allowance
is exhausted, the ISA remains attractive precisely because returns inside it
are invisible to HMRC: they do not push up the income that further compresses
the personal allowance. The GIA, by contrast, adds income and gains to the SA
return and can interact with the taper, higher-rate bands and PSA/dividend
positions.

### 3. Can I invest practice cash inside the limited company?

You can, and it is common. The risks are:

First, corporation tax on investment income inside the company: currently 19%
at small-profits scale (profits up to £50,000) and 25% at the main rate
(profits above £250,000), with marginal relief between (house position §2,
§5). There is no PSA, ISA or personal CGT AEA inside the company.

Second, the close investment-holding company (CIHC) trap: if your dental
company comes to exist "wholly or mainly" for holding investments rather than
trading, it becomes a CIHC under CTA 2010 s.18N, and under s.18A(1)(b) it
loses access to the small-profits rate entirely. All profits then pay
corporation tax at 25% regardless of size. A single-surgery practice drawing
professional income predominantly from NHS work is unlikely to become a CIHC,
but an incorporated dentist who retires from practice while leaving a large
invested fund inside the company faces a real risk.

Third, the extraction layer: to use the money personally you must take
salary (income tax and NIC) or dividends (35.75% higher-rate, 39.35%
additional-rate in 2026/27). There is no personal wrapper to absorb it.

The tax that has already been paid inside the company at 19% or 25% does not
disappear; it reduces the pot before extraction, and the extraction itself
carries a further personal tax cost. Whether retaining inside vs extracting
and wrapping personally is more efficient depends on the gap between CT rates
and personal tax rates, the investment timeline, and the eventual exit
scenario. A regulated financial planner and your accountant should model
both paths with your specific numbers.

### 4. How does a spouse or civil partner help with investment tax?

Each spouse or civil partner has their own annual ISA allowance (£20,000),
their own CGT exempt amount (£3,000), their own dividend allowance (£500) and
their own personal savings allowance (£1,000 basic / £500 higher / nil
additional). Transfers of assets between spouses who live together are treated
as no gain / no loss under TCGA 1992 s.58, so there is no CGT cost in
shifting assets to the spouse with more capacity to use their allowances.

In practice: if one dentist-spouse has exhausted their allowances and the
other has basic-rate income, investment assets can be transferred at nil gain
and the returns sheltered at a lower personal rate or within the lower-earner's
PSA/dividend/AEA headroom. The arrangements must be genuine: the lower-earner
must actually own and receive the income from the transferred assets (the
settlements legislation, ITTOIA 2005 ss.619-648, can look through
arrangements that are not genuine transfers of beneficial ownership).

### 5. What is the Lifetime ISA and is it relevant to me as a dentist?

The Lifetime ISA (LISA) allows a 25% government bonus (up to £1,000/year)
on savings of up to £4,000 per year. It can be used to buy a first home or
accessed penalty-free from age 60. You must open the account before your 40th
birthday, and contributions stop at age 50.

For most mid-career dentists already funding the NHS pension, the LISA is
usually not the most efficient vehicle because:
- The pension gives immediate income-tax relief at your marginal rate (40%
  or 45% for higher-earning dentists), which exceeds the 25% LISA bonus.
- The LISA has a property price cap and a 25% withdrawal charge on
  non-qualifying withdrawals (the charge effectively claws back the bonus and
  part of your own money; the writer must confirm the current property price
  cap from gov.uk/lifetime-isa/buying-property at write time).
- The LISA £4,000 limit comes out of your overall £20,000 ISA allowance.
- Accessing money before age 60 for reasons other than first-home purchase
  triggers the withdrawal charge.

For a dentist in the early twenties who has not yet bought a first home and
whose pension contributions are limited, the LISA can be worth combining with
a standard Stocks and Shares ISA. For an established principal, the standard
ISA is almost always the more flexible tool.

### 6. What is the "bed-and-ISA" and how does it work?

A bed-and-ISA is the practice of selling an investment held in a GIA and
immediately rebuying it inside a Stocks and Shares ISA subscription. The
sale crystallises any gain (which uses the £3,000 AEA if within it, or is
taxable above it), and the ISA subscription shelters all future growth and
income permanently.

The mechanics: you sell in the GIA on day one, the settlement proceeds arrive,
and you subscribe to the ISA (up to the annual £20,000 limit) to buy back
the same or similar asset. Some platforms allow this in a single step. The
key constraint is the ISA annual subscription limit: you can migrate at most
£20,000 per year per person into ISA shelter. A dentist with a significant GIA
portfolio should run a bed-and-ISA each tax year to transfer as much as the
limit and their gain position allows, ideally using the AEA in full (selling
enough to realise £3,000 of gains if the portfolio allows). A spouse's AEA can
be used in parallel if assets are transferred under s.58 first.

### 7. Does investing in a buy-to-let property count as "wealth management" on this page?

This page covers financial investment wrappers (ISA, GIA, company surplus).
The tax treatment of a buy-to-let property alongside a dental practice,
including rental income, the finance-cost restriction, capital gains on sale,
and the SDLT additional-rate surcharge, is covered in full at
[dental-associate-buy-to-let-tax-implications]. Cross-refer there for anything
property-related. This page does not repeat that content.

### 8. At what income level does it make more sense to extract and wrap, rather than leave money in the company?

There is no universal threshold: the comparison depends on the gap between
your CT rate inside and your personal tax rate on extraction, the length of
the investment horizon, and what happens to the company. Some general
observations for 2026/27:

- If the company is comfortably inside the small-profits band (profits below
  £50,000 after salary and pension), initial CT is 19%. Your higher-rate
  dividend tax on extraction is 35.75%. The company "pre-funds" the
  investment at 19% but you eventually pay 35.75% to get it out: the net
  personal return depends on how long the money compounds inside.
- If the company risks the CIHC classification (growing investment portfolio,
  reducing or nil trading income), the CT rate on investment income rises to
  25%, and the extraction cost adds on top.
- For a dentist who is 10 to 20 years from retirement, ISA contributions over
  that runway can shelter £200,000 to £400,000 per person (£400,000 to
  £800,000 for a couple) permanently. That capacity is destroyed if the surplus
  sits in the company instead.

The modelling is specific to your numbers, your timeline and your company's
trading position. Get a regulated financial planner to run the comparison.

### 9. What should I ask an FCA-regulated financial planner before the first meeting?

Tax wrappers and their treatment are factual (and are what this page covers).
Choosing what to hold inside them, how to structure a portfolio for your
retirement timeline, how to combine the NHS pension with personal investments,
and whether to draw now or defer: all of that requires regulated financial
advice. Look for an IFA or financial planner authorised by the Financial
Conduct Authority (register.fca.org.uk) with demonstrable experience of dental
professional clients.

Questions worth raising at a first meeting:

- What is my target retirement income and how does the NHS pension project
  against it (NHSBSA annual statement)?
- What is my ISA capacity over the next 10 to 20 years and how should I
  prioritise pension top-up vs ISA vs GIA?
- If I hold investments inside the company, what is the modelled after-tax
  return vs extracting and wrapping?
- How should I structure assets between myself and my spouse to use both sets
  of allowances efficiently?
- At what income or wealth level does the CIHC risk become a live concern for
  my company?
- What does my exit plan (practice sale, retirement) mean for the timing of
  asset extraction and wrapper contributions?

### 10. What is a close investment-holding company and how does it affect a dental company?

A close investment-holding company (CIHC) is a close company that exists
"wholly or mainly" for holding investments rather than trading (CTA 2010
s.18N). A typical active dental practice is not a CIHC because its purpose
is the trade of dentistry. The risk arises when:

- A principal retires but leaves a portfolio of investments inside the company
  without closing it.
- The trading income drops significantly (e.g. a winding-down or part-time
  principal) while the investment portfolio grows.
- A company is set up primarily to hold assets (property, equities) alongside
  or after a practice.

The tax consequence under CTA 2010 s.18A(1)(b): the CIHC loses access to the
small-profits rate and pays corporation tax at 25% on all profits, regardless
of how small they are. There is no undistributed-income surcharge (house
position §15): the only extra costs are the rate upgrade and the double-charge
on extraction. A dental company that is genuinely trading throughout is not
at risk. The risk is transitional, typically on or after practice sale or
significant winding-down.

### 11. Can I use a pension and an ISA in the same tax year?

Yes. There is no interaction between pension contributions and ISA
subscriptions. You can contribute up to your annual allowance to pensions
(subject to taper, carry-forward, and the 100%-of-earnings cap per FA 2004
s.190) and subscribe up to £20,000 to ISAs in the same tax year. They are
entirely separate limits. If you hold a LISA, the £4,000 LISA contribution
comes out of the £20,000 overall ISA allowance for the year.

### 12. What happens to ISA holdings on death?

On death, ISA holdings can be transferred to a surviving spouse or civil
partner as an "additional permitted subscription" (APS), allowing the
spouse to subscribe an extra amount equal to the deceased's ISA value
without it counting against the survivor's own £20,000 annual ISA
allowance. The APS effectively preserves the ISA shelter across the death
of one spouse. For wider estate planning (IHT, BPR on practice assets),
see house position §4.B and the succession-planning page at
[dental-practice-succession-planning-family-transfer].
