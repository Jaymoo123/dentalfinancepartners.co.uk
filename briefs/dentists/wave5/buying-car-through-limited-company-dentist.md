# Buying a Car Through a Limited Company: A Dentist's Decision Guide

## Statutory anchor

The benefit-in-kind (BIK) company-car charge is calculated under **ITEPA 2003 ss.114-148** ("the car benefit rules"): list price multiplied by the appropriate percentage (determined by CO2 emissions and, for ultra-low-emission vehicles, electric range). The resulting BIK value is taxed on the director/employee at their marginal income-tax rate and triggers **Class 1A NIC on the employer** (the dental company), reported and paid via **P11D/P11D(b)** for the tax year.

The alternative route (personal ownership + mileage claim) uses the **Approved Mileage Allowance Payment (AMAP) scheme** (ITEPA 2003 ss.229-236): the company pays the director 55p/mile for the first 10,000 business miles and 25p/mile thereafter from 6 April 2026 (45p/25p applied to 5 April 2026). The 55p rate was confirmed at HMRC manual EIM31240 (s.230(2) ITEPA 2003), effective from the 2026/27 tax year.

VAT input-tax block on car purchase: **VAT (Input Tax) (Exemption) Order 1992 / Value Added Tax (Cars) Order 1992** (generally blocks input VAT on purchase of cars where any private use is possible; full recovery only where the car is used exclusively for business with no private availability). Leasing: **50% of input VAT on lease rentals** is recoverable where some private use exists (standard practice for a dental director's car). WRITE-TIME FETCH REQUIRED: confirm 50% lease rule at VIT52530 or VAT Notice 700/64 section 5.4.

Capital allowances on the car sit on the **WDA page** (writing-down-allowance-cars-dentists). One sentence and link only -- do NOT reproduce rate tables.

Class 1A NIC: **15%** on the P11D BIK value (employer rate from 6 April 2025, locked §5 / §13 house positions).

3. Should the car be owned by the company or by the director personally? Work through a side-by-side comparison at a typical dental director profit level (e.g. £120,000 salary+dividends = higher-rate taxpayer): (a) company car route: BIK tax on director + Class 1A NIC cost to company; (b) personal mileage route: AMAP at 55p/mile, no BIK, no Class 1A, but no corporation-tax deduction for the car cost (except via WDA on personal vehicle -- link). Which route wins depends on annual business mileage and the specific car's CO2/list-price.

4. Does the maths change for an EV? Walk through a worked comparison: a £45,000 new EV through the company at 4% BIK (2026/27) vs a £45,000 EV owned personally (no WDA via AMAP, 100% FYA taken by the company if owned by it). Quantify the total annual tax cost for both routes at a higher-rate taxpayer.

5. What is the fuel benefit and when does it apply? The **fuel benefit** (ITEPA 2003 s.149) is a separate BIK charge on free private fuel provided by the company: **fuel benefit multiplier £29,200 for 2026/27** (s.150(1) ITEPA 2003; SI 2025/1254; confirmed EIM25580, fetched 2026-07-09, up from £28,200 in 2025/26 and £27,800 in 2024/25) x appropriate percentage. Almost always NOT worth it: the company would need to provide large quantities of private fuel before the charge is offset. Practical rule: do not provide free private fuel. Note: electricity used to charge a company EV is NOT "fuel" within the fuel benefit rules (EIM23900), so the fuel benefit charge does not apply to EV charging.

6. How does P11D/P11D(b) work and what are the deadlines? P11D filed by 6 July after the tax year end; Class 1A NIC paid by 22 July (electronic). The company pays Class 1A at 15% on the BIK value. The director is taxed via PAYE coding adjustment or self-assessment.

7. What are the VAT rules on a company car purchase vs lease? (a) Purchase: VAT on the car cost is blocked unless exclusive business use with no private availability -- almost never met for a dental director (VIT52700, fetched 2026-07-09). (b) Lease: 50% of VAT on lease rentals is recoverable. WRITE-TIME FETCH REQUIRED: confirm exact statutory basis for 50% lease rule at VIT52530 / VAT Notice 700/64. (c) EV charging at the workplace: company can recover VAT on electricity costs for a company car (electricity is not "fuel" for fuel-benefit purposes, EIM23900). Home charging: the employer can reimburse the director for home-charging costs without triggering a separate BIK charge, provided reimbursement relates solely to the company vehicle (s.239(2) ITEPA 2003, confirmed EIM23900, fetched 2026-07-09). Note: the reimbursement is for electricity cost, calculated using HMRC's advisory electric rate (AER) for business mileage or actual metered cost. WRITE-TIME FETCH REQUIRED: confirm current AER figure at gov.uk/guidance/advisory-electric-rate and confirm VAT recoverability on home-charging reimbursement.

8. Does leasing (rather than purchasing) through the company change the BIK and tax analysis? The BIK is calculated on the list price regardless of whether the company bought or leased -- the BIK rules do not care about the financing method. However, leasing affects corporation tax deductibility (lease rentals are revenue-deductible; a purchase goes through WDA -- one sentence + link to WDA page) and affects VAT (50% input-tax recovery on lease payments).

9. What is the NHS pension trap interaction? A dental director taking a large company-car BIK has effectively accepted a non-cash benefit in place of salary or dividend -- but the BIK does not affect NHS pension accrual directly (pension accrual is on PAYE salary only, §2.C). However, the BIK increases the director's total taxable income, which could push them further into the taper for pension annual allowance (§2.B). Cross-ref §2.C and §5.

10. When is the company car route clearly better, and when is the mileage route clearly better? Provide a rule-of-thumb decision tree: low business mileage + petrol car = mileage route usually wins; high business mileage + EV = company car often wins because 4% BIK on an EV is very low and the corporation tax deduction (WDA/100% FYA) is available to the company.

## Worked example data (Stage 2 verified figures -- RUN writer uses these directly)

### Scenario assumptions (2026/27, higher-rate taxpayer: 40% income tax)

- Director is a dental principal, salary + dividends totalling ~£120,000, firmly higher-rate (40% IT on income; 15% Class 1A NIC on BIK value borne by the company)
- Corporation tax rate: 25% (profits above £250,000 for simplicity; adjust with marginal-relief note if practice profits are between £50k-£250k)
- Business mileage: 8,000 miles per year
- Petrol car option: list price £35,000, CO2 ~122g/km (rounds to 120g band = **30%** appropriate percentage 2026/27)
- EV option: list price £45,000, 0g/km = **4%** appropriate percentage 2026/27
- Personal car + AMAP: director owns the car personally, company pays 55p/mile for first 10,000 miles (2026/27)

---

### Option A: Company-owned EV (£45,000 list price, 4% BIK, 2026/27)

**Director's income tax cost:**
- BIK value = £45,000 x 4% = £1,800
- Income tax at 40% = £1,800 x 40% = **£720/year**

**Company's Class 1A NIC cost:**
- Class 1A = £1,800 x 15% = **£270/year**

**Company's CT saving on running costs** (fuel/insurance/servicing paid by company):
- Deductible running costs: assume £3,000/year. CT saving = £3,000 x 25% = £750

**Company's CT saving on car capital cost:**
- 100% FYA available (new zero-emission car, to 31 March 2027 CT / 5 April 2027 IT, per WDA page). CT saving in year 1 = £45,000 x 25% = **£11,250** (if purchased before FYA deadline)
- After FYA deadline: WDA at 100% pool (zero-emission cars go into main pool from April 2027 at 14% WDA -- see WDA page)

**Total annual tax cost (director):** £720 income tax
**Total annual company tax cost:** £270 Class 1A NIC, partially offset by CT deduction on running costs
**Net first-year position:** Low director BIK tax + significant CT saving from 100% FYA makes this attractive for a new EV.

---

### Option B: Company-owned petrol car (£35,000 list price, 30% BIK, 2026/27)

**Director's income tax cost:**
- BIK value = £35,000 x 30% = £10,500
- Income tax at 40% = £10,500 x 40% = **£4,200/year**

**Company's Class 1A NIC cost:**
- Class 1A = £10,500 x 15% = **£1,575/year**

**Company's CT saving on car capital cost:**
- Petrol 122g/km: main pool (18% WDA before April 2026; from April 2026 WDA falls per FA 2026 s.28 -- see WDA page). No 100% FYA.
- CT saving year 1: approx £35,000 x 14% (post-FA 2026 main-rate WDA) x 25% CT = **£1,225**

**Company's CT saving on running costs** (fuel/insurance/servicing):
- Assume £3,500/year. CT saving = £3,500 x 25% = £875

**Total annual director tax cost:** **£4,200/year income tax** (plus risk of fuel benefit charge if private fuel provided: £29,200 x 30% x 40% = £3,504 additional tax -- almost never worth it)
**Total annual company cost:** £1,575 Class 1A NIC

**Verdict vs EV route:** The petrol BIK costs the director £3,480/year more in income tax than the EV route. For a 3-year lease, the cumulative difference is over £10,000 in director tax alone, before accounting for the 100% FYA available on the EV.

---

### Option C: Personal car + AMAP at 55p/mile, 8,000 business miles (2026/27)

**AMAP received by director:**
- 8,000 miles x 55p = **£4,400/year** (tax-free to the director, no NIC)

**Company's CT deduction on AMAP payments:**
- £4,400 x 25% CT = **£1,100 CT saving**

**Director's tax cost:** £0 income tax, £0 NIC (AMAP is below the approved amount)

**What the director loses vs company-car route:**
- No capital allowances on the car via the company (the director takes personal WDA on a personally owned car -- link to WDA page; 14% main-rate or 6% special-rate on a personal car used for business)
- No CT deduction for the car purchase cost directly (only via the AMAP payments)
- Wear-and-tear and depreciation borne personally

**Verdict:** For 8,000 business miles, AMAP pays £4,400 tax-free. The company gets a CT deduction on those payments. There is NO BIK tax and NO Class 1A NIC. For a petrol car, the AMAP route saves the director ~£4,200/year in income tax vs the company-petrol-car BIK (Option B). For an EV, the company-EV route (Option A) costs only £720/year in BIK tax but delivers a £11,250 first-year CT saving via 100% FYA -- making the company-EV route potentially superior for a new EV purchase even at modest mileage.

---

### Summary decision table (2026/27 illustrative, higher-rate taxpayer, 8,000 business miles)

| Route | Director annual tax cost | Company annual NIC cost | Company CT benefit (year 1) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Company EV (£45k, 4%) | £720 | £270 | £11,250 (100% FYA) |
| Company petrol (£35k, 30%) | £4,200 | £1,575 | ~£1,225 (14% WDA) |
| Personal + AMAP (55p/8,000mi) | £0 | £0 | £1,100 (CT deduction on AMAP) |

The EV route dominates on director tax cost and year-1 CT savings. The AMAP route dominates on simplicity and avoids all BIK complexity. The company petrol route is rarely optimal for higher-rate taxpayers.

**FAQ 1: What is a benefit in kind on a company car and how is it calculated?**
A benefit in kind (BIK) is a taxable perk you receive from your company that is not cash. When your dental company owns a car that is available for your private use, HMRC treats this as a BIK. The charge is: list price of the car multiplied by an appropriate percentage (set by HMRC based on CO2 emissions), giving the BIK value. You pay income tax on the BIK value at your marginal rate (40% if you are a higher-rate taxpayer). Your company also pays Class 1A National Insurance at 15% on the same BIK value. The calculation is under ITEPA 2003 ss.114-148.

**FAQ 2: What is the BIK rate on an electric car for 2026/27?**
For a pure electric car (zero CO2 emissions) the appropriate percentage is 4% in 2026/27 (confirmed HMRC EIM24705). On a £45,000 EV, the BIK value is £1,800 (£45,000 x 4%). As a 40% taxpayer you pay £720 in income tax on the BIK; your company pays Class 1A NIC of £270. Compare that to a £35,000 petrol car at 120g/km (30% appropriate percentage): the BIK is £10,500, costing you £4,200 in income tax and your company £1,575 in Class 1A NIC. The EV BIK is dramatically lower. Note that the EV appropriate percentage is set to rise in 2027/28 and beyond; check the latest HMRC rates before signing a long lease.

**FAQ 3: Is it better to buy a car through my dental company or claim mileage personally?**
It depends on three things: the type of car, your annual business mileage, and whether you want simplicity or tax efficiency. For a petrol car with moderate mileage (say 8,000 miles a year), the personal-car-plus-AMAP route is almost always better: you get 55p/mile tax-free (from 6 April 2026), your company gets a corporation tax deduction on those payments, and there is no BIK charge and no Class 1A NIC. For a new electric car, the maths can flip: the 4% BIK is low, and if your company purchases the EV it can claim a 100% first-year allowance (for cars bought before 31 March 2027), saving up to 25% of the car's cost in corporation tax in year one. Run the numbers for your specific car and mileage before deciding.

**FAQ 4: What is the fuel benefit charge and should I let my company pay for my private fuel?**
The fuel benefit is an additional BIK charged when a company provides free private fuel for a company car. For 2026/27 the multiplier is £29,200 (up from £28,200 in 2025/26; confirmed HMRC EIM25580, s.150(1) ITEPA 2003, SI 2025/1254). This is multiplied by the car's appropriate percentage to get the fuel benefit BIK. For a petrol car at 30%: £29,200 x 30% = £8,760 additional BIK, costing a 40% taxpayer an extra £3,504 per year in income tax. To break even, your company would need to pay for a very large amount of private fuel. The practical rule: do not allow your company to pay for private fuel. Always keep private and business fuel separate and reimburse the company for any private element. Electric vehicles are different: electricity is not "fuel" for fuel benefit purposes (HMRC EIM23900), so the fuel benefit charge does not apply to EV charging at all.

**FAQ 5: Can my dental company claim VAT on a car it buys or leases?**
For a purchased car, HMRC blocks input VAT recovery unless the car is used exclusively for business purposes with no possibility of private use. In practice, a dental director's company car is always available for private use, so VAT on the purchase price cannot be reclaimed. For a leased car, the rules are more generous: 50% of the input VAT on lease rental payments is recoverable where there is some private use. This makes leasing slightly more tax-efficient on VAT than buying outright. (Confirm exact statutory basis with your accountant: VAT Notice 700/64 and HMRC VIT manual.) Running costs such as servicing and tyres are not subject to the car-purchase block and VAT is recoverable on those in the normal way, subject to your partial-exemption position as a dental practice.

**FAQ 6: What happens with EV charging and VAT?**
If your company installs a charging point at the surgery or pays for electricity to charge a company EV at the workplace, it can recover the input VAT on that electricity in the normal way (subject to your practice's partial-exemption position for dental work). If you charge the company EV at home and your company reimburses you, the reimbursement does not create a separate taxable benefit provided it relates solely to the company car and is calculated on a business-mileage basis (s.239(2) ITEPA 2003, EIM23900). The reimbursement rate to use is HMRC's advisory electric rate (AER); check the current figure at gov.uk before setting up the arrangement.

**FAQ 7: What is P11D and when does my company file it?**
P11D is the form your dental company submits to HMRC to report benefits in kind provided to directors and employees, including any company car. The P11D must be filed by 6 July following the end of the tax year (so by 6 July 2027 for 2026/27 benefits). Alongside it, the company files a P11D(b) and pays Class 1A NIC at 15% on the total BIK value by 22 July (19 July if paying by cheque). The director does not pay the income tax on the BIK via the P11D directly; instead HMRC usually adjusts the PAYE tax code or the director settles via self-assessment.

**FAQ 8: Does buying a car through the company affect my NHS pension?**
The BIK from a company car is not pensionable pay for NHS Pension Scheme purposes. NHS pension accrual as an officer is based on PAYE salary only, not benefits in kind or dividends (see §2.C). However, a company-car BIK adds to your total taxable income, which could push you closer to (or over) the pension annual allowance taper thresholds: threshold income of £200,000 and adjusted income of £260,000 (see §2.B). If you are already near those thresholds, a large BIK from a petrol car could reduce your tapered annual allowance. An EV at 4% BIK typically adds little to adjusted income. Cross-reference your total income picture with your accountant before taking a company car that might trigger taper.

**FAQ 9: What is the difference between buying and leasing a car through the company?**
The BIK calculation is the same either way: it is based on the car's list price regardless of how the company financed it. The differences are in capital allowances and VAT. If the company buys the car, the purchase goes through the capital allowances pool (or claims 100% FYA if new and electric before March 2027) -- see the WDA page (writing-down-allowance-cars-dentists) for the detailed rates. If the company leases the car, the lease rentals are a revenue deduction, giving a CT saving in each year of the lease, and 50% of the VAT on those rentals is recoverable. For a new EV, buying and claiming 100% FYA often beats leasing on pure tax efficiency in year one; leasing gives a steady annual CT deduction rather than a front-loaded one.

**FAQ 10: When does the 100% first-year allowance on an electric car end?**
The 100% first-year allowance for new zero-emission cars is available for cars purchased before 31 March 2027 (for corporation tax purposes) or 5 April 2027 (income tax), as confirmed in the writing-down-allowance-cars-dentists guide. After those dates, zero-emission cars will be pooled at the relevant WDA rate. If you are considering buying a new EV through your company for the 100% FYA benefit, the car must be new (not used) and the purchase must complete before these dates. See the WDA page for full details.

**FAQ 11: Can my company own a second-hand EV and get the same tax benefits?**
Not quite. The 100% first-year allowance only applies to new zero-emission cars (CAA 2001 s.45AA). A used EV goes into the main capital allowances pool at the standard WDA rate. The BIK rules use the original manufacturer's list price (not the price you paid), so a second-hand EV can still trigger a BIK based on a list price that is higher than what the company paid. This is a notable trap: a used £45,000 EV bought for £30,000 still generates a BIK on the £45,000 list price. The 4% appropriate percentage still applies (zero CO2), so the BIK is still lower than a petrol car, but the capital allowance benefit of buying new is lost.

**FAQ 12: What is the mileage allowance rate from my dental company for 2026/27?**
From 6 April 2026, the HMRC-approved mileage allowance payment (AMAP) rate for cars and vans is 55p per mile for the first 10,000 business miles in the tax year, and 25p per mile for any miles above that (EIM31240; s.230(2) ITEPA 2003). These rates replaced the old 45p/25p rates which applied up to 5 April 2026. If your company pays you at or below these rates for business mileage in your personal car, the payments are entirely tax-free to you and there is no NIC. If your company pays less than 55p, you can claim mileage allowance relief (MAR) for the shortfall. If it pays more than 55p, the excess is taxable.
