# Tax Classification of Dental Hygienists and DCPs

## Statutory anchor

**Primary legislation and guidance:**

- Employment status (no single statute): case-law factors from *Ready Mixed
  Concrete v Minister of Pensions* [1968] 2 QB 497, applied via HMRC
  Employment Status Manual (ESM) ESM0500+ (key factors: control ESM0516+,
  personal service/substitution ESM0530+, financial risk ESM0540+, mutuality
  ESM0543+, integration ESM0545+). CEST guidance at ESM11000+.
- **ITEPA 2003 Part 2** (income from employment, PAYE obligations) vs
  **ITTOIA 2005 Part 2** (trading income, self-employment). The split drives
  everything downstream: tax collection method, NIC class, and expenses regime.
- **Class 4 NIC (self-employed):** SSCBA 1992 s.15. 2026/27 rates verified at
  gov.uk: **6%** on profits £12,570 to £50,270, **2%** above £50,270.
  Small Profits Threshold 2026/27: **£7,105** (below this, Class 2 treated as
  paid but no NIC due; above, Class 2 treated as paid automatically since 6
  April 2024 per the National Insurance Contributions Act 2024).
  Voluntary Class 2: **£3.65 per week** for those below the SPT who wish to
  protect their NI record. (gov.uk verified 2026-07-09.)
- **Class 1 NIC (employed):** employee primary NIC deducted by employer through
  PAYE; employer secondary NIC at 15% above the £5,000 secondary threshold
  (house_positions §5/§13).
- **Trading allowance:** £1,000 per tax year (ITTOIA 2005 Part 6A, ss.783AD
  onwards, inserted by Finance (No. 2) Act 2017; verified at legislation.gov.uk
  2026-07-09: s.783AD defines the £1,000 allowance, s.783AE sets full-relief
  eligibility, s.783AF applies it to trade profits, s.783AI governs partial
  relief). A DCP with gross self-employed receipts below £1,000 in a tax year
  need not declare them; above that threshold, the allowance is an alternative
  to deducting actual expenses (partial relief under s.783AI where claiming
  actual expenses would be more beneficial).
- **GDC dental care professionals register:** established under Dentists Act 1984
  s.36B; professions registrable defined in regulations under s.36A(2). The six
  DCP titles are: **dental hygienist, dental therapist, orthodontic therapist,
  clinical dental technician, dental nurse, dental technician**. (Source: GDC
  registration pages; cross-checked against the Dentists Act 1984 s.36A/36B
  framework. Note: SI 2006/1671 on legislation.gov.uk is a transitional order,
  not the approved-titles instrument; the GDC-published register categories
  confirm the six titles.)
- **VAT exemption for DCPs:** VATA 1994 Sch 9 Group 7 -- the exemption covers
  the supply of medical/dental care "by a person on... the dental care
  professionals register" (Dentists Act 1984 s.36B referenced in the Sch 9
  note). A self-employed hygienist supplying dental hygiene services is
  therefore exempt from VAT; the VAT registration threshold (£90,000) counts
  only taxable turnover, not exempt income. This removes a practical VAT
  concern for most self-employed DCPs. Cross-ref house_positions §6.
- **Direct access (2013):** GDC permitted dental hygienists and therapists to
  treat patients without a dentist's prescription from 2013 onward. DCPs with
  direct-access patients may be structurally more independent than those who
  only treat on prescription, which is a relevant status factor.
- **House position lock:** house_positions §1.C (locked 2026-07-09) confirms
  the facts-based spectrum approach for DCP status. No blanket "hygienists are
  self-employed" position applies. house_positions §8.A locks the 2026/27 NIC
  figures (Class 4 6%/2%, SPT £7,105, voluntary Class 2 £3.65/week).

## Framing differentiator (anti-cannibalisation)

**What this page is NOT:**

1. It is not the associate-status page (`dental-associate-vs-self-employed-tax-
   employment-status`). That page covers dentists operating under the BDA model
   associate agreement, NHS performers list registration, and a predominantly
   self-employed orthodoxy. Most hygienists and therapists have no BDA model
   agreement and are not on the NHS performers list; the analytical starting
   point is different.

2. It is not the HMRC-challenge page (`when-hmrc-may-challenge-associate-self-
   employment`). That page covers the risk of a challenge to an existing
   self-employed associate arrangement. This page covers a hygienist or therapist
   who may genuinely be employed (PAYE) and needs to know which regime applies,
   not how to defend a status that has already been taken.

3. It is not the employer-facing nurse/payroll page (`employing-first-dental-
   nurse-practice-manager-cost-tax`). That page writes for the principal deciding
   whether to hire staff and what the employer payroll cost is. This page writes
   for the DCP themselves deciding whether they are self-employed or employed and
   what they must do about tax. A bidirectional link between this page and that
   page is mandatory (see Cross-links below).

**The genuine gap this page fills:**

A dental hygienist or therapist may work at two or three practices, one on a
PAYE contract (e.g. one day a week at an NHS mixed practice), another on a
self-employed sessional-fee basis, and a third on direct-access private work
they invoice themselves. This mixed-status reality is under-served in the
existing corpus and has no BDA-model comparator.

Additional specific differentiators:

- DCPs are not on the NHS performers list (only dentists are listed performers).
  The NHS payment model for hygienists operates through the principal's contract
  or through private fee collection; this changes the "financial risk" analysis
  versus an associate with UDA targets.
- Hygienist sessional fees (often a fixed day-rate or hourly rate) look more
  like wages than a percentage-of-fee arrangement, which shifts the MOO and
  control analysis.
- The direct-access model (invoicing patients directly) is a fact pattern with
  stronger self-employment indicators than a sessional arrangement under the
  principal's clinical governance framework.

## Employment-status factor analysis (writer brief)

### The five core factors for a DCP arrangement

The writer must walk each factor as it presents in a DCP context (not a generic
employment law overview):

**1. Personal service and substitution (ESM0530)**

The key test is whether the worker has a *genuine* right to send a suitably
qualified substitute, not whether they have actually used it. From ESM0530
(verified 2026-07-09):

- If the engager can veto any substitute without limit, substitution is illusory
  and the factor points to employment.
- If the DCP can genuinely send a GDC-registered substitute of equivalent
  qualification (who would be paid by the DCP, not the practice), this points
  toward self-employment.
- A contract that says "personal service required" without more is not
  conclusive; reality governs.
- Most hygienist sessional contracts in practice do NOT include a genuine
  substitution right; this is a risk factor.

**DCP-specific angle:** A dental hygienist is a regulated professional; only
another GDC-registered dental hygienist or therapist can treat patients. The
pool of eligible substitutes is defined and limited, but that does not make the
right fictional -- a genuine right to arrange a peer substitute, paying them
directly, counts.

**2. Control (ESM0516+)**

Control asks: who decides what the DCP does, when, where and how? Indicators
pointing to employment:

- Practice dictates the clinical materials and equipment used (not the DCP's
  own).
- Practice dictates which patients the DCP sees and in what order.
- Practice requires the DCP to work set days, set hours, at the practice's
  direction.
- Practice's clinical governance framework requires treatment on the principal's
  prescription only (pre-direct-access model).

Indicators pointing to self-employment:

- DCP uses their own equipment or materials (loupes, scalers, products).
- DCP carries their own indemnity (not covered by the practice's policy).
- DCP works direct-access: they choose which patients to take, set their own
  clinical approach and are not working under a dentist's prescription.
- DCP can decline sessions or rearrange scheduling without penalty.

**3. Mutuality of obligation (ESM0543)**

MOO asks: is the engager obliged to offer work, and is the worker obliged to
accept it? From ESM0543 (verified 2026-07-09): MOO establishes that a contract
exists; it does not determine what kind of contract. During an active session,
MOO is always satisfied (the practice pays and the DCP works). What matters for
status is whether there is an *overarching* obligation -- the practice must
offer sessions and the DCP must show up -- or whether each session is a
separate engagement that either side can decline.

**DCP-specific angle:** A sessional arrangement (e.g. "we would like you
Tuesdays and Thursdays, let us know your availability") with no guaranteed
hours and no obligation to offer or accept can point toward self-employment.
A fixed rota where the DCP is on the weekly timetable with no ability to
opt out points toward employment.

**4. Financial risk (ESM0540+)**

Does the DCP bear genuine financial risk? Factors:

- Self-employed indicators: DCP invoices per session or per patient, can make a
  profit or loss depending on efficiency, provides their own professional
  indemnity, has invested in their own equipment, may have overheads.
- Employment indicators: fixed day-rate regardless of patient throughput, no
  exposure to bad debts, practice-supplied equipment, practice-supplied
  indemnity (often dental nurses).

**5. Integration (ESM0545)**

Is the DCP "part and parcel of the organisation"? This factor carries less
weight than control/MOO/substitution in modern case law, but it is relevant:

- A long-standing hygienist with a practice email address, named on the
  website as staff, attending practice team meetings, using a set surgery,
  looks more integrated.
- A sessional hygienist who brings their own kit, sees patients and leaves,
  is less integrated.

## Status factor walk-through: sessional day-rate engagement (worked example C)

**Scenario:** A dental hygienist (GDC-registered) works every Wednesday at a
private practice. The practice pays £350 per day regardless of how many patients
are seen. The practice supplies the dental chair, suction and instruments; the
hygienist uses their own ultrasonic scaler. The practice selects the patients and
arranges the appointment book. There is no written contract. The hygienist also
works two days a week at a second practice on a direct-access private basis
(invoices patients directly).

**Factor-by-factor:**

| Factor | Wednesday practice | Conclusion |
|--------|-------------------|------------|
| Personal service | No substitution right in practice; the practice expects that specific hygienist | Points to employment |
| Control | Practice chooses patients, books appointments, supplies main equipment; hygienist has clinical autonomy within treatments | Mixed: scheduling control = employment; clinical technique = neutral |
| MOO | Fixed day each week, implied expectation to attend; no guaranteed-minimum clause but consistent expectation | Points to employment (weak) |
| Financial risk | Fixed day-rate, no bad-debt risk, practice supplies core equipment; own scaler is minor investment | Points to employment |
| Integration | Named on website, set surgery, weekly presence | Points to employment |

**Verdict for Wednesday practice:** the balance of factors points to
**employment** for this engagement. The DCP should be on PAYE for the £350
per day.

**The direct-access practice (two days):** own patient bookings, own invoices,
own clinical decisions, could decline sessions, financial risk on collection --
this engagement is likely **self-employed**.

**Result:** mixed status is not only possible but correct here. The DCP pays
Class 1 NIC via PAYE on Wednesday earnings and Class 4 NIC on direct-access
profit; they must register for Self Assessment because of the self-employed
income.

## Worked examples (writer must include all three)

### Example A: Mixed hygienist (2 days PAYE + 2 days self-employed), 2026/27

**Facts:**

- 2 days per week employed at Practice A: salary £24,000 per year (PAYE,
  Class 1 NIC deducted at source).
- 2 days per week self-employed at Practice B: gross receipts £28,000, allowable
  expenses £6,000 (GDC retention fee £200, indemnity £400, CPD £600, equipment
  £800, mileage 3,000 miles between practices at 55p (AMAP rate from 6 April 2026) = £1,650, professional
  subscriptions £200, accountancy £500, other £1,650). Net self-employed profit
  = **£22,000**.

**Self Assessment position:**

The DCP must file a Self Assessment return because they have self-employed
income (the PAYE income is also reported to confirm the overall picture).

**Income tax 2026/27:**

Total income: £24,000 (PAYE) + £22,000 (self-employed profit) = £46,000.
Personal allowance: £12,570.
Taxable income: £33,430.
All within basic rate band (£0 to £37,700):
Tax = £33,430 x 20% = **£6,686**.

Tax already collected via PAYE on £24,000 salary:
PAYE income less PA (£24,000 - £12,570) = £11,430 x 20% = **£2,286** (already
deducted).

Remaining tax via Self Assessment on self-employed profit:
£6,686 - £2,286 = **£4,400 via SA**.

**Class 4 NIC 2026/27 on self-employed profit (£22,000):**

Lower Profits Limit: £12,570.
Class 4 band: £22,000 - £12,570 = £9,430 x 6% = **£565.80**.
(No Class 4 at 2% because profit does not exceed £50,270.)

**Class 2 NIC:** Profit £22,000 exceeds the SPT of £7,105. Class 2 is
automatically treated as paid from 6 April 2024; no payment due for 2026/27.

**Total additional tax/NIC via SA:** approximately **£4,966** (£4,400 income tax
+ £565.80 Class 4 NIC, rounded).

**Payments on account:** because the SA liability exceeds £1,000, the DCP will
face payments on account for 2026/27 (50% on 31 January 2027 and 50% on
31 July 2027), plus a balancing payment the following January. Writers must
warn first-time SA filers about this "double bill" in the first year.

### Example B: Low-profit case below the Small Profits Threshold

**Facts:**

- A dental nurse works full-time PAYE at one practice (salary £28,000).
- Separately, she provides training sessions to dental nurses at two other
  practices: total receipts £5,800 per year, expenses (travel, materials) £400,
  net profit £5,400.

**Self Assessment:** Yes, required (self-employed income, even below SPT).

**Income tax on self-employed profit:**

Total income: £28,000 + £5,400 = £33,400. PA £12,570. Taxable: £20,830.
All within basic rate: £20,830 x 20% = £4,166.
PAYE tax already paid on £28,000: (£28,000 - £12,570) x 20% = £3,086.
Extra via SA: £4,166 - £3,086 = **£1,080**.

**Class 4 NIC:** Self-employed profit £5,400 is below the Lower Profits Limit
of £12,570. **No Class 4 NIC due.**

**Class 2 NIC:** Profit £5,400 is below the SPT of £7,105. Class 2 is NOT
automatically treated as paid. She can pay voluntarily at £3.65 per week
(£189.80 per year) to protect her NI record -- relevant if she has gaps in
her contribution record. If her NI record is already complete from PAYE
employment (35 qualifying years for full State Pension), voluntary Class 2 is
unnecessary.

**Trading allowance check:** Gross receipts £5,800 exceed the £1,000 trading
allowance. The full allowance is not available as a simple deduction here; she
can either (a) claim actual expenses of £400, giving profit £5,400, or
(b) elect partial relief under ITTOIA 2005 s.783AI, treating the first £1,000
of receipts as covered by the allowance and calculating profit on the
excess £4,800. Actual expenses (£400) produce a lower profit; actual expenses
win here and she should use them, not the trading allowance.

## FAQ expansion (8-12 with full draft answers)

The writer must draft FAQ schema-ready content for all items below. These are
the brief-level draft answers; the writer will expand to publication standard.

**Q1. How do I know if I am employed or self-employed as a dental hygienist?**

A: There is no single test. HMRC weighs five main factors: whether you must
provide personal service (or can send a suitably qualified substitute); how
much control the practice has over what you do, when, and how you do it;
whether there is a mutual obligation to offer and accept work; whether you bear
genuine financial risk; and how integrated you are into the practice. No single
factor is decisive. A hygienist working fixed days under the practice's full
direction, using the practice's equipment and booked into their appointment
system, is more likely to be employed. One who invoices per patient, provides
some of their own equipment, carries their own indemnity and sees direct-access
patients is more likely to be self-employed. HMRC's CEST tool
(gov.uk/guidance/check-employment-status-for-tax) will take you through these
questions for a specific engagement.

**Q2. I work at two practices. Can I be employed at one and self-employed at the
other?**

A: Yes. Status is decided engagement by engagement, not person by person. If
Practice A controls your hours, books your patients and pays you a set salary,
you are employed there. If Practice B pays you a sessional fee for work you
largely self-manage, you may be self-employed for that engagement. You will
need to file a Self Assessment return to report the self-employed income; the
employed income from Practice A will be collected through PAYE as normal.

**Q3. The practice pays me a fixed day-rate. Does that make me employed?**

A: A fixed day-rate is one factor pointing toward employment (it resembles a
wage and removes financial risk), but it is not conclusive. What matters is the
overall picture. If alongside the day-rate you can send a substitute, you
provide some of your own equipment, you carry your own indemnity and you can
decline sessions without penalty, other factors may outweigh the fixed-rate
indicator. If the day-rate comes with fixed hours, no substitution right,
practice-supplied equipment and a guaranteed slot each week, the overall
picture points toward employment regardless of the label.

**Q4. What NIC do I pay as a self-employed hygienist?**

A: For 2026/27 you pay Class 4 NIC at 6% on profits between £12,570 and
£50,270, and at 2% on profits above £50,270. There is no separate Class 2 bill
to pay: if your profits are above the Small Profits Threshold of £7,105, Class 2
is automatically treated as paid (National Insurance Contributions Act 2024).
If your profits are below £7,105 you can choose to pay voluntary Class 2 at
£3.65 per week to protect your NI record, but you are not required to. Class 4
NIC is calculated on your Self Assessment return alongside your income tax.

**Q5. I am employed (PAYE) as a hygienist. Do I need to file a Self Assessment
return?**

A: Not automatically. If your only income is PAYE employment and your tax is
collected correctly by your employer, you do not need to file Self Assessment.
You do need to file if you also have self-employed income from another practice,
if your total income exceeds £150,000, or if you have other untaxed income (for
example, rental income). If you are unsure, check HMRC's online tool at
gov.uk/check-if-you-need-to-send-a-self-assessment-tax-return.

**Q6. What expenses can I claim as a self-employed hygienist?**

A: You can claim expenses that are wholly and necessarily for the business.
Common allowable items include: your annual GDC retention fee; professional
indemnity insurance; CPD costs directly related to your clinical work; equipment
you buy and use for professional purposes (scalers, loupes); professional
subscriptions; accountancy fees; and mileage between practices (at the HMRC
approved mileage rate: 55p per mile for the first 10,000 business miles per
year from 6 April 2026, then 25p per mile). You cannot claim ordinary commuting from home to a
regular place of work -- but if you travel from one practice to another during
the working day, that inter-practice mileage is claimable. Keep records of all
expenditure and of your business mileage.

**Q7. Does the £1,000 trading allowance apply to me?**

A: The trading allowance (ITTOIA 2005 Part 6A) lets you earn up to £1,000 of
self-employed gross income per tax year without declaring it or paying tax. If
your gross self-employed receipts in the year are £1,000 or less, you do not
need to report them. If your receipts exceed £1,000, you have a choice: claim
the £1,000 allowance (under the partial relief route in s.783AI) and pay tax on
the excess, or claim your actual allowable expenses. If your actual expenses
are more than £1,000, it will usually be better to claim actual expenses. Most
self-employed hygienists with meaningful self-employed income will have actual
expenses (GDC fee, indemnity, CPD) that exceed £1,000, making the allowance
less useful in practice.

**Q8. Does working on a direct-access basis change my employment-status
analysis?**

A: Yes, and usually in your favour if you are trying to establish self-employed
status. Since 2013, GDC regulations permit dental hygienists and dental
therapists to see patients directly without a dentist's written prescription for
most routine hygiene and therapy work. If you book your own direct-access
patients, collect your own fees, invoice them yourself and bear the financial
risk of that work, those facts are strong self-employment indicators (financial
risk, control, financial independence). Compare this to a hygienist who only
works on the principal's prescription, sees patients booked by the practice,
and is paid by the practice regardless of collection: that arrangement has fewer
self-employment markers.

**Q9. What VAT does a self-employed hygienist need to worry about?**

A: Dental hygiene services supplied by a person on the GDC dental care
professionals register are exempt from VAT under VATA 1994 Schedule 9 Group 7.
Because your income is exempt (not zero-rated), it does not count toward the
£90,000 VAT registration threshold. In practice, most self-employed hygienists
do not need to register for VAT at all, even with substantial turnover. An
exception would arise if you supply a significant volume of non-exempt services
(for example, some cosmetic or non-dental work) taking your taxable turnover
above £90,000, but this is uncommon.

**Q10. What happens about Making Tax Digital?**

A: Making Tax Digital for Income Tax (MTD ITSA) will apply to you if your
gross qualifying income exceeds the relevant threshold. The thresholds are:
£50,000 from 6 April 2026, £30,000 from 6 April 2027, £20,000 from 6 April
2028 (house_positions §9). "Qualifying income" includes self-employed income
and rental income. If you are above the threshold for your start date, you must
use MTD-compatible software to maintain digital records and submit quarterly
updates to HMRC instead of a single annual return. If you are already below
the threshold, watch for the lower limits in 2027 and 2028.

**Q11. My practice has told me I am self-employed but I think I might actually
be employed. What should I do?**

A: The contract label does not determine status; the actual working arrangements
do. If you believe you are genuinely employed but are treated as self-employed,
you can use HMRC's CEST tool to assess your position, and you can write to HMRC
to request a determination. If HMRC agrees you are employed, the practice (as
employer) is liable for the PAYE and employer NIC that should have been deducted.
However, this can be complicated in practice, and you may want advice from a
specialist dental accountant before raising it. The risk of mis-classification
sits primarily with the practice, not with you personally, as HMRC pursues the
employer for undeducted PAYE.

**Q12. I am a dental nurse. Does this page apply to me?**

A: Yes, dental nurses are one of the six GDC-registered DCP categories and are
within the scope of this analysis. In practice, dental nurses are more commonly
employed (PAYE) than hygienists or therapists, because their role is more
closely integrated into the practice day-to-day and they are less often in
a position to provide their own equipment or see patients independently. However,
a dental nurse who provides locum or agency cover at multiple practices under
genuinely self-directed arrangements can be self-employed. Run the same five-
factor test as for any other DCP. If you are employed, the employer-side payroll
analysis is covered in the linked page for practice owners (see Cross-links
below).
