### Statutory citations (all WebFetch-verified at write time, Stage 2)

**TCGA 1992 s.165 gift holdover relief** — verified legislation.gov.uk 2026-07-09:
- Section title: "Relief for gifts of business assets."
- Qualifying assets: used for the purposes of a trade, profession or vocation carried on by the transferor or their personal company; OR unlisted shares/securities in a trading company or in the transferor's personal company.
- Joint claim: required by the transferor and the person acquiring the asset (transferor alone where the transferee is a settlement's trustees).
- s.165(3)(ba): no holdover where the transferee is a company (for share disposals).
- Effect: held-over gain reduces the transferee's acquisition cost; CGT is deferred, not extinguished.
- HMRC manual: CG66880 (introduction to s.165 holdover; verified 2026-07-09 — correct reference).
- Claim form: HMRC HS295 "Relief for gifts and similar transactions" + separate "Claim for Hold-over Relief" PDF (verified gov.uk 2026-07-09; no longer requires retention of the original form per October 2024 update).

**IHTA 1984 s.113A PET condition** — verified legislation.gov.uk 2026-07-09:
- Section title: "Transfers within seven years before death of transferor."
- Key condition: for BPR to apply on a failed PET the donee must have owned the original property (or qualifying replacement) throughout the period from the date of the chargeable transfer to the date of the transferor's death, AND the property must still qualify as relevant business property at the date of death.

**FA 2026 Schedule 12 BPR reform** — verified legislation.gov.uk 2026-07-09:
- £2.5m 100% relief allowance (verbatim from the statute: "£2,500,000").
- Commencement: on or after 6 April 2026.
- Above-allowance rate: "reduced by 50%" (i.e. 50% BPR on value above the allowance).
- Indexed from 6 April 2031 (CPI, rounded to nearest £1,000).
- Rolling 7-year basis: allowance is reduced by prior qualifying transfers within the 7 years before the current transfer.

**TCGA 1992 s.62 CGT uplift on death** — verified legislation.gov.uk 2026-07-09:
- Section title: "Death: general provisions."
- Key rule: assets devolving on death are deemed acquired at market value at the date of death (s.62(1)(a)); the deceased is not treated as making a disposal on death (s.62(1)(b)). All pre-death appreciation escapes CGT entirely.

**HMRC manuals** — verified 2026-07-09:
- CG66880: "Reliefs: Gifts and Capital Gains Tax: Relief for Gifts of Business Assets: Introduction" — correct reference for s.165 holdover.
- IHTM25000: "Business relief and businesses: Contents" — comprehensive BPR index including the £2.5m 100% allowance provisions.
- IHTM25xxx (within the same manual): conditions for relevant business property, ownership requirements, s.113A PET clawback.

## Worked-example data (writer uses these verbatim; all figures verified)

### Example A: Gift of £3.2m practice and premises vs the £2.5m BPR allowance

Scenario: Dr Jones owns an unincorporated dental practice. The business assets (including goodwill) are valued at £2.0m and the surgery freehold at £1.2m. Total: £3.2m. She has made no prior qualifying BPR transfers in the last 7 years. She gifts the whole practice to her daughter on 1 May 2026.

IHT position on the gift (assuming it is a PET and Dr Jones survives 7 years):
- No IHT while Dr Jones survives 7 years.
- But if Dr Jones dies within 7 years AND her daughter still owns the qualifying business property (s.113A condition):
  - £2.5m at 100% BPR = nil IHT on the first £2.5m.
  - £0.7m above the allowance: 50% BPR reduces the chargeable value to £0.35m.
  - IHT on £0.35m at 40% = £140,000 (before taper relief if applicable).
- 100%/50% split: the £2.5m allowance should be applied to the 100%-relief category assets first (unquoted business property); if the freehold only qualifies at 50% under s.105(1)(d) as a standalone asset (because it is used in the business but held separately), the writer must flag the asset-composition question and cross-link to the relevant HMRC IHTM guidance. Hedge: the exact apportionment between 100% and 50% categories depends on asset classification; the figures above assume the whole £3.2m qualifies for 100% BPR under s.105(1)(a) as a sole proprietorship interest, which is the typical position for an unincorporated practice owner who also owns the premises AND uses them wholly in the trade (s.105(1)(d) plus the wholly-or-mainly qualification in s.112). Writer: frame this as illustrative and cross-reference to the worked note below.
- Writer note: if Dr Jones makes a second qualifying gift within 7 years (e.g. a second practice), the £2.5m allowance is reduced by the amount used in the first transfer.

### Example B: Holdover vs pay-BADR-now comparison

Scenario: Dr Patel owns 100% of a dental company. He gifts his shares to his son. The market value of the shares is £800,000. His original acquisition cost (or s.162 rolled-in base cost) is £50,000. His chargeable gain before any relief: £750,000.

Option 1 — Holdover (TCGA 1992 s.165):
- Dr Patel's CGT: nil (gain held over).
- Son's acquisition cost: £800,000 minus £750,000 held-over gain = £50,000.
- When the son sells (say, in 10 years at £1.2m): gain = £1,200,000 minus £50,000 = £1,150,000. CGT at the son's rate at that time (unknown; likely 18%+ on the shares if he independently qualifies for BADR; standard rate if not). If standard CGT at 18% (assuming rate unchanged): tax = £207,000.
- Total tax deferral value: paying nothing now vs paying £135,000 now (see Option 2) looks better — but if the son sells at standard rates (not BADR), he may pay more in total.

Option 2 — Pay BADR now at 18%:
- Dr Patel's CGT: £750,000 at 18% = £135,000.
- Son's acquisition cost: market value £800,000 (no holdover; market-value rule under TCGA 1992 s.17).
- When the son sells at £1.2m: gain = £400,000. If BADR applies: 18% = £72,000.
- Total tax: £135,000 + £72,000 = £207,000.
- BUT Dr Patel uses £750,000 of his £1m BADR lifetime limit — only £250,000 remaining.

Planning note the writer must draw out: the comparison is NOT simply "holdover = cheaper." It depends on: (a) the son's likely exit timing and tax rate; (b) whether Dr Patel has other BADR-qualifying disposals he needs the lifetime limit for; (c) whether BADR rates rise further (a real risk if future Budgets increase the rate above 18%). The time value of money also favours holdover for a long-term hold. Frame as a modelling exercise, not a prescription; advise the reader to model both with their accountant.

### Example C: Failed PET and the s.113A scenario

Scenario: Dr Singh gives away his unincorporated practice (value £1.8m, all 100% BPR-qualifying) to his daughter in June 2026. Dr Singh dies in November 2029 (3 years and 5 months after the gift). His daughter sold the practice in February 2029 (before his death).

IHT position:
- The gift is a PET; within 7 years of death so potentially chargeable.
- BPR under s.113A: the daughter no longer holds the qualifying business property (she sold it before Dr Singh's death and has not reinvested in qualifying replacement property). BPR does NOT apply.
- The full £1.8m is a failed PET. It uses the nil-rate band (£325,000 in 2026/27) first; the remainder is chargeable at 40% subject to taper relief.
- Taper relief (years 3-7): the gift was between 3 and 4 years before death, so taper relief reduces the additional IHT charge by 20%. (Taper applies to the IHT charge, not the asset value: on £1.475m above the nil-rate band at 40% = £590,000 baseline IHT charge; 20% taper = £472,000 payable.)
- Key planning point: a gift of a business that the donee then sells is the classic s.113A trap. Advise readers that any plan to gift and immediately monetise destroys the BPR protection on a failed PET.

## FAQ expansion (10 entries, full draft answers)

```yaml
faqs:
  - question: "What is gift holdover relief and how does it apply to a dental practice transfer?"
    answer: >
      Gift holdover relief under TCGA 1992 s.165 lets a dental practice owner transfer business assets
      (or shares in their dental company) to a family member without triggering an immediate capital gains
      tax bill. The gain that would otherwise be chargeable is "held over": the owner pays no CGT on the
      day of the gift, and the recipient takes the asset at the owner's original cost (reduced by the
      held-over gain) rather than the market value. CGT is deferred, not cancelled -- it becomes payable
      when the recipient eventually sells. The relief applies to assets used in the trade (goodwill,
      premises, equipment) and to unlisted shares in a dental company where the transferor holds at
      least 5% of voting rights. Both the giver and the recipient must sign a joint claim using the
      HMRC holdover relief form (available with helpsheet HS295).

  - question: "Is it better to hold over the gain or pay CGT now using BADR at 18%?"
    answer: >
      There is no universal answer: it depends on the recipient's likely exit timing, both parties'
      remaining BADR lifetime limits, and expectations about future CGT rates. Holding over saves cash
      today but passes a lower base cost to the recipient, who will pay CGT on a larger gain when they
      eventually sell (potentially at rates higher than 18%). Paying CGT now at 18% BADR (the rate from
      6 April 2026 on qualifying gains up to the £1m lifetime limit) banks the current rate and gives
      the recipient a full market-value base cost. The right answer is a spreadsheet comparison with an
      accountant. One factor that often tips the balance: if the recipient plans to hold the practice
      for decades, the time value of deferral usually wins; if they plan to sell within a few years, paying
      now at 18% may cost less in total than holdover followed by a sale at a future rate that could be higher.

  - question: "How does IHT business property relief apply to a dental practice?"
    answer: >
      A dental practice run as a sole trader or partnership, and shares in an unquoted dental company,
      normally qualify as relevant business property under IHTA 1984 s.105, provided the owner has
      held the business for at least 2 years (s.106). From 6 April 2026 (FA 2026 Schedule 12), 100%
      BPR is capped by a £2.5m allowance per person: qualifying business value up to £2.5m is fully
      exempt from IHT; value above the allowance gets 50% relief. Before 6 April 2026, 100% relief
      was uncapped. A practice with goodwill, equipment and surgery premises combined worth more than
      £2.5m now faces a 50% IHT charge on the excess -- which at 40% means an effective rate of 20%
      on value above the threshold. The allowance operates on a rolling 7-year basis, so prior
      qualifying transfers within that window reduce the amount available.

  - question: "What is the 7-year PET rule and does it apply to a gift of a dental practice?"
    answer: >
      A lifetime gift of business assets or company shares to an individual is a potentially exempt
      transfer (PET) for IHT purposes. If the giver survives for 7 full years, the gift falls outside
      the IHT net entirely. If the giver dies within 7 years, the gift becomes chargeable. Taper relief
      reduces the IHT charge on the excess above the nil-rate band (currently £325,000) at a sliding
      scale: the additional charge is reduced by 20% in years 3-4, 40% in years 4-5, 60% in years 5-6,
      and 80% in years 6-7. Business property relief (up to £2.5m at 100% from 6 April 2026) can further
      reduce or eliminate the charge, but only if the recipient still holds qualifying business property
      at the date of the giver's death (the s.113A condition). A recipient who has sold the practice
      before the giver dies loses BPR on the failed PET entirely.

  - question: "What happens to CGT if I hold my practice until death rather than gifting it now?"
    answer: >
      Under TCGA 1992 s.62, assets passing on death are not treated as a disposal by the deceased.
      The beneficiary acquires the assets at their market value at the date of death. This wipes out
      all the capital gain that accrued during the owner's lifetime: the beneficiary only pays CGT on
      gains from the date of death onwards. This CGT uplift is lost if a lifetime gift is made with
      holdover, because the recipient takes a lower (held-over) base cost, not the full market value.
      The trade-off: holding until death preserves the CGT uplift and avoids starting the 7-year IHT
      clock, but means no transfer of control during the owner's lifetime. Post-6 April 2026, IHT BPR
      on a death transfer is also capped at £2.5m (100% relief), with 50% relief above -- so a large
      practice may still face some IHT even if the CGT uplift is secured.

  - question: "Does transferring a dental practice to a family member require NHS contract novation?"
    answer: >
      Yes, for an asset transfer (including a gift of the practice as a going concern). The NHS contract
      stays with the original contractor; an asset gift transfers the business assets but not the contract.
      The recipient must apply for a novation of the contract with the NHS commissioner, who has discretion
      over whether to approve it and may use the opportunity to renegotiate contract value (historically
      5-10% reductions have been seen at novation). A share transfer -- giving shares in a company that
      holds the NHS contract -- avoids novation entirely, because the contract stays inside the company.
      This is one of the strongest arguments for incorporating a practice before an internal transfer:
      a share gift sidesteps the novation risk.

  - question: "What is the joint claim requirement for holdover relief?"
    answer: >
      Both the person making the gift and the person receiving it must sign the HMRC holdover relief
      claim form together. The claim is submitted to HMRC using the "Claim for Hold-over Relief" form
      (available via helpsheet HS295 on gov.uk). If the recipient is the trustee of a settlement, the
      giver can claim alone. There is no provision for the recipient to claim without the giver's
      involvement (except for certain trustee disposals). Practically, this means the family transfer
      needs both parties actively engaged in the paperwork before completion.

  - question: "Can I give my dental company shares to my child using holdover relief if they are setting up a new company to receive them?"
    answer: >
      No. TCGA 1992 s.165(3)(ba) blocks holdover relief where shares or securities are transferred
      to a company as the transferee. If the child is receiving the shares personally, holdover is
      available. If the plan is for a new company controlled by the child to receive the shares, holdover
      does not apply to that disposal. This is a common structuring trap in practice succession. The
      personal-to-personal gift route preserves holdover; routing through a company does not.

  - question: "How does the £2.5m BPR allowance work if I have two practices?"
    answer: >
      The £2.5m is a per-person allowance, not a per-asset allowance. It operates on a rolling 7-year
      lookback: the allowance is reduced by any qualifying business/agricultural property transfers made
      in the 7 years before the current transfer. So if you gift practice A (worth £1.5m) in 2026 and
      practice B (worth £1.5m) in 2029, the 2029 gift has only £1m of 100% allowance remaining (£2.5m
      minus the £1.5m used in 2026). The excess £0.5m of practice B's value gets 50% BPR. Sequencing
      large transfers matters: gifts spaced more than 7 years apart each get a fresh £2.5m allowance,
      but in practice most dentists cannot afford to wait that long between transfers.

  - question: "What planning steps should a practice owner take 3 to 5 years before an internal transfer?"
    answer: >
      First, review the corporate structure. If the practice is unincorporated and a share gift is
      preferred (to avoid NHS contract novation), incorporate using s.162 relief now -- incorporation
      needs at least 2 years before transfer for BADR qualifying conditions to be met on the shares.
      Second, if staying unincorporated and planning a gift, start the 7-year PET clock as early as
      possible: a gift made now that is still within the 7-year window at death will attract a partial
      IHT charge; one made 7 years before death is completely exempt. Third, review the BADR lifetime
      limit: if the owner has used some of their £1m limit on prior disposals, the holdover route may
      become more attractive (no limit, but the recipient inherits a low base cost). Fourth, document
      the 2-year BPR ownership condition and ensure the practice assets are not tainted as wholly or
      mainly investment (s.112 excepted assets risk for practices with significant non-trading investment
      portfolios). Finally, get a professional valuation of the practice to anchor both the CGT gain
      and the IHT PET value.
```
