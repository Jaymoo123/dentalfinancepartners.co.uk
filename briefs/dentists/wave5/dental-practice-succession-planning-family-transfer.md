# Succession Planning for Dental Practice Owners: Internal Transfer Routes

## Statutory anchor

### Primary anchors

- **§4.A** — Disposal timing (TCGA 1992 s.28 unconditional/conditional); earn-out mechanics. Relevant to staged internal transfers where price is deferred.
- **§12** — Partnership changes: incoming/outgoing partners; ITTOIA 2005 s.850 profit transparency; SP D12 goodwill part-disposal; FA 2003 Sch 15 SDLT on land; ITA 2007 s.398 qualifying loan interest on buy-in.
- **§5** — Profit extraction (LLP vs company vs partnership); dividend rates 10.75/35.75/39.35% from 6 Apr 2026 (FA 2026 s.4).
- **§5.B** — Finance-cost deductibility for buy-in borrowing.

### New statutory anchors (verified at legislation.gov.uk on 2026-07-09 for this brief)

**TCGA 1992 s.165 gift holdover relief** (verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12/section/165):
- Applies to: (a) assets used in the transferor's trade, profession or vocation (including those used in their "personal company" — a company in which the individual holds 5%+ of voting rights); (b) unquoted shares or securities of a trading company, or shares constituting the transferor's personal company.
- Condition: the disposal must not be at arm's length; a joint claim by transferor and transferee is required (or transferor alone where the transferee is a trustee).
- Does NOT apply where the transferee is a company (for share disposals) — TCGA 1992 s.165(3)(ba).
- Effect: the chargeable gain on the disposal is held over and reduces the transferee's acquisition cost. CGT is deferred, not extinguished; it crystallises when the transferee disposes of the asset.
- Interaction with BADR: holdover and BADR are alternatives on the same disposal. If holdover is claimed, the transferor pays no CGT now but the transferee inherits a lower base cost and will pay CGT (likely at standard rates, not BADR, on their eventual disposal unless they independently qualify). A transferor may elect NOT to hold over in order to use BADR at 18% and bank the lower rate before rates rise further; a new holdover gains time but may exit at a higher rate. This is the core planning tension and must be spelled out.
- s.165 applies to both incorporated (share gift) and unincorporated (business-asset gift) routes, subject to the transferee-is-a-company exclusion.

**IHT Business Property Relief (IHTA 1984 ss.103-114, as amended by Finance Act 2026 Sch 12)** (verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1984/51/section/104 and https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2026/11/schedule/12 on 2026-07-09):
- Pre-6 April 2026: 100% BPR on unquoted business property (including a dental sole trader's business assets and unquoted shares in a dental company) where the 2-year ownership condition (s.106) is met.
- From 6 April 2026 (FA 2026 Sch 12, commencement per para 17): a new **£2.5m 100% relief allowance** (IHTA 1984 s.124D, as inserted by FA 2026 Sch 12) operates on a rolling 7-year lookback basis. Transfers within the £2.5m allowance get 100% BPR; the value ABOVE the allowance gets **50% BPR** (s.104(1) baseline). The allowance is cumulative across chargeable transfers made by the individual in the 7 years before the current transfer; it reduces by amounts already used.
- **CRITICAL NOTE for Stage 2 and HP-lock review:** the task prompt referred to a "£1m 100% relief allowance." Verification at legislation.gov.uk (IHTA 1984 s.124D as inserted by FA 2026 Sch 12) shows the correct figure is **£2.5m**, not £1m. Flag raised — see F-101 below. The brief and any Stage 2 content must use £2.5m.
- BPR on a LIFETIME gift (PET): if an owner gives away practice assets or shares and survives 7 years, no IHT. Within 7 years and the donor dies: taper relief reduces the IHT charge on the PET at years 3-7 (20%/40%/60%/80% reduction in the additional IHT at years 3/4/5/6/7 respectively). BPR applies to reduce the value of the gift if the donee still holds qualifying business property at the date of death (s.113A IHTA 1984).
- The 2-year ownership condition (s.106) must be met by the **transferor** at the time of the transfer; the transferee acquires fresh time unless replacement property rules apply.

**CGT uplift interaction with IHT (base cost uplift on death):** where practice assets or shares pass on death (rather than by lifetime gift), the beneficiary takes the assets at the probate value for CGT purposes (TCGA 1992 s.62 uplift). A lifetime gift with holdover eliminates the uplift for the transferee (they inherit the held-over cost). This is relevant where the owner is considering whether to hold the practice until death (full IHT BPR + CGT uplift if practice qualifies) versus making a lifetime gift (holdover defers CGT, BPR may still apply on the gift if the donee holds qualifying property for 7 years, but the CGT uplift is lost). The brief must flag this as a decision tree, not a prescription.
