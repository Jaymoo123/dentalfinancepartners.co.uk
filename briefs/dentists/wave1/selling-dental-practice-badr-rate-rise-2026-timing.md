## 3. H2 section outline (target body 2,800-3,500 words)

1. **The deadline in one line.** BADR rises from 14% to 18% on 6 April 2026; a £1m qualifying gain costs £40k more CGT if it disposes on/after that date. Set the stakes, then immediately flag that the date that matters is the date of disposal, not the date you shake hands (developed below). [HP §4]
2. **What BADR is, and the rate it now carries.** Reduced CGT on qualifying business disposals, £1m lifetime limit per individual, the full rate schedule with date bands (10% to 5 Apr 2025; 14% 6 Apr 2025 to 5 Apr 2026; 18% from 6 Apr 2026). [HP §4]
3. **Why the disposal date, not the handshake, governs.** TCGA 1992 s.28: for an unconditional contract the disposal date is the date the contract is made (exchange); for a conditional contract it is the date the last condition is satisfied. Explain the practical consequence for a typical practice SPA and why "completion" is a loose word that needs care (see §5 worked nuance). [HP §4 + GAP, see flags]
4. **The two-year qualifying clock.** Conditions must be held throughout the 2-year period ending on disposal: trading business/company; for a share sale 5% ordinary share capital + 5% voting rights + officer/employee + 5% economic entitlement. Tie the clock to the deadline (if you incorporated recently, the clock runs from incorporation, not from when you opened the practice). [HP §4]
5. **What actually qualifies on a dental sale.** Sole trader / partnership going-concern disposal; company share sale; sale of goodwill plus assets as a going concern. Note an asset sale by a company does not give the individual BADR (the company pays CT, then extraction is taxed again). Cross-link asset-vs-share page. [HP §4]
6. **The £40k question, worked.** Side-by-side: £1m gain disposing 5 Apr 2026 (£140k CGT) vs 6 Apr 2026 (£180k CGT). Then a larger gain that breaches the £1m lifetime limit so the excess sits at the 24% main CGT rate regardless of timing. [HP §4]
7. **Where the lifetime limit changes the maths.** £1m is per individual and cumulative across a lifetime. Two equal partners each have their own £1m. Gains above £1m do not get BADR at all, so the rate-rise lever only bites on the first £1m. [HP §4]
8. **What can and cannot be brought forward.** Realistic deal timeline (commonly several months from heads of terms to completion), commissioner consent / NHS contract novation as a gating item, lender timelines, due diligence. Why "complete before 5 April" is often not in the seller's gift. Cross-link NHS contract novation page. [HP §3 novation, §4]
9. **When chasing the old rate is the wrong call.** A rushed sale at a weak multiple, an under-normalised EBITDA, or accepting a structurally poor buyer can cost far more than the 4-point rate difference. Frame: the rate rise is a tie-breaker, not a strategy. Numbers showing a 5% price concession dwarfing the £40k. [HP §4 multiples]
10. **Interaction with incorporation and the 24-month trap.** If you incorporated to access a share sale, you cannot dispose for BADR until 24 months of qualifying conditions are met; rushing into the 5 April window may not even be possible. Cross-link s162 page. [HP §4 s162]
11. **Other timing-sensitive consequences of selling.** Year-of-sale income spike, annual-allowance taper risk if pension input also high that year, NHS pension considerations on retirement. Keep brief and cross-link. [HP §2.B, §4]
12. **A sensible decision framework.** Checklist: confirm you qualify, confirm the 2-year clock is clear, get a realistic completion date from your broker/solicitor, model both rate scenarios, then let commercial terms lead. [HP §4]

## 5. Worked examples (actual numbers, tagged)

**Example 1 · The headline £40k step-up [HP §4; BADR 14% to 5 Apr 2026, 18% from 6 Apr 2026].**
Dr Okafor sells her practice as a sole-trader going concern. Qualifying gain after base cost and costs: £1,000,000 (within the £1m lifetime limit, none previously used).
- Disposal date 5 April 2026: £1,000,000 × 14% = **£140,000** CGT.
- Disposal date 6 April 2026: £1,000,000 × 18% = **£180,000** CGT.
- Difference for one day of disposal timing: **£40,000**.

**Example 2 · Gain above the lifetime limit (the lever only bites on the first £1m) [HP §4; BADR rates as above, main CGT 24% higher-rate].**
Dr Mensah has a £1,400,000 qualifying gain, no prior BADR used.
- Disposal 5 April 2026: first £1m at 14% (£140,000) + £400,000 at 24% (£96,000) = **£236,000**.
- Disposal 6 April 2026: first £1m at 18% (£180,000) + £400,000 at 24% (£96,000) = **£276,000**.
- The rate rise still only moves the first £1m, so the difference is again **£40,000**, not more. The excess £400k is rate-rise-agnostic.

**Example 3 · Why the rate is a tie-breaker, not a strategy [HP §4; multiples 0.9 to 1.2× mixed practice].**
Dr Bianchi could complete on 4 April 2026 at a rushed price, or 10 July 2026 after proper EBITDA normalisation lifts the agreed price by 5% on a £1,500,000 deal (£75,000 more headline consideration).
- The rate rise on the first £1m of gain costs at most £40,000 extra by waiting.
- The 5% better price adds £75,000 of consideration (roughly £75,000 of extra qualifying gain, taxed at 18% = £13,500 CGT, net circa £61,500 in the seller's pocket).
- Waiting for the stronger deal is the better outcome despite the higher rate. Chasing 5 April here would have been a false economy.

### Writer guardrails specific to A1
- State the BADR rate **with its date band every time** it appears (HP §4 practical rule). Never write "BADR is 14%" without the date qualifier.
- Do NOT oversimplify the disposal-date rule to "completion date governs". The precise rule (TCGA 1992 s.28) is: unconditional contract = date of the contract (exchange); conditional contract = date the last condition is satisfied. Many dental SPAs are unconditional at exchange, in which case **exchange**, not completion, is the disposal date. Write this carefully: the planning point is to understand which your contract is, and to use a conditional structure deliberately if the parties want the disposal to fall on a later date. (This corrects the brief's shorthand; see flags.)
- Frame the deadline as a tie-breaker; lead the reader to commercial sense over rate-chasing (HP §4).
- Do NOT state BADR is still 10% (HP §4 explicit prohibition).
