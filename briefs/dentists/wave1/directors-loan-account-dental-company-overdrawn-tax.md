## 3. H2 section outline (target body 2,800-3,500 words)

1. **What the director's loan account is, and why dentists drift into one** — drawings taken ahead of declared profit (variable NHS/private income, lumpy month-ends) book to the DLA; explain the difference between a loan you owe the company (overdrawn) and money the company owes you (in credit).
2. **The close-company point** — a single-owner or small dental company is a "close company"; s455 exists to stop owners extracting profit as an untaxed loan instead of taxed salary or dividend.
3. **The s455 charge: rate and mechanism** — a charge equal to the **dividend upper rate (currently 33.75%, rising to 35.75% from 6 April 2026)** on the loan balance outstanding at the accounting-period end; it is paid as if it were corporation tax for that period.
4. **The 9-month-and-1-day repayment rule** — s455 is only due if the loan is still outstanding 9 months and 1 day after the accounting-period end (the normal CT payment date); repay in time and no charge arises.
5. **s458 relief: getting the s455 back** — when the loan is repaid, released or written off, the s455 is reclaimable (form L2P), but relief is deferred until 9 months and 1 day after the end of the period in which repayment happens, and must be claimed within 4 years; explain the cash-flow drag.
6. **Bed-and-breakfasting: the anti-avoidance that stops you "repaying" round year-end** — the 30-day rule (£5,000) and the intentions/arrangements rule (£15,000); a repayment matched to a redraw is ignored, so dividends declared just to clear the DLA then redrawn do not work.
7. **Benefit-in-kind on a loan over £10,000** — if the overdrawn balance exceeds £10,000 at any point in the tax year and the owner pays no (or below-official-rate) interest, a taxable BIK arises at the **official rate of interest (3.75% from 6 April 2025)**; reported on P11D, with Class 1A employer NIC.
8. **The two charges can stack** — s455 (company-level, reclaimable) AND the BIK income tax + Class 1A (personal/employer-level, not reclaimable) can both bite on the same overdrawn balance; worked through so dentists see they are separate.
9. **Writing off the loan instead of repaying it** — a written-off DLA is taxed on the director as a deemed dividend (and there are NIC points); rarely the tax-efficient answer, explain why.
10. **How a dentist clears or avoids an overdrawn DLA properly** — declare a dividend out of distributable profits (mind the 2026/27 rate rise), vote a bonus (NIC cost), charge official-rate interest to kill the BIK, or fund repayment personally before the 9-month deadline.
11. **Why drawings discipline beats the cleanup** — monthly management accounts and a drawings policy keep the DLA in check; tie to the variable nature of dental income.
12. **Common mistakes** — treating the DLA as free money; clearing it with a dividend the company cannot legally pay (no distributable profits); ignoring the £10,000 BIK trip-wire; assuming the s455 is lost forever; redrawing within 30 days and losing the relief.

## 5. Worked examples (each tagged to HP §N + rate + effective date)

**Example A — s455 on an overdrawn balance (HP §5 extension; rate 33.75%, 2025/26).**
A dental company owner's drawings run £40,000 ahead of declared salary/dividends; the DLA is overdrawn £40,000 at the 31 March 2026 year-end and is not cleared by the deadline. s455 = **33.75% × £40,000 = £13,500**, payable as if CT on 1 January 2027 (9 months and 1 day after 31 March 2026). Note: a loan made in an accounting period ending after 6 April 2026 would attract the **35.75%** rate (FA 2026 s.4, HP §5). Effective dates tagged.

**Example B — s458 reclaim timing and cash-flow drag (HP §5 extension).**
The owner repays the £40,000 in, say, August 2027. The £13,500 s455 is reclaimable under s458, but relief is only given 9 months and 1 day after the end of the period in which repayment falls (so roughly January 2029 for a 31 March 2028 period-end), and must be claimed within 4 years. Show that HMRC held £13,500 for around two years even though the charge is ultimately "temporary".

**Example C — the £10,000 benefit-in-kind, official rate 3.75% (HP §5 extension; official rate from 6 April 2025).**
Overdrawn balance £40,000 across the 2025/26 tax year, no interest charged. BIK ≈ **3.75% × £40,000 = £1,500** of taxable benefit; a 40% owner pays £600 income tax, and the company pays Class 1A NIC at 15% on the £1,500 (£225). This sits ON TOP of the s455 in Example A and is NOT reclaimable. Charging the company official-rate interest removes the BIK. Effective date: official rate 3.75% from 6 April 2025.

**Example D — bed-and-breakfasting fails (HP §5 extension; £5,000/30-day and £15,000 arrangements rules).**
The owner "repays" the £40,000 DLA two weeks before year-end and redraws £38,000 three weeks later. Because repayments and redraws of £5,000+ fall within 30 days (and there is a clear intention to redraw, the £15,000 arrangements limb), the repayment is matched to the new advance and ignored for s455. The s455 still bites on the balance. Tag: 2025/26.
