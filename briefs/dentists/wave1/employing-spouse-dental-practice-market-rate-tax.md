## 3. H2 section outline (target body 2,800-3,500 words)

1. **Why owners look at spouse employment** — the income-splitting logic when one partner pays 40%/45% and the other has unused basic-rate band or personal allowance; frame as a legitimate planning tool, not a scheme.
2. **The two tests that govern everything: wholly-and-exclusively plus market-rate** — ITTOIA 2005 s.34 / CTA 2009 s.54; a salary is deductible only if paid for genuine work actually done and not excessive for that work (BIM37740). State both limbs must hold.
3. **Genuine work: what a spouse can legitimately do in a dental practice** — practice-manager duties, bookkeeping, HR, marketing, compliance admin, reception, decontamination-record oversight; the role must be real and needed.
4. **Market rate: how to evidence it** — benchmark against what an unconnected person would be paid for the same role and hours; document the comparison; the £-per-hour test, not a round number plucked to use the personal allowance.
5. **The PAYE and RTI mechanics** — register as an employer, run the spouse through payroll under RTI, payslips, real-time submissions; the salary must actually be paid into the spouse's own account, not just booked.
6. **Employer NIC and the Employment Allowance trap** — employer NIC at 15% on earnings above the £5,000 secondary threshold (2025/26); the Employment Allowance is generally NOT available to a single-director company whose only other employee is the director, and family-member restrictions; model the NIC cost into the saving.
7. **Salaried spouse vs shareholder spouse: two different routes** — employment (CT-deductible salary, must be market-rate) versus a genuine shareholding paying dividends (not a "payment for work" so not subject to the market-rate wage test, but governed by settlements legislation and the *Arctic Systems* outcome); explain the choice and that they can co-exist.
8. **Worked saving: the income-split arithmetic** — a higher-rate owner moves a market-rate salary to a spouse with spare basic-rate band; quantify the net household saving and where it disappears once NIC and a non-market rate are factored in.
9. **Where HMRC challenges and what it costs** — excessive pay disallowed as to the excess (BIM37740), salary recharacterised, the owner taxed at marginal rate on the disallowed amount, interest and behaviour-based penalties; the classic "£20k for 3 hours of admin" red flag.
10. **The evidence file that survives an enquiry** — written contract, job description, timesheets/hours log, board minute, the market-rate benchmark, RTI history and bank evidence of payment.
11. **How spouse employment fits the wider extraction plan** — it is one lever alongside salary, dividends and employer pension contributions; sequence it inside the annual extraction review, not in isolation.
12. **Common mistakes** — paying the personal allowance with no work behind it; never running payroll; paying into the owner's account; "employing" a spouse who already has a full-time job elsewhere with no capacity; ignoring auto-enrolment duties once the spouse is a genuine employee.

## 5. Worked examples (each tagged to HP §N + rate + effective date)

**Example A — the income-split saving when the rate is genuine (HP §5; rates 2025/26).**
Owner is a higher-rate taxpayer; the practice genuinely needs a part-time practice manager. Spouse has no other income. A market-rate salary of, say, £15,000 for the role:
- Company: salary is CT-deductible. At the small-profits rate **19%** (profits ≤ £50,000, HP §5, FY2025/26) the deduction saves the company £2,850 of CT on that £15,000; at the **25%** main rate it saves £3,750.
- Spouse: £15,000 salary uses the £12,570 personal allowance (HP §5) then 20% basic rate on £2,430 = £486 income tax; employee NIC applies above the primary threshold.
- Versus extracting the same £15,000 as a dividend to the owner at the **upper rate 33.75%** (2025/26, HP §5) before the 2026/27 rise.
- Net household effect: the saving is the gap between the owner's marginal cost of that income and the spouse's near-zero/basic-rate cost, less employer NIC. Show the figure and stress it only holds because the role and rate are real.

**Example B — employer NIC eats into the saving (HP §5; 2025/26 employer NIC 15% above £5,000 secondary threshold).**
Same £15,000 salary: employer NIC at **15%** on earnings above the £5,000 secondary threshold = 15% × £10,000 = £1,500 cost to the company (no Employment Allowance for a single-director company employing only family). Net the £1,500 against the CT saving and the income-split gain to show the true household benefit. Effective date: 2025/26 employer NIC rate and £5,000 threshold.

**Example C — the excessive-pay disallowance (HP §5; BIM37740).**
Owner pays a spouse £25,000 for roughly 3 hours a week of basic admin. HMRC accepts a market rate of, say, £4,000 for that work and **disallows the £21,000 excess** (BIM37740, "disallow the excess"). The company loses CT relief on £21,000; the owner is exposed to additional tax at their marginal rate on the disallowed amount, plus interest and a penalty of up to 30% (careless) of the tax underpaid. Tag: 2025/26, HP §5.
