## 3. H2 section outline (target 2,800 to 3,500 words)

1. **What changed for 2026/27 and why the split needs a fresh look** — dividend ordinary 8.75% to 10.75%, upper 33.75% to 35.75% from 6 Apr 2026 (FA 2026 c.11 s.4, §5); £500 allowance unchanged; CT unchanged. The split logic is the same, the numbers shift.
2. **The two moving parts: a small salary plus dividends on top** — why owner-directors take a low salary (CT-deductible, builds state pension record) then dividends (no NIC, but now taxed at the higher 2026/27 rates) (§5).
3. **The salary level decision, part 1: the thresholds that matter** — personal allowance £12,570; secondary (employer NIC) threshold £5,000; primary (employee NIC) threshold; Lower Earnings Limit £6,708 for state-pension qualifying years (§5, §8). Define each.
4. **The salary level decision, part 2: the employment allowance trap for a sole director** — a one-director company with no other employee above the secondary threshold CANNOT claim the Employment Allowance. This is decisive: it pushes many sole-director dentists toward a lower salary (~£6,708) rather than £12,570, because the employer NIC on the slice above £5,000 is not relieved (§5). Verified at gov.uk.
5. **So which salary: £6,708 vs £12,570 for the sole-director dentist** — £6,708 secures the qualifying year and minimises employer NIC; £12,570 uses the full personal allowance but triggers employer NIC at 15% on £7,570 (≈£1,136) which the sole director cannot offset with Employment Allowance. Show the trade-off; the CT relief on the extra salary partly offsets the employer NIC. Where there is a second employee (e.g. a genuinely employed spouse, §5), Employment Allowance may be claimable and £12,570 wins.
6. **Dividends on top at the new 2026/27 rates** — £500 dividend allowance free; then 10.75% within the basic band (to £50,270 total income), 35.75% in the higher band (to £125,140), 39.35% above (§5). Date every rate as 2026/27.
7. **The £500 dividend allowance and where it bites** — it is an allowance not a nil-band-on-top; dividends in it still use up basic-rate band. Clarify the common misconception.
8. **Worked optimal extraction for a typical owner, £100,000 profit (2026/27)** — Example 1. Full salary-plus-dividend build, CT, dividend tax by band, net in hand.
9. **Worked optimal extraction at £150,000 profit (2026/27)** — Example 2. Shows the upper-rate 35.75% dividend cost and the retain-vs-extract decision.
10. **2025/26 vs 2026/27: what the rate rise costs on the same extraction** — Example 3. Hold the split constant, show the extra dividend tax in 2026/27.
11. **The factors that should override pure tax efficiency** — NHS pension: salary is pensionable for the officer route, dividends are not, so a rock-bottom salary maximises tax efficiency but can shrink pensionable pay (§2.C); the annual allowance ceiling for high earners (§2.B); cash-flow and mortgage-affordability needs; retained earnings for reinvestment. Cross-link B3.
12. **A practical extraction checklist for 2026/27** — set salary by Employment Allowance status, declare dividends only from post-CT distributable reserves with paperwork, watch the £50,270 and £125,140 thresholds, review mid-year, weigh pensionable pay before cutting salary to the floor. **Example 1 — £100,000 company profit, sole director, 2026/27 (§5, §8).**
- *Salary £12,570* (illustrate this choice, then note the £6,708 alternative below). Employer NIC at 15% on (£12,570 − £5,000) = £7,570 → ≈ £1,136. Salary + employer NIC = £13,706, deductible for CT.
- *Profit after salary cost:* £100,000 − £13,706 = £86,294. CT: marginal relief applies (between £50k and £250k). Effective CT ≈ 22.75% on this band → CT ≈ £19,600 (writer to compute precisely with the 3/200 fraction). Distributable ≈ £66,700.
- *Dividends:* £500 allowance free; the owner already used the £12,570 PA via salary, so dividends from £12,570 up to £50,270 (≈£37,200 after the allowance slice) at 10.75%; the remainder into the higher band at 35.75%.
- *Alternative salary £6,708:* employer NIC ≈ £256 only; more profit stays in the company to suffer CT but less employer NIC; the owner has more unused personal allowance to set against dividends. Show the net comparison; for a sole director without Employment Allowance the two outcomes are close, with £6,708 usually marginally ahead on total tax but £12,570 ahead on pensionable salary (§2.C).
