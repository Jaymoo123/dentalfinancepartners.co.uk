## 3. H2 section outline (target 2,800-3,500 words)

1. **Why the split is a tax decision, not just an accounting one.** One agreed total price, two tax worlds: goodwill (capital, CGT/BADR for the seller) and plant/fixtures (capital allowances). State up front this page is about the tax consequence of the line, and point to the valuation companion for the percentage. [HP §4, §7]
2. **The two buckets defined for tax.** Goodwill and goodwill-like intangibles vs tangible plant, machinery, fixtures and integral features. What counts as a fixture in a surgery (chairs, X-ray, compressors, autoclaves) vs integral features (electrical, water, heating, ventilation). [HP §7]
3. **Seller side, the goodwill slice: CGT and BADR.** Goodwill gain is a capital gain; with BADR, 14% to 5 Apr 2026, 18% from 6 Apr 2026, £1m lifetime limit. Self-built goodwill often has a near-zero base cost, so most of the goodwill slice is gain. [HP §4]
4. **Seller side, the equipment slice: balancing charges and balancing allowances.** This is the part the valuation page does not cover. If the seller claimed capital allowances (AIA/WDAs) on equipment, selling it for more than its tax written-down value triggers a **balancing charge** (clawback of past relief, taxed as income in the trade), and selling for less gives a **balancing allowance**. So a high equipment allocation can create an income-tax charge for the seller, not just a capital gain. [HP §7]
5. **The CAA 2001 s.198 fixtures election: the mechanism that fixes the equipment number.** Joint buyer/seller election fixing the value attributed to fixtures, made by notice to HMRC within 2 years (CAA 2001 s.201). It can be set anywhere from £1 up to original cost (subject to disposal-value caps). Why missing it forfeits the buyer's fixtures allowances entirely. This is the single most preventable loss in a dental purchase. [HP §7]
6. **The buyer side: AIA on equipment vs 6.5% on goodwill.** Tangibles can attract 100% AIA (£1m limit) in year one; goodwill acquired on/after 1 April 2019 gets fixed-rate relief at 6.5% per year, and only where the qualifying-IP condition and 6× cap are met (not automatic). So the buyer usually prefers more allocated to plant. [HP §4, §7]
7. **Where the interests collide (and where they align).** A grid: seller wants more goodwill if equipment allocation would trigger a balancing charge; buyer wants more equipment for fast AIA; but the s.198 election forces a single agreed fixtures figure that binds both. Show that the election value chosen has equal and opposite effects (high figure: seller balancing charge up, buyer allowances up; low figure: the reverse). [HP §4, §7]
8. **Integral features and the 6% pool.** Why some of the "equipment" is actually special-rate (6% WDA) not main-rate (18%), and that AIA is best directed at the special-rate items first. Relevance to a surgery fit-out being sold on. [HP §7]
9. **SBA and the building fabric.** Structures and Buildings Allowance at 3% may cover qualifying construction/premises spend (post 29 Oct 2018), distinct from plant; it does not cover the land or the pooled plant. Keep brief. [HP §7]
10. **HMRC and artificial allocations.** The split must be commercially justifiable and supported by a professional valuation of the tangibles; HMRC can reallocate a tax-motivated split, with tax, interest and penalties for both parties. The s.198 figure must be consistent with the SPA allocation. [HP §4, §7]
11. **Asset sale vs share sale changes the whole question.** On a share sale the company keeps its assets at their existing tax values, so there is no apportionment of the price between goodwill and plant for the buyer in the same way (and no s.198 election); the apportionment analysis is an asset-sale issue. Cross-link. [HP §4]
12. **A practical sequence to get the split right.** Get a tangibles valuation, model the seller's balancing-charge exposure, model the buyer's AIA vs 6.5% position, agree the SPA allocation and the matching s.198 figure together, diarise the 2-year election deadline. [HP §4, §7]

## 5. Worked examples (actual numbers, tagged)

**Example 1 · The same total, two different splits, two different tax outcomes [HP §4 BADR 14% to 5 Apr 2026; HP §7 AIA £1m].**
Practice sold (asset sale) for £800,000, seller a sole trader, disposal in 2025/26.
- Split A, 80% goodwill / 20% equipment: goodwill £640,000, equipment £160,000.
- Split B, 65% goodwill / 35% equipment: goodwill £520,000, equipment £280,000.
- Buyer in year one: Split A gives £160,000 AIA + 6.5% on £640,000 goodwill (£41,600) = £201,600 of first-year relief; Split B gives £280,000 AIA + 6.5% on £520,000 (£33,800) = £313,800. The buyer is roughly £112k of in-year deductions better off under Split B. Their preference is clear.

**Example 2 · The seller's balancing-charge sting on a high equipment allocation [HP §7 balancing charge; HP §4].**
Same practice. The seller had claimed AIA on equipment that now has a **tax written-down value of £20,000**.
- Under Split B (equipment £280,000), the disposal value of the plant is capped at original cost; assume original cost was £120,000. The £120,000 disposal value less £20,000 written-down value gives a **£100,000 balancing charge**, taxed as trading income (potentially at 40% higher rate = £40,000 income tax), not at the 14% BADR rate.
- Under Split A (equipment £160,000, but disposal value still capped at the £120,000 original cost for allowances), the balancing-charge mechanics on the pooled plant are similar, but more of the consideration sits in goodwill (capital, BADR at 14%). The seller generally prefers the goodwill-heavy split to keep the slice in the 14% world rather than the income-tax world.
- Lesson: the buyer's preferred split and the seller's preferred split pull in opposite directions, and the s.198 fixtures figure is where that tension is resolved.

**Example 3 · The forgotten s.198 election [HP §7 s.198 / s.201 2-year limit].**
Dr Reyes buys a practice with fixtures (chairs, compressor, X-ray) the seller had pooled. The SPA allocates £90,000 to those fixtures, but no s.198 election is signed and the 2-year window (CAA 2001 s.201) lapses.
- Result: Dr Reyes **cannot claim capital allowances on those fixtures at all**. At, say, an effective relief value of around 25 to 26% over time, that is roughly £22,000 to £23,000 of relief lost permanently, for the sake of a one-page joint election. Diarise the deadline.
