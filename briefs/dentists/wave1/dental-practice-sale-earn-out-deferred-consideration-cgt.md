## 3. H2 section outline (target 2,800-3,500 words)

1. **Why a dental sale often is not paid all at once.** Corporate acquirers and some independent buyers structure the price with retentions, deferred slices and performance-linked earn-outs (often tied to retained UDA delivery, patient retention or EBITDA). Set up that the timing and certainty of the money changes the CGT treatment. [HP §4]
2. **Three shapes of consideration.** Cash on completion; deferred consideration that is **ascertainable** (fixed amount, paid later); and **unascertainable** contingent consideration (an earn-out whose amount is not yet calculable). The tax differs by shape. [HP §4]
3. **Ascertainable deferred consideration: taxed up front.** If the amount is fixed at the date of disposal (even if paid in future instalments), the full amount is brought into the CGT computation at disposal; you may discount only for a genuine risk it will not be paid (not merely for deferral). [HP §4]
4. **Unascertainable consideration and the Marren v Ingles principle.** The right to receive future unascertainable consideration is itself a separate chargeable asset (a "chose in action"), valued at disposal and taxed then. When the future sums arrive they are capital sums derived from that asset (TCGA 1992 s.22), giving a second disposal. Two chargeable events from one earn-out. [HP §4 + GAP]
5. **The second disposal: gain or loss when the earn-out pays out.** If the cash received exceeds the value placed on the right at sale, a further gain; if less, a loss (and the loss-carry-back election under TCGA 1992 s.279A may help). Walk the mechanics plainly. [HP §4 + GAP]
6. **BADR on the earn-out element: the trap.** BADR is straightforward on the completion cash and on ascertainable deferred consideration (qualifying conditions met to the date of the original disposal). But the **second disposal** (the chose in action maturing) is a disposal of a right, not of the business, so BADR usually does **not** apply to the gain on that second event unless specific conditions are engineered. This is the central planning point. Note the rate context: BADR 14% to 5 Apr 2026, 18% from 6 Apr 2026. [HP §4 + GAP]
7. **Loan notes as an alternative to a cash earn-out.** Where consideration is fixed, taking QCBs or non-QCB loan notes can defer or shape the gain; the BADR position on later redemption needs care (an election may be needed to preserve relief). Keep this as an "and there are structures" section, not a how-to. [HP §4]
8. **Instalment relief: paying the CGT over time.** TCGA 1992 s.280 lets HMRC allow CGT to be paid by instalments where consideration is payable by instalments over a period exceeding 18 months, spread over up to 8 years. Useful where the seller is taxed up front on money not yet received. [HP §4 + GAP]
9. **Valuing the right at the date of sale.** How a reasonable market value is put on an unascertainable earn-out (expected value, probability of hurdles, discounting); why a defensible valuation matters because it sets the base cost for the second disposal. [HP §4]
10. **Dental-specific earn-out hurdles and their risks.** Earn-outs tied to retained UDA delivery, NHS contract novation succeeding, or private patient retention. Why a clawback/retention against a failed novation interacts with the contingent-consideration analysis. Cross-link novation page. [HP §3, §4]
11. **Employment-linked earn-outs: the income-tax danger.** If the earn-out is conditioned on the seller staying on and working (and looks like disguised remuneration), HMRC may treat part as employment income (taxed as earnings + NIC), not capital. How to keep it on the capital side. [HP §4]
12. **A seller's checklist before signing an earn-out.** Confirm whether each slice is ascertainable or not, get the right valued, model both disposals, check BADR availability on each event, consider s.280 instalments, and document everything. [HP §4]

## 5. Worked examples (actual numbers, tagged)

**Example 1 · Ascertainable deferred consideration, taxed up front [HP §4; BADR 14% to 5 Apr 2026].**
Dr Haddad sells for £900,000: £600,000 on completion and a **fixed** £300,000 payable in 12 months. Base cost negligible, disposal 2025/26, within the £1m BADR limit.
- The full £900,000 is ascertainable at disposal, so the whole gain (circa £900,000) enters the CGT computation now: £900,000 × 14% = **£126,000** CGT, due on the normal date even though £300,000 arrives a year later.
- Because the £300,000 is payable more than 18 months out? No: here it is 12 months, so s.280 instalment relief is not available. If the deferral exceeded 18 months, s.280 could spread the CGT (see Example 3).

**Example 2 · Unascertainable earn-out, the two disposals and the BADR trap [HP §4; Marren v Ingles; BADR 14% to 5 Apr 2026, 18% from 6 Apr 2026].**
Dr Iqbal sells for £700,000 cash plus an earn-out of "20% of retained private patient revenue over two years", uncapped and uncertain.
- Disposal 1 (at sale, 2025/26): cash £700,000 plus the **valued right** to the earn-out, say independently valued at £150,000. Total proceeds £850,000. Assuming BADR conditions met and within the £1m limit, gain (circa £850,000) at 14% = **£119,000**. The base cost of the chose in action becomes £150,000.
- Disposal 2 (when the earn-out pays, say 2027/28): the right matures and actually pays **£230,000**. Capital sum derived from the asset (TCGA 1992 s.22): proceeds £230,000 less base cost £150,000 = **£80,000 gain**. This is a disposal of the right, not of the business, so **BADR generally does not apply**; at the 2027/28 main CGT rate (24% on current rules) that is **£19,200**, versus £11,200 if BADR had applied at 18%. The £8,000 difference is the cost of the earn-out structure on the contingent slice.

**Example 3 · Instalment relief on long-dated deferred consideration [HP §4; TCGA 1992 s.280].**
Dr Novak sells for £1,000,000: £400,000 on completion and £600,000 in three equal annual instalments of £200,000 starting at month 24 (so the instalment period exceeds 18 months).
- The full £1,000,000 is ascertainable, taxed at disposal: £1,000,000 × 14% (2025/26, within limit) = **£140,000** CGT.
- Because consideration is payable by instalments over a period exceeding 18 months, the seller may ask HMRC under **s.280** to pay that CGT by instalments over up to 8 years, easing the cash-flow mismatch of being taxed on money not yet in hand.
