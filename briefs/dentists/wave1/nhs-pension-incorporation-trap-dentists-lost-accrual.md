## 3. H2 section outline (target 2,800 to 3,500 words)

1. **The trap in one sentence** — incorporate, pay yourself a small salary plus dividends, and only the salary is pensionable for the officer route; dividends are not. The tax saving is visible; the pension loss is invisible until retirement (§2.C).
2. **How NHS pensionable pay works before you incorporate: practitioner vs officer** — for practitioner dentists (sole trader or partnership) pensionable earnings derive from net NHS-derived income under scheme rules, year-end certificate, tiered contribution (§2.C). For an incorporated principal taking a PAYE salary, the dentist is treated as an officer and only the PAYE salary is pensionable (§2.C). Define both terms plainly.
3. **The 2015 CARE scheme: what you are actually accruing** — everyone active accrues in the 2015 section from 1 Apr 2022; 1/54th of each year's pensionable earnings, active revaluation CPI + 1.5% (§2). So pensionable pay is not a side issue: it is the input to a lifetime guaranteed income.
4. **Why dividends fall outside pensionable pay** — pension input is built on earnings, and dividends are investment returns on shares, not earnings; only the salary runs through PAYE and into the officer pensionable figure (§2.C). State plainly: dividends are not pensionable.
5. **An important nuance for NHS-contract-holding principals** — where an incorporated principal holds the NHS GDS/PDS contract, NHSBSA pensions the principal's NHS-derived profit up to the contract's ceiling figure through the practitioner mechanism, so the picture is not "salary only" for every incorporated dentist. The clean "dividends are not pensionable" trap bites hardest for (a) incorporated associates / non-contract-holders on the officer route, and (b) the portion of profit above the NHS pensionable ceiling. Writer: present the trap as the default risk while flagging this contract-holder exception, and tell readers to confirm their own pensionable-pay treatment with NHSBSA. [See house-positions GAP flag in section 6.]
6. **Worked example: pensionable pay before vs after incorporation** — Example 1. The £150k practitioner who becomes a £30k-salary, £100k-dividend director and cuts pensionable pay (the §2.C illustration), with the contract-ceiling caveat noted.
7. **Worked example: one year of lost CARE accrual** — Example 2. Convert the drop in pensionable pay into lost 1/54th accrual for a single year, then into a lifetime annual-pension figure.
8. **Worked example: the 10 to 15 year run to retirement** — Example 3. Compound the annual lost accrual over a realistic pre-retirement window, with CPI + 1.5% active revaluation, to a total lost guaranteed annual pension and an indicative capital-equivalent. This is the number that must sit beside the incorporation tax saving.
9. **Setting the pension loss against the tax saving** — never weigh the incorporation tax saving alone (§2.C, §5). Show the two side by side: the post-FA-2026 tax saving (now narrower, §5) vs the lost CARE accrual. Cross-link B1.
10. **Can you rebuild it? Salary, Added Pension, MPAVCs and private pensions** — raising the PAYE salary restores some pensionable pay (but adds NIC and reduces the dividend efficiency, §5); Added Pension buys extra DB accrual in-scheme; Money Purchase AVCs and private pensions are DC and do not replicate the guaranteed CARE benefit; all are constrained by the £60,000 annual allowance and taper (§2.B). Honest verdict: a private pot rarely matches the NHS DB value pound for pound.
11. **The annual allowance angle for high earners** — high-earning principals with large historic service can breach the £60,000 annual allowance on pension growth alone; tapering applies where threshold income > £200,000 and adjusted income > £260,000, down to a £10,000 floor (§2.B). Incorporation changes the income mix and can interact with this; flag it, do not over-claim.
12. **The decision framework** — model pensionable-pay treatment first, then the tax saving, then the lost accrual; confirm your own position with NHSBSA because it turns on whether you hold the contract and on the ceiling figure. Incorporate for the right reasons (liability, reinvestment, sale planning), not for a tax saving that a pension loss may swallow. (No CTA in body; LeadForm auto-injects.)

## 5. Worked examples (actual numbers, tagged to §, dated)

> Writer note: figures illustrative; tell readers to confirm pensionable-pay treatment with NHSBSA. Tag the 2015 CARE mechanics to §2 and the annual allowance to §2.B. Dividend/tax figures dated 2026/27 where used.

**Example 1 — pensionable pay before vs after incorporation (§2.C).**
- Before: a practitioner principal with £150,000 of NHS-derived pensionable profit (sole trader). Pensionable pay ≈ £150,000 (subject to scheme permitted-expenses rules).
- After (officer route, non-contract-holder framing): switches to £30,000 PAYE salary + £100,000 dividend. Pensionable pay falls to £30,000. Drop in pensionable pay ≈ £120,000 for that year.
- Caveat to state: a principal who HOLDS the NHS contract may pension NHS-derived profit up to the contract ceiling via the practitioner mechanism, so confirm the actual figure. Tag: §2.C; 2015 scheme §2.

**Example 2 — one year of lost CARE accrual (§2, §2.C).**
- Lost pensionable pay £120,000 × 1/54th = ≈ £2,222 of lost pension earned in that single year (in today's terms, before active revaluation).
- That £2,222 is a guaranteed annual pension for life from normal pension age, revalued each year by CPI + 1.5% while active (§2). Even one year is material. Tag: 1/54th + CPI + 1.5% (§2).

**Example 3 — the 10 to 15 year run to retirement (§2, §2.C).**
- If the reduced pensionable pay persists, the lost accrual compounds: ≈ £2,222 of lost annual pension per year × 12 years ≈ £26,000+ of lost guaranteed annual pension (illustrative; writer to layer in CPI + 1.5% active revaluation on each tranche, which increases the figure).
- Capital-equivalent framing: a guaranteed, inflation-linked annual pension of that size would cost a very large sum to buy as an annuity in the open market, which is why the NHS DB benefit is hard to replace with a DC pot. Set this total beside the post-FA-2026 incorporation tax saving from B1 (now narrower at 2026/27 dividend rates, §5). Tag: §2, §2.C, §5.

**Example 4 (optional) — annual allowance interaction for a high earner (§2.B).**
- A principal with large historic service whose pension growth already approaches the £60,000 annual allowance: incorporation changes the income mix and can move them across the £200,000 threshold income / £260,000 adjusted income taper points (§2.B). Flag the interaction; recommend a Scheme Pays / specialist review rather than asserting a single outcome. Tag: §2.B figures (2025/26), £10,000 floor.

**BRIEF_DRIFT / house-positions nuance flagged (do NOT re-frame house positions; manager to reconcile at HP-lock gate):**
- §2.C states flatly that for an incorporated principal "only the PAYE salary is pensionable, and dividends are not pensionable." The NHSBSA self-service FAQ KA-02080 (verified 2026-06-03) presents a **more nuanced position for NHS-contract-holding principals**: a limited-company dental practice receiving NHS income may pension NHS-derived income **up to the contract ceiling figure** through the practitioner mechanism, without separately allocating salary vs dividends. So the blanket "salary only / dividends never pensionable" rule is precisely right for the **officer route (incorporated associate / non-contract-holder)** and for **profit above the NHS pensionable ceiling**, but is an over-simplification for an incorporated **principal who holds the contract**. Recommend a §2.C refinement distinguishing (i) incorporated associate/officer (salary-only pensionable) from (ii) incorporated contract-holding principal (NHS-derived profit pensionable up to the ceiling). B3 has been written to honour §2.C's framing as the default trap while surfacing this exception and directing readers to NHSBSA. This is a content-accuracy nuance, not a contradiction of the tax facts.
