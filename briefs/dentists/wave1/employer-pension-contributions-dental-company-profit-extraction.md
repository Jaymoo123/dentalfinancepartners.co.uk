## 3. H2 section outline (target body 2,800-3,500 words)

1. **Why employer pension contributions beat salary and dividend for the right slice of profit** — set the scene against the parent guide; the company pays the contribution gross into the director's private pension, no PAYE, no NIC, with a CT deduction.
2. **The corporation-tax deduction: wholly-and-exclusively** — FA 2004 s.196; the contribution is deducted in computing trade profits at the **CT rate (19%/25%, marginal relief between, HP §5)**; relief is given in the period the contribution is PAID (no accruals), so timing matters at year-end.
3. **No NIC on employer contributions** — unlike salary, an employer pension contribution attracts neither employer (15%) nor employee NIC; quantify the NIC saving versus paying the same value as bonus.
4. **No income tax in the director's hands on the way in** — the money lands in the pension untaxed (vs a dividend, which is post-CT and then dividend-taxed); the trade-off is access at retirement under pension rules.
5. **The £60,000 annual allowance** — HP §2.B; the headline cap on tax-relieved pension input each year; for a private DC pension this is broadly the contribution paid.
6. **Tapering for high earners** — HP §2.B; taper where **threshold income > £200,000 AND adjusted income > £260,000**, reducing £1 for every £2 over £260,000 down to a **£10,000** floor; flag that employer contributions count towards adjusted income.
7. **Carry-forward of unused allowance** — use the current year first, then carry forward unused allowance from the **previous 3 tax years**; lets an owner make a large one-off employer contribution (e.g. after a strong year) if scheme membership covered those years.
8. **The private-pension route vs the NHS scheme** — the critical dental distinction (HP §2.C cross-link): the NHS Pension Scheme is a separate DB arrangement; this route uses a **private/personal DC pension (SIPP or workplace scheme)** funded by the company; an incorporated principal whose dividends are not pensionable (§2.C) can rebuild retirement provision THIS way.
9. **The NHS annual-allowance interaction** — HP §2.B; NHS pension growth (pension input amount) plus private employer contributions are aggregated against the SAME £60,000 allowance; a high-service NHS dentist may have little headroom; model both before contributing, and remember Scheme Pays only settles the NHS-side charge.
10. **The "excessive remuneration" point for owner-directors** — BIM46035; HMRC can challenge an employer contribution for a controlling director/close relative if the total package is excessive for the work done; keep the contribution within a commercially justifiable remuneration package.
11. **Practical timing and mechanics** — pay before the company year-end to get relief that year; one-off vs regular; the £40,000+ spreading rule for very large contributions; ensure the scheme is registered and the company is named contributor.
12. **Where this sits in the annual extraction plan** — sequence pension contributions alongside a small salary and dividends; review every year as profit, allowance headroom and the 2026/27 dividend rise shift the maths.

## 5. Worked examples (each tagged to HP §N + rate + effective date)

**Example A — the no-NIC, CT-relieved extraction (HP §5; CT 19%/25%, 2025/26).**
The company contributes **£40,000** to the owner's SIPP. CT deduction at the **19%** small-profits rate (profits ≤ £50,000, HP §5) saves the company £7,600 of CT; at the **25%** main rate it saves £10,000. No employer NIC (saving 15% vs the same value paid as bonus, HP §5 NIC), no income tax to the owner on the way in. Contrast with extracting £40,000 as a dividend at the **upper rate 33.75%** (2025/26, HP §5). Effective date 2025/26.

**Example B — within the £60,000 allowance and carry-forward headroom (HP §2.B).**
Owner has made no pension contributions for 3 years while building the company. Current-year allowance **£60,000** plus carry-forward of unused allowance from the **previous 3 years** (subject to scheme membership in those years) allows a large one-off employer contribution after a strong year. Show the headroom build and that the CT relief lands in the period of payment (FA 2004 s.196). Tag: 2025/26 allowance.

**Example C — the taper bites for a high-earning principal (HP §2.B).**
Owner-director with adjusted income of **£300,000**. Adjusted income exceeds £260,000 by £40,000; allowance reduces by £1 for every £2 = **£20,000 reduction**, giving a tapered allowance of **£40,000** (2025/26). A planned £50,000 employer contribution would create a £10,000 excess taxed at the marginal rate. Employer contributions themselves count towards adjusted income, so model before paying. Effective date 2025/26.

**Example D — the NHS-plus-private aggregation trap (HP §2.B + §2.C).**
A principal still accruing in the NHS 2015 scheme (1/54th CARE, HP §2.C) has a pension input amount of, say, £45,000 from NHS growth. With a standard £60,000 allowance, only ~£15,000 of headroom remains for a private employer contribution before an annual-allowance charge. The dentist who incorporated and lost dividend pensionability (§2.C) cannot simply pour £40,000 into a SIPP without checking NHS growth first. Tag: 2025/26 allowance; NHS accrual 1/54th.
