## 3. H2 section outline (target body 2,800-3,500 words)

1. **What Scheme Pays actually is** — the option to make the NHS Pension Scheme settle your annual-allowance charge to HMRC in exchange for a permanent reduction in your eventual benefits, rather than finding the cash personally (§2.B). Make clear this is a cash-flow tool, not a way to escape the charge.
2. **When a dentist needs it: the charge, not the allowance** — recap in two lines that the charge arises when your pension input amount (DB growth, not contributions) plus any private contributions exceeds the £60,000 annual allowance (or your tapered allowance), taxed at your marginal rate; link out to the AA page and the taper/SIPP pages rather than re-teaching them (§2.B).
3. **Mandatory Scheme Pays: the statutory right** — the scheme MUST pay if the charge attributable to that scheme exceeds £2,000 AND the pension input amount for that scheme exceeded the standard £60,000 annual allowance (Finance Act 2004 s.237B). State plainly that mandatory Scheme Pays is tested against the STANDARD allowance, so a charge caused only by the taper falling below £60,000 is not automatically covered by the mandatory route (cross-link C3).
4. **Voluntary Scheme Pays: filling the gap** — where the charge is £2,000 or less, or arises because of the taper (input below £60,000), the NHS scheme offers a voluntary facility so the member can still settle from the scheme; the difference is who carries responsibility for paying HMRC on time and for any interest.
5. **The election deadline: 31 July** — the mandatory notice must reach NHS Pensions by 31 July in the year following the tax year the charge arose (Finance Act 2004 s.237BA). Explain the extended deadline where the scheme tells you about an input-amount change on or after 2 May (3 months from notification, capped at 6 years from the end of the tax year), which matters because NHS pension statements are routinely late.
6. **The hard cut-off: you cannot elect after you draw the benefits** — a Scheme Pays notice cannot be given once you have become actually entitled to all your benefits under that scheme (s.237B); this is why members retiring soon must get the election in first, and cross-links C4/C5.
7. **How the reduction is calculated** — the charge paid becomes a notional negative DC amount / a debit against your benefits that grows with interest (CPI plus the scheme's SCAPE-linked factor) until you retire, then permanently reduces your pension (and lump sum where relevant). Worked below.
8. **Mandatory plus voluntary in one election** — a single application before 31 July can be assessed under the mandatory facility, the voluntary facility, or a combination, so the member does not have to choose the wrong box; explain why submitting before the deadline preserves the most options.
9. **Scheme Pays vs paying from your own pocket** — a genuine decision: paying personally preserves full pension but needs the cash now; Scheme Pays preserves cash but permanently reduces the pension and accrues interest. Frame the breakeven as depending on the interest factor versus the member's own cost of capital and investment alternative.
10. **Carry forward first: often there is no charge to pay** — before any election, unused annual allowance from the previous three tax years (current year first) frequently absorbs a one-off input spike, so the Scheme Pays question never arises; link the AA page (§2.B).
11. **The McCloud overlay** — rollback of remedy-period service revised many members' pension input amounts and re-opened annual-allowance positions, so some dentists received revised statements and have an extended window; keep this light and cross-link the McCloud page (§2.A).
12. **Practical checklist and common errors** — missing the 31 July deadline (election then drops to voluntary and the member carries the interest); assuming mandatory Scheme Pays covers a taper-driven charge (it may not); leaving the election until after retirement (too late); forgetting the debt grows with interest.

## 5. Worked examples (each tagged to HP §2.B + figure + effective date)

**Example A — mandatory Scheme Pays threshold met (HP §2.B; £60,000 AA; £2,000 charge floor; s.237B).**
A practice-owner dentist with long historic 1995-section service has a pension input amount of £85,000 in 2025/26 against the standard £60,000 annual allowance, no carry forward left, so £25,000 is charged at the 45% additional rate = £11,250. Because the input exceeded the standard £60,000 and the charge exceeds £2,000, mandatory Scheme Pays applies: the dentist can require the NHS scheme to pay the £11,250. Tag: 2025/26, AA £60,000, s.237B £2,000 floor.

**Example B — the 31 July deadline and the cost of missing it (HP §2.B; s.237BA).**
The same dentist must lodge the mandatory Scheme Pays election by 31 July 2027 (the year after the 2025/26 tax year). If they submit on, say, 1 September 2027, the election is treated under the voluntary facility instead, and the dentist (not the scheme) remains responsible for any HMRC interest from the 31 January 2027 payment date. Show the small interest cost to make the deadline tangible. Tag: 2025/26 charge, deadline 31 July 2027, s.237BA.

**Example C — taper-driven charge needs the voluntary route (HP §2.B; taper; cross-link C3).**
A high-earning principal with threshold income of £230,000 and adjusted income of £300,000 has a tapered allowance of £40,000 (£260,000 floor: £40,000 over £260,000 ÷ 2 = £20,000 reduction from £60,000). Pension input is £55,000, so the £15,000 excess is charged. Because the input (£55,000) is BELOW the standard £60,000, mandatory Scheme Pays does not bite on the taper-driven part; the dentist uses voluntary Scheme Pays instead. Lesson: the taper changes which Scheme Pays route is available. Tag: 2025/26, taper £200k/£260k to £10k floor.

**Example D — Scheme Pays vs cash, the breakeven (HP §2.B).**
On the £11,250 charge from Example A, paying personally costs £11,250 now and leaves the pension intact; Scheme Pays leaves the cash invested but creates a debit that grows with the scheme interest factor and permanently trims the pension at retirement. Sketch the decision: if the dentist can earn more on the retained cash than the scheme charges in interest, and values the cash flow, Scheme Pays often wins, but the permanent pension reduction must be modelled, not assumed away. Tag: 2025/26, illustrative.
