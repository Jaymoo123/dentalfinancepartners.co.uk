## 3. H2 section outline (target body 2,800-3,500 words)

1. **The two exemptions in Group 7 that matter here** — Item 2/2A exempts the supply of dental prostheses by a person on the dentists'/DCP register AND by a dental technician; explain that the prostheses exemption is wider than the medical-care exemption because it explicitly names the technician (§6).
2. **What counts as a "dental prosthesis"** — partial and full dentures, fixed and removable bridges, crowns and inlays, obturators, splints and specially-designed appliances, dental palates, individual artificial teeth, and gold supplied as part of those (HMRC's own list, VATHLT2490).
3. **The "for the dental benefit of a named patient" condition** — the exemption applies when the prosthesis is for the dental benefit of a patient; a stock item not tied to a patient's treatment is treated differently. Make the "named patient" point clearly.
4. **Lab-to-practice supplies: the in-house vs external lab question** — a dental technician (in-house or an external commercial lab) supplying a prosthesis for a named patient makes an exempt supply; explain what this means for an external lab's own VAT position (it may itself be partly exempt and have its own recovery problem).
5. **The practice side: lab fees are an exempt cost, so the VAT on them is usually irrecoverable** — where a lab does charge VAT (e.g. on a standard-rated element), the practice generally cannot reclaim it because it relates to exempt treatment; tie to the partial-exemption page.
6. **What falls OUTSIDE the prosthesis exemption** — items separable from treatment and not prostheses (toothbrushes, toothpaste, floss, whitening kits sold as products) are standard-rated; consumables genuinely used in and inseparable from the exempt treatment follow the treatment and are exempt (VATHLT2500).
7. **Orthodontic appliances and aligners** — where they form part of a course of dental treatment by a registered professional they follow the exempt treatment; sold as a standalone product they may be standard-rated. Frame as fact-dependent.
8. **Imported and EU-sourced prostheses** — a note that prostheses imported into the UK can qualify for exemption/relief so an overseas lab does not create a hidden VAT cost; keep light and link to the gov.uk guidance.
9. **Gold and precious-metal content** — gold supplied as part of an exempt prosthesis is within the exemption; gold supplied separately is not. A short, precise carve-out.
10. **Where this interacts with practice registration and recovery** — exempt lab/prosthesis income does not count towards the £90,000 threshold (§6); a practice with a product-sales line still has to watch the standard-rated tail. Cross-link the threshold and partial-exemption pages.
11. **Record-keeping for labs and practices** — patient-linked job records, separation of prosthesis income from any standard-rated product income, and clear invoicing of any taxable element.
12. **Common errors** — assuming everything a lab invoices is exempt; charging VAT on a named-patient prosthesis; treating a retail whitening kit as exempt because it is "dental"; an external lab missing its own partial-exemption exposure.

## 5. Worked examples (each tagged to HP §6 + figure + effective date)

**Example A — external lab supplying a crown for a named patient (HP §6; Sch 9 Group 7 Item 2A).**
An external commercial lab makes a porcelain crown for a named patient on the dentist's prescription and invoices the practice £180. Because the technician is supplying a prosthesis for the dental benefit of a named patient, the supply is exempt; no VAT on the £180. The practice cannot (and need not) reclaim VAT because none was charged. Tag: 2025/26.

**Example B — the standard-rated tail in the same practice (HP §6).**
The same practice sells take-home whitening kits and electric toothbrushes at reception, £30,000 a year. These are separable products, not prostheses or treatment, so they are standard-rated at 20%. That £30,000 is taxable turnover counting towards the £90,000 threshold (§6), and it creates the partial-exemption recovery the practice can then claim on related costs (cross-link A1). Tag: 2025/26.

**Example C — lab fee with a mixed invoice (HP §6).**
A lab invoices £200 for a named-patient bridge (exempt) plus £40 for a generic stock model kit not tied to a patient (standard-rated, £8 VAT). Only the £8 element carries VAT; because it relates to the practice's exempt treatment, the practice generally cannot reclaim it. Show how splitting the invoice line correctly avoids over- or under-charging VAT. Tag: 2025/26.
