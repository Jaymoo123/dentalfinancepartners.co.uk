## 3. H2 section outline (target body 2,800-3,500 words)

1. **The same purchase, a year apart in relief** — buy a chair in the last week of your accounting period and the AIA lands this year; buy it the first week of the next and relief slips a full tax year. This page is about *when*, not *what* (assume AIA basics, link the AIA page) (§7).
2. **When expenditure is "incurred": the rule that drives timing** — for capital allowances, expenditure is generally incurred on the date you become **unconditionally obliged to pay** (broadly contract/delivery), with a special rule: if payment is due **more than 4 months** later, it is incurred on the payment-due date. State this precisely because it is the lever the whole article turns on (§7).
3. **Hire purchase and the incurred date** — under HP the asset is treated as bought (and the cash price incurred) when it is **brought into use**, so an HP deal completed and the kit in use before year-end pulls the whole cash price into this year's AIA even though most instalments are unpaid. Cross-link B3 (§7).
4. **The £1m AIA is a per-period allowance: use it or lose it** — the £1m resets each accounting period and does not carry forward. Spend below £1m and the unused balance is simply gone; there is no rollover. This is the "wasting AIA" trap on both sides: spending nothing in a high-profit year, or bunching spend so it spills over the cap (§7).
5. **Short accounting periods prorate the AIA** — a period shorter than 12 months scales the £1m down pro rata (a 9-month period gives 9/12 × £1m = £750,000). Relevant on incorporation, a year-end change, or a first/final period. Show the proration so a reader changing year-end does not over-claim (§7).
6. **Straddling the AIA: periods that span a rate or limit change** — where an accounting period spans a change in the AIA limit (or, currently relevant, the 18%→14% main-rate WDA change on 1 April 2026), the allowance/rate is apportioned by time, and there can be a cap on how much of the post-change spend gets the higher treatment. Explain the day-apportionment principle and flag that spend timing within a straddling period can matter (§7; cross-ref the 14% change in B1).
7. **Accelerate or defer? Matching relief to your profit** — the core planning section. **Accelerate** (buy before year-end) when this year's profits are high, or this year's marginal rate is higher, or to use AIA that would otherwise be wasted. **Defer** (buy after year-end) when next year's profits/rate will be higher, or this year is a loss/low-profit year where the relief is worth less. Tie to the 6 Apr 2026 dividend-rate rise and the 14% WDA step as reasons the timing calculus shifts (§7).
8. **Don't create or waste a loss** — claiming full AIA in a low-profit year can push the practice into a loss that is relieved at a low value, while the same spend in a profitable year saves tax at the marginal rate. Note that AIA is not compulsory; you can claim less than the maximum and leave expenditure in the pool to write down, which is sometimes the better answer in a loss year (§7).
9. **Bunching vs spreading across two periods** — a £1.4m fit-out in one period wastes £400k of AIA (over the £1m cap, into the pool at 14%); splitting it across two accounting periods (some before year-end, some after) keeps more inside two separate £1m AIAs. Show when phasing the spend beats doing it all at once (§7).
10. **Companies: full expensing and the 40% FYA reduce the timing pressure** — for a company, full expensing (100%, no cap, new main-rate plant) and the new 40% FYA (from 1 January 2026) mean the £1m cap bites less, but the *period* in which spend is incurred still decides the year of relief. Note the company-vs-unincorporated difference and link the full-expensing page (§7).
11. **The practical year-end checklist** — review planned spend against this year's vs next year's expected profit; confirm the incurred date (contract, delivery, payment terms, HP in-use date); check whether the period is short or straddling; decide accelerate/defer/phase; document the decision. Frame as the action list.
12. **Common errors** — assuming the *payment* date governs (it is usually the obligation/in-use date); leaving AIA unused in a high-profit year; bunching spend over the £1m cap; over-claiming AIA in a short period; claiming full AIA in a loss year and wasting the relief; ignoring the 18%→14% step when modelling above-AIA spend.

## 5. Worked examples (each tagged to HP §7 + figure + effective date)

**Example A — one week either side of year-end (HP §7; incurred date).**
A practice with a 31 March year-end plans a £60,000 equipment order. Delivered and contracted 28 March 2027: the £60,000 AIA lands in 2026/27, cutting that year's tax. Delivered 6 April 2027 instead: relief slips into 2027/28. If 2026/27 is the higher-profit year, accelerating saves tax a year earlier and at the higher marginal rate. Show both timelines. Tag: 2026/27 vs 2027/28, §7.

**Example B — short period proration after a year-end change (HP §7; £1m pro-rated).**
A practice shortens its accounting period to 9 months on incorporation. The AIA for that period is 9/12 × £1,000,000 = **£750,000**, not £1,000,000. A £900,000 fit-out in that 9-month period exceeds the prorated cap by £150,000, which goes into the pool at 14% rather than getting 100% relief. Phasing some spend into the next full-year period would have kept it inside AIA. Tag: 9-month period, 2026/27, §7.

**Example C — phasing a £1.4m group fit-out across two periods (HP §7; £1m cap).**
A group plans £1.4m of equipment. Done in one period: £1m AIA + £400,000 into the main pool (14% WDA = £56,000 year-one relief on the excess). Split £700k into the period ending 31 March 2027 and £700k into the period ending 31 March 2028: both tranches sit inside a fresh £1m AIA, so the full £1.4m gets 100% relief. Show the relief difference. Tag: 2026/27 + 2027/28, §7.

**Example D — not claiming full AIA in a loss year (HP §7; AIA is optional).**
A practice has a low-profit year and £40,000 of new equipment. Claiming the full £40,000 AIA wipes out the small profit and creates relief worth little this year. Claiming partial AIA (or none) and leaving the spend in the pool to write down at 14% preserves more relief for profitable future years. Demonstrate that AIA is a maximum, not a mandate. Tag: 2026/27, §7.
