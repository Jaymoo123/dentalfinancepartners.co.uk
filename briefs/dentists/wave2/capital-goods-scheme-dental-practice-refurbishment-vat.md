## 3. H2 section outline (target body 2,800-3,500 words)

1. **Why a one-off VAT recovery is not the end of the story for big spend** — for a partially exempt dental practice, large capital spend gets its VAT recovery locked in at year one under partial exemption, but the Capital Goods Scheme (CGS) then keeps adjusting it for years as taxable use changes (§6 context).
2. **What the CGS catches: the £250,000 threshold** — capital expenditure on land, a building or part of a building, civil engineering, and refurbishment/fit-out works with a VAT-exclusive value of £250,000 or more; a separate £50,000 threshold catches computers, aircraft, ships (rarely relevant to a practice). Below £250,000, ordinary partial exemption alone applies.
3. **What "refurbishment" means here** — a single refurbishment/fit-out project costing £250,000+ excluding VAT is a CGS item even if no land changes hands; this is the trigger most dental owners do not see coming on a major surgery rebuild.
4. **The adjustment period: 10 intervals for land and buildings** — land/buildings/refurbishment run for 10 intervals (about 10 years), 5 intervals for computers; each interval is normally a VAT year.
5. **The baseline and the adjustment percentage** — interval one fixes the "baseline" recovery (the partial-exemption %); each later interval compares that year's taxable-use % to the baseline; the difference is the adjustment percentage (§6).
6. **The adjustment formula, in plain numbers** — adjustment = total input VAT on the item / number of intervals × adjustment percentage; positive (more taxable use) means a further reclaim, negative (more exempt use) means a clawback. Worked below.
7. **Why dental practices usually face a clawback, not a refund** — a practice that adds cosmetic/private taxable income (high taxable %) at fit-out, then drifts back to mostly-exempt NHS work, sees taxable use fall and VAT clawed back; the opposite (more cosmetic over time) gives a refund.
8. **The interaction with partial exemption** — the CGS sits ON TOP of the annual partial-exemption calculation; the baseline IS the partial-exemption recovery %, and each interval's taxable-use % comes from that year's longer-period calculation. Cross-link A1.
9. **Selling, closing or deregistering mid-period** — a sale or a move to wholly exempt use during the 10 years triggers a final adjustment (a deemed taxable or exempt use for the remaining intervals); this links straight to the deregistration page (A4) and to a practice sale.
10. **Opting to tax the property, and the buy-vs-lease angle** — a brief, careful note that buying a freehold/long lease where the seller has opted to tax brings VAT into the purchase price and into the CGS; the lease-vs-buy decision has a VAT dimension. Keep light, link the lease-vs-buy page.
11. **Records you must keep for 10+ years** — the CGS record (cost, input tax, baseline %, each interval's adjustment) has to survive longer than normal VAT records; flag the retention trap.
12. **Common errors** — assuming year-one recovery is final; missing that a £250k+ fit-out is a CGS item with no land involved; forgetting the interval adjustments and getting a surprise assessment; ignoring the final adjustment on sale or deregistration.

## 5. Worked examples (each tagged to HP §6 + figure + effective date)

**Example A — a £300,000 fit-out enters the CGS (HP §6; £250,000 threshold, 10 intervals).**
A practice spends £300,000 (VAT-exclusive) refurbishing into a mixed NHS/cosmetic site; VAT of £60,000 is incurred. Because it exceeds £250,000, it is a CGS item over 10 intervals. At interval one the partial-exemption recovery is 20% taxable use, so £12,000 is recovered and becomes the baseline. Tag: 2025/26 figures, CGS per VAT Notice 706/2.

**Example B — a clawback when NHS share rises (HP §6; CGS formula).**
By interval four, cosmetic income has shrunk and taxable use is only 10%. Adjustment = £60,000 / 10 × (10% − 20%) = £6,000 / interval × (−10 percentage points expressed as the adjustment %) → a clawback of £600 for that interval (HMRC repays/claws the £60,000 ÷ 10 = £6,000 attributable to the interval, multiplied by the −10% change = −£600). The practice repays £600 to HMRC for interval four. Show the arithmetic step by step so it is unambiguous. Tag: 2025/26, CGS.

**Example C — a refund when cosmetic grows (HP §6; CGS formula).**
By interval seven the practice has built a busy implant/cosmetic suite and taxable use is 40%. Adjustment = £6,000 (interval share) × (+20 percentage points vs the 20% baseline) = +£1,200 reclaimable from HMRC for that interval. Demonstrates the scheme cuts both ways and rewards a genuine shift towards taxable supplies. Tag: 2025/26, CGS.
