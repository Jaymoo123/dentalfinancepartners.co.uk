## 3. H2 section outline (target body 2,800-3,500 words)

1. **One test governs all dental VAT liability** — every supply by a registered dentist/DCP is exempt only if its principal purpose is to protect, maintain or restore the patient's health; cosmetic-only work with no therapeutic purpose is standard-rated. Set up that this single test, not the treatment's name, decides the answer (§6).
2. **Where the test comes from** — Sch 9 Group 7 implements the EU/CJEU "medical care" concept; the principal-purpose wording derives from CJEU case law (d'Ambrumenil on medical purpose, and Skatteverket v PFC Clinic on cosmetic surgery being exempt only where it treats a health condition). HMRC adopts this in VATHLT2480 (cosmetic dentistry). Keep it accurate but readable.
3. **"Principal" means the main purpose, judged on the facts** — a treatment with an incidental cosmetic benefit is still exempt if its main purpose is health; a treatment with an incidental health benefit is still standard-rated if its main purpose is appearance. The dividing line is the dominant purpose at the point of treatment.
4. **The decision framework (the core of the page)** — a repeatable 4-step test the reader can apply to any treatment: (1) Is the supplier a registered dentist/DCP? (2) What is the documented clinical reason / presenting complaint? (3) Is the principal purpose health or appearance? (4) What contemporaneous evidence supports that? Present as a clear flow.
5. **Worked through the classic borderlines** — tooth whitening (standard-rated when purely cosmetic; can be exempt as part of a treatment programme, e.g. internal bleaching of a discoloured non-vital tooth); veneers (cosmetic vs restorative); orthodontics (health/function vs appearance); composite bonding; gum contouring. Apply the framework to each in a paragraph, not a deep how-to.
6. **HMRC's actual stance on cosmetic dentistry** — VATHLT2480: cosmetic services performed as part of a supply of dental treatment are a single exempt supply; cosmetic dentistry outside any healthcare context is standard-rated; "each case on its own facts", and HMRC notes purely-cosmetic dentistry is comparatively rare. Quote-faithfully, no overstatement.
7. **The single vs multiple supply question** — when a cosmetic element is part of a larger course of exempt treatment, it can be a single exempt supply; when it is genuinely a standalone product/treatment, it is a separate standard-rated supply. The "single vs multiple supply" principle is the technical crux for mixed treatments.
8. **Facial aesthetics: the headline case, in brief** — Botox and dermal fillers for appearance are the textbook standard-rated example; medical Botox (hyperhidrosis, bruxism-related, migraine) can be exempt on a principal-purpose basis. Keep this short and link DOWN to the dedicated facial-aesthetics page for the operational handling (no repetition).
9. **The evidence trail that wins an HMRC review** — clinical notes recording the presenting complaint and clinical rationale, the treatment-plan classification, separated income coding, and a default-to-standard-rate-when-genuinely-borderline policy; this is the practical protection.
10. **Why getting it wrong cuts both ways** — wrongly exempting standard-rated cosmetic work risks an assessment plus interest and penalties; wrongly standard-rating exempt care over-charges patients and may distort the partial-exemption and threshold positions. Cross-link A1 and the threshold page.
11. **When to take advice** — a new cosmetic line, an HMRC liability query, or a treatment you genuinely cannot place are the trigger points.
12. **Common errors** — deciding by treatment name not principal purpose; assuming all whitening is standard-rated (or all exempt); no contemporaneous clinical note; treating a clearly cosmetic add-on as part of an exempt plan to avoid VAT.

## 5. Worked examples (each tagged to HP §6 + figure + effective date)

**Example A — whitening, two ways (HP §6; principal-purpose test, VATHLT2480).**
Patient 1 wants whitening purely to brighten healthy teeth for a wedding: principal purpose is appearance, so standard-rated at 20%. Patient 2 has a single discoloured non-vital tooth following trauma and root treatment; internal bleaching restores the tooth's appearance as part of a clinical course: principal purpose is restorative/health, so exempt. Same procedure name, opposite VAT outcome, decided by the documented clinical reason. Tag: 2025/26.

**Example B — Botox, two ways (HP §6; principal-purpose; links to facial-aesthetics page).**
A dentist administers Botox to soften glabellar lines for appearance: standard-rated. The same dentist administers Botox to manage diagnosed bruxism/masseter hypertrophy causing pain and tooth wear: principal purpose is health, so exempt on a principal-purpose basis with clinical notes. Point to the facial-aesthetics page for the income-handling mechanics; do not repeat them. Tag: 2025/26.

**Example C — the single-supply mixed treatment (HP §6; single vs multiple supply).**
A patient has a course of treatment to restore a broken-down anterior tooth (clinically necessary, exempt) and asks for two adjacent healthy teeth to be veneered purely for symmetry (cosmetic). The restorative work is exempt; the two cosmetic veneers are a separate standard-rated supply unless genuinely part of the clinical restoration. Show how to split and evidence the two elements rather than treating the whole plan as exempt. Tag: 2025/26.
