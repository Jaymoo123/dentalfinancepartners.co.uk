## 3. H2 section outline (target body 2,800-3,500 words)

1. **Why this question is different for a principal** — set the frame: the well-known "dividends are not pensionable" trap is the DEFAULT for an incorporated ASSOCIATE, but a dental PRINCIPAL who actually HOLDS the GDS/PDS contract sits in a different category (the provider, not a performer working through a PSC). State up front that this page is about that distinction and that the reader must confirm their own NHSBSA position (§2.C hedge).
2. **The two roles the regulations care about: provider vs performer** — define the NHS Pension Scheme's split. A PROVIDER is the contractor that holds the GDS/PDS contract; a PERFORMER (associate) carries out the work under it. Pensionability follows this status, not the company label. This is the hinge of the whole page.
3. **The associate-through-a-company trap (the settled bad case)** — confirm the hard rule: a PERFORMER (associate) who sets up as a limited company cannot be a member of the NHS Pension Scheme, because the regulations do not permit a performer to pension through a company. Keep this tight, cross-link the existing incorporation-trap page rather than re-teaching it (§2.C).
4. **The provider-through-a-company position (the more favourable case)** — present the position evidenced by NHSBSA's NPE provider guidance: a GDS/PDS contractor CAN incorporate, and the provider GDPs (the GDP shareholders) can remain in the NHS Pension Scheme. Frame carefully as "NHSBSA's provider guidance indicates", not as a guarantee for every structure.
5. **Salary and dividends up to the contract ceiling** — for a provider, the guidance indicates that BOTH salary AND dividends actually taken can be treated as NHS pensionable income, with no need to apportion between NHS and private work, UP TO the contract's net-pensionable-earnings ceiling; income left in the company is not pensionable. This is the crucial nuance that distinguishes the provider from the associate.
6. **The net pensionable earnings ceiling** — explain the NPE ceiling: NHS pensionable pay for a practice is capped at a defined slice of the GDS/PDS contract value (the working figure in NHSBSA guidance is 43.9% of contract value as the maximum pensionable pay distributable across the practice's dentists). Above that ceiling, profit (however taken) is NOT pensionable for anyone, incorporated or not. Worked below.
7. **Where the §2.C trap STILL bites a principal** — even for a provider, two limbs of the trap survive: (a) profit ABOVE the NPE ceiling is not pensionable regardless of structure; (b) only income ACTUALLY EXTRACTED (salary plus dividends) is pensionable, so profit retained in the company for reinvestment is not pensioned. Be explicit that incorporation is therefore still not pension-neutral.
8. **Why this is genuinely contested / case-specific (the honest hedge)** — state plainly that the interaction of the practitioner mechanism, the provider/performer split and a particular company's share and contract structure is fact-sensitive; commentary and practice vary; and the regulations have been read differently in different cases. Direct the reader to confirm their own position in writing with NHSBSA before relying on it. Do NOT assert the answer for the reader's specific facts (§2.C hedge, locked).
9. **The NHSBSA confirmation step** — practical instruction: a principal considering incorporation should get NHSBSA's position on their specific provider/contract/share structure in writing, complete the annual net-pensionable-earnings declaration correctly, and not assume the associate trap applies to them (nor that the provider position applies if they are really a performer).
10. **Modelling the pension cost alongside the tax saving** — per the §2.C / §5 rule, never present the incorporation tax saving alone: model the pension accrual that is and is not preserved under the provider position, including the ceiling cap and the retained-profit point. Cross-link §5 (the structure decision).
11. **What changed and when (context, kept light)** — note the chronology only as background: the regulations have not permitted a performer to pension through a company for many years (the November 2011 cut-off is the commonly cited marker for performers), while the provider route has continued. Keep factual, do not over-date.
12. **Decision checklist** — are you a provider (contract holder) or a performer? Is your income within the NPE ceiling? Are you actually extracting it as salary/dividend or retaining it? Have you confirmed your specific position with NHSBSA in writing? Frame as questions, not answers.

## 5. Worked examples (each tagged to HP §2.C + figure + effective date)

**Example A — the associate-through-a-company trap (HP §2.C; performer = not pensionable).**
An associate earning £120,000 of NHS-derived income decides to operate through a limited company. As a PERFORMER, the regulations do not let them pension through the company, so NHS pension accrual on that income stops entirely. This is the settled bad case and the existing incorporation-trap page owns the detail; C2 uses it only as the contrast. Tag: 2025/26, performer route.

**Example B — the provider within the ceiling (HP §2.C; provider position; 43.9% NPE ceiling).**
A single-handed principal holds a GDS contract worth £300,000. The NPE ceiling caps pensionable pay at roughly 43.9% of contract value = about £131,700 for the practice. The principal incorporates, draws £40,000 salary and £90,000 dividend (£130,000 total, within the ceiling). Per NHSBSA provider guidance, both the salary and the dividend actually taken can be treated as pensionable up to the ceiling, so accrual is largely preserved, unlike the associate in Example A. Flag clearly: this depends on the principal being a provider and on confirming the position with NHSBSA. Tag: 2025/26, NPE ceiling working figure 43.9% of contract value.

**Example C — the ceiling and retained-profit limbs that still bite (HP §2.C).**
The same practice generates £180,000 of distributable profit. Even as a provider, only income within the ~£131,700 ceiling is pensionable, so the ~£48,000 above it is not pensioned however it is taken. And if the principal leaves £30,000 in the company for reinvestment rather than extracting it, that £30,000 is not pensioned either. Lesson: the trap is narrowed for a provider but not eliminated; incorporation is still not pension-neutral. Tag: 2025/26.
