## 3. H2 section outline (target body 2,800-3,500 words)

1. **The finance method is also a tax decision** — the same dental chair gives a completely different tax outcome depending on how you fund it. This page is about the fork between getting **capital allowances** (you are treated as owning the asset) and getting **deductible rentals** (you are renting). Assume the reader knows what AIA/capital allowances are (link the general equipment guide).
2. **Route 1 — outright purchase: capital allowances on the full cost** — pay cash, you own it, you claim AIA/full expensing on the full price in the year of purchase. Simplest, most front-loaded relief; costs the most cash now (§7).
3. **Route 2 — hire purchase: allowances on the cash price, as if owned** — under HP you are treated as owning the asset for capital allowances from the moment you bring it into use; you claim AIA/allowances on the **cash price** (the capital element), even though you pay in instalments. The **interest/finance charge is separately deductible** as a revenue expense over the term. This is the route that gives both immediate allowances and spread payments (§7).
4. **Route 3 — finance lease: no capital allowances, rentals deductible** — under a finance lease the lessor (the finance company) owns the asset and claims the allowances; the practice does **not** get capital allowances. Instead the practice deducts the **rentals** (broadly following the accounting depreciation/finance charge) as a revenue expense over the lease term. Relief is spread, not front-loaded (§7).
5. **Route 4 — operating lease: pure rental, fully deductible** — a true rental (short of the asset's useful life, asset returned at the end); no ownership, no capital allowances, the **rental payments are fully deductible** as a trading expense as incurred. Often used for fast-depreciating IT/imaging that you want to refresh (§7).
6. **The comparison table: ownership, allowances, what you deduct, VAT, balance sheet** — a single grid: outright / HP / finance lease / operating lease across the columns "Who owns it for tax", "Capital allowances?", "What you deduct", "VAT treatment", "On your balance sheet?". This is the page's centrepiece.
7. **VAT, briefly: HP reclaims up front, leasing reclaims per payment** — on HP a VAT-registered practice can reclaim the VAT on the asset up front; on a finance/operating lease the VAT is charged on each rental and reclaimed per payment. For a partly-exempt dental practice this interacts with partial exemption (cross-link the partial-exemption page); keep light. Note most dental supplies are exempt, so VAT recovery on equipment is often restricted anyway.
8. **Total relief is often similar; the timing and cash flow differ** — over the asset's life the total tax relief under HP/purchase (allowances) and under leasing (rentals) tends to converge; the real differences are **timing of relief** (front-loaded under AIA vs spread under leasing) and **cash flow**. Make this the honest, balanced takeaway, not a "buying always wins" line.
9. **When each route earns its place for a dental practice** — outright/HP when the practice wants the front-loaded AIA relief and intends to keep the kit; operating lease for fast-obsolescing imaging/IT you want to refresh; finance lease rarely the tax-optimal choice for a profitable owner-occupier but sometimes the only finance available. Frame as a decision, not a verdict.
10. **The interaction with the £1m AIA and year-end timing** — HP and purchase both feed the AIA, so the finance choice and the year-end timing decision (B5) are linked: an HP deal "incurred" before year-end can pull the whole cash-price into this year's AIA. Cross-link B5.
11. **Cars are different (a short warning)** — none of this applies cleanly to cars: cars are excluded from AIA, leased-car deductions are restricted, and the rules differ. Point to the dedicated treatment and move on.
12. **Common errors** — assuming a finance lease gives capital allowances (it does not); double-counting (claiming allowances *and* deducting rentals); ignoring the up-front VAT advantage of HP; choosing finance purely on monthly cost without modelling the tax timing; treating an operating lease asset as if owned.

## 5. Worked examples (each tagged to HP §7 + figure + effective date)

**Example A — same £30,000 chair, four finance routes (HP §7; AIA £1m).**
A £30,000 dental chair, 2026/27. **Outright:** claim £30,000 AIA in year one (100% relief now). **HP** (deposit + 36 instalments): claim £30,000 AIA on the cash price when brought into use, *plus* deduct the finance interest over the term. **Finance lease:** no allowances; deduct the rentals (roughly £10,000/year over 3 years) as a revenue expense. **Operating lease:** no allowances; deduct each rental as paid. Show that purchase and HP front-load the relief while the leases spread it. Tag: 2026/27, §7.

**Example B — the cash-flow vs timing trade-off (HP §7).**
A higher-rate sole-trader associate weighs outright purchase (full £30,000 deduction now, saving ~£12,600 tax at ~42% combined but £30,000 cash out) against an operating lease (smaller deductions each year, no large cash outflow). Show the year-one tax saving and the cash position side by side so the reader sees the trade-off rather than a single "winner". Tag: 2026/27, §7.

**Example C — HP brings the whole cash price into this year's AIA (HP §7; B5 link).**
A practice with a 31 March year-end signs an HP agreement and brings a £45,000 OPG machine into use in March 2027. The full £45,000 cash price enters the 2026/27 AIA even though only the deposit has been paid, because HP is treated as a purchase for capital allowances. Contrast with a finance lease, where nothing enters the AIA. Cross-link B5. Tag: 2026/27, §7.
