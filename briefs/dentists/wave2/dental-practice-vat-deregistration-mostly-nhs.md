## 3. H2 section outline (target body 2,800-3,500 words)

1. **Why a dental practice ends up deregistering** — a practice that registered because of a cosmetic/product line, or because it once crossed £90,000 of taxable turnover, may later see that taxable income shrink (cosmetic line closed, mostly-NHS shift); once it falls away, staying registered is admin with little recovery benefit (§6).
2. **Two routes off the register: voluntary vs compulsory** — voluntary deregistration when taxable turnover for the next 12 months will be below the deregistration threshold; compulsory notification within 30 days when the practice stops making taxable supplies altogether (e.g. drops the cosmetic line entirely).
3. **The deregistration threshold: £88,000** — you can ask HMRC to cancel when VAT-taxable (non-exempt) turnover for the coming 12 months will be under £88,000 (VAT-exclusive); contrast with the £90,000 registration threshold (the £2,000 gap stops practices ping-ponging on and off, §6).
4. **What "taxable turnover" means for this test** — exempt dental care and exempt prosthesis income do NOT count; only standard-rated cosmetic-only work, product sales and any taxable rents; cross-link the threshold and cosmetic pages so the reader gets the test right.
5. **The deemed supply: the sting in the tail** — on deregistration the practice is treated as supplying (selling to itself) the business assets on hand on which it recovered input VAT, and must account for output VAT on their current value; legal basis Sch 4 para 8.
6. **The £1,000 let-out** — no deemed-supply VAT is due if the total VAT on the assets would be £1,000 or less; explain how a small practice with modest equipment often falls under this, while one that recently kitted out a cosmetic suite may not (§6 gap to lock).
7. **Valuing the assets: replacement cost now, not original cost** — assets are valued at what it would cost to replace them in their current condition, so depreciated kit is valued low; worked below.
8. **The Capital Goods Scheme final adjustment** — if the practice has a CGS item (a £250,000+ refurbishment/property) still in its 10-year period, deregistration triggers a final adjustment for the remaining intervals, treated as a deemed disposal; this can be a larger number than the ordinary deemed supply, so flag it loudly and cross-link A3.
9. **Timing the exit and the last return** — choose the effective date of cancellation, account for the deemed supply on the final return, settle any outstanding VAT, and keep records for six years after deregistration.
10. **Should you deregister at all? the trade-off** — coming off loses the ability to reclaim the (already small) recoverable input VAT and means re-registering with admin if the cosmetic line revives; staying on keeps recovery but means continued returns. Frame as a genuine decision, not automatic.
11. **Re-registration if taxable income comes back** — a note that if the cosmetic/product line grows again past £90,000 the practice must re-register, and the on/off cost.
12. **Common errors** — forgetting the deemed-supply charge and getting an assessment; missing the CGS final adjustment; treating exempt income as if it counted towards the £88,000 test; deregistering then immediately breaching the registration threshold again.

## 5. Worked examples (each tagged to HP §6 + figure + effective date)

**Example A — voluntary deregistration below £88,000 (HP §6; deregistration threshold £88,000).**
A practice that registered when its cosmetic line peaked now expects standard-rated taxable turnover of only £40,000 for the coming year (the rest exempt NHS/private dental care). Because that is below the £88,000 deregistration threshold, it can ask HMRC to cancel. Exempt dental income is ignored for the test even though total practice turnover is far higher. Tag: 2025/26 (threshold £88,000).

**Example B — the deemed-supply charge and the £1,000 let-out (HP §6; Sch 4 para 8; £1,000 de minimis).**
On deregistration the practice holds cosmetic-suite equipment on which it recovered input VAT, now worth £4,000 at replacement-in-current-condition value. Deemed-supply output VAT = 20% × £4,000 = £800. Because £800 is under £1,000, no VAT is actually due. Show that the same kit valued at £6,000 would give £1,200 of VAT, over the £1,000 line, so the whole £1,200 becomes payable. Tag: 2025/26.

**Example C — the CGS final adjustment dwarfs the deemed supply (HP §6; CGS interaction, A3).**
The practice still has a £300,000 cosmetic fit-out (£60,000 VAT) in interval 6 of its 10-year CGS period, recovered at 20% taxable use. Deregistering to wholly exempt use triggers a final adjustment treating the remaining 4 intervals as 0% taxable use: roughly £6,000 per interval × 4 × the 20-point fall = a clawback in the low thousands, materially bigger than the £800 deemed supply in Example B. Lesson: the CGS, not the equipment deemed supply, is usually the real deregistration cost for a practice that recently refurbished. Tag: 2025/26, CGS per VAT Notice 706/2.
