## 3. H2 section outline (target body 2,800-3,500 words)

1. **What partial retirement is** — the flexibility, available across all NHS pension sections from 1 October 2023 (1 April 2024 in Northern Ireland), to draw between 20% and 100% of your pension benefits WITHOUT leaving your job and WITHOUT a break in service, then keep working (§2 gap to lock).
2. **Who can use it and when** — you must be over your minimum pension age (generally 55, rising), and you must agree a new working arrangement that reduces your pensionable pay or commitment. For dentists specifically the condition is a 10% reduction in COMMITMENT (level of engagement), reflecting the practitioner model, rather than a salaried-pay cut.
3. **The 10% reduction condition** — you must reduce pensionable pay/commitment by at least 10%; for dentists this is the 10% reduction in commitment. The reduction is required for 12 MONTHS; after 12 months you can increase commitment again without losing the partial retirement. Be precise: this is the single condition members most often get wrong.
4. **Drawing 20% to 100%: the choice** — you choose what proportion to crystallise (minimum 20%, maximum 100%), in up to two drawdown events. Explain the trade-off: drawing more now gives more income now but less future pension; drawing the minimum keeps most benefits growing.
5. **You keep accruing: re-accrual in the 2015 scheme** — crucially, after partial retirement you continue to BUILD pension in the 2015 CARE scheme (1/54th of pensionable earnings, active revaluation CPI+1.5%), so you are drawing one pot while a new one accrues. This is the headline advantage over fully retiring (§2).
6. **The tax interaction: pension income plus continued earnings** — the drawn pension is taxable income that stacks ON TOP of your continued self-employed/associate profit, potentially pushing the combined total into a higher band; the tax-free lump sum (where the section provides one) is separate. Worked below. This is the page's tax core.
7. **The annual allowance angle while drawing and accruing** — because you are still accruing, you still have a pension input amount and an annual-allowance test; and if you flexibly access a DC AVC pot as part of this, the £10,000 money purchase annual allowance can be triggered. Cross-link C3.
8. **Early-draw reductions still apply** — if you partially retire BEFORE your normal pension age, the drawn portion is actuarially reduced just as a full early pension would be; partial retirement does not switch the reduction off. Cross-link C5, which owns the reduction detail.
9. **Scheme Pays debts crystallise on the drawn portion** — any annual-allowance charge previously settled via Scheme Pays creates a debit that is recovered when benefits are paid, including the partially-drawn portion; flag this so the drawn figure is not a surprise. Cross-link C1.
10. **Partial retirement vs full retirement vs retire-and-return** — position the three: partial retirement keeps you in post and accruing with a 10% step-down; full retirement ends accrual; retire-and-return needs a 24-hour break and re-employment. Keep retire-and-return light (it is a separate route).
11. **Practical sequence for a dentist** — model the income (drawn pension + reduced-commitment profit), check the band impact, confirm the 10% commitment reduction is genuine and documented, time the drawdown, and apply four months ahead (the standard NHS application lead time).
12. **Common errors** — failing to make a genuine 10% commitment reduction (the application fails); assuming accrual stops (it continues in 2015); forgetting the drawn pension is taxable on top of earnings; partially retiring early without modelling the actuarial reduction; ignoring an outstanding Scheme Pays debit.

## 5. Worked examples (each tagged to HP §2 + figure + effective date)

**Example A — drawing 50% and the band impact (HP §2; partial retirement from 1 Oct 2023).**
A 58-year-old principal with a £40,000 full NHS pension entitlement draws 50% (£20,000) under partial retirement and reduces commitment by 10%, cutting practice profit from £90,000 to about £80,000. Combined taxable income = £80,000 profit + £20,000 pension = £100,000, all in the higher-rate band and at the edge of the £100,000 personal-allowance taper. Lesson: the drawn pension stacks on earnings and can hit the allowance taper. Tag: 2025/26, partial retirement rules from 1 Oct 2023.

**Example B — re-accrual while drawing (HP §2; 1/54th CARE, CPI+1.5%).**
The same dentist keeps working at reduced commitment and continues to accrue in the 2015 scheme at 1/54th of pensionable earnings, with active revaluation CPI+1.5%. So while £20,000 a year is being paid out, a fresh slice of 2015 pension is being built, which a full retirement would have forgone. Tag: 2025/26, 2015 CARE 1/54th, revaluation CPI+1.5%.

**Example C — partial retirement before normal pension age (HP §2; actuarial reduction, cross-link C5).**
A dentist whose 2015-scheme normal pension age is 67 partially retires at 60, drawing 40% of benefits seven years early. That drawn portion is actuarially reduced for early payment (roughly 5% per year as a rule of thumb, exact factors set by the scheme actuary), so the 40% slice pays less than its unreduced face value. Partial retirement does not remove the reduction. Cross-link C5 for the reduction detail. Tag: 2025/26, early-draw actuarial reduction.
