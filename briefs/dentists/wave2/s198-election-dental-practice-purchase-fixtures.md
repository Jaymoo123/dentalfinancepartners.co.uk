## 3. H2 section outline (target body 2,800-3,500 words)

1. **The election most practice buyers never hear about until it is too late** — when you buy a practice with existing fixtures (chairs plumbed in, surgery wiring, HVAC, cabinetry), the capital allowances on those fixtures do not pass automatically. A joint s.198 election fixes the value, and without it the buyer's claim can be lost entirely (§7).
2. **What a "fixture" is for s.198 (and what it is not)** — fixtures are plant and machinery that have become part of the building: the integral features (electrical, water, heating, ventilation) plus plumbed-in or bolted-down surgery kit. Loose, moveable equipment is dealt with separately (an ordinary apportionment, not the fixtures regime). Draw the line clearly.
3. **How the election works: a joint, signed, fixed value** — s.198 lets seller and buyer **jointly elect** the amount attributed to the fixtures; that figure becomes the buyer's qualifying expenditure *and* the seller's disposal value, so the two sides always match (CAA 2001 s.198) (§7).
4. **The cap: you cannot elect more than the seller's original cost or the sale price** — the elected figure cannot exceed the lower of the seller's original qualifying expenditure on the fixture or the actual sale price (s.198). This is why a buyer cannot simply invent a high number; it is bounded by history.
5. **The 2-year deadline (s.201): the cliff edge** — the election must be made within **2 years of the buyer acquiring the interest** (CAA 2001 s.201). Miss it and the parties cannot fix the value by election; the buyer's fixtures claim is, in practice, lost. This is the single most preventable loss in a practice purchase (§7).
6. **The negotiation: the buyer and the seller want opposite things** — the core of the article. The **buyer wants a HIGH elected value** (more qualifying expenditure to write down, faster future relief). The **seller wants a LOW elected value**, because a high disposal value triggers a **balancing charge** that claws back capital allowances the seller already claimed over the years. The election value is therefore a genuine commercial negotiation, not a box-tick.
7. **The seller's balancing charge, explained in numbers** — walk through why a high election hurts the seller: the fixtures sit in the seller's pool with a low written-down value after years of allowances; electing a high disposal value brings a large balancing charge into the seller's final tax computation. Show the mechanics so the buyer understands the seller's resistance (§7; CAA 2001 s.55-s.59 balancing adjustments).
8. **The fixed-value requirement and the pooling requirement** — since April 2014, the buyer can only claim if (a) the seller "pooled" the qualifying expenditure before the sale, and (b) a fixed value is agreed (by s.198 election or, failing agreement, by a First-tier Tribunal determination). State both conditions; a buyer who skips due diligence on whether the seller ever pooled can find there is nothing to elect over.
9. **Why the s.198 value sits inside the wider price apportionment** — the s.198 fixtures figure is a sub-component of the §4 split between goodwill, property and tangibles. A buyer pushing a high fixtures value is also shifting the apportionment; the seller's CGT/BADR position on goodwill interacts. Cross-link §4. Keep the CGT detail light and link out.
10. **Due diligence: what to demand before exchange** — the seller's capital allowances history, confirmation the fixtures were pooled, the existing s.198 statement from when the seller bought (if any), and a draft election agreed in the contract. Frame this as a financial due-diligence item (cross-link the due-diligence checklist).
11. **Getting the election into the deal: solicitor and accountant working together** — the election is a tax document but it lives in the sale contract; it needs the accountant to value and the solicitor to paper it. Flag the hand-off where it commonly falls through the cracks.
12. **Common errors** — assuming allowances pass automatically; missing the 2-year deadline; failing to check the seller pooled; accepting the seller's low election without modelling the lost future relief; not specifying the election in the heads of terms so it becomes a post-completion afterthought.

## 5. Worked examples (each tagged to HP §7 + figure + effective date)

**Example A — the buyer-versus-seller tension in numbers (HP §7; s.198 cap, balancing charge).**
A practice sells in 2026/27. The fixtures originally cost the seller £120,000; after years of allowances the seller's pool value for them is £20,000. The seller proposes electing **£1** (legal minimum) to avoid any balancing charge. The buyer wants to elect the **market value, say £90,000**, to claim allowances on £90,000. If they elect £90,000, the seller faces a balancing charge on roughly £70,000 (disposal value £90,000 less pool value £20,000), increasing the seller's tax bill; if they elect £1, the buyer gets almost nothing to claim. Show how the parties land on a negotiated figure (often near tax written-down value or a price-adjusted compromise). Tag: 2026/27, §7.

**Example B — the cost of missing the s.201 deadline (HP §7; 2-year limit).**
A buyer completes in May 2026, never makes the election, and discovers the omission in late 2028. The 2-year window from acquisition has closed; no election can be made and the fixtures claim is lost. Quantify: if the fixtures element was ~£80,000, the lost relief at, say, 6%/14% pools or AIA is real money, permanently forfeited (not deferred). Tag: acquisition May 2026, deadline May 2028, §7.

**Example C — the pooling trap (HP §7; fixed-value + pooling requirements).**
A buyer agrees a £70,000 fixtures election, then learns at completion that the previous seller-of-the-seller never pooled the fixtures, so the chain is broken and the current seller has no pooled expenditure to transfer. Use this to show why due diligence on the seller's pooling history matters before exchange, not after. Tag: 2026/27, §7.
