## 3. H2 section outline (target body 2,800-3,500 words)

1. **What "taking it early" means** — drawing your NHS pension before the normal pension age (NPA) of the relevant section, in exchange for a PERMANENT actuarial reduction, because the pension is paid for longer (§2). Distinguish from partial retirement (C4) and ill-health retirement (the existing page, no reduction).
2. **Your normal pension age by section** — set the baseline: 1995 section NPA 60, 2008 section NPA 65, 2015 section NPA linked to State Pension Age (currently 66/67/68). "Early" means before the NPA of the benefits in question, and most dentists now have benefits across more than one section after McCloud (§2, §2.A).
3. **The minimum pension age: how early you can go** — generally 55 (rising under the government timetable), so the earliest draw window for most dentists is 55 up to the section NPA; below that, only ill-health retirement applies (cross-link the existing page).
4. **The actuarial reduction: how much it costs** — the reduction grows the earlier you draw; as a rule of thumb it is in the region of a few per cent per year before NPA (the precise factors are set by the scheme actuary and published by NHSBSA, and differ by section and by years early). State the principle and the rough magnitude, direct to the NHSBSA factors for the exact number, do NOT assert a single fixed percentage. Worked below.
5. **ERRBO: buying out the reduction (2015 scheme)** — explain the Early Retirement Reduction Buy Out: a 2015-scheme member can pre-pay to buy out up to three years of the reduction, taking benefits earlier without the penalty for those years; contributions get tax relief and do not count toward the annual allowance. Note it must be arranged in advance.
6. **The tax impact of drawing early** — the reduced pension is taxable income; if the dentist keeps working (associate or principal), it stacks on top of trading profit and can push the combined income into higher bands or the £100,000 personal-allowance taper; the tax-free lump sum (1995/2008 sections) is separate. Worked below.
7. **Drawing early while still earning: the common dentist scenario** — many dentists draw early but keep doing some clinical sessions; cover the band-stacking, the fact that continued NHS work may or may not be pensionable depending on the route taken, and the abatement rules that can apply to 1995-section members who return. Keep abatement light and section-specific.
8. **Lump sum and commutation** — the 1995/2008 sections provide a lump sum (1995 automatic, 2008/2015 by commutation at 12:1), generally tax-free within the lump-sum allowance; drawing early reduces the lump sum too. Note commutation gives up £1 of annual pension for £12 of lump sum, a separate decision from the early-draw decision.
9. **The annual allowance does not vanish** — if the dentist keeps accruing (e.g. partial-route or continued pensionable work), the annual-allowance test continues; and flexibly accessing any DC AVC triggers the £10,000 MPAA. Cross-link C3.
10. **An outstanding Scheme Pays debt is recovered now** — any earlier annual-allowance charge settled via Scheme Pays is recovered when benefits are paid, so the early-drawn pension is reduced both for the actuarial factor AND for the Scheme Pays debit; flag both so the figure is not a shock. Cross-link C1.
11. **Early retirement vs partial retirement: which decision** — position the two side by side: full early retirement gives the most income now but the largest permanent reduction and ends accrual; partial retirement (C4) keeps you accruing with a smaller drawn slice. Frame as a genuine modelling exercise.
12. **Common errors** — assuming the reduction is temporary (it is permanent); quoting a single fixed reduction percentage (it varies by section and years early); forgetting the early pension is taxable on top of continued earnings; overlooking ERRBO until it is too late to arrange; ignoring an outstanding Scheme Pays debit and abatement on return.

## 5. Worked examples (each tagged to HP §2 + figure + effective date)

**Example A — drawing the 2015 pension early and the rough reduction (HP §2; 2015 NPA = SPA).**
A dentist with a 2015-section NPA of 67 draws benefits at 60, seven years early. As a rule of thumb the reduction is in the region of a few per cent per year (exact factors set by the scheme actuary, published by NHSBSA), so a £20,000 unreduced 2015 pension might pay materially less after the early-retirement factor. State the principle and direct to the NHSBSA factor tables for the precise figure rather than asserting an exact number. Tag: 2025/26, 2015 NPA linked to SPA.

**Example B — the tax stack while still working (HP §2; 2025/26 bands).**
The same dentist draws a reduced pension of, say, £16,000 and continues two days of associate work earning £45,000 profit. Combined taxable income = £61,000, taking them into the higher-rate band; the pension is taxed at the marginal rate on top of the trading profit, not in isolation. Lesson: model the combined figure, not the pension alone. Tag: 2025/26, higher-rate threshold £50,270.

**Example C — ERRBO bought in advance (HP §2; 2015 ERRBO).**
A dentist who plans to retire at 65 against a 2015 NPA of 67 pre-pays ERRBO to buy out two years of reduction, so at 65 they take benefits without the early-retirement penalty for those two years; the ERRBO contributions attracted tax relief and did not count toward the annual allowance. Contrast with a dentist who did not arrange ERRBO and takes the full two-year reduction. Tag: 2025/26, 2015 ERRBO (up to 3 years).
