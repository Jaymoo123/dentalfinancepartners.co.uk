## 3. H2 section outline (target body 2,800-3,500 words)

1. **The allowance that catches what AIA misses** — AIA gives 100% relief on plant and machinery, but the *building itself* (walls, floors, roof, the shell) is not plant and gets no AIA. SBA is the separate relief that picks up that building expenditure at 3% a year. Open by drawing the line: plant = AIA; structure = SBA (§7).
2. **SBA is not AIA: the four differences that matter** — (1) SBA covers *structures*, AIA covers *plant*; (2) SBA is 3% a year over **33⅓ years**, AIA is 100% in year one; (3) SBA has no annual cap, AIA has the £1m cap; (4) SBA needs a written **allowance statement**, AIA does not. State this head-on and link the AIA page (§7).
3. **What qualifies for SBA on a dental practice** — qualifying construction, conversion and renovation of the commercial building: building/construction works, conversion of premises into a surgery, renovation, and qualifying professional fees (architect, design). Used in a qualifying activity (the dental trade) (§7).
4. **What does NOT qualify: land, plant, and residential** — the **cost of the land** is excluded; anything that is **plant/integral features already claimed** (electrical, water, HVAC, fixtures) is excluded (it goes in the pools, see B1); residential parts (a flat above the surgery) are excluded. This carve-out is where dentists double-count or miss spend (§7).
5. **The rate and the period: 3% straight-line over 33⅓ years** — SBA is a flat 3% of the qualifying cost each year (straight-line, not reducing balance), giving relief over **33 and one-third years** (§7; CAA 2001 Part 2A). Contrast the slow, steady SBA with the front-loaded AIA so the reader understands the trade-off.
6. **The 29 October 2018 gateway** — SBA only applies where the construction contract was entered into (or the building first brought into use / expenditure incurred) **on or after 29 October 2018**. Premises older than that, with no qualifying post-2018 works, get no SBA on the original structure. State the date precisely (§7).
7. **The allowance statement: no statement, no claim** — a buyer/claimant must hold a written **allowance statement** recording the qualifying cost and the date the building was first brought into use; on a sale the allowance passes to the buyer who needs that statement to continue claiming. Flag this as the SBA equivalent of the s.198 trap (cross-link B2's "get the paperwork in the deal" theme).
8. **Buying premises with existing SBA: the statement passes to you** — when you buy a building already in its SBA period, the remaining 3%-a-year allowance transfers to you for the rest of the 33⅓ years, but only if you obtain the seller's allowance statement. Due-diligence item (cross-link the due-diligence checklist).
9. **SBA and the disposal/CGT interaction (kept light)** — SBA claimed reduces the building's base cost for CGT on a later sale (a clawback through the capital gain, not a balancing charge). Note it, link the practice-sale content, do not go deep.
10. **Squat builds and major conversions: where SBA earns its keep** — a squat practice built or converted from scratch, or a major premises extension, is the classic SBA case: a large structural spend that AIA cannot reach but SBA can relieve over time. Cross-link the squat-vs-existing page.
11. **How SBA, the pools and the CGS all sit on one refurbishment** — a single large refurbishment can generate SBA (structure), main-pool and special-rate plant (B1), and a VAT Capital Goods Scheme item (§6.B) all at once. One spend, three regimes, different taxes. Keep the summary tight and link out.
12. **Common errors** — claiming AIA on the building structure (wrong relief); missing SBA entirely on a post-2018 build/conversion; failing to obtain the allowance statement on a purchase; double-counting integral features in both SBA and the special-rate pool; forgetting the land is excluded.

## 5. Worked examples (each tagged to HP §7 + figure + effective date)

**Example A — a squat build split between SBA and the plant pools (HP §7; SBA 3%, AIA £1m).**
A dentist builds out a squat practice in 2026/27 for £400,000 (VAT-exclusive), excluding the £150,000 land. Apportionment: building structure (shell, partitions, construction works, professional fees) £230,000; plant and integral features (chairs, X-ray, electrical, water, HVAC) £170,000. The £170,000 of plant goes to AIA (100% now, directed at the 6% items first, see B1). The £230,000 structure gets **SBA at 3% = £6,900 a year** for 33⅓ years. The £150,000 land gets nothing. Tag: 2026/27, §7.

**Example B — SBA vs AIA on the same £230,000, to show the trade-off (HP §7).**
Contrast: if the £230,000 structure could somehow be plant (it cannot), AIA would relieve all £230,000 now. As SBA, it relieves £6,900 a year. Over the first five years SBA gives £34,500; AIA would have given £230,000 in year one. This is the cost of the spend being structure not plant, and why a correct B1 pool-split (maximising what is genuinely plant) is worth real money. Tag: 2026/27, §7.

**Example C — buying premises mid-SBA-period (HP §7; allowance statement).**
A buyer acquires a practice freehold in 2026 whose structure was built in 2020 with £180,000 of qualifying SBA cost. Roughly 6 of the 33⅓ years have run; the buyer obtains the seller's allowance statement and continues claiming **£5,400 a year (3% of £180,000)** for the remaining ~27⅓ years. Without the statement, the buyer cannot claim. Tag: built 2020, bought 2026, §7.
