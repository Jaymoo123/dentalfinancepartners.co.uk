## 3. H2 section outline (target body 2,800-3,500 words)

1. **Why a dental practice is partially exempt** — exempt dental care (Sch 9 Group 7, §6) plus standard-rated supplies (cosmetic-only work, retail product sales, any taxable rents) means input VAT is only partly recoverable; set the scene that this page is about reclaiming VAT, not charging it.
2. **The three buckets every cost falls into** — directly attributable to taxable supplies (recoverable in full), directly attributable to exempt supplies (irrecoverable), and residual/overhead (rent, utilities, reception, accountancy, shared equipment) that must be apportioned. This is the single concept most owners get wrong.
3. **The standard method: the turnover calculation** — recoverable residual % = value of taxable supplies / value of all supplies, rounded up to the next whole percent (2 decimal places only above £400,000 residual input tax a month, §6); worked through with a dental income split.
4. **The de minimis rule: when you can recover everything** — exempt input tax not more than £625 a month on average (£7,500 a year) AND not more than 50% of total input tax means the practice is treated as fully taxable and recovers 100%; explain both limbs must be met (§6).
5. **The simplified de minimis tests** — the two HMRC simplifications (test one: total input tax ≤ £625/month and exempt supplies ≤ 50% of total supplies; test two: total input tax less directly-attributable-taxable ≤ £625/month and ≤ 50%) so a small practice can skip the full calculation in-year.
6. **When the standard method is unfair, and the special method (PESM)** — if the turnover split does not reflect actual cost usage (e.g. a small cosmetic line that consumes a large, capital-heavy room), apply to HMRC in writing for a Partial Exemption Special Method based on floor area, staff time, room hours or transaction counts; HMRC must approve before use, and a "fair and reasonable" declaration is required.
7. **The provisional-then-final shape of the year** — recover provisionally each quarter on the current period's figures (or the prior year's recovery % if agreed), then true up at year end.
8. **The annual adjustment** — recalculate recovery across the whole VAT year (the "longer period") so seasonal income swings even out; the difference is repaid to or reclaimed from HMRC on the first return after the year end; this is also where the de minimis test is re-run for the year as a whole (§6, VAT Regs 1995 regs 99-111).
9. **Capital items sit outside this and run for years** — flag that large property/refurbishment spend over £250,000 is governed by the Capital Goods Scheme, a separate 10-year adjustment, and cross-link the dedicated page rather than covering it here.
10. **Record-keeping that survives an HMRC review** — coded income streams, an attribution log for every significant cost, the special-method approval letter, and the annual-adjustment workings retained for six years.
11. **Common errors** — treating all overheads as fully recoverable; forgetting the annual adjustment; applying a special method without written HMRC approval; missing that the de minimis test is an annual test not just a quarterly one; ignoring exempt input tax when product retail is small but real.
12. **When to get advice** — a growing cosmetic or product line, a planned fit-out, or a first HMRC partial-exemption query are the trigger points.

## 5. Worked examples (each tagged to HP §6 + figure + effective date)

**Example A — the standard method residual recovery (HP §6; £90,000 threshold context, 2025/26).**
A registered single-site practice has £620,000 exempt dental income and £80,000 standard-rated cosmetic/product income (total £700,000, all VAT-exclusive). Residual recovery % = 80,000 / 700,000 = 11.43%, rounded up to 12%. On £40,000 of residual input VAT (rent, utilities, reception, accountancy), the practice recovers 12% = £4,800. Directly-attributable-taxable VAT (e.g. on cosmetic-only consumables) is recovered in full on top; directly-attributable-exempt VAT is nil-recovered. Tag: 2025/26.

**Example B — the de minimis rule gives full recovery (HP §6; £625/month and 50% limbs).**
A mostly-NHS practice has total input VAT of £18,000 for the year, of which only £6,000 is attributable to (or residual against) exempt supplies. £6,000 / 12 = £500 a month, which is under £625, and £6,000 is 33% of £18,000, under 50%. Both limbs met, so the practice is treated as fully taxable for the year and recovers the whole £18,000. Show how adding a larger cosmetic room could break the £625 limb and flip the result. Tag: 2025/26.

**Example C — a special method beats the standard method (HP §6; PESM, fair-and-reasonable).**
A practice runs a dedicated implant/cosmetic suite that occupies 30% of the floor area but generates only 11% of turnover. The turnover-based standard method would under-recover the VAT on that room's running costs. An agreed floor-area special method recovers ~30% of the suite's apportionable overheads, a materially better and defensible result. Note HMRC must approve in writing first and a fair-and-reasonable declaration is required. Tag: 2025/26, VAT Regs 1995 regs 99-111 (§6).
