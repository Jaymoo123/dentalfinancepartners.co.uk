## 3. H2 section outline (target body 2,800-3,500 words)

1. **Why one fit-out invoice is really three tax categories** — a surgery refit is never a single allowance: it splits into main-rate-pool plant, special-rate-pool integral features, and non-qualifying building structure (which may instead reach SBA, see B4). Getting the split right decides how fast relief comes (§7).
2. **The main-rate pool: 18% now, 14% from April 2026** — general plant and machinery (chairs, cabinetry, delivery units, X-ray, suction, autoclaves, IT) sits in the main pool at the writing-down rate. State the rate change clearly: 18% WDA reduces to **14%** for expenditure relieved from 1 April 2026 (companies) / 6 April 2026 (sole traders and partnerships), with a hybrid rate across a straddling period (§7; CAA 2001 s.56). This is the rate that bites only on spend *above* the AIA.
3. **The special-rate pool: 6% integral features** — the slow pool. Integral features in a dental fit-out: the electrical and lighting installation, cold and hot water systems, space and water heating, air conditioning and mechanical ventilation, lifts. These attract only **6%** WDA on a reducing balance (§7; CAA 2001 ss.104A-104D). In a surgery refit these are a large slice (compressed air, suction, LEV extraction wiring, surgery-grade lighting, HVAC).
4. **What is NOT a capital allowance at all: the building structure** — walls, floors, ceilings, doors, general decoration and the shell are not plant; they get no plant-and-machinery allowance and instead may qualify for SBA at 3% (cross-link B4). Drawing this line wrong (claiming AIA on building works) is the classic dental error.
5. **The order that matters: direct AIA at the 6% items first** — AIA gives 100% relief up to £1m and can be allocated to *either* pool. Because the special-rate pool only writes down at 6% a year (a ~38-year drag), AIA is worth most when pointed at the 6% integral features first, leaving the faster 18%/14% main-pool items to write down. This is the single most valuable planning move in the article (§7).
6. **A line-by-line dental fit-out, categorised** — a practical table walking a refit invoice item by item into main pool / special rate / structure / SBA: dental chair and delivery unit (main), cabinetry and worktops (main), surgery lighting panels (main, but borderline if hard-wired into the lighting system), electrical installation and rewire (special), water and waste plumbing (special), HVAC and air handling (special), compressor and suction (main, free-standing plant), partition walls and flooring (structure / SBA).
7. **The 40% first-year allowance and full expensing as an alternative to pooling** — for spend above the £1m AIA, a company can use **full expensing** (100% on new main-rate plant) and the **50% FYA** on new special-rate integral features; from 1 January 2026 a permanent **40% FYA** also applies to new main-rate plant for both companies and unincorporated businesses (excludes second-hand assets and cars). Note these only matter above the AIA ceiling for a typical single-site practice (§7; cross-link B5 and the full-expensing page).
8. **Why a quantity surveyor or specialist apportionment beats a builder's invoice** — a builder bills "fit-out: £180,000" in one line; HMRC relief depends on a just and reasonable apportionment of that figure across the categories. A specialist capital allowances apportionment routinely surfaces hidden integral features that a single-line invoice buries (cross-reference the case-study angle).
9. **The Capital Goods Scheme shadow on a big fit-out** — for a partly-exempt practice (mixed NHS and cosmetic), the same fit-out spend over £250,000 also enters the VAT Capital Goods Scheme; the capital-allowance split and the CGS run on the same invoice but are different taxes (cross-link the CGS page). Keep light.
10. **Disposal: balancing charges and balancing allowances** — when a pooled asset is later sold or scrapped, sale proceeds are deducted from the pool; if the pool goes negative a **balancing charge** claws relief back, if it goes positive on cessation a **balancing allowance** is given. Flag that AIA-relieved items still carry a disposal value (a common surprise on a later refit or sale). Cross-link §4 (price split on a practice sale).
11. **Records and the apportionment trail you must keep** — keep the costed schedule that supports the pool split (invoices, the QS apportionment, the asset register), because the split is what HMRC tests on enquiry.
12. **Common errors** — claiming AIA on building structure; dumping the whole fit-out into the main pool and missing the 6% items entirely (or vice versa); pointing AIA at fast 18%/14% items and leaving the slow 6% items to crawl; ignoring the post-April-2026 14% main rate when modelling spend above the AIA; forgetting the disposal value on AIA-relieved kit.

## 5. Worked examples (each tagged to HP §7 + figure + effective date)

**Example A — a £150,000 surgery fit-out split across the pools (HP §7; AIA £1m, 6% special rate, 18%/14% main rate).**
A practice spends £150,000 (VAT-exclusive) refitting two surgeries in the 2026/27 period. A just-and-reasonable apportionment gives: main-rate plant (chairs, cabinetry, X-ray, compressor) £70,000; special-rate integral features (electrical, water, HVAC) £55,000; building structure (partitions, flooring, decoration) £25,000. AIA covers £125,000 of qualifying plant in full. Direct AIA at the **£55,000 special-rate items first** (they would otherwise crawl at 6%), then the remaining £70,000 AIA at the main-rate plant: all £125,000 of qualifying plant gets 100% relief this year. The £25,000 structure gets no plant allowance but may reach SBA at 3% (cross-link B4). Tag: 2026/27, §7.

**Example B — why the AIA direction matters when spend exceeds £1m (HP §7; 6% vs 14% drag).**
Show the counterfactual at scale: if a group fit-out of £1.2m forces some spend out of AIA, putting the *14% main-pool* items outside AIA loses far less than leaving the *6% integral features* outside it. Demonstrate with the £55,000 of integral features: relieved at 6%, year-one relief is £3,300 and it takes ~38 years to write down; the same £55,000 inside AIA is relieved in full immediately. Tag: 2026/27, §7.

**Example C — the 14% rate change on the residual main pool (HP §7; s.56 18%→14%).**
A company with a March year-end has £40,000 of main-pool spend that did not fit inside AIA in an accounting period straddling 1 April 2026. The WDA on that pool is a hybrid of 18% (pre-April) and 14% (post-April), apportioned by days. Show the hybrid calculation and contrast with a full-14% year, so the reader sees the slower future relief on above-AIA spend. Tag: companies 1 Apr 2026 / income tax 6 Apr 2026, §7.
