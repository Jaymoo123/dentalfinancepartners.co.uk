## 3. H2 section outline (target body 2,800-3,500 words)

1. **One allowance, two sources** — the headline: the £60,000 annual allowance is a single limit that covers EVERYTHING added to your pensions in the tax year, so your NHS pension growth and any SIPP/personal-pension contributions are ADDED TOGETHER, not measured separately (§2.B). This is the misconception the page corrects.
2. **The NHS side: pension input amount, not contributions** — for the NHS defined-benefit scheme, what counts is the pension INPUT AMOUNT (the capitalised increase in your promised pension over the year), not the contributions deducted from your pay. Recap briefly and link the existing pages rather than re-deriving the 16× formula (§2.B).
3. **The private side: gross contributions** — for a SIPP or personal pension (defined contribution), what counts is the GROSS contribution paid in (your payment plus the basic-rate relief added by the provider, plus any employer contribution). Explain the grossing-up so the reader counts the right number.
4. **Adding the two: the aggregate test** — walk the addition: NHS input amount + gross SIPP contributions = total pension input for the year, tested against £60,000 (or your tapered allowance). Worked below. This is the core of the page.
5. **Why high-earning dentists get caught** — a dentist with long NHS service can already have a £40,000 to £60,000 NHS input amount before adding a penny privately, so even a modest SIPP top-up tips them over. The instinct "I can pay in up to £60,000" is wrong because the NHS has usually already used most of it.
6. **The taper shrinks the shared allowance** — for high earners the £60,000 may already be tapered toward the £10,000 floor (threshold income > £200,000 AND adjusted income > £260,000), so the room for ANY private contribution is far smaller; link the taper page (it owns the taper detail, §2.B).
7. **The relevant-earnings cap on tax relief (a separate limit)** — distinguish the annual allowance from the tax-relief limit: personal contributions only get relief up to 100% of relevant UK earnings (capped at the annual allowance). Make clear these are two different ceilings a dentist must satisfy at once.
8. **Carry forward across both sources** — unused allowance from the previous three years (current year first) is shared across NHS and private inputs, so a dentist with headroom can still make a larger SIPP contribution; this is the main planning lever and links the AA page (§2.B).
9. **The money purchase annual allowance trap** — if the dentist has already FLEXIBLY ACCESSED a DC pot (e.g. taken taxable income from a SIPP), the £10,000 money purchase annual allowance caps further DC contributions and removes carry forward for the DC element; flag this for dentists drawing private pensions while still working (cross-link C4/C5).
10. **Working out the combined position before the tax year ends** — practical sequence: estimate the NHS input amount (from the NHS statement, often late), gross up any planned SIPP contribution, add them, compare to the (possibly tapered) allowance, apply carry forward, and size any SIPP contribution to fit. Do this BEFORE making the contribution, not after.
11. **If you go over: the charge and how to pay it** — the excess is taxed at your marginal rate; you can pay it personally or, for the NHS-attributable part, via Scheme Pays; the SIPP-attributable part generally cannot use NHS Scheme Pays. Cross-link C1.
12. **Common errors** — treating the £60,000 as available for private contributions on top of the NHS growth; using net rather than gross SIPP figures; ignoring the taper; forgetting the MPAA after flexibly accessing a DC pot; making the SIPP contribution before checking the NHS statement.

## 5. Worked examples (each tagged to HP §2.B + figure + effective date)

**Example A — NHS growth already eats most of the allowance (HP §2.B; £60,000 AA).**
A dentist has an NHS pension input amount of £48,000 in 2025/26. They plan a £15,000 gross SIPP contribution, thinking they are "well under £60,000". Aggregate input = £48,000 + £15,000 = £63,000, which is £3,000 OVER the £60,000 allowance. With no carry forward, the £3,000 excess is charged at 40% = £1,200. Lesson: the SIPP room was only £12,000, not £15,000. Tag: 2025/26, AA £60,000.

**Example B — carry forward rescues a larger SIPP (HP §2.B; 3-year carry forward).**
The same dentist has £20,000 of unused allowance carried forward from the previous three years. Aggregate input £63,000 minus £60,000 = £3,000 excess, fully absorbed by the £20,000 carry forward, so no charge, and they could have contributed up to £32,000 to the SIPP before a charge bit. Lesson: check carry forward before sizing the contribution. Tag: 2025/26, carry forward 3 prior years.

**Example C — the taper plus a SIPP (HP §2.B; taper £200k/£260k to £10k floor).**
A principal with threshold income £210,000 and adjusted income £290,000 has a tapered allowance of £45,000 (£30,000 over £260,000 ÷ 2 = £15,000 reduction from £60,000). Their NHS input amount is £42,000. That leaves only £3,000 of room for any SIPP contribution before a charge. A planned £10,000 SIPP top-up would push £7,000 over. Lesson: the taper can shrink the shared allowance below the NHS growth alone. Tag: 2025/26, tapered allowance £45,000.

**Example D — the MPAA after flexibly accessing a SIPP (HP §2.B; MPAA £10,000).**
A dentist drawing taxable income from a SIPP while still working has triggered the £10,000 money purchase annual allowance. Any further DC/SIPP contribution is capped at £10,000 with no carry forward for that DC element, even though their overall (NHS-inclusive) allowance might appear higher. Lesson: flexibly accessing a DC pot changes the rules for future private contributions. Tag: 2025/26, MPAA £10,000.
