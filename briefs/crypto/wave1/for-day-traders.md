- HP 16: trader status usually a BAD outcome (income up to 45% plus Class 4 NIC vs 24% CGT); mainly matters for loss-relief edge cases. https://www.gov.uk/income-tax-rates and https://www.gov.uk/hmrc-internal-manuals/cryptoassets-manual/crypto20250
- HP 17: day-trading in shares/forex/CFDs uses the same badges of trade; spread-betting winnings generally outside tax (gambling), losses unrelievable. https://www.gov.uk/hmrc-internal-manuals/business-income-manual/bim22015
- HP 18: personal foreign-exchange gains can be chargeable gains; the personal-spending exemption is narrow. https://www.gov.uk/hmrc-internal-manuals/capital-gains-manual/cg78300

Consistency rules: UK default. Trader status framed as usually undesirable, never as a prize. CGT rate band-split. Spread-betting: winnings out of tax AND losses unrelievable (state both sides, never sell it as a tax-free win with relievable downside). No investment advice.

## Hallucination danger zones

- Do NOT frame trader status as a goal or a benefit (HP 16). It is usually worse; it mainly helps in loss-relief edge cases only.
- Do not claim high frequency alone makes someone a trader (HP 15).
- Spread-betting: winnings generally outside tax but losses are equally unrelievable (HP 17). Never present it as tax-free upside with usable downside.
- CGT rate is band-split, never flat 18% (HP 2).
- The status checker is a scenario tool, not a binding HMRC determination; it ends at "speak to us".
- Forex personal-spending exemption is narrow, not general (HP 18).
- No credential claims, no named expert. No em-dashes. No investment or price views.
