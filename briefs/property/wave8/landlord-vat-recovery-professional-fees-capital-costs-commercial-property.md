- **Framing differentiator (Stage 2, 2026-05-25):**

> Commercial-property landlord input-tax recovery — the three-step framework for evaluating recoverability across the property lifecycle. Page walks: (a) **The statutory framework** — VATA 1994 s.24 defines "input tax" as VAT on supply of goods or services TO a taxable person for the purposes of any business carried on by them. VATA 1994 s.26 grants the deduction right where the input tax is attributable to taxable (standard / reduced / zero-rated) supplies in the course of business; (b) **The three-step recoverability decision tree** — **Step 1: Is the underlying property in a taxable-supply state?** Commercial property opted to tax (or about to be — see C1 pillar) generates taxable rents = full recovery on directly-attributable input tax. Residential property generates exempt rents (Sch 9 Gr 1) = no recovery on directly-attributable input tax. Mixed portfolio = partial-exemption apportionment (see C5). Self-storage = standard-rated by default (see C8) = full recovery. **Step 2: Is the cost directly attributable or residual?** Directly attributable to taxable supplies = full recovery (e.g. legal fees for the opted-property purchase contract). Directly attributable to exempt supplies = no recovery (e.g. legal fees for an unopted residential portfolio acquisition). Residual / mixed-attribution costs (e.g. headline professional fees on a mixed-portfolio acquisition) = partial-exemption apportionment via standard method (reg 101) or special method (reg 102 — see C5). **Step 3: Is the claim within the time window?** Per SI 1995/2518 reg 29 — claims for input tax must be made on a return for the prescribed accounting period in which the input tax credit is attributable, subject to the 4-year time limit (the cap is in VATA 1994 s.80 + reg 29 architecture); (c) **The professional-fee categorisation** — three cost buckets across the lifecycle: (i) **acquisition fees** (legal, surveyor, financing arrangement): recoverable IF the acquired property is to be put to taxable use (opted commercial / self-storage / reduced-rate-converted-then-sold-zero-rated). Pre-opt acquisition fees on commercial property where the option is granted POST-acquisition are typically recoverable provided the intent to opt is documented and the option is in force when supplies start (HMRC accepts intention-based recovery); (ii) **capital improvement works**: recoverable subject to CGS adjustment (SI 1995/2518 regs 112-116) where capital expenditure ≥£250k. CGS adjusts recoverable input tax over 10 intervals based on actual taxable-use ratio. Mid-life change of use (opt revocation; tenant switch from commercial to residential) triggers clawback in respect of remaining intervals; (iii) **operating-period professional fees** (lettings agent commissions, property management, insurance brokerage, accountancy, legal on tenant disputes): recoverable on the property's current taxable-supply state. Mixed-portfolio operating fees typically fall residual = partial-exemption apportionment; (d) **The pre-opt fee trap** — landlord buys commercial property for £2m + £400k VAT (vendor opted). Landlord has not yet registered for VAT and has not opted to tax. Pre-registration recovery available within 4 years for services (6 months) / 4 years for goods retained per Wave 5 page — but landlord must register, opt to tax, and intend taxable use. If landlord intends to rent residential (Sch 9 Gr 1 exempt), the £400k acquisition VAT is non-recoverable. Often missed at the structuring stage; (e) **The opt-to-tax recovery cascade** — opting commercial property post-acquisition allows recovery of: (i) acquisition VAT (subject to pre-registration windows); (ii) capital improvement VAT (subject to CGS adjustments); (iii) ongoing professional-fee VAT (full recovery if directly attributable to opted-property rents). The cascade is the typical reason landlords opt — but balance against tenant-impact (commercial tenant in partial-exemption position cannot fully recover 20% on rent); (f) **The 4-year claim window for missed input tax** — reg 29 + s.80 architecture allows late claims for under-recovered input tax on a current return for items not previously claimed, subject to 4 years from end of prescribed accounting period in which the right to deduct first arose. Useful catch-up route for landlords who missed pre-registration or early-period recoveries. Cross-references C1 (option-to-tax framework — Step 1 enabler), C5 (partial-exemption residual apportionment — Step 2 sub-routine), C9 (registration as gating step — must be registered to claim).
