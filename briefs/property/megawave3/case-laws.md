This is a case-law INDEX page — the "statutory anchor" function here is structurally different from a substantive page. The case-law citations themselves ARE the anchors. Each case must be verbatim-verified at write time per §16.35 — wrong citation form, wrong year, wrong court name, or wrong holding wording is the typical failure mode for case-law index pages. ## Framing differentiator (anti-templating, anti-cannibalisation)

The competitor template for "property tax case laws" pages is a chronological list of 20-40 cases with one-line summaries, no decision-grouping, no holding-detail, no court-level information, and no operative consequence. Useful for SEO keyword-stuffing; useful for nobody making a tax decision. The article-shape collapses the substantive value of case-law into a directory.

This page reverses that with two design choices:

1. **Cases grouped by the tax question they decide** — SDLT mixed-use rate-line; SDLT uninhabitable test; BPR investment line; badges of trade; PPR family-home boundaries; salaried-member rules; reasonable excuse; discovery assessment; CIS employment-vs-self-employment; VAT TOGC + option-to-tax; trust + property + IHT. The grouping is the differentiator — a landlord searching for "the case behind why my granny annexe is GROB" can find it (Hood + Buzzoni); a landlord searching for "the case behind why my paddock-as-garden SDLT residential-vs-mixed-use was disputed" can find it (Hyman / Goodfellow / Faiers trilogy).

## Key questions this page must answer

1. **Why does case-law matter for property tax decisions when the statute is the primary source?** — Statute sets the framework; case-law tells the landlord what HMRC + tribunals interpret the words to MEAN. The badges-of-trade cluster (Marson v Morton five-case cluster) tells the landlord what "trading" looks like even though the statute does not enumerate. The SDLT mixed-use trilogy tells the landlord what "non-residential character" looks like in practice. The Pawson decision tells the landlord that ordinary BTL letting is INVESTMENT for BPR even where the landlord is active in management. Without case-law grounding, the statute is a sentence — landlords act on the case-law interpretation, not the statutory text alone.
2. **The case-law table (decision-grouped, table-formatted)** — Sessions build a single integrated table with columns: (a) Tax question; (b) Case name; (c) Court level (UKSC > CA > UT > FTT > PC where applicable); (d) Year + citation; (e) Operative holding (one sentence); (f) What changed because of it (one line); (g) Forward-link to substantive deep-dive page. **Critical:** every case citation verbatim-verified at write per §16.35. The table is the page's primary content — sessions must NOT collapse into prose paragraphs that lose the lookup-density.
3. **SDLT mixed-use rate-line — the trilogy** — *Hyman* [2021] UKUT (paddock-as-garden held residential, not mixed-use); *Goodfellow* [2019] UKFTT (woodland adjacent to garden held residential, not mixed-use); *Faiers* [2023] UKFTT (commercial-use claim rejected on non-residential character test). The trilogy narrowed the mixed-use claim landlords had used to pay non-residential SDLT rates on rural acquisitions. **What changed:** HMRC's enquiry approach hardened post-trilogy; landlords on borderline mixed-use claims now face an uphill burden of proof. §1.J pre-locked.
4. **SDLT uninhabitable property — Bewley** — *PN Bewley Ltd v HMRC* [2019] UKFTT (uninhabitable derelict bungalow held non-residential for SDLT purposes — landlord paid non-residential rates not residential). **What changed:** opened a narrow uninhabitability route for SDLT mitigation on derelict-property purchases; HMRC scrutiny since has narrowed the practical scope. §1.C pre-locked. Sessions must NOT over-claim Bewley — it is fact-specific to genuinely uninhabitable property, not "needs renovation".
5. **BPR investment line — Pawson** — *Pawson v HMRC* [2013] UKUT (TCC) — ordinary buy-to-let letting held INVESTMENT for BPR purposes, even where the landlord is active in management; therefore no BPR available. Limits the BPR claim to genuine FHL-with-services or property-as-trade businesses. **What changed:** essentially closed the BPR-for-BTL route as a planning lever; only the genuine-trade subset (FHL-with-substantial-services + property-development-trade) qualifies. §22.1 pre-locked.
6. **Badges of trade — the five-case cluster** — *Marson v Morton* [1986] (the nine badges canonical statement); *Iswera v IRC* [1965] (PC — single transaction can be trading); *Salt v Chamberlain* [1979] (frequency line); *Page v Lowther* [1983] (single property flip held to be trading); *Pickford v Quirke* (1927) (repeated transactions establish trade). **What changed:** these cases sit alongside Part 8ZB CTA 2010 / Part 9A ITA 2007 (Finance Act 2016 transactions in UK land regime) — the statutory regime catches the same trading patterns but the case-law shapes border-case classification. §28.5 pre-locked.
7. **PPR family-home boundaries — Lewis / Goodwin / Higgins** — *Lewis v Lady Rook* [1992] (CA — "garden or grounds" of a dwelling has a permitted area + necessary-for-enjoyment test; PPR does not extend to large grounds beyond the limits); *Goodwin v Curtis* [1998] (CA — minimum-residence period needs quality of occupation, not just calendar duration); *Higgins v HMRC* [2019] EWCA Civ (period of ownership = period of beneficial ownership, so a flat-not-yet-built has no "ownership" until beneficial completion; PPR clock starts on beneficial completion). **What changed:** Higgins corrected HMRC's earlier position; PPR claims on off-plan completions are now grounded on Higgins. Sessions cross-reference §5 CGT residential.
8. **Salaried-member rules — BlueCrest 2024** — ***BlueCrest Capital Management v HMRC* [2024] UKSC 33** — Condition B "significant influence over the affairs of the partnership" does NOT require influence over the whole partnership; influence over a significant PART can be enough. NARROWED the safe-harbour reading the FTT / UT had taken. **What changed:** matrix-silo'd member roles may not qualify post-BlueCrest; LLPs reviewing member structures must re-test against the narrowed Condition B reading. §11.C.X.2 pre-locked.
9. **Reasonable excuse — Perrin / Martland penalties framework** — *Perrin v HMRC* [2018] UKUT (4-step reasonable-excuse framework: identify the relevant facts; identify the relevant law; ask whether what the taxpayer did satisfied the reasonable-excuse test; consider whether the taxpayer remedied the failure without unreasonable delay). *Martland v HMRC* [2018] UKUT (late-appeal three-stage framework — length + reason + balance). **What changed:** Perrin / Martland are the operative test-frameworks for penalty + late-appeal challenges across HMRC penalty regime. Cross-reference §27.2 + §27.4 + §27.7.
10. **Discovery assessment + competent officer — Tooth 2021** — *Tooth v HMRC* [2021] UKSC — Supreme Court narrowed the "stale" discovery framework taxpayers had relied on for protection; discovery is not stale merely because HMRC took time to act on information already available. **What changed:** taxpayer protection on stale discovery is narrower than pre-Tooth understanding; discovery assessment time limits (TMA s.34 + s.36 + §27.1) apply on more cases. §27.1 pre-locked.
11. **GROB + family-home arrangements — Hood / Buzzoni** — *Hood v HMRC* [2018] EWCA Civ (granny-annexe + retention-of-benefit arrangements held GROB); *Buzzoni v HMRC* [2013] EWCA Civ (GROB tracing through corporate / trust arrangements). **What changed:** narrowed the planning routes around family-home gifting; "gift-with-strings-attached" arrangements caught even where indirect benefit is the link. §22.11 pre-locked.
12. **The methodology section — why some cases are NOT on the list** — Sessions briefly justify selection: (a) cases must LAND on landlord-relevant tax decisions; (b) cases must have current operative force (not superseded by later statute or higher-court ruling); (c) cases are limited to UK courts + tribunals (FTT / UT / CA / SC / UKSC + PC where applicable); (d) the list is curated not exhaustive — landlords with niche fact-patterns may need cases not on this list; (e) sessions verify each case at BAILII / Justis at write per §16.35.

## FAQ expansion (RUN session polishes prose; 10-12 FAQs target)

1. **Q: Is Pawson still the leading BPR case for BTL landlords?**
   A: Yes. *Pawson v HMRC* [2013] UKUT 050 (TCC) remains the operative authority on the investment-vs-trade line for BPR purposes on landlord property businesses. Ordinary BTL letting is INVESTMENT for BPR, even where the landlord is active in management; no BPR is available. The Pawson line has been reinforced by subsequent FTT decisions and is the working assumption for BPR planning on landlord portfolios. BPR is restricted to genuine TRADE subsets — FHL-with-substantial-services and property-development-trade are the operative qualifying lines, both requiring fact-pattern-specific analysis.

2. **Q: Does Bewley work for properties that just need renovation?**
   A: No. *PN Bewley Ltd v HMRC* [2019] UKFTT 65 (TC) is fact-specific to genuinely UNINHABITABLE derelict property — not property requiring renovation. The Bewley uninhabitable test is narrow: structural defects making the property incapable of being used as a dwelling at the date of acquisition. HMRC scrutiny post-Bewley has further narrowed the practical scope; a property requiring renovation but capable of being lived in (even at basic standard) typically does NOT qualify for non-residential SDLT rates under Bewley. Sessions must NOT over-claim Bewley.

3. **Q: What does BlueCrest mean for my LLP partnership?**
   A: ***BlueCrest Capital Management v HMRC* [2024] UKSC 33** narrowed Condition B of the salaried-member regime (ITA 2007 s.863A). Pre-BlueCrest, FTT/UT had read Condition B "significant influence over the affairs of the partnership" to require whole-LLP influence. Post-BlueCrest, the Supreme Court held that influence over a SIGNIFICANT PART of the LLP's affairs can be enough. Practical implication: matrix-silo'd member roles (e.g., a senior partner managing only one office or one practice area) may not clear Condition B safe harbour and may be reclassified as salaried members. LLPs should re-test member structures against the narrowed Condition B reading post-BlueCrest.

4. **Q: Is the SDLT mixed-use trilogy fully closed or still open for new cases?**
   A: The trilogy (*Hyman*, *Goodfellow*, *Faiers*) is operationally CLOSED for the borderline residential-vs-mixed-use claims most landlords were running. HMRC's enquiry approach has hardened; FTT decisions post-trilogy have consistently followed the restrictive reading; no UT or CA-level overturning has emerged. New cases at FTT level continue but rarely succeed on the same fact-pattern as the trilogy targets (paddock-as-garden; woodland adjacent to garden; ancillary land claimed as commercial-use). The page monitors post-2025 cases as watchpoint per Stage 2 research target list.

5. **Q: Where can I read these cases for free?**
   A: **BAILII** (https://www.bailii.org) is the primary free access point for UK tribunal + court decisions including FTT, UT, CA, SC, UKSC, and CJEU. The tribunals' own decisions database (https://www.gov.uk/tax-and-chancery-tribunal-decisions) hosts FTT and UT decisions. Older case-law (pre-2003 typically) may not be on BAILII; the standard legal databases (Westlaw UK, LexisLibrary) carry the full archive but require subscription. For the cases in the table, BAILII covers the operative post-2003 decisions; the older badges-of-trade cluster (Pickford v Quirke 1927; Iswera 1965; Marson v Morton 1986) is in standard legal databases.

6. **Q: Why isn't *Cherry* (or another case I've heard about) in the table?**
   A: The table is CURATED, not exhaustive. The selection rule: cases that land on landlord-relevant tax decisions with current operative force. Cases that are superseded, fact-specific without broader operative meaning, or that don't engage the operative landlord tax decisions are excluded. If you have a fact-pattern relying on a case not in the table, talk to us — the case may still be relevant to your specific situation; the table is the practitioner-relevance shortlist, not the directory.

7. **Q: How often does the case-law table need updating?**
   A: Continuously, in principle. The page is monitored under the §27.1 / §27.2 / §11.C.X.2 / §22.1 cluster-update watch — any new UT or CA-level decision affecting one of the operative authorities triggers a page update. RUN session adds new cases at write time where they meet the table's selection rule. Post-2025 case-law to watch: follow-on BlueCrest FTT/UT decisions; follow-on SDLT mixed-use cases at UT or CA level; follow-on Pawson-line BPR cases.

8. **Q: What is the difference between a UKSC case and a UKFTT case in operational force?**
   A: UKSC (UK Supreme Court) — highest court; binding on all lower courts and tribunals. CA (Court of Appeal) — binding on UT, FTT, and lower courts (subject to UKSC overrule). UT (Upper Tribunal) — binding on FTT (subject to CA / UKSC overrule). FTT (First-tier Tribunal) — first-instance; binding on the parties but only persuasive (not binding) on other FTT panels. Operationally for landlord planning: UKSC and CA are the strongest authorities; UT decisions are operative for FTT-level disputes; FTT decisions are useful for understanding HMRC's approach + likely outcomes but can be challenged by subsequent FTT panels on different facts.

9. **Q: Does *Tooth* mean HMRC can always rely on discovery?**
   A: No, but HMRC's position is stronger than it was pre-Tooth. *Tooth v HMRC* [2021] UKSC 17 narrowed the "stale" discovery framework taxpayers had relied on for protection: discovery is not stale merely because HMRC took time to act on information already available. The discovery assessment time limits (TMA s.34 ordinary 4-year; s.36 carelessness 6-year; s.36 deliberate 20-year) still apply; the question Tooth addressed was whether discovery within the time-limit window could be defeated by an earlier window of HMRC awareness. Post-Tooth, the answer is generally no. Sessions cross-reference §27.1 for the discovery framework + practical implications for landlord enquiry defence.

10. **Q: Does *Ready Mixed Concrete* apply to CIS subcontractor arrangements?**
    A: Yes, by cross-reference. The *Ready Mixed Concrete* three-element test (mutuality of obligation + control + integration / consistency with employment) is the foundational employment-vs-self-employment test. For a CIS contractor, the risk is that a long-running CIS-registered subcontractor on a single site is reclassified as an employee — at which point the contractor faces PAYE + NIC liability on historic payments. CIS-registered status does NOT establish self-employment for employment purposes; the working pattern must clear the Ready Mixed Concrete test independently. Sessions cross-link to §35.2 employment-status floor + the CIS-deductions page (cis-deduction-by-contractors-in-the-uk in this same batch).

11. **Q: Is *Higgins* the only case on PPR period of ownership for off-plan purchases?**
    A: *Higgins v HMRC* [2019] EWCA Civ 1860 is the operative authority on PPR period-of-ownership for off-plan / pre-completion completion-date cases. Earlier HMRC practice had treated the period of ownership as starting from legal completion; Higgins held that the period of ownership = period of BENEFICIAL ownership, which can start later (on beneficial completion of a property that did not exist at exchange). For an off-plan flat purchased pre-construction, the PPR clock starts when beneficial ownership of the actual flat begins. RUN session monitors for any UKSC challenge to Higgins (none to date) + any HMRC operative-guidance updates.

12. **Q: Will more cases be added to the table?**
    A: Yes — the page is monitored for new case-law watchpoint additions per the Stage 2 research target list: post-2025 BlueCrest follow-on FTT/UT decisions; post-2025 SDLT mixed-use trilogy follow-on cases at UT/CA; post-2025 IHT BPR decisions affecting the Pawson line; post-2025 Higgins-line PPR completion-date cases. RUN session at next page-refresh cycle adds new cases where they meet the table's selection rule.
