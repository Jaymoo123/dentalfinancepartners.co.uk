**Operative case-law clusters** (each pre-locked elsewhere; sessions verify):

- **SDLT mixed-use rate-line trilogy** — *Hyman v HMRC* [2021] UKUT 0068 (TCC); *Goodfellow v HMRC* [2019] UKFTT 750 (TC); *Faiers v HMRC* [2023] UKFTT 297 (TC). §1.J pre-locked.
- **SDLT uninhabitable property test** — *PN Bewley Ltd v HMRC* [2019] UKFTT 65 (TC). §1.C pre-locked.
- **BPR investment line for landlords** — *Pawson v HMRC* [2013] UKUT 050 (TCC). §22.1 pre-locked.
- **Badges of trade — trading-vs-investment** — *Marson v Morton* [1986] 1 WLR 1343; *Iswera v IRC* [1965] 1 WLR 663 (PC); *Salt v Chamberlain* [1979] STC 750; *Page v Lowther* [1983] STC 799; *Pickford v Quirke* (1927) 13 TC 251. §28.5 pre-locked.
- **PPR family-home boundaries** — *Lewis v Lady Rook* [1992] STC 171 (CA); *Goodwin v Curtis* [1998] STC 475 (CA); *Higgins v HMRC* [2019] EWCA Civ 1860 (CA). Sessions verify at BAILII per §16.35 — Higgins is the PPR-on-off-plan-purchase line (period of ownership = period of beneficial ownership, not period of legal completion).
- **Salaried-member rules — LLP context** — ***BlueCrest Capital Management v HMRC* [2024] UKSC 33**. §11.C.X.2 pre-locked. Supreme Court narrowed Condition B "significant influence" — influence over a significant PART of the LLP's affairs can be enough.
- **Reasonable excuse — penalties** — *Perrin v HMRC* [2018] UKUT 156 (TCC) (4-step framework); *Christine Perrin v HMRC* (same case, sometimes cited differently); *Martland v HMRC* [2018] UKUT 178 (TCC) (late-appeal three-stage framework). Sessions verify citations per §16.35.
- **Discovery assessment — competent officer** — *Tooth v HMRC* [2021] UKSC 17 (Supreme Court reading of "stale" discovery framework — narrowed taxpayer protection). §27.1 pre-locked.
- **Construction industry — employment vs self-employment line** — *Ready Mixed Concrete (South East) Ltd v Minister of Pensions and National Insurance* [1968] 2 QB 497 (three-element test still operative). §35.2 cross-reference.
- **VAT option-to-tax / Sch 10** — *CLP Holding Co Ltd v HMRC* (option-to-tax disapplication trajectory); *Robinson Family Ltd v HMRC* [2012] UKFTT 360 (TC) (TOGC + property in business transfer). Stage 2 verifies citations per §16.35.
- **Trust + property + IHT** — *Hood v HMRC* [2018] EWCA Civ 2405 (GROB and granny-annexe-type arrangements); *Buzzoni and Others v HMRC* [2013] EWCA Civ 1684 (GROB tracing rules). §22.11 pre-locked.

Useful for SEO keyword-stuffing; useful for nobody making a tax decision. The article-shape collapses the substantive value of case-law into a directory.

This page reverses that with two design choices:

1. **Cases grouped by the tax question they decide** — SDLT mixed-use rate-line; SDLT uninhabitable test; BPR investment line; badges of trade; PPR family-home boundaries; salaried-member rules; reasonable excuse; discovery assessment; CIS employment-vs-self-employment; VAT TOGC + option-to-tax; trust + property + IHT. ## Key questions this page must answer

1. **Why does case-law matter for property tax decisions when the statute is the primary source?** — Statute sets the framework; case-law tells the landlord what HMRC + tribunals interpret the words to MEAN. The badges-of-trade cluster (Marson v Morton five-case cluster) tells the landlord what "trading" looks like even though the statute does not enumerate. The SDLT mixed-use trilogy tells the landlord what "non-residential character" looks like in practice. The Pawson decision tells the landlord that ordinary BTL letting is INVESTMENT for BPR even where the landlord is active in management. Without case-law grounding, the statute is a sentence — landlords act on the case-law interpretation, not the statutory text alone.
2. **The case-law table (decision-grouped, table-formatted)** — Sessions build a single integrated table with columns: (a) Tax question; (b) Case name; (c) Court level (UKSC > CA > UT > FTT > PC where applicable); (d) Year + citation; (e) Operative holding (one sentence); (f) What changed because of it (one line); (g) Forward-link to substantive deep-dive page. **Critical:** every case citation verbatim-verified at write per §16.35. The table is the page's primary content — sessions must NOT collapse into prose paragraphs that lose the lookup-density.
3. **SDLT mixed-use rate-line — the trilogy** — *Hyman* [2021] UKUT (paddock-as-garden held residential, not mixed-use); *Goodfellow* [2019] UKFTT (woodland adjacent to garden held residential, not mixed-use); *Faiers* [2023] UKFTT (commercial-use claim rejected on non-residential character test). The trilogy narrowed the mixed-use claim landlords had used to pay non-residential SDLT rates on rural acquisitions. **What changed:** HMRC's enquiry approach hardened post-trilogy; landlords on borderline mixed-use claims now face an uphill burden of proof. §1.J pre-locked.
4. **SDLT uninhabitable property — Bewley** — *PN Bewley Ltd v HMRC* [2019] UKFTT (uninhabitable derelict bungalow held non-residential for SDLT purposes — landlord paid non-residential rates not residential). **What changed:** opened a narrow uninhabitability route for SDLT mitigation on derelict-property purchases; HMRC scrutiny since has narrowed the practical scope. §1.C pre-locked. Sessions must NOT over-claim Bewley — it is fact-specific to genuinely uninhabitable property, not "needs renovation".
5. **BPR investment line — Pawson** — *Pawson v HMRC* [2013] UKUT (TCC) — ordinary buy-to-let letting held INVESTMENT for BPR purposes, even where the landlord is active in management; therefore no BPR available. Limits the BPR claim to genuine FHL-with-services or property-as-trade businesses. **What changed:** essentially closed the BPR-for-BTL route as a planning lever; only the genuine-trade subset (FHL-with-substantial-services + property-development-trade) qualifies. §22.1 pre-locked.
6. **Badges of trade — the five-case cluster** — *Marson v Morton* [1986] (the nine badges canonical statement); *Iswera v IRC* [1965] (PC — single transaction can be trading); *Salt v Chamberlain* [1979] (frequency line); *Page v Lowther* [1983] (single property flip held to be trading); *Pickford v Quirke* (1927) (repeated transactions establish trade). **What changed:** these cases sit alongside Part 8ZB CTA 2010 / Part 9A ITA 2007 (Finance Act 2016 transactions in UK land regime) — the statutory regime catches the same trading patterns but the case-law shapes border-case classification. §28.5 pre-locked.
7. **PPR family-home boundaries — Lewis / Goodwin / Higgins** — *Lewis v Lady Rook* [1992] (CA — "garden or grounds" of a dwelling has a permitted area + necessary-for-enjoyment test; PPR does not extend to large grounds beyond the limits); *Goodwin v Curtis* [1998] (CA — minimum-residence period needs quality of occupation, not just calendar duration); *Higgins v HMRC* [2019] EWCA Civ (period of ownership = period of beneficial ownership, so a flat-not-yet-built has no "ownership" until beneficial completion; PPR clock starts on beneficial completion). **What changed:** Higgins corrected HMRC's earlier position; PPR claims on off-plan completions are now grounded on Higgins. Sessions cross-reference §5 CGT residential.
8. **Salaried-member rules — BlueCrest 2024** — ***BlueCrest Capital Management v HMRC* [2024] UKSC 33** — Condition B "significant influence over the affairs of the partnership" does NOT require influence over the whole partnership; influence over a significant PART can be enough. NARROWED the safe-harbour reading the FTT / UT had taken. **What changed:** matrix-silo'd member roles may not qualify post-BlueCrest; LLPs reviewing member structures must re-test against the narrowed Condition B reading. §11.C.X.2 pre-locked.
9. **Reasonable excuse — Perrin / Martland penalties framework** — *Perrin v HMRC* [2018] UKUT (4-step reasonable-excuse framework: identify the relevant facts; identify the relevant law; ask whether what the taxpayer did satisfied the reasonable-excuse test; consider whether the taxpayer remedied the failure without unreasonable delay). *Martland v HMRC* [2018] UKUT (late-appeal three-stage framework — length + reason + balance). **What changed:** Perrin / Martland are the operative test-frameworks for penalty + late-appeal challenges across HMRC penalty regime. Cross-reference §27.2 + §27.4 + §27.7.
10. **Discovery assessment + competent officer — Tooth 2021** — *Tooth v HMRC* [2021] UKSC — Supreme Court narrowed the "stale" discovery framework taxpayers had relied on for protection; discovery is not stale merely because HMRC took time to act on information already available. **What changed:** taxpayer protection on stale discovery is narrower than pre-Tooth understanding; discovery assessment time limits (TMA s.34 + s.36 + §27.1) apply on more cases. §27.1 pre-locked.
11. **GROB + family-home arrangements — Hood / Buzzoni** — *Hood v HMRC* [2018] EWCA Civ (granny-annexe + retention-of-benefit arrangements held GROB); *Buzzoni v HMRC* [2013] EWCA Civ (GROB tracing through corporate / trust arrangements). **What changed:** narrowed the planning routes around family-home gifting; "gift-with-strings-attached" arrangements caught even where indirect benefit is the link. §22.11 pre-locked.
12. **The methodology section — why some cases are NOT on the list** — Sessions briefly justify selection: (a) cases must LAND on landlord-relevant tax decisions; (b) cases must have current operative force (not superseded by later statute or higher-court ruling); (c) cases are limited to UK courts + tribunals (FTT / UT / CA / SC / UKSC + PC where applicable); (d) the list is curated not exhaustive — landlords with niche fact-patterns may need cases not on this list; (e) sessions verify each case at BAILII / Justis at write per §16.35.
