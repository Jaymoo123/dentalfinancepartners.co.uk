# Airbnb After the Pandemic: How UK Short-Let Taxation Has Evolved Since 2020

## Statutory anchor
- Primary (FHL abolition): Finance Act 2025 (specifically the Schedule abolishing FHL as a separate tax regime from 6 April 2025 for individuals / 1 April 2025 for companies); §6 lock for the abolition transition; §25.7 lock for the FHL capital-allowances transitional under FA 2025 Sch 5.
- Primary (digital-platform reporting / DAC7-equivalent): International Tax Compliance (Amendment) Regulations 2023 (SI 2023/817) implementing the UK adoption of OECD Model Reporting Rules for Digital Platforms (the DAC7-equivalent) — Stage 2 sub-agent re-verifies the SI number + commencement at write time. Reporting period began 1 January 2024; first reports due 31 January 2025 covering calendar 2024. Operators include Airbnb, Booking.com, Vrbo, and similar OTA platforms. Reports furnished to HMRC list each host's annual gross receipts + property addresses + identifiers.
- Primary (council-tax second-home premium): Levelling-up and Regeneration Act 2023 (LURA 2023) s.80 inserting Local Government Finance Act 1992 s.11C (+ s.11D regulations gate) — empowering English billing authorities to charge up to 100% council tax premium on second homes (s.11C(2): "no resident" + "substantially furnished"). s.11C(3) requires the first determination at least one year before the financial year to which it relates; earliest statutory effective date 1 April 2024 (FY 2024/25) for LAs that determined in 2023, with most tourism-area LAs first imposing the premium from 1 April 2025 (FY 2025/26). §30.4 lock (premium powers post-LURA 2023, MW3 Stage 2b F-105 refinement).
- Primary (RRA 2025 interaction — STL is OUTSIDE the assured tenancy regime): RRA 2025 (2025 c. 26) Royal Assent 27 October 2025, periodic-from-grant default in force 1 May 2026 via SI 2026/421. **Short-let / serviced accommodation is excluded from the assured tenancy regime under HA 1988 Sch 1 (holiday lets carve-out) and therefore from the RRA reform**; STL operators sit outside the periodic-default regime. §20.3 lock by reference (the carve-out is logical from the HA 1988 starting point).
- Primary (VAT on short-let / TOMS): VATA 1994 s.53 (Tour Operators' Margin Scheme) — relevant where the host operates as a multi-property let-management business including in-house bookings; §29 cluster VAT framework.
- Primary (income tax on short-let receipts): ITTOIA 2005 Part 3 (property income) — post-FHL-abolition all short-let receipts default to ordinary property income; the trading-vs-investment line under §28 (CTA 2010 Part 8ZB + ITA 2007 Part 9A) may bite for the most-active hosts; §28 lock.
- Supporting: §6 (FHL abolition transition headline); §25.7 (FHL capital-allowances transitional); §28 (transactions-in-UK-land + trading-vs-investment); §29 cluster (VAT for property — TOMS, reduced-rate residential conversions, partial exemption); §30 (council tax framework); §1.A-§1.O (SDLT residential-vs-mixed-use line where the host owns at scale); §17.4 (NRCGT if the host becomes non-resident).
- House position reference: §6 + §25.7 + §28 + §29 + §30 + §20.3 (HA 1988 Sch 1 STL carve-out, by reference). **No new HP lock needed.** The page synthesises locked architecture across six clusters; the synthesis itself does not create a new statutory position.

## Key questions this page must answer

1. **What happened to UK Airbnb between March 2020 and April 2022** — the inflection: demand collapse in two weeks of the first lockdown; the temporary VAT-reduced-rate (5%) for hospitality between 15 July 2020 and 31 March 2022 under the COVID-19 fiscal-support package; the suburban + coastal shift (Cornwall, Lake District, Pembrokeshire all booked out summer 2020 and again 2021); urban demand returning slowly through 2022; by 2023 the urban market had recovered but with a structural increase in suburban + countryside hosts.
2. **What changed in tax policy 2020-2026** — FOUR pillars: (a) FHL abolition announced 22 November 2023 in the Autumn Statement; legislated in FA 2025; effective 6 April 2025 for individuals / 1 April 2025 for companies. (b) DAC7-equivalent OTA platform reporting via SI 2023/817 — Airbnb, Booking.com, Vrbo all reporting host data to HMRC from January 2025 covering 2024. (c) LURA 2023 s.80 inserting LGFA 1992 s.11C — second-home council-tax premium up to 100% (statutory floor 1 April 2024 under s.11C(3) one-year notice rule; commonly imposed by tourism-area LAs from 1 April 2025). (d) RRA 2025 commencement May 2026 (indirectly relevant — STL is OUTSIDE the assured regime, but the broader policy signal is the UK government tightening on landlord-side regulation).
3. **What FHL abolition actually means for a 2026 Airbnb host** — pre-April-2025, hosts meeting the FHL qualifying tests (140 days available + 105 days actually let + average lets under 31 days) got: capital allowances on furniture / fixtures; loss relief against other income (sideways relief); CGT rollover + Business Asset Disposal Relief; pension-relevant earnings treatment. Post-abolition: none of those reliefs. Rental income defaults to ordinary property income under ITTOIA 2005 Part 3 with the s.24 finance-cost restriction biting. Capital allowances on plant & machinery elements continue under CAA 2001 Part 2 (the transitional under FA 2025 Sch 5 preserves the existing pool — §25.7 lock). Forward-link to `serviced-accommodation-tax-fhl-abolition-april-2025` for the mechanic deep-dive.
4. **What DAC7 / platform reporting means in practice** — Airbnb and similar platforms now report each host's annual gross receipts + property identifiers + listing data directly to HMRC. The host's SA return is no longer the only HMRC-side source; HMRC can cross-check declared rental income against reported platform receipts. Hosts with several hundred pounds a year of unreported short-let income are now within HMRC's data set; the Let Property Campaign route (§27.6) is the voluntary-disclosure pivot for hosts who have under-declared.
5. **What the council-tax second-home premium means** — LURA 2023 s.80 inserts LGFA 1992 s.11C empowering English billing authorities to charge up to 100% premium on dwellings where "(a) there is no resident of the dwelling, and (b) the dwelling is substantially furnished" (s.11C(2) verbatim). s.11D inserts the regulations gate for excepted classes. The s.11C(3) one-year notice rule means the earliest LA implementation was 1 April 2024; most tourism-area LAs first imposed it from 1 April 2025. Many authorities (Cornwall, Devon, Lake District councils) adopted the maximum 100% premium from 1 April 2025 — a property with a £2,400 normal council tax bill now pays £4,800. The premium is NOT deductible against rental income because it is a charge on the second-home owner; it IS deductible against trading income only if the host operates as a genuine FHL-equivalent trade post-abolition (the trading-vs-investment line — §28).
6. **What the trading-vs-investment line looks like for serviced accommodation post-FHL** — §28 lock (CTA 2010 Part 8ZB + ITA 2007 Part 9A — transactions-in-UK-land regime). The four-conditions test in s.356OB / s.517B determines whether the operator is trading rather than investing. The badges-of-trade case-law framework (Marson v Morton; Salt v Chamberlain; West v Phillips) applies. Most one-or-two-property Airbnb hosts remain investors; the most-active operators (10+ properties, daily turnover, in-house staff, full hotel-style services) may cross into trading and recover some of the FHL-abolition lost ground. Forward-link to §28-anchored deep-dive (if one ships).
7. **The VAT line** — the abolished 5% reduced rate (15 July 2020 to 31 March 2022) is GONE; standard-rate 20% returned 1 April 2022. The VAT registration threshold (currently £90,000 from 1 April 2024 per FA 2024 s.27) catches a high-occupancy Airbnb host with several properties; once registered the host either applies the Tour Operators' Margin Scheme (VATA 1994 s.53) for in-house bookings or charges VAT on the rental margin. Forward-link to `toms-vat-serviced-accommodation`.
8. **The mortgage-and-lender shift** — most BTL mortgages exclude short-let / Airbnb operation; lenders have tightened criteria post-COVID. Specialist serviced-accommodation lenders price 1.5%-2.0% above standard BTL rates. Consent-to-let on a residential mortgage is harder to obtain for short-let purposes. The Section 24 finance-cost restriction (§4 lock) applies to short-let mortgage interest the same way it applies to BTL.
9. **Regional regulatory pressure** — Scottish short-let licensing (Civic Government (Scotland) Act 1982 + 2022 SI) is in force from October 2022; Welsh short-let licensing on the way; London short-let-90-day rule under GLA Act 1999 s.25 (90-day-per-year cap on entire-home lets — enforced via Airbnb's auto-throttling); various English authorities adopting Article 4 directions to remove permitted-development rights for change-of-use to short-let. Stage 2 surfaces the regional map at one-paragraph depth + forward-links to a regional deep-dive page (cluster sibling) where applicable.
10. **Where the market sits in 2026 — and the strategic question for hosts** — synthesising the above: the post-COVID Airbnb market is larger than pre-COVID in unit count, more rural-skewed, more regulated, and more visible to HMRC. The pre-2025 tax-favoured FHL position is gone; the council-tax premium adds 50%-100% to operating cost in premium-tourism areas; platform reporting closes the under-declaration gap. The strategic question for hosts in 2026 is whether to (a) stay as a single-property investor under ordinary property-income tax + s.24, (b) scale into the trading-side of §28 by adding properties + services + staff, (c) convert to BTL where the unit is in a market that supports both, or (d) sell, taking the CGT hit at the 24% residential rate. Forward-link to `serviced-accommodation-vs-buy-to-let-tax-comparison-2026` for the decision matrix.

## FAQ expansion (RUN session polishes prose; 10-12 FAQs target)

1. **Q: How did the UK Airbnb market change between 2020 and 2026?**
   A: Demand collapsed in March 2020 with the first lockdown — Airbnb's UK gross bookings fell by approximately 90% within three weeks (RUN session verifies the figure at write). The May-October 2020 rebound went almost entirely to rural and coastal whole-home lets; Cornwall, the Lake District, and Pembrokeshire were booked out two summers running. Urban demand returned slowly through 2022. By 2026 the market is larger in unit count than pre-COVID, structurally more rural-skewed, more regulated (Scottish licensing from October 2022; English Article 4 directions in multiple boroughs), and more visible to HMRC (DAC7-equivalent platform reporting from January 2025).

2. **Q: What tax-policy changes have hit short-let hosts since 2020?**
   A: Four pillars. (i) FHL abolition announced 22 November 2023 (Autumn Statement), legislated by FA 2025, effective 6 April 2025 for individuals and 1 April 2025 for companies. (ii) DAC7-equivalent platform reporting under SI 2023/817 — Airbnb, Booking.com, Vrbo, and similar platforms report annual host receipts to HMRC; first reports were due 31 January 2025 for calendar 2024. (iii) Levelling-up and Regeneration Act 2023 s.80 powers — English billing authorities can charge up to 100% council-tax premium on dwellings that are substantially furnished but not used as a sole or main residence; many tourism-area councils adopted the maximum 100% from 1 April 2025. (iv) RRA 2025 commencement 1 May 2026 — STL is carved out of the assured tenancy regime, but the broader policy signal is the tightening on residential-let regulation.

3. **Q: What does FHL abolition actually mean for an Airbnb host?**
   A: Pre-April-2025, FHL-qualifying hosts got capital allowances on furniture and fixtures, sideways loss relief against other income, CGT rollover and Business Asset Disposal Relief on eventual disposal, and pension-relevant-earnings treatment for the trading profits. Post-abolition, none of those reliefs are available. Rental income defaults to ordinary property income under ITTOIA 2005 Part 3 with the s.24 finance-cost restriction biting on mortgage interest. The existing capital-allowances pool at 6 April 2025 (individuals) / 1 April 2025 (companies) is grandfathered under FA 2025 Sch 5 — pool writing-down continues, no new additions in FHL form. New furniture / replacement items fall under ITTOIA 2005 s.311A replacement-of-domestic-items relief (where the property is investment-side) or the ordinary plant-and-machinery pool (where the host is on the trading side of the §28 line).

4. **Q: What does DAC7 / platform reporting mean in practice?**
   A: Airbnb, Booking.com, Vrbo, and similar OTA platforms now report each host's annual gross receipts + property identifiers + listing data directly to HMRC. The host's SA return is no longer the only HMRC-side data source; HMRC can cross-check declared rental income against platform-reported receipts. Hosts who have been under-declaring are now within HMRC's data set, and the Let Property Campaign route is the voluntary-disclosure pivot for catching up.

5. **Q: What is the second-home council-tax premium and where does it bite?**
   A: LURA 2023 s.80 inserts a statutory power for English billing authorities to charge up to 100% premium on dwellings that are substantially furnished but not occupied as a sole or main residence. Many tourism-area councils (Cornwall, Devon, parts of the Lake District, North York Moors) adopted the maximum 100% premium from 1 April 2025. A property with a £2,400 normal council-tax bill now pays £4,800. The premium is NOT directly deductible against rental income because it is a charge on the second-home owner; it IS deductible against trading income where the host operates on the trading side of the §28 line.

6. **Q: How does my Airbnb interact with the RRA 2025 commencement?**
   A: Indirectly. STL is excluded from the assured tenancy regime under HA 1988 Sch 1 (holiday-let carve-out) and therefore from the RRA 2025 periodic-from-grant default. STL operators sit outside the periodic-default regime that landed 1 May 2026. The broader policy signal — government tightening on residential-let regulation — is relevant context but does not directly affect day-to-day STL operations.

7. **Q: What is the trading-vs-investment line post-FHL?**
   A: The §28 architecture (CTA 2010 Part 8ZB + ITA 2007 Part 9A) and the badges-of-trade case-law (*Marson v Morton*, *Salt v Chamberlain*, *West v Phillips*) determine whether a host is trading or investing. Most one-or-two-property Airbnb hosts remain investors. The most-active operators (10+ properties, daily turnover, in-house staff, full hotel-style services) may cross into trading and recover some of the FHL-abolition lost ground. The flip is the most material strategic question for the largest operators after April 2025.

8. **Q: What is the VAT position?**
   A: The temporary 5% reduced rate (15 July 2020 to 31 March 2022) is gone; standard 20% returned 1 April 2022. The VAT registration threshold of £90,000 from 1 April 2024 (FA 2024 s.27) catches a high-occupancy host with several properties. Once registered, the host either charges 20% on the rental margin to guests directly or applies the Tour Operators' Margin Scheme (VATA 1994 s.53) where bought-in components (cleaning, utilities, breakfast) are sold-on as a packaged offering. Sibling page `toms-vat-serviced-accommodation` walks TOMS mechanics.

9. **Q: What about my BTL mortgage — does it cover Airbnb?**
   A: Most standard BTL mortgages explicitly exclude short-let / Airbnb operation. Specialist serviced-accommodation lenders typically price 1.5%-2.0% above standard BTL rates. Consent-to-let on a residential mortgage is harder to obtain for short-let purposes than for long-let.
