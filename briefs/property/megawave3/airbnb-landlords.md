# Airbnb Landlords: The Operational Tax + Accounting Handbook (2026)

## Statutory anchor
- Primary (income tax architecture, post-FHL-abolition default): ITTOIA 2005 Part 3 (property income) — s.264 (charge), s.270 (deductions on the same basis as a trade), s.271A (cash basis default for individuals under £150,000 property + trading turnover), s.272A-272B (finance cost restriction); §4 lock.
- Primary (FHL abolition transitional): Finance Act 2025 Sch 5 — preserves the existing capital-allowances pool at 6 April 2025 (individuals) / 1 April 2025 (companies); §6 lock + §25.7 lock.
- Primary (capital allowances on plant + machinery elements of an FHL-historic Airbnb fitout): CAA 2001 Part 2 (ss.21-204) — plant and machinery; CAA 2001 ss.51A-51N (Annual Investment Allowance, current £1 million per FA 2025 s.X — Stage 2 verifies at write time); §25.2 + §25.3 lock.
- Primary (replacement of domestic items relief — applies POST-FHL-abolition to short-let units now treated as ordinary residential property businesses): ITTOIA 2005 s.311A (Conditions A-D); §25.x by reference (replacement-items is at the operational floor, not architectural).
- Primary (VAT — TOMS and standard rate): VATA 1994 s.53 (Tour Operators' Margin Scheme); Sch 9 Group 1 (exempt land default + holiday accommodation carve-out to standard rate per item 1(d) of Group 1); §29.2 + §29.6 lock. VAT registration threshold £90,000 from 1 April 2024 (FA 2024 s.27 — Stage 2 verifies).
- Primary (digital-platform reporting / DAC7-equivalent): International Tax Compliance (Amendment) Regulations 2023 (SI 2023/817); Airbnb / Booking.com / Vrbo report annual gross receipts + property identifiers + host details to HMRC; first reports 31 January 2025 covering calendar 2024. Cross-checked by HMRC against host SA returns.
- Primary (council tax + business rates line): LGFA 1992 s.66 (definition of dwelling) — Airbnb / FHL property may qualify for business rates rather than council tax where let "commercially" for at least 140 days a year and actually let for 70 days (England) — Non-Domestic Rating (Definition of Domestic Property) (England) Order 2022 (SI 2022/1265). LURA 2023 s.81 second-home council-tax premium up to 100% from 1 April 2025 if business-rates qualification not met; §30.4 lock.
- Primary (Money Laundering Regulations 2017 — letting-agents trigger): SI 2017/692 reg 8(2) + reg 14 — letting agents handling rents above €10,000 monthly are within MLR 2017 scope; a multi-property Airbnb host who outsources management to an agent meets this floor at the agent-side, not host-side, but the trigger is operationally relevant.
- Supporting (RRA 2025 by reference — STL is outside): RRA 2025 (2025 c. 26); HA 1988 Sch 1 holiday-let carve-out keeps STL outside the assured tenancy regime and outside the RRA periodic-default reform. §20.3 lock by reference.
- Supporting (badges of trade — trading-vs-investment line for the largest hosts): CTA 2010 Part 8ZB + ITA 2007 Part 9A; §28 lock for the trading-vs-investment statutory architecture. *Marson v Morton* [1986] STC 463 + sibling case-law for the badges test.
- House position reference: §4 (s.24); §6 (FHL abolition); §25.2 + §25.3 + §25.7 (capital allowances + AIA + FHL transitional); §28 (trading-vs-investment); §29.2 + §29.6 (VAT exempt land + Group 1 holiday-accommodation carve-out + TOMS); §30.4 (council tax second-home premium); §20.3 by reference (RRA STL carve-out). **No new HP lock needed.** All architecture pre-locked.

## Key questions this page must answer

1. **The Airbnb landlord's annual cycle — month-by-month** — January (Self Assessment SA100 + SA105 property pages filed for prior tax year; platform 1099-K equivalent / DAC7 report from Airbnb arrives covering prior calendar year); April (tax-year end; if first property, register for SA via TMA 1970 s.7 notification within 6 months); May-July (occupancy peak — daily booking entries to ledger; cleaner invoices weekly); September (mid-year review of receipts; if total turnover crossing £90,000, register for VAT); October (HMRC may issue notice to file; SA100 paper deadline 31 October if filing paper); January again. The yearly rhythm hits MTD-ITSA mandate threshold (£50,000 from April 2026 / £30,000 from April 2027 per §19.1) for most professional hosts.
2. **How OTA gross-receipt reports reconcile to HMRC returns** — Airbnb's annual statement reports "gross receipts" as the amount paid by guests including service fees, sometimes including cleaning fees collected on the host's behalf, sometimes net of OTA commissions. Booking.com presents the figure differently. The host's SA return must report gross-of-OTA-commission receipts in box 5 SA105 (UK property income) and deduct the OTA commission in the relevant deduction box. Mismatch between the platform's reported figure and the host's declared figure triggers HMRC enquiry. The reconciliation discipline is non-negotiable since DAC7 went live January 2025.
3. **The cash-basis vs accruals decision** — ITTOIA 2005 s.271A defaults individual landlords under £150,000 turnover to cash basis. Most single-property Airbnb hosts stay on cash basis (simpler ledger; receipts recognised on payment-into-host-account date). Larger hosts (£150,000+ turnover, multi-property operations) move to accruals — typically because the cash-basis £500 finance-cost-deduction-cap penalises mortgage-funded operations (the cap is verified per §27.x at write time; Stage 2 confirms the current cap). Companies use accruals by default.
4. **Capital allowances on the surviving FHL-pool** — under the FA 2025 Sch 5 transitional (§25.7 lock), capital allowances claimed on plant + machinery up to 5 April 2025 (individuals) / 31 March 2025 (companies) continue under the existing pool basis. NO NEW additions to FHL-style pool post-abolition — capital expenditure on furniture + appliances from April 2025 falls under either (a) the ordinary plant-and-machinery pool for the property-as-business (where the trading-vs-investment line under §28 places the host into a trade) or (b) the s.311A replacement-of-domestic-items relief (where the host is in a property business). For investment-side hosts, NO new capital allowances; the asset goes into base cost for CGT instead.
5. **The s.24 finance-cost restriction — what it looks like in practice** — mortgage interest on the Airbnb property is no longer a deduction against rental profit; it is converted to a 20% basic-rate tax reducer. For a higher-rate-band Airbnb host with £20,000 annual mortgage interest and £40,000 gross receipts, the tax bill (pre-reducer) is computed on the £40,000 receipts less non-interest expenses; the reducer is then 20% × £20,000 = £4,000 credit against tax due. Worked example placeholder for Stage 2 with rate-by-reference framing per §16.27.
6. **VAT registration trigger + the TOMS question** — VAT threshold £90,000 (rate-by-reference). A two-property Airbnb host with £60,000 each is at £120,000 gross — over the threshold. Registration is from the day the threshold was crossed (or expected to be crossed in the next 30 days). Choice of scheme: (a) ordinary VAT charging 20% on the rental margin to guests directly; (b) Tour Operators' Margin Scheme (VATA 1994 s.53) if the host buys-in components (cleaning, utilities, breakfast) and resells the package. TOMS computes VAT on the host's margin (selling price minus bought-in component cost), not on gross. Forward-link to `toms-vat-serviced-accommodation`.
7. **Council tax vs business rates — the 140/70-day test** — Non-Domestic Rating (Definition of Domestic Property) (England) Order 2022 (SI 2022/1265) requires both: 140 days available for letting and 70 days actually let in the year. Property meeting both qualifies for business rates (eligible for small-business-rates relief — typically 100% relief for rateable values under £12,000 = zero rates bill). Property failing both stays under council tax and is exposed to the LURA 2023 s.81 second-home premium (up to 100% uplift). The 140/70 test was tightened from earlier 14-day standard in 2022 specifically to remove low-utilisation FHL claimers from business-rates relief — sessions verify the current thresholds at gov.uk at write time.
8. **The OTA fee + cleaning fee accounting** — Airbnb takes a host-side commission (typically 3% per booking, sometimes more under split-fee or simplified-fee structures) + a guest-side service fee. Most hosts see the host-side commission as a deductible operating expense (allowable per ITTOIA 2005 s.270 trade-equivalent deduction basis). Cleaning fees: if charged to the guest as a separate line item, the host receives the gross cleaning fee (taxable income) and pays the cleaner (deductible expense). The two-way flow nets out neutral on profit but boosts gross receipts — relevant for the £90,000 VAT threshold and the £150,000 cash-basis line.
9. **Records, evidence, audit trail (TMA 1970 s.12B + Sch 24 FA 2007 inaccuracy-penalty)** — 6-year record retention for property-business records. Required documentation: per-booking ledger entries; cleaner + management invoices; OTA monthly statements; utility bills + council tax statements; capital-expenditure invoices with date + supplier; mortgage interest certificate annually; insurance schedules. Insufficient records exposes the host to Sch 24 FA 2007 inaccuracy penalty (§27.2 lock — up to 30% non-deliberate, 70% deliberate, 100% deliberate-and-concealed). Forward-link to §27 enquiry-mechanics cluster.
10. **Succession + exit options — what happens at the end** — three routes: (a) **continue as investment** through retirement or transfer to family (CGT on transfer under TCGA 1992 s.17 deemed market-value to connected persons; gift-with-holdover unavailable because investment-side property does not qualify under s.165 + Sch 7); (b) **convert to BTL** (run the property as ordinary buy-to-let; lower management overhead; lower income; tax regime aligned to standard property income); (c) **sell** — CGT at 24% residential rate on the gain; 60-day in-year reporting under TCGA 1992 Sch 2 paras 6-12; PPR not available unless the property was ever a main residence. The §22.1 BPR Pawson line means the asset does NOT receive BPR for IHT purposes regardless of how active the operation looks (forward-link to `serviced-accommodation-bpr-eligibility-pawson-test`). Forward-link to `serviced-accommodation-vs-buy-to-let-tax-comparison-2026` for the conversion-vs-stay decision.

## Worked-example data (RUN session uses these as canvas)

### Example 1 — A two-property host's annual cycle (calendar 2026 → tax year 2026/27)

- **January 2026:** SA100 + SA105 for tax year 2024/25 filed by 31 January 2025 (assumes prior-year position); Airbnb DAC7 annual statement for calendar 2025 arrives, reporting gross receipts of £42,800 + £31,200 = £74,000.
- **April 2026:** new tax year 2026/27 starts. Mortgage interest certificate from lender (calendar 2025) lands. Capital expenditure log up to 5 April 2026 closed (Property A new sofa £680 — replacement of domestic items under ITTOIA 2005 s.311A; Property B replacement boiler £1,800 — capital improvement, base-cost only).
- **May-September 2026:** occupancy peak. Daily booking-ledger entries; weekly cleaner invoices; monthly Airbnb statement reconciliation. Council tax for the year (likely with 100% LURA 2023 s.80 premium in tourism-area locations) ratable from 1 April 2026.
- **End September 2026:** mid-year review of gross receipts. If combined gross receipts on the 12-month rolling basis crossing £90,000, register for VAT under VATA 1994 s.3 within 30 days (registration date is the start of the month after the threshold-crossing month).
- **October 2026:** HMRC notice to file SA may arrive. SA100 paper deadline 31 October 2026.
- **Up to 31 January 2027:** SA100 + SA105 for tax year 2025/26 filed online (covering 6 April 2025 to 5 April 2026 letting period). Box 5 of SA105 reports gross-of-OTA-commission receipts; OTA commission deducted in the relevant deduction box.
- **Tax year 2026/27 forward:** if gross property + trading turnover ≥ £50,000, mandatory MTD ITSA from 6 April 2026 (gov.uk MTD collection confirmed). Quarterly updates start.

### Example 2 — DAC7 reconciliation discipline

Airbnb annual DAC7 statement for calendar 2025 reports host Mr Singh's two properties at £42,800 + £31,200 = £74,000 gross receipts. Mr Singh's SA105 (tax year 2025/26 covering 6 April 2025 to 5 April 2026) must reconcile to a corresponding portion of this figure plus the corresponding portion of calendar 2024 + calendar 2026.

- **The Airbnb statement is calendar-year; the SA return is tax-year.** A worked reconciliation table splits the platform figures by tax-year-quarter and reaggregates. Where the host operates accruals-basis, receipts vs receivable timing also breaks the simple per-day attribution.
- **Box 5 SA105:** report gross-of-OTA-commission receipts.
- **OTA commission deduction:** report in the relevant deduction box as an operating expense; do NOT net at top-line.
- **Cleaning fees charged to guests:** taxable income (the host receives them), the cleaner cost is a deductible expense. The two-way flow nets out neutral on profit but inflates gross receipts — relevant to the VAT threshold + the £150k cash-basis line.

### Example 3 — The s.24 reducer worked through

Mrs Patel: higher-rate-band salary £62,000; one Airbnb property; gross receipts £24,000; allowable expenses excluding mortgage interest £4,800; mortgage interest £8,400.

- **Pre-s.24 reducer profit:** £24,000 - £4,800 = £19,200 (mortgage interest NOT deducted from the rental profit line).
- **Marginal-rate tax on £19,200:** 40% × £19,200 = £7,680.
- **Section 24 reducer:** 20% × £8,400 mortgage interest = £1,680 credit against tax due.
- **Net rental tax:** £7,680 - £1,680 = £6,000.
- **Cashflow effective rate:** £6,000 on £15,600 actual cash margin (gross less interest less expenses) = approximately 38.5% — material reducer-binding for the higher-rate band.

### Example 4 — Records-and-evidence floor (TMA 1970 s.12B)

Six-year record retention applies to property-business records (TMA 1970 s.12B sole-trader + property analogue). Required documentation:

- Per-booking ledger entries with check-in / check-out dates + gross-of-OTA receipt amounts.
- Monthly OTA statements (Airbnb, Booking.com, Vrbo, others).
- Cleaner + management invoices (dated, named, with VAT registration number if cleaner is VAT-registered).
- Utility bills + council tax statements (especially showing any LURA 2023 s.80 premium).
- Building + contents insurance schedules.
- Capital expenditure invoices with date + supplier + asset description.
- Mortgage interest certificate (annual, from lender).
- Land Registry / leasehold title documents (for the CGT base-cost evidence on eventual disposal).
- Where the host claims partial business-rates relief under the 140/70-day FHL self-catering test (RUN session verifies the controlling SI at write — Pick 4 surfaced SI 2022/1265 as wrong number for this test): an availability + actual-let calendar evidencing the 140 days available + 70 days actually let in the relevant period.

Insufficient records exposes the host to Sch 24 FA 2007 inaccuracy penalty (§27.2 lock — up to 30% non-deliberate, 70% deliberate, 100% deliberate-and-concealed).

### Example 5 — Exit options decision tree

For a host considering exit, the four strategic routes:

- **(a) Continue as investment + transfer to family later:** CGT on transfer under TCGA 1992 s.17 deemed market-value to connected persons. Holdover relief under s.165 + Sch 7 NOT available (investment-side property does not qualify for s.165 business holdover). IHT planning by gift-with-holdover unavailable. BPR not available (Pawson line + §22.1 lock).
- **(b) Convert to BTL:** lower management overhead, lower receipts, tax regime aligned to standard property income. Most one-or-two-property hosts where the location supports both modes. Sibling `serviced-accommodation-vs-buy-to-let-tax-comparison-2026` walks the decision.
- **(c) Sell:** CGT at 24% residential rate on the gain. 60-day in-year return under TCGA 1992 Sch 2 paras 6-12 (extended from 30 days on 27 October 2021 per FA 2022 s.23). PPR not available unless property was ever main residence.
- **(d) Scale and trade (the §28 flip):** add properties + services + staff; cross into trading-side under CTA 2010 Part 8ZB + ITA 2007 Part 9A + badges of trade. Recover some FHL-abolition lost ground (capital allowances on plant + machinery; trade-loss relief routes; pension-relevant earnings). Most viable for 10+ property professional operators with in-house staff.

## FAQ expansion (RUN session polishes prose; 10-12 FAQs target)

1. **Q: What is the working host's annual cycle in 2026?**
   A: Calendar year: DAC7 annual statement from Airbnb arrives January (covering prior calendar year). Tax year boundary 6 April. Occupancy peak May-September. Mid-year VAT-threshold check September. SA paper deadline 31 October. SA online deadline 31 January following tax-year end. Quarterly MTD-ITSA updates from 6 April 2026 for hosts at or above the £50,000 gross-property-and-trading-turnover threshold (the threshold uses gross, not net). Records held 6 years under TMA 1970 s.12B.

2. **Q: How do I reconcile Airbnb's annual report to my SA return?**
   A: Airbnb reports a calendar-year gross receipts figure to HMRC under DAC7-equivalent platform-reporting Regulations (SI 2023/817 — The Platform Operators (Due Diligence and Reporting Requirements) Regulations 2023). Your SA105 (UK property pages) reports tax-year figures. Re-attribute the calendar-year platform total across the relevant tax-year-quarter split. Report gross-of-OTA-commission in box 5 SA105; deduct OTA commission in the appropriate deduction box. Cleaning fees charged to guests are taxable income; cleaner costs are deductible. Mismatch between platform-reported and declared income is the standard DAC7-era enquiry trigger.

3. **Q: Cash basis or accruals?**
   A: Cash basis is the default for individual landlords with property + trading turnover under £150,000 (ITTOIA 2005 s.271A). Most single-property Airbnb hosts stay on cash. Larger hosts (multi-property £150k+ turnover) move to accruals — typically because the cash-basis finance-cost-deduction cap (RUN session verifies the current cap at write) penalises mortgage-funded operations. Companies use accruals by default.

4. **Q: What about capital allowances after FHL abolition?**
   A: The FHL-pool existing at 6 April 2025 (individuals) / 1 April 2025 (companies) is grandfathered under FA 2025 Sch 5 — pool writing-down continues; no new additions in FHL form. New capital expenditure on furniture + appliances from April 2025 falls under either (a) the ordinary plant-and-machinery pool with Annual Investment Allowance (CAA 2001 s.51A; £1m AIA cap per F(No.2)A 2023 substitution from 11 July 2023) where the host is on the trading side of the §28 line; or (b) ITTOIA 2005 s.311A replacement-of-domestic-items relief where the host is on the investment side. For investment-side hosts, NO new capital allowances; replacement items via s.311A.

5. **Q: How does the s.24 reducer hit me?**
   A: Mortgage interest is converted to a 20% basic-rate tax reducer rather than a profit deduction. For a higher-rate-band Airbnb host with £8,400 mortgage interest, the reducer is 20% × £8,400 = £1,680 credit. Tax is computed on gross-less-non-interest-expenses; the reducer offsets the tax bill. The discipline flips a cashflow-profitable property into an income-tax-loss after the reducer where mortgage interest is high relative to rental margin. Sibling Section 24 cluster pillar walks the mechanic in depth.

6. **Q: When do I need to register for VAT?**
   A: The VAT registration threshold is £90,000 from 1 April 2024 (FA 2024 s.27). A two-property Airbnb host averaging £4,000/month gross receipts crosses £90,000 inside the 12-month rolling-window measurement. Registration is from the start of the month after the threshold-crossing month. Choice of scheme on registration: (a) standard 20% VAT on the rental margin charged to guests (VATA 1994 Sch 9 Group 1 Item 1(e) carve-out makes holiday accommodation standard-rated, not exempt); (b) Tour Operators' Margin Scheme (VATA 1994 s.53) where the host buys-in components (cleaning, utilities, breakfast) and resells the package. TOMS computes VAT on the host's margin (selling price minus bought-in component cost), not on gross.

7. **Q: How does council tax vs business rates work for my Airbnb?**
   A: The FHL self-catering business-rates test under the Non-Domestic Rating (Definition of Domestic Property) (England) Order (RUN session verifies the operative SI at write; Pick 4 surfaced SI 2022/1265 as wrong number) requires both 140 days available for letting + 70 days actually let in the year. Property meeting both qualifies for business rates (eligible for small-business-rates relief — typically 100% relief for rateable values under £12,000 = zero rates bill). Property failing both stays under council tax and is exposed to the LURA 2023 s.80 second-home premium up to 100%. The 140/70 test was tightened from earlier 14-day standard in 2022 specifically to remove low-utilisation FHL claimers from business-rates relief.

8. **Q: How is the OTA commission treated?**
   A: Airbnb takes a host-side commission (typically 3% per booking under the standard split-fee structure) and a guest-side service fee. The host-side commission is a deductible operating expense (allowable per ITTOIA 2005 s.270 trade-equivalent deduction basis). Cleaning fees charged to guests as a separate line item are gross taxable income; the cleaner cost is a deductible expense. The two-way cleaning flow nets out neutral on profit but inflates gross receipts for VAT-threshold and £150k-cash-basis-line measurement.

9. **Q: What records do I need to keep, and for how long?**
   A: Six years under TMA 1970 s.12B. Per-booking ledger; monthly OTA statements (Airbnb, Booking.com, Vrbo); cleaner and management invoices; utility bills and council tax statements (especially showing any LURA 2023 s.80 premium); building and contents insurance schedules; capital-expenditure invoices with date + supplier; mortgage interest certificate annually; title documents. Where claiming partial business-rates relief, an availability + actual-let calendar evidencing 140 days available + 70 days actually let. Insufficient records exposes you to Sch 24 FA 2007 inaccuracy penalty (up to 30% non-deliberate, 70% deliberate, 100% deliberate-and-concealed).

10. **Q: What are my exit options when I want out?**
    A: Four routes. (a) Continue as investment and transfer to family later — CGT on transfer at TCGA 1992 s.17 connected-party market value, no s.165 holdover for investment property, no BPR (Pawson + §22.1 lock). (b) Convert to BTL — lower receipts, lower management, standard property-income regime. (c) Sell — CGT at 24% residential rate; 60-day in-year return at completion. (d) Scale into the trading side of the §28 line by adding properties + services + staff — recovers some FHL-abolition lost ground (AIA + trade-loss relief + pension-relevant earnings).

11. **Q: Am I caught by MLR 2017 as a host?**
    A: Generally no — the host is not in scope under reg 8(2) directly. The host's letting agent IS in scope under reg 8(2)(f) if managing rents at or above €10,000-equivalent monthly (HMRC supervision routing per reg 14 + the gov.uk who-needs-to-register guidance). The agent runs CDD on the host as part of its supervision; the host receives but does not run CDD on guests. A multi-property host who manages directly + offers tax-advisory or company-secretarial services to other landlords (rare but it happens) may itself be in scope under reg 8(2)(c) external accountants and tax advisers — see Pick 1 in this batch for the professional-conduct framing.

12. **Q: How does this page differ from `air-bnb-and-post-covid`?**
    A: That page is the temporal-narrative retrospective — what changed between 2020 and 2026 and why. This page is the end-to-end practitioner handbook for the working host (annual cycle, OTA-to-SA reconciliation, records discipline, VAT trigger, exit options). Read the retrospective for the policy-and-market arc; read this handbook for the day-to-day operational picture. The two siblings cross-link bidirectionally as narrative + operational companions.
