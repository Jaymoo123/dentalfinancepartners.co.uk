# Don't pay twice: an introduction to UK tax treaties for property owners (and the four assumptions that catch landlords out)

## Statutory anchor
- **Primary (DTAs and foreign tax credit framework):**
  - **TIOPA 2010 Part 2** (ss.2-134) — UK domestic legislation giving effect to double taxation arrangements + unilateral relief. **TIOPA 2010 s.18** verified against legislation.gov.uk at write time (`https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2010/8/section/18`) heading "Entitlement to credit for foreign tax reduces UK tax by amount of the credit" (Part 2 Chapter 2 — "Effect to be given to credit for foreign tax allowed against UK tax"). Section establishes that credit allowed against UK income tax, corporation tax, and capital gains tax under treaty or unilateral relief arrangements reduces the relevant UK tax by the credited amount.
  - **TIOPA 2010 s.130** — unilateral relief from double taxation. Operates where no bilateral treaty covers the situation, applying broadly equivalent credit relief by UK statute alone.
  - **TIOPA 2010 s.2** — confirms HMRC power to give effect to DTAs by Order in Council.
- **Primary (the OECD Model framework most UK DTAs follow):**
  - **OECD Model Tax Convention on Income and on Capital (2017)** — the structural template for most modern UK bilateral treaties. UK treaties broadly follow OECD Model with state-specific variations. **Critical: quote the article number from the SPECIFIC bilateral treaty being written about, not the OECD Model number, because numbering can differ slightly** (HP §16.1 lock).
- **Primary (the UK domestic charges that DTAs allocate but do not displace):**
  - **ITTOIA 2005 Part 3** — UK property income for individuals (s.260 onwards). Charged regardless of landlord residence.
  - **CTA 2009 Part 4** — UK property income for companies. Charged regardless of company residence.
  - **TCGA 1992 s.1A + Sch 1A / 1B / 4AA** — Non-Resident CGT (FA 2019 rewrite of the older ss.14B-14H structure). UK land disposals by non-residents taxed by domestic statute; treaty Art 13 generally allocates source-state taxing rights to UK so the regimes align rather than conflict, per HP §16.3.
  - **FA 1995 Sch 23 + SI 1995/2902** — Non-Resident Landlord scheme. **Statutory, NOT treaty** per HP §16.6: NRL withholding bites the moment a landlord becomes non-UK-resident regardless of treaty position; treaty residence does not displace the 20% basic-rate withholding unless NRL1 / NRL2 / NRL3 approval is obtained.
- **Primary (the OECD-Model article map used throughout UK treaties — HP §16.1):**
  - **Art 4** — Residence (tie-breaker cascade for individuals and entities).
  - **Art 6** — Income from immovable property (UK is source state for UK property income).
  - **Art 7** — Business profits (permanent establishment threshold).
  - **Art 10 / 11** — Dividends / interest (withholding rates).
  - **Art 13** — Capital gains; Art 13(1) source-state taxing rights on immovable property gains; Art 13(4) property-rich-entity-share extension in modern treaties.
  - **Art 22** — Capital (wealth taxes — UK has none; relevant for incoming Spanish / French residents).
  - **Art 23** — Methods for elimination of double taxation (exemption vs credit method varies by treaty).
  - **Art 24** — Non-discrimination.
  - **Art 25** — Mutual Agreement Procedure (competent-authority dispute resolution).
- **Supporting (specific UK treaty notes per HP §16.5):**
  - UK-US (2001 + 2002 protocol) — saving clause (Art 1(4)) preserves US right to tax US citizens worldwide.
  - UK-France (2008, in force 2009) — Art 24A capital gains UK retains taxing rights on UK property gains.
  - UK-Spain (2013) — Art 13(4) property-rich entity rule applies.
  - UK-India (1993) — older treaty, no Art 13(4) indirect-disposal provision; UK NRCGT applies on UK property-rich shares regardless.
  - UK-Luxembourg (signed 7 June 2022, in force 22 November 2023; effective UK income tax / CGT 6 April 2024, corporation tax 1 April 2024) — brought into modern OECD line per HP §16.3 Wave 5 housekeeping lock.
  - UK-Crown Dependencies (Jersey, Guernsey, Isle of Man — modern 2018+ treaties) — OECD form, Art 13(4) indirect-disposal applies.
- **House position reference:** §10 (DTAs general — locked structure), **§16 in full (Wave 2 DTA extension locked 2026-05-22)**, §16.1 (OECD Model 2017 article map), §16.2 (Art 6 immovable property), §16.3 (Art 13 capital gains + NRCGT override), §16.4 (Art 4 residence tie-breaker cascade), §16.5 (specific UK treaty notes), **§16.6 (NRL is statutory not treaty — load-bearing for this page)**; cross-references §17 (Leaving the UK) for departing-landlord context; §17.4 (NRCGT) for the source-state CGT interaction.

## Framing differentiator (anti-templating, anti-cannibalisation)
This is the **general DTA introduction / orientation** page. It is **not** a country-specific deep-dive (sibling B1 UK-Isle of Man, B2 UK-Spain, B3 UK-India, and the existing site pages on specific treaties cover those). It is **not** a chapter-and-verse OECD Model walkthrough. It is the page a landlord reads when they first encounter the cross-border element of their situation and need to understand the conceptual framework before clicking through to the country-specific page.

The framing differentiator is the **"four assumptions that catch landlords out"** opener — the page leads with the predictable misconceptions and corrects each one against the statute:

1. **"A DTA means I don't pay UK tax on my UK property because I'm not UK-resident."** Wrong. DTAs allocate taxing rights; they do not eliminate them. Article 6 of every OECD-Model treaty gives the source state (UK) primary taxing rights over UK property income. ITTOIA 2005 Part 3 / CTA 2009 Part 4 continue to charge UK source taxation. The treaty determines whether the destination state can also tax (most don't, on UK source property income; some do but with a credit).
2. **"The NRL withholding doesn't apply to me because I'm treaty-resident in [destination]."** Wrong. NRL is **statutory** under FA 1995 Sch 23 + SI 1995/2902. Treaty residence does not displace NRL. Per HP §16.6 verbatim: "Treaty residence does not displace NRL; even a treaty-resolved non-UK resident must apply for gross-payment approval." Tenants / letting agents withhold 20% from gross rent regardless of treaty position unless NRL1 / NRL2 / NRL3 approval is in hand.
3. **"My destination country's tax credit is automatic — I don't need to do anything."** Wrong. Foreign tax credit must be **claimed**, not assumed. Per HP §10 + §16.6 do-not-write list. The relevant tax return (UK self-assessment if claiming credit against UK tax under TIOPA 2010 s.18 / s.130; destination state's return if claiming credit against destination-state tax) must affirmatively claim the credit with evidence of foreign tax paid.
4. **"All UK DTAs are basically the same — they all follow the OECD Model."** Mostly true but with material exceptions. UK-US has a saving clause that preserves US worldwide taxation of citizens. UK-India is an older 1993 treaty without Art 13(4) indirect-disposal provision. UK-France has its own Art 24A capital gains article. UK-Luxembourg only joined the modern OECD line in 2022 (in force from late 2023). Quote the article number from the specific bilateral treaty, not the OECD Model number (per HP §16.1).

The page must then explain — in plain terms — what a DTA actually is (a bilateral agreement between two states allocating taxing rights to prevent double taxation and prevent fiscal evasion), the structural OECD framework most UK treaties follow, the four allocation articles most relevant to property landlords (Art 4 residence, Art 6 immovable property, Art 13 capital gains, Art 23 elimination method), and the practical pathway (identify your residence under each country's domestic law → apply the treaty's Art 4 tie-breaker if dual-resident → apply Art 6 / 13 to allocate taxing rights → apply Art 23 to determine which state gives credit → file in both jurisdictions accordingly).

## Key questions this page must answer
1. **What is a double taxation agreement (DTA)?** (A bilateral treaty between two states allocating taxing rights over cross-border income, capital gains, and capital. UK has DTAs with ~130 jurisdictions, primarily in OECD Model 2017 form. Purpose: prevent double taxation + prevent fiscal evasion.)
2. **What does a DTA actually do — and what does it not do?** (Allocates taxing rights between the two states. Does NOT exempt income that the source state's domestic law charges. Does NOT override domestic anti-avoidance rules. Does NOT remove statutory mechanisms like NRL withholding.)
3. **If I'm not UK-resident but I own UK rental property, does the treaty exempt me from UK tax?** (No. Article 6 of every OECD-Model treaty gives the source state — the UK — primary taxing rights over UK property income. UK domestic ITTOIA 2005 Part 3 / CTA 2009 Part 4 continues to charge. The treaty governs whether the destination state can ALSO tax, not whether the UK can.)
4. **What is the NRL scheme and why does it apply even when I'm treaty-resolved non-UK-resident?** (FA 1995 Sch 23 + SI 1995/2902 — statutory withholding by tenants / letting agents at 20% basic rate. Per HP §16.6: treaty residence does not displace NRL. NRL1 / NRL2 / NRL3 approval needed to receive rent gross. This is the single most common misconception in cross-border property tax.)
5. **What is the Article 4 residence tie-breaker?** (The cascade applied where an individual is resident under both states' domestic law. Per HP §16.4: (1) permanent home — available in one state only? (2) centre of vital interests — closer personal and economic relations? (3) habitual abode — where do they habitually live? (4) nationality — if abode in both or neither? (5) mutual agreement procedure — competent authorities agree.)
6. **What is the Article 6 immovable property rule?** (Source-state primary taxing rights. UK retains primary taxing rights over UK property income. "Immovable property" includes rights to variable / fixed payments for working of mineral deposits and natural resources. Ships, boats and aircraft are not immovable property — Art 8 covers those. DTAs do not generally exempt UK property income — they allocate taxing rights.)
7. **What is the Article 13 capital gains rule, and how does it interact with UK NRCGT?** (Art 13(1) typically gives source state taxing rights on immovable property gains. Art 13(4) extends to gains on alienation of shares in property-rich entities (≥50% of value from immovable property) in modern treaties. UK NRCGT at TCGA 1992 s.1A + Sch 1A / 1B / 4AA applies whether or not the treaty assigns UK taxing rights — where a treaty's Art 13(4) is narrower than NRCGT (older treaties), UK statute still imposes NRCGT. Per HP §16.3.)
8. **What about the Article 23 elimination method — when does each state give credit?** (Exemption method vs credit method varies by treaty. Most UK treaties use the credit method for income earned in the source state. For UK property owned by a destination-state resident, the destination state typically credits UK tax paid against its own tax on the same income / gain. Exemption method is rare in modern UK treaties.)
9. **Are there UK treaties that don't follow the standard OECD pattern, and why does it matter?** (Yes. Per HP §16.5: UK-US has a saving clause (Art 1(4)) preserving US worldwide taxation of US citizens — a US-citizen UK-resident landlord files US returns regardless of UK residence. UK-India (1993) is an older treaty without Art 13(4) indirect-disposal extension; UK NRCGT still applies on property-rich shares. UK-France (2008) has its own Art 24A. UK-Luxembourg only joined the modern OECD line in 2022, effective 2024.)
10. **How do I actually claim foreign tax credit relief?** (Not automatic — must be claimed. Per TIOPA 2010 s.18 (verified at write time as "Entitlement to credit for foreign tax reduces UK tax by amount of the credit", Part 2 Chapter 2): credit allowed under treaty or unilateral relief reduces UK tax by the credit amount. Claim on the relevant tax return with evidence of foreign tax paid. Where no bilateral treaty covers the situation, unilateral relief under TIOPA 2010 s.130 applies.)
11. **What is the Mutual Agreement Procedure (MAP) at Art 25, and when does it matter?** (Competent-authority dispute resolution between the two states. Relevant where the two states disagree on treaty interpretation or where double taxation has actually arisen despite the treaty. For most landlords, MAP is a backstop, not a first-line tool — the bilateral treaty article on the relevant point usually resolves cleanly.)
12. **Practical step-by-step for the cross-border landlord.** (Stage 2 to develop: (1) identify domestic residence in each state; (2) apply Art 4 tie-breaker if dual-resident; (3) identify the relevant allocation article (Art 6 for property income, Art 13 for property gains); (4) check UK statutory overlay (NRL, NRCGT) — these run alongside the treaty, they do not stop it; (5) identify Art 23 elimination method to determine which state gives credit; (6) file in both jurisdictions with consistent positions; (7) claim foreign tax credit where applicable. Walk through one concrete cross-border landlord scenario.)

### Example 2 — The Article 4 residence tie-breaker walked

Mr Almeida (Portuguese national; lived in London 2018-2024 working in finance; relocated to Lisbon May 2024; UK BTL portfolio of two flats retained for income; spends January and August in UK each year visiting family + managing portfolio; Portuguese tax resident from May 2024 under Portuguese domestic rules; UK tax resident under SRT for 2024/25 because of sufficient ties (UK accommodation + UK 90+ days + UK family); dual-resident for 2024/25).

**Art 4 tie-breaker cascade per UK-Portugal DTA (mirrors OECD Model — HP §16.4):**

1. **Permanent home — available in one state only?** Mr Almeida has a home in Lisbon (newly purchased apartment) AND maintains a London flat available to him (rented to neither; furnished + available for his use during visits). Permanent home in BOTH states → cascade continues.
2. **Centre of vital interests — closer personal and economic relations?** Personal: extended family in both states. Economic: UK rental income £85k/year vs Lisbon employment income £180k/year. New employment + new home in Lisbon → centre of vital interests likely in Lisbon → tie-breaker resolves to PORTUGAL-RESIDENT for treaty purposes.

**Tax consequences:**

- For UK domestic law: Mr Almeida is UK-resident for 2024/25 under SRT (sufficient ties).
- For UK-Portugal DTA: Mr Almeida is treaty-resident in Portugal.
- Result: Mr Almeida is treated as a treaty-resolved non-UK resident for Art 6 (UK property income), Art 7 (business profits), Art 13 (capital gains), Art 10/11 (dividends/interest) purposes.

**For UK rental income (Art 6 + ITTOIA 2005 Part 3):**

- UK retains source-state primary taxing rights per Art 6.
- ITTOIA 2005 Part 3 continues to charge UK income tax on the £85k UK rental income.
- NRL scheme bites: from the date Mr Almeida became non-UK-resident under SRT (or earlier — NRL bites from physical non-residence not treaty residence), tenants / agents withhold 20% basic rate unless NRL1 approval held.
- Portugal also taxes the UK rental income (Portuguese residents tax on worldwide income).
- Art 23 elimination: UK-Portugal DTA grants Portugal credit for UK tax paid (credit method). Mr Almeida claims credit on Portuguese return for the UK tax paid → avoids double tax.

**Practical lesson:** Art 4 tie-breaker is the critical mechanism for dual-resident individuals. Quotation from the specific bilateral treaty essential (UK-Portugal in this case; UK-Spain / UK-France / etc may differ in detail). Most cascade resolutions stop at step 2 (centre of vital interests); steps 3 (habitual abode) and 4 (nationality) rarely needed; step 5 (mutual agreement) is a backstop.

### Example 3 — Article 6 + Article 23 elimination walked for a Spanish-resident UK property owner

Mrs Romero (Spanish national; lived in Spain her entire life; never UK-resident; inherited a London BTL from her UK-resident uncle in 2022; rental income £42k/year; spends one week per year in UK visiting tenant + managing repairs).

**UK domestic charges:**

- ITTOIA 2005 Part 3: UK rental income £42k UK-taxable regardless of Mrs Romero's residence. Personal allowance available if she is an EU national (verify post-Brexit personal allowance availability for Spanish nationals at write per ITA 2007 s.56 + s.56A).
- NRL scheme: bites from the start of the letting (NRL is statutory; treaty residence irrelevant). Tenant / agent withholds 20% unless NRL1 approval held.
- UK CGT under TCGA 1992 s.1A applies if Mrs Romero sells the flat (60-day NRCGT return required regardless of treaty).

**UK-Spain DTA application (mirrors OECD Model):**

- Art 6 (immovable property): UK is source state for UK property income → primary taxing rights to UK. Spain (residence state) may also tax under Spanish domestic law on worldwide income.
- Art 23 (elimination): UK-Spain DTA uses credit method on the Spain side. Spain credits UK tax paid against Spanish tax on the same income.

**Tax flow:**

- UK side: Mrs Romero files SA100 + SA105 annually. UK tax computed on £42k less allowable expenses + personal allowance if available. UK rate: 20% basic / 40% higher / 45% additional depending on threshold + other UK income. NRL withholding (if no NRL1) generates credit against UK liability.
- Spain side: Mrs Romero declares UK rental income on Spanish return. Spanish tax computed at applicable Spanish rates. UK tax paid is credited under Art 23 against Spanish tax.

**Net effect:** Mrs Romero pays the HIGHER of UK tax and Spanish tax overall — Spain credits the UK tax against Spanish liability; if Spanish tax > UK tax, she pays the excess in Spain; if Spanish tax < UK tax, no further Spanish tax due (but UK tax not refunded in Spain).

**Practical lesson:** Art 6 + Art 23 is the core mechanic for cross-border landlords. Source state taxes first; residence state taxes second with credit relief. Both states' returns must be filed. Foreign tax credit must be claimed in the residence state with evidence of source-state tax paid. The classical "I'll just pay in Spain" or "I'll just pay in the UK" framings are both wrong — both jurisdictions tax, with elimination via Art 23.

### Example 4 — The NRL-statutory-not-treaty trap (the most common misconception)

Ms Hartmann (German national; treaty-resident in Germany for past 8 years; UK BTL inherited from UK relative 2023; rental income £36k/year managed by London letting agent).

**Common misconception:**

- Ms Hartmann assumes that because she is treaty-resident in Germany, the UK-Germany DTA "exempts" her UK rental from UK tax + UK withholding.

**What the statute actually says:**

- NRL scheme under FA 1995 Sch 23 + SI 1995/2902 is STATUTORY, NOT treaty. Per HP §16.6 verbatim: "Treaty residence does not displace NRL; even a treaty-resolved non-UK resident must apply for gross-payment approval."
- Letting agent must withhold 20% basic rate from gross rent monthly + remit quarterly to HMRC under NRL6 returns. The agent has no statutory discretion to disregard withholding based on treaty position.
- Ms Hartmann's UK-Germany DTA position does NOT relieve the agent of the statutory withholding obligation.

**Operational route:**

- Ms Hartmann applies for NRL1 approval (individual non-resident landlord). Approval depends on UK tax-compliance history — for a new inheritor, HMRC may require an initial compliance period before granting NRL1.
- Once NRL1 granted: letting agent pays rent gross from approval date.
- Ms Hartmann still self-assesses annually on UK rental profits under ITTOIA 2005 Part 3. UK tax paid in UK; credit claimable in Germany under UK-Germany DTA Art 23 (credit method) on the German return.

**What the DTA actually does for Ms Hartmann:**

- Allocates taxing rights: UK gets primary taxing rights on UK property income per Art 6; Germany may also tax with Art 23 credit for UK tax.
- Provides the framework for foreign tax credit claim in Germany.
- Does NOT exempt UK source tax; does NOT displace NRL withholding.

**Practical lesson:** NRL is the single most-misframed UK tax mechanic for cross-border landlords. Treaty residence is irrelevant to NRL withholding; only NRL1 / NRL2 / NRL3 approval changes the agent's withholding behaviour. Per HP §16.6 — load-bearing for the page's whole NRL section.

### Example 5 — The UK-US saving-clause trap for a US-citizen UK landlord

Mrs Anderson (US citizen; UK-resident under SRT since 2015; UK BTL portfolio of three flats generating £95k UK rental income; also holds US-listed equity portfolio + US 401(k)).

**UK domestic position:**

- UK-resident under SRT → UK income tax + CGT on worldwide income + gains under ITTOIA 2005 + TCGA 1992.
- UK rental £95k taxable as UK property income (Part 3 ITTOIA — UK source so charged whether resident or not, but Mrs Anderson is UK-resident here so all income taxable anyway).

**UK-US treaty position (UK-US 2001 + 2002 protocol):**

- Art 1(4) saving clause (HP §16.5): the US preserves the right to tax US citizens on worldwide income REGARDLESS of UK treaty residence.
- Result: Mrs Anderson files US Form 1040 declaring worldwide income (including UK rental + UK gains + US 401(k) income) at US rates.
- US foreign tax credit (Form 1116) available against US tax for UK tax paid on the same income.

**Tax flow:**

- UK side: HMRC SA100 + SA105 with UK rental + worldwide-residence basis. UK tax 20% basic / 40% higher / 45% additional rate on the £95k UK rental + other UK / worldwide income at UK rates.
- US side: IRS 1040 with worldwide income; US tax at applicable US rates. Form 1116 credit for UK tax paid on UK-source income; FEIE (Foreign Earned Income Exclusion) may apply to UK employment income.

**The saving clause trap:**

- Mrs Anderson cannot "treaty-out" of US taxation by being UK-resident — the saving clause preserves US worldwide taxation. Both returns filed annually for life or until US citizenship surrendered.
- Specialist US-UK cross-border tax advice essential (US-UK is the most complex bilateral DTA from a UK private-client perspective).

**Practical lesson:** the UK-US saving clause (Art 1(4)) is the single biggest structural deviation from the OECD Model among UK DTAs. US citizens cannot escape US worldwide taxation by changing residence. Page must surface this prominently for US-citizen-landlord readers; the page can flag the broader US-UK complexity without claiming to resolve it (refer to specialist US-UK adviser).

### Example 6 — The "DTAs are automatic" misconception walked (foreign tax credit must be claimed)

Mr Knowles (UK-resident; small German equity portfolio paying £4,200/year dividends; German withholding tax 26.375% (German Solidaritätszuschlag + 25% standard) deducted at source).

**UK position:**

- Mr Knowles UK-tax-resident → German dividends UK-taxable as foreign dividend income at UK dividend tax rates (8.75% basic / 33.75% higher / 39.35% additional from FY2023+).
- UK-Germany DTA Art 10 caps German withholding at 15% on portfolio dividends; Art 23 (UK-side elimination) credits UK tax for the (capped) German tax paid.

**Two-step claim process:**

1. **Claim the treaty-reduced rate at source.** Mr Knowles must file the relevant German tax-treaty claim form (e.g., German BZSt form) WITH HIS GERMAN BROKER / WITHHOLDING AGENT to obtain the treaty-reduced 15% rate at source. Without this, 26.375% is withheld; Mr Knowles must then claim the excess back via German refund procedure.
2. **Claim foreign tax credit on UK self-assessment.** On UK SA100 + foreign-income pages (SA106), declare the German dividend gross + claim foreign tax credit for German tax paid (capped at treaty rate per DTA). UK tax computed at UK dividend rates; German tax credited.

**What goes wrong without active claiming:**

- If Mr Knowles fails to claim treaty rate at source: 26.375% German tax withheld; UK only credits up to the 15% treaty cap → 11.375% German tax economically lost unless claimed back via German refund (3+ year process).
- If Mr Knowles fails to claim FTC on UK SA100: full UK dividend tax paid on the gross dividend without German credit → economic double tax.

**Practical lesson:** foreign tax credit is NEVER automatic. Both the source-state treaty-reduced-rate claim AND the residence-state foreign-tax-credit claim must be actively pursued with evidence of foreign tax paid. Per TIOPA 2010 s.18 verbatim ("Entitlement to credit for foreign tax reduces UK tax by amount of the credit", Part 2 Chapter 2) — the credit must be CLAIMED. Specialist tax adviser essential for cross-border-asset clients.

### Example 7 — Misframings the page must correct (verbatim do-not-write list)

- **Misframe 1:** "A DTA means I don't pay UK tax on my UK property because I'm not UK-resident." FALSE — DTAs allocate taxing rights; they do not eliminate them. Article 6 of every OECD-Model treaty gives the SOURCE state (UK) primary taxing rights over UK property income. ITTOIA 2005 Part 3 / CTA 2009 Part 4 continue to charge UK tax.
- **Misframe 2:** "The NRL withholding doesn't apply to me because I'm treaty-resident in [destination]." FALSE — NRL is STATUTORY under FA 1995 Sch 23 + SI 1995/2902. Per HP §16.6: "Treaty residence does not displace NRL". Tenants / agents withhold 20% from gross rent regardless of treaty position unless NRL1 / NRL2 / NRL3 approval is in hand.
- **Misframe 3:** "Foreign tax credit is automatic — I don't need to do anything." FALSE — must be CLAIMED on the relevant tax return with evidence of foreign tax paid. Per TIOPA 2010 s.18 verbatim ("Entitlement to credit for foreign tax reduces UK tax by amount of the credit", Part 2 Chapter 2) and the applicable DTA Art 23.
- **Misframe 4:** "All UK DTAs are basically the same — they all follow the OECD Model." MISLEADING — UK-US has Art 1(4) saving clause preserving US worldwide taxation of US citizens; UK-India 1993 is older without Art 13(4) indirect-disposal provision; UK-France 2008 has its own Art 24A; UK-Luxembourg only joined the modern OECD line in 2022 (in force 22 November 2023; effective UK IT/CGT 6 April 2024; CT 1 April 2024 per HP §16.3 Wave 5 lock). Quote article number from the SPECIFIC bilateral treaty per HP §16.1.
- **Misframe 5:** "The DTA decides what tax I owe in the UK." MISLEADING — UK domestic law (ITTOIA 2005 / CTA 2009 / TCGA 1992 / IHTA 1984) decides the UK tax charge; the DTA allocates which state gets primary taxing rights and provides elimination relief, but does not override domestic charging provisions on UK source income / gains.
- **Misframe 6:** "Art 4 tie-breaker is optional — I can choose to be UK-resident or non-UK-resident for treaty purposes." FALSE — Art 4 tie-breaker is a mandatory cascade applied where dual residence exists under both states' domestic law. Not optional; resolves to a single treaty residence based on the factual cascade.
- **Misframe 7:** "Mutual Agreement Procedure (Art 25) is the first line of dispute resolution." MISLEADING — MAP is a BACKSTOP for cases where the two states genuinely disagree on treaty interpretation OR where double taxation actually arises despite the treaty. For most landlords, the bilateral treaty article on the specific point resolves cleanly without invoking MAP.
- **Misframe 8:** "If a DTA's Article 13 doesn't extend to property-rich shares, NRCGT doesn't apply either." FALSE — UK NRCGT at TCGA 1992 s.1A + Sch 1A / 1B / 4AA applies whether or not the treaty assigns UK taxing rights. For older treaties (e.g., UK-India 1993) without Art 13(4), UK statute still imposes NRCGT on UK property-rich shares per HP §16.3.
- **Misframe 9:** "Once I'm treaty-resolved non-UK-resident, my UK rental income is taxed only in the destination country." FALSE — Art 6 gives UK source-state primary taxing rights; ITTOIA 2005 Part 3 charges UK tax; destination country may also tax with credit relief via Art 23.
- **Misframe 10:** "Non-doms can use a DTA to avoid UK tax entirely." LARGELY OBSOLETE post-6 April 2025 + FALSE on the framing anyway. FA 2025 replaced the domicile-based remittance basis with the Foreign Income and Gains (FIG) regime + Long-Term Resident (LTR) test per HP §17.6. Treaty residence + DTA mechanics are SEPARATE from the FIG/TRF/LTR architecture. The "non-dom + DTA" framing is now stale.
- **Misframe 11:** "OECD Model article numbers can be cited interchangeably with any UK treaty's article numbers." FALSE per HP §16.1 — most UK treaties broadly follow OECD Model but the numbering varies (e.g., UK-France Art 24A capital gains; older treaties number articles differently). Quote the article number from the SPECIFIC bilateral treaty being written about, not the OECD Model number.
- **Misframe 12:** "Unilateral relief under TIOPA 2010 s.130 only applies where there's no treaty." MOSTLY TRUE but with nuance — s.130 unilateral relief operates where no bilateral treaty covers the specific situation. It can also operate alongside a treaty where the treaty does not provide adequate relief for a particular type of income or where the relief mechanism is unclear. RUN session checks INTM162000+ for current HMRC framing.
- **Misframe 13:** "If two countries' DTAs both grant taxing rights, I'm taxed twice." FALSE — the elimination article (Art 23) is the operative mechanism. One state taxes; the other state gives credit (credit method) or exempts (exemption method). Double taxation only arises where the treaty interpretation differs between the two states (then MAP applies) or where one state's domestic law fails to grant the relief the treaty mandates.

## FAQ expansion (RUN session polishes prose; 12-14 FAQs target)

1. **Q: What is a double taxation agreement (DTA)?**
   A: A bilateral treaty between two states allocating taxing rights over cross-border income, capital gains, and capital. UK has DTAs with ~130 jurisdictions, primarily in OECD Model 2017 form. Purpose: prevent double taxation + prevent fiscal evasion. Domestic-law machinery giving effect to DTAs in the UK is in TIOPA 2010 Part 2 (ss.2-134), with the Order in Council mechanism at s.2.

2. **Q: What does a DTA actually do — and what does it not do?**
   A: Allocates taxing rights between the two states (which state taxes which income / gain first) + provides elimination relief (foreign tax credit or exemption) for the residence state. Does NOT exempt income that the source state's domestic law charges. Does NOT override domestic anti-avoidance rules. Does NOT remove statutory mechanisms like NRL withholding (per HP §16.6).

3. **Q: If I'm not UK-resident but I own UK rental property, does the treaty exempt me from UK tax?**
   A: No. Article 6 of every OECD-Model treaty gives the source state — the UK — primary taxing rights over UK property income. UK domestic ITTOIA 2005 Part 3 (individuals) / CTA 2009 Part 4 (companies) continues to charge UK tax. The treaty governs whether the destination state can ALSO tax, not whether the UK can. Most modern UK DTAs use the credit method on the destination side per Art 23.

4. **Q: What is the NRL scheme and why does it apply even when I'm treaty-resolved non-UK-resident?**
   A: FA 1995 Sch 23 + SI 1995/2902 — statutory withholding by tenants / letting agents at 20% basic rate. Per HP §16.6: treaty residence does NOT displace NRL. NRL1 (individuals) / NRL2 (jointly-held) / NRL3 (corporate) approval needed to receive rent gross. This is the single most common misconception in cross-border property tax.

5. **Q: What is the Article 4 residence tie-breaker?**
   A: The cascade applied where an individual is resident under BOTH states' domestic law. Per HP §16.4: (1) permanent home — available in one state only? (2) centre of vital interests — closer personal + economic relations? (3) habitual abode — where do they habitually live? (4) nationality — if abode in both or neither? (5) mutual agreement procedure — competent authorities agree. Most cascades stop at step 2.

6. **Q: What is the Article 6 immovable property rule?**
   A: Source-state primary taxing rights. UK retains primary taxing rights over UK property income. "Immovable property" includes rights to variable / fixed payments for working of mineral deposits and natural resources. Ships, boats and aircraft are NOT immovable property — Art 8 covers those. DTAs do not generally exempt UK property income; they allocate taxing rights between source and residence states.

7. **Q: What is the Article 13 capital gains rule, and how does it interact with UK NRCGT?**
   A: Art 13(1) typically gives source state taxing rights on immovable property gains. Art 13(4) extends to gains on alienation of shares in property-rich entities (≥50% of value from immovable property) in MODERN treaties. UK NRCGT at TCGA 1992 s.1A + Sch 1A / 1B / 4AA applies whether or not the treaty assigns UK taxing rights — for older treaties (e.g., UK-India 1993) without Art 13(4), UK statute still imposes NRCGT on UK property-rich shares per HP §16.3.

8. **Q: What about Article 23 — when does each state give credit?**
   A: Exemption method vs credit method varies by treaty. Most modern UK treaties use the CREDIT method for income earned in the source state. For UK property owned by a destination-state resident, the destination state typically credits UK tax paid against its own tax on the same income / gain. Exemption method is rare in modern UK treaties.

9. **Q: Are there UK treaties that don't follow the standard OECD pattern?**
   A: Yes. Per HP §16.5: UK-US (2001 + 2002 protocol) has Art 1(4) saving clause preserving US worldwide taxation of US citizens — a US-citizen UK-resident landlord files US returns regardless of UK residence. UK-India (1993) is an older treaty without Art 13(4) indirect-disposal extension; UK NRCGT still applies on property-rich shares. UK-France (2008) has its own Art 24A. UK-Luxembourg only joined the modern OECD line in 2022 (signed 7 June 2022; in force 22 November 2023; effective UK IT/CGT 6 April 2024; CT 1 April 2024 per HP §16.3 Wave 5 housekeeping lock).

10. **Q: How do I actually claim foreign tax credit relief?**
    A: Not automatic — must be claimed. Per TIOPA 2010 s.18 verbatim ("Entitlement to credit for foreign tax reduces UK tax by amount of the credit", Part 2 Chapter 2): credit allowed under treaty or unilateral relief reduces UK tax by the credited amount. Claim on the relevant tax return (UK self-assessment SA106 foreign-income pages for individuals; equivalent CT600 schedules for companies) with evidence of foreign tax paid. Where no bilateral treaty covers the situation, unilateral relief under TIOPA 2010 s.130 applies.

11. **Q: What is the Mutual Agreement Procedure (MAP) at Art 25, and when does it matter?**
    A: Competent-authority dispute resolution between the two states. Relevant where the two states disagree on treaty interpretation OR where double taxation has actually arisen despite the treaty. For most landlords, MAP is a backstop, not a first-line tool — the bilateral treaty article on the relevant point usually resolves cleanly without MAP escalation.

12. **Q: I'm a US citizen UK-resident. Can I use the UK-US treaty to avoid US tax?**
    A: No. The UK-US treaty Art 1(4) saving clause preserves the US right to tax US citizens on worldwide income. As a US citizen, you file US Form 1040 declaring worldwide income for life or until US citizenship surrendered. UK tax paid is credited against US tax via Form 1116. Specialist US-UK adviser essential — the UK-US is the most complex bilateral DTA from a UK private-client perspective.

13. **Q: Is the old "non-dom + DTA" framework still relevant?**
    A: LARGELY OBSOLETE post-6 April 2025. FA 2025 replaced the domicile-based remittance basis with the Foreign Income and Gains (FIG) regime + Temporary Repatriation Facility (TRF) + Long-Term Resident (LTR) test for IHT (per HP §17.6 Wave 5 domicile reform lock). Treaty residence + DTA mechanics are SEPARATE from FIG/TRF/LTR; the legacy "non-dom + treaty residence" planning structure is now stale.

14. **Q: Step-by-step for the cross-border landlord — what do I actually do?**
    A: (1) Identify domestic residence in each state. (2) Apply Art 4 tie-breaker if dual-resident. (3) Identify the relevant allocation article (Art 6 for property income; Art 13 for property gains; Art 10/11 for dividends / interest). (4) Check UK statutory overlay (NRL, NRCGT) — these run alongside the treaty, they do not stop it. (5) Identify Art 23 elimination method to determine which state gives credit. (6) File in both jurisdictions with consistent positions + claim foreign tax credit where applicable. Specialist cross-border tax adviser essential for any non-trivial position.
