# Contaminated land remediation and risk assessment: the ATED relief architecture for contaminated dwellings, the LRR financial relief cross-reference, and the source-pathway-receptor risk-assessment precondition

## Statutory anchor
- **Primary (the ATED-side reliefs that bite on contaminated dwellings):**
  - **FA 2013 s.134** "Property rental businesses: transitional relief" (verified verbatim at legislation.gov.uk 2026-05-27). Relief applies where the dwelling is unoccupied and one of four conditions is met: (1) "steps are being taken to secure that the interest will be sold without undue delay"; (2) "steps are being taken to demolish the dwelling" with any replacement constructed for "relievable" use; (3) "steps are being taken to convert the dwelling into a different dwelling" for relievable use; (4) "steps are being taken to secure that the dwelling will be converted into a building other than a dwelling without undue delay". Day X qualifies for s.134 relief where preceded by s.133 qualifying-rental-business days. "Without undue delay" is defined as delay only "justified by commercial considerations or cannot be avoided". No explicit overall time limit but the "without undue delay" anchor disciplines long-pause cases.
  - **FA 2013 s.138** "Property developers" — dwelling held as trading stock by a property developer carrying on a property development trade for at least 2 years (HP §18.3). Available for contaminated-land sites being prepared for development as trading stock.
  - **FA 2013 s.141** "Property traders" — dwelling held as trading stock by a property trader (buy-sell business). Available where contaminated-land remediation is incidental to a property-trading business.
  - **FA 2013 s.137** "Dwellings open to the public" — commercially run and open to public for ≥28 days/year; rarely operative for contaminated sites but Stage 2 considers.
- **Primary (the LRR financial relief on remediation expenditure):**
  - **CTA 2009 Part 14 (ss.143-218)** "Remediation of contaminated or derelict land" — the operative Land Remediation Relief (LRR) statute. **s.143** establishes "qualifying land remediation expenditure" requirements: expenditure on relevant land in a contaminated or derelict state, on relevant remediation activities, that is allowable as a deduction in computing profits. **100% deduction** for qualifying expenditure (current CT-period deduction) PLUS **50% additional deduction** under s.1149 CTA 2009 / s.1147 — total **150% relief** for qualifying LRR claimants.
  - **CTA 2009 s.146** "Polluter exclusion" — LRR is denied where the claimant or a connected person caused the contamination. Sessions writing on LRR-and-developer pages must walk this exclusion: a developer who buys contaminated land from an arms-length seller can claim; a developer who contaminated the site itself, or who is connected to the polluter, cannot.
  - **CTA 2009 s.1149** "Additional deduction for qualifying land remediation expenditure" — the 50% additional deduction mechanic.
  - **CTA 2009 s.1147** "Entitlement to relief: company" — the entitlement provisions.
  - Existing site pages on LRR detail: `land-remediation-relief-150-percent-claim-mechanics-ltdco-developer-investor` and `land-remediation-relief-cta-2009-part-14-100-50-percent-deduction-polluter-exclusion`. Stage 2 cross-links rather than duplicating.
- **Primary (the contaminated-land regulatory regime that defines the underlying "contaminated" status):**
  - **Environmental Protection Act 1990 Part 2A (ss.78A-78YC)** — the statutory contaminated-land regime. **s.78A** defines "contaminated land" as land that appears to the local authority to be in such a condition that "significant harm is being caused or there is a significant possibility of such harm being caused" OR "significant pollution of controlled waters is being caused or there is a significant possibility of such pollution being caused". The local authority is the primary contamination-determination body; HMRC's LRR / ATED scope cross-applies the EPA 1990 Part 2A definitional architecture.
  - **DEFRA Technical Guidance (CLR Report 11 + subsequent)** — the operative risk-assessment framework. The source-pathway-receptor (SPR) conceptual model: contamination is established where there is (a) a contaminant source, (b) a pathway, and (c) a receptor (human, ecological, controlled-water) — all three must be present and linked for the land to be "contaminated" under EPA 1990 Part 2A. Sessions writing on risk-assessment copy must walk the SPR model explicitly; the popular "any soil contaminant = contaminated land" simplification is wrong.
- **Primary (the SDLT-adjacent doctrine for severely-contaminated property):**
  - **PN Bewley v HMRC [2019] UKFTT 65 (TC)** — the SDLT "uninhabitable property" test (HP §1.C). Where a property is so dilapidated, contaminated, or structurally compromised that it is "not suitable for use as a dwelling" at the effective date of the SDLT transaction, the property may be charged at the **non-residential** SDLT rates rather than the higher residential / 3%-surcharge / 15%-flat-rate / FBT rates. Bewley is the leading authority. Sessions on contaminated-land + ATED must distinguish: ATED scope depends on the dwelling being a residential dwelling worth >£500k on the operative valuation date (FA 2013 s.94 + s.116 + s.117); a Bewley-qualifying severely-contaminated property may fall outside ATED scope entirely (no "dwelling" exists in the FA 2013 sense). HP §1.C carries the locked framing.
- **Primary (the NRCGT and CGT interaction at disposal):**
  - **TCGA 1992 s.1A + Schs 1A/1B/4AA** (HP §17.4) — non-resident CGT on UK land disposals. Disposal of a contaminated-land site by a non-resident corporate landlord triggers NRCGT regardless of contamination status (though the contamination affects the disposal proceeds figure, not the charging mechanism).
  - **TCGA 1992 s.38** — allowable expenditure on CGT calculation: enhancement expenditure that is reflected in the state or nature of the asset at disposal. Remediation expenditure that improves the land's state (decontamination → marketable) is allowable as enhancement expenditure under s.38(1)(b). LRR-claimed expenditure cannot also be claimed as enhancement expenditure (the standard no-double-relief discipline) — Stage 2 verifies the LRR / s.38 interaction.
- **House position reference:** §1.C (Bewley uninhabitable-property test — Wave 7 SDLT extension lock 2026-05-24), §2 (ATED bands 2026/27 verified gov.uk 2026-05-22), §17.4 (NRCGT — TCGA 1992 s.1A + Schs 1A/1B/4AA), **§18 (Wave 3 ATED extension full)**, §18.3 (relief catalogue — ss.133-150 including s.134 transitional, s.138 developer, s.141 trader), §18.4 (return mechanics + valuation), §18.5 (ATED-CGT abolition + NRCGT interaction). ## Framing differentiator (anti-templating, anti-cannibalisation)

This page is the **cross-regime page** on contaminated-land tax architecture for envelope owners. It sits in the ATED bucket because the operative ATED relief decision is what triggers many envelope owners to engage with contaminated-land taxation; but the page must address the full cross-regime picture because contaminated-land taxation is irreducibly multi-statute. The framing differentiator:

1. **ATED is the first lens, not the only lens.** For an envelope owner contemplating a contaminated-land remediation programme, the immediate questions are: (a) does ATED apply during the works? (b) which ATED relief (if any) bites? (c) what is the ongoing return obligation? Sessions answer these first, then move to LRR / SDLT / NRCGT.
2. **s.134 transitional relief is the most-frequently-operative ATED relief on contaminated sites.** Where the dwelling moves from s.133 rental use to a contaminated-state non-use, the s.134 four-conditions architecture (sell / demolish / convert-to-different-dwelling / convert-to-non-residential — all "without undue delay") is the route. Sessions must walk the s.134 conditions carefully — the "without undue delay" anchor disciplines long-pause cases and is the most common failure mode for ATED relief on contaminated sites.
3. **s.138 / s.141 developer / trader reliefs require trading-stock characterisation.** A contaminated-land site held as INVESTMENT does NOT qualify for s.138 / s.141 — only sites held as trading stock by a developer (2-yr trade gateway) or trader. Sessions distinguish investment vs trading-stock characterisation (cross-applies the Wave 9 Bucket B Pickford / Marson / Ramsay framework from HP §22 / §28).
4. **LRR (CTA 2009 Part 14) is the financial relief on the remediation EXPENDITURE, separate from ATED.** LRR delivers 150% relief (100% standard + 50% additional) on qualifying land remediation expenditure for companies in CT scope. For non-resident corporate landlords post-6-April-2020 (B17), LRR is now in scope (was IT-regime previously). Sessions cross-link to the existing LRR pages (`land-remediation-relief-150-percent-claim-mechanics-ltdco-developer-investor` + `land-remediation-relief-cta-2009-part-14-100-50-percent-deduction-polluter-exclusion`) rather than duplicating; the differentiation is that B18 is the ATED-first orientation that walks LRR as supporting context.
5. **The polluter-exclusion at CTA 2009 s.146.** LRR is denied where the claimant or a connected person caused the contamination. Sessions walking developer-side contaminated-land strategies must walk this exclusion as the most common practitioner trap.
6. **The risk-assessment precondition: SPR model under DEFRA technical guidance.** Contamination status is established via the source-pathway-receptor (SPR) framework. Sessions must walk SPR: source (the contaminant), pathway (e.g. soil-water-soil-air-soil-plant), receptor (human / ecological / controlled-water). Where any of source / pathway / receptor is missing, the land is NOT "contaminated" under EPA 1990 Part 2A — and LRR / ATED-relief-on-contamination claims fail at the threshold. SPR is the operative gatekeeper.
7. **The Bewley distinction for severely-contaminated property (HP §1.C).** Where contamination is so severe that the property is not "suitable for use as a dwelling" at any point in the relevant period, the property may fall outside the FA 2013 "dwelling" definition entirely (ATED scope question) AND may be charged at non-residential SDLT rates at acquisition (Bewley). Sessions distinguish Bewley scenarios (no-longer-a-dwelling) from contaminated-but-still-a-dwelling scenarios (within ATED scope but eligible for s.134 / s.138 / s.141 relief).
8. **The NRCGT + s.38 enhancement-expenditure interaction at disposal.** Remediation expenditure that improves the land's state is enhancement expenditure under TCGA 1992 s.38(1)(b) at disposal. LRR-claimed expenditure cannot double-relieve as enhancement expenditure. Sessions walk the no-double-relief discipline + the ATED-CGT abolition (HP §18.5) framing.
9. **The risk-assessment documentation pattern.** Phase 1 desk study + Phase 2 intrusive investigation (soil sampling, groundwater monitoring) + Phase 3 remediation strategy + Phase 4 verification report. Each phase generates documentation that supports both EPA 1990 Part 2A determinations AND HMRC LRR / ATED-relief claims. Sessions walk the four-phase pattern explicitly.
10. **The local-authority determination layer.** EPA 1990 Part 2A determinations are made by the local authority (s.78A) — not by HMRC, not by the developer. HMRC accepts local-authority determinations as evidence of contaminated status; absence of formal determination is not necessarily fatal (the source-pathway-receptor framework can be established by the claimant via professional consultant reports) but is a meaningful evidential weakness. Sessions walk the local-authority engagement pattern.

## Key questions this page must answer
1. **Does ATED apply to a contaminated UK residential dwelling held by a non-natural person?** (Generally yes if the property remains a "dwelling" under FA 2013 s.116-117 and is worth >£500k on the operative valuation date. The contamination may reduce the taxable value (s.102 valuation moment) but does not remove the property from scope. Bewley-extreme contamination may take the property outside the dwelling definition entirely — fact-specific.)
2. **Which ATED reliefs are available for contaminated-land scenarios?** (s.134 transitional relief — for unoccupied periods where steps are being taken to sell / demolish / convert / convert-to-non-residential without undue delay; s.138 property-developer trade relief — for sites held as trading stock by a 2-yr+ developer; s.141 property-trader relief — for sites held as trading stock by a property trader. All claimed on the return per Sch 33 para 1 — no automatic application.)
3. **What is the "without undue delay" standard under s.134?** (Per s.134 definition: delay is acceptable only if "justified by commercial considerations or cannot be avoided". Sessions walk the evidential pattern — planning timelines, contractor lead times, remediation programme dates, supply-chain documentation.)
4. **What is Land Remediation Relief (LRR) and who can claim?** (CTA 2009 Part 14. 150% total relief (100% standard deduction + 50% additional deduction) on qualifying land remediation expenditure. Companies in CT scope — UK-resident and (post-6-April-2020) non-resident corporate landlords. s.146 polluter exclusion: claimant or connected person did not cause the contamination.)
5. **What is "qualifying land remediation expenditure"?** (Per s.143 CTA 2009: expenditure on relevant land in a contaminated or derelict state, on relevant remediation activities, allowable as a deduction in computing profits. Stage 2 walks the detailed conditions.)
6. **What is the source-pathway-receptor (SPR) risk-assessment model?** (DEFRA technical guidance. Contamination requires: source (contaminant), pathway (route of exposure), receptor (human / ecological / controlled-water). All three must be present and linked. Absence of any one element means the land is not "contaminated" under EPA 1990 Part 2A.)
7. **How is "contaminated land" defined under EPA 1990 Part 2A?** (Per s.78A: land that appears to the local authority to be in such a condition that significant harm or pollution is being caused or there is a significant possibility of such harm or pollution.)
8. **Who makes the contamination determination — HMRC, the local authority, or the claimant?** (Primary determination: local authority under EPA 1990 Part 2A s.78A. HMRC accepts local-authority determinations as evidence. Absence of formal determination is not fatal — claimant can establish SPR via professional consultant reports — but is an evidential weakness.)
9. **What is the Bewley test and when does it apply?** (PN Bewley v HMRC [2019] UKFTT 65 — SDLT test for property so dilapidated / contaminated / structurally compromised it is "not suitable for use as a dwelling" at the effective date. Non-residential SDLT rates apply; HP §1.C carries the locked framing. Bewley scenarios may also fall outside ATED's "dwelling" definition — sessions must distinguish from contaminated-but-still-a-dwelling cases.)
10. **What is the four-phase risk-assessment documentation pattern?** (Phase 1 desk study + Phase 2 intrusive investigation (soil sampling, groundwater monitoring) + Phase 3 remediation strategy + Phase 4 verification report. Each phase generates documentation supporting EPA 1990 Part 2A determinations and HMRC relief claims.)
11. **What is the no-double-relief discipline between LRR and TCGA s.38 enhancement expenditure?** (Remediation expenditure improving the land's state is enhancement expenditure under s.38(1)(b) at disposal. LRR-claimed expenditure cannot also be claimed as enhancement expenditure on disposal. Sessions walk the discipline.)
12. **What is the polluter-exclusion trap for developers?** (CTA 2009 s.146. Developer who caused the contamination, or who is connected to the polluter, cannot claim LRR. Common scenario: corporate group with one entity that operated a contaminating industrial use, then transfers the land to a sibling entity for redevelopment — the sibling is connected and cannot claim LRR. Sessions walk this carefully.)

### Example 1 — The cross-regime decision tree for a contaminated dwelling (the page's first concrete artefact)

Render as semantic HTML `<ol>`. This is the cross-regime-anchor an envelope owner runs before committing to remediation:

1. **Is the property still a "dwelling" under FA 2013 s.116 + s.117?** (If severely contaminated to the point of being "not suitable for use as a dwelling" at the relevant date, Bewley analysis applies — see step 2. If still a dwelling, ATED scope continues — proceed to step 3.)
2. **Bewley alternative path:** if Bewley applies, the property may fall outside ATED scope (no "dwelling" exists in the FA 2013 sense) AND may be charged at non-residential SDLT rates per HP §1.C. Document the Bewley-state with contemporaneous evidence (photographs, surveyor reports, EPA 1990 Part 2A determination if any). Cross-link to SDLT Bewley sibling.
3. **If still a dwelling and worth >£500k on operative valuation date: ATED applies for the chargeable period.** Identify the operative ATED relief: (a) s.134 transitional if dwelling unoccupied + sell / demolish / convert / convert-to-non-residential "without undue delay"; (b) s.138 if held as trading stock by 2-yr+ developer; (c) s.141 if held as trading stock by property trader; (d) other reliefs if conditions met.
4. **Apply s.134 conditions if going the transitional route:** dwelling unoccupied throughout the relief period; one of four "steps being taken" conditions met (sell / demolish / convert-to-different-dwelling / convert-to-non-residential); "without undue delay" discipline maintained with contemporaneous evidence (planning timelines, contractor engagements, supply-chain documentation).
5. **Run the SPR risk-assessment per DEFRA guidance.** Phase 1 desk study + Phase 2 intrusive investigation + Phase 3 remediation strategy + Phase 4 verification report. Establish source (contaminant), pathway (route of exposure), receptor (human / ecological / controlled-water) for EPA 1990 Part 2A determination.
6. **Apply CTA 2009 Part 14 LRR (if claimant is a company in CT scope):** check s.143 qualifying-land-remediation-expenditure conditions; verify s.146 polluter exclusion is NOT triggered (claimant + connected persons did not cause contamination); claim 100% standard deduction + 50% additional deduction = 150% relief.
7. **At disposal: NRCGT + s.38 enhancement-expenditure analysis.** TCGA 1992 s.1A + Schs for non-resident corporates. s.38(1)(b) enhancement expenditure includes remediation improving the land's state — but NO double-relief with LRR-claimed expenditure.
8. **Document everything contemporaneously.** Local authority engagement + SPR phase reports + remediation programme + contractor records + cost ledger split between LRR-claimed and non-LRR expenditure.

### Example 2 — The s.134 transitional relief walked (the most-common ATED scenario)

Bridge Holdings Limited (UK ltd-co; owns a £1.6m London town house acquired 2018; let throughout 2018-2024 to unconnected tenants; s.133 rental relief claimed throughout). In December 2024, the let ends. A pre-purchase environmental survey commissioned by the prospective buyer surfaces historical contamination from a former dry-cleaning business on the site (Phase 1 desk study reveals 1950s-1970s solvent contamination; Phase 2 confirms TCE / PCE soil contamination at 4-6m depth). The sale falls through. Bridge Holdings now needs to remediate or demolish.

**ATED analysis (2024/25 onwards):**

- Property is unoccupied from December 2024.
- s.133 rental relief no longer available (no qualifying rental business).
- Question: does s.134 transitional relief apply?

**s.134 four-conditions analysis:**

- Condition (1) "steps being taken to sell without undue delay": Bridge engages estate agent in January 2025 + new buyer found August 2025 + completion January 2026. Sale executed within 13 months of let-end. "Without undue delay" arguably met (sale frustrated by contamination disclosure; commercial-considerations justify delay). PASS for the 2024/25 + 2025/26 segments.
- Alternative Condition (4) "steps being taken to convert to non-residential": if Bridge instead committed to converting the building to non-residential (office / mixed-use), s.134 relief would apply for the conversion period subject to "without undue delay".

**ATED relief claim:**

- 2024/25 chargeable period: s.134 relief claimed for the unoccupied-with-sale-pending portion (Jan-March 2025, 90 days). Pre-Jan 2025: s.133 rental relief claimed (Apr-Dec 2024, 275 days). Combined claim-only return; ATED tax £0.
- 2025/26 chargeable period: s.134 relief claimed for the full period (Apr-Mar 2025-26; sale completed January 2026 within the period). Claim-only return; ATED tax £0.

**Cross-regime: LRR analysis (if Bridge incurs remediation expenditure):**

- If Bridge spends £180,000 on TCE / PCE remediation (Phase 3 remediation strategy + Phase 4 verification) BEFORE the eventual sale or conversion, LRR analysis applies.
- s.146 polluter exclusion: Bridge did not cause the dry-cleaning contamination (1950s-1970s historical use by unrelated prior owner). PASS.
- s.143 qualifying-land-remediation-expenditure: TCE / PCE remediation is qualifying activity on contaminated land. PASS.
- LRR claim: 100% standard deduction (£180k against CT profits) + 50% additional deduction (£90k additional) = **150% relief on £180k = effective £270k deduction**.
- For a 25% CT-rate company: tax saving = £270k × 25% = **£67,500 LRR tax saving**.
- Plus the LRR-claimed £180k cannot also be claimed as s.38 enhancement expenditure on subsequent disposal (no-double-relief discipline).

**Operational lesson:** s.134 transitional + LRR can stack effectively for contaminated-dwelling scenarios. Document the "without undue delay" rationale contemporaneously; engage environmental consultants for the four-phase SPR risk-assessment; structure remediation invoicing to support both LRR and any s.134 evidence.

### Example 3 — The "without undue delay" failure mode

Magnolia Property Investments Limited (UK ltd-co; owns a £2.4m London BTL flat acquired 2017; let throughout 2017-2022). In May 2022, the let ends + Magnolia's directors discuss converting the flat to non-residential use (office or commercial). Magnolia files claim-only ATED returns for 2022/23 + 2023/24 + 2024/25 + 2025/26 claiming s.134 transitional relief on the "convert to non-residential" condition. NO planning application filed. NO contractor engaged. NO programme dates set. Director minutes record "exploring options".

**s.134 analysis (challenged at HMRC enquiry):**

- Condition (4) "steps being taken to secure that the dwelling will be converted into a building other than a dwelling without undue delay".
- "Steps being taken" requires demonstrable action — planning applications, contractor engagements, programme dates, architects' instructions.
- "Without undue delay" requires that delay be "justified by commercial considerations or cannot be avoided".
- Magnolia has NO planning application + NO contractor + NO programme dates after 4 years (May 2022 - May 2026). "Steps being taken" cannot be sustained on the evidence.
- s.134 relief DENIED for 2022/23 + 2023/24 + 2024/25 + 2025/26.

**Tax consequence (4 chargeable periods of denied relief):**

- 2022/23 chargeable amount: £29,150 (2022/23 band 3 £2m-£5m, indexed figures; Stage 2 verifies).
- 2023/24 chargeable amount: £29,150 (assume same).
- 2024/25 chargeable amount: £31,050.
- 2025/26 chargeable amount: £31,050.
- **Total ATED due: c. £120,400.**
- Plus FA 2007 Sch 24 inaccuracy penalty: deliberate-not-concealed prompted = 35-70% on the £120,400 → £42,140 to £84,280 penalty.
- Plus interest under FA 2009 s.101 + FA 2009 s.102.
- Plus Sch 33 para 3 amendment window closed for 2022/23 + 2023/24 + 2024/25 (within 2026 timing); the response track is direct correspondence with HMRC's ATED team + voluntary disclosure framing.

**Remediation route:**

- Honest amendment / disclosure: refile claim-only returns acknowledging no s.134 relief; pay full ATED + interest + reduced Sch 24 penalty for unprompted disclosure (15-30% prompted-vs-unprompted matrix).
- Engage planning consultant + architect immediately to begin genuine conversion programme; document the new programme commencement as evidence of genuine future "steps being taken" for current chargeable period.
- Consider de-envelope alternative (B5 strategic).

**Operational lesson:** s.134 transitional relief is not a parking-bay for "we'll get to it eventually". The "without undue delay" anchor disciplines long-pause cases. Contemporaneous evidence of genuine steps (planning applications, contractor engagements, programme dates) essential; "exploring options" or "considering" cannot sustain a multi-year s.134 claim.

### Example 4 — The polluter-exclusion trap for connected developers

Ironwood Developments Group:
- Ironwood Industrial Limited (Cayman; operated a contaminating chemicals manufacturing facility on a UK site from 1998-2019; left the site contaminated with various solvents).
- Ironwood Property Limited (UK; same beneficial-owner group; acquired the contaminated land from Ironwood Industrial in 2020 for £4m, intending to remediate and redevelop as £30m residential scheme).

**LRR analysis per CTA 2009 s.146 polluter exclusion:**

- Ironwood Property is the prospective LRR claimant (the entity incurring remediation expenditure on the now-acquired contaminated land).
- s.146 polluter exclusion: relief is denied where the claimant OR A CONNECTED PERSON caused the contamination.
- Ironwood Industrial (the polluter) and Ironwood Property are CONNECTED PERSONS within the same beneficial-owner group.
- s.146 exclusion BITES. **LRR DENIED.**

**Tax consequence:**

- Ironwood Property's remediation expenditure (assume £2.5m over 18 months): NO 100% standard deduction + NO 50% additional = NO 150% LRR relief.
- Lost LRR tax saving (25% CT rate): £2.5m × 1.5 × 25% = **£937,500 lost LRR tax saving**.
- Standard deduction may still be available as a normal trading expense (or as enhancement expenditure on eventual disposal under TCGA s.38(1)(b)) — but the 50% additional deduction is permanently lost.

**Structural alternatives:**

- Sell the contaminated site to a genuinely unconnected developer at arms-length consideration; new buyer can claim LRR on their remediation expenditure. Group exits the LRR-eligible deal but recovers the land's value at sale.
- Restructure to break the s.146 connected-persons link (typically not feasible without losing economic control — connected-persons rules are sticky).
- Accept the LRR exclusion and proceed without the additional deduction.

**Operational lesson:** the polluter-exclusion trap is the single most expensive LRR mistake. Corporate groups that include a polluting sibling cannot move the contaminated land internally and claim LRR on the receiving entity. Sessions on developer-side strategy must walk this carefully BEFORE any intra-group transfer of contaminated land.

### Example 5 — The Bewley distinction walked (severely-contaminated property out of ATED scope)

Cregeen Holdings Limited (UK ltd-co; acquired a former Victorian school building in 2024 for £620k, intending to convert to residential apartments). At acquisition, the building was: (a) un-glazed throughout (glass removed by metal thieves); (b) roof partially collapsed; (c) all internal services stripped (no electricity / gas / water); (d) significant asbestos contamination; (e) Japanese knotweed infestation throughout grounds; (f) structural engineer report: building "incapable of being occupied as a dwelling in its current state; substantial remedial works required before any habitation is possible".

**Bewley analysis at SDLT effective date (acquisition):**

- Per PN Bewley v HMRC [2019] UKFTT 65 (TC): the test is whether the property is "suitable for use as a dwelling" at the effective date.
- Cregeen's building: no glazing + partial roof + no services + asbestos + structural engineer evidence of unsuitability. PASSES Bewley.
- SDLT at non-residential rates (no residential 3% surcharge; no 15% flat rate; no FBT) — significant SDLT saving on £620k acquisition. Cross-link HP §1.C + sibling Bewley page.

**ATED analysis for the immediate chargeable period after acquisition:**

- Is the property a "dwelling" under FA 2013 s.116-117?
- The same Bewley logic potentially extends: if the building is not suitable for use as a dwelling, it may fall outside the FA 2013 "dwelling" definition for ATED scope.
- Cregeen's building, in its post-acquisition pre-remediation state, is arguably NOT a dwelling for FA 2013 purposes → ATED scope does not apply.
- Sub-£500k status irrelevant if scope test fails first.

**ATED analysis as remediation progresses:**

- As Cregeen's remediation programme advances (roof reinstated; services reconnected; asbestos removed; structural works completed), the building moves from non-dwelling status back to dwelling status.
- At the point the building becomes "suitable for use as a dwelling" (Bewley state ends), ATED scope re-attaches if value > £500k on the operative valuation date.
- Operational discipline: identify the date of "Bewley-state termination" with contemporaneous evidence (building control sign-off; habitation certificates; survey reports); from that date, ATED applies.

**LRR analysis on remediation expenditure:**

- Asbestos removal + Japanese knotweed remediation are qualifying remediation activities per CTA 2009 s.143.
- Cregeen is not the polluter (former school operator caused the contamination historically) — s.146 exclusion not triggered.
- LRR claim: 150% relief on qualifying expenditure.

**Operational lesson:** the Bewley distinction matters for ATED scope as well as SDLT rates. Severely-contaminated / dilapidated buildings may fall outside the FA 2013 dwelling definition entirely; LRR remains available on the remediation expenditure during the conversion. Document the Bewley-state carefully + the transition back to dwelling-state to support the scope-attachment discipline.

### Example 6 — Misframings the page must correct (verbatim do-not-write list)

- **Misframe 1:** "Any contaminated land qualifies for Land Remediation Relief." FALSE per CTA 2009 s.143 — LRR requires QUALIFYING land remediation expenditure on relevant land in a contaminated or derelict state, on relevant remediation activities, allowable as a deduction. The SPR risk-assessment establishes "contaminated" status under EPA 1990 Part 2A first; LRR conditions then apply.
- **Misframe 2:** "Polluter exclusion only applies to the entity that caused the pollution." FALSE per CTA 2009 s.146 — the exclusion catches the CLAIMANT or any CONNECTED PERSON. Intra-group transfer of contaminated land from the polluting sibling to a sibling redevelopment company does NOT escape the exclusion — both are connected for s.146 purposes.
- **Misframe 3:** "ATED s.134 transitional relief covers any unoccupied period." FALSE per FA 2013 s.134 — relief requires (a) the dwelling to be unoccupied AND (b) one of four specific "steps being taken" conditions (sell / demolish / convert-to-different-dwelling / convert-to-non-residential) AND (c) "without undue delay" discipline. "We may sell someday" or "we're thinking about converting" fails the evidential test.
- **Misframe 4:** "All ATED reliefs are automatic where conditions are met." FALSE per FA 2013 Sch 33 para 1 + s.159 — reliefs MUST BE CLAIMED on the return. Claim-only return discipline applies even where tax due after relief is £0.
- **Misframe 5:** "Land Remediation Relief gives 150% deduction for any contamination-related expenditure." MISLEADING per CTA 2009 s.143 — only QUALIFYING land remediation expenditure attracts the 150% relief (100% standard + 50% additional). Capital cost of the underlying land or building is NOT qualifying. Routine maintenance is NOT qualifying. Remediation must address contaminated or derelict state.
- **Misframe 6:** "Source-pathway-receptor risk assessment is optional." FALSE per DEFRA Statutory Guidance + EPA 1990 Part 2A — SPR is the operative framework for establishing "contaminated land" status. Without SPR analysis demonstrating source + pathway + receptor (all three linked), the land is not "contaminated" under EPA 1990 Part 2A and LRR claims are exposed to HMRC challenge at threshold.
- **Misframe 7:** "Bewley uninhabitable-property test only applies to SDLT, not ATED." MISLEADING — the Bewley reasoning extends to the FA 2013 "dwelling" definition for ATED scope. Severely-contaminated property that is "not suitable for use as a dwelling" may fall outside ATED scope entirely. HP §1.C carries the SDLT framing; the ATED extension is fact-specific but doctrinally available.
- **Misframe 8:** "Remediation expenditure can be claimed as both LRR and TCGA s.38 enhancement expenditure." FALSE per the standard no-double-relief discipline — LRR-claimed expenditure is excluded from s.38(1)(b) enhancement expenditure on subsequent disposal. The two reliefs are mutually exclusive on the same expenditure.
- **Misframe 9:** "Local authority contamination determination is required for LRR." MISLEADING — local-authority EPA 1990 Part 2A determination is the gold-standard evidence, but ABSENCE of formal determination is not necessarily fatal. The claimant can establish SPR via professional consultant reports (Phase 1 + Phase 2 + Phase 3 + Phase 4 documentation). Local-authority engagement is operationally valuable but not a strict precondition.
- **Misframe 10:** "Phase 1 desk study alone is sufficient evidence of contamination." FALSE — Phase 1 establishes potential contamination based on historical land-use research; Phase 2 intrusive investigation (soil samples, groundwater monitoring) is required to CONFIRM contamination. LRR claims based solely on Phase 1 are exposed to HMRC challenge.
- **Misframe 11:** "Property held as investment can qualify for ATED s.138 / s.141 developer / trader relief." FALSE per FA 2013 ss.138 + 141 — both reliefs require trading-stock characterisation. Property held as INVESTMENT (with rental yield being primary purpose) is on the wrong side of the business / investment line; Pawson / Ramsay analysis applies. Trading-stock evidence: 2-yr+ developer trade (s.138), buy-sell trader pattern (s.141), accounting treatment as stock (not investment property), commercial intent at acquisition.
- **Misframe 12:** "LRR was abolished or restricted after Brexit." FALSE — CTA 2009 Part 14 LRR remains in force. Post-Brexit changes to State Aid considerations affected some LRR sub-categories (e.g. derelict land remediation reliefs that previously fell within EU State Aid framework) but the core LRR architecture continues. RUN session verifies current legislative state at write per §16.35.
- **Misframe 13:** "Non-resident corporate landlords cannot claim LRR." FALSE post-6 April 2020 per FA 2019 Sch 5 + B17 — non-resident corporate landlords are within CT scope post-2020 and can claim LRR on qualifying remediation expenditure on UK property held by them. The pre-2020 IT-regime had no LRR analogue; the 2020 transition opened LRR to non-resident corporate landlords.

## FAQ expansion (RUN session polishes prose; 12-14 FAQs target)

1. **Q: My company owns a £1.4m London BTL flat that's now contaminated. Does ATED still apply?**
   A: GENERALLY YES, if the property remains a "dwelling" under FA 2013 s.116-117 and is worth >£500k on the operative valuation date. The contamination may reduce the taxable value (s.102 valuation moment) but doesn't remove the property from scope. The exception: Bewley-extreme contamination making the property "not suitable for use as a dwelling" may take the property outside FA 2013 dwelling definition entirely — fact-specific.

2. **Q: Which ATED relief applies during a contaminated-land remediation?**
   A: Most commonly FA 2013 s.134 transitional relief if the dwelling is unoccupied AND steps are being taken to (a) sell, (b) demolish, (c) convert to a different dwelling, or (d) convert to a building other than a dwelling — all "without undue delay". Alternative: s.138 property-developer trade relief if the site is held as trading stock by a 2-yr+ developer; s.141 property-trader relief for a trader. All claimed on the ATED return per Sch 33 para 1.

3. **Q: What does "without undue delay" mean under s.134?**
   A: Per FA 2013 s.134 definition: delay is acceptable only if "justified by commercial considerations or cannot be avoided". Contemporaneous evidence required: planning timelines, contractor lead times, remediation programme dates, supply-chain documentation. "We're thinking about it" or "exploring options" fails. Multi-year claims without active programme dates are at high risk of HMRC challenge.

4. **Q: What is Land Remediation Relief (LRR)?**
   A: CTA 2009 Part 14. A CT-side relief delivering 150% total deduction (100% standard + 50% additional under s.1149) on qualifying land remediation expenditure on contaminated or derelict land. Available to companies in CT scope — UK-resident AND non-resident corporate landlords post-6 April 2020 (B17). The 150% relief multiplied by the company's CT rate (25% main / 19% SPR) gives the tax saving on the qualifying expenditure.

5. **Q: Who is excluded from LRR under the polluter exclusion?**
   A: Per CTA 2009 s.146: the claimant OR any CONNECTED PERSON who caused the contamination. Common trap: corporate group with one entity that operated a contaminating industrial use, then transfers the land to a sibling entity for redevelopment — the sibling is connected and CANNOT claim LRR. The exclusion is sticky; intra-group transfers do not escape it. Only genuinely arms-length acquirers with no connection to the polluter qualify.

6. **Q: What's the source-pathway-receptor (SPR) risk-assessment model?**
   A: DEFRA technical guidance framework for establishing contaminated-land status under EPA 1990 Part 2A. Contamination requires THREE elements linked together: (a) source (the contaminant — e.g. TCE solvent, asbestos, lead, hydrocarbons); (b) pathway (the route of exposure — e.g. soil-water, soil-air, ingestion); (c) receptor (the receiving entity — human, ecological, controlled-water). Where any of source / pathway / receptor is missing, the land is NOT "contaminated" under EPA 1990 Part 2A and LRR / ATED-relief claims fail at threshold.

7. **Q: How is "contaminated land" defined under EPA 1990 Part 2A?**
   A: Per EPA 1990 s.78A: land that appears to the local authority to be in such a condition that "significant harm is being caused or there is a significant possibility of such harm being caused" OR "significant pollution of controlled waters is being caused or there is a significant possibility of such pollution being caused". The local authority is the primary determination body; HMRC accepts local-authority determinations as evidence for LRR / ATED-relief purposes.

8. **Q: What's the Bewley test and does it apply to my contaminated property?**
   A: PN Bewley v HMRC [2019] UKFTT 65 (TC) — SDLT test for property so dilapidated / contaminated / structurally compromised it is "not suitable for use as a dwelling" at the SDLT effective date. Non-residential SDLT rates apply (no 3% surcharge / 15% flat rate / FBT). HP §1.C carries the locked framing. Bewley-extreme cases may also fall outside ATED's "dwelling" definition — distinguish from contaminated-but-still-a-dwelling scenarios (within ATED scope, eligible for s.134 / s.138 / s.141 relief).

9. **Q: What documentation do I need for the four-phase risk-assessment?**
   A: Phase 1 desk study (historical land-use research, regulatory consultations, preliminary risk model); Phase 2 intrusive investigation (soil sampling, groundwater monitoring, contaminated-land specialist consultant reports); Phase 3 remediation strategy (design + cost estimates + regulatory engagement); Phase 4 verification report (post-remediation sampling confirming successful remediation; sign-off by environmental consultant). Each phase generates documentation supporting EPA 1990 Part 2A status + HMRC LRR / ATED-relief claims.

10. **Q: Can I claim LRR AND s.38 enhancement expenditure on the same remediation?**
    A: NO. The standard no-double-relief discipline: LRR-claimed remediation expenditure is excluded from TCGA 1992 s.38(1)(b) enhancement expenditure on subsequent disposal. Choose the relief that produces the better tax outcome — typically LRR (150% deduction at CT rate) outperforms s.38 enhancement (1:1 against gain at CT rate) UNLESS no current-period taxable profits exist to absorb the LRR deduction.

11. **Q: My company didn't cause the contamination — can I claim LRR?**
    A: GENERALLY YES per CTA 2009 s.146 (subject to other conditions). If the contamination was caused by a prior owner / occupier with no connection to your company or its connected persons, the polluter exclusion does not bite. Evidence: title chain showing arms-length acquisition; environmental history showing pre-acquisition contamination cause; absence of any connection between your company's beneficial-ownership group and the polluter's group.

12. **Q: Post-2020, can my non-resident corporate landlord claim LRR on UK property?**
    A: YES post-6 April 2020 per FA 2019 Sch 5 + CTA 2009 Part 4 (B17). Non-resident corporate landlords are now in CT scope for UK property income → can claim LRR on qualifying remediation expenditure on UK property. The pre-2020 IT-regime had no LRR analogue; the 2020 transition opened LRR to non-resident corporate landlords. Strict s.146 polluter-exclusion analysis still required.

13. **Q: My contaminated property is held as investment. Can s.138 / s.141 developer / trader relief apply?**
    A: NO. FA 2013 ss.138 + 141 require TRADING-STOCK characterisation. Property held as INVESTMENT (rental yield as primary purpose) fails the business / investment test (Pawson / Ramsay analysis). For investment-held contaminated property, s.134 transitional relief is the route (if conditions met); s.138 / s.141 are unavailable. Recharacterisation from investment to trading stock requires substantive business change + commercial intent + accounting treatment.

14. **Q: What's the operational compliance discipline for a contaminated-dwelling ATED + LRR scenario?**
    A: (a) Commission Phase 1 desk study at acquisition / contamination discovery; (b) progress through Phase 2 intrusive investigation + Phase 3 remediation strategy + Phase 4 verification; (c) document local-authority engagement (EPA 1990 Part 2A determination if any); (d) file annual ATED return + claim s.134 / s.138 / s.141 relief as appropriate (with contemporaneous "without undue delay" evidence for s.134); (e) maintain remediation expenditure ledger split between LRR-qualifying and non-qualifying expenditure; (f) claim LRR on the CT600 in the period of expenditure; (g) preserve documentation for subsequent NRCGT / disposal analysis (avoiding s.38 / LRR double-relief).
