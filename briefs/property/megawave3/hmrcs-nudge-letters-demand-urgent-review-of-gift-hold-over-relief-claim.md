# HMRC's Nudge Letters Demand Urgent Review of Gift Hold-Over Relief Claim: A Landlord Response Guide

## Statutory anchor

- **Primary — hold-over relief on business-asset gifts:** TCGA 1992 **section 165** ("Relief for gifts of business assets") + **Schedule 7** Parts 1-2 (extending the relief to agricultural property + settled property; restricting the relief for non-business-asset components). Verified live 2026-05-27 at `https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12/section/165`. The critical operative point: s.165 relief is **restricted to assets used for the purposes of a trade, profession or vocation** carried on by the transferor or by their personal company. **Investment property does NOT qualify** — buy-to-let portfolios, residential lettings, and pure-rental businesses are outside the s.165 gate per *Pawson* + the settled HMRC view (cross-ref §22.1 BPR investment-line lock).
- **Primary — hold-over relief on chargeable IHT gifts:** TCGA 1992 **section 260** ("Gifts on which inheritance tax is chargeable etc"). Verified live 2026-05-27 at `https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12/section/260`. s.260 relief applies to chargeable transfers within the meaning of IHTA 1984 — typically CLTs (chargeable lifetime transfers into trust) — and operates by deferring the donor's CGT and transferring the held-over gain to the recipient's base cost.
- **Primary — settlor-interested trust exclusion:** TCGA 1992 **sections 169B-169G** — excludes s.260 hold-over relief where the trust is settlor-interested (the settlor or settlor's spouse can benefit). Verified live 2026-05-27. §22.4 + §22.12 of house_positions.md lock the operational depth on settlor-interested-trust exclusions; sessions writing must NOT cite s.169G as the settlor definition (s.169G subsections (2)-(5) were omitted by FA 2009 — F-4 / Wave 6 Bucket B lock).
- **Primary — non-resident disposal restriction (FA 2019 amendment):** s.260 hold-over relief is restricted where the disposal involves UK land and the transferee is UK-resident (FA 2019 anti-avoidance, narrowing the route for non-resident-to-UK-resident hold-over claims).
- **Supporting — HMRC enquiry powers triggered by nudge letter:** TMA 1970 s.9A (enquiry into self-assessment return) + s.29 (discovery assessment if the return is out of enquiry-window); s.31A (30-day appeal window if HMRC issues an assessment); §27.1 (Discovery assessment time limits — Wave 7 lock) for the 4 / 6 / 12 / 20 year framework.
- **Supporting — penalty exposure if the hold-over claim is found invalid:** Schedule 24 FA 2007 (inaccuracy penalties — careless 0%-30%, deliberate 20%-70%, deliberate-concealed 30%-100%); §27.2 lock. The 60-day nudge-letter window is operationally HMRC's "give the taxpayer a chance to come forward unprompted" mechanic — unprompted-disclosure floors apply if the taxpayer self-corrects within the window.
- **House position reference:** §27 (HMRC enquiry + tax compliance mechanics — Wave 7 lock) primary; §27.1 (discovery time limits) + §27.2 (Sch 24 inaccuracy penalties) operative for the post-nudge enquiry path; §22.4 + §22.12 (settlor-interested trust ss.169B-169G framework) operative for the s.260 limb. ## Worked-example data (RUN session uses these as canvas)

### Example 1 — Landlord gifted BTL property to adult child, claimed s.165 in error

- **Aldridge-Estate, the donor:** in 2022/23 the landlord gifted a £400,000 BTL property (acquired 2010 for £180,000) to an adult child. The donor claimed s.165 hold-over relief, treating the rental activity as a "business" carried on for s.165 purposes. The 2024/25 self-assessment return reflected the £220,000 held-over gain as deferred to the child's base cost.
- **Trigger:** 2026 HMRC nudge letter asks the donor to review the s.165 claim and confirm or amend. The letter states HMRC's view that a pure-rental BTL property is investment, not trading, and so falls outside the s.165 gate.
- **Statutory analysis:** s.165(2)(a) requires the asset to be used for the purposes of a trade carried on by the donor. Pure-rental BTL activity is investment per the settled HMRC view + the Pawson analysis applied by analogy. The s.165 claim was invalid.
- **Effect:** the CGT crystallises on the 2022/23 disposal date at residential rates (28% pre-April-2024; 24% post-April-2024). On a £220,000 gain at 24% the consequential tax is ~£52,800. Schedule 24 careless-unprompted floor (0% per F-5 / Wave 7 lock) may apply if the donor self-corrects within the 60-day nudge-letter window; prompted floor 15% applies if HMRC has to escalate.

### Example 2 — Gift into discretionary trust (s.260 route), settlor-interested trap

- **Mawell-Estate, the donor:** in 2023/24 the donor settled a £600,000 commercial-property holding into a discretionary trust as a CLT (chargeable lifetime transfer). The donor claimed s.260 hold-over relief on the £350,000 gain. The trust deed includes the donor's spouse among the discretionary beneficiaries.
- **Trigger:** 2026 HMRC nudge letter asks the donor to review the s.260 claim. HMRC's view: the trust is settlor-interested under TCGA 1992 s.169B because the donor's spouse can benefit; s.169B disapplies the s.260 hold-over relief.
- **Statutory analysis:** s.169B + s.169C exclude s.260 hold-over where the donor or donor's spouse can benefit from the trust property. The settlor-interest catches discretionary trusts where the spouse is a named beneficiary.
- **Effect:** the £350,000 gain crystallises in the donor's hands at the date of the CLT. Residential rates do not apply (commercial property); the standard CGT rate of 20% post-April-2024 applies for higher-rate taxpayers. Consequential CGT ~£70,000. Note: §22.12 lock prohibits citing s.169G subsections (2)-(5) as the settlor definition (omitted by FA 2009) — the operative definition is in TCGA 1992 s.169E referencing the ITTOIA 2005 s.625 trust-settlor framework as adapted.

### Example 3 — Agricultural property gift, partial-bifurcation failure

- **Singh-Estate, the donor:** in 2023/24 the donor gifted a 12-hectare property to an adult child. The property is 8 hectares of agricultural land + a farmhouse, 3 hectares of paddock with no commercial-agricultural activity, and a 1-hectare residential garden. The donor claimed s.165 + Sch 7 hold-over on the whole disposal.
- **Trigger:** 2026 HMRC nudge letter asks the donor to confirm the agricultural / non-agricultural bifurcation under TCGA 1992 Sch 7 Part 1.
- **Statutory analysis:** Sch 7 Part 1 extends s.165 to agricultural property held for agricultural purposes. The 3 hectares of paddock + 1 hectare of residential garden do NOT qualify as agricultural per the BPR / APR settled HMRC view + the case law on "agriculture". The s.165 claim survives on 8 hectares + farmhouse but fails on 4 hectares.
- **Effect:** the donor must amend the return to bifurcate; only the agricultural portion qualifies for hold-over. The non-agricultural portion crystallises as a normal s.17 market-value disposal. Worked-example numbers must be reconstructed at write time per §16.27.

### Example 4 — Nudge letter received, taxpayer unsure of original claim basis

- **Carmichael-Estate, the recipient:** received a nudge letter in late 2026 referring to a 2018/19 hold-over claim. The recipient does not remember the original transaction and cannot quickly verify the underlying property type.
- **Operational route:** 60-day window starts from receipt. Step 1: request a copy of the 2018/19 return + computation from the original agent (or HMRC under TMA 1970 access powers). Step 2: characterise the underlying property type (trade-vs-investment line for s.165; trust-vs-direct for s.260). Step 3: if the claim was valid, respond confirming. ## FAQ expansion (RUN session polishes prose; 10-12 FAQs target)

1. **Q: What is an HMRC nudge letter and what does it demand?**
   A: An HMRC nudge letter (also called a "One Too Many" letter) is operational HMRC practice. It identifies a tax position HMRC believes may be incorrect and invites the taxpayer to review and respond, typically within 60 days. The letter does not itself open a formal enquiry; it operates as a soft prompt to encourage self-correction before HMRC escalates to s.9A enquiry or s.29 discovery.

2. **Q: When is hold-over relief on a gift available under TCGA 1992 section 165?**
   A: Section 165 applies only to gifts of "business assets" used for the purposes of a trade, profession or vocation carried on by the donor (or by their personal company). Schedule 7 Part 1 extends the relief to agricultural property held for agricultural purposes. Pure investment property, including buy-to-let residential portfolios, is OUTSIDE the section-165 gate.

3. **Q: Why does BTL rental not qualify for section 165 relief?**
   A: The settled HMRC view is that residential lettings are investment activity, not trading. The Upper Tribunal decision in Pawson v HMRC [2013] UKUT 50 (a BPR case) established the same trade/investment line by analogy. A buy-to-let landlord is investing in property; they are not carrying on a trade for tax purposes. Section 165 is therefore not available on the gift of a BTL property.

4. **Q: When is section 260 hold-over relief available?**
   A: TCGA 1992 s.260 applies to chargeable transfers within the meaning of IHTA 1984 — typically chargeable lifetime transfers (CLTs) into discretionary or interest-in-possession trusts. The relief defers the donor's CGT by reducing the recipient's base cost. Section 260 is the principal hold-over route for gifts into trust where IHT enters the picture.

5. **Q: What is the settlor-interested trust exclusion?**
   A: Sections 169B and 169C of TCGA 1992 disapply section 260 hold-over relief where the trust is settlor-interested. A trust is settlor-interested if the donor, the donor's spouse, or (in some cases) the donor's minor children can benefit from the trust property. The exclusion catches most discretionary trusts where the donor's spouse is a named beneficiary.

6. **Q: I received a nudge letter on a hold-over claim. What is the 60-day window?**
   A: The 60-day window is HMRC's operational practice — not a statutory deadline. Within this period the taxpayer should review the prior claim, characterise the underlying property type, and respond. If the claim was valid, write to HMRC confirming. If the claim was invalid, self-correct via the Digital Disclosure Service (DDS) within the window; this typically secures the unprompted-disclosure mitigation floor under Schedule 24 FA 2007.

7. **Q: What penalty exposure applies if my hold-over claim was wrong?**
   A: Schedule 24 FA 2007 applies inaccuracy penalties where a return understated tax. Standard maxima are 30% careless, 70% deliberate, 100% deliberate-concealed. Mitigation floors apply on unprompted disclosure (0% non-deliberate per F-5 / Wave 7 lock, no 12-month qualifier under Sch 24 para 10, distinct from Sch 41 para 13 which has a 12-month qualifier on the equivalent failure-to-notify floor). Offshore Category 2 (1.5x) or Category 3 (2x) uplifts apply where overseas elements are involved.

8. **Q: What time limits does HMRC have to assess after a nudge letter?**
   A: TMA 1970 s.29 governs discovery assessments. Standard time limit is 4 years from the end of the relevant tax year; extended to 6 years for careless behaviour; extended to 20 years under s.36(1A) for deliberate behaviour; extended to 12 years under s.36A for offshore innocent error per FA 2019. A nudge letter typically arrives within the 4-6 year window for the underlying transaction.

9. **Q: Can I appeal an amended assessment that follows a nudge letter?**
   A: Yes. TMA 1970 s.31A provides a 30-day appeal window from the date of HMRC's amendment or assessment. Alternative Dispute Resolution (ADR) is available before formal escalation. The First-tier Tribunal hears statutory appeals; the Upper Tribunal in Perrin v HMRC [2018] UKUT 156 sets the reasonable-excuse four-stage test, and Martland v HMRC [2018] UKUT 178 governs late-appeal admission.

10. **Q: Are agricultural-property gifts always within section 165?**
    A: Schedule 7 Part 1 extends section 165 to agricultural property held for agricultural purposes. The bifurcation between agricultural and non-agricultural use is a common failure point — a mixed estate of farmland, paddock, residential garden, and farmhouse needs careful apportionment. Only the genuinely agricultural element qualifies; the residential garden + non-agricultural paddock do not.

11. **Q: How does this nudge letter campaign compare with the BADR nudge letter campaign?**
    A: Both campaigns share the operational "One Too Many" mechanic but operate on different statutory regimes. The BADR campaign targets prior Business Asset Disposal Relief claims under TCGA 1992 ss.169H-169SA. The hold-over campaign targets prior s.165 + s.260 claims. The failure points differ: BADR fails on the trading-company / 2-year-qualifying-period / 5%-economic-interest tests; hold-over fails on the trade/investment line, the settlor-interested-trust trap, and the agricultural bifurcation.

12. **Q: What if I cannot meet the 60-day window?**
    A: Communicate with HMRC promptly. Silence is the worst outcome and may trigger a s.9A enquiry or s.29 discovery assessment. HMRC will typically grant a reasonable extension where the taxpayer engages constructively and explains the operational difficulty. The 60-day window is operational practice, not statutory; the underlying enquiry-and-discovery time limits at s.29 and s.36 are the binding constraints.
