# UK-India Double Taxation Convention: what Indian-resident landlords of UK property and emigrants to India face

## Statutory anchor
- **Primary (treaty):** 1993 UK-India Double Taxation Convention, entered into force 25 October 1993, effective UK income tax / CGT 6 April 1994 and UK corporation tax 1 April 1994. Amended by the 2013 UK-India Protocol (in force 27 December 2013; UK withholding taxes effective 27 December 2013, UK CT 1 April 2014). Modified by the Multilateral Instrument (MLI) effective from 1 January 2020 (varying by tax type). Published at gov.uk "India: tax treaties".
- **Primary (UK domestic — overrides treaty for UK situs property):**
  - **ITTOIA 2005 Part 3** (UK property income, individuals).
  - **CTA 2009 Part 4** (UK property income, companies).
  - **TCGA 1992 s.1A + Sch 1A / 1B / 4AA** (NRCGT — Finance Act 2019 rewrite; captures indirect disposals of property-rich entity shares regardless of treaty silence on Art 13(4)).
  - **FA 1995 Sch 23 + SI 1995/2902** (Non-Resident Landlord scheme — statutory withholding).
  - **TIOPA 2010 s.18 + s.130** (foreign tax credit relief framework).
- **Supporting:** 2013 UK-India Protocol; OECD Model 2017 Commentary on Art 6 / 13 / 4; HMRC International Manual entries on UK-India.
- **House position reference:** §10 (DTAs general), §16 (DTAs Wave 2 extension), **§16.5 specifically** ("UK-India treaty (1993): older treaty; Art 13(1) covers immovable property gains; no Art 13(4) indirect-disposal provision (so UK NRCGT applies on UK property-rich shares regardless)"), §16.6 (NRL is statutory, not treaty), §17.4 (NRCGT regime), §17.5 (NRL operational mechanics).

## Framing differentiator (anti-templating, anti-cannibalisation)
This page is the **UK-India bilateral specific** landlord page — narrow scope, deep mechanics. It is NOT the general "what is a DTA" intro (B20 `dont-pay-twice-an-introduction-to-tax-treaties` covers that). The whole page sits in the India bilateral posture: a UK-NRI (non-resident Indian) or Indian-resident OCI holding UK BTL property; an Indian incorporated company holding UK property; a UK-resident landlord who has emigrated to India and continues to let UK property. The framing differentiator is the **treaty-vs-statute interaction on indirect disposals**: the 1993 convention is OLDER than the OECD model's Art 13(4) indirect-disposal extension, so the treaty itself does NOT allocate UK taxing rights over Indian-resident gains on shares in UK property-rich entities — BUT UK domestic NRCGT (TCGA 1992 s.1A + Sch 1A) does, and HMRC's position is that this is treaty-compatible because the UK is exercising rights the treaty does not deny. Indian-resident shareholders selling UK property-SPV shares need to know: treaty silence does NOT give exemption; the 60-day NRCGT return obligation applies. Sibling pages B1 (UK-IoM modern 2018) and B2 (UK-Spain 2013, MLI-modified) take parallel form but with state-specific notes — the IoM and Spain pages have Art 13(4); the India page does not. ## Key questions this page must answer
1. Does the 1993 UK-India Convention exempt an Indian-resident landlord from UK tax on UK rental income? (No — Art 6 allocates source-state taxing rights to the UK; UK domestic charges under ITTOIA 2005 Part 3 / CTA 2009 Part 4 apply.)
2. Does the treaty displace the NRL scheme? (No — NRL is statutory under FA 1995 Sch 23 + SI 1995/2902; Indian-resident landlords still apply for NRL1 gross-payment approval.)
3. How does Art 13 apply on direct disposal of UK land by an Indian resident? (Art 13(1) gives UK source-state taxing rights on immovable property gains; NRCGT under TCGA 1992 s.1A applies; 60-day return required.)
4. **The critical question:** how does Art 13 apply on disposal of shares in a UK property-rich entity by an Indian resident? (The 1993 treaty does NOT contain an Art 13(4) indirect-disposal extension — yet UK NRCGT under TCGA 1992 Sch 1A Part 4 + Sch 4AA captures indirect disposals regardless. HMRC's view: statute overrides treaty silence; the UK is exercising rights the treaty does not deny. Stage 2 verifies HMRC International Manual entries.)
5. How does Art 4 tie-breaker apply where an individual is dual-resident under UK SRT and Indian 182-day rule? (Cascade: permanent home, centre of vital interests, habitual abode, nationality, mutual agreement.)
6. How does the 2013 Protocol modify the 1993 Convention? (Stage 2 verifies precise scope — broadly tightens limitation-of-benefits provisions, updates withholding rates on dividends / interest / royalties.)
7. How does the MLI modification (effective from 2020) further modify the Convention? (Principal purpose test, treaty-abuse provisions strengthened; Stage 2 verifies the specific MLI Articles applied to UK-India.)
8. How does the foreign tax credit work for Indian-resident landlords claiming UK tax paid? (Claimed via Indian return under DTAA Art 24; India operates the credit method; UK has no symmetric FTC because UK is the source state.)
9. How does the FIG regime under ITTOIA 2005 ss.845A-845J (FA 2025 s.37) apply to an OCI / Indian-citizen newly resident in the UK with Indian property income? (Standard 4-year FIG window; Indian property income is foreign income within s.845H categories — Stage 2 verifies verbatim list at write time.)
10. How does s.10A TCGA 1992 temporary non-residence apply to a UK-resident landlord who moves to India for 4 years then returns? (Gain recapture in return year on UK assets owned at departure — UK land already in NRCGT, so s.10A primarily catches non-UK situs gains. The 5-year-or-less window is the trigger.)
11. Are Indian companies inside or outside UK ATED? (ATED applies to non-natural persons including non-UK companies; an Indian company holding a UK dwelling worth more than £500,000 is in scope — cross-reference §18 + Bucket B ATED picks.)

### Example 1 — The treaty-vs-statute matrix (the page's first concrete artefact, the operative differentiator)

Render as semantic HTML table; this is the most-misunderstood single piece of UK-India bilateral commentary:

| Scenario | 1993 Treaty allocation | UK Statutory charge | Operative position |
|---|---|---|---|
| Indian-resident receives UK rental income | UK source-state (Art 6) | ITTOIA 2005 Part 3 (individual) / CTA 2009 Part 4 (company) | UK tax applies; treaty + statute aligned |
| Indian-resident sells UK land directly | UK source-state (Art 13(1) on immovable property) | NRCGT under TCGA 1992 s.1A | UK tax applies; treaty + statute aligned; 60-day NRCGT return required |
| Indian-resident sells shares in UK property-rich SPV (e.g. ≥75% UK land value; ≥25% holder stake) | **TREATY SILENT** — 1993 treaty Art 13 does NOT contain a property-rich-entity indirect-disposal extension; falls to the residuary Art 13(5) / Art 22 framework which typically allocates to residence state (India) | NRCGT under TCGA 1992 Sch 1A Part 4 + Sch 4AA | **UK STATUTORY CHARGE APPLIES regardless of treaty silence.** HMRC's view: the UK is exercising taxing rights the treaty does not expressly deny; this is treaty-compatible. 60-day NRCGT return required. Indian-side FTC available under DTAA Art 24 / Indian Income Tax Act s.90 |
| Indian-resident NRI sells flat in own name | UK source-state (Art 13(1)) | NRCGT under TCGA 1992 s.1A | Aligned; 60-day return |
| Indian company holds UK dwelling worth >£500k | UK retains source rights (no treaty bar) | ATED under FA 2013 Part 3 | Annual charge per §18.1; s.133 relief claim if let commercially; 30 April return |
| UK-resident moves to India + sells non-UK shares during non-residence + returns within 5 tax years (4-of-7 preceding gateway met) | India taxes the gain as Indian-resident at time of disposal | UK CGT under TCGA 1992 s.10A on return (gain deemed to arise in return year) | UK + India both tax; UK gives FTC for Indian CGT paid via TIOPA 2010 s.18 |

Operational anchor: the **treaty-silence ≠ treaty-exemption** point is the operative learning. Indian-resident readers selling UK property-SPV shares often assume that because the 1993 treaty doesn't allocate UK taxing rights, the UK cannot charge. That is wrong. Statute applies regardless; treaty silence is not exemption.

### Example 2 — Mrs Iyengar, Mumbai-resident NRI individual landlord (the most common scenario)

Mrs Iyengar (Mumbai-resident throughout 2026/27; UK-non-resident under SRT; OCI cardholder) owns a Manchester BTL flat. Annual gross rent £14,400; agent's fees £1,440; mortgage interest £3,200; service charge + repairs £1,800. Position:
- **Treaty:** UK has primary taxing rights under Art 6.
- **NRL:** Letting agent withholds 20% (£2,880/year) unless Mrs Iyengar holds NRL1 approval. She applies for NRL1 on instructing the agent.
- **UK Self-Assessment:** Mrs Iyengar registers for SA + files SA100 + SA105 annually. Property profit after deductible expenses = £11,160; mortgage interest restricted to 20% basic-rate tax credit (£640) per ITA 2007 s.274A. UK income tax: basic-rate £2,232; minus £640 credit = £1,592 UK income tax due.
- **Personal allowance:** UK personal allowance available for non-residents under ITA 2007 s.56 based on nationality / Commonwealth status (Indian nationals retain UK personal allowance under domestic law — verify currency at write).
- **NRL offset:** £2,880 withheld appears on NRL6; credited against UK SA. UK refund: £1,288.
- **Indian side:** Mrs Iyengar reports the UK rental on her Indian ITR-2 + Schedule FA (foreign assets / income). Under DTAA Art 24 + Indian Income Tax Act s.90 + Rule 128, India gives FTC for UK income tax paid (£1,592) against any Indian income tax on the same income. Indian Form 67 (online filing within Indian return) is the FTC claim mechanism. If Indian effective rate exceeds UK effective rate, residual Indian tax is payable; if not, no residual.
- **Common misframing:** "I'm NRI / OCI — UK rental income is exempt under the treaty." FALSE — Art 6 allocates source-state taxing rights to UK.

### Example 3 — The Art 13 indirect-disposal trap (the page's load-bearing example)

Mr Mehta (Bangalore-resident; UK-non-resident under SRT; Indian citizen) owns 30% of the shares in a Jersey-incorporated holding company ("Mayfair Property HoldCo Ltd") whose sole asset is a £8.2m London office building. The other 70% is held by his Indian business partner. In 2026/27 he sells his 30% stake to an unrelated buyer for £2.5m (base cost £900k acquired in 2018; gain £1.6m).
- **Property-rich entity test:** HoldCo's value >75% derived from UK land (sole asset) → property-rich under Sch 1A para 6.
- **Threshold-holder test:** Mr Mehta held ≥25% at any time in the 2 years before disposal (he held 30% throughout) → substantial holder under Sch 1A para 8.
- **NRCGT applies** under TCGA 1992 s.1A + Sch 1A Part 4 (indirect disposals). 60-day return required.
- **1993 treaty position:** Art 13(1) covers gains from alienation of immovable property situated in the UK — but the shares Mr Mehta is selling are SHARES in a HoldCo, not immovable property. The 1993 treaty does NOT contain an Art 13(4)-equivalent indirect-disposal provision. The gain on the shares therefore falls to the residuary Art 13(5) (alienation of any property other than that mentioned in paragraphs 1-4) which typically allocates to the residence state (India).
- **Statutory override:** UK NRCGT under Sch 1A Part 4 applies regardless. HMRC's position (verify HMRC International Manual at write): the UK is exercising taxing rights not expressly denied by the treaty; treaty silence is not exemption. The 60-day NRCGT return is required + UK CGT at 18%/24% (residential rate applies even for indirect disposal where underlying is residential; non-residential rate 10%/20% pre-October-2024 / 18%/24% from 30 October 2024 — verify per §17.4 at write).
- **Indian side:** Mr Mehta also reports the gain on his Indian ITR. India operates the credit method under Art 24 + s.90; UK CGT paid is creditable against Indian CGT on the same gain. India-side LTCG (long-term capital gain) treatment depends on Indian holding-period rules (currently 2+ years for unlisted equity shares qualifies as LTCG; verify Indian Income Tax Act + recent Finance Act at write).
- **Treaty-shopping concern:** the MLI principal-purpose test (effective from 2020) may be relevant if the Jersey HoldCo structure was inserted for the purpose of obtaining UK / India treaty benefits without commercial substance — but the analysis turns on the actual facts; bona fide commercial structures are typically not affected.
- **Common misframing:** "The 1993 treaty doesn't cover indirect disposals so there's no UK tax." FALSE — UK statutory NRCGT applies regardless. This is the trap that catches Indian-resident shareholders selling UK property-SPV stakes.

### Example 4 — Art 4 tie-breaker for a dual-resident individual

Dr Singh (born in Delhi; UK consultancy work; spent 165 UK days in 2026/27; rents a flat in Marylebone + retains family home in Delhi; spouse + adult children in Delhi). SRT analysis: 165 UK days under sufficient-ties test with 4 ties (accommodation, work, 90-day, family) → UK-resident. Indian domestic law: spent >182 India days OR habitual abode in India OR Indian taxable presence threshold met → Indian-resident. Dual-resident. Art 4 tie-breaker cascade per OECD Model:
- **(a) Permanent home:** Available in both UK + India → does not resolve.
- **(b) Centre of vital interests:** Family + personal + economic closer to Delhi (spouse + children + primary bank accounts + social ties) → resolves to India.
- Dr Singh is treaty-resident in India. UK retains source-state taxing rights on UK consultancy income (Art 7 if PE; Art 14 independent personal services); UK property income / gains under Art 6 / 13.
- **Effect on UK domestic:** Dr Singh remains UK-resident under SRT for non-treaty UK matters (SA registration; NRL etc); treaty residence affects allocation only.

### Example 5 — UK landlord emigrates to India + s.10A 5-year trap

Mr Chowdhury (UK-resident throughout 2018/19 to 2025/26; satisfies s.10A "preceding 4-of-7" test). He moves to Mumbai in June 2026 for a permanent corporate role. Retains UK BTL portfolio + sells a portfolio of US shares in 2028/29 for a £140,000 gain while Indian-resident. In 2030/31, after 4 complete tax years non-UK-resident (2026/27-2029/30), he returns to UK.
- **s.10A position per §17.3:** Mr Chowdhury's non-UK residence period is 4 complete tax years — within the "5 years or less" window. He had been UK-resident in 4+ of the 7 preceding tax years. He is a "temporary non-resident".
- **Effect:** the £140,000 gain on the US shares disposed during non-residence is deemed to arise in the 2030/31 return year and is chargeable to UK CGT.
- **UK property gain:** Mr Chowdhury's UK BTL portfolio is in NRCGT throughout his non-residence per §17.4. If he sells UK BTL during non-residence, the 60-day NRCGT return + UK CGT at 18%/24% applies — no s.10A overlay (UK land already in NRCGT regardless of return).
- **Indian side:** India taxed him on the £140,000 gain in 2028/29 (Indian-resident at time of disposal). UK CGT on the same gain in 2030/31 is creditable against the Indian CGT paid in 2028/29 under DTAA Art 24 (lower of the two), but only by amendment of the Indian return if not previously offset — practical messy, requires Indian s.155 rectification or revised return within the available window.
- **Planning lesson:** to break s.10A, Mr Chowdhury needs more than 5 complete tax years non-UK-resident. A 4-year stint with planned return crystallises the recapture risk.

### Example 6 — Indian company holding UK BTL + ATED

UK Realty India Private Limited (Mumbai-registered Indian private limited company; Indian-resident company; UK-non-resident) buys a £900,000 Birmingham city-centre apartment in 2026. Annual rental £42,000. Position:
- **NRL:** UK letting agent withholds 20% unless NRL2 approval held.
- **UK CT on rental:** UK Realty India Private Limited is within UK CT under CTA 2009 Part 4 (non-resident companies in UK CT since 6 April 2020). Rental profit after deductible expenses taxed per §21.A: 19% SPR if augmented profits ≤£50k; 25% main rate above £250k; CIHC carve-out per s.18N(2)(b) applies (commercial letting to unconnected tenant) — SPR preserved.
- **ATED per §18:** £900,000 dwelling → £500,001-£1m band → 2026/27 charge £4,600 per §18.1 (verify at write). UK Realty India Private Limited files ATED return by 30 April 2026 + pays £4,600. s.133 property-rental-business relief available — claim on return (relief is not automatic — file even when no tax is due to avoid Schedule 55 FA 2009 penalty points).
- **NRCGT on disposal:** When sold (say 2032 for £1.08m; gain £180k), 60-day NRCGT return; UK CT on chargeable gain at prevailing CT rate. ATED-CGT abolished from 6 April 2019 per §18.5.
- **MLI principal-purpose test:** the MLI imports a PPT into the 1993 Convention from 1 January 2020. If the Indian private limited company structure was inserted purely to access treaty benefits, PPT can deny benefits — but standard commercial-investment structures with genuine Indian business substance are typically unaffected.
- **Indian side:** Indian corporate income tax on worldwide income. UK CT paid on UK rental is creditable in India under s.90 + Rule 128 + Form 67.

### Example 7 — FIG regime for an OCI / Indian citizen newly resident in UK

Ms Reddy (Indian citizen; OCI cardholder; resident in Hyderabad 2016/17-2025/26; never previously UK-resident; never previously claimed FIG / remittance basis) takes up a UK employment in November 2026; becomes UK-resident under SRT from 2026/27. She retains a Hyderabad rental property generating ₹14,40,000/year (~£14,000) + a small Indian portfolio.
- **FIG qualifying-new-resident test (s.845B(1) per §17.8):** (a) UK-resident 2026/27 → yes; (b) NOT UK-resident in any of 10 preceding tax years → yes; (c) not previously disqualified → yes; (d) age >10 → yes. **She qualifies.**
- **Duration:** Up to 4 tax years (2026/27-2029/30). Per-year claim per s.845A.
- **Coverage:** Indian rental income is foreign property income within s.845H. If she claims FIG for 2026/27, the Indian rental + the Indian portfolio income is UK-exempt for the year.
- **Cost:** Claiming FIG forfeits her UK personal allowance + dividend allowance + CGT AEA for 2026/27 — same architecture as historic remittance basis per §17.8. For her ~£14,000 Indian rental + modest portfolio income, the lost personal allowance (£12,570 × 20% = £2,514 UK tax shielded if she didn't claim) likely OUTWEIGHS the FIG benefit. She should do the arithmetic before claiming.
- **Claim deadline:** 31 January 2028 for 2026/27 per s.845A + §17.8.
- **Indian-side treatment:** Her Indian-source rental remains taxable in India regardless of UK FIG (FIG is a UK-side relief; not an Indian-side exemption). If FIG is claimed, the Indian rental is taxed only in India; no UK tax to credit against on the Indian return for that year.

### Example 8 — UK-domicile-of-origin emigrant to India + new IHT LTR test

Mr Vaidyanathan (UK-born; UK-domicile of origin; UK-resident throughout 2007/08 to 2025/26 — 19 of preceding 20 tax years). He moves to Bangalore in 2026 intending to settle permanently in India.
- **Pre-FA-2025 architecture (no longer in force):** he would remain UK-domiciled for IHT until 4+ tax years non-UK-resident under s.267(1)(b), then domicile of choice in India possible (subject to high evidential bar).
- **Post-FA-2025 LTR regime per §22.X:** Mr Vaidyanathan is a long-term UK resident — UK-resident in 19 of preceding 20 tax years. On departure he remains LTR for the IHT-tail-period per §22.X.2: for 19 prior UK-resident years, required consecutive non-UK tax years to lose LTR is **9 years**. His worldwide-asset IHT exposure extends until tax year 2035/36 at earliest.
- **Effect:** Mr Vaidyanathan's Indian-acquired Bangalore property is within UK IHT (worldwide assets) for at least 9 tax years post-departure. His UK property portfolio is in UK IHT throughout regardless (UK situs).
- **Indian side:** India does NOT have inheritance / estate tax — the *Estate Duty Act 1953* was repealed in 1985 + no successor regime introduced (verify currency at write — periodic proposals to reintroduce). So no Indian-side IHT to credit against UK IHT under TIOPA / treaty mechanics.
- **Pre-FA-2025 advice wrong:** any pre-2025 planning that assumed "stay non-UK 3+ years to escape UK IHT" is obsolete for long-term UK residents per §22.X.2 table.

### Example 9 — Misframings the page must correct (verbatim do-not-write list)

- **Misframe 1:** "The 1993 UK-India Convention exempts Indian-residents from UK tax on UK rental income." FALSE — Art 6 allocates source-state taxing rights to UK; UK domestic charge under ITTOIA 2005 / CTA 2009 applies.
- **Misframe 2:** "NRL withholding doesn't apply to OCI / NRI landlords because the treaty disapplies it." FALSE — NRL is statutory per §16.6.
- **Misframe 3 — THE LOAD-BEARING MISFRAME:** "The 1993 treaty is silent on indirect disposals of UK property-rich entity shares, so no UK tax applies." FALSE — UK statutory NRCGT under TCGA 1992 Sch 1A Part 4 captures the disposal regardless; treaty silence is NOT exemption; HMRC's position is that exercising statutory rights the treaty does not expressly deny is treaty-compatible.
- **Misframe 4:** "The 1993 treaty contains an Art 13(4) provision on indirect disposals." FALSE — the 1993 treaty pre-dates the OECD Model's Art 13(4) extension; competitor pages often default-assume modern-treaty form and are wrong here.
- **Misframe 5:** "Selling UK property as an Indian-resident avoids UK CGT under the treaty." FALSE — Art 13(1) allocates UK source-state rights on direct disposal of immovable property; NRCGT under TCGA 1992 s.1A applies; 60-day return required.
- **Misframe 6:** "60-day NRCGT return is only required if there is tax to pay." FALSE per §17.4.
- **Misframe 7:** "Temporary non-residence is 4 years." FALSE per §17.3 — s.10A is "5 years or less".
- **Misframe 8:** "Indian companies are outside UK ATED because they're not UK-incorporated." FALSE — ATED applies to non-natural persons including non-UK companies per §18.2.

## FAQ expansion (RUN session polishes prose; 12-14 FAQs target)

1. **Q: I'm Indian-resident (NRI / OCI) and own a UK rental flat — does the 1993 UK-India Convention exempt me from UK tax?**
   A: No. Article 6 of the 1993 Convention allocates source-state taxing rights to the UK on income from UK immovable property. UK domestic charge under ITTOIA 2005 Part 3 applies regardless of your Indian residence. The Convention's role is to prevent double tax — India (the residence state) gives a foreign tax credit for UK tax paid under Article 24 + Indian Income Tax Act s.90 + Rule 128 + Form 67. You still register for UK Self-Assessment and operate the NRL scheme.

2. **Q: Do I still need to operate the NRL scheme as a Mumbai-resident landlord?**
   A: Yes. The Non-Resident Landlord scheme is statutory under FA 1995 Schedule 23 + SI 1995/2902. Treaty residence does not displace the obligation. The only mechanism to receive UK rent gross (without the 20% letting-agent / tenant withholding) is NRL1 / NRL2 / NRL3 approval. Apply for NRL1 on instructing the agent.

3. **Q: When I sell my UK BTL flat as an Indian-resident, what's the position?**
   A: NRCGT under TCGA 1992 s.1A applies. Article 13(1) of the 1993 Convention allocates UK source-state rights on alienation of immovable property situated in the UK + UK statutory charge runs in parallel. 60-day NRCGT return required regardless of whether tax is due. Residential rates 18% basic / 24% higher band (post-30 October 2024); AEA £3,000 for individuals.

4. **Q: I'm an NRI selling shares in a UK property-rich holding company — the 1993 treaty doesn't cover indirect disposals, so I'm exempt from UK tax, right?**
   A: No. This is the most common trap on the UK-India treaty. The 1993 Convention is silent on Article 13(4) property-rich-entity indirect disposals because it pre-dates the OECD Model's extension to that scenario. But UK NRCGT under TCGA 1992 Schedule 1A Part 4 captures indirect disposals of shares in entities deriving more than 75% of value from UK land where you hold 25%+ at any time in the 2 years before disposal. UK statutory charge applies regardless of treaty silence. HMRC's position is that the UK is exercising taxing rights the treaty does not expressly deny — treaty-compatible. The 60-day NRCGT return is required and Indian-side FTC under DTAA Article 24 + s.90 is the relief mechanism.

5. **Q: How does the Article 4 tie-breaker work for someone dual-resident under UK SRT and Indian residence rules?**
   A: Standard OECD Model Article 4 cascade: (i) permanent home; (ii) centre of vital interests (family / personal / economic); (iii) habitual abode; (iv) nationality; (v) mutual agreement procedure. Indian domestic residence test is broadly "182 India days OR habitual abode + India taxable presence threshold met" per Indian Income Tax Act s.6 (verify current Indian-side rules at write — Indian thresholds revised by Finance Act 2020 for high-net-worth Indian citizens; periodic updates). Tie-breaker resolution affects treaty allocation only; UK SRT residence stands for non-treaty UK matters.

6. **Q: What did the 2013 UK-India Protocol change in the Convention?**
   A: The 2013 Protocol amended the 1993 Convention to tighten the limitation-of-benefits provisions and update certain withholding rates on dividends, interest, and royalties (HMRC International Manual + the published Protocol text — verify exact scope at write). The 2013 Protocol is an amendment, not a replacement — the operative treaty is the 1993 Convention as amended by the 2013 Protocol.

7. **Q: How did the MLI further change the UK-India Convention?**
   A: The MLI imported several treaty-abuse provisions into the 1993 Convention as amended, effective from 1 January 2020. The most consequential is the Principal Purpose Test (PPT) — treaty benefits can be denied where obtaining the benefit was a principal purpose of the relevant arrangement, unless granting the benefit would accord with the object and purpose of the treaty provisions. For standard commercial-investment structures with genuine Indian business substance, PPT is typically not engaged.

8. **Q: I'm an OCI cardholder moving to the UK for a new job — can I claim the FIG regime?**
   A: Yes if you meet the qualifying-new-resident test under ITTOIA 2005 s.845B(1): UK-resident in the current year AND not UK-resident in any of the 10 preceding tax years AND not previously disqualified AND over age 10. Per §17.8, FIG runs for up to 4 tax years with per-year claim. Indian rental + other Indian foreign income falls within s.845H. Claiming costs your UK personal allowance + dividend allowance + CGT AEA for that year — for modestly-sized Indian foreign income the personal-allowance loss can outweigh the FIG benefit. Do the arithmetic before claiming. Claim deadline: 31 January 12 months after end of relevant tax year.

9. **Q: I moved to India in 2026 but plan to return to UK in 2030 — what's my CGT position?**
   A: If your non-UK residence period is 5 years or less AND you were UK-resident in 4+ of the 7 tax years before departure, s.10A TCGA 1992 makes you a "temporary non-resident". Gains on non-UK assets disposed during your non-residence are deemed to arise in your year of return and are chargeable to UK CGT then. To escape, you need MORE than 5 complete tax years non-UK-resident. UK property gains are in NRCGT throughout regardless of return.

10. **Q: My Indian private limited company holds a £900k Birmingham apartment — am I in UK ATED?**
    A: Yes. ATED applies to non-natural persons including non-UK companies per §18.2. A £900k apartment falls in the £500,001-£1m band: 2026/27 charge £4,600 (verify §18.1 at write). Claim the s.133 property-rental-business relief on the annual return if you let commercially to unconnected tenants. The return is mandatory even where relief applies; failing to file triggers Schedule 55 FA 2009 penalty points and exposure to the OTM letters campaign per §18.7.

11. **Q: I'm UK-domicile of origin moving to India to retire — does the new IHT LTR test apply to me?**
    A: Yes if you are a long-term UK resident (UK-resident for 10 of the previous 20 tax years per IHTA 1984 s.6A — see §22.X). LTR status extends post-departure through a tail period per §22.X.2: scales 3 years if you were UK-resident in 13 or fewer of preceding 20 years; 9 years if you were UK-resident in 19 of preceding 20; 10 years if all 20. For long-term UK residents emigrating to India, your worldwide-asset IHT exposure can extend up to a decade post-departure. Indian-side IHT does NOT mirror — India repealed the Estate Duty Act in 1985 and has no successor regime (subject to periodic reintroduction proposals; verify currency at consultation date).

12. **Q: How does the foreign tax credit work — do I claim from HMRC or from the Indian Income Tax Department?**
    A: India is the residence state and gives the FTC against UK tax paid, via DTAA Article 24 + Indian Income Tax Act s.90 + Rule 128 + Form 67 (online filing within the Indian return). You pay UK tax first (SA or CT), then claim Indian FTC on your Indian ITR. The UK side does NOT generally credit Indian tax against UK tax on the same UK-source income (UK is the source state with primary rights).

13. **Q: My letting agent says the treaty means they don't need to withhold 20% NRL on my Manchester flat — is that right?**
    A: No. The tenant or agent is liable for the unwithheld tax if they don't withhold (no time limit for unprompted discovery). NRL withholding is statutory; treaty residence does not displace it. Until you hold NRL1 / NRL2 / NRL3 approval, the agent must withhold 20% + account quarterly to HMRC.
