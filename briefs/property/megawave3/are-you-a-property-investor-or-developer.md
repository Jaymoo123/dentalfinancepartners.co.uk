# Investor or Developer? The Tax Line that Decides Your Margin

## Statutory anchor

- Primary (companies — transactions in UK land regime): CTA 2010 Part 8ZB ss.356OA-356OT, inserted by Finance Act 2016 c. 24 s.77(1). Effective per FA 2016 s.81 for disposals on or after 5 July 2016. §28.1 lock.
- Primary (individuals — transactions in UK land regime): ITA 2007 Part 9A ss.517A-517U, inserted by FA 2016 c. 24 s.79(1) + s.82(1). Effective for disposals on or after 5 July 2016. §28.1 lock.
- Primary (four-conditions test): CTA 2010 s.356OB / ITA 2007 s.517B — Condition A (acquisition main-purpose test), Condition B (derived-property acquisition main-purpose test), Condition C (trading-stock test — deterministic), Condition D (development main-purpose test). Statutory language "main purpose, or one of the main purposes" — disjunctive; multiple main purposes can coexist. §28.2 lock.
- Primary (chargeable-person rule + 6-month associated-persons window): CTA 2010 s.356OB(2) + s.356OB(8) / ITA 2007 s.517B(2) + s.517B(8). Catches the person acquiring/holding/developing the land, plus associated persons at any time in the period beginning when project activities begin and ending 6 months after disposal. §28.2 lock — Stage 2b refinement 2026-05-25.
- Primary (indirect disposals via property-rich entities): CTA 2010 s.356OD / ITA 2007 s.517D — three-condition framework specific to indirect disposals (50% derivation from UK land + arrangement + main-purpose). §28.3 lock.
- Primary (anti-fragmentation): CTA 2010 s.356OH / ITA 2007 s.517H — attributes activities to chargeable person where fragmented across multiple persons/entities. Defeats the "separate developer, separate seller, separate profit-recipient" planning pattern. §28.4 lock.
- Primary (anti-avoidance umbrella): CTA 2010 s.356OK / ITA 2007 s.517K — arrangements for avoiding tax. Wider catch-all over the four-conditions + fragmentation rules.
- Primary (Residential Property Developer Tax — companies above £25m allowance): FA 2022 Part 2 ss.31-53. 4% additional CT charge on residential property developer profits above the £25m group allowance. IN FORCE per FA 2022 s.51 commencement (APs beginning on or after 1 April 2022). §28.7 lock — STATUS CORRECTED 2026-05-25; legislation.gov.uk shows no outstanding effects; the "repealed FA 2024 s.81" claim was wrong.
- Primary (CGT alternative — chargeable-gain treatment where investment): TCGA 1992 s.1A (NRCGT for non-residents); TCGA 1992 Sch 2 paras 6-12 (60-day in-year reporting on residential disposals); §5 lock (residential CGT rates 18%/24% from 30 October 2024).
- Primary (appropriation to trading stock — deemed CGT disposal at MV): TCGA 1992 s.161 (appropriation INTO trading stock = market-value disposal for CGT, with subsequent trading profit measured against the MV; s.162 incorporation relief NOT available for trading-stock appropriations). §28.6 lock.
- Primary (trading-profit computation): CTA 2009 Part 3 (companies); ITTOIA 2005 Part 2 (individuals) — trading profit at marginal income-tax rates plus Class 4 NIC (individuals) or CT rate (companies, with RPDT overlay above threshold).
- Supporting (badges of trade — caselaw framework): *Marson v Morton* [1986] 1 WLR 1343 (the nine badges); *Iswera v IRC* [1965] 1 WLR 663 (single transaction can be trading); *Salt v Chamberlain* [1979] STC 750 (frequency line); *Page v Lowther* [1983] STC 799 (single property flip held to be trading); *Pickford v Quirke* (1927) 13 TC 251 (repeated transactions establish trade). §28.5 lock.
- Supporting (IHT-side trading-vs-investment line — NOT interchangeable with tax-side): *Pawson v HMRC* [2013] UKUT 050 (TCC) — BPR investment line for IHT. The Pawson test and the Part 8ZB / Part 9A test are overlapping but distinct; a landlord can fail Pawson (investment for BPR) AND be caught by Part 8ZB / Part 9A (trading for CT/IT) on the same operation. §22.1 + §28.8 lock.
- House position reference: §28 (full architecture pre-locked Wave 8); §28.1 (statutory architecture), §28.2 (four-conditions test), §28.3 (indirect disposals), §28.4 (anti-fragmentation), §28.5 (badges of trade), §28.6 (trading-stock), §28.7 (RPDT IN FORCE), §28.8 (cross-references with §5 / §21 / §22.1 / §17.4 / §1), §28.9-11 (citations / things to flag / do-not-write). **No new HP lock needed** — pre-locked from Wave 8.

## Key questions this page must answer

1. **The two routes — the consequence-matrix in numbers** — Investor side: rental income via property business (ITTOIA 2005 Part 3 individuals; CTA 2009 Part 4 companies), disposal as CGT chargeable gain (TCGA 1992; 18%/24% residential bands for individuals from 30 October 2024 per §5; CT on the company gain). Developer side: profits as trading income (ITTOIA 2005 Part 2 individuals; CTA 2009 Part 3 companies), marginal income-tax rates (up to 45%) plus Class 4 NIC for individuals; CT plus RPDT 4% surcharge for companies above £25m residential developer profit allowance. Worked example placeholder: £500,000 gain on a flipped property. Investor side (CGT 24% on £400,000 gain net of base cost): ~£96,000 tax. Developer side (income tax 40% + Class 4 NIC ~9%): ~£196,000 — twice the bill. The page must make this number-difference visible at the top.
2. **The four-conditions test in plain language — what each condition catches** — Condition A: did you buy intending profit-from-disposal? (acquisition main-purpose test — applied AT ACQUISITION). Condition B: did you buy an interest in something that derives value from land, intending profit-from-disposal of the land? (covers SPV shares, partnership interests in property-rich entities). Condition C: are you carrying the land as trading stock in your accounts? (deterministic — if you booked it as inventory you're caught; no main-purpose evaluation needed). Condition D: did you develop the land intending profit-from-disposal? (applied AT DEVELOPMENT — catches buy-to-let landlords who later decide to develop and sell; can catch a long-held property if the development decision flipped intent). The "main purpose, or one of the main purposes" wording is disjunctive — investment intent ALONGSIDE profit intent does not defeat the test where profit intent is genuine and substantial.
3. **The badges of trade — the nine-factor caselaw framework** — *Marson v Morton* (1986) sets the canonical nine badges: (1) subject matter (commodities = trading; investment assets = capital); (2) length of ownership (short = trading); (3) frequency of similar transactions (multiple = trading); (4) supplementary work (development = trading); (5) circumstances of realisation (forced sale = capital; marketed sale = trading); (6) motive at acquisition (profit-intent = trading); (7) financing method (short-term borrowing for resale = trading); (8) profit-seeking motive; (9) way the asset was actually used. No single badge is determinative. The post-FA-2016 statutory regime sits ALONGSIDE the badges — Part 8ZB / Part 9A can catch a transaction that fails the badges test (statutory override at four-conditions level); the badges can support a trading conclusion even where Part 8ZB / Part 9A does not apply (border cases).
4. **The single-transaction trap — *Page v Lowther* + *Iswera*** — A common landlord assumption is "I'm only doing one flip, so I'm not a trader". *Page v Lowther* [1983] STC 799 holds that a single property transaction can be trading where intent at acquisition was profit-from-disposal + active steps to enhance value (development, marketing, structured sale). *Iswera v IRC* [1965] 1 WLR 663 (Privy Council) confirms single transactions can be trading at common law. Combined with Condition A / Condition D of the statutory regime, the "I only did one" defence is not reliable — the question is intent + activity, not frequency.
5. **The 6-month associated-persons window — s.356OB(8) / s.517B(8)** — The chargeable person under s.356OB(2) / s.517B(2) catches not just the person who acquires/holds/develops, but ALSO persons associated with that person at "any time in the period beginning when the activities of the project begin and ending 6 months after the disposal". Operative consequence: a landlord cannot insulate the profit by, say, selling to a connected company immediately before disposal and routing the gain through that company. The 6-month window catches the association even if the relationship dissolved post-disposal. Property partnerships, holding-company structures, and family-property arrangements must all map their relationships against this window.
6. **The anti-fragmentation rule — s.356OH / s.517H** — Where activities are fragmented across multiple persons (one entity acquires, another develops, a third sells), the fragmentation rule attributes the activities to the chargeable person for the main-purpose tests. Defeats the planning pattern of separating "the developer" (genuinely active) from "the seller" (passive on paper). For landlord-developers operating through multi-company structures, the fragmentation rule means the OVERALL coordinated activity is assessed, not the individual entity-level activity. Cross-reference s.356OK / s.517K (arrangements for avoiding tax) as the wider anti-avoidance umbrella.
7. **Indirect disposals — selling SPV shares does NOT escape the regime** — s.356OD / s.517D applies a three-condition framework specific to indirect disposals: (i) profit from disposal of property deriving ≥50% of value from UK land; (ii) person is party to or concerned in an arrangement concerning the land; (iii) main purpose of the arrangement is to deal in or develop the land AND realise profit from a disposal of property deriving value from that land. Catches the "slice of the action" pattern where a developer takes profit-share via a corporate intermediary rather than direct land disposal. Tracing rules at s.356OM / s.517N walk through multi-tier structures. The historic planning route of "hold land in SPV, sell SPV shares for CGT" is largely closed for development-intent acquisitions.
8. **The trading-stock test — Condition C is deterministic** — If land is "held as trading stock" in the books, you are caught — no main-purpose evaluation. "Trading stock" is determined by the accounting treatment: inventory line on the balance sheet (not fixed asset). The trap for landlord-incorporators: appropriating an existing BTL into a development SPV "as trading stock" triggers TCGA 1992 s.161 — a deemed disposal at market value for CGT at that moment, with subsequent trading profit measured against the MV. **s.162 incorporation relief is NOT available** for trading-stock appropriations (s.162 covers going-concern business transfers). The route to incorporate a property development trade is CTA 2010 Part 22 (transfers of trade) — distinct procedural path. Sessions on incorporation must distinguish the going-concern route (s.162) from the trading-stock appropriation route (s.161 + Part 22).
9. **RPDT — IN FORCE; 4% additional CT charge above £25m residential developer profit** — FA 2022 Part 2 ss.31-53 in force per FA 2022 s.51 commencement (APs from 1 April 2022). Applies to companies (and groups) carrying on residential property development with profits above the £25m group allowance. Rate: 4% additional CT charge. Below threshold = no RPDT. Verified live on legislation.gov.uk 2026-05-25 — the previously-circulated "repealed FA 2024 s.81" claim is wrong; FA 2024 s.81 returns 404 and no RPDT-repeal provision exists in FA 2024 or FA(No.2) 2024. Sessions must verify current status against legislation.gov.uk at write time per §16.27 rate-by-reference discipline.
10. **The planning levers — what keeps a portfolio on the investment side** — (a) **Long-hold intent at acquisition** documented in board minutes / acquisition correspondence / financing documentation. The intent test is fact-sensitive; contemporaneous evidence carries weight in any later HMRC enquiry. (b) **Rent-bearing periods before disposal** — a long-let BTL with arm's-length tenants is far harder for HMRC to recast as trading stock than a vacant or development-period acquisition. (c) **Investment-style record-keeping** — fixed-asset treatment in the accounts; no inventory line; long-term financing (not bridge / development loans). (d) **Non-developer entity structure** — a property investment company (using SIC 68209 — letting and operating of own or leased real estate) rather than a developer entity (SIC 41100 — development of building projects); the Companies House filings telegraph intent. (e) **No coordinated activities that look like fragmented development** — avoid the s.356OH / s.517H trap. None of these levers are absolute — Part 8ZB / Part 9A can still catch a transaction where the substantive facts are trading — but they shift the centre of gravity of the badges-of-trade evaluation toward investment.

## Worked-example data (RUN session uses these as canvas)

### Example 1 — The consequence matrix: £500,000 gain on a flipped property

Mr Patel buys a tired terrace for £600,000 cash in early 2026, spends £100,000 over 4 months renovating, lists at £1.2m, sells in August 2026 for £1.2m. Gross proceeds £1.2m; total cost £700,000; pre-tax gain £500,000.

- **Investor-side treatment (CGT residential 24% from 30 October 2024):** treats gain as a chargeable disposal under TCGA 1992. Tax: 24% × £500,000 = **£120,000**. Reporting via 60-day in-year return under TCGA 1992 Sch 2 paras 6-12.
- **Developer-side treatment (trading — income tax + Class 4 NIC for individual):** treats £500,000 as trading profit. Income tax at 45% additional rate (assuming Mr Patel's other income already exceeds the higher-rate threshold) = £225,000; Class 4 NIC at current rates (RUN session verifies — circa 9% on the band up to upper profits limit + 2% above) adds approximately £15,000-£20,000. Total ~£240,000-£245,000. **Roughly 2× the investor-side tax bill.**
- **The deciding question:** which side of the line does HMRC see this on? With a 4-month hold, substantial supplementary work, marketed sale, and profit-intent on the evidence (financing structure was a 6-month bridge loan; Mr Patel signed an off-plan resale contract before completing the renovation), the four-conditions test (Conditions A + D) plus the nine badges of trade (frequency-of-1 mitigates, but supplementary work + financing + intent build the case) point HEAVILY toward developer treatment. *Page v Lowther* is the precedent.

Inline aside CTA: "We model the investor-vs-developer tax decision before you commit to the project, not after the sale. The difference is often the price of the next deposit."

### Example 2 — Condition D catches the buy-to-let landlord who decides to develop

Ms Singh has held a 4-flat HMO in Leeds since 2014 as a long-term rental investment. In 2025 the freeholder offers to sell the freehold + airspace rights for £150,000. Ms Singh acquires, applies for planning permission to add 2 floors and create 6 additional flats, completes the build in 2026 at total cost £900,000, and sells the 6 new flats off-plan during construction for £1.8m total (£900,000 development profit).

- **Original 4 HMO flats:** held as investment throughout; no Condition A risk; no Condition C (not in stock); no Condition D (not developed). Continue as investment.
- **New 6 flats from the airspace development:** Condition D engages — Ms Singh acquired the airspace AND undertook development "with the main purpose of realising a profit from disposing of the land". The earlier long-hold investment intent on the existing HMO does NOT insulate the new development from Condition D — the test applies to the development decision, not the original acquisition.
- **Tax outcome:** £900,000 development profit as trading income under ITA 2007 Part 9A; income tax at 45% + Class 4 NIC = approximately £435,000-£450,000. If Ms Singh had treated this as CGT (£900,000 × 24% = £216,000), she would have underpaid by £200,000+ and faced discovery assessment under TMA 1970 s.29 plus Sch 24 penalties.
- **The planning lever:** Ms Singh could have structured this via a separate property development SPV from the outset, kept the existing HMO investment in her personal name, and incorporated the development in the SPV under CTA 2010 Part 22 (NOT s.162). The SPV's £900,000 profit would face CT at 25% (~£225,000) plus RPDT 4% surcharge IF group residential developer profit exceeded £25m (not applicable at this scale) — a significantly different outcome from individual marginal rates.

Inline aside CTA: "Before you start a development on an existing long-held property, talk to us about Condition D and the SPV-development structure. The decision happens at planning-permission stage, not at sale."

### Example 3 — The 6-month associated-persons window catches the connected-company route

Mr Patel owns 100% of Patel Property Ltd (a BTL SPV). He acquires Site A in October 2025 for £400,000 with development intent; develops in 2026 at cost £300,000; total cost £700,000. Three days before completion of the build in November 2026, he transfers Site A to a connected company Patel Holdings Ltd (also 100% Patel-owned) at "market value" of £700,000 (the cost). Patel Holdings then sells the finished site to a third party in December 2026 for £1.1m (£400,000 gain).

- **The argument Mr Patel hopes for:** Patel Property Ltd took no profit (sold at cost £700,000 to connected party); Patel Holdings Ltd booked £400,000 gain but argues this is a holding-period gain of a few weeks, not a development profit, taxed as CGT not trading. Plausible naive reading.
- **What CTA 2010 s.356OB(2) + s.356OB(8) actually do:** the "chargeable person" rule catches not just the person acquiring / holding / developing, but also persons ASSOCIATED with that person "at any time in the period beginning when the activities of the project begin and ending 6 months after the disposal" (s.356OB(8) verbatim per §28.2 lock). Patel Holdings was an associated person at all relevant times (common 100% ownership). The fragmentation rule at s.356OH treats the development activities by Patel Property AND the disposal by Patel Holdings as a single coordinated scheme attributable to the chargeable person.
- **Outcome:** the £400,000 gain is recast as trading profit; CT at 25% + RPDT consideration (not applicable below £25m allowance). The connected-company route does NOT escape the regime.

Inline aside CTA: "The 6-month associated-persons window is the single most-litigated trap in Part 8ZB / Part 9A. Talk to us about structuring before you transfer between connected entities."

### Example 4 — Investment-side planning levers in numbers

Same facts as Example 1, but Mr Patel restructures upfront for investor-side treatment:

- **Investment-style acquisition.** Purchases via a property holding company; SIC code 68209 (letting and operating of own real estate), NOT 41100 (development).
- **Long-term financing.** 10-year fixed BTL mortgage at 5%, NOT 6-month bridge loan. Financing pattern signals investment intent.
- **Rent-bearing period.** Lets the property at market rent for 36 months while undertaking lower-intensity improvements (kitchen, bathroom, decorating — repair-bordering-improvement, deductible against rental income with capital element added to base cost).
- **Sale 5 years later.** Sells for £1.4m (capital appreciation + accrued improvements). Gain: £1.4m − £700k cost = £700,000.
- **Investor-side treatment.** Chargeable gain under TCGA 1992; CGT residential at 24% (rate-by-reference per §5) = £168,000. Contrast Example 1's developer-side ~£240,000 on a smaller gain (£500,000). The structural choices made BEFORE the acquisition shift the centre of gravity toward investment classification.

The planning levers in numbers: SIC code (£0 cost, signals intent); long-term financing (cheaper basis-point cost; signals intent); rent-bearing period (£60,000-£100,000 in rental income over 36 months — positive contribution while signalling investment); held longer (5 years vs 4 months — frequency / period-of-ownership badges).

## FAQ expansion (RUN session polishes prose; 10-12 FAQs target)

1. **Q: What is the difference between a property investor and a property developer for UK tax purposes?**
   A: An **investor** holds property for rental income with capital appreciation as a bonus; disposals are CGT chargeable gains (TCGA 1992; 18%/24% residential bands from 30 October 2024 per §5; CT for companies). A **developer** acquires + improves + sells property with profit-from-disposal as the main purpose; the resulting profit is trading income (ITTOIA 2005 Part 2 individuals / CTA 2009 Part 3 companies) taxed at marginal income-tax rates up to 45% plus Class 4 NIC for individuals, or CT plus RPDT 4% surcharge for companies above the £25m group allowance. The tax difference between the two routes is often 2× or more on the same gross gain.

2. **Q: What are the four conditions in the Part 8ZB / Part 9A test?**
   A: Per CTA 2010 s.356OB / ITA 2007 s.517B (verbatim per §28.2 lock): (A) acquisition main-purpose test — was the main purpose at acquisition profit-from-disposal? (B) derived-property acquisition main-purpose test — same, for interests deriving value from land (SPV shares, partnership interests); (C) trading-stock test (deterministic — if held as trading stock, caught with no main-purpose evaluation); (D) development main-purpose test — was the main purpose at development profit-from-disposal? Any ONE of A-D being met catches the transaction. The "main purpose, or one of the main purposes" wording is disjunctive — multiple main purposes can coexist; investment intent alongside profit intent does not defeat the test.

3. **Q: If I buy a renovation project intending to live in it then change my mind, am I caught?**
   A: This is a Condition A / Condition D border case. If the main purpose at ACQUISITION was owner-occupation (with profit-from-disposal not a main purpose), Condition A fails. If you then complete the renovation, occupy the property as your only or main residence, and later sell — the disposal would normally fall within Principal Private Residence relief under TCGA 1992 s.222. But if shortly after the renovation completes you decide NOT to occupy and instead market-sell, HMRC may argue the development phase carried profit-intent as a main purpose, engaging Condition D. The fact-sensitive assessment turns on contemporaneous evidence: financing arrangements, marketing activity, actual occupation, length of ownership. Document the original intent in writing at acquisition.

4. **Q: Does Condition D apply if I develop only 5% of the property?**
   A: Condition D applies wherever development has taken place AND the main purpose of that development was profit-from-disposal. There is no statutory de minimis. In practice, light-touch improvements (kitchen / bathroom refresh; cosmetic redecoration) are unlikely to constitute "developing the land" in the s.356OB sense — that language envisages substantial works changing the character or value of the property. Substantial extensions, additional storeys, change of use, structural reconfiguration are the typical Condition D triggers. For border cases, the test is fact-sensitive; specialist advice essential before committing.

5. **Q: Can I incorporate a developer portfolio under s.162 incorporation relief?**
   A: No — s.162 is for going-concern BUSINESS transfers, not for trading-stock appropriations. Per TCGA 1992 s.161 + §28.6 lock, appropriation INTO trading stock is a deemed disposal at market value for CGT, with no s.162 relief available. The route to incorporate a property development trade is CTA 2010 Part 22 (transfers of trade — applies at the company level to intra-group transactions). For a sole-trader developer incorporating into a company, the s.161 deemed disposal triggers immediately; planning needs to manage the CGT crystallisation event upfront, not at incorporation.

6. **Q: What is the 6-month associated-persons window?**
   A: Per CTA 2010 s.356OB(2) + s.356OB(8) / ITA 2007 s.517B(2) + s.517B(8) (verbatim per §28.2 lock), the "chargeable person" rule catches not just the person acquiring / holding / developing the land, but ALSO persons associated with that person "at any time in the period beginning when the activities of the project begin and ending 6 months after the disposal". Operative effect: cannot insulate the profit by transferring to a connected company immediately before disposal, even if the relationship dissolves after disposal. The s.356OH / s.517H anti-fragmentation rule complements this by attributing fragmented activities to the chargeable person.

7. **Q: Is RPDT still in force or has it been repealed?**
   A: **Currently IN FORCE per FA 2022 Part 2 ss.31-53** (per §28.7 lock — STATUS CORRECTED 2026-05-25). Applies for accounting periods beginning on or after 1 April 2022; 4% additional CT charge on residential property developer profits above the £25m group allowance. The widely-circulated "repealed by FA 2024 s.81" claim is wrong — FA 2024 s.81 returns 404 on legislation.gov.uk; FA 2024 + FA(No.2) 2024 contents contain no RPDT-repeal provision. RUN session re-verifies status at write per §16.27 rate-by-reference discipline.

8. **Q: Can I escape Part 8ZB by holding the property in an SPV and selling SPV shares?**
   A: Largely no. CTA 2010 s.356OD / ITA 2007 s.517D applies a three-condition framework specific to indirect disposals via property-rich entities (≥50% derivation from UK land + arrangement + main-purpose). Both the direct land disposal route and the share disposal route are caught. Tracing rules at s.356OM / s.517N walk through multi-tier structures. The historic "hold land in SPV, sell SPV shares for CGT" planning route is largely closed where development-intent or profit-from-disposal intent is genuine.

9. **Q: What is the IHT-side Pawson test and how does it interact?**
   A: *Pawson v HMRC* [2013] UKUT 050 (TCC) is the IHT-side BPR investment-line test — a property letting business is presumed to be wholly or mainly investment unless the activities clearly cross into trading territory. The Pawson test and the Part 8ZB / Part 9A test are overlapping but DISTINCT. A landlord can fail Pawson (investment for BPR — no BPR available) AND be caught by Part 8ZB / Part 9A (trading for CT/IT) on the same portfolio. The two tests are not interchangeable; sessions must keep the IHT side (Pawson) and the CT/IT side (Part 8ZB / Part 9A) architecturally separate.

10. **Q: How does the trading classification interact with FHL transitional rules?**
    A: The Furnished Holiday Lettings regime was abolished from 6 April 2025 (individuals) / 1 April 2025 (companies) per FA 2025 Sch 5; the transitional capital-allowances pool is preserved per §6 + §25.7. FHL classification was a special carve-out from rental-business treatment — it did NOT make a host a "developer" under Part 8ZB / Part 9A. Post-FHL-abolition, large short-let operators (multi-unit channel-manager hosts) may face Part 8ZB / Part 9A scrutiny if the activity pattern looks more like developer-flip than passive let — see the C11 sibling page (`booking-com-a-complete-guide-for-the-hosts`) for the OTA-host operational angle.

11. **Q: What are the planning levers that keep me on the investment side?**
    A: (a) **Long-hold intent at acquisition** documented contemporaneously (board minutes, acquisition correspondence, financing documentation); (b) **rent-bearing periods before disposal** (a long-let BTL with arm's-length tenants is far harder to recast as trading stock); (c) **investment-style record-keeping** (fixed-asset treatment in accounts; no inventory line; long-term financing rather than bridge/development loans); (d) **non-developer entity structure** (SIC 68209 — letting and operating of real estate, NOT SIC 41100 — development of building projects); (e) **avoid coordinated activities that look like fragmented development** (s.356OH / s.517H trap). None of these levers are absolute, but they shift the centre of gravity of the badges-of-trade evaluation.

12. **Q: I've done one flip — am I a trader?**
    A: A single transaction CAN be trading under *Iswera v IRC* [1965] 1 WLR 663 (Privy Council) and *Page v Lowther* [1983] STC 799 — where intent at acquisition was profit-from-disposal + active steps to enhance value, single-transaction trading is established. Combined with Condition A / Condition D of the statutory regime, the "I only did one" defence is unreliable. The question is intent + activity, not frequency. Document the intent for any single transaction the same way you would for a portfolio.
