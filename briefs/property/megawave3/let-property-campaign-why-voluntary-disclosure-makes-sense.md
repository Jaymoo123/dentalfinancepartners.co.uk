# Why Voluntary Disclosure via the Let Property Campaign Makes Sense: The Strategic Rationale for Residential Landlords

## Statutory anchor

- **Primary — the voluntary-disclosure architecture HMRC offers:** Schedule 41 FA 2008 paragraph 13 is the **operative incentive mechanism** — the unprompted-disclosure mitigation floors (0% non-deliberate within 12 months / 10% non-deliberate after / 20% deliberate-not-concealed / 30% deliberate-and-concealed) exist precisely to reward early voluntary disclosers with lower penalties than the prompted-disclosure floors HMRC applies when it has indicated awareness of the failure (10% / 35% / 50% respectively). The bargain HMRC offers is: pay less penalty, in exchange for surfacing the failure HMRC would otherwise have to find. Verified per §27.3 Wave 7 lock 2026-05-24.
- **Primary — what "unprompted" status means as the structural pivot:** unprompted-disclosure status exists ONLY while HMRC has not yet indicated awareness of the failure — no nudge letter, no enquiry, no Connect-system match notified. The window of unprompted-status erodes day-by-day as HMRC's data-matching architecture (Connect, third-party data, Land Registry, Companies House cross-reference, lettings-agent reports, council-tax-records cross-reference, the §22.16 TRS register, mortgage-application records) feeds new matches into HMRC's risk model. The strategic point: the unprompted-status window is rising-cost-of-delay, not stable.
- **Primary — closure benefit:** TMA 1970 s.29 (discovery threshold) read with the s.28A closure-notice framework — once an LPC disclosure is accepted by HMRC and paid in full, the years covered are **closed** for s.29 discovery purposes (subject to the s.29(4)/(5) re-opening conditions where the disclosure was incomplete or deliberately understated). The certainty-of-closure benefit is the structural counterweight to the rising-cost-of-delay risk: voluntary disclosure converts indeterminate s.36 / s.36A long-tail risk (4 / 6 / 12 / 20 years backwards) into closed, paid, finished status. House-position-locked at §27.4 + §27.6 Wave 7 lock 2026-05-24.
- **Primary — the prompted-disclosure floor as the cost of waiting:** Sch 41 para 13 prompted-disclosure floors (10% / 35% / 50%) versus unprompted (0% / 20% / 30%). The step-function differential is the **discovery-cost premium**: waiting until HMRC discovers raises the floor by 10 percentage points (non-deliberate), 15 points (deliberate-not-concealed), 20 points (deliberate-concealed) — on top of the full Cat 1/2/3 maximum bracketing. For an offshore Cat 3 landlord, the cost of waiting can run from a 30% prompted-deliberate-concealed floor to a 200% Sch 41 para 6A-uplift maximum — a 170-percentage-point spread that is the most operationally significant variable in the voluntary-disclosure decision.
- **Primary — extended-time-limit reach as the long-tail risk:** TMA 1970 s.36(1) (careless 6 years) + s.36(1A) (deliberate 20 years) + s.36A FA 2019 (offshore innocent-error 12 years) — HMRC's reach into prior years escalates with behaviour category and territory. A landlord with 8 years of undisclosed UK rental income who self-discloses limits the exposure to the disclosed years; a landlord who waits until HMRC discovers via Connect-system match faces full s.36(1) / s.36(1A) reach into the same 8 years plus interest accrual. The voluntary-disclosure decision is therefore not "how many years do I owe" — it's "how many years am I exposed to" under each scenario, which is two different questions.
- **Primary — the §27.6 route-architecture rationale:** LPC is HMRC's published gentlest-entry-point voluntary disclosure route for the residential landlord population. WDF (offshore), DDS (UK non-rental), and CoP9 / CDF (criminal-prosecution-immunity for deliberate-fraud cases) are sequentially more specialist + more demanding routes. The strategic point: LPC is the route HMRC designed for the typical landlord (accidental, careless, late-realised) and the strategic logic is to engage at the gentlest available entry-point rather than wait for HMRC to escalate to a more demanding route.
- **Primary — Connect-system data-matching as the discovery probability driver:** HMRC's Connect system (operational since 2010, expanded continuously since) cross-references taxpayer data against third-party data sources at scale. Sources include Land Registry (property ownership), Companies House (LtdCo + FIC directorships), lettings-agency reports (rental-agent-managed properties under §19.13), council-tax records (occupancy patterns suggesting let), the §22.16 TRS register (trust property), mortgage-application records (BTL mortgages presuppose rental income), and increasingly online platform reports (Airbnb / Booking.com per the §19.13 + DAC7 EU-directive equivalent). The strategic point: discovery probability is NOT a stable random variable — it is rising over time as data sources expand.
- **House position reference:** §27.6 (Voluntary disclosure routes — LPC + WDF + DDS + CoP9 — primary anchor for the route-architecture rationale); §27.3 (Sch 41 mitigation matrix — operative); §27.1 (discovery time limits — counterfactual reach); §27.4 (s.28A closure-notice architecture — certainty-of-closure mechanism); §27.5 (CoP9 boundary — for landlords whose self-assessment puts them in deliberate-concealed criminal-prosecution-exposure territory). No NEW HP LOCK NEEDED — fully covered by Wave 7 §27 lock.

## Worked-example data (RUN session uses these as canvas)

### Example 1 — The unprompted-vs-prompted differential as the structural asymmetry argument

- **Stratton-Estate, the still-unprompted landlord:** has 4 years of undisclosed rental on a 2-property portfolio. Total undisclosed tax across the 4 years ≈ £12,800 (£3,200/year average). Behaviour: non-deliberate (sloppy assumption that net-income-below-PA-threshold meant no return required — wrong, but plausibly non-deliberate). No nudge letter, no enquiry. Most recent year (2024/25) within 12-month window for 0% floor; earlier 3 years at 10% unprompted floor.
- **Unprompted outcome (today):** Sch 41 floors apply — 0% on 2024/25 tax (£3,200 × 0% = £0) + 10% on 2021/22 to 2023/24 tax (£9,600 × 10% = £960). Total penalty estimate: £960. Plus interest from each year's due date (cumulative ≈ £1,400 at current rate).
- **Prompted counterfactual (if HMRC issues nudge letter or opens s.29 enquiry first):** Sch 41 prompted floors apply — 10% across all 4 years (£12,800 × 10% = £1,280). Marginal penalty differential: £320 (Stratton-Estate keeps £320 by acting now versus after a nudge letter). Plus the 2024/25 0%-window closes ~31 January 2027.
- **Operational point:** the strategic argument is NOT just the £320 — it is the certainty of capture. Once HMRC issues the nudge letter, prompted-status is locked in for the identified years; the unprompted-window cannot be reopened. The choice is between an action Stratton-Estate controls (disclose now, lock in 0% on the recent year + 10% on earlier) and an outcome HMRC controls (nudge letter triggers prompted-status across the identified years). The strategic argument is the asymmetry between controlled-outcome and uncontrolled-outcome.

### Example 2 — The long-tail-reach argument: how s.36 widens the assessment scope

- **Underhill-Estate, the late-realiser:** has 11 years of undisclosed rental on a single BTL property. Realises the situation in 2026. Behaviour self-assessment: non-deliberate for years 1-6 (genuinely unaware), then careless for years 7-11 (becoming-aware-but-deferring). No nudge letter.
- **Voluntary-disclosure-scope (under LPC):** Underhill-Estate elects to disclose all 11 years voluntarily (the disclosure must be complete and frank to engage the unprompted-floor mitigation). Sch 41 unprompted floor applies — 0% on the recent-year-within-12-months + 10% on earlier non-deliberate years + 20% on careless years (if HMRC characterises those years as deliberate-not-concealed). Scope of years disclosed: 11 (the full undisclosed history).
- **Discovery counterfactual (if HMRC opens s.29 first):** s.36(1) 6-year careless reach into years 6-11 (the most recent 6 years carry careless behaviour); s.36(1A) 20-year deliberate reach only if HMRC characterises any year as deliberate (could reach back to year 1 plus). Prompted-disclosure floor applies — 10% / 35% / 50%. Aggregate scope HMRC can assess: 6-20 years depending on behaviour-categorisation.
- **Operational point:** the long-tail argument is COUNTER-INTUITIVE for the landlord. Voluntary disclosure of ALL 11 years sounds "worse" than waiting for HMRC to "only" find 6 years. But the discovery counterfactual is NOT 6-year prompted-disclosure cleanly — it is 6+ year reach AT PROMPTED floors (the higher band) PLUS potentially 20-year deliberate reach if HMRC characterises any year as deliberate. The voluntary route is calibrated to (a) protect against the 20-year reach (deliberate categorisation is far less likely on a voluntary disclosure with a clean compliance narrative); (b) lock in unprompted floors; (c) close all 11 years cleanly. The strategic argument: voluntary disclosure trades certain-and-low against uncertain-and-high.

### Example 3 — The Connect-system data-matching argument: rising probability over time

- **Westhampnett-Estate, the data-visible landlord:** holds 5 BTL properties across 3 UK cities; all properties are mortgaged with named UK lenders; rental income is collected via UK letting agents; properties are registered with Land Registry under the landlord's own name. The landlord has never declared rental income. Discovery probability is rising every year as HMRC's Connect system expands its data sources.
- **2020 discovery probability:** moderate. Connect was operational but the data-source list was narrower; landlord-without-SA-return + landlord-with-multiple-Land-Registry-titles + letting-agent-data was already in the model.
- **2026 discovery probability:** materially higher. Additional data sources now include: §22.16 TRS register (for any trust-held property), §19.13 letting-agent enhanced reporting (under FA 2024 + SI 2024 instruments for MTD ITSA preparation), online-platform reports (Airbnb / Booking.com under DAC7 + UK equivalent), mortgage-application records (cross-matched with rental-income-presupposed BTL products), council-tax-records (occupancy patterns inconsistent with declared residence).
- **2028 discovery probability:** further higher. The MTD ITSA mandate at £20k qualifying income (April 2028) brings additional letting-agent reporting + software-vendor data flows into the Connect ecosystem. Properties Westhampnett-Estate holds today will be in the £20k cohort by 2028; the data flows will increase.
- **Operational point:** the rising-discovery-probability argument is the timing-of-disclosure argument. Strategic logic: disclose while the discovery-probability is lower (i.e. NOW), capturing the unprompted-floor before the rising probability flips to discovery + prompted-floor. The argument is structurally about the asymmetry between controlled-disclosure-timing and uncontrolled-discovery-timing.

### Example 4 — The route-architecture argument: why LPC is the gentlest entry-point

- **Hollybank-Estate, the cross-stream landlord:** has (a) undisclosed UK rental from 3 BTL properties = LPC territory; (b) undisclosed offshore rental from a property in a Cat 2 territory = WDF territory; (c) some general UK non-rental income (small consulting fees never declared) = DDS territory. Behaviour self-assessment across all three streams: non-deliberate.
- **Route-architecture analysis:** LPC for the UK rental stream + WDF for the offshore rental + DDS for the non-rental UK income. Each route is calibrated to its specific stream; using LPC for the offshore would be procedurally wrong (offshore engages the FtC overlay regime under FA 2017 Sch 18 if any pre-30-September-2018 years are in scope); using WDF for the UK rental would unnecessarily import the offshore reporting framework.
- **Why LPC is the gentlest:** Sch 41 + Sch 24 underpin LPC + DDS equally; both engage the unprompted-disclosure floor architecture. WDF additionally engages the FtC overlay (FA 2017 Sch 18 — 200% standard / 100% reduced penalty cliff for pre-30-September-2018 offshore matters). CoP9 / CDF additionally engages the criminal-prosecution-immunity-conditional-on-full-disclosure architecture. The sequence (LPC / DDS / WDF / CoP9) is calibrated by population — LPC is the largest-population gentlest route for the typical landlord.
- **Operational point:** the route-architecture argument is about MATCHING the route to the conduct + asset profile. Hollybank-Estate engages all three voluntary-disclosure routes in parallel; using LPC for everything would be procedurally wrong; not using LPC for the UK rental would forfeit the route HMRC designed for the population.

### Example 5 — The closure-of-exposure argument: what s.28A acceptance produces

- **Penbury-Estate, the post-disclosure landlord:** has just submitted an LPC disclosure covering 6 years of undisclosed UK rental. HMRC issues an acceptance letter with closure-notice equivalent treatment under TMA 1970 s.28A. Penbury-Estate has paid in full.
- **What closure means structurally:** the 6 disclosed years are now closed for TMA 1970 s.29 discovery purposes (subject to s.29(4)/(5) re-opening only where the disclosure was incomplete or deliberately understated — neither applies here). Penbury-Estate's future SA returns are accepted as routine; the Connect-system risk-rating drops materially; any future property disposal's CGT computation engages the closed-period rental history cleanly (acquisition cost + capital improvements + period-of-let-treatment all on record).
- **What did NOT close:** the future tax obligation (Penbury-Estate's 2025/26 SA return is the next live obligation; LPC closes the past, not the future); the interest already paid (LPC does not "discount" interest, only penalty); the duty to continue declaring rental income going forward (post-LPC, Penbury-Estate is a routine SA filer + MTD ITSA participant from April 2026 if above threshold).
- **Operational point:** the closure-of-exposure benefit is the structural reassurance that LPC produces a finished outcome. Without LPC, Penbury-Estate carries indeterminate s.36 / s.36A long-tail exposure (4 / 6 / 12 / 20 years backwards) for life; with LPC, the past is settled and the future is clean.

## FAQ expansion (RUN session polishes prose; 10-12 FAQs target)

1. **Q: Why does HMRC offer penalty mitigation for voluntary disclosure?**
   A: HMRC's Schedule 41 paragraph 13 (failure-to-notify) and Schedule 24 paragraph 10 (inaccuracy) build a calibrated bargain: disclosure-before-HMRC-indicates-awareness unlocks lower penalty floors than disclosure-after. The policy rationale is to incentivise self-correction at scale — HMRC recovers tax + interest + reduced penalty without enforcement cost; the taxpayer escapes the higher prompted-disclosure floors. Declining the bargain is leaving value on the table.

2. **Q: What is the structural asymmetry between unprompted and prompted disclosure?**
   A: For failure-to-notify (Sch 41 para 13), unprompted-disclosure floors are 0% non-deliberate (within 12 months) or 10% (after), 20% deliberate, 30% deliberate-and-concealed. Prompted-disclosure floors are 10%, 35%, 50%. The 10 / 15 / 20-percentage-point step-function differential is the cost of waiting until HMRC indicates awareness. For offshore Cat 2 / Cat 3 territories, the Sch 41 para 6A uplift (1.5x / 2x) compounds the differential.

3. **Q: What is "unprompted status" as a strategic concept?**
   A: Unprompted-status exists ONLY while HMRC has not indicated awareness of the failure: no nudge letter, no enquiry, no Connect-system match notified. The window erodes day-by-day as HMRC's data-matching architecture expands. Strategic logic: the unprompted-status window is rising-cost-of-delay, not stable. Acting while still unprompted captures the lower-floor architecture HMRC designed for early disclosers.

4. **Q: What does the certainty-of-closure benefit mean?**
   A: TMA 1970 s.28A closure-notice architecture + s.29 discovery threshold combine to produce a finished outcome on LPC acceptance. The disclosed years are closed for HMRC discovery purposes (subject to s.29(4)/(5) re-opening only where the disclosure was incomplete or deliberately understated). Without disclosure, the landlord carries indeterminate s.36 / s.36A long-tail exposure (up to 20 years on deliberate behaviour) for the rest of their life. Closure converts that into settled.

5. **Q: How does HMRC's Connect system change the discovery calculation?**
   A: Connect cross-references taxpayer records against expanding third-party data sources: Land Registry (property ownership), Companies House (LtdCo / FIC directorships), letting-agent reports (rental-agent-managed properties), council-tax records (occupancy patterns), §22.16 TRS register (trust property), mortgage-application records (BTL mortgages presuppose rental income), online-platform reports (Airbnb / Booking.com under DAC7 equivalent). Discovery probability is rising over time as data sources expand — it is NOT a stable random variable.

6. **Q: Why is LPC the gentlest voluntary-disclosure route?**
   A: HMRC's voluntary-disclosure routes are sequenced by demanding-ness. LPC is calibrated for the residential-landlord population (the largest voluntary-disclosure candidate cohort); DDS is the general UK-tax voluntary-disclosure umbrella for non-LPC cases; WDF adds the FtC overlay for offshore matters; CoP9 / CDF is the criminal-prosecution-immunity route for suspected serious fraud. LPC is the gentlest entry-point because it engages Sch 41 + Sch 24 floors directly without overlay regimes.

7. **Q: When does the strategic logic point AWAY from LPC?**
   A: LPC is NOT right when: (a) the behaviour is deliberate-and-concealed with criminal-prosecution exposure — escalate to CoP9 / CDF; (b) the assets / income are offshore in Cat 2 / Cat 3 territories with pre-30-September-2018 years — engage WDF + FtC overlay; (c) HMRC has already opened an enquiry — the disclosure happens within the open enquiry, not as standalone LPC; (d) the income / assets are non-rental — use general DDS, not LPC.

8. **Q: What about the cost of disclosure?**
   A: The cost is tax + interest + penalty. Tax is the underlying liability (no mitigation under LPC). Interest accrues continuously under TMA 1970 s.86 (Bank base + 4pp; current rate verified at write time) from each year's due date; LPC does NOT mitigate interest. Penalty is the variable LPC mitigates — Sch 41 / Sch 24 unprompted floors are the rescue. For multi-year disclosures, interest is often the largest line item; sessions should be honest about this.

9. **Q: Why does the reputational argument matter?**
   A: For landlords with public-facing roles (LtdCo directors, FCA-regulated firm employees, registered professionals, education-sector employees with safeguarding duties), HMRC disclosure-status interacts with regulatory fit-and-proper tests. A closed LPC disclosure is materially better for fit-and-proper considerations than a published HMRC penalty assessment for deliberate-concealment behaviour. Voluntary disclosure pre-empts the reputational adverse outcome.

10. **Q: What is the operational timeline from notification to closure?**
    A: Three steps. Notify via gov.uk LPC notification form (week 1); receive Disclosure Reference Number (DRN) from HMRC (typical 1-2 weeks); prepare and submit full disclosure within 90 days (typical 4-8 weeks for a multi-year case requiring rental-records reconstruction); pay on submission. HMRC responds in 4-12 weeks; closure on acceptance. Total notify-to-closure cycle: typically 4-9 months.

11. **Q: How does the downstream-engagement-cost argument work?**
    A: Closed LPC files do NOT trigger downstream Connect-system flags or HMRC's risk-rating of adjacent property holdings. Open / undisclosed positions DO raise the risk-rating of all of the landlord's HMRC-visible holdings (other rental property, the LtdCo, personal trading income, future property disposals). The strategic argument: voluntary disclosure normalises ALL of the landlord's tax positions, not just the disclosed years.

12. **Q: What is the next step?**
    A: Begin with the LPC notification at gov.uk/guidance/let-property-campaign. For case-specific input on behaviour-categorisation + year-coverage + supporting-evidence preparation, engage a property-tax specialist before submitting the formal disclosure. Cross-link to the LPC penalty calculator for numerical estimation, the LPC orientation page for the definitional framing, the LPC rescue page for the panic-moment immediate-action framework, and the benefits-tally page for the tactical mitigation-floor breakdown.
