### Example 2 — Council-tax second-home premium on a 2-month void between tenancies

Ms Singh holds a BTL in Plymouth and is considering adding a second BTL in Cardiff. Her Plymouth property has a 2-month void window between tenants in 2026.

- **Plymouth (LURA 2023 Part 4 framework — England):** Plymouth City Council has exercised the 100% second-home / long-term-empty premium powers. The premium applies to **empty + second-home** properties; let properties are typically OUT of premium scope while a tenancy is in place. **The trap:** the 2-month void window itself does NOT trigger premium classification (long-term-empty threshold is 1 year+); the property remains in standard council-tax band during the void. Mr Patel's risk is RE-classification triggers IF the property sits empty 12+ months continuously.
- **Cardiff (LGF(W)A 2024 framework — Wales — up to 300% premium ceiling):** Cardiff Council has exercised the Welsh second-home / long-term-empty premium powers; current premium rate per Cardiff Council's published policy (RUN verifies at cardiff.gov.uk at write). The Welsh second-home definition can capture a let-property between tenancies where the property is treated as the landlord's "second home" rather than tenant-occupied. The Welsh exemption set differs from England.
- **Operative implication:** the cash-on-hold council-tax overhead at Cardiff exceeds Plymouth at premium-rate ceilings, but for actively-let property with short voids, both councils typically remain in standard-band scope. Sessions clarify the THRESHOLDS not the premium-ceilings as the operative variable.

### Example 3 — Rent Smart Wales and the single-property English landlord

Mrs Singh, an English-domiciled landlord living in Plymouth, inherits a single property in Cardiff and decides to let it.

- **Rent Smart Wales obligation (Housing (Wales) Act 2014 Part 1):** Mrs Singh MUST register herself as a landlord with Rent Smart Wales (annual fee, RUN verifies at rentsmart.gov.wales at write) AND either obtain a landlord licence (further fee + training requirement) OR engage a Rent-Smart-Wales-licensed managing agent. There is NO single-property exemption; the obligation applies regardless of portfolio size.
- **Failure consequences:** unlicensed letting can result in fixed-penalty notices, prosecution, and rent-repayment-order applications from tenants per RHWA 2016 (operative tenant-side remedy).
- **Plymouth comparison:** the equivalent residential property in Plymouth would attract Rent Smart Wales obligations ONLY if it is in Wales. Plymouth's selective licensing under Housing Act 2004 Part 3 applies in DESIGNATED wards only; many Plymouth wards are NOT covered, and many landlords have no licensing obligation at all on the Plymouth side.
- **The cross-border tax / regulatory cost differential:** Mrs Singh faces an annual licensing-and-training overhead on the Cardiff side that she does not face on the Plymouth side (where her property is in an undesignated ward); this overhead reduces the after-tax yield on the Cardiff property by ~£100-£300/year vs Plymouth in undesignated areas. Not a tax change; a regulatory cost line.

## FAQ expansion (RUN session polishes prose; 10-12 FAQs target)

1. **Q: Does Section 24 work differently in Wales for my Cardiff BTL?**
   A: No. ITTOIA 2005 s.272A finance cost restriction applies UK-wide and is uniform between England, Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland. Your Cardiff BTL is treated identically to your Plymouth BTL on the income-tax side: 100% of rental profit is taxable (no interest deduction); a 20% basic-rate credit is available on finance costs. No tax-planning lever exists by choosing one jurisdiction over the other on the income-tax limb.

2. **Q: If I am an English landlord buying my first Welsh property, do I need to register with Rent Smart Wales?**
   A: Yes. Housing (Wales) Act 2014 Part 1 requires every landlord of a Welsh residential let to register with Rent Smart Wales, regardless of whether you live in Wales or have any other Welsh property. You must also either obtain a landlord licence (which requires training) or appoint a Rent-Smart-Wales-licensed agent. There is no single-property exemption. The obligation arises on the first let, not after a portfolio threshold. There is no equivalent mandatory all-landlords scheme in England — Plymouth operates selective licensing in specific wards only under Housing Act 2004 Part 3.

3. **Q: What happens to my Cardiff property's council tax if it sits empty for two months between tenancies?**
   A: A 2-month void typically does NOT trigger Cardiff Council's second-home or long-term-empty premium. The Welsh long-term-empty threshold is generally 1 year+ of continuous emptiness; the second-home premium attaches to properties classified as second homes (not let to tenants). A short void between tenancies, with the property being prepared for re-letting, remains within the standard council-tax band. RUN session verifies Cardiff Council's specific policy on void-between-tenancy treatment at cardiff.gov.uk at write. Plymouth applies the equivalent threshold under LURA 2023 Part 4.

4. **Q: Is the 4-year refund window for Welsh LTT only or does it cover my English replacement purchase?**
   A: The Welsh 4-year refund window applies to Welsh LTT Higher Residential Property transactions only. If you sell your English main residence and replace it with another English purchase, the FA 2003 Sch 4ZA para 3 + para 8 framework gives you a 3-year window from the new-property completion to claim refund of the additional-dwellings surcharge. If your replacement purchase is in Wales, the LTTADA 2017 4-year window applies. Mixed scenarios (English sale, Welsh purchase or vice versa) follow the rules of the JURISDICTION of the surcharge being refunded. Talk to us about the cross-jurisdictional timing if you are sequencing transactions across the England-Wales border.

5. **Q: Do I need separate accountants for my Plymouth and Cardiff properties?**
   A: No, one accountant can handle both, but they must be able to operate competently in both the English SDLT + LURA 2023 council-tax framework AND the Welsh LTT + LGF(W)A 2024 + Rent Smart Wales framework. The cross-jurisdictional integration is the practitioner skill, not the entity choice. Your income-tax return reports both jurisdictions' rental income together (UK-resident landlords are taxed on worldwide UK property income on a single SA105 / CT600); the local-jurisdiction differences (SDLT vs LTT; English vs Welsh council tax; selective vs mandatory licensing; RRA 2025 vs RHWA 2016 tenancy regime) are operational layers your accountant must understand.

6. **Q: Does the RRA 2025 apply to my Cardiff property?**
   A: No. The Renters' Rights Act 2025 applies to ENGLAND only. Your Cardiff property is governed by the Renting Homes (Wales) Act 2016 (in force since 1 December 2022) — a structurally different regime with occupation contracts (standard / secure / introductory), different no-fault notice mechanics, and Rent Smart Wales as the licensing framework. Your Plymouth property is governed by the RRA 2025 (Section 21 abolition, periodic-tenancy default, PRS database, Landlord Redress Scheme). The two regimes are NOT interchangeable; if you cross-port practices between the two, you risk non-compliance on whichever jurisdiction's regime you have under-engaged with.

7. **Q: Is the Cardiff Article 4 direction relevant if I'm not planning an HMO conversion?**
   A: Generally no. Cardiff Council's Article 4 directions in Plasnewydd, Cathays, and Roath remove the permitted-development right to convert a C3 dwelling-house to a C4 HMO without planning permission in those specific wards. If your Cardiff investment is a single-tenant let, a family let, or a let to fewer than 3 unrelated occupants, the Article 4 direction has no operational impact. If you contemplate converting a single-let to an HMO in those wards, you face the planning-permission process + cost + refusal risk. Other Cardiff wards may have their own Article 4 designations — verify with Cardiff Council planning at the property level.

8. **Q: How does CGT work on my Plymouth or Cardiff property when I sell?**
   A: Uniform UK-wide. TCGA 1992 + FA 2024 + Autumn Budget 2024 residential CGT rates are 18% (basic-rate band) and 24% (higher-rate band) from 30 October 2024. Both your Plymouth and Cardiff properties report on the same SA / CT computation; 60-day in-year residential CGT reporting per TCGA 1992 Sch 2 paras 6-12 applies UK-wide. No tax-planning lever by choosing one city over the other on the exit side. The Annual Exempt Amount applies once per individual (not per property, not per jurisdiction).

9. **Q: Does the second-home council tax premium apply if I let my property to tenants?**
   A: Generally no. The English LURA 2023 Part 4 premium and the Welsh LGF(W)A 2024 premium attach to PROPERTY USE classification — empty properties (long-term-empty premium) and second homes (second-home premium). A property that is occupied by tenants under a tenancy agreement is typically OUT of premium scope while the tenancy is in place. The risk is short voids between tenancies (typically under-1-year remains in standard band) and properties used by the landlord as a second home (premium applies). Verify the council's specific policy at the property level.

10. **Q: Can I claim Land Remediation Relief on a Plymouth regeneration-corridor purchase?**
    A: Potentially yes, if the property is contaminated within the meaning of FA 2009 + CTA 2009 Part 14 (Land Remediation Relief). The relief applies to qualifying expenditure on cleaning up contamination on land acquired for trading or property-business purposes. Plymouth's regeneration corridor includes plots with previous industrial use that may qualify. The relief is a 150% deduction (or 50% additional relief above the 100% deduction baseline) for qualifying expenditure. Specialist advice is essential — the relief is fact-specific to the contamination type and the landlord's structure (the relief is a CT-side mechanic for corporate landlords + a property-business deduction structure for individuals where applicable). Talk to us if your Plymouth or Cardiff investment includes a contaminated-land component.

11. **Q: Does the SDLT 2% non-resident surcharge apply to me as a UK-resident landlord buying in Wales?**
    A: The SDLT 2% non-resident surcharge under FA 2003 Sch 9A applies to ENGLAND only and only to non-UK-resident purchasers. As a UK-resident landlord buying in Wales, you are subject to the Welsh LTT regime (which has different rate-bands and no separate non-resident surcharge layer comparable to Sch 9A). If you are a non-UK-resident purchaser, the SDLT 2% surcharge applies to your Plymouth purchase under Sch 9A; the Welsh LTT regime applies to your Cardiff purchase without an equivalent non-resident surcharge layer.

12. **Q: How does the MTD for ITSA mandate apply to cross-border landlords?**
    A: MTD for ITSA applies UK-wide to landlords with qualifying property income above the mandate threshold. Your combined Plymouth + Cardiff rental income counts as a single "property business" for MTD-qualifying-income purposes (ITTOIA 2005 + ITA 2007). You file quarterly updates for your combined property business, not separately per jurisdiction. The April 2026 (£50,000+) / April 2027 (£30,000+) / April 2028 (£20,000+ — Spring Statement 2025 confirmation, RUN verifies state at write) mandate thresholds apply to combined UK property income. RUN session verifies current mandate state at write per §19.1.
