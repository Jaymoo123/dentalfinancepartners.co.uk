# ATED relief clawback under FA 2013 s.135: the look-forward and look-back trap when a non-qualifying individual (s.136) occupies the dwelling

## Statutory anchor
- **Primary (the relief whose conditions clawback unwinds):**
  - **FA 2013 s.133** — "Property rental businesses" (verified verbatim at legislation.gov.uk 2026-05-27). A day qualifies for relief if the single-dwelling interest is "being exploited as a source of rents or other receipts (other than excluded rents) in the course of a qualifying property rental business". A "qualifying property rental business" is one "run on a commercial basis and with a view to profit". Relief is **DENIED** if "a non-qualifying individual is permitted to occupy the dwelling" on that day.
- **Primary (the clawback / look-forward + look-back mechanic):**
  - **FA 2013 s.135** — "Non-qualifying occupation: look-forward and look-back" (verified verbatim at legislation.gov.uk 2026-05-27). Look-forward: if a non-qualifying individual occupies a dwelling on any day, "No subsequent day in that chargeable period, or in any of the subsequent 3 chargeable periods" can be treated as relievable unless a day of qualifying use under s.133(1)(a) intervenes. Look-back: earlier days are not relievable if "a relevant person is entitled to the single-dwelling interest on that day," unless a s.133(1)(a) qualifying-use day falls between the earlier day and the non-qualifying occupation day. "Relevant person" includes (i) the person entitled on the non-qualifying occupation day, or (ii) any partnership member who exploited the interest in a property rental business.
- **Primary (definition of non-qualifying individual):**
  - **FA 2013 s.136** — "Meaning of 'non-qualifying individual'" (verified at legislation.gov.uk 2026-05-27). Subsection (1) sets out the eight-category catalogue covering: (a) individuals entitled to the interest (otherwise than as partnership member); connected persons; partnership members; settlors of trusts holding the interest; relatives of connected persons / settlors; spouses / civil partners of relatives; participants in collective investment schemes; and persons connected to those participants. Cross-applied to s.133 and s.135, and to the developer variant at s.139.
- **Primary (developer-chain analogue):**
  - **FA 2013 s.139** — "Property developers: exchange of dwellings" (verified verbatim at legislation.gov.uk 2026-05-27). Sub-section (2): "A day is not relievable by virtue of this section if on that day a non-qualifying individual is permitted to occupy the dwelling." Developer-exchange path has the same non-qualifying-individual exclusion architecture as s.133 rental.
- **Primary (the CIHC qualifying-purpose carve-out interaction):**
  - **CTA 2010 s.18N(3)** — close investment-holding company test. Where the enveloped company's purpose is letting commercially to non-connected persons, it escapes CIHC treatment (and gets the small-profits 19% rate). Letting to connected persons OR their spouses OR relatives of connected persons OR relatives' spouses defeats the qualifying-purpose carve-out. The CIHC test mirrors but is NOT identical to the s.136 non-qualifying-individual catalogue, sessions must not conflate.
- **Primary (interim relief mechanic):**
  - **FA 2013 ss.99-100 + s.101** — interim relief allows the chargeable amount to be reduced for relief-eligible portions of the chargeable period. The interim relief claim is on the return; clawback under s.135 generates adjusted chargeable amount returns under s.163 if non-qualifying occupation supervenes mid-period.
  - **FA 2013 s.163** — "Return of adjusted chargeable amount"; required where relief gained or lost during the chargeable period.
- **House position reference:** §2 (ATED bands 2026/27, verified gov.uk 2026-05-22), **§18 (Wave 3 ATED extension full)**, §18.3 (relief catalogue), §18.4 (return mechanics + relief clawback architectural note: "Relief is **clawed back** where conditions cease to be met within a period of non-qualifying occupation. The most common clawback trigger is occupation by a connected non-qualifying individual, director's family member moving in for a few months while between rentals"), §18.8 (do-not-write list), §21.A (CT three-figure framework, connection to CIHC), §21.5 (CIHC depth, qualifying-purpose carve-out and connected-occupant interaction).

## Framing differentiator (anti-templating, anti-cannibalisation)
This page is the **clawback-mechanic deep-dive** for the most punitive ATED relief structure: the s.135 look-forward / look-back rules. The popular framing, "if a family member moves in, you lose the year's relief", is **structurally wrong on the look-forward side**: s.135 extends loss of relief into the next 3 chargeable periods unless a qualifying-use day under s.133(1)(a) intervenes. A single weekend of a director's daughter staying in the dwelling at year-end can poison the next 3 chargeable years of relief if the dwelling sits vacant or off-let pending refurbishment.

The framing differentiator is the **architecturally honest treatment of the look-forward + look-back combination**: relief is fragile, period-extension is harsh, and the only escape is interposing genuine qualifying-use days (commercial arm's-length letting to unconnected tenants). This page sits **adjacent to but distinct from** B12 (`ated-relief-for-related-persons-and-market-rent-a-complete-guide`, which covers the WHEN-can-relief-coexist-with-connected-party-let question + market-rent positioning) and B5 (the strategic-decision ATED page).

Specifically the page must:

1. **Refuse the year-loss collapse.** Look-forward is up to **3 subsequent chargeable periods** under s.135. Sessions writing "you lose the year" misstate the architecture, the answer is "you lose up to 3 subsequent years unless a s.133(1)(a) qualifying-use day intervenes."
2. **Walk the s.136 non-qualifying individual eight categories.** The catalogue is wider than most owners assume. Settlors of trusts holding the interest are caught; partnership members are caught; spouses of relatives of connected persons are caught (multiple-hop family).
3. **Anchor the look-back mechanic.** The s.135 look-back rule unwinds earlier days where the same "relevant person" was entitled. A director who acquired the dwelling in 2023 and lets it commercially throughout 2023-2025, then allows his daughter to occupy briefly in early 2026, may lose earlier relief retrospectively if no qualifying-use day under s.133(1)(a) sits between the 2023-2025 occupation period and the 2026 daughter-occupation event.
4. **Explain the qualifying-use intervention.** A s.133(1)(a) day is one on which the dwelling is being exploited as a source of rents in the course of a qualifying property rental business. A short commercial arm's-length let (e.g. to a contractor unconnected with the owner) can sever look-forward / look-back chains if genuinely qualifying.
5. **Note the s.163 adjusted-chargeable-amount return obligation.** Where relief is lost mid-period, a s.163 ACA return is required, separate from the annual s.159 return. Failure to file the ACA return triggers Sch 55 items 11A / 11B as for the annual return.
6. **Cross-reference the CIHC qualifying-purpose carve-out (CTA 2010 s.18N(3))**, the connected-occupant test under s.18N(3) is a parallel-but-different test that also poisons company-level tax treatment. Sessions must not conflate s.136 (ATED) with s.18N(3) (CT), but should note both bite together for connected occupancy.
7. **Practical mitigation patterns.** Genuine arm's-length commercial-let interposition; documentation of non-occupation (director's family member visits but does not "occupy"); short-let through a regulated agent at market rent. These work only where the documentation is contemporaneous.
8. **Penalty consequences if clawback discovered late.** Clawback-triggered ACA return omitted → Sch 55 late-filing penalties + Sch 24 inaccuracy on the original-period return (where the s.135 look-back retrospectively unwinds the original relief). Cross-link to B8 + B9.

## Key questions this page must answer
1. **What triggers ATED relief clawback?** (FA 2013 s.135: non-qualifying occupation on any day. Even a single day with a non-qualifying individual permitted to occupy the dwelling triggers loss of relief on that day AND look-forward + look-back extension.)
2. **Who is a "non-qualifying individual" under s.136?** (Eight-category catalogue: (a) individual entitled to the interest (otherwise than as partnership member), and seven additional categories covering connected persons, partnership members, settlors, relatives, spouses of relatives, CIS participants, and connected-to-CIS-participant individuals. Stage 2 must verbatim-verify the full catalogue against legislation.gov.uk.)
3. **The look-forward rule.** What is the period of subsequent disqualification? (Per s.135: the chargeable period of the non-qualifying occupation PLUS the subsequent 3 chargeable periods. Subsequent days are not relievable unless a s.133(1)(a) qualifying-use day intervenes.)
4. **The look-back rule.** How far back can s.135 unwind prior relief? (No statutory time limit on look-back; the rule operates by "earlier day where a relevant person is entitled", back to acquisition by the relevant person, unless a s.133(1)(a) qualifying-use day falls between that earlier day and the non-qualifying occupation day.)
5. **Who is the "relevant person" for look-back?** (Per s.135: (i) the person entitled on the non-qualifying occupation day, or (ii) any partnership member who exploited the interest in a property rental business.)
6. **What is a "qualifying-use day" under s.133(1)(a)?** (A day on which the dwelling is being exploited as a source of rents or other receipts in the course of a qualifying property rental business, i.e. run on a commercial basis and with a view to profit. The intervention day must itself be qualifying; a vacant period does NOT intervene.)
7. **The s.163 adjusted-chargeable-amount return obligation.** (Where relief gained or lost mid-period, an ACA return is required separately from the annual s.159 return. Sch 55 items 11A + 11B both apply for late filing.)
8. **The CIHC interaction at CTA 2010 s.18N(3).** (Connected occupant defeats the company's qualifying-purpose carve-out from CIHC treatment. CIHC = 25% main rate, no small-profits rate, regardless of profit level. The s.18N(3) test is parallel to but NOT identical to s.136. Sessions must cite both where connected occupancy occurs.)
9. **Common scenarios that trigger clawback.** (a) Director's family member uses the dwelling between tenants. (b) Settlor occupies a dwelling held by a corporate trustee. (c) Partner in a partnership-with-corporate-member uses the dwelling personally. (d) CIS participant occupies a unit in a fund-held dwelling. (e) Spouse of a relative of a connected person, multi-hop family caught by s.136 categories.
10. **Mitigation patterns that work.** (a) Genuine arm's-length commercial let to an unconnected tenant interposing a qualifying-use day (s.133(1)(a)). (b) Contemporaneous documentation showing non-occupation (visits without overnight stay, no key issue, no possession). (c) Charging market rent and physical separation evidence, but this only avoids the connected-occupant test if the occupant is NOT within the s.136 catalogue (see B12 for the market-rent angle when occupant IS within the catalogue).
11. **Penalty exposure from late-discovered clawback.** (Sch 55 + Sch 56 + Sch 24 in parallel: late ACA return generates Sch 55 escalator; under-declared chargeable amount via Sch 24 inaccuracy on the original return; offshore Category 2 / 3 uplift if overseas-corporate filer. Unprompted disclosure mitigation under Sch 24 paras 9-10 + Sch 55 paras 17-18 if disclosed before HMRC contact. Cross-link B8 + B9.)
12. **Pre-decision check for owners.** Before allowing any connected-person occupation, the owner / director / trustee should: (a) take advice on s.136 category coverage, (b) ensure a contemporaneous qualifying-use day (s.133(1)(a)) interposes, (c) prepare the s.163 ACA return for affected periods, (d) document the start + end of any non-qualifying occupation precisely.

## Worked-example data (RUN session uses these as canvas)

### Example 1 — The eight-category s.136 catalogue (the page's first concrete artefact)

Render as semantic HTML `<ol>`. Each row paraphrases the statutory category; RUN session quotes the verbatim subsection language at write per §16.35:

1. **(a) Individual entitled to the interest** (otherwise than as partnership member): the natural-person owner / beneficiary if the structure is direct.
2. **(b) Persons connected with (a):** anyone in the s.993 connected-persons catalogue — relatives, spouses, business partners, controlled companies.
3. **(c) Partnership members** entitled (as partnership members) to the interest: every partner where the interest is held in a partnership.
4. **(d) Settlors** of any trust where the trustees hold the interest: relevant for corporate trustees holding UK property for settlor-or-family use.
5. **(e) Relatives of category (a) or (d) individuals:** parents, grandparents, children, grandchildren, siblings (per ITA 2007 s.994 relative definition).
6. **(f) Spouses or civil partners** of relatives in (e): widens to multi-hop family relations (the spouse of a settlor's brother is caught).
7. **(g) Participants in any collective investment scheme** holding the interest: relevant for fund / unit-trust structures.
8. **(h) Persons connected with (g):** widens to CIS-participant family / controlled-company chain.

Operational anchor: the catalogue is wide. Most owners under-estimate categories (e) and (f). Multi-hop family connections (the spouse of your settlor's sibling) ARE within s.136 even though the relationship is not intuitive. Specialist pre-occupation analysis is essential.

### Example 2 — Look-forward 3-period mechanic walked

Rosewood Property Holdings Limited (UK ltd-co holding a £2.4m London BTL; let to unconnected commercial tenants throughout 2022/23, 2023/24, 2024/25, 2025/26; s.133 relief claimed on each annual ATED return; relief approved). October 2026: the director's adult daughter (a Category (e) relative of a connected person under s.136) stays in the dwelling between tenancies for 4 weeks while looking for her own flat. No qualifying-use day under s.133(1)(a) occurs during her stay (the dwelling is vacant + the lease has expired).

- **The clawback trigger:** the daughter is permitted to occupy the dwelling on each day of her 4-week stay. Each day is a non-qualifying-occupation day per s.135.
- **Loss of relief for 2026/27:** the 4 weeks in October 2026 + every subsequent day in 2026/27 (until the next qualifying-use day) is not relievable. If the dwelling remains vacant through to a new commercial let starting 1 March 2027, the entire period from October 2026 to 28 February 2027 is unrelievable. ATED at £9,450 annual × (151 days / 365) = approximately £3,910 chargeable for 2026/27.
- **Look-forward 3-period extension per s.135:** "No subsequent day in that chargeable period, or in any of the subsequent 3 chargeable periods" can be treated as relievable UNLESS a s.133(1)(a) qualifying-use day intervenes. The new commercial let starting 1 March 2027 IS a qualifying-use day → IF that letting genuinely meets the qualifying-rental-business test (commercial basis + view to profit + unconnected tenant), it severs the look-forward chain from 1 March 2027.
- **Best-case interpretation:** if the 1 March 2027 letting is unequivocally qualifying, only the 4 weeks of daughter-occupation + the vacant period from late October 2026 to 28 February 2027 is unrelievable (= £3,910 ATED). Relief is restored from 1 March 2027 + 2027/28 + 2028/29 + 2029/30 are unaffected.
- **Worst-case interpretation:** if the 1 March 2027 letting is NOT qualifying (e.g. let to another relative, or a connected company), the look-forward extension applies. 2027/28 + 2028/29 + 2029/30 ATED of £9,450 per year (subject to indexation) = c. £28,350 of ATED would become payable across the 3 subsequent years. The 4-week daughter visit potentially cost c. £32,000+ in lost relief.
- **Operational lesson:** the period between commercial tenancies is the most-dangerous window for connected-person occupation. Even a brief visit can poison up to 3 subsequent chargeable years if no qualifying-use day reliably intervenes.

### Example 3 — Look-back mechanic walked

Ironwood Cayman Holdings Limited (Cayman company holding a £4.5m London apartment; let to unconnected commercial tenants throughout 2022/23, 2023/24, 2024/25; s.133 relief claimed and approved on each ATED return). January 2026: the company's UBO (Mr Ironwood, ultimate beneficial owner via a Cayman trust where he is the settlor) visits London for 6 weeks while attending business meetings + uses the apartment for the duration. Mr Ironwood is a Category (d) settlor under s.136.

- **The clawback trigger:** Mr Ironwood is permitted to occupy the dwelling on each day of his 6-week stay in January-February 2026 (in the 2025/26 chargeable period). Each day is non-qualifying-occupation day per s.135.
- **Look-back mechanic:** s.135 unwinds earlier days "where a relevant person is entitled to the single-dwelling interest on that day," unless a s.133(1)(a) qualifying-use day intervenes between the earlier day and the non-qualifying-occupation day. The "relevant person" is the company (entitled on Mr Ironwood's occupation day).
- **Earlier-day analysis:** the company has been entitled throughout 2022/23 + 2023/24 + 2024/25 + 2025/26. Commercial letting throughout those years should be qualifying-use days per s.133(1)(a) — IF the letting genuinely meets the qualifying-rental-business test. If yes, the commercial-letting days INTERVENE between every earlier day and the January 2026 Mr Ironwood occupation day → look-back is severed; no earlier-day relief is lost.
- **The trap:** if any of the earlier "commercial letting" days were NOT genuinely qualifying (e.g. occasional short-let to a connected party; sham letting; gap periods between tenants), the intervening days do not sever the look-back chain. Any earlier non-qualifying or vacant days are unrelievable retrospectively + the company owes ATED for those days.
- **Practical impact:** if the company had a 3-month vacant period in 2024/25 between tenants (where no qualifying letting was in place), those 3 months become non-relievable in retrospect. ATED at 2024/25 figures (£32,200 annual, £2m-£5m band) × (90/365) = c. £7,940 of retrospectively-payable ATED for 2024/25. Plus Sch 24 inaccuracy on the 2024/25 return (which claimed full relief) — careless behaviour at unprompted-disclosure-floor 0% if voluntarily disclosed; prompted 15% on enquiry; deliberate-not-concealed up to 70% (or 105% Category 3 Cayman uplift).
- **s.163 ACA return obligation:** the company must file an adjusted chargeable amount return for 2025/26 (where the relief was lost mid-period) AND consider re-opening 2024/25 if material days are unwound.
- **Operational lesson:** continuous commercial letting WITH no vacant gaps is the only fully-protective pattern. Even a brief vacant period exposes the look-back chain.

### Example 4 — Mitigation pattern: the genuine qualifying-use day interposition

Sycamore Holdings Limited (UK ltd-co holding a £1.8m London BTL; let to unconnected commercial tenants throughout). A director's nephew (Category (e) relative of a connected person) is between rented flats in March 2026 + wants to stay in the BTL for 4 weeks. Sycamore's director seeks tax advice on whether the s.135 clawback can be mitigated.

**Specialist advice and operational plan:**

1. Document the nephew's existing commercial tenancy ending date precisely (e.g. 28 February 2026).
2. Enter into a genuine arm's-length commercial AST with the nephew at full market rent (verified comparable evidence; standard AST terms; rent paid into a Sycamore-controlled account; deposit lodged; agent involvement if normally used).
3. Run the nephew's letting for 4 weeks at market terms.
4. **Critical interpretive question:** is letting to the nephew a "qualifying-rental-business" day under s.133(1)(a)? The s.133 qualifying-rental-business test requires commercial basis + view to profit — the market rent + arm's-length AST may satisfy this. BUT the s.135 clawback ALSO requires that the occupant NOT be a non-qualifying individual under s.136. The nephew IS a Category (e) non-qualifying individual. Per s.133's own language, relief is DENIED if a non-qualifying individual is permitted to occupy — the s.136 catalogue test BITES on the day of nephew-occupation regardless of market rent.
5. **Conclusion:** market-rent letting to the nephew does NOT cure the s.135 trigger. The nephew is non-qualifying under s.136; days of his occupation are non-qualifying days; s.135 look-forward extension applies. Market rent is necessary but NOT sufficient (see B12 for the related-persons-market-rent deep-dive).
6. **The genuine mitigation pattern:** the only way to interpose a qualifying-use day is to let to a NON-CATEGORY-(s.136) tenant on commercial terms — a contractor, a colleague, a third party. The nephew cannot be the interpositioning tenant.
7. **Pragmatic alternative:** if the nephew genuinely needs accommodation, Sycamore's director should consider arranging a third-party rental (paid for personally or through other family resources) rather than using the BTL. The £4,600-£32,200 annual ATED + the 3-period look-forward exposure outweigh the short-term accommodation saving.

### Example 5 — CIHC interaction (CTA 2010 s.18N(3))

Pomegranate Holdings Limited (UK ltd-co; sole shareholder + director Ms Pomegranate; holds a £950k London flat let commercially to Ms Pomegranate's adult son Jacob on a formal AST at full market rent £42k/year; ATED s.133 relief claimed historically).

- **ATED s.135 + s.136 analysis:** Jacob is Ms Pomegranate's son → Category (e) relative of a connected person → non-qualifying individual under s.136. Each day of Jacob's occupation is non-qualifying-occupation day. Look-forward 3-period extension applies. **ATED relief LOST throughout Jacob's tenancy + look-forward 3 chargeable periods.**
- **Parallel CIHC analysis at CTA 2010 s.18N(3):** the s.18N(3) catalogue is distinct: it lists connected persons + their spouses + relatives of connected persons + relatives' spouses (per HP §21.5). Jacob is a relative of Ms Pomegranate (a connected person of the company via her director / shareholder relationship) → s.18N(3) catches him → company's qualifying-purpose carve-out from CIHC treatment is DEFEATED → company is a CIHC → **25% main rate CT on rental profits and investment income; no SPR; no marginal relief.**
- **Combined exposure:**
  - **ATED:** £4,600/year band 1 (£500k-£1m, £950k flat) × 4 years (current + 3-period look-forward) = £18,400 ATED that would otherwise have been £0 with relief.
  - **CT:** £42,000 annual rental × 25% (vs 19% SPR otherwise) = £10,500 vs £7,980 = £2,520 additional annual CT × 4 years = £10,080 incremental CT.
  - **Total 4-year incremental tax exposure:** c. £28,480 for letting to Jacob at market rent.
- **The conflation trap:** sessions writing "s.18N(3) and s.136 are the same test" are wrong. Both bite together for connected-person occupancy, BUT they have distinct statutory anchors, distinct catalogues (similar but not identical), and distinct consequences (CT vs ATED). Cite both at the right level.
- **Operational lesson:** family-occupant arrangements in enveloped property are operationally the worst case for ATED + CT. Strongly consider de-enveloping (see B5 for the strategic decision) where family use is intended long-term.

## FAQ expansion (RUN session polishes prose; 12-14 FAQs target)

1. **Q: What triggers ATED relief clawback under FA 2013 s.135?**
   A: A single day of non-qualifying occupation. Per FA 2013 s.135, if a "non-qualifying individual" (catalogued at s.136) is permitted to occupy the dwelling on any day, that day is not relievable AND look-forward / look-back extensions apply. Look-forward: no subsequent day in that chargeable period or in any of the subsequent 3 chargeable periods is relievable unless a qualifying-use day under s.133(1)(a) intervenes. Look-back: earlier days where a "relevant person" was entitled are not relievable unless a qualifying-use day intervenes between that earlier day and the non-qualifying-occupation day.

2. **Q: Who is a "non-qualifying individual" under s.136?**
   A: An eight-category catalogue: (a) the individual entitled to the interest (other than as partnership member); (b) persons connected with (a); (c) partnership members entitled as partnership members; (d) settlors of any trust where the trustees hold the interest; (e) relatives of category (a) or (d) individuals; (f) spouses or civil partners of those relatives; (g) participants in collective investment schemes holding the interest; (h) persons connected with category (g) participants. Categories (e) and (f) widely under-estimated — multi-hop family (the spouse of your settlor's brother, for example) IS caught.

3. **Q: My BTL ltd co's director's adult daughter wants to stay in the property between tenants for 4 weeks — what's the exposure?**
   A: Significant. The daughter is a Category (e) relative of a connected person under s.136 → each day of her occupation is non-qualifying. Per s.135 look-forward: those 4 weeks AND every subsequent day in that chargeable period are not relievable UNTIL a qualifying-use day under s.133(1)(a) intervenes. If the dwelling is vacant for some weeks after her stay, that vacant period is also not relievable. If no qualifying use intervenes in subsequent chargeable periods, the look-forward extension means up to 3 additional chargeable years of lost relief. For a £2.4m dwelling (£32,200 annual ATED 2026/27) the worst-case exposure is c. £128,800 ATED across 4 chargeable years — a price tag the 4-week visit was not worth.

4. **Q: I let the dwelling at market rent to my adult son on a formal AST — does that count as "commercial letting" and avoid the clawback?**
   A: NO. Per FA 2013 s.133 + s.136, relief is denied if a non-qualifying individual is permitted to occupy on that day, REGARDLESS of rent paid. Your son is a Category (e) relative → non-qualifying individual. Market rent + formal AST does NOT cure the s.135 trigger. Market rent is necessary but not sufficient when the occupant is within s.136. The CIHC carve-out at CTA 2010 s.18N(3) is separately defeated (see Example 5). See B12 for the deeper market-rent / related-persons coexistence analysis.

5. **Q: What is a "qualifying-use day" that intervenes to sever the look-forward / look-back chain?**
   A: Per FA 2013 s.133(1)(a), a day on which the dwelling is being exploited as a source of rents (other than excluded rents) in the course of a qualifying property rental business, i.e. one run on a commercial basis and with a view to profit. The intervening day must itself be qualifying. A VACANT day does NOT intervene. A day of non-qualifying-individual occupation does NOT intervene. Only genuine commercial letting to an unconnected tenant interposes.

6. **Q: How far back can the s.135 look-back rule unwind prior relief?**
   A: No statutory time limit. Look-back operates by "earlier day where a relevant person is entitled to the single-dwelling interest on that day," all the way back to the relevant person's acquisition of the interest. The only break is an intervening qualifying-use day. Practical impact: any vacant period that wasn't separated from a later non-qualifying-occupation day by a qualifying-use day becomes retrospectively non-relievable. Sch 24 inaccuracy bites on the earlier returns that claimed full relief.

7. **Q: I'm a settlor of a Cayman trust whose corporate trustee holds a London apartment — am I really a non-qualifying individual if I visit?**
   A: YES. Per FA 2013 s.136 Category (d), settlors of any trust where the trustees hold the interest are within the non-qualifying-individual catalogue. Your visits + any occupation trigger s.135. The trust structure does NOT insulate. Common trap for offshore-trust corporate-trustee structures. Specialist private-client advice essential before any settlor-side use.

8. **Q: What is the s.163 adjusted chargeable amount return?**
   A: A separate ATED return required where the chargeable amount changes mid-period because relief is gained or lost. Per FA 2013 s.163, the ACA return must be filed within 30 days of the change. Late filing triggers FA 2009 Sch 55 items 11B (ATED ACA return item) and the £100/£200/£300 escalator independently of the annual s.159 return. Where clawback under s.135 occurs mid-period, the ACA return is the operative compliance mechanism.

9. **Q: Does the clawback rule apply to the developer-exchange relief at s.139?**
   A: YES. Per FA 2013 s.139(2), "A day is not relievable by virtue of this section if on that day a non-qualifying individual is permitted to occupy the dwelling." The same s.136 catalogue + s.135 look-forward / look-back architecture applies. Developer-chain structures have the same exposure.

10. **Q: How does the CIHC test at CTA 2010 s.18N(3) interact with s.135 clawback?**
    A: Parallel but separate. Per HP §21.5, the s.18N(3) connected-occupant exclusion list catches connected persons + their spouses + relatives of connected persons + their spouses, defeating the company's qualifying-purpose carve-out from CIHC → 25% main CT rate, no SPR. The s.136 catalogue is separately defined and catches a similar-but-not-identical population. Where a connected-person occupies a UK ltd-co's enveloped dwelling, BOTH bite together (ATED relief lost + 25% CT main rate locked in). Distinct statutory anchors; cite both.

11. **Q: I disclosed a brief connected-occupation event voluntarily — what are the penalty consequences?**
    A: Sch 55 + Sch 56 + Sch 24 in parallel. Late s.163 ACA return generates Sch 55 escalator (£100/£200/£300 base + tax-geared paras 5-6 / 6A uplift). Sch 24 inaccuracy on the original s.159 annual return where it claimed full relief — careless 0% unprompted (within 12 months) / 15% prompted floor; deliberate-not-concealed 20%/35% floors. Offshore Category 2 / 3 uplift for overseas-corporate structures. Disclosure mitigation under Sch 24 para 10 + Sch 55 paras 17-18: unprompted disclosure within 12 months produces the lowest floor. Cross-link to B8 + B9 for the full penalty + appeal architecture.

12. **Q: Can I rely on documentation showing my family member just visited without "occupying"?**
    A: Contemporaneous documentation is helpful BUT the s.135 + s.136 statutory test is whether the individual was "permitted to occupy". This is a fact-and-degree question: overnight stays, possession of keys, ability to come and go without supervision, exclusive use of bedroom etc all point toward occupation; brief day-visits without overnight stay or possession typically do not. The evidential standard is HMRC-tight in clawback cases. Practical guidance: NO overnight stays by s.136-catalogue individuals where relief is being claimed; document visits precisely (date, duration, purpose, no overnight, no key issued); if there is any doubt, prepare the s.163 ACA return as a precaution.

13. **Q: My company has had a vacant period between tenants — does that itself trigger clawback?**
    A: NOT automatically. Vacant days are NOT non-qualifying-occupation days per s.135 (no individual is "permitted to occupy"). BUT vacant days are also NOT qualifying-use days under s.133(1)(a) (no commercial letting). The risk is the COMBINATION: if a later non-qualifying-occupation event occurs, the look-back rule unwinds earlier-claimed relief UNLESS qualifying-use days intervene — and a vacant period does not intervene. Continuous commercial letting with no vacant gaps is the only fully-protective pattern.

14. **Q: What is the single biggest pre-occupation check before allowing any connected-person use?**
    A: Take specialist tax advice on whether the proposed occupant falls within ANY of the eight s.136 categories. If yes, then EITHER (a) decline the occupation, OR (b) accept the clawback and prepare the s.163 ACA return for the affected period + plan to interpose a genuine qualifying-use day to limit look-forward exposure, OR (c) restructure (e.g. de-envelope — see B5). Specialist advice essential — the cost of an analysis is trivial compared to the multi-year ATED exposure from a mishandled connected-occupation event.
