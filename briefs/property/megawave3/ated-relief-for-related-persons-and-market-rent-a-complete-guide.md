# ATED relief for related persons and market rent: when does paying market rent unlock the rental relief and when does s.136 block it regardless

## Statutory anchor
- **Primary (the relief whose conditions related-persons + market-rent must satisfy):**
  - **FA 2013 s.133** — "Property rental businesses" (verified verbatim at legislation.gov.uk 2026-05-27). A day qualifies for relief if the single-dwelling interest is "being exploited as a source of rents or other receipts (other than excluded rents) in the course of a qualifying property rental business." A "qualifying property rental business" is one "run on a commercial basis and with a view to profit." Relief is **DENIED** if "a non-qualifying individual is permitted to occupy the dwelling" on that day.
  - The s.133 architecture has **two independent conditions** that BOTH must be satisfied for relief on a given day:
    1. The "commercial basis and with a view to profit" test, this is the market-rent gateway.
    2. The "no non-qualifying individual permitted to occupy" test, this is the s.136 catalogue gateway.
- **Primary (definition of non-qualifying individual, the operative catalogue):**
  - **FA 2013 s.136** — "Meaning of 'non-qualifying individual'" (verified at legislation.gov.uk 2026-05-27). Eight-category catalogue covering: (a) individuals entitled to the interest (otherwise than as partnership member); connected persons; partnership members; settlors of trusts holding the interest; relatives of connected persons / settlors; spouses / civil partners of relatives; participants in collective investment schemes; persons connected to those participants. The catalogue extends to **multiple-hop family relationships**, spouse of a relative of a connected person is caught.
- **Primary (clawback if s.136 occupation supervenes):**
  - **FA 2013 s.135** — "Non-qualifying occupation: look-forward and look-back" (verified verbatim at legislation.gov.uk 2026-05-27). Look-forward up to 3 subsequent chargeable periods; look-back to earlier days held by the same "relevant person" unless a s.133(1)(a) qualifying-use day intervenes. Cross-reference B11 for the clawback mechanic deep-dive.
- **Primary (the genuine market-rent + related-person reliefs):**
  - **FA 2013 s.145** — "Provision of dwelling to certain employees etc." (employee accommodation relief). Qualifying employee must be provided the dwelling "for performance of duties" + tight definition of qualifying employee (s.146 + s.147 controlling-interest carve-outs).
  - **FA 2013 s.138** — Property developer trade relief (dwelling held as trading stock by a property developer for at least 2 years).
  - **FA 2013 s.139** — Property-developer exchange-of-dwellings variant (sub-section (2): same non-qualifying-individual exclusion as s.133).
  - **FA 2013 s.141** — Property trader relief (held as trading stock by a property trader).
  - **FA 2013 s.137** — Dwellings open to the public (commercially run + open to public for at least 28 days per year).
  - **FA 2013 s.149** — Social housing relief (registered provider).
  - **FA 2013 s.150** — Charitable use relief.
  - **FA 2013 ss.134-135 (subset)** — demolition / conversion intention.
- **Primary (the CIHC parallel test at company level):**
  - **CTA 2010 s.18N(3)** — close investment-holding company test. The CIHC qualifying-purpose carve-out fails for connected occupancy at the company-tax level (separate from but overlapping with ATED s.136). HP §21.5 carries the full CIHC connected-occupant catalogue: connected person + spouse + relative + relative's spouse. Sessions writing related-persons pages must address both the ATED s.136 effect AND the CT s.18N(3) effect, they are independent and both bite.
- **Primary (market rent + connected party, wider tax architecture):**
  - **ITTOIA 2005 / CTA 2009 Part 4** — UK property income charge regardless of connected-party status; income tax / corporation tax applies on the rental income at the prevailing rate (with §24 finance-cost restriction for individuals, full CT rate for companies).
  - **TCGA 1992 s.18** — connected-party CGT mark-to-market on disposal (relevant for eventual de-enveloping or sale to family).
  - **CTA 2010 s.444** — connected-party transactions for CT.
- **House position reference:** §2 (ATED bands 2026/27, verified gov.uk 2026-05-22), **§18 (Wave 3 ATED extension full)**, §18.3 (relief catalogue, full table of s.133 / s.137 / s.138 / s.141 / s.144 / s.145 / s.149 / s.150 / ss.134-135), §18.4 (return mechanics + relief clawback architectural note), §18.8 (do-not-write list including "ATED reliefs are automatic where the dwelling is let" anti-pattern), §21.A (CT three-figure framework, CIHC), §21.5 (CIHC depth, qualifying-purpose carve-out + connected-occupant catalogue).

## Framing differentiator (anti-templating, anti-cannibalisation)
This page is the **related-persons-and-market-rent decision page**, the page that answers the question "I want to let our company-owned £1.5m flat to my daughter; if I charge market rent, can I claim the s.133 relief?" The popular answer (yes-with-documentation) is **wrong**. The correct answer is **almost always no** because the s.136 catalogue blocks regardless of rent level.

The framing differentiator is the **structural-correctness refusal of the market-rent myth**:

1. **Market rent is necessary but NOT sufficient.** s.133 has two independent conditions: (a) commercial basis + view to profit (market-rent gateway), AND (b) no non-qualifying individual occupation (s.136 catalogue gateway). Both must hold. A market-rent let to a director's daughter satisfies (a) but fails (b), relief denied; the £4,600-£303,450 ATED charge applies in full; s.135 look-forward + look-back bites surrounding periods.
2. **The s.136 catalogue is wider than owners assume.** Eight categories including multi-hop family (spouse of a relative of a connected person), partnership members, settlors, CIS participants. Sessions writing related-persons copy must walk the catalogue explicitly, the popular "relative" mental shorthand misses the half of the catalogue (settlor / partnership / CIS).
3. **The genuine market-rent + related-person reliefs are narrow.** s.145 employee accommodation (with the controlling-interest carve-outs at s.146 + s.147 that exclude many family-employee patterns). s.138 / s.139 property-developer trade (transient, narrow). The relief catalogue does NOT contain a "let to family at market rent" door.
4. **The CIHC parallel hit at company level.** Even if (per impossibilam) the ATED relief survived, the company-level CT consequence kicks in via s.18N(3): connected occupancy defeats the qualifying-purpose carve-out, the company becomes a CIHC, and the 25% CT main rate applies regardless of profit level. Sessions must walk both effects.
5. **The honest decision-page conclusion.** For a company / partnership / CIS owning a £500k+ residential dwelling and wishing to provide it to family, the answer is usually: (a) de-envelope to personal name, OR (b) accept the full ATED charge and CIHC-rate CT, with both quantified. The "let at market rent and claim the relief" path almost never works for connected occupancy.
6. **Where market rent DOES matter.** For arm's-length lets to **unconnected** tenants who happen to be friends or distant acquaintances, sessions must address the "where does the s.136 catalogue stop" question. Acquaintances who are NOT connected persons / relatives / settlors / partnership members / CIS participants ARE outside the catalogue; market-rent commercial-terms lets to them DO qualify for s.133 relief.
7. **The s.135 clawback overlay.** Even one day of non-qualifying occupation triggers look-forward up to 3 chargeable periods + look-back unwinding earlier periods. Sessions writing related-persons copy must cross-link to B11 for the clawback mechanic.

## Key questions this page must answer
1. **Does paying market rent to a related person unlock the s.133 rental relief?** (Almost never. s.133 has two independent conditions: market-rent (commercial basis + view to profit) AND no non-qualifying-individual occupation per s.136. Market rent satisfies the first; the s.136 catalogue blocks the second for connected occupancy regardless of rent level.)
2. **Who is a "non-qualifying individual" under s.136?** (Eight-category catalogue per HP §18.3 + the s.136 verbatim text: (a) individuals entitled to the interest, connected persons, partnership members, settlors, relatives of connected persons / settlors, spouses of relatives, CIS participants, persons connected to CIS participants. Stage 2 verbatim-verifies each category against legislation.gov.uk.)
3. **What does "connected person" mean operationally for ATED?** (CTA 2010 s.1122 + s.1123 connection rules, the standard cross-Act connection catalogue. Director + their spouse + their relatives + the spouses of those relatives + companies controlled by them + trusts they settled. Cross-applied through s.136 to ATED.)
4. **Are there ANY market-rent + related-person ATED reliefs?** (Narrow: s.145 employee accommodation (with s.146 + s.147 controlling-interest carve-outs that exclude controlling shareholders, their spouses, and connected persons, so a typical family-employee pattern fails). s.138 / s.139 property-developer trade (dwelling held as trading stock; transient). s.137 dwellings open to the public (commercial entertainment use, not residential family). No relief covers a vanilla "let at market rent to family member" structure.)
5. **What about the s.145 employee accommodation door — does it work for a family employee?** (Tight definition: qualifying employee must be provided the dwelling "for performance of duties"; s.146 + s.147 controlling-interest carve-outs exclude controlling shareholders + their spouses + their connected persons + employees with material interest in the employer. A director's spouse / child employed as company secretary typically does NOT qualify under s.145 because of the controlling-interest carve-out.)
6. **The CIHC parallel hit at company level (CTA 2010 s.18N(3)).** (Connected occupancy defeats the qualifying-purpose carve-out from CIHC treatment. CIHC = 25% main rate, no small-profits relief regardless of profit level. The s.18N(3) catalogue is parallel to but NOT identical to s.136; sessions must address both. HP §21.5 carries the s.18N(3) connected-occupant detail.)
7. **The s.135 clawback overlay.** (Cross-link to B11. A single day of s.136 occupation triggers look-forward up to 3 chargeable periods + look-back. Owners considering brief family visits must understand the period-extension architecture.)
8. **What documentation is required to support a genuine arm's-length commercial let to an UNCONNECTED person?** (Tenancy agreement at market rent; AST (or RRA equivalent post-commencement); contemporaneous market evidence for rent (comparable lettings); tenant outside the s.136 catalogue, no connected-person status, no settlor / partnership / CIS link. The let must run on a commercial basis with a view to profit, short voids do not invalidate.)
9. **The de-envelope alternative for family-use intentions.** (Where the owner's underlying intention is family occupation, the structurally honest answer is usually de-envelope: distribute the dwelling in specie or sell to the family member at market value. SDLT-on-distribution + CGT-on-deemed-disposal mechanics apply; the personal-name post-de-envelope tax landscape includes §24 finance-cost restriction. Stage 2 cross-links to existing incorporation + de-envelope pillars.)
10. **The accept-the-charge alternative.** (Where keeping the dwelling enveloped is strategically right (succession, anonymity, multi-property structure), the owner accepts the full ATED charge + CIHC-rate CT for periods of connected occupancy. £1.5m dwelling at 2026/27 £9,450 ATED + 25% CT on rental income.)
11. **Penalty exposure if connected occupancy is unreported.** (Sch 24 inaccuracy on the original-period return for unreported non-qualifying occupation; Sch 55 escalator on omitted s.163 ACA returns; offshore Category 2 / 3 uplift for overseas-corporate filers. Cross-link B8 + B9.)
12. **Pre-decision discipline for owners.** Before allowing any connected-person occupancy: (a) walk the s.136 catalogue with the prospective occupant; (b) walk the s.18N(3) parallel catalogue at company-tax level; (c) take advice on the s.135 look-forward / look-back exposure; (d) decide between de-envelope, accept-the-charge, or rebuild around s.145 employee structure with controlling-interest analysis.

### Example 1 — The two-conditions decision tree (the page's first concrete artefact)

Render as semantic HTML `<ol>`. This is the s.133 architecture that resolves the market-rent myth:

1. **Identify the proposed occupant.** Director's daughter / son? Spouse of a partner in the partnership? Settlor of the trust holding the interest? Spouse of your sibling? CIS participant in the fund? Run through each of the s.136 eight categories with the prospective occupant.
2. **Apply the s.136 catalogue gateway (Condition (b) of s.133).** If the occupant is within ANY of the 8 categories, relief is DENIED on that day regardless of rent. Stop here. Market rent does NOT cure the s.136 trigger.
3. **If outside the s.136 catalogue:** apply the commercial-basis-and-view-to-profit gateway (Condition (a) of s.133). Test: is the rent at market level (comparable evidence); is the tenancy on standard AST terms (or RRA equivalent post-commencement); is the let intended to produce a profit (after costs); is the let documented contemporaneously? Mark each as PASS / FAIL.
4. **If both conditions hold:** s.133 relief is available. File the ATED return as a claim-only return; document the s.133 claim. ATED tax = £0.
5. **If only Condition (a) holds (commercial-basis OK but occupant in s.136):** relief is NOT available. Full ATED charge applies. PLUS:
   - **s.135 clawback overlay:** look-forward up to 3 subsequent chargeable periods + look-back to earlier days (cross-link B11).
   - **CIHC effect (CTA 2010 s.18N(3)):** company becomes a CIHC; 25% main CT rate locked in on all rental + investment profits.
6. **The honest restructure question.** Given the combined ATED + CIHC + s.135 exposure for connected occupancy, the structurally-rational options are typically: (a) de-envelope the dwelling (sell to family member at market value OR distribute in specie — see B5 for the strategic decision), OR (b) restructure via genuine s.145 employee accommodation IF the controlling-interest carve-outs at s.146 + s.147 are cleared.

### Example 2 — The s.136 catalogue applied to a typical family-occupant scenario

Honeysuckle Holdings Limited (UK ltd-co; sole shareholder + director Mrs Honeysuckle; holds a £1.4m London flat acquired 2019 via the ltd-co for "future family use"). Mrs Honeysuckle's nephew Tom (her sister's son) is starting a London internship + wants to rent the flat. Mrs Honeysuckle is willing to charge full market rent of £42k/year + put in place a formal AST. Question: does s.133 relief apply?

**s.136 catalogue walk:**

- **Category (a) — individual entitled to the interest:** Mrs Honeysuckle is connected (director + sole shareholder) but Tom is not himself entitled — pass on (a) for Tom.
- **Category (b) — connected persons:** is Tom connected with Mrs Honeysuckle for s.136 purposes? Per CTA 2010 s.1122, "connected" is widely defined; immediate family + spouses. Tom is Mrs Honeysuckle's NEPHEW (i.e. her sister's son). A nephew is technically NOT in the basic CTA 2010 s.1122 list of relatives (sibling, parent, grandparent, child, grandchild) but s.1123 extends connection in some contexts. Verify exact statutory scope at write — Stage 2 must check whether nephew falls within s.1122 "relative" definition.
- **Category (e) — relatives of category (a) or (d) individuals:** Mrs Honeysuckle is a connected person of the company per Category (b). Her relatives are caught under Category (e). Per ITA 2007 s.994, "relative" means "brother, sister, ancestor or lineal descendant". Tom is Mrs Honeysuckle's sister's son — i.e. a NIECE / NEPHEW relationship, NOT a direct lineal descendant. Whether nephew counts as "relative" under ITA 2007 s.994 is contested historically — older HMRC interpretation excluded nieces / nephews but updated guidance may differ. **Stage 2 must verify the operative HMRC position at write per §16.35.**
- **Category (f) — spouses or civil partners of relatives:** if Tom IS a "relative" per Category (e), his spouse is also caught. If not, Category (f) doesn't bite.

**Result:** the s.136 status of nieces / nephews is the contested edge case. If Tom is caught (the conservative position; HMRC may take this view via Category (b) connected-persons widely construed), s.133 relief is denied + Honeysuckle owes £9,450 annual ATED + look-forward 3 periods if no qualifying-use day intervenes + CIHC at 25% CT on rental. If Tom is NOT caught (the narrower interpretation; nephew falls outside both s.1122 connected-persons and s.994 relatives), s.133 relief is potentially available IF Conditions (a) commercial-basis is met.

**Operational lesson:** even for an edge-case relationship (nephew), the analysis is non-trivial. Specialist tax advice essential BEFORE arranging the let. The conservative position protects against penalty exposure; the aggressive position requires defensible legal analysis + documentation.

### Example 3 — The s.145 employee accommodation door + controlling-interest carve-outs

Magnolia Holdings Limited (UK ltd-co; sole shareholder + director Mr Magnolia; holds a £1.1m London flat acquired 2018; intended as accommodation for a "company secretary"). Mr Magnolia's wife Mrs Magnolia is the company secretary, paid £8,000/year for company-secretarial duties. They wish to claim FA 2013 s.145 employee accommodation relief on the basis that Mrs Magnolia uses the flat to perform her duties (the company's registered office is at the flat).

**s.145 + s.146 + s.147 analysis:**

- **s.145(1):** relief is available where the dwelling is provided to a qualifying employee for the performance of duties. Mrs Magnolia performs company-secretarial duties from the flat. Surface compliance: PASSES.
- **s.146(1) controlling-interest carve-out:** s.145 relief is NOT available where the employee has a "material interest" in the company OR is otherwise connected with controlling shareholders. Mrs Magnolia is the SPOUSE of Mr Magnolia, who is the SOLE SHAREHOLDER + SOLE DIRECTOR. Under s.146(2), Mrs Magnolia is treated as connected with the controlling shareholder → her "material interest" is presumed via the spouse rule. **s.146 carve-out BITES → s.145 relief UNAVAILABLE.**
- **s.147 further provisions:** additional carve-outs for senior employees of substantial controlling shareholders. Not the operative bite here; s.146 already excludes.
- **Alternative — s.133 rental?** Mrs Magnolia is Mr Magnolia's spouse → connected person under CTA 2010 s.1122 (Category (b) of s.136) AND relative of a connected person under Category (e) → non-qualifying individual under s.136. s.133 relief DENIED regardless of market rent. CIHC effect at s.18N(3) — connected occupancy defeats the carve-out → 25% CT on rental.

**Result:** the "company secretary" structure fails BOTH s.145 (controlling-interest carve-out) AND s.133 (s.136 catalogue). Magnolia owes full ATED £9,450 + CIHC-rate 25% CT on the £8,000 "rental" or imputed-benefit value (which under ITTOIA 2005 / CTA 2009 Part 4 + benefits-in-kind code may be additionally charged). The "company secretary + family member + provide the flat" structure is a popular but operationally-broken pattern. Specialist tax advice should have surfaced this BEFORE the structure was put in place.

### Example 4 — The genuine arm's-length commercial let (outside the s.136 catalogue)

Walnut Holdings Limited (UK ltd-co; sole shareholder + director Mr Walnut; holds a £1.8m London BTL). Mr Walnut wishes to let the BTL to Susan Ferris (a consultant he met professionally at an industry conference; she is unconnected to him + his family + the company). Market rent £58k/year; standard 12-month AST; deposit lodged; agent handles the letting.

**s.136 catalogue walk:**

- **Category (a) — Susan is not entitled to the interest.** Pass.
- **Category (b) — Susan is not connected with Mr Walnut for CTA 2010 s.1122 purposes** (no family relationship; not in business together; she does not control or co-control any company with Mr Walnut). Pass.
- **Category (c) — Susan is not a partnership member entitled to the interest.** Pass.
- **Category (d) — Susan is not a settlor of any trust holding the interest.** Pass.
- **Categories (e) + (f) — Susan is not a relative or spouse of any Category (a) or (d) individual.** Pass.
- **Categories (g) + (h) — Susan is not a CIS participant or connected to one.** Pass.

**Susan is OUTSIDE the s.136 catalogue.** s.136 gateway PASSES.

**s.133 commercial-basis test (Condition (a)):**

- Market rent £58k/year (comparable to similar London BTL units at similar value): PASSES "commercial basis".
- Profit-oriented: gross rental £58k − running costs c. £18k = c. £40k operating margin → "with a view to profit" PASSES.
- Standard AST: PASSES tenancy formality.

**Both conditions PASS → s.133 relief AVAILABLE.** Walnut files claim-only ATED return; £0 ATED tax. Rental income taxed at company CT (SPR 19% if profits below £50k SPR limit per associated-company divisor; marginal-relief band for higher profits; 25% main rate above £250k upper). CIHC analysis: Susan is unconnected → s.18N(2)(b) carve-out PRESERVED → company NOT a CIHC → SPR / marginal-relief available.

**Result:** the genuine arm's-length commercial-let to an unconnected tenant produces the expected tax outcome: ATED £0 (via s.133 relief), CT at 19-25% (CIHC carve-out preserved), Sch 55 risk minimised (claim-only return filed timely). This is the structurally-correct ATED rental-relief use case. The contrast with Examples 2 + 3 illustrates why related-persons structures are operationally broken.

### Example 5 — De-envelope as the structurally-honest alternative for family use

Olive Property Holdings Limited (UK ltd-co; sole shareholder + director Mr Olive; bought a £1.6m Surrey home in 2018 via the ltd-co for family use; Mrs Olive + 2 children currently occupy + Mr Olive splits time between Surrey and a London rental flat). Question: keep enveloped or de-envelope?

- **Current position (enveloped):**
  - ATED: £9,450 annual (band 2 £1m-£2m) — no relief (family occupancy; s.136 + s.18N(3) both bite). Pre-FY-paid annually + Sch 55 escalator if missed.
  - CT: CIHC at 25% main rate on any company income (here: no rental income — family-occupied). NO SPR despite low profits.
  - ECCTA director / PSC verification + Companies House annual filings + corporate accounting fees: c. £1,800-£3,000/year.
  - Lost PRR on eventual sale: if the company sells, chargeable gain at 25% CT; vs personal-name Mr Olive's PRR under TCGA 1992 ss.222-226 would typically give £0 CGT.
  - **Annual carry: ~£11,500-£13,000/year + future-disposal CT exposure.**
- **De-envelope option:**
  - Distribute the £1.6m property in specie to Mr Olive (or sell to him at market value).
  - SDLT analysis: distribution-in-specie as return of capital may be SDLT-free if cleanly structured; debt assumption triggers SDLT (full residential rates 0/2/5/10/12 + 3% additional-dwelling surcharge from 30 October 2024 — verify current band at write). Specialist SDLT essential.
  - CT on company's deemed disposal at market value: chargeable gain at 25% CT (CIHC) — if cost £1.4m + value £1.6m, £200k gain × 25% = £50,000 CT.
  - IT on Mr Olive's side (dividend treatment): £1.6m − share capital c. £100 = c. £1.6m effective dividend at additional-rate 39.35% = c. £630,000 IT. Capital-distribution route via MVL: CGT at 18%/24% × £1.6m = up to £384,000 (less AEA £3,000).
  - **Total upfront de-envelope cost: c. £400-700k (depending on structuring + Mr Olive's marginal IT rate).**
- **Post-de-envelope position:**
  - Mr Olive holds the £1.6m home personally. PRR applies to his main-residence use; spouse-deemed-occupation under TCGA 1992 s.222 may extend.
  - No ATED. No CIHC. No ECCTA per-director compliance for an inactive ltd-co.
  - §24 finance-cost restriction only relevant if mortgaged + let (not relevant here for owner-occupier).
  - On future sale: PRR potentially eliminates CGT entirely.
- **Verdict:** depending on holding-horizon + appreciation profile + Mr Olive's marginal IT rate, de-envelope is typically cost-effective vs continuing enveloped. The c. £11-13k annual carry × 10+ years (£110-130k) plus the eventual CGT exposure on sale typically exceeds the upfront de-envelope cost. BUT the upfront cost is large + the structuring is specialist (SDLT + CT + IT + capital-vs-income routes). Engage specialist tax + legal team.

### Example 6 — Misframings the page must correct (verbatim do-not-write list)

- **Misframe 1:** "If you charge market rent to a family member, the s.133 ATED rental relief applies." FALSE per s.133 + s.136 — relief is DENIED if a non-qualifying individual is permitted to occupy regardless of rent. Market rent satisfies Condition (a) commercial-basis-with-view-to-profit; the s.136 catalogue gateway separately blocks Condition (b). Both must pass.
- **Misframe 2:** "Settlors of trusts are not in the s.136 catalogue." FALSE — Category (d) explicitly catches settlors of trusts where the trustees hold the interest. Common trap for Cayman-trust corporate-trustee structures.
- **Misframe 3:** "Partnership members are not non-qualifying individuals if the partnership pays market rent." FALSE — Category (c) catches partnership members entitled (as partnership members) to the interest. Both conditions of s.133 must independently hold; market rent doesn't cure Category (c).
- **Misframe 4:** "Section 145 employee accommodation relief covers a director's spouse who is the company secretary." FALSE per s.146 controlling-interest carve-out — spouse of controlling shareholder is excluded.
- **Misframe 5:** "The s.136 catalogue only covers immediate family." FALSE — Category (e) extends to relatives per ITA 2007 s.994 (parents, grandparents, children, grandchildren, siblings); Category (f) extends to SPOUSES of those relatives (multi-hop family). Category (d) catches settlors; (g)+(h) catch CIS-related persons.
- **Misframe 6:** "ATED s.136 and CTA 2010 s.18N(3) are the same test." FALSE — parallel-but-distinct tests with different statutory anchors and slightly different catalogues. Both bite together for connected occupancy.
- **Misframe 7:** "Connected-person treatment is removed if the family member pays full market rent through a third-party agent." FALSE — the s.136 catalogue test turns on the occupant's status (connected / relative / settlor / etc.), NOT on payment route. Routing rent through a third-party agent is operationally normal but does NOT change the s.136 analysis.
- **Misframe 8:** "Look-forward 3-period extension only applies if there's actual loss to HMRC." FALSE per s.135 — the 3-period extension operates mechanically when a non-qualifying-occupation day occurs (unless a qualifying-use day intervenes); HMRC loss is not the trigger.
- **Misframe 9:** "If you're caught in the s.136 catalogue, the only option is to pay the full ATED." FALSE — alternatives include de-envelope (sell or distribute to family at market value); restructure to s.145 employee structure if controlling-interest carve-outs can be navigated (rare); accept the charge as a strategic cost of keeping the structure (succession / anonymity reasons).
- **Misframe 10:** "ATED reliefs are automatic where the dwelling is let on a tenancy." FALSE per HP §18.8 anti-pattern — reliefs MUST be CLAIMED on a return; claim-only return is mandatory. Failing to file even where relief applies triggers Sch 55 escalator.
- **Misframe 11:** "Charging market rent + signing an AST cures the CIHC 25% rate." FALSE per CTA 2010 s.18N(3) — the connected-occupant exclusion is independent of rent / tenancy formality; CIHC bites when the occupant falls within the s.18N(3) list regardless of commercial terms.
- **Misframe 12:** "I can let to my nephew because nephew is not a 'relative' under s.994." MISLEADING (and risk-prone) — the s.994 definition limits "relative" to "brother, sister, ancestor or lineal descendant" so nephews / nieces are technically OUTSIDE the basic s.994 list. BUT (a) HMRC interpretation may use broader connection rules under CTA 2010 s.1122 + s.1123; (b) the practical risk of HMRC challenge + Sch 24 inaccuracy penalty if the position is wrong is high. Specialist tax advice essential; conservative position protective.
- **Misframe 13:** "The relief catalogue contains a 'family at market rent' door." FALSE — ss.137 / 138 / 139 / 141 / 145 / 149 / 150 / 134-135 / 144 each carry tight conditions; no relief is structured around "family member tenant at market rent" as the qualifying use.

## FAQ expansion (RUN session polishes prose; 12-14 FAQs target)

1. **Q: I want to let our company's £1.5m London flat to my daughter at full market rent on a formal AST. Can I claim the s.133 property rental business ATED relief?**
   A: ALMOST CERTAINLY NO. FA 2013 s.133 has TWO independent conditions: (a) commercial basis with a view to profit (the market-rent gateway); (b) no non-qualifying individual permitted to occupy (the s.136 catalogue gateway). Your daughter is a relative of a connected person under FA 2013 s.136 Category (e) → non-qualifying individual. Condition (b) FAILS. Relief is DENIED regardless of rent level. Full ATED charge (£9,450 for 2026/27 band 2) applies + CIHC at 25% CT on the rental income + s.135 look-forward up to 3 subsequent chargeable periods. Market rent is necessary but NOT sufficient.

2. **Q: Who is a "non-qualifying individual" under s.136?**
   A: Eight-category catalogue per FA 2013 s.136: (a) individuals entitled to the interest (other than as partnership member); (b) persons connected with (a); (c) partnership members entitled (as partnership members) to the interest; (d) settlors of any trust where the trustees hold the interest; (e) relatives of category (a) or (d) individuals; (f) spouses / civil partners of those relatives; (g) participants in collective investment schemes holding the interest; (h) persons connected with (g). Includes multi-hop family (spouse of your settlor's sibling) — wider than most owners realise.

3. **Q: I'm letting to my nephew — is he in the catalogue?**
   A: The technical answer depends on the operative HMRC interpretation. ITA 2007 s.994 defines "relative" as "brother, sister, ancestor or lineal descendant" — narrowly read, nephews / nieces are excluded. BUT CTA 2010 s.1122 + s.1123 connected-persons rules extend more widely in some contexts and HMRC may apply broader connection. The practical risk-balanced answer: take specialist tax advice BEFORE arranging the let; the conservative position is to treat nephew as caught (relief denied; ATED full); the aggressive position requires defensible legal opinion + contemporaneous documentation. The cost of an opinion is trivial compared to a multi-year ATED + Sch 24 exposure if the position is wrong.

4. **Q: Does the s.145 employee accommodation relief work for a spouse who is the company secretary?**
   A: ALMOST CERTAINLY NO. FA 2013 s.145 grants relief where a qualifying employee is provided the dwelling for performance of duties. BUT s.146 + s.147 controlling-interest carve-outs exclude employees with material interest + employees connected with controlling shareholders. Your spouse, the company secretary of a company in which you are the controlling shareholder, is excluded via the spouse-of-controlling-shareholder route per s.146. s.145 relief UNAVAILABLE. Plus the s.133 alternative is independently blocked by s.136 Category (e) (spouse is a relative of a connected person). The "spouse as company secretary + company provides the flat" structure is a popular but operationally-broken pattern.

5. **Q: What if I let through a third-party agent so the family member's rent goes through an intermediary?**
   A: Routing rent through a third-party agent is operationally normal but does NOT change the s.136 catalogue analysis. The s.136 test turns on the OCCUPANT's status (connected / relative / settlor / partnership member / etc.), not on the rent payment route. Market-rent-via-agent-arrangement to a family member fails Condition (b) of s.133 just as a direct payment arrangement does. Specialist tax advice essential.

6. **Q: Where is the genuine market-rent + related-person ATED relief door?**
   A: There isn't a vanilla "let to family at market rent" door in the relief catalogue. The narrow doors that involve any related-person component are: (a) s.145 employee accommodation (with the s.146 / s.147 controlling-interest carve-outs that exclude most family-employee patterns); (b) s.138 / s.139 property developer trade (transient — dwelling held as trading stock for at least 2 years); (c) s.137 dwellings open to the public (commercial-entertainment use, not residential family). For arm's-length lets to UNCONNECTED persons (no family / connection / settlor link), s.133 relief is available — see Example 4.

7. **Q: How does the CIHC parallel test at CTA 2010 s.18N(3) interact with ATED s.136?**
   A: They are parallel but distinct tests. The s.18N(3) connected-occupant exclusion list per HP §21.5 catches connected persons + their spouses + relatives of connected persons + relatives' spouses — defeating the company's qualifying-purpose carve-out from CIHC treatment (25% main CT rate locked in, no SPR). The s.136 ATED catalogue is separately defined (8 categories incl settlors + partnership members + CIS) and bites independently for ATED relief denial. Where a connected-person occupies a UK ltd-co's enveloped dwelling, BOTH bite together. Distinct statutory anchors; cite both separately.

8. **Q: If I'm denied s.133 relief, what's the actual cost?**
   A: For a £1.5m London flat let to a relative for the full 2026/27 chargeable period: (a) ATED £9,450 (band 2); (b) CIHC at 25% CT on rental income vs SPR 19% = c. £2,520/year additional CT on £42k rental; (c) s.135 look-forward 3 subsequent chargeable periods if no qualifying-use day intervenes = potentially £37,800 additional ATED across 4 years. Plus ECCTA + Companies House admin + corporate accounting overhead. Plus loss of PRR on eventual disposal vs personal-name. Aggregate 4-year incremental cost: c. £50-70k depending on specifics. Significantly more for larger dwellings.

9. **Q: What is the s.135 clawback overlay?**
   A: A single day of non-qualifying-individual occupation (per s.136) triggers FA 2013 s.135: (a) loss of relief for that day; (b) look-forward extension — no subsequent day in that chargeable period or in any of the subsequent 3 chargeable periods is relievable unless a s.133(1)(a) qualifying-use day intervenes; (c) look-back to earlier days where the same "relevant person" was entitled unless a qualifying-use day intervenes. A single weekend visit by a relative can poison up to 4 chargeable years of relief. Cross-link B11 for the clawback mechanic deep-dive.

10. **Q: Should I just de-envelope if I want family use?**
    A: Often YES. The combined ATED + CIHC + s.135 exposure for family occupancy in enveloped property typically exceeds the de-envelope transaction costs over a 5-10 year holding horizon. De-envelope options: (a) distribute in specie to the family member as return of capital (SDLT-neutral if cleanly structured + no debt assumption); (b) sell to family member at market value (SDLT charge at residential rates + 3% surcharge from 30 October 2024); (c) MVL solvent dissolution + capital distribution. Each route has different IT / CGT / SDLT consequences for the company + the recipient. Specialist tax + legal team essential. See B5 for the strategic-decision context.

11. **Q: What if I accept the charge — does anything mitigate the CIHC effect?**
    A: For a company already structurally a CIHC (connected occupancy throughout), the 25% main CT rate is locked in for all profits regardless of level. Mitigation options are limited: (a) keep rental + investment income low (reduces the absolute CT bill but doesn't change the rate); (b) restructure into multiple separate SPVs each with NON-connected occupancy (genuine arm's-length tenants; risks: dispersal of family use intent); (c) consider partial de-envelope of certain dwellings. The architectural reality is that family-occupied enveloped property is structurally tax-inefficient; mitigation only works at the margin.

12. **Q: Penalty exposure if I claim s.133 relief on a return where I actually had family occupation?**
    A: Sch 24 inaccuracy on the original return for the under-declared chargeable amount. Behaviour-tiered: careless 0-30% (with 0% unprompted < 12 months / 15% prompted floor); deliberate-not-concealed 20-70%; deliberate-concealed 30-100%. Offshore Category 2 / 3 uplift for overseas-corporate filers (up to 200%). Plus Sch 55 escalator for any omitted s.163 ACA returns. Plus interest under FA 2009 ss.101-102. Unprompted disclosure within 12 months produces the lowest mitigation floor — cross-link B8 + B9 for the full penalty architecture.

13. **Q: What documentation supports a defensible UNCONNECTED arm's-length let?**
    A: (i) Tenant credit-check + identity verification confirming no family / connection / partnership / CIS link; (ii) market-rent comparable evidence (3+ similar local lettings at similar value); (iii) standard AST (or RRA equivalent post-commencement) on commercial terms; (iv) rent paid into a Sycamore-controlled account (not waived / discounted off-record); (v) deposit lodged per landlord deposit-protection scheme; (vi) agent involvement if normally used (Rightmove / Zoopla listing evidence); (vii) contemporaneous business records evidencing commercial-basis-with-view-to-profit (running costs, target yield, void analysis). Audit-defensible package.

14. **Q: Where does the s.136 catalogue actually stop — what counts as "outside"?**
    A: Outside the catalogue means: NOT one of the 8 categories enumerated in s.136. Practically: arm's-length professional contacts; unconnected friends (in the s.1122 connected-persons sense — no business / family / control relationship); third-party tenants found via Rightmove / Zoopla / lettings agent (where no pre-existing s.1122 connection exists); corporate tenants whose directors / shareholders are unconnected. Where ANY ambiguity exists (long-standing friend who has done business with you; second cousin; relative-of-spouse-of-relative), specialist tax advice essential. The catalogue is sticky — multi-hop relationships catch more than owners assume.
