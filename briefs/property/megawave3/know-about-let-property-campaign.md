# Know About Let Property Campaign: The Landlord's Guide to HMRC's Voluntary Disclosure Route

## Statutory anchor

- **Primary — LPC as published HMRC campaign:** Let Property Campaign published guidance at **gov.uk/guidance/let-property-campaign** — HMRC campaign open since **9 September 2013**, no announced end date. The LPC is a **published administrative disclosure route**, not a standalone statutory regime; it operates within the existing Schedule 41 FA 2008 (failure-to-notify) penalty framework and Schedule 24 FA 2007 (inaccuracy) penalty framework, unlocking unprompted-disclosure mitigation floors for taxpayers who self-correct via the LPC channel rather than waiting for HMRC discovery.
- **Primary — underlying statutory framework:**
  - **Schedule 41 FA 2008** (failure-to-notify) — the operative penalty framework when a landlord has never registered for self-assessment for their rental income. Verified at `https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2008/9/schedule/41` per Wave 7 §27.3 lock 2026-05-24. Sch 41 para 13 imposes the disclosure mitigation: unprompted floor 0% (non-deliberate within 12 months) / 20% (deliberate) / 30% (deliberate-concealed); prompted floor 10% / 35% / 50%. Categories 2 and 3 offshore uplifts apply where rental property is overseas.
  - **Schedule 24 FA 2007** (inaccuracy) — the operative penalty framework when the landlord HAS filed self-assessment returns but those returns are inaccurate / under-stated. Standard maxima 30% / 70% / 100%; F-5 / Wave 7 correction logged 2026-05-24 — Sch 24 para 10 imposes NO 12-month qualifier on the careless-unprompted 0% floor (the 12-month qualifier is at Sch 41 para 13 only). Sessions writing must not collapse Sch 24 + Sch 41 disclosure floors.
  - **TMA 1970 section 7** — the chargeability-notification obligation that the LPC remediates. A taxpayer with new chargeability must notify HMRC within 6 months of the end of the tax year in which liability arose; failure triggers Sch 41 exposure. The LPC sits as the remediation route for that failure.
  - **TMA 1970 section 29** — HMRC's discovery power, with the time-limit framework at section 34 (4 years) / section 36(1) (careless 6 years) / section 36(1A) (deliberate 20 years) / section 36A (offshore innocent-error 12 years per FA 2019). §27.1 Wave 7 lock.
- **Primary — three-step LPC mechanic (HMRC published process):**
  - **Step 1 — Notify:** landlord notifies intent to disclose via the gov.uk LPC notification form. No penalty consequence at this stage. HMRC issues an acknowledgement and a Disclosure Reference Number (DRN).
  - **Step 2 — Disclose:** within **90 days** of HMRC's acknowledgement, the landlord submits a full disclosure — tax + interest + proposed penalty calculation, by tax year covered, with supporting evidence (rental income figures, allowable deductions, supporting documents for cost basis on any disposals during the disclosure period).
  - **Step 3 — Pay:** the full disclosed liability (tax + interest + penalty) is paid on or before submission of the disclosure. Time-to-pay arrangements may be agreed in advance with HMRC where the liability is material; this is the operational exception, not the rule.
- **Eligibility scope:** LPC is for **residential landlords** (UK + non-UK resident) with undisclosed rental income. Excludes: **companies** (LtdCo route differs — voluntary disclosure via DDS or CT-specific channels, not LPC; cross-link to A11 `how-making-tax-digital-affects-limited-companies` for the LtdCo angle); **trusts** (TRS route under §22.16 Wave 7 lock for trust-registration obligations; rental disclosure for trust property uses the SA900 amendment or DDS route); **non-residential property** (commercial / mixed-use disclosure uses the general DDS route, not LPC). House position §27.6 Wave 7 lock confirms the eligibility scope verbatim.
- **House position reference:** §27.6 (Voluntary disclosure routes — LPC + WDF + general "voluntary disclosure" — Wave 7 lock) primary; §27.3 (Sch 41 failure-to-notify) operative; §27.2 (Sch 24 inaccuracy) operative; §27.1 (discovery time limits) operative; §27.5 (CoP9 / CDF) for the criminal-prosecution boundary. No NEW HP LOCK NEEDED — the architecture is locked across §27.1-§27.6.

## Worked-example data (RUN session uses these as canvas)

### Example 1 — Accidental landlord, never registered, 3 years of undisclosed rental

- **Aldridge-Estate, the accidental landlord:** inherited a property in 2022 and let it out from January 2023. Did not register for self-assessment or declare rental income for 2022/23, 2023/24, or 2024/25. Gross rents £14,000 / £18,000 / £19,000 per year. No prior contact from HMRC.
- **Statutory analysis:** failure-to-notify under TMA 1970 s.7. Sch 41 FA 2008 applies. The taxpayer is "never-notified" (no SA return filed for the relevant years), so Sch 41 is the operative penalty regime — Sch 24 does not engage.
- **LPC route:** the landlord notifies HMRC via gov.uk LPC form (Step 1). Within 90 days of HMRC's acknowledgement and Disclosure Reference Number (DRN), the landlord submits the full disclosure with tax + interest + penalty calculation by tax year (Step 2). Pays on submission (Step 3).
- **Penalty exposure:** Sch 41 para 13 unprompted floor 0% (non-deliberate) WITH the 12-month qualifier — disclosure must come within 12 months of the tax becoming payable for the 0% floor. The 2022/23 tax was due 31 January 2024; the 12-month qualifier is exceeded for that year, so the unprompted floor moves to the next band (typically 10%). The 2023/24 and 2024/25 disclosures may still qualify for the 0% floor depending on the timing.
- **Operational point:** sessions writing must NOT collapse the Sch 41 12-month qualifier into the unprompted-disclosure narrative; some years may qualify and others may not. F-5 / Wave 7 lock differentiates Sch 41 (has 12-month qualifier) from Sch 24 (does not).

### Example 2 — Landlord with filed returns but under-declared rental income

- **Mawell-Estate, the landlord:** has filed self-assessment returns since 2018/19 but only declared 70% of rental income (under-declaring the income from one property in a 4-property portfolio). 2024/25 review identifies the historic understatement.
- **Statutory analysis:** the landlord HAS filed returns, so Sch 24 FA 2007 (inaccuracy penalties) is the operative regime — NOT Sch 41. The understatement is "deliberate" if the omission was knowing; "careless" if it was sloppy bookkeeping.
- **LPC route:** the LPC route is available for inaccurate-return cases just as it is for never-notified cases. The Sch 24 mitigation framework engages instead of Sch 41.
- **Penalty exposure:** Sch 24 para 10 mitigation — careless-unprompted 0% floor (NO 12-month qualifier per F-5 / Wave 7 lock); careless-prompted floor 15%; deliberate-unprompted floor 20%; deliberate-prompted floor 35%; deliberate-concealed floor 30% or 50% depending on prompted/unprompted. The absence of the 12-month qualifier on the Sch 24 careless-unprompted 0% floor is the load-bearing differentiation from Sch 41.
- **Operational point:** the LPC orientation page must accurately distinguish Sch 41 (never-notified, with 12-month qualifier on 0% floor) from Sch 24 (inaccurate-return, no 12-month qualifier). Sessions writing must NOT import the Sch 41 12-month qualifier into Sch 24 commentary.

### Example 3 — Non-resident landlord with UK rental, 6-year disclosure window

- **Singh-Estate, the non-resident landlord:** UK-tax-resident in Spain, owns a UK BTL property let since 2019. Rents received gross (no NRL-scheme withholding). Never registered for UK self-assessment. The UK rental income should have been declared on a non-resident landlord SA return.
- **Statutory analysis:** failure to notify under TMA 1970 s.7 + s.8 (non-resident landlord variant). Sch 41 FA 2008 applies. The overseas element brings Category 2 / Category 3 territory uplifts under Sch 41 para 6A — Cat 2 = 1.5x, Cat 3 = 2x — but the LANDLORD's residence (Spain, Cat 1) does not engage the offshore uplift; the offshore uplift applies to assets / income located in non-UK territories. UK-source rental income from a UK property remains Cat 1 regardless of landlord residence.
- **LPC route:** the LPC route accommodates non-resident landlords with UK rental income. The WDF (Worldwide Disclosure Facility) is the alternative route where the rental income is OFFSHORE rather than the landlord. Here, the rental is UK-source so LPC is correct.
- **Operational point:** sessions writing must NOT route a non-resident landlord with UK-source rental income to WDF. WDF is for offshore-source income or asset disclosures; UK BTL rental disclosed by a non-resident landlord goes through LPC.

### Example 4 — Disclosure boundary: LPC vs CoP9

- **Carmichael-Estate, the landlord:** has 8 years of undisclosed rental income across a 6-property portfolio. The undeclared amount is ~£280,000 of gross rents over the 8 years. The landlord became aware of the obligation 4 years ago but did not act, fearing penalty exposure.
- **Statutory analysis:** the 4-year delay AFTER becoming aware risks characterisation as deliberate conduct under Sch 41 / Sch 24. The 20-year discovery window under TMA 1970 s.36(1A) opens. The aggregate amount and the deliberate-conduct element approach the threshold where HMRC may consider criminal-prosecution interest (the threshold is fact-specific; serious-fraud cases typically involve aggregated under-declaration into six figures with clear deliberate concealment).
- **LPC vs CoP9 question:** LPC is appropriate where the conduct is non-deliberate or carelessly-deliberate but not actively concealed. Where HMRC perceives serious fraud, CoP9 / CDF representation is the appropriate route (criminal-prosecution immunity conditional on full disclosure). The landlord should engage specialist tax-investigation counsel before choosing the route.
- **Operational point:** the LPC orientation page must signpost the CoP9 / CDF boundary at §27.5 lock. Sessions writing must NOT route serious-fraud-suspected cases to LPC; the appropriate route is CoP9.

## FAQ expansion (RUN session polishes prose; 10-12 FAQs target)

1. **Q: What is the Let Property Campaign?**
   A: HMRC's Let Property Campaign (LPC) is a published voluntary-disclosure route for residential landlords with undisclosed rental income, opened 9 September 2013 with no announced end date. The campaign operates within the existing Schedule 41 FA 2008 (failure-to-notify) and Schedule 24 FA 2007 (inaccuracy) penalty frameworks, unlocking unprompted-disclosure mitigation floors for taxpayers who self-correct via the LPC channel.

2. **Q: Who can use the Let Property Campaign?**
   A: Residential landlords (UK and non-UK resident) with undisclosed rental income from residential property. Excluded: limited companies (use Digital Disclosure Service or CT-specific channels); trusts (use SA900 amendment or DDS); commercial-property landlords (use DDS); landlords whose offshore-source income or assets need disclosure (use the Worldwide Disclosure Facility).

3. **Q: What is the three-step LPC process?**
   A: Step 1 — Notify HMRC via the gov.uk LPC notification form. HMRC issues an acknowledgement and Disclosure Reference Number (DRN). Step 2 — within 90 days of acknowledgement, submit a full disclosure (tax + interest + proposed penalty by tax year, with supporting evidence). Step 3 — pay the full disclosed liability on or before submission of the disclosure. Time-to-pay arrangements may be agreed in advance where the liability is material.

4. **Q: What penalty mitigation does the LPC unlock?**
   A: For never-notified cases (Schedule 41 FA 2008), unprompted-disclosure floors are 0% non-deliberate (with a 12-month qualifier — disclosure within 12 months of tax becoming payable) / 20% deliberate / 30% deliberate-concealed. For inaccurate-return cases (Schedule 24 FA 2007), unprompted floors are 0% careless (NO 12-month qualifier per F-5 / Wave 7 lock) / 20% deliberate / 30% deliberate-concealed. Prompted floors are higher in both regimes.

5. **Q: How many years of rental income do I need to disclose?**
   A: The disclosure period mirrors HMRC's discovery window under TMA 1970 s.36. Four years for innocent error; six years for careless behaviour; twelve years for offshore innocent error under s.36A (FA 2019); twenty years for deliberate behaviour under s.36(1A). Sessions should be honest with readers that a deliberate-behaviour characterisation opens a 20-year reach-back; understating the disclosure period to "save" tax does not survive HMRC scrutiny and worsens the penalty position.

6. **Q: Does the LPC apply to non-resident landlords with UK rental?**
   A: Yes. The LPC accommodates non-UK-resident landlords with UK-source rental income. The non-resident landlord scheme (NRL) governs the operational withholding; LPC remediates undisclosed historic rental. Sessions writing must NOT route a non-resident landlord with UK-source rental income to the Worldwide Disclosure Facility — WDF is for offshore-source income / assets, not UK BTL.

7. **Q: What evidence do I need to provide on disclosure?**
   A: Rental income by tax year (gross rents per property); allowable deductions (repairs, agent fees, mortgage interest interaction with Section 24 restriction post-2020/21); any disposals during the disclosure period requiring CGT computation; supporting evidence (bank statements, agent statements, expense receipts). A complete and substantiated disclosure protects the unprompted-disclosure floor; an incomplete or inaccurate disclosure risks Sch 24 deliberate-concealed penalties at 100% maximum plus criminal-prosecution exposure.

8. **Q: What is the difference between LPC and the Digital Disclosure Service?**
   A: The LPC is a campaign-specific route for residential landlords with undisclosed rental income. The Digital Disclosure Service (DDS) is HMRC's general voluntary-disclosure umbrella covering most other UK tax matters (employment income, dividends, self-employment, capital gains, non-rental property matters). Sessions sometimes describe DDS as "the LPC for everything else"; the gov.uk pages run in parallel and overlap operationally.

9. **Q: What is the boundary between LPC and CoP9?**
   A: LPC is for non-deliberate, carelessly-deliberate, or unconcealed-deliberate conduct where the landlord self-corrects voluntarily. Code of Practice 9 (CoP9) / Contractual Disclosure Facility (CDF) is HMRC's civil-route framework for SUSPECTED SERIOUS FRAUD, offering criminal-prosecution immunity if the taxpayer makes a full and frank disclosure within the 60-day window. Where HMRC's letter raises serious-fraud suspicion (substantial sums, deliberate concealment, multiple years), the appropriate route is CoP9, not LPC.

10. **Q: What happens if I do not disclose and HMRC discovers the rental?**
    A: HMRC can issue a discovery assessment under TMA 1970 s.29 within the time-limit framework at s.34 / s.36. Prompted-disclosure penalty floors apply (10% / 35% / 50% under Sch 41; 15% / 35% / 50% under Sch 24). Aggregate exposure on multi-year undisclosed rental can run to 50%-100% of the underlying tax plus interest plus, in serious cases, criminal-prosecution proceedings. The LPC route's unprompted-disclosure mitigation is typically materially better than the discovered-cases position.

11. **Q: What if my disclosure is incomplete or false?**
    A: An incomplete or false LPC disclosure exposes the taxpayer to a worse position than no disclosure at all. The Sch 24 deliberate-concealed penalty at 100% maximum applies on the additional concealed amount; the deliberate-concealment characterisation opens the 20-year reach-back under s.36(1A); criminal-prosecution risk attaches to the concealed element. Sessions should warn readers that the LPC route works only with a full and frank disclosure.

12. **Q: Where does this orientation page sit relative to other LPC pages on the site?**
    A: This page is the definitional + process-walkthrough layer (what LPC is, who can use it, three-step mechanic, penalty architecture, route comparison). Sibling pages cover decision layers (why disclose), tool layers (penalty calculator), and remediation narratives (for the non-expert cohort). Readers seeking the orientation read should land here; readers comparing LPC against doing nothing should land on the decision-layer page; readers needing penalty numbers should land on the calculator.
