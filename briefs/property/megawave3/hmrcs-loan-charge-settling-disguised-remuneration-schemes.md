# HMRC's Loan Charge: Settling Disguised Remuneration Schemes — A Property-Business Director Guide

## Statutory anchor

- **Primary — loan charge itself:** Finance (No.2) Act 2017 **Schedule 11** ("Employment income provided through third parties: loans etc outstanding on 5 April 2019"). Verified live 2026-05-27 at `https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2017/32/schedule/11`. Schedule 11 is the operative provision that crystallises a one-off income tax + NICs charge on the outstanding balance of disguised-remuneration loans as at 5 April 2019. **In force**, with amendments per FA 2020.
- **Primary — Morse-review amendments restructuring eligibility:** Finance Act 2020 **Schedule 2** (Part 1 — "Limiting income tax charge on loans"). Verified at `https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2020/14/schedule/2` (Stage 2 to re-verify the section-by-section anchors at write time). FA 2020 Sch 2 reformed the loan charge to: (i) **limit charge to loans made on or after 9 December 2010** (was 6 April 1999); (ii) **carve out loans made between 9 December 2010 and 5 April 2016 where the taxpayer disclosed and HMRC did not act**; (iii) **introduce a deferral / instalment route** for taxpayers paying via PAYE; (iv) **deny double-charging** where pre-2019 income tax had already been settled.
- **Primary — disguised remuneration head-rule:** Income Tax (Earnings and Pensions) Act 2003 **Part 7A** ("Employment income provided through third parties") ss.554A-554Z21. Inserted by FA 2011 Sch 2 from 6 April 2011. Part 7A is the bedrock statutory framework; the FA (No.2) 2017 Sch 11 loan charge is the 2019 sweep-up bolted onto Part 7A.
- **Supporting — settlement framework:** HMRC's published "**Disguised Remuneration: Settling your tax affairs**" guidance (settlement opportunity published in successive iterations from August 2017 onwards; the November 2017 published settlement terms; the September 2018 follow-up settlement opportunity; the 30 September 2020 extended deadline after the Morse review). The "Settlement Opportunity" is **administrative HMRC practice, not a statutory regime** — sessions writing on it must not frame it as a statutory route on equal footing with the loan charge itself.
- **Supporting — voluntary-disclosure route boundary:** §27.5 (CoP9 / CDF — Wave 7 lock); §27.6 (LPC + WDF + general voluntary disclosure — Wave 7 lock). The loan-charge settlement route is **distinct from** LPC (LPC is for residential landlords with undisclosed rental income), distinct from WDF (offshore), and adjacent to but separate from CoP9 / CDF (HMRC-initiated civil fraud investigation with criminal-prosecution immunity). Sessions must NOT collapse loan-charge settlement into the LPC route.
- **House position reference:** §27 (HMRC enquiry + tax compliance mechanics — Wave 7 lock) for the general penalty / mitigation architecture; §27.5 (CoP9 / CDF) for the criminal-prosecution boundary; §27.6 (voluntary disclosure routes) for the comparison framework. **NEW HP LOCK CANDIDATE FLAGGED — §27.10 "Disguised remuneration loan charge + settlement framework" mini-lock** to anchor FA (No.2) 2017 Sch 11 + FA 2020 Sch 2 Morse amendments + ITEPA 2003 Part 7A architecture for future sessions writing in this space. Flag-but-do-not-block.

## Worked-example data (RUN session uses these as canvas)

### Example 1 — Property-development LtdCo director with 2015 EBT loan

- **Aldgate Developments Ltd, the director:** in 2015 the director participated in an EBT-based remuneration scheme. £180,000 was contributed by the company to an offshore EBT, then loaned to the director on apparently-commercial terms. The director has continued to hold the loan with no repayment activity. Outstanding loan balance as at 5 April 2019: £180,000.
- **Loan-charge crystallisation:** FA (No.2) 2017 Sch 11 imposes a one-off income tax + NICs charge on the £180,000 at the director's marginal rate for 2018/19. Assuming 40% marginal income tax band and 2% Class 1 NICs (employee-side), the charge approaches £75,600 (40% × £180,000 = £72,000 income tax + £3,600 employee NICs at 2%) plus employer NICs (13.8% × £180,000 = £24,840) settled via the LtdCo wrapper. Total exposure ~£100k+.
- **FA 2020 Morse cut-off analysis:** the 2015 loan post-dates 9 December 2010, so the FA 2020 carve-out does NOT exempt it. The loan IS within the charge.
- **Operational route:** the director has not yet settled. Voluntary disclosure to HMRC's Counter Avoidance team is the residual route now that the September 2020 settlement-opportunity deadline has closed. Sch 24 + Sch 41 penalty exposure attaches; unprompted-disclosure floors apply if the director self-corrects before HMRC initiates.

### Example 2 — Pre-2010 contractor-loan (Morse carve-out applies)

- **Mawell-Estate, the director:** in 2008 the director participated in a contractor-loan arrangement; £95,000 in loans were outstanding at 5 April 2019. The 2008 loans pre-date the FA 2020 cut-off of 9 December 2010.
- **Effect of FA 2020 Sch 2:** the Morse review carve-out exempts loans made before 9 December 2010 from the loan charge. The £95,000 is NOT within the charge.
- **Residual exposure:** the underlying ITEPA 2003 Part 7A architecture still operates on the loan if the underlying employment income should have been declared at the time. HMRC's discovery powers under TMA 1970 s.29 + s.36(1A) (20-year deliberate-behaviour limb) may still apply to a 2008-era arrangement if HMRC argues deliberate non-declaration. The director is OUT of the loan-charge limb but possibly still in the underlying-Part-7A limb.
- **Operational point:** the FA 2020 cut-off is not a blanket pre-2010 amnesty; it removes the loan-charge sweep-up but does not retire the underlying disguised-remuneration framework. Sessions writing must NOT collapse "pre-9-December-2010 = no exposure" — the discovery-window architecture survives.

### Example 3 — Remuneration trust used inside property-development LtdCo

- **Singh-Estate Developments Ltd, the directors:** in 2017 the company participated in a remuneration-trust structure marketed as a "non-loan" alternative to EBT. £240,000 was contributed; £200,000 reached the directors in 2017/18 and 2018/19 in the form of distributions.
- **Loan-charge analysis:** the loan charge bites on OUTSTANDING LOANS at 5 April 2019. Where the structure operates by distribution (not loan), the loan-charge limb may not engage. However, the ITEPA 2003 Part 7A head-rule applies if the distributions are treated as earnings under the s.554A gateway tests. Operationally, HMRC has treated remuneration-trust structures as within Part 7A regardless of the "non-loan" labelling.
- **Operational route:** voluntary disclosure to the Counter Avoidance team. The remuneration-trust structures fall within the Settlement Opportunity scope at HMRC operational level even though the loan-charge does not literally bite. Cross-link to A8 sibling MW3 content distinguishes this from the LPC (rental disclosure) route.

### Example 4 — CoP9 boundary case (deliberate concealment suspected)

- **Carmichael-Estate, the director:** participated in a 2013-2017 contractor-loan arrangement and was advised by the scheme promoter that the structure was "tax-free". The director did not file any disclosure-of-tax-avoidance-scheme (DOTAS) return at the time. HMRC has issued a letter offering CoP9 representation.
- **Effect of CoP9:** CoP9 is HMRC's Code of Practice 9 / Contractual Disclosure Facility (CDF) — a civil-route alternative to criminal prosecution, conditional on full and frank disclosure. CoP9 carries criminal-prosecution immunity if the disclosure is comprehensive. The Settlement Opportunity (loan-charge-specific) is NOT the appropriate route here; the director should engage specialist representation and respond to the CoP9 letter within the 60-day window.
- **Operational point:** CoP9 representation is specialist territory (§27.5 Wave 7 lock). Sessions writing must signpost CoP9 as a referral route, NOT collapse it into the published settlement opportunity. The two routes operate on different statutory bases and protect different reader interests.

## FAQ expansion (RUN session polishes prose; 10-12 FAQs target)

1. **Q: What is HMRC's loan charge and when does it apply?**
   A: The loan charge is a one-off income tax and NICs charge under Finance (No.2) Act 2017 Schedule 11 on the outstanding balance of disguised-remuneration loans as at 5 April 2019. It applies to loans made through arrangements that fall within ITEPA 2003 Part 7A (employment income provided through third parties), regardless of when the loan was made (subject to the 9 December 2010 Morse cut-off discussed below).

2. **Q: What did the Morse review change?**
   A: Sir Amyas Morse's independent review reported in December 2019; Finance Act 2020 Schedule 2 enacted the recommendations. The Morse amendments: (i) limited the loan charge to loans made on or after 9 December 2010 (originally the charge swept from 6 April 1999); (ii) carved out loans made between 9 December 2010 and 5 April 2016 where the taxpayer had disclosed the arrangement and HMRC had not acted; (iii) introduced a PAYE-instalment deferral route; (iv) prevented double-charging where pre-2019 income tax had already been settled.

3. **Q: What is the disguised-remuneration head-rule, and how does it relate to the loan charge?**
   A: The head-rule is ITEPA 2003 Part 7A (sections 554A-554Z21), inserted by Finance Act 2011 Schedule 2 from 6 April 2011. Part 7A treats certain payments and benefits provided through third parties as employment earnings. The FA (No.2) 2017 Schedule 11 loan charge is a 2019 sweep-up of outstanding-loan balances under Part-7A-type structures; the two regimes are layered and can apply concurrently on different aspects of the same scheme.

4. **Q: Can I still use the Settlement Opportunity?**
   A: The published Settlement Opportunity operated through successive iterations from August 2017 with the most recent extended deadline of 30 September 2020 under the Morse framework. As of May 2026 the published opportunity is closed to new entrants. Residual disclosure routes continue via direct correspondence with HMRC's Counter Avoidance team; sessions should NOT promise readers a published opportunity remains open.

5. **Q: How does the loan-charge route compare with the Let Property Campaign?**
   A: LPC is for residential landlords with undisclosed rental income; the loan charge is for participants in disguised-remuneration arrangements. The two regimes are entirely separate. A landlord who has BOTH undisclosed rental income AND a loan-charge exposure needs two parallel disclosure streams (LPC for the rental side; Counter Avoidance for the loan-charge side). Sessions writing must NOT collapse the loan-charge disclosure into the LPC route.

6. **Q: What penalty exposure attaches to a loan-charge disclosure?**
   A: Two regimes can apply on different aspects of the same scheme. Schedule 24 FA 2007 inaccuracy penalties (max 30% careless / 70% deliberate / 100% deliberate-concealed) apply where prior returns understated employment income. Schedule 41 FA 2008 failure-to-notify penalties apply where chargeability was never declared. Unprompted-disclosure floors apply if the taxpayer self-corrects before HMRC initiates contact.

7. **Q: Does the loan charge apply to a director who repaid the loan before 5 April 2019?**
   A: The loan charge bites on amounts OUTSTANDING at 5 April 2019. A bona fide repayment before that date removes the amount from the charge. However, HMRC has scrutinised repayments to ensure they are not artificial or circular (e.g. repayment funded by a new loan from the same scheme); a repayment that lacks economic substance can be ignored by HMRC under the general anti-avoidance principle. Sessions writing should warn readers that "I repaid it" is not a complete answer without evidencing genuine economic discharge.

8. **Q: What time limits apply to HMRC's discovery powers in this context?**
   A: TMA 1970 s.36 governs the extended discovery-assessment time limits. The standard 4-year limit applies for innocent error; 6 years for careless behaviour; 20 years under s.36(1A) for deliberate behaviour. HMRC has consistently characterised disguised-remuneration participation as deliberate where the scheme had clear avoidance characteristics, opening the 20-year window for assessments reaching back to early 2000s arrangements.

9. **Q: Is the loan charge the same regime as the close-company section 455 loan charge?**
   A: No. CTA 2010 sections 455 to 464A impose a separate close-company charge on loans to participators (currently at 35.75% from 2026/27 onwards per F-9 / Wave 7 lock). The s.455 regime operates on the close-company side and is refundable on loan repayment under s.458. The FA (No.2) 2017 Sch 11 loan charge is an income-tax + NICs charge on the borrower in a disguised-remuneration arrangement and is NOT refundable on repayment. Sessions writing must NOT conflate the two regimes.

10. **Q: I received a CoP9 letter rather than a Settlement Opportunity invitation. What is the difference?**
    A: Code of Practice 9 (CoP9) is HMRC's civil-route framework for suspected serious fraud, with conditional immunity from criminal prosecution if the taxpayer makes a full and frank disclosure within the 60-day window. The Settlement Opportunity is a published administrative route for loan-charge disclosure. CoP9 is specialist territory; readers receiving a CoP9 letter should engage tax-investigation counsel before responding. Sessions writing should signpost CoP9 as a referral route rather than collapse it into general loan-charge framing.

11. **Q: What about contractor-loan schemes where I was an employee of a UK umbrella company?**
    A: The loan charge applies regardless of whether the participant was a contractor, employee, or director, provided the arrangement fell within ITEPA 2003 Part 7A. The umbrella-company variant typically operates with the umbrella as the engager and an offshore entity as the loan-source; the FA (No.2) 2017 Sch 11 charge bites on the worker as the borrower. Property-business directors who used contractor-loan umbrella arrangements are within scope on the same architecture as a property-trade director using an EBT directly.

12. **Q: How does the loan charge interact with MTD ITSA?**
    A: The loan charge is a one-off historic-loan crystallisation at 5 April 2019. MTD ITSA is an ongoing digital reporting regime for current-year rental and self-employment income from 6 April 2026 onwards. The two regimes do not statutorily overlap. A landlord-director settling a loan charge runs a parallel MTD ITSA cycle for their current rental activity; the loan-charge tax liability is a one-off settlement, not an ongoing MTD-reportable item.
