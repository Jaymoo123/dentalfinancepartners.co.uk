- Primary (existing commonhold statutory framework — the BASE the reform builds on): Commonhold and Leasehold Reform Act 2002 (c. 15) Part 1 ss.1-70 — commonhold land + registration + unit-holders + common parts + commonhold association + community statement + termination + miscellaneous. **Verified structure at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2002/15/contents on 2026-05-27**: s.1 (commonhold land) + ss.2-6 (registration mechanics) + ss.7-10 (effect of registration) + ss.11-24 (commonhold unit) + ss.25-30 (common parts) + ss.31-36 (governance — community statement + commonhold association constitution + voting) + ss.37-42 (operation — enforcement + commonhold assessment + building safety assessment + reserve fund + ombudsman) + ss.43-56 (termination — voluntary winding-up + court succession + 100%/80% acquisition thresholds) + ss.57-70 (miscellaneous — multiple-site commonholds + Crown application + interpretation). **Critical:** the 2002 Part 1 commonhold framework exists but has been used in fewer than 20 schemes since enactment due to operational design flaws the Law Commission 2020 report addressed; the forthcoming Bill is the operational reset to make commonhold workable as the default tenure. §31.B (NEW LOCK NEEDED per F-103).

- Primary (in-force leasehold reform already enacted — material context for landlord investors): **Leasehold and Freehold Reform Act 2024 (c. 22) Part 2** — phased commencement 2024-2027. Four headline changes per §31.3 pre-locked: (1) abolishes 2-year qualifying-ownership prerequisite; (2) extends LRHUDA 1993 Chapter II new-lease term from existing-lease + 90 years to 990-year peppercorn; (3) abolishes marriage value at the 80-year unexpired-term boundary; (4) regulates deferment / capitalisation rates (post-Sportelli 5% no longer default). **CRITICAL: commencement is phased and not all Part 2 provisions are in force as of 2026-05-27 per §31.3.** Stage 2 sub-agent MUST verify the in-force position on the write date against the gov.uk commencement-orders register per §16.35. §31.3 pre-locked.
- Primary (in-force ground rent ban on new leases): **Leasehold Reform (Ground Rent) Act 2022 (c. 1)** — in force 30 June 2022 (residential long leases) + 1 April 2023 (retirement homes). Bans monetary ground rent on most new long residential leases (more than 21 years); permitted ground rent is a peppercorn. Penalty £500 to £30,000 per breach. **Critical:** does NOT apply to leases granted before 30 June 2022; the forthcoming Bill is expected to extend reform to existing leases (ground rent buyout / cap mechanism) per White Paper commitments. §31.B partial cross-link (NEW LOCK candidate covers ground-rent treatment of existing leases under reformed regime).
- Primary (in-force building safety + leaseholder protections — material to service-charge income stream for freehold investors): **Building Safety Act 2022 (c. 30) ss.116-125 + Schedule 8** — in force 28 June 2022. Leaseholder protections from cladding remediation costs (Sch 8 para 8 — absolute protection for qualifying leases); non-cladding low-value carve-out (Sch 8 para 4 — properties valued ≤ £175k / £325k Greater London on 14 February 2022 fully exempt); non-cladding graduated cap (Sch 8 para 6 — £10k / £15k base, scaling to £50k / £100k for high-value properties). **Amendments by LFRA 2024 commenced via SI 2024/1018 effective 31 October 2024 per §26.2 pre-locked.** Stage 2 sub-agent verifies post-LFRA-2024 Sch 8 text at write per §16.35. §26.2 + §26 cluster pre-locked.

Commonhold and Leasehold Reform Act 2002 Part 2 Chapter 5 ss.150-159 — extended service-charge meaning + consultation requirements + statements of account + document inspection + jurisdiction + separate accounts + minor amendments + administration charges + estate management schemes (note ss.152-154 omitted by LFRA 2024). The forthcoming Bill is expected to further reform service-charge transparency (standardised statement format + tightened consultation thresholds + leaseholder access to documents + restriction of buildings-insurance commissions paid to managing agents). §31.B + §29 (VAT-side cross-reference for VAT-on-service-charges where applicable).
- Primary (forfeiture statutory framework — the regime the Bill is expected to reform): Law of Property Act 1925 s.146 + County Courts Act 1984 s.138 + LTA 1987 s.81 (small-arrears protection) + CLRA 2002 s.168 (breach determination prerequisite for forfeiture). ### Example 2 — Freehold-of-leasehold-block investor (Patel Holdings Ltd, 12-flat block)

Patel Holdings Ltd owns the freehold of a 12-flat purpose-built block acquired 2018 for £400,000. The block generates: (a) ground rent income £6,000/year (12 flats × £500/year ground rent on pre-LRGRA-2022 leases); (b) buildings insurance commission £2,400/year (15% commission on £16,000 premium); (c) occasional lease-extension premium income (one extension per ~2 years at £25,000+ premium).

**Investment-impact analysis post-LFRA-2024 + post-White-Paper:**

- **Ground-rent income £6,000/year:** at risk under forthcoming Bill (ground-rent reform for existing leases). Options under consultation include cap (e.g., £250/year cap) or buyout mechanism. Worst-case scenario: ground-rent stream extinguishes; capital value of freehold reduces by 10-15× annual ground rent = £60,000-£90,000 reduction.
- **Buildings-insurance commission £2,400/year:** at risk under forthcoming Bill (commission restriction signalled in White Paper). Worst-case scenario: commission stream extinguishes; capital value reduces by 5-10× = £12,000-£24,000.
- **Lease-extension premium income:** marriage value abolished under LFRA 2024 per §31.3 — extension premiums for sub-80-year-unexpired-term leases reduce by 40-60%. 990-year peppercorn extension term means lessee acquires longer protection, but the freeholder's premium-income window per claim is materially reduced. Worst-case scenario: blended extension-premium stream halves.
- **Forfeiture remedy:** reform signalled in White Paper. If abolished, ultimate-enforcement remedy for service-charge / ground-rent arrears removed. Freehold-investment-value risk premium increases.
- **Commonhold conversion route:** under forthcoming Bill, leaseholders gain a conversion pathway. If 12 leaseholders in this block converge on conversion, Patel Holdings Ltd ceases to hold the freehold (potentially a TCGA 1992 s.22 part-disposal or full-disposal event) + ground-rent income + commission stream all extinguish.

**Timing-of-trigger-event question:** Patel Holdings Ltd's decision is whether to dispose of the freehold now (before further reform reduces value) or hold (anticipating market has priced in expected reform). The page surfaces this as a scenario-analysis question, not directional advice.

### Example 5 — Managing-agent business (BlockMan Property Services Ltd, 25-block portfolio)

BlockMan manages 25 leasehold blocks (total ~400 leaseholder units) under standard service-charge management contracts. Annual revenue: ~£250,000 (management fees) + ~£60,000 (buildings-insurance commissions at 15% on aggregate £400,000 premium portfolio).

- **Service-charge transparency reforms:** tightened reporting + leaseholder access requirements increase operational cost (standardised statement format; consultation reforms; leaseholder document inspection). Net effect: lower margin on existing management contracts.
- **RTM simplification:** Lower thresholds + simpler procedures accelerate leaseholder takeover of management. Worst-case scenario: 20% of BlockMan's 25 blocks shift to RTM-company management over 3-5 years; revenue base reduces by ~£50,000.

- **Forfeiture reform:** reduces BlockMan's enforcement-action revenue (arrears recovery via forfeiture threat); operational impact limited.
- **Cumulative scenario:** combined revenue impact 25-35% reduction over 5-10 years if all reform components land as signalled. 3. **Q: What is the difference between LFRA 2024 and the forthcoming Bill?**
   A: LFRA 2024 (Leasehold and Freehold Reform Act 2024) is the PREVIOUS reform wave — enacted, in phased commencement 2024-2027. Key changes: abolition of 2-year qualifying ownership; 990-year peppercorn extension; marriage value abolition; deferment-rate regulation. The FORTHCOMING Bill (following Commonhold White Paper March 2025) is the NEXT reform wave — not yet introduced. Key proposals: default-commonhold for new flats; service-charge transparency reforms; forfeiture reform; ground-rent reform for existing leases; commonhold conversion pathway; RTM simplification. The Bill is the legislative vehicle for the White Paper substance; both work cumulatively with LFRA 2024.

4. **Q: Will my existing ground rent be banned?**
   A: NOT YET as of seed-write 2026-05-27. The Leasehold Reform (Ground Rent) Act 2022 bans NEW long-lease ground rents (in force 30 June 2022) but does NOT apply to existing leases — contractual ground rent on pre-2022 leases continues. The forthcoming Bill is expected to extend reform to existing leases (cap / buyout mechanism / phased reduction); the precise mechanism is under consultation. RUN session verifies consultation outcome + Bill text at write per §16.35 STRICT GATE. Sessions must NOT write "existing ground rents are banned" until enacted.

5. **Q: Can I convert my leasehold block to commonhold today?**
   A: TECHNICALLY YES under CLRA 2002 Part 1 framework — but operationally NO at scale. The 2002 framework requires 100% unit-holder consent + freeholder co-operation (or court succession at 80% threshold). Fewer than 20 commonhold schemes exist in England + Wales since 2004 due to operational design flaws the Law Commission 2020 report addressed. The forthcoming Bill is the operational reset to make commonhold workable — with the conversion pathway expected to lower the consent threshold + clarify the freeholder-exit mechanism + clarify tax treatment. Today's conversion is theoretically available but practically uncommon.

6. **Q: Does marriage value still apply to my lease extension?**
   A: COMMENCEMENT-DEPENDENT. LFRA 2024 abolishes marriage value (per §31.3 pre-locked) — but commencement is phased. If your extension-claim notification is PRE the relevant Part 2 commencement date, OLD rules apply (marriage value included). If POST, NEW rules apply (marriage value abolished). RUN session verifies LFRA 2024 commencement state at write per §31.3 STRICT GATE. Sessions must NOT write "marriage value is abolished for all enfranchisement" without the commencement caveat.

7. **Q: What happens to the service-charge regime if commonhold becomes the default?**
   A: For NEW flats: leasehold + service-charge regime ends; commonhold + commonhold-assessment regime takes over. For EXISTING leasehold blocks: the leasehold + service-charge regime continues unless the block converts to commonhold via the forthcoming Bill's conversion pathway. The forthcoming Bill is ALSO expected to reform the service-charge regime for blocks remaining in leasehold tenure (standardised statement format + tightened consultation + leaseholder document access + buildings-insurance commission restriction). RUN session verifies White Paper service-charge-reform detail at write per §16.35.

8. **Q: Should I dispose of my freehold-of-leasehold-block portfolio now or hold?**

The forthcoming Bill is expected to clarify, with the natural policy direction being a conversion-relief (to avoid creating a tax penalty on the route the Government wants to promote). 10. **Q: Is forfeiture being abolished?**
    A: UNCERTAIN as of seed-write 2026-05-27. The Commonhold White Paper March 2025 signals forfeiture reform (Law Commission 2006 + 2020 reports both recommended substantial reform or abolition). The forthcoming Bill is expected to address forfeiture, but the precise reform (abolition vs substantial reform vs procedural reform) is not yet determined. The Commonhold White Paper March 2025 covers England + Wales. Welsh Government has parallel intentions for Welsh leasehold reform; the precise Welsh legislative timetable is separate from the England Bill.
