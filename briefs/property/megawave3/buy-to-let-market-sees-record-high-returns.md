# "BTL Returns Hit Record High" — What the Headlines Leave Out

## Statutory anchor

- Primary (Section 24 finance cost restriction — the load-bearing post-2017 reform): ITTOIA 2005 s.272A — restricts deduction of finance costs to a 20% basic-rate tax credit. Phased in over FA 2015 Sch 24 timetable; in full force from 6 April 2020. §4 lock.
- Primary (CGT residential rates 18%/24% from 30 October 2024): TCGA 1992 + FA 2024 c. 3 + Autumn Budget 2024 changes effective 30 October 2024 (residential lower rate held at 18% per FA 2024; higher rate cut from 28% to 24% per Autumn Budget 2024). §5 lock — Stage 2 verifies verbatim against legislation.gov.uk at write per §16.27 + §16.35.
- Primary (SDLT additional-dwellings 5% surcharge — increased from 3% to 5% on 31 October 2024 per Autumn Budget 2024): FA 2003 Sch 4ZA. §1 + §1.I pre-locked. Sessions verify verbatim at write per §16.27.
- Primary (60-day in-year reporting on residential CGT disposals): TCGA 1992 Sch 2 paras 6-12 — applies to disposals on or after 27 October 2021 (60-day window from 27 October 2021; previously 30-day from 6 April 2020). §5 anchor.
- Primary (income-tax bands + marginal rates affecting BTL income): ITA 2007 + FA 2024 — basic 20%, higher 40%, additional 45%. Personal allowance taper from £100,000. §4 lock includes the post-Section-24 marginal-rate landscape.
- Primary (April 2027 property income tax surcharge — house framing per §7): position pre-locked; sessions surface as "watchpoint" not as in-force rate per §16.27 + §7. Verify state at write — if not enacted, frame conditionally.
- Primary (MTD for ITSA — operational data-quality overhead that affects realised-vs-headline-returns): ITA 2007 + SI 2021/1076 + commencement timetable. §3 + §19 architecture pre-locked. Sessions verify current commencement state at write per §19.1 mandate timeline.

## Framing differentiator (anti-templating, anti-cannibalisation)

The competitor template for "BTL market returns" pages is a repackaging of industry data-release press notes (Paragon Bank, Hamptons, Landbay, BVA BDRC, etc.) restating the headline gross-yield figure, adding a paragraph on regional variation, and closing with a generic "speak to a mortgage adviser" CTA. The article-shape is **tax-blind** — the headline returns are presented at the gross level with no overlay of Section 24, no SDLT-on-purchase consideration, no 60-day CGT reporting on exit, no marginal-rate impact for higher-rate landlords. A landlord reading the competitor-shape article comes away thinking the headline yield IS the cash position, which it has not been since 6 April 2020 for higher-rate landlords on individual-side property income.

## Key questions this page must answer

1. **What "BTL returns" means in the major data series — three different things often labelled identically** — Sessions distinguish: (a) **Gross rental yield** = annual rent / property price, pre-cost. Cited by most headline figures. Ignores mortgage interest, void periods, repairs, agent fees, licensing, tax. (b) **Net rental yield** = (annual rent - operating costs) / property price. Excludes finance costs in some publishers' methodology, includes in others. Sessions verify methodology per data release at write. (c) **Total return** = net rental yield + capital appreciation. Reflects the actual landlord-experience over a hold period; meaningful for portfolio decisions but rarely the headline figure. **Methodology cross-walks:** Paragon Bank quotes gross yield in its lender-side data; Hamptons quotes net yield in some series, total return in others; LandlordZONE / Mortgages for Business / BVA BDRC use different baselines. Stage 2 builds a quick methodology comparison table at write.
2. **The Section 24 overlay — why "record gross yields" do not translate to record after-tax cash for higher-rate landlords** — ITTOIA 2005 s.272A restricts finance-cost deduction to a 20% basic-rate tax credit; the landlord's marginal-rate exposure is on **gross-of-finance** rental profit. For a higher-rate-band landlord (40%) carrying a typical LTV mortgage, the post-Section-24 after-tax cash position can be a third or more lower than the pre-Section-24 calculation. Worked example placeholder: £30,000 annual rental income on a £400,000 property with £12,000 annual mortgage interest. Pre-Section 24: taxable profit £18,000, tax at 40% = £7,200, after-tax cash £10,800. Post-Section 24: taxable profit £30,000 (no interest deduction), tax at 40% = £12,000, minus 20% credit on £12,000 interest = £2,400, net tax £9,600, after-tax cash £8,400 (assuming all finance cost relievable). **Difference: £2,400 per year per property** — and the landlord's gross yield headline is unchanged in either world. Sessions verify all calculation steps + the operative s.272A wording at write per §16.35.
3. **The SDLT additional-dwellings 5% surcharge — the cash hit before any yield is earned** — FA 2003 Sch 4ZA. Surcharge increased from 3% to 5% on 31 October 2024 per Autumn Budget 2024. On a £250,000 BTL purchase, the additional-dwellings surcharge alone is £12,500 — payable on completion, immediately reducing the cash return on year-1 invested capital. Sessions verify the post-October-2024 5% rate against current legislation at write per §16.27. The page must surface this on the entry-point so the headline-yield reader has the SDLT cost in view from the start.
4. **The 60-day in-year CGT reporting + 18%/24% bands — exit-side tax-decay** — TCGA 1992 Sch 2 paras 6-12 (60-day reporting). FA 2024 + Autumn Budget 2024 residential rates: 18% basic-rate-band, 24% higher-rate-band (the higher rate was 28% pre-Autumn-Budget-2024; cut to 24% from 30 October 2024 — net taxpayer relief). Sessions clarify: the rate-cut is a partial offset to the SDLT surcharge increase + Section 24 income-side restriction; the post-2024 framework is a re-balancing, NOT a return to pre-2017 economics. Total-return calculations on a hold-and-sell BTL must include the residential CGT layer at exit, not just rental yield over hold.
5. **The MTD for ITSA operational overhead — quarterly reporting cost as a yield drag** — §19 architecture. The phased mandate from April 2026 (£50,000+ qualifying income) + April 2027 (£30,000+) + April 2028 (£20,000+ — Spring Statement 2025 confirmation, sessions verify state at write per §19.1) imposes quarterly software-cost + record-keeping-time overhead. For smaller portfolios with thin margins post-Section-24, the MTD overhead becomes a meaningful yield-line item. Sessions surface this without overstating — it's a cost, not a regime change to net yields.
6. **Ltd Co route as the structural workaround — but with its own cost stack** — Section 24 does NOT apply to corporate landlords (CTA 2009 Pt 4 + corporation tax on company rental profit; finance-cost deduction is normal cost-of-business). Many post-2017 landlords have switched to or started in Ltd Co structures. But the Ltd Co route carries: corporation tax + dividend/salary extraction tax + accountancy / filing cost + SDLT-on-incorporation (where applicable) + non-tax-free use of equity. The page surfaces this as an option for some, not a universal answer; forward-links to §21 / §22 incorporation cluster pages rather than re-walking. **Critical anti-templating:** sessions must NOT default to "incorporate to fix Section 24" — that framing is generic and ignores the case-by-case calculation. Frame as a calculation, not a default.
7. **The Renters' Rights Act 2025 risk-side overlay (England only) — possession-uncertainty as a yield-volatility input** — §20 architecture. Section 21 abolition + extended Section 8 mandatory grounds + periodic-tenancy-default + Landlord Redress Scheme change the operational risk profile of an English BTL. The yield-line is unaffected in headline terms; the **realisation risk** around the yield rises because of slower-possession in disputed cases + the PRS database / Ombudsman compliance overhead. Sessions characterise this factually per §20.12 commencement timeline, NOT as a tax-rate change.
8. **The April 2027 property income surcharge — watchpoint, not in-force** — §7 architecture. Sessions verify against current statutory state at write per §16.27; if not enacted, frame conditionally ("the surcharge, if enacted, would..."). Page does NOT assert in-force; the watchpoint is named so the reader can re-check it at their decision date.
9. **Regional and jurisdictional variation — the headline UK figure obscures Wales / Scotland / NI differences** — Headline UK BTL-return figures average across England (SDLT + RRA 2025 + selective licensing in designated wards), Wales (LTT + Rent Smart Wales mandatory all-landlord licensing + Renting Homes (Wales) Act 2016), Scotland (LBTT + ADS + Private Housing (Tenancies) (Scotland) Act 2016 + Letting Agent Code of Practice), and Northern Ireland (SDLT applies, separate landlord registration regime). §23 architecture pre-locked. Sessions surface the jurisdictional split as one source of headline-figure variation; the page does NOT do a full cross-jurisdictional walk-through (other pages do that).
10. **The bottom-line decision framework — when is "record returns" a real signal vs a methodology artefact?** — Sessions close with a brief decision framework: (a) look at the methodology behind the figure; (b) translate to net yield by deducting expected operating cost + finance cost + Section-24-adjusted-tax for the landlord's marginal band; (c) factor in SDLT on entry + CGT on exit at the residential band; (d) compare to opportunity-cost benchmarks (equity index, savings rate, alternative property structures). The framework returns the headline question to the landlord's actual decision: "is THIS deal — at MY marginal rate, MY LTV, MY post-tax position — likely to deliver THIS yield in cash?" Sessions emphasise the per-landlord calculation, not a sentiment-claim about the market overall.

### Example 2 — The Section 24 overlay on a higher-rate landlord (the central worked example)

Mr Patel is a higher-rate-band landlord (40% marginal). He owns a single BTL property valued at £400,000 with a 75% LTV mortgage at 5% interest. Annual rental income £30,000.

**Pre-Section 24 (the picture for tax years before 2017):**
- Rental income: £30,000
- Less: mortgage interest deduction (£300,000 × 5%): £15,000
- Taxable rental profit: £15,000
- Income tax at 40%: £6,000
- **After-tax cash from rental: £9,000**

**Post-Section 24 (current — fully in force from 6 April 2020 per ITTOIA 2005 s.272A + FA 2015 Sch 24):**
- Rental income: £30,000
- Mortgage interest is NOT deductible from rental income.
- Taxable rental profit: £30,000 (no interest deduction; assume no other ops costs for illustration)
- Income tax at 40% on £30,000: £12,000
- Less: 20% basic-rate credit on finance cost (20% × £15,000): £3,000
- Net tax: £9,000
- **After-tax cash from rental: £30,000 - £15,000 (interest paid) - £9,000 (net tax) = £6,000**

**Difference: £3,000/year lower after-tax cash post-Section 24, even though the headline gross yield is UNCHANGED.** The two statements ("yields are at record highs" and "after-tax cash for higher-rate landlords has compressed") are not contradictory; the tax-decay sits underneath the yield headline. RUN session re-verifies the s.272A wording + the FA 2015 Sch 24 phasing schedule at legislation.gov.uk at write per §16.35.

### Example 3 — The SDLT 5% additional-dwellings cash hit on entry

Ms Singh is contemplating a £250,000 second BTL purchase. She already owns her main residence.

**Pre-31 October 2024 (3% additional-dwellings surcharge):**
- Standard SDLT: £125,000 × 0% + £125,000 × 2% = £2,500
- Additional-dwellings surcharge at 3%: £250,000 × 3% = £7,500
- Total SDLT: **£10,000**

**Post-31 October 2024 (5% additional-dwellings surcharge per FA 2024 Autumn Budget):**
- Standard SDLT: £125,000 × 0% + £125,000 × 2% = £2,500
- Additional-dwellings surcharge at 5%: £250,000 × 5% = £12,500
- Total SDLT: **£15,000**

**The cash hit on entry has increased by £5,000 on this single transaction.** When industry data releases announce record gross yields, they typically do not net the SDLT cost against the yield calculation — the £15,000 entry cost on a £250,000 BTL takes ~5% off the year-1 cash-on-invested-capital return before a single rental payment is collected. Mr Patel sells his £400,000 BTL for £500,000 after a 5-year hold; gain £100,000 (before reliefs and AEA).

**Pre-30 October 2024 (residential higher-rate CGT 28%):**
- Gain: £100,000

## FAQ expansion (RUN session polishes prose; 10-12 FAQs target)

1. **Q: What does gross yield mean in Paragon's data vs net yield in Hamptons?**
   A: Gross yield = annual rent / property price, before any cost deduction. Cited in most lender-side research because it is methodologically simple and the lender is concerned with the cash-flow-to-debt-service capacity at the property level. Net yield = (annual rent - operating costs) / property price; depending on the publisher's methodology, "operating costs" may include or exclude finance costs. Total return = net yield + capital appreciation; the most meaningful figure for a landlord's actual experience, but rarely the headline. The "record high" claim in any publisher's release should be read against their specific methodology.

2. **Q: Is the headline yield the cash I will see?**
   A: No, not for higher-rate-band landlords with mortgaged BTL. After Section 24 fully phased in from 6 April 2020, mortgage interest is NOT deductible from rental income; the basic-rate credit at 20% does not fully offset the marginal-rate exposure of higher-rate (40%) or additional-rate (45%) landlords on gross-of-finance rental profit. Your after-tax cash on a typical mortgaged BTL is materially below the headline gross yield — sometimes by a third or more, depending on LTV and your marginal band.

3. **Q: Did the October 2024 SDLT change cancel out the CGT rate cut?**
   A: Not exactly — the two changes pull in opposite directions but at different points in the BTL lifecycle. The SDLT additional-dwellings surcharge increased from 3% to 5% on 31 October 2024, increasing the cash hit on PURCHASE. The CGT residential higher rate cut from 28% to 24% on 30 October 2024 reduced the tax on EXIT. For a hold of 5+ years with strong capital appreciation, the CGT cut can outweigh the SDLT increase on a per-pound basis; for a short hold with limited appreciation, the SDLT entry cost dominates. The overall direction is a re-balancing of where the tax falls within the lifecycle, not a net tax cut for BTL.

4. **Q: Does Section 24 affect Ltd Co landlords?**
   A: No. Section 24 (ITTOIA 2005 s.272A) applies to individuals + partnership members (on their share of partnership rental profit). Corporate landlords compute property profits under CTA 2009 Part 4 with normal cost-of-business deductibility for finance costs. This is why post-2017 many landlords switched to Ltd Co structures. But the Ltd Co route carries its own cost stack: corporation tax (25% main rate from 1 April 2023 — RUN verifies); dividend / salary extraction tax on profits taken out; accountancy + filing cost; SDLT-on-incorporation (where applicable); non-tax-free use of equity. Whether the Ltd Co route is the right answer is a case-by-case calculation, NOT a default.

5. **Q: Do MTD costs reduce my yield?**
   A: Yes, marginally. Making Tax Digital for ITSA (SI 2021/1076) imposes quarterly digital reporting on landlords above the qualifying-income threshold. The thresholds phase in: April 2026 (£50,000+); April 2027 (£30,000+); April 2028 (£20,000+ — Spring Statement 2025 confirmation; RUN verifies state at write per §19.1). Operationally this means software cost + record-keeping time. For smaller portfolios with thin margins post-Section-24, the MTD overhead becomes a meaningful yield-line item; for larger portfolios with existing accountancy infrastructure, it is mostly absorbed. The cost is real but not regime-changing for net yield.

6. **Q: Is the April 2027 property income surcharge in force yet?**
   A: As of the time of this writing, the property income surcharge is a Budget proposal that has not been enacted in the form widely discussed in industry briefings; RUN session verifies the statutory state at write per §16.27. The page frames it as a "watchpoint" rather than an in-force rate — landlords should check the surcharge's current state at their decision date. If enacted, the surcharge would add to higher-rate landlords' marginal exposure on rental income; the after-tax cash position would compress further.

7. **Q: What about the Renters' Rights Act 2025 — does that change my yield calculation?**
   A: The RRA 2025 (England) does not change the tax-side yield calculation but does affect the RISK profile around the realised yield. Section 21 abolition + extended Section 8 mandatory grounds + periodic-tenancy default + PRS database + Landlord Redress Scheme can mean slower possession in disputed cases + a compliance overhead. Tax-side yield headlines do not reflect this risk-side overlay; for a landlord modelling realised yield, the RRA 2025 framework is a possession-uncertainty input. RUN session forward-links to §20 / §20.12 commencement pages.

8. **Q: Are the headline UK BTL-return figures the same in Wales and Scotland?**
   A: No. Headline UK figures average across England (SDLT + RRA 2025 + selective licensing in designated wards), Wales (LTT + Rent Smart Wales mandatory all-landlord licensing + Renting Homes (Wales) Act 2016), Scotland (LBTT + ADS + Private Housing (Tenancies) (Scotland) Act 2016 + Letting Agent Code of Practice), and Northern Ireland (SDLT applies + separate landlord registration regime). Devolved-jurisdiction differences on stamp-duty-equivalents + licensing + tenancy regimes produce regional variation that the headline UK figure obscures.

9. **Q: How does the methodology comparison help me decide whether to buy?**
   A: Step 1: identify which methodology the headline figure is using (gross yield / net yield / total return). Step 2: translate to NET yield for your situation — deduct expected operating costs + finance costs + Section-24-adjusted-tax for your marginal band. Step 3: factor in SDLT on entry (5% additional-dwellings surcharge) + CGT on exit (18%/24% residential bands). Step 4: compare to opportunity-cost benchmarks (equity index, savings rate, alternative property structures). The decision returns to YOUR specific marginal rate, YOUR LTV, YOUR post-tax position — not the publisher's headline.

10. **Q: Does the 60-day CGT reporting affect my realised return?**
    A: Yes, on the operational side. TCGA 1992 Sch 2 paras 6-12 (from 27 October 2021; 30-day from 6 April 2020) requires a UK residential disposal to be reported + tax paid within 60 days of completion. The disposal is then included again in the year-end SA / CT return for true-up. Operationally this means cash-flow on exit: CGT paid in 60 days rather than 31 January following the tax year. For a landlord planning a 5-year-hold exit, the 60-day reporting can compress the cash availability — sessions surface this as a working-capital point rather than a rate change.

11. **Q: Can I avoid the SDLT 5% surcharge by buying through a corporate structure?**
    A: No — the additional-dwellings 5% surcharge applies to companies acquiring residential property worth £40,000+ in MOST cases (FA 2003 Sch 4ZA); some narrow corporate-exemption routes exist but the general position is that corporate acquisitions of residential BTL property pay the surcharge. ATED (Annual Tax on Enveloped Dwellings) is a separate annual charge applying to companies holding residential property worth £500,000+ — that is a holding-side cost, not a substitute for the SDLT surcharge.

12. **Q: How can I model the after-tax cash for my specific BTL?**
    A: The model needs: (a) purchase price + SDLT/LTT cost; (b) LTV + mortgage interest rate; (c) gross rental income; (d) operating costs (insurance, repairs, agent, licensing); (e) your marginal-rate band; (f) hold period assumption + capital appreciation assumption; (g) Section 24 calculation (rental income taxed at marginal; 20% basic-rate credit on finance); (h) CGT on exit at 18%/24%. The page closes with a 5-year P&L worked example as Example 4. For your specific portfolio, talk to us about a bespoke model.
