# "BTL Returns Hit Record High" — What the Headlines Leave Out

## Statutory anchor

- Primary (Section 24 finance cost restriction — the load-bearing post-2017 reform): ITTOIA 2005 s.272A — restricts deduction of finance costs to a 20% basic-rate tax credit. Phased in over FA 2015 Sch 24 timetable; in full force from 6 April 2020. §4 lock.
- Primary (CGT residential rates 18%/24% from 30 October 2024): TCGA 1992 + FA 2024 c. 3 + Autumn Budget 2024 changes effective 30 October 2024 (residential lower rate held at 18% per FA 2024; higher rate cut from 28% to 24% per Autumn Budget 2024). §5 lock — Stage 2 verifies verbatim against legislation.gov.uk at write per §16.27 + §16.35.
- Primary (SDLT additional-dwellings 5% surcharge — increased from 3% to 5% on 31 October 2024 per Autumn Budget 2024): FA 2003 Sch 4ZA. §1 + §1.I pre-locked. Sessions verify verbatim at write per §16.27.
- Primary (60-day in-year reporting on residential CGT disposals): TCGA 1992 Sch 2 paras 6-12 — applies to disposals on or after 27 October 2021 (60-day window from 27 October 2021; previously 30-day from 6 April 2020). §5 anchor.
- Primary (income-tax bands + marginal rates affecting BTL income): ITA 2007 + FA 2024 — basic 20%, higher 40%, additional 45%. Personal allowance taper from £100,000. §4 lock includes the post-Section-24 marginal-rate landscape.
- Primary (April 2027 property income tax surcharge — house framing per §7): position pre-locked; sessions surface as "watchpoint" not as in-force rate per §16.27 + §7. Verify state at write — if not enacted, frame conditionally.
- Primary (MTD for ITSA — operational data-quality overhead that affects realised-vs-headline-returns): ITA 2007 + SI 2021/1076 + commencement timetable. §3 + §19 architecture pre-locked. Sessions verify current commencement state at write per §19.1 mandate timeline.
- Supporting (mortgage interest rate environment): Bank of England Bank Rate is the operative anchor for BTL finance cost. Sessions characterise current rate factually and verify against Bank of England Monetary Policy Committee announcement at write per §16.27. NOT a statutory citation — economic input to the yield calculation.
- Supporting (Renters' Rights Act 2025 — possession-risk variable affecting realised returns): §20 architecture pre-locked. Section 21 abolition + extended Section 8 mandatory grounds change the operational risk-return profile post-RRA 2025; sessions surface as a yield-affecting overlay not as a rate change.
- Supporting (Welsh / Scottish jurisdictional split for landlords holding cross-border portfolios): §23 LTT + LBTT architecture pre-locked.
- House position reference: §4 (Section 24 — primary anchor); §5 (CGT residential — primary anchor for total-return calculation); §1 + §1.I (SDLT additional-dwellings); §7 (April 2027 property income surcharge — watchpoint); §19 (MTD for ITSA); §20 (RRA 2025 — risk-side); §23 (devolved jurisdictions — out-of-scope mostly, mention briefly). **No new HP lock needed** — all anchors pre-locked, page integrates the post-Section-24 + post-October-2024 framework to decode headline-yield commentary.

## Framing differentiator (anti-templating, anti-cannibalisation)

The competitor template for "BTL market returns" pages is a repackaging of industry data-release press notes (Paragon Bank, Hamptons, Landbay, BVA BDRC, etc.) restating the headline gross-yield figure, adding a paragraph on regional variation, and closing with a generic "speak to a mortgage adviser" CTA. The article-shape is **tax-blind** — the headline returns are presented at the gross level with no overlay of Section 24, no SDLT-on-purchase consideration, no 60-day CGT reporting on exit, no marginal-rate impact for higher-rate landlords. A landlord reading the competitor-shape article comes away thinking the headline yield IS the cash position, which it has not been since 6 April 2020 for higher-rate landlords on individual-side property income.

This page reverses that: the data series and methodology differences are explained at the entry-point (so the landlord knows what they are reading), and then the Section 24 + SDLT + 60-day + CGT 24% overlay is applied to show what those headline returns translate to in after-tax cash. **The bottom-line framing:** headline yields can be at record highs WHILE the after-tax cash position for higher-rate-band landlords has compressed — the two statements are not contradictory once the tax-decay layer is visible. The page must make this paradox the centrepiece, not bury it as a caveat.

## Key questions this page must answer

1. **What "BTL returns" means in the major data series — three different things often labelled identically** — Sessions distinguish: (a) **Gross rental yield** = annual rent / property price, pre-cost. Cited by most headline figures. Ignores mortgage interest, void periods, repairs, agent fees, licensing, tax. (b) **Net rental yield** = (annual rent - operating costs) / property price. Excludes finance costs in some publishers' methodology, includes in others. Sessions verify methodology per data release at write. (c) **Total return** = net rental yield + capital appreciation. Reflects the actual landlord-experience over a hold period; meaningful for portfolio decisions but rarely the headline figure. **Methodology cross-walks:** Paragon Bank quotes gross yield in its lender-side data; Hamptons quotes net yield in some series, total return in others; LandlordZONE / Mortgages for Business / BVA BDRC use different baselines. Stage 2 builds a quick methodology comparison table at write.
2. **The Section 24 overlay — why "record gross yields" do not translate to record after-tax cash for higher-rate landlords** — ITTOIA 2005 s.272A restricts finance-cost deduction to a 20% basic-rate tax credit; the landlord's marginal-rate exposure is on **gross-of-finance** rental profit. For a higher-rate-band landlord (40%) carrying a typical LTV mortgage, the post-Section-24 after-tax cash position can be a third or more lower than the pre-Section-24 calculation. Worked example placeholder: £30,000 annual rental income on a £400,000 property with £12,000 annual mortgage interest. Pre-Section 24: taxable profit £18,000, tax at 40% = £7,200, after-tax cash £10,800. Post-Section 24: taxable profit £30,000 (no interest deduction), tax at 40% = £12,000, minus 20% credit on £12,000 interest = £2,400, net tax £9,600, after-tax cash £8,400 (assuming all finance cost relievable). **Difference: £2,400 per year per property** — and the landlord's gross yield headline is unchanged in either world. Sessions verify all calculation steps + the operative s.272A wording at write per §16.35.
3. **The SDLT additional-dwellings 5% surcharge — the cash hit before any yield is earned** — FA 2003 Sch 4ZA. Surcharge increased from 3% to 5% on 31 October 2024 per Autumn Budget 2024. On a £250,000 BTL purchase, the additional-dwellings surcharge alone is £12,500 — payable on completion, immediately reducing the cash return on year-1 invested capital. Sessions verify the post-October-2024 5% rate against current legislation at write per §16.27. The page must surface this on the entry-point so the headline-yield reader has the SDLT cost in view from the start.
4. **The 60-day in-year CGT reporting + 18%/24% bands — exit-side tax-decay** — TCGA 1992 Sch 2 paras 6-12 (60-day reporting). FA 2024 + Autumn Budget 2024 residential rates: 18% basic-rate-band, 24% higher-rate-band (the higher rate was 28% pre-Autumn-Budget-2024; cut to 24% from 30 October 2024 — net taxpayer relief). Sessions clarify: the rate-cut is a partial offset to the SDLT surcharge increase + Section 24 income-side restriction; the post-2024 framework is a re-balancing, NOT a return to pre-2017 economics. Total-return calculations on a hold-and-sell BTL must include the residential CGT layer at exit, not just rental yield over hold.
5. **The MTD for ITSA operational overhead — quarterly reporting cost as a yield drag** — §19 architecture. The phased mandate from April 2026 (£50,000+ qualifying income) + April 2027 (£30,000+) + April 2028 (£20,000+ — Spring Statement 2025 confirmation, sessions verify state at write per §19.1) imposes quarterly software-cost + record-keeping-time overhead. For smaller portfolios with thin margins post-Section-24, the MTD overhead becomes a meaningful yield-line item. Sessions surface this without overstating — it's a cost, not a regime change to net yields.
6. **Ltd Co route as the structural workaround — but with its own cost stack** — Section 24 does NOT apply to corporate landlords (CTA 2009 Pt 4 + corporation tax on company rental profit; finance-cost deduction is normal cost-of-business). Many post-2017 landlords have switched to or started in Ltd Co structures. But the Ltd Co route carries: corporation tax + dividend/salary extraction tax + accountancy / filing cost + SDLT-on-incorporation (where applicable) + non-tax-free use of equity. The page surfaces this as an option for some, not a universal answer; forward-links to §21 / §22 incorporation cluster pages rather than re-walking. **Critical anti-templating:** sessions must NOT default to "incorporate to fix Section 24" — that framing is generic and ignores the case-by-case calculation. Frame as a calculation, not a default.
7. **The Renters' Rights Act 2025 risk-side overlay (England only) — possession-uncertainty as a yield-volatility input** — §20 architecture. Section 21 abolition + extended Section 8 mandatory grounds + periodic-tenancy-default + Landlord Redress Scheme change the operational risk profile of an English BTL. The yield-line is unaffected in headline terms; the **realisation risk** around the yield rises because of slower-possession in disputed cases + the PRS database / Ombudsman compliance overhead. Sessions characterise this factually per §20.12 commencement timeline, NOT as a tax-rate change.
8. **The April 2027 property income surcharge — watchpoint, not in-force** — §7 architecture. Sessions verify against current statutory state at write per §16.27; if not enacted, frame conditionally ("the surcharge, if enacted, would..."). Page does NOT assert in-force; the watchpoint is named so the reader can re-check it at their decision date.
9. **Regional and jurisdictional variation — the headline UK figure obscures Wales / Scotland / NI differences** — Headline UK BTL-return figures average across England (SDLT + RRA 2025 + selective licensing in designated wards), Wales (LTT + Rent Smart Wales mandatory all-landlord licensing + Renting Homes (Wales) Act 2016), Scotland (LBTT + ADS + Private Housing (Tenancies) (Scotland) Act 2016 + Letting Agent Code of Practice), and Northern Ireland (SDLT applies, separate landlord registration regime). §23 architecture pre-locked. Sessions surface the jurisdictional split as one source of headline-figure variation; the page does NOT do a full cross-jurisdictional walk-through (other pages do that).
10. **The bottom-line decision framework — when is "record returns" a real signal vs a methodology artefact?** — Sessions close with a brief decision framework: (a) look at the methodology behind the figure; (b) translate to net yield by deducting expected operating cost + finance cost + Section-24-adjusted-tax for the landlord's marginal band; (c) factor in SDLT on entry + CGT on exit at the residential band; (d) compare to opportunity-cost benchmarks (equity index, savings rate, alternative property structures). The framework returns the headline question to the landlord's actual decision: "is THIS deal — at MY marginal rate, MY LTV, MY post-tax position — likely to deliver THIS yield in cash?" Sessions emphasise the per-landlord calculation, not a sentiment-claim about the market overall.
