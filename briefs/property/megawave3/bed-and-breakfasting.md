# Bed-and-Breakfasting: What the 30-Day Rule Does (and Doesn't) Mean for Landlords

## Statutory anchor

- Primary (the original "bed-and-breakfasting" anti-avoidance — 30-day matching for shares): TCGA 1992 s.106A, in force as currently amended (verified 2026-05-27 against https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12/section/106A). Section 106A(5) sets the operative rule: "if within the period of thirty days after the disposal the person making it acquires securities of the same class, the securities disposed of shall be identified—(a) with securities acquired by him within that period, rather than with other securities." Operates section-by-section with s.106A(4) — earlier disposals matched before later ones. Applies to **securities** only — not to land or buildings.
- Primary (general identification rules for capital gains — pooling architecture for securities): TCGA 1992 s.104 (the s.104 pool — same-day, then s.106A 30-day, then s.104 pool). The 30-day rule is a layer in the share-identification waterfall, not a free-standing prohibition. The page must clarify the architecture for readers who think "bed-and-breakfasting was banned" — it was NOT banned; the matching rule simply means a same-class repurchase within 30 days is treated as cancelling the disposal for loss-claiming purposes.
- Primary (connected-persons deemed-market-value rule — applies to ALL assets including land): TCGA 1992 s.17 — disposals between connected persons are deemed to take place at market value, regardless of actual consideration. §24.4 + §24.6 + §1.O cross-references.
- Primary (connected-persons no-loss-against-other-gains rule — the property-side trap): TCGA 1992 s.18 — losses on disposals to connected persons are RING-FENCED; they can only be set against future gains on disposals to the SAME connected person, not against general gains. For a landlord considering selling a depressed-value property to a spouse / parent / sibling / company under common control to crystallise a loss + repurchase, s.18 is the gate.
- Primary (connected-persons definition): TCGA 1992 s.286 — connected persons include spouses, civil partners, lineal ancestors and descendants, siblings, partnerships and their members, companies under common control, and persons in the same group of companies.
- Primary (inter-spouse no-gain-no-loss rule — the spouse "bed-and-spousing" mechanic): TCGA 1992 s.58(1) — disposals between spouses or civil partners living together are at no-gain-no-loss; transferee acquires at transferor's base cost. The legitimate spouse-side route uses s.58: spouse A sells outside-the-marriage, spouse B independently acquires; the s.58 transfer route only works for movements WITHIN the couple. Sham-spouse-onward-sale patterns risk Ramsay-line challenge under the anti-avoidance umbrella (W.T. Ramsay v IRC [1981] STC 174).
- Primary (the 1998 reform — why the page's framing matters): pre-FA 1998, "bed-and-breakfasting" of shares was a routine tax-planning move (sell shares at a loss / inside the annual exemption, rebuy the same shares next day, restart the holding period). FA 1998 inserted s.106A (then s.106) into TCGA 1992 — the 30-day matching rule — which destroyed the routine practice. Legitimate post-1998 substitutes: (a) **bed-and-ISA** (sell outside ISA, repurchase inside ISA — different beneficial holder, no matching); (b) **bed-and-SIPP** (sell outside SIPP, repurchase inside SIPP — different beneficial holder); (c) **bed-and-spouse** (s.58 inter-spouse transfer + spouse's onward sale — different identification holder); (d) **30-day-plus wait** (sell, wait >30 days, rebuy — matching rule does not apply). These ARE legitimate.
- Primary (property side — direct repurchase by same person): no specific anti-bed-and-breakfasting rule for direct property. The general rule is that a sale-and-rebuy of the same physical property by the same person at arm's length is unusual (the second acquisition needs a willing seller); where it occurs, the gain / loss on the first disposal stands and the second purchase establishes a new base cost. **The trap is on the connected-persons side via s.17 (deemed MV) + s.18 (no-loss ring-fence) — not via a 30-day rule.**
- Primary (annual exempt amount — the planning lever pre-1998 + post-1998 for legitimate routes): TCGA 1992 s.3 — currently £3,000 from 6 April 2024 (FA 2024 reduction from £6,000 in 2023/24 and £12,300 in 2022/23). Verify against gov.uk at write time per §16.27. The CGT annual exempt amount reduction makes the "use my annual exemption" planning lever materially less valuable than pre-2023.
- Supporting (Ramsay anti-avoidance umbrella): W.T. Ramsay Ltd v IRC [1981] STC 174; Furniss v Dawson [1984] STC 153; *MacNiven v Westmoreland Investments* [2001] STC 237; *Mayes v HMRC* [2011] EWCA Civ 407. The Ramsay line catches composite transactions with no commercial substance designed to obtain a tax result. Property-side bed-and-breakfasting attempts that involve circular movement of value between connected persons risk Ramsay-line recharacterisation.
- Supporting (operational case-law on connected-persons disposals): *Burdge v Pyne* [1969] 1 All ER 467 + general s.18 + s.286 case-law on what "connected" means; HMRC manual CG14580+ on connected-persons disposals.

## Key questions this page must answer

1. **What "bed-and-breakfasting" actually means — historical context** — Pre-FA-1998, "bed-and-breakfasting" of shares was a routine practice: sell shares at end-of-day (typically into a market-maker), repurchase the same class of shares next morning. Result: realised loss (if loss-position) deductible against general gains AND restart of the holding period; OR realised gain (if profit-position) using the annual exempt amount AND base cost reset. The "B&B" nickname came from the overnight stay between sell-and-rebuy. Pre-1998 the practice was widely used; investment advisers routinely structured year-end positions around it.
2. **What FA 1998 changed — the 30-day rule (TCGA 1992 s.106A)** — FA 1998 inserted (then s.106, now s.106A) into TCGA 1992. The operative provision (s.106A(5)): if within 30 days after a disposal the same person acquires securities of the same class, the disposal is matched against the post-disposal purchase, NOT against any earlier-acquired stock. Operative effect: the loss or gain is effectively cancelled because the holding has not, in substance, been broken. The 30-day rule is the **identification rule** — it changes how the share-pool maths works — not a prohibition on the trade itself.
3. **Why the 30-day rule applies to shares NOT property** — s.106A is in the "Identification of securities" group of provisions in TCGA 1992 (ss.104-106A). It applies to "securities" — shares, debentures, and analogous instruments. Direct interests in land and buildings are NOT securities; the s.106A matching rule does not apply to them. Mechanically, a property is unique (one specific dwelling at one specific address) — there is no "same class" of property to match against. The conceptual difficulty of pooling identical-class assets that drove the share-side rule simply does not arise for direct land disposals.
4. **The actual property-side traps — s.17 (deemed MV) + s.18 (no-loss to connected)** — Where a landlord considers selling a property to a spouse / parent / sibling / company under common control to crystallise a loss or restart base cost: **(a) s.17** treats the disposal as at market value regardless of actual consideration. Selling Dad the £400,000 BTL for £200,000 doesn't reduce the gain; the gain is computed on £400,000 deemed proceeds. **(b) s.18** ring-fences any LOSS realised on the disposal — the loss can only be set against future gains on disposals to the SAME connected person, not against general gains. Combined effect: the connected-persons sale-and-rebuy attempts to crystallise a loss against general gains DO NOT WORK at all. The connected-persons trap is the operative gate for property, not the 30-day rule.
5. **Connected-persons definition — who is caught (s.286)** — Spouses and civil partners; lineal ancestors and descendants (parents / grandparents / children / grandchildren); siblings; aunts / uncles / nieces / nephews (via the broader s.286(4) "relative" definition); partnerships and their members; companies under common control (>50% ownership or voting); the same group of companies. The connected-persons reach is broad — most family arrangements fall within it. Friends / business associates without ownership / corporate control links are NOT connected. The page must spell out the test because clients routinely assume "I'll sell it to my cousin's husband" is at arm's length when it may be (cousin's husband is not a relative under s.286) or may not be (depending on partnership / corporate structures).
6. **The spouse-NGNL route — TCGA 1992 s.58 + the bed-and-spouse pattern for shares** — s.58 provides that disposals between spouses / civil partners living together are at no-gain-no-loss: the transferee takes the transferor's base cost; no gain or loss arises on the transfer. The share-side "bed-and-spouse" pattern uses s.58: spouse A sells shares on the market (realising the gain / loss); spouse B independently buys the same class on the market (no matching with A's sale because s.106A applies to the SAME PERSON only; spouse B is a different person). Result: gain realised by A + new base cost for B, without the 30-day matching rule biting. For property, the analog would be: spouse A sells the property on the market; spouse B then buys a different property. The s.106A rule never engages because property is not a security; but the s.17 + s.18 connected-persons rules bite IF A sells to B rather than to a third party (which is the more common scenario being asked about).
7. **The post-1998 legitimate share-side substitutes** — (a) **Bed-and-ISA** — sell outside ISA wrapper, rebuy inside ISA wrapper. The acquirer is the ISA manager (a different beneficial holder); the s.106A "same person" gate does not engage. (b) **Bed-and-SIPP** — same logic for self-invested personal pensions. (c) **Bed-and-spouse** — per above. (d) **30-day-plus wait** — sell, wait >30 days, rebuy. The rule does not catch acquisitions outside the 30-day window. (e) **Same-day rule (s.105)** — same-day disposal + acquisition is matched first; doesn't help with bed-and-breakfasting. None of these substitutes have a direct property analog because direct property does not face the matching-rule problem in the first place.
8. **Where bed-and-breakfasting attempts on property can go wrong — Ramsay** — If a landlord attempts to engineer a composite series of property transactions designed to obtain a tax result (e.g. sell to a connected company, sell back, claim loss / restart base cost), the Ramsay-line case-law (W.T. Ramsay Ltd v IRC [1981] STC 174; Furniss v Dawson [1984] STC 153) catches the arrangement where it lacks commercial substance. HMRC may recharacterise the composite transaction or invoke the General Anti-Abuse Rule (GAAR; FA 2013 Part 5). Property-side bed-and-breakfasting is unusual but not unheard-of among taxpayers attempting to combine s.17 + s.58 movements within a family group; the page must warn against this without giving a how-to.
9. **The reduced annual exempt amount — why crystallisation is less valuable post-2023** — TCGA 1992 s.3 annual exempt amount was £12,300 in 2022/23, reduced to £6,000 in 2023/24 and to £3,000 from 6 April 2024 (verify against gov.uk at write time per §16.27). The reduction is the practical reason the share-side bed-and-breakfasting and its substitutes have lost most of their value: realising a £3,000-per-year gain inside the annual exemption barely moves the needle for a typical investor. For landlords, the same logic applies: structuring a disposal to use the annual exemption is far less attractive at £3,000 than at £12,300. Time-spread realisation (each spouse, each year, each within annual exemption) is the working strategy where families have appreciated portfolios; bed-and-breakfasting variants are not the lever they once were.
10. **When the question is really about something else — the trading-line trap** — A landlord who repeatedly buys and sells properties in short cycles, even at arm's length to third parties, sits in Part 8ZB / Part 9A territory (Wave 8 cluster + C8 sibling decision-guide). The frequency badge of trade (*Pickford v Quirke*) + the supplementary-work badge (*Marson v Morton*) catches the operator who looks too much like a flipper. The page should signal that **even legitimate sale-and-rebuy patterns can accumulate into a trading classification** if the volume grows. Forward-link to C8.

## Worked-example data (RUN session uses these as canvas)

### Example 1 — The classic share-side bed-and-breakfasting (historic)

Mr Patel, 1996, holds 1,000 shares in XYZ plc with base cost £5 each (£5,000) and current market value £3 each (£3,000). He wants to crystallise the £2,000 loss against his other CGT gains for 1995/96 BUT keep his economic position in XYZ.

- **Pre-FA-1998 routine:** Mr Patel sells the 1,000 shares on the open market in late March 1996, realising the £2,000 loss. The next morning (1 April 1996, after the broker's overnight stay) he buys 1,000 XYZ shares at £3 each, restoring his economic position. New base cost £3,000.
- **Tax outcome (pre-FA-1998):** £2,000 loss recognised in 1995/96, settable against general gains. Economic position unchanged. Holding period restarted.
- **Post-FA-1998 same facts:** TCGA 1992 s.106A(5) applies. The 1 April 1996 purchase (within 30 days of the late-March sale) IS matched against the sale. The loss is effectively cancelled; the new shares step into the original base cost as if no sale had occurred for matching purposes. The 1995/96 loss claim fails.

### Example 2 — The connected-persons trap (property side)

Mr Patel owns a BTL flat acquired 2015 for £200,000; current market value £400,000. Property market has dipped; current market value £350,000. He wants to sell to his daughter Priya for £200,000 to crystallise the loss (and pass on the property at a low base cost for his retirement planning).

- **The plan:** Mr Patel sells to Priya for £200,000 cash. Recognises £150,000 loss (£200k consideration vs £350k cost — but wait, that doesn't add up arithmetically; let me restate). Restating: Mr Patel acquired at £200k; current MV £350k; current low-point MV £250k (in a dip). He wants to sell to Priya at £180k to crystallise a £20k loss against other gains.
- **TCGA 1992 s.17 (deemed market value):** the disposal between connected persons (father / daughter — s.286(2)(a) covers lineal descendant) is treated as at market value REGARDLESS of actual consideration. The consideration on Mr Patel's CGT computation is £250k (MV), not £180k (cash paid). Gain computation: £250k − £200k = £50k GAIN, not the planned loss.
- **TCGA 1992 s.18 (loss ring-fence):** even if Mr Patel had structured to actually generate a loss between connected persons (e.g. property in a true negative-equity position), the loss would be RING-FENCED — settable only against future gains on disposals from Mr Patel to Priya specifically, NOT against Mr Patel's other gains.
- **Outcome:** the connected-persons sale-and-rebuy attempts to crystallise a loss against general gains DO NOT WORK at all. The s.17 + s.18 combination is the operative gate.

### Example 3 — The legitimate spouse-NGNL + onward-sale pattern

Mr Patel holds 10,000 shares in XYZ plc with base cost £15,000 (acquired 2018) and current market value £45,000. He is a higher-rate (40%) taxpayer; his wife Priya is below the higher-rate threshold and has unused CGT annual exemption.

- **Step 1 (s.58 transfer):** Mr Patel transfers all 10,000 shares to Priya under TCGA 1992 s.58 — no-gain-no-loss. Priya inherits the £15,000 base cost; Mr Patel has no CGT event.
- **Step 2 (open-market sale by Priya):** Priya sells 10,000 shares to a third party on the open market for £45,000. Gain: £45,000 − £15,000 = £30,000. Annual exempt amount £3,000 (2026/27) absorbs £3,000; balance £27,000 taxable. At Priya's lower-rate basic-rate 18% (assuming residential threshold not relevant for shares, but the share-side CGT rates would apply) — RUN session verifies current share-side CGT rates.
- **Why this works:** the s.106A 30-day matching rule operates on the SAME PERSON only. Priya is a different person from Mr Patel; Priya's sale is not matched against any subsequent purchase Mr Patel might make. The legitimate "bed-and-spouse" pattern for shares is alive and operational.
- **Property-side equivalent:** the same pattern is NOT replicated for property because the connected-persons s.17 + s.18 rules bite where the sale is to the spouse (s.286 covers spouses); the sale must be to a third party to escape s.17. The s.58 transfer + onward-sale-by-spouse pattern for shares does NOT have a clean property analog because shares can be repurchased from the open market (different physical instruments of same class), whereas the same physical property cannot easily be repurchased.

### Example 4 — Why the reduced annual exempt amount destroys the lever

Mr Patel holds appreciated BTL property; he and Mrs Patel co-own. Each year they plan to harvest capital gains by partial disposals within their combined annual exempt amounts.

- **2022/23 framework:** annual exempt amount £12,300 per person × 2 = £24,600 combined. Partial property disposal generating £24,600 gain would be fully covered by combined annual exemptions; £0 tax.
- **2026/27 framework:** annual exempt amount £3,000 per person × 2 = £6,000 combined. Same £24,600 partial-disposal target → £18,600 taxable gain. At residential 24% (rate per §5) = £4,464 tax.
- **Operational implication:** the post-2023 reduced exempt amount has destroyed most of the value of bed-and-breakfasting-adjacent annual-exemption-harvesting strategies for property landlords. Spreading disposals across tax years no longer materially reduces the tax bill; the planning lever has lost ~75% of its effectiveness.

## FAQ expansion (RUN session polishes prose; 10-12 FAQs target)

1. **Q: Can I bed-and-breakfast a property to crystallise a loss?**
   A: No — direct property is not caught by the 30-day matching rule (TCGA 1992 s.106A applies to SECURITIES only), but the practical traps are elsewhere. If you sell to a connected person (spouse, parent, sibling, company under common control), the s.17 deemed-market-value rule treats the disposal at MV regardless of actual consideration, and s.18 ring-fences any loss to future disposals to the SAME connected person only. If you attempt to sell on the open market and rebuy the same physical property, the second purchase needs a willing seller (rare), and a circular arrangement risks Ramsay-line recharacterisation.

2. **Q: What is the 30-day rule and when does it apply?**
   A: TCGA 1992 s.106A(5) sets the 30-day matching rule: if within 30 days after a disposal of securities the same person acquires securities of the SAME CLASS, the disposal is matched against the post-disposal purchase rather than against earlier-acquired stock. Applies to SECURITIES (shares, debentures, analogous instruments) — NOT to land or buildings. Operates as a layer in the share-identification waterfall (same-day s.105 → 30-day s.106A → s.104 pool); does NOT prohibit the trade, just changes the identification maths.

3. **Q: What happens if I sell to my spouse and they sell it on?**
   A: The s.58 inter-spouse transfer is at no-gain-no-loss (spouse acquires at your base cost). The spouse's onward sale is then a separate disposal, with the gain computed on the spouse's base cost (which equals your original base cost). For SHARES, this is the "bed-and-spouse" pattern — legitimate post-1998 substitute because the s.106A 30-day matching applies to the SAME PERSON only; the spouse is a different person. For PROPERTY, the same architecture is available IF the spouse subsequently sells to a THIRD PARTY (open market). Note: HMRC may apply Ramsay-line scrutiny to inter-spouse transfers immediately followed by onward-sale; document the commercial reasoning if the sequence is tax-driven.

4. **Q: Who counts as "connected" under TCGA 1992 s.286?**
   A: Spouses and civil partners; lineal ancestors and descendants (parents / grandparents / children / grandchildren); siblings; aunts / uncles / nieces / nephews via the broader s.286(4) "relative" definition; partnerships and their members; companies under common control (>50% ownership or voting); the same group of companies. Friends and business associates WITHOUT ownership / corporate control links are NOT connected. Cousins are NOT connected under the strict definition (s.286(4) lists specific relatives; cousins fall outside).

5. **Q: Does the 30-day rule apply if I rebuy through a company?**
   A: No — s.106A applies to the SAME PERSON only. A company is a different legal person from its shareholder. The historic "individual sells, individual's company buys" pattern escapes s.106A matching for the share-side. BUT: where the company is connected with the individual (>50% ownership), the disposal triggers s.17 (deemed market-value) and s.18 (loss ring-fence) on the SHARE-SIDE disposal — the connected-persons rules bite even though s.106A does not. Combined effect: the 30-day rule does not match, but the connected-persons rules ring-fence any loss.

6. **Q: What is the current CGT annual exempt amount?**
   A: £3,000 from 6 April 2024 per FA 2024 reduction (rate-by-reference per §16.27 — RUN session verifies at write). Compare £6,000 in 2023/24 and £12,300 in 2022/23. The reduction has materially reduced the value of crystallisation strategies that target the annual exempt amount; for landlords with appreciated property, year-by-year time-spread realisation is far less effective than pre-2023.

7. **Q: What is the legitimate "bed-and-ISA" pattern?**
   A: Sell shares outside the ISA wrapper, immediately repurchase the same class inside the ISA wrapper. The acquirer is the ISA manager (different beneficial holder), so the s.106A "same person" gate does NOT engage. Outside-ISA disposal realises any loss or gain; inside-ISA holding then grows tax-free. Same logic applies to bed-and-SIPP. NO property analog — direct property cannot be held in an ISA or SIPP wrapper (residential property is excluded from SIPP-eligible assets; ISAs hold only listed investments).

8. **Q: What if I wait 31 days between sale and rebuy?**
   A: The s.106A matching rule does NOT apply to acquisitions OUTSIDE the 30-day window. A sale followed by a rebuy >30 days later is matched under the standard share-pool architecture (s.104 pool); the loss or gain on the sale stands, and the rebuy establishes a new acquisition into the pool. The "30-day-plus wait" is the simplest legitimate post-1998 substitute for share-side bed-and-breakfasting. For property, the same logic applies in principle but is rarely operationally feasible (the same physical property does not typically come back to market 30+ days later).

9. **Q: Can I crystallise a property gain using my annual exemption by selling part of the property?**
   A: Partial disposals of land are capital events under TCGA 1992 — disposal of part of an asset is a disposal for CGT purposes. The annual exempt amount £3,000 (2026/27) absorbs gains up to that level. Practically, partial property disposals are unusual (selling a strip of garden, or a single flat from a portfolio); each requires legal conveyancing and is not as straightforward as crystallising a share-side gain. The reduced annual exempt amount further limits the value of the strategy.

10. **Q: How does Ramsay apply to property bed-and-breakfasting attempts?**
    A: The Ramsay anti-avoidance principle (*W.T. Ramsay Ltd v IRC* [1981] STC 174; *Furniss v Dawson* [1984] STC 153) catches composite transactions designed to obtain a tax result that lack commercial substance. Property-side bed-and-breakfasting attempts involving circular movement of value between connected persons (sell to connected co, sell back; multi-step inter-spouse transfers with onward-sale immediately) risk Ramsay-line recharacterisation. HMRC may also invoke the General Anti-Abuse Rule (GAAR; FA 2013 Part 5). Document commercial reasoning contemporaneously; do not engineer multi-step sequences solely for the tax result.

11. **Q: What is the s.18 loss-ring-fence and why does it matter?**
    A: TCGA 1992 s.18 provides that losses realised on disposals to CONNECTED PERSONS are RING-FENCED — they can only be set against future gains on disposals to the SAME connected person, not against general gains. Operative effect: a landlord cannot crystallise a property loss against general gains by selling to a connected party (spouse, parent, family company). The connected-party route to loss-crystallisation is closed by s.18.

12. **Q: Does repeated buying and selling of property make me a trader?**
    A: Potentially yes. A landlord who repeatedly buys and sells properties in short cycles — even at arm's length to third parties — sits in CTA 2010 Part 8ZB / ITA 2007 Part 9A territory (the C8 sibling decision-guide covers this in depth). The frequency badge of trade (*Pickford v Quirke* (1927) 13 TC 251) plus the supplementary-work badge (*Marson v Morton* [1986] 1 WLR 1343) catches the operator who looks too much like a flipper. Even legitimate sale-and-rebuy patterns can accumulate into a trading classification if the volume grows.
