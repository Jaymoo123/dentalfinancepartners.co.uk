# The Benefits of Participating in HMRC's Let Property Campaign: A Decision-Layer Guide for Residential Landlords

## Statutory anchor

- **Primary — failure-to-notify framework (the alternative path if LPC is NOT used):** Schedule 41 FA 2008 — penalties for failure to notify chargeability. Sch 41 para 5 (behaviour categories), Sch 41 para 13 (disclosure-mitigation matrix), Sch 41 para 20 (reasonable-excuse defence). House-position-locked at §27.3 with verification 2026-05-24. Without LPC the landlord faces these maxima: non-deliberate 30% Cat 1 / 45% Cat 2 / 60% Cat 3; deliberate-not-concealed 70% / 105% / 140%; deliberate-and-concealed 100% / 150% / 200%.
- **Primary — disclosure obligation underlying the failure:** Taxes Management Act 1970 section 7 — notice of liability to income tax + CGT within 6 months of the end of the tax year. Verified live 2026-05-27 at `https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1970/9/section/7`. Missing this is the failure that Sch 41 penalises.
- **Primary — discovery + extended-time-limit assessment (what HMRC does if you don't use LPC):** TMA 1970 s.29 (discovery threshold) + s.34 (ordinary 4 years) + s.36(1) (careless 6 years) + s.36(1A) (deliberate 20 years). House-position-locked at §27.1 with verification 2026-05-24 against the legislation.gov.uk current-as-amended text. For offshore matters s.36A FA 2019 gives HMRC 12 years even without behaviour element.
- **Supporting — inaccuracy penalties (where SA returns were filed but inaccurate):** Schedule 24 FA 2007 (where the landlord filed SA returns but understated rental income — different statutory hook from a never-notified scenario). Sch 24 para 4A offshore Category uplift.
- **Supporting — interest on unpaid tax:** TMA 1970 s.86 (interest on income tax + CGT); FA 2009 s.101 (general HMRC interest framework). Interest is not penalty but accrues continuously and is NOT mitigated by LPC.
- **Supporting — LPC operational anchor:** HMRC published guidance at gov.uk/guidance/let-property-campaign. Campaign open since 9 September 2013 with no announced end date (per §27.6 lock).
- **House position reference:** §27.6 (LPC + WDF + DDS + CoP9 route choice — primary anchor); §27.3 (Sch 41 unprompted-disclosure mechanics — the mitigation engine LPC unlocks); §27.1 (TMA 1970 s.29 / s.36 / s.36A — the counterfactual HMRC-discovery pathway); §27.5 (CoP9 boundary — LPC is NOT appropriate where deliberate-conduct criminal-prosecution exposure exists). No NEW HP LOCK NEEDED.

## Worked-example data (RUN session uses these as canvas)

### Example 1 — Single landlord with £40k undisclosed across 4 years (the headline LPC vs counterfactual)

- **Mr Larchwood** owns a single BTL flat in Birmingham let since 2021. Gross rents £14,000/year; mortgage interest + agent fees + repairs leave ~£10,000 net rental profit. He has never declared the income (overlooked because he assumed the agent or his employer-PAYE picked it up). Four tax years undisclosed (2021/22 + 2022/23 + 2023/24 + 2024/25); total net rental profit ~£40,000. Marginal income tax rate: basic 20% (income otherwise within Personal Allowance + Primary Threshold band).
- **LPC route under Sch 41 unprompted floor:**
  - Tax: £40,000 × 20% = **£8,000 income tax owed**.
  - Interest under TMA 1970 s.86: cumulative ~£1,000 over the 4-year arrears period (rough estimate at current 7.5%/7.75% HMRC interest rate; RUN session re-verifies rate at write time).
  - Sch 41 para 13 unprompted floor: **non-deliberate after 12 months = 10%**. Penalty: 10% × £8,000 = **£800**.
  - **LPC total settlement: £9,800.**
- **Counterfactual (no LPC, HMRC discovery via nudge letter under §27):**
  - Tax: £8,000 (same).
  - Interest: £1,000 (same).
  - Sch 41 para 13 prompted floor: **non-deliberate prompted = 35%**. Penalty: 35% × £8,000 = **£2,800**.
  - **Counterfactual total: £11,800.**
- **LPC benefit: £2,000 saved on penalty + closure of exposure for s.29 discovery purposes + avoidance of nudge-letter follow-up cycle and the s.36 6-year reach for older years.**

### Example 2 — Multi-property landlord with offshore property income (LPC NOT eligible; WDF + FtC route)

- **Mrs Chetwood** owns 3 properties in Portugal let since 2014. UK-resident and UK-domiciled. Has never declared the Portuguese rental on UK return (assumed Portuguese withholding was final). Gross Portuguese rents €30,000/year for 11 years.
- **Routing decision:** LPC is residential-UK-only. Mrs Chetwood's offshore rentals route via WDF (Worldwide Disclosure Facility) inside the DDS umbrella, with the FtC overlay biting hard on pre-30-September-2018 years.
- **Cost projection:** for pre-September-2018 years (2014-2018), Sch 18 FA 2017 FtC minimum 200% penalty applies, reducible to 100% on full unprompted disclosure. For post-September-2018 years, standard Sch 41 unprompted floor applies (10% non-deliberate after 12 months). Asset-based penalty (up to 10% of property value) may apply where lost tax exceeds £25,000 in any tax year.
- **Why this matters in an LPC-benefits page:** the page must clearly flag that LPC does NOT cover offshore property income; landlords like Mrs Chetwood must use WDF instead and face a materially higher cost-benefit profile than the LPC route Mr Larchwood used in Example 1.

### Example 3 — Cost-of-NOT-disclosing: nudge letter triggering prompted floor

- **Mrs Helmesley** owns 2 BTL properties; 3 tax years undisclosed with ~£18,000/year net rental profit. Total undisclosed profit £54,000; basic-rate tax £10,800. She has been considering LPC for 6 months but not yet acted. HMRC issues a §27-cluster nudge letter ("Our records suggest you may have rental income — please review and respond within 60 days").
- **Nudge-letter consequence:** receipt of the nudge letter does NOT automatically convert the disclosure to "prompted" for Sch 41 purposes; HMRC operational practice is that the nudge letter is informal and a disclosure made in response can still be unprompted IF made within the nudge-letter response window AND HMRC has not yet opened a formal enquiry under s.9A or issued a s.29 assessment.
- **Discipline:** Mrs Helmesley should respond to the nudge letter by notifying LPC within the response window. If she delays past the response window and HMRC opens a formal enquiry, the disclosure becomes prompted (35% Sch 41 floor instead of 10%, doubling the penalty on the same £10,800 tax).
- **Page framing:** the cost-of-NOT-disclosing section should walk this exact decision: nudge letter received → notify LPC within window → preserve unprompted floor → settle within 90 days. Delay risks £1,000-£3,000 extra penalty per £10k of underlying tax.

### Example 4 — Joint-property under Form 17 with one spouse using LPC

- **Mr and Mrs Pickford** own a joint BTL property at 75% Mr / 25% Mrs under a Form 17 election made in 2019. The 75% share Mr Pickford → £15,000/year net rental profit; the 25% share Mrs Pickford → £5,000/year. Mr Pickford declared his share each year; Mrs Pickford did not (assumed her share was de minimis below the trading allowance + property allowance).
- **Trading + property allowance interaction:** the £1,000 property allowance does NOT cover £5,000 net profit. Mrs Pickford has a notification obligation under TMA 1970 s.7 for each year that her net rental profit exceeded the allowance.
- **LPC route for Mrs Pickford:** she alone uses LPC for her 25% share (4 tax years × £5,000 net = £20,000 net rental profit, basic-rate tax £4,000, Sch 41 unprompted 10% floor → £400 penalty + interest). Mr Pickford's return is already complete and accurate; he does not need to use LPC.
- **Page framing:** joint-property situations under Form 17 election can produce uneven-disclosure scenarios where only one spouse needs LPC. The benefits-quantification section should cover the part-disclosure pattern in addition to the headline single-landlord case.

## FAQ expansion (RUN session polishes prose; 10-12 FAQs target)

1. **Q: What is the headline benefit of using the Let Property Campaign instead of waiting for HMRC?**
   A: The unprompted-disclosure floor under Schedule 41 FA 2008 paragraph 13 is materially lower than the prompted-disclosure floor that HMRC would apply if they discover the undeclared income first. For non-deliberate failures, the unprompted floor is 0% within 12 months / 10% after 12 months; the prompted equivalent is 10% / 35%. For a landlord with several years of undisclosed rental income, that is typically a 2-5x difference in the penalty bill.

2. **Q: What is the closure-of-exposure benefit of an accepted LPC disclosure?**
   A: Once HMRC accepts a complete LPC disclosure and the liability is paid, the disclosed matter is closed for discovery purposes under TMA 1970 s.29, subject to the s.29(4)/(5) re-opening conditions (incomplete disclosure or deliberate inaccuracy can be re-opened). The landlord gains certainty: HMRC cannot re-open the disclosed years absent fresh evidence of incompleteness or deliberate misrepresentation in the disclosure itself.

3. **Q: What is the cost-of-NOT-using-LPC counterfactual that landlords should weigh?**
   A: Three layers. First, the prompted-disclosure Sch 41 floor (10% non-deliberate / 35% deliberate-not-concealed / 50% deliberate-and-concealed) replaces the unprompted floor. Second, HMRC's s.36 extended assessment reach catches 6 years (careless) or 20 years (deliberate) of undeclared income; s.36A FA 2019 reaches 12 years for offshore innocent-error. Third, interest under TMA 1970 s.86 continues to accrue at the official rate throughout, and HMRC nudge-letter pathways under §27 frequently precede formal assessment, eroding the unprompted floor before the landlord acts.

4. **Q: Can limited companies use the Let Property Campaign?**
   A: No. LPC is restricted to individual landlords (UK-resident or non-resident) and certain trustees who hold rental income personally. Limited companies with undisclosed Corporation Tax exposure use the general DDS catch-all route (or CoP9 / CDF for deliberate-conduct cases). The page covers LPC eligibility explicitly so LtdCo operators are signposted to the correct alternative.

5. **Q: Can trustees use LPC?**
   A: LPC is open to certain trustees holding rental income personally (typically bare trusts or simple income-only trusts). Complex trust structures (discretionary trusts, accumulation-and-maintenance trusts) generally use the general DDS catch-all route or specialist disclosure routes depending on facts. The Trust Registration Service obligations under §22.16 sit alongside any disclosure step.

6. **Q: What about non-resident landlords with UK property income — can they use LPC?**
   A: Yes. Non-resident landlords (NRL) with UK residential rental income are eligible for LPC. The interaction with the NRL scheme (gross or net election; 20% withholding by letting agent or tenant absent NRL1 approval) is operational rather than a disclosure-route bar. Non-resident landlords with undisclosed UK rental still notify LPC via the same gov.uk landlord disclosure form.

7. **Q: My rental property is offshore — can I still use LPC?**
   A: No. LPC is UK-property-only. Landlords with offshore property income use the Worldwide Disclosure Facility (WDF) inside the DDS umbrella, with the Schedule 18 FA 2017 Failure-to-Correct overlay biting for pre-30-September-2018 matters. The WDF cost-benefit profile is materially different from LPC because of the FtC 200%/100% minimum.

8. **Q: What is the reasonable excuse defence sitting alongside LPC?**
   A: Schedule 41 FA 2008 paragraph 20 disapplies the failure-to-notify penalty where the taxpayer had a reasonable excuse and rectified the failure within a reasonable time of the excuse ceasing. Perrin v HMRC [2018] UKUT 156 sets out the four-stage test. Where reasonable excuse applies, the penalty falls to nil. LPC mitigation is the floor in the absence of reasonable excuse; the two defences operate independently.

9. **Q: What happens if I cannot pay the disclosed liability in full within 90 days?**
   A: HMRC will typically agree a time-to-pay arrangement under TMA 1970 s.108 + CRCA 2005 where the landlord demonstrates inability to pay in full. Interest under TMA 1970 s.86 continues to accrue at the official rate during the time-to-pay period. The disclosure closes on agreed-figure basis and the instalment arrangement runs alongside; the closure-of-exposure benefit is not blocked by time-to-pay.

10. **Q: What records do I need to support my LPC disclosure?**
    A: Rental statements, agent invoices, mortgage statements, repair invoices, tenancy agreements, bank statements showing rental receipts. The statutory record-retention floor under TMA 1970 s.12B is 5 years from 31 January following the tax year. Where records are missing for older years, the landlord should make a reasonable best-estimate disclosure with documented assumptions; HMRC has discretion to accept reasonable estimates absent contemporaneous records, although missing records may shift the disclosure into a longer review cycle.

11. **Q: Does LPC confer criminal-prosecution immunity?**
    A: No. LPC is a civil-resolution mechanism. Only the Contractual Disclosure Facility (CDF) under Code of Practice 9 confers criminal-prosecution immunity for the conduct described in the formal admission. Where deliberate fraud is involved with material criminal exposure, the correct route is CoP9 / CDF, not LPC. The boundary matters: a landlord with deliberate-and-concealed exposure should take specialist advice before notifying LPC.

12. **Q: What is the forward path after my LPC disclosure is complete?**
    A: The disclosed years are closed (subject to s.29(4)/(5)). Continuing rental income from the disclosed period onwards must be brought into the regular self-assessment cycle. If the landlord's qualifying income exceeds the MTD ITSA threshold from 6 April 2026 (£50k phase 1), 6 April 2027 (£30k phase 2), or 6 April 2028 (£20k phase 3) per §19.1 lock, the cycle moves to MTD ITSA. Record-keeping discipline under TMA 1970 s.12B continues regardless of the filing mode.
