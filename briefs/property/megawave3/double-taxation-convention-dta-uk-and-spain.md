# UK-Spain Double Taxation Convention: what Spanish-resident landlords of UK property and emigrating UK landlords face

## Statutory anchor
- **Primary (treaty):** 2013 UK-Spain Double Taxation Convention, signed 14 March 2013, in force 12 June 2014. Effective UK withholding taxes 12 June 2014; UK corporation tax 1 April 2015; UK income tax and capital gains tax 6 April 2015. Modified by the Multilateral Instrument (MLI) — UK effective dates 1 January 2023 (withheld taxes), 1 April 2023 (UK CT), 6 April 2023 (UK income tax + CGT). Published at gov.uk "Spain: tax treaties". Follows OECD Model 2017 form.
- **Primary (UK domestic — overrides treaty for UK situs property):**
  - **ITTOIA 2005 Part 3** (UK property income, individuals).
  - **CTA 2009 Part 4** (UK property income, companies).
  - **TCGA 1992 s.1A + Sch 1A / 1B / 4AA** (NRCGT — Finance Act 2019 rewrite).
  - **FA 1995 Sch 23 + SI 1995/2902** (Non-Resident Landlord scheme — statutory withholding).
  - **TIOPA 2010 s.18 + s.130** (foreign tax credit relief framework).
- **Supporting:** OECD Model 2017 Commentary on Articles 6 / 13 / 4 / 23; Spanish *Ley del Impuesto sobre el Patrimonio* (wealth tax — Spain-side credit framework only).
- **House position reference:** §10 (DTAs general), §16 (DTAs Wave 2 extension), §16.5 (UK-Spain treaty 2013: Art 13(4) property-rich entity rule applies; Spanish wealth tax creditable in Spain only — UK has no wealth tax to credit), §16.6 (NRL is statutory, not treaty), §17.4 (NRCGT regime), §17.5 (NRL operational mechanics).

## Framing differentiator (anti-templating, anti-cannibalisation)
This page is the **UK-Spain bilateral specific** landlord page — narrow scope, deep mechanics. It is NOT the general "what is a DTA" intro (B20 `dont-pay-twice-an-introduction-to-tax-treaties` covers that) and it is NOT the abstract OECD-model walkthrough. The whole page sits in the Spain bilateral posture: a Spanish-resident individual or a Spanish incorporated company holding UK BTL property, OR a UK-resident landlord who has emigrated to Spain and continues to let UK property. The framing differentiator is the **two-way credit asymmetry**: UK retains primary taxing rights over UK property (Art 6) and UK property gains (Art 13(1) + Art 13(4) indirect), Spain credits UK tax paid under its tax-credit method, AND Spanish wealth tax on UK-property holdings is creditable only on the Spanish side. UK landlords who think the treaty exempts them from UK SA self-assessment are wrong; Spanish landlords who think Spanish wealth tax is mirrored on the UK side are also wrong. Sibling pages B1 (UK-IoM) and B3 (UK-India) take parallel form but with state-specific notes; cross-links go between the three. Existing site page `uk-spain-dta-property-uk-resident-spanish-holiday-home` covers the OPPOSITE direction (UK-resident with Spanish holiday home) — this page is the INBOUND direction (Spanish-resident with UK property) + the UK-to-Spain emigrant case, with bidirectional forward-link.

## Key questions this page must answer
1. Does the 2013 UK-Spain Convention exempt a Spanish-resident landlord from UK tax on UK rental income? (No — Art 6 allocates source-state taxing rights to the UK; UK domestic charges under ITTOIA 2005 Part 3 / CTA 2009 Part 4 apply.)
2. Does the treaty displace the NRL scheme? (No — NRL is statutory under FA 1995 Sch 23 + SI 1995/2902. Spanish-resident landlords still apply for NRL1 gross-payment approval.)
3. How does Art 13(4) apply on disposal of shares in a UK property-rich entity by a Spanish resident? (Yes — UK retains taxing rights on shares in property-rich entities; NRCGT regime under TCGA 1992 s.1A + Sch 1A applies; 60-day return required.)
4. How does Art 4 tie-breaker apply where an individual is dual-resident under UK SRT and Spanish 183-day / *centro de intereses vitales* rule? (Cascade: permanent home, centre of vital interests, habitual abode, nationality, mutual agreement.)
5. How does Spanish wealth tax (impuesto sobre el patrimonio) interact with UK property? (Creditable against Spanish wealth tax in Spain only; UK has no symmetric wealth tax, so no UK-side credit. Stage 2 verifies current Spanish band thresholds and regional variations — e.g. Madrid 100% bonification.)
6. How does the MLI modification (effective from 2023) affect the 2013 Convention in practice? (Principal purpose test imported; treaty-abuse provisions strengthened. Stage 2 verifies the specific MLI Articles applied to UK-Spain.)
7. How does the FIG regime under ITTOIA 2005 ss.845A-845J (FA 2025 s.37) apply to a Spanish citizen newly resident in the UK with Spanish property income? (Standard 4-year FIG window; Spanish property income is foreign income within s.845H categories — Stage 2 verifies verbatim list at write time.)
8. How does s.10A TCGA 1992 temporary non-residence apply to a UK-resident landlord who moves to Spain for 4 years then returns? (Gain recapture in return year on UK assets owned at departure — UK land already in NRCGT, so s.10A primarily catches non-UK situs gains. The 5-year-or-less window is the trigger.)
9. Are Spanish companies inside or outside UK ATED? (ATED applies to non-natural persons including non-UK companies; a Spanish *sociedad limitada* holding a UK dwelling worth more than £500,000 is in scope — cross-reference §18 + Bucket B ATED picks.)
10. How does the new long-term residence IHT test (IHTA 1984 ss.6A-6C + ss.267ZC-267ZF, FA 2025 architecture) apply to a UK-domiciled-of-origin emigrant to Spain? (10-of-20 residence test; the tail period for IHT exposure can extend up to 10 years post-departure depending on the individual's prior UK-residence years; cross-reference §22.X.)

### Example 1 — The two-way credit asymmetry table (the page's first concrete artefact)

Render as semantic HTML table; this is the trap-anchor for both Spanish-resident and emigrating-UK readers:

| Item | UK source | Spain source | UK side outcome | Spain side outcome |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| UK rental income, Spanish-resident landlord | YES | NO | UK tax under ITTOIA 2005 Part 3; NRL withholding 20% unless NRL1 | Spanish tax with FTC for UK tax paid (Art 23 credit method) |
| Spanish holiday-home rental income, UK-resident landlord | NO | YES | UK tax on worldwide income (arising basis); FTC for Spanish tax paid (TIOPA 2010 s.18) | Spanish non-resident tax under Spanish IRNR |
| UK property gain on disposal, Spanish-resident seller | YES | NO | NRCGT under TCGA 1992 s.1A; 60-day return; 18% / 24% residential | Spanish tax with FTC for UK CGT paid |
| Spanish property gain on disposal, UK-resident seller | NO | YES | UK CGT on worldwide gains; FTC for Spanish CGT paid | Spanish CGT under IRNR / IRPF |
| Spanish wealth tax on UK property holdings | N/A | Spain charges Spanish wealth tax on worldwide assets (subject to regional bonification) | UK has no wealth tax — no UK side mirror | Spanish wealth tax (impuesto sobre el patrimonio) applies; credit for any equivalent foreign wealth tax — but there is no UK wealth tax to credit |
| UK ATED on Spanish-incorporated company's UK dwelling >£500,000 | YES | NO | ATED under FA 2013 Part 3 (non-resident company in scope) | N/A for ATED — Spanish company side has no equivalent |

Operational anchor: the 2013 treaty allocates taxing rights to source state for immovable property (Art 6 + Art 13). Both sides operate FTC on their resident side. The asymmetry that catches Spanish-resident clients: Spanish wealth tax has NO UK mirror — you cannot crystallise a UK credit for Spanish wealth tax paid because UK does not impose wealth tax on UK property.

### Example 2 — Sr Hernández, Spanish-resident individual landlord (the most common scenario)

Sr Hernández (Madrid-resident throughout 2026/27; UK-non-resident under SRT; Spanish citizen) owns a Bristol BTL flat let to a tenant. Annual gross rent £16,200; agent's fees £1,620; mortgage interest £3,800; service charge + repairs £1,900. Position:
- **Treaty:** UK has primary taxing rights under Art 6.
- **NRL:** Letting agent withholds 20% (£3,240/year) unless Sr Hernández holds NRL1 approval. He applies for NRL1 on instructing the agent.
- **UK Self-Assessment:** Sr Hernández must register for SA + file SA100 + SA105 annually. Rental income £16,200 minus deductible expenses (excluding mortgage interest, restricted to 20% basic-rate tax credit under ITA 2007 s.274A per §4) = taxable property profit £12,680; mortgage-interest tax credit £760 (20% of £3,800) reduces UK income tax. At basic-rate UK tax on £12,680 = £2,536; minus £760 credit = £1,776 UK income tax due.
- **NRL withholding offset:** £3,240 withheld appears on NRL6; credited against UK SA liability. UK refund due: £1,464.
- **Personal allowance:** Spanish nationals retain UK personal allowance under domestic law (UK-EU citizens; verify post-Brexit framework currency at write — the personal allowance for EEA nationals is preserved per ITA 2007 s.56 + the residual UK-Spain treaty Art 24 non-discrimination if domestic rule shifts).
- **Spanish side:** Sr Hernández reports the UK rental on his Spanish IRPF return. Under the 2013 Convention Art 23 (credit method), Spain gives FTC for UK income tax paid (£1,776) against any Spanish IRPF on the same income. The Spanish IRPF on foreign property income runs to marginal-rate scales (currently 19% / 24% / 30% / 37% / 45% / 47% by income band — verify with Spanish *Agencia Tributaria* at write). If Spanish marginal rate exceeds UK effective rate, residual Spanish tax is payable; if not, no residual.
- **Common misframing:** "Como soy residente fiscal en España, el alquiler en Reino Unido no se grava allí." FALSO — Art 6 attribuye derechos al estado fuente (UK).

### Example 3 — Sra Vázquez, Madrid-resident, UK property + the wealth-tax asymmetry

Sra Vázquez (Madrid-resident; high-net-worth; UK-non-resident under SRT) owns a £4.2m central-London townhouse held in her own name + a £1.6m Brighton flat let to a tenant. Spanish wealth tax position:
- **Spanish *impuesto sobre el patrimonio*:** Spanish residents are liable on worldwide assets (including UK property at market value). Standard state-level scale runs from 0.2% to 3.5% across bands; the personal exemption is around €700,000 + €300,000 for principal residence (verify at write). Both UK properties enter Sra Vázquez's Spanish wealth tax base.
- **Madrid regional bonification:** Madrid applies a 100% bonification on the wealth tax (effectively zero state-level wealth tax for Madrid residents per Comunidad de Madrid policy — politically contested; verify currency at write).
- **Solidaridad de las Grandes Fortunas (SGF):** The temporary Spanish "Solidarity Tax on Large Fortunes" enacted Ley 38/2022 imposes an additional state-level wealth charge above €3m + €700,000 exemption — Sra Vázquez's £4.2m London townhouse alone pushes her over the SGF threshold. The Madrid regional bonification does NOT eliminate SGF (SGF was specifically designed to override regional bonifications). Verify at write — SGF status and rate framework politically contested.
- **UK side:** UK has no wealth tax. There is NOTHING to credit on the UK side for Spanish wealth tax / SGF paid. The Spanish-side wealth tax is structurally one-way.
- **UK IHT:** Sra Vázquez's UK property is within UK IHT regardless of her LTR status per §22.X.4 (UK situs property is always in UK IHT). Worked: at her death the £4.2m London townhouse and £1.6m Brighton flat are in her UK IHT estate; NRB £325k + RNRB up to £175k (with taper at £2m+); IHT at 40% on the rest. Total UK IHT exposure on UK property roughly £2.1m before reliefs / spousal exemption. Spanish *impuesto sobre sucesiones y donaciones* may also apply (Spain-side inheritance tax — heavily devolved; Madrid 99% bonification; other regions higher).
- **Operational implication:** the structurally one-way wealth-tax dynamic means UK-property holding cannot be wealth-tax-neutral for Spanish residents. The IHT cross-exposure is the other end of the same problem.

### Example 4 — Art 4 tie-breaker for a dual-resident individual

Sra Romero (born in Madrid; UK consulting work for a London firm; spent 178 UK days in 2026/27; rents a flat in Battersea + retains the family home in Madrid; husband + adult children in Madrid). SRT analysis: 178 UK days = within sufficient-ties test; 4 ties present (accommodation, work, 90-day, family) → UK resident. Spanish domestic law: >183 Spain days + centre of vital interests in Spain → Spanish resident. Dual-resident. Art 4 tie-breaker:
- **(a) Permanent home:** Available in both → does not resolve.
- **(b) Centre of vital interests:** Family + economic + personal closer to Madrid (husband, children, principal residence, social ties) → resolves to Spain. Sra Romero is treaty-resident in Spain.
- **(c)** + **(d)** not reached.
- **Effect:** UK retains source-state taxing rights on her UK consulting income (Art 14 — independent personal services; or Art 7 if PE) and on any UK property income / gains (Art 6 / 13). For non-treaty UK purposes (e.g. SA registration, NRL withholding on any UK property) she remains UK-resident under SRT.

### Example 5 — UK landlord emigrates to Spain + the s.10A 5-year trap

Mr Brown (UK-resident throughout 2018/19 to 2025/26; was UK-resident in 4 or more of the 7 tax years before departure → satisfies s.10A "preceding 4-of-7" test). He moves to Marbella in June 2026, intending to settle permanently. He retains his Manchester BTL flat (let throughout). In 2028/29, while Spanish-resident, he sells a Spanish-purchased portfolio of non-UK shares for a £180,000 gain. In 2030/31, after 4 complete tax years non-UK-resident (2026/27 to 2029/30), he returns to UK due to family reasons.
- **s.10A position per §17.3:** Mr Brown's period of non-UK residence was 4 complete tax years — within the "5 years or less" temporary-non-residence window. He had been UK-resident in 4+ of the 7 preceding tax years. He is therefore a "temporary non-resident".
- **Effect:** the £180,000 gain on the non-UK shares disposed during non-residence is **deemed to arise in the year of return** (2030/31) and is chargeable to UK CGT in that year. The 5-year clock is calendar-tax-year, not rolling — he needs more than 5 complete tax years non-UK-resident to escape recapture.
- **UK property gain — separate analysis:** Mr Brown's Manchester BTL is in NRCGT throughout his non-residence (TCGA 1992 s.1A). If he sells it while non-resident, 60-day NRCGT return + UK CGT at 18%/24% applies — no s.10A overlay (UK land is already in NRCGT regardless of return).
- **Spanish side:** Spain taxes him on the £180,000 gain in 2028/29 under Spanish CGT (he is Spanish-resident at the time of disposal). The Spanish CGT he pays in 2028/29 is creditable against the UK CGT charged in 2030/31 under the 2013 Convention Art 23 — but only the lower of the two amounts is the operative credit. If Spanish CGT exceeds UK CGT on the same gain, the excess Spanish CGT is not recoverable from UK side (FTC is one-way; UK does not refund foreign tax against UK tax that is lower).
- **Planning lesson:** to break the s.10A trap, Mr Brown needs more than 5 complete tax years non-UK-resident. A 4-year stint with planned return crystallises the recapture risk.

### Example 6 — Spanish *sociedad limitada* holding UK BTL + ATED

Inversiones Ibérica SL (Spanish *sociedad limitada*; Spanish-resident company; UK-non-resident) buys a London BTL flat in 2026 for £780,000 (above £500,000 ATED threshold). Annual rental £36,000 to an unconnected tenant. Position:
- **NRL:** Letting agent withholds 20% (£7,200/year) unless Inversiones Ibérica holds NRL2 approval.
- **UK CT on rental:** Inversiones Ibérica is within UK CT under CTA 2009 Part 4. Rental profit after deductible expenses taxed at UK CT rates per §21.A (likely SPR at 19% if augmented profits ≤£50k). CIHC carve-out at s.18N(2)(b) applies (commercial letting to unconnected tenant) → SPR preserved.
- **ATED per §18:** UK dwelling worth £780,000 → £500,001-£1m band → 2026/27 charge £4,600 (verify §18.1 at write). Inversiones Ibérica files ATED return by 30 April 2026 + pays £4,600. The s.133 property-rental-business relief is available (commercial letting to unconnected tenant) — claim on return; failing to file even where relief applies triggers Schedule 55 FA 2009 penalty points + exposure to the OTM letters campaign per §18.7.
- **NRCGT on disposal:** When sold (say 2032 for £960,000; gain £180,000), 60-day NRCGT return required; UK CT on chargeable gain at prevailing CT rate. ATED-CGT abolished from 6 April 2019 per §18.5.
- **MLI principal-purpose test (PPT):** the MLI imports a PPT into the 2013 Convention from 1 January 2023. If the principal purpose of any treaty-benefit claim by Inversiones Ibérica appears to HMRC to be obtaining the benefit (rather than commercial substance), treaty benefits can be denied. For the standard commercial-letting Spanish *SL* structure with genuine economic activity in Spain, PPT is typically not engaged — but treaty-shopping structures (Spanish SL inserted purely for treaty access) are at risk.
- **Spanish side:** the Spanish SL is within Spanish corporate income tax on its worldwide income. UK CT paid on UK rental is creditable in Spain under Art 23 and Spanish domestic CFC + FTC rules. The structural Spanish-incorporation route typically yields no UK-tax saving on rental + gain; the structural reasons are usually Spanish-side commercial / fiduciary / asset-protection driven.

### Example 7 — FIG regime for a Spanish citizen arriving in UK

Sra Castro (Spanish citizen; resident in Madrid through tax year 2025/26; never previously UK-resident; not previously claimed FIG / remittance basis) moves to London in September 2026 for a new UK employment. She becomes UK-resident under SRT from 2026/27. She retains a Madrid rental flat generating €18,000/year rental.
- **FIG qualifying new resident test (s.845B(1) per §17.8):** (a) UK-resident 2026/27 → yes; (b) NOT UK-resident in any of preceding 10 years → yes; (c) not previously disqualified → yes; (d) over age 10 at start of 2026/27 → yes. **She qualifies.**
- **Duration:** Up to 4 tax years (2026/27 to 2029/30). Per-year claim under s.845A.
- **Coverage:** Spanish rental income is foreign property income within s.845H categories. If she claims FIG for 2026/27, the Spanish €18,000 rental is exempt from UK tax for the year.
- **Cost:** Claiming FIG forfeits her UK personal allowance + dividend allowance + CGT AEA for 2026/27 (same architecture as historic remittance basis). The arithmetic: she must compare lost UK personal allowance (£12,570 × 20% = £2,514 of UK tax shielded if she didn't claim) against the UK tax on £18,000 foreign rental that the claim avoids (£18,000 × 20% basic-rate = £3,600 at most, less Spanish IRNR / IRPF FTC). For modestly-sized foreign income, the personal-allowance loss can outweigh the FIG benefit.
- **Claim deadline:** 31 January 2028 for the 2026/27 claim per s.845A + §17.8.
- **MLI consideration:** treaty-residence under Art 4 for the part-year 2026/27 may be relevant — if she is Spanish-treaty-resident at the start of the year and UK-treaty-resident from the move, Spanish wealth tax on UK property continues to apply to the Spanish-side period; FIG does not override Spanish taxation of her Spanish-source income (FIG is a UK-side relief only).

### Example 8 — UK-domiciled-of-origin emigrant to Spain + the new IHT LTR test

Mr Wright (UK-born; UK-domicile of origin; UK-resident throughout 2008/09 to 2025/26 — 18 of preceding 20 tax years). He moves to Valencia in 2026 with the intent to settle permanently. He retains a UK BTL portfolio.
- **Under pre-FA-2025 architecture (no longer in force):** he would remain UK-domiciled for IHT until 4+ tax years non-UK-resident under IHTA 1984 s.267(1)(b), then domicile of choice in Spain might displace UK domicile (subject to common-law domicile-of-choice tests — high evidential bar).
- **Under FA 2025 LTR regime per §22.X (now in force from 6 April 2025):** Mr Wright is a long-term UK resident — UK-resident in 18 of the previous 20 tax years (well above the 10-of-20 threshold). On departure he remains LTR for the IHT-tail-period in §22.X.2 — for 18 prior UK-resident years, the required consecutive non-UK tax years to lose LTR is **8 years**. His IHT exposure to worldwide assets extends until tax year 2034/35 at the earliest (8 complete non-UK years).
- **Effect:** Mr Wright's Spanish-acquired Valencia property is within UK IHT (worldwide assets) for at least 8 tax years post-departure. His UK BTL portfolio is in UK IHT throughout regardless of LTR status (UK situs).
- **Pre-FA-2025 advice now wrong:** any pre-2025 planning that assumed "stay non-UK 3+ years to escape UK IHT" is obsolete for individuals with 14+ prior UK-resident years per the §22.X.2 table.
- **Spanish-side IHT:** Spain has its own *impuesto sobre sucesiones y donaciones* (heavily devolved by region; Madrid 99% bonification; other regions higher). UK IHT paid on the same assets is creditable against Spanish IHT under the 2013 Convention Art 23 (where applicable) and Spanish domestic law.

### Example 9 — Misframings the page must correct (verbatim do-not-write list)

- **Misframe 1:** "The 2013 UK-Spain Convention exempts Spanish-residents from UK tax on UK rental income." FALSE — Art 6 allocates source-state taxing rights to UK; UK domestic charge under ITTOIA 2005 / CTA 2009 applies; Spain gives FTC.
- **Misframe 2:** "Spanish-resident landlords don't need to operate NRL withholding because the treaty disapplies it." FALSE — NRL is statutory per §16.6; tenant / agent must withhold 20% regardless of treaty residence.
- **Misframe 3:** "Spanish wealth tax paid on UK property holdings is creditable against UK CGT or income tax." FALSE — UK has no wealth tax; there is nothing to mirror; the credit is one-way (Spain credits UK CGT, not vice versa).
- **Misframe 4:** "The Madrid 100% bonification eliminates wealth tax for Madrid residents holding UK property." ## FAQ expansion (RUN session polishes prose; 12-14 FAQs target)

1. **Q: I'm Spanish-resident and own a UK rental flat — does the 2013 UK-Spain Convention mean I don't pay UK tax?**
   A: No. Article 6 of the 2013 Convention allocates source-state taxing rights to the UK on income from UK immovable property. UK domestic charge under ITTOIA 2005 Part 3 applies. The Convention's role is to ensure no double tax by obliging Spain (the residence state) to give a foreign tax credit for UK tax paid under Article 23. You still register for UK Self-Assessment, file SA100 + SA105 annually, and operate the NRL scheme (apply for NRL1 to receive rent gross — otherwise the agent withholds 20%).

2. **Q: My UK letting agent says they must withhold 20% under NRL even though I'm Spanish-resident — is that right?**
   A: Yes. The Non-Resident Landlord scheme is statutory under FA 1995 Schedule 23 + SI 1995/2902. Treaty residence does not displace the obligation. The only mechanism to receive gross rent is HMRC NRL1 (landlord-side application) or NRL2 / NRL3 (agent / tenant routes). Apply for NRL1 on instructing the agent; approval is typically within six weeks.

3. **Q: When I sell my UK property as a Spanish resident, what do I owe and when?**
   A: NRCGT under TCGA 1992 s.1A and Schedules 1A / 1B / 4AA applies. Residential rate is 18% basic / 24% higher-band on the gain (post-30 October 2024). AEA £3,000 for individuals; nil for non-resident companies. 60-day NRCGT return required regardless of whether tax is due. Rebasing rules: residential property acquired before 6 April 2015 defaults to 5 April 2015 rebasing; non-residential acquired before 6 April 2019 defaults to 5 April 2019. Indirect disposals of UK-property-rich entity shares are also caught under Sch 1A Part 4.

4. **Q: Does Article 13(4) of the treaty cover indirect disposals of shares in UK property-rich entities?**
   A: Yes. Article 13(4) of the 2013 Convention extends taxing rights on alienation of shares deriving more than 50% of their value from immovable property in the source state to the situs state (UK). UK NRCGT under Sch 1A Part 4 captures the same disposal at the 75%-value-from-UK-land + 25%-holder-stake test. Treaty allocation and UK statutory charge align — no conflict.

5. **Q: How does the Article 4 tie-breaker work for dual-residents?**
   A: Cascade per OECD Model Article 4 form: (i) permanent home (resolved if only in one state); (ii) centre of vital interests (family / economic / personal); (iii) habitual abode; (iv) nationality; (v) mutual agreement procedure between competent authorities. Tie-breaker resolves treaty allocation only — it does not change your domestic UK residence under SRT for non-treaty UK purposes.

6. **Q: I pay Spanish wealth tax on my London townhouse — can I claim that against UK tax?**
   A: No. The UK has no wealth tax. There is no UK-side mirror to credit. The Spanish wealth tax (impuesto sobre el patrimonio) is structurally one-way. Spain itself credits foreign equivalent-wealth-tax against the Spanish wealth tax (under Spanish domestic law and treaty Article 23), but there is no foreign equivalent to credit for UK property because the UK does not impose wealth tax. The Solidarity Tax on Large Fortunes (SGF) introduced by Ley 38/2022 adds an additional state-level charge above the €3m + €700k threshold that overrides regional bonifications such as Madrid's 100% wealth-tax bonification. Verify SGF rate and threshold currency at consultation date.

7. **Q: I'm UK-resident and emigrating to Spain — does the s.10A temporary non-residence rule trap me?**
   A: It traps you if your period of non-UK residence is **5 years or less** AND you were UK-resident in 4 or more of the 7 tax years before departure. Per §17.3 verified against HMRC CG26540, the trap deems any gain realised on assets owned at departure (and disposed during non-residence) to arise in the year of return. To break the trap you need MORE THAN 5 complete tax years non-UK-resident. UK land already in NRCGT under TCGA 1992 s.1A is unaffected by s.10A (it is taxed when disposed regardless); s.10A primarily catches non-UK situs gains realised during the non-residence period.

8. **Q: My Spanish *sociedad limitada* holds my London BTL flat — am I in UK ATED?**
   A: Yes if the dwelling is worth more than £500,000. ATED applies to non-natural persons including non-UK companies per §18.2. A Spanish SL holding a £780,000 London dwelling falls in the £500,001-£1m band (2026/27 charge £4,600 per §18.1 — verify at write). Claim the s.133 property-rental-business relief on the annual return if you let commercially to unconnected tenants (relief is not automatic — file even when no tax is due to avoid Schedule 55 FA 2009 penalty points).

9. **Q: How did the MLI change the 2013 UK-Spain Convention?**
   A: The Multilateral Instrument modifications took effect for UK withheld taxes from 1 January 2023; UK CT from 1 April 2023; UK income tax + CGT from 6 April 2023. The most consequential change is the importation of a Principal Purpose Test (PPT) into the Convention — treaty benefits can be denied where obtaining the benefit was a principal purpose of any arrangement, unless granting the benefit would accord with the object and purpose of the relevant treaty provisions. Verify the synthesised treaty text at gov.uk Spain tax treaties for exact MLI-modified wording at write.

10. **Q: I'm a Spanish national arriving in UK for the first time — can I claim the FIG regime?**
    A: Yes if you meet the qualifying-new-resident test under ITTOIA 2005 s.845B(1): UK-resident in the current year AND not UK-resident in any of the 10 preceding tax years AND not disqualified AND over age 10. Per §17.8, FIG runs for up to 4 tax years with a per-year claim under s.845A. Foreign property income (e.g. your Spanish rental) falls within s.845H categories and is UK-exempt if you claim. Claiming costs your UK personal allowance + dividend allowance + CGT AEA for that year — do the arithmetic before claiming.

11. **Q: I'm UK-domicile-of-origin moving to Spain to retire — does the new IHT residence test catch me?**
    A: Yes if you are a long-term UK resident (UK-resident for 10 of the previous 20 tax years per IHTA 1984 s.6A — see §22.X). LTR status extends post-departure through a tail period per §22.X.2: 3 years if you were UK-resident in 13 or fewer of the preceding 20 years; scaling up to 10 years if you were UK-resident in all 20. For long-term UK residents this can extend worldwide-asset IHT exposure for up to a decade after emigration. UK situs property (your UK BTL portfolio) stays in UK IHT regardless of LTR status. Pre-FA-2025 "3-year tail" advice anchored on s.267(1)(b) deemed-domicile is obsolete.

12. **Q: I moved to Spain in 2026 but returned to UK in 2030 (4 complete tax years non-UK-resident) — am I caught by s.10A?**
    A: Yes if you sold any non-UK assets during your non-residence and you were UK-resident in 4 or more of the 7 tax years before your 2026 departure. The 4-year stint is within the 5-year-or-less window; gains realised during non-residence are deemed to arise in your 2030/31 return year and are chargeable to UK CGT then. The Spanish CGT you paid in the year of disposal is creditable under Article 23 of the Convention — but only up to the UK CGT amount; excess Spanish CGT is not refundable from the UK side. To escape s.10A, the period of non-UK residence must be more than 5 complete tax years.

13. **Q: How does the Spanish *impuesto sobre sucesiones y donaciones* interact with UK IHT on my UK property?**
    A: Spain operates a heavily devolved inheritance and gift tax. Madrid currently applies a 99% bonification; other autonomous communities apply higher effective rates. Where the same UK property triggers both UK IHT (under IHTA 1984 — UK situs property is always in UK IHT) and Spanish *sucesiones* on the resident heir, Spanish domestic law provides a credit for foreign equivalent taxes against the Spanish charge. The 2013 Convention does not contain a specific IHT article (it covers income tax + CGT + Spanish corporate tax). The cross-credit is therefore Spanish domestic, not treaty-based — verify with a Spanish *asesor fiscal* at write time as regional rules and credit mechanics vary.
