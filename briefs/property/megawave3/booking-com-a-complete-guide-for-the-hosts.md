# Booking.com for UK Hosts: The Reverse-Charge VAT + Commission Practitioner Picture

## Statutory anchor

- Primary (income tax architecture, post-FHL-abolition default for short-let receipts): ITTOIA 2005 Part 3 (property income) — s.264 (charge), s.270 (deductions on the same basis as a trade), s.271A (cash-basis default for individuals under £150,000 property + trading turnover), s.272A-272B (finance-cost restriction). §4 lock.
- Primary (VAT — UK host receives services FROM a non-UK supplier; the reverse-charge mechanism): VATA 1994 s.7A (place of supply of services — B2B general rule supplier is established where the customer is) + VATA 1994 s.8 (services from outside the UK — recipient accounts for VAT). HMRC VAT Notice 741A (Place of supply of services) confirms the reverse-charge treatment for B2B services received from offshore. Booking.com B.V. is Dutch-domiciled; commission charged to a UK host is a B2B service supplied from the Netherlands. **The UK host is required to account for output VAT on the commission as if they had supplied the service to themselves, AND to claim it back as input VAT in the same return** (subject to partial exemption restriction where the host makes exempt supplies). §29 lock — VAT architecture.
- Primary (VAT — onward supply by the UK host to guests, Sch 9 Group 1 carve-out): VATA 1994 Sch 9 Group 1 Item 1(a) — holiday accommodation grants are STANDARD-RATED, NOT exempt. The host's onward supply to the guest is standard-rated where over the £90,000 VAT threshold (FA 2024 s.27; rate-by-reference); below threshold the host is not registered. §29.2 lock.
- Primary (VAT — Tour Operators' Margin Scheme alternative): VATA 1994 s.53 (TOMS) — applies where the host buys-in identifiable components (cleaning, breakfast supply, third-party utilities) and resells the package. VAT computed on the host's MARGIN, not gross. Most Booking.com hosts who add the breakfast component plus on-property extras (e.g. spa, bike hire) fall potentially within TOMS. §29.6 lock by reference.
- Primary (VAT registration threshold — when Booking.com hosts cross the line): VATA 1994 Sch 1 (the registration threshold). £90,000 from 1 April 2024 (FA 2024 s.27 — rate-by-reference per §16.27). Booking.com hosts at the 4-6 property scale routinely cross this; aggregation across all activities at the same legal-person level.
- Primary (digital-platform reporting — DAC7-equivalent in UK): International Tax Compliance (Amendment) Regulations 2023 (SI 2023/817). Booking.com B.V. must annually report UK host details + gross receipts + property identifiers to HMRC under the OECD's Model Rules for Reporting by Digital Platforms (cross-referenced with EU DAC7 since Booking.com is also reporting in the Netherlands). First reports 31 January 2025 covering calendar 2024. HMRC cross-checks against host SA returns. Stage 2 verifies SI 2023/817 + current reporting cycle at write time.
- Primary (council tax / business rates — the 140/70-day test for England): Non-Domestic Rating (Definition of Domestic Property) (England) Order 2022 (SI 2022/1265) — Booking.com let property meeting both 140 days available + 70 days actually let qualifies for business rates (often £0 under small-business rates relief for rateable values under £12,000). Property failing both stays under council tax + potential 100% second-home premium under LURA 2023 s.81 from 1 April 2025. §30.4 lock.
- Primary (FHL transitional capital allowances pool for legacy Booking.com hosts): Finance Act 2025 Sch 5 — preserves existing capital-allowances pool at 6 April 2025 (individuals) / 1 April 2025 (companies). §6 + §25.7 lock.
- Primary (badges of trade — trading-vs-investment line for the largest Booking.com hosts): CTA 2010 Part 8ZB + ITA 2007 Part 9A; §28 lock. Hosts operating 5+ units with channel-manager / property-management overlay are at trading-classification risk under the four-conditions + badges framework. Forward-link to C8 decision-guide.
- Primary (Money Laundering Regulations 2017 — letting-agent / property-manager trigger): SI 2017/692 reg 8(2) + reg 14. A multi-property Booking.com host who outsources management to a property manager meets the MLR-trigger floor at the property-manager side. Not directly host-side; operationally relevant where the host hires a property manager.
- Primary (Renters' Rights Act 2025 — STL carve-out from assured tenancy regime): RRA 2025 (2025 c. 26); HA 1988 Sch 1 — short-term lets via Booking.com remain outside the assured tenancy regime and outside the RRA 2025 periodic-default reform. §20.3 lock by reference.
- Supporting (operational case-law on what counts as "trade" for short-let businesses): *Pawson v HMRC* [2013] UKUT 050 (TCC) — IHT-side BPR test for serviced accommodation (forward-link to existing site page on Pawson). *Marson v Morton* [1986] STC 463 and sibling badges-of-trade case-law for the trading line on multi-unit operations. §28.5 lock.
- House position reference: §4 (s.24); §6 + §25.7 (FHL abolition + transitional pool); §28 (trading-vs-investment); §29.2 + §29.6 (VAT exempt land + Group 1 holiday-accommodation carve-out + TOMS); §29.8 (registration threshold + reverse-charge implication); §30.4 (council-tax + business-rates line); §20.3 by reference (RRA STL carve-out). **No new HP lock needed** — all architecture pre-locked. Possible §29 mini-extension if Stage 1b wants to pin the reverse-charge VAT mechanic for OTA commission specifically (Booking.com B.V. / Vrbo / Expedia all non-UK; Airbnb has a UK entity so commission is UK-side — the asymmetry is operationally important); informational, not blocking.

## Framing differentiator (anti-templating, anti-cannibalisation)

This page is the **Booking.com-specific operational picture** — what is structurally distinct about running a property through Booking.com that does NOT apply to an Airbnb-only host. Where the C5 sibling page (`airbnb-landlords`) is the operational handbook for the Airbnb host (F-102 cannib watch active), this page must NOT mirror that framing. The differentiator survives because Booking.com is structurally a different OTA:

- **Different commission entity (VAT angle).** Booking.com B.V. is Dutch-domiciled; the commission is a B2B service supplied from the Netherlands to the UK host. The UK host must account for VAT under the reverse-charge mechanism (VATA 1994 s.7A + s.8) — output VAT on the commission as a self-supply, simultaneously claimed back as input VAT (subject to partial exemption). Airbnb, by contrast, operates partly through a UK entity (Airbnb Payments UK Ltd) for UK-side host commission, which sidesteps the reverse-charge complication for most Airbnb-side hosts. The asymmetry between OTAs on VAT treatment is the headline operational distinction.
- **Different commission model.** Booking.com charges higher commission than Airbnb (typically 15-18% vs Airbnb's 3-15% under split-fee or simplified-fee structures); Genius and Preferred Partner programs add 2-4% commission uplift in exchange for visibility boost. The host's net rate per booking is structurally lower; the gross-receipts figure reported to HMRC is correspondingly higher relative to net cash flow.
- **Different breakfast / extras flow.** Booking.com hosts often include breakfast or charge extras through the platform (spa, bike hire, late check-out fees). Booking.com's commission is computed on the GROSS rate INCLUDING extras, so the host pays commission on every line item. The TOMS-versus-standard-rate decision is more often decisive for Booking.com hosts because the buy-in component (breakfast supply, third-party services) is more often present than for pure Airbnb listings.
- **Different no-show / late-cancellation treatment.** Booking.com offers strict, semi-flexible, and flexible cancellation policies; under strict + non-refundable rates the host can keep the full rate on a no-show, but the commission is still due even on the no-show retention. The accounting line is "received income + commission expense" not net; the host must record both sides.
- **Different professional / channel-manager integration.** Booking.com is more commonly the OTA-of-choice for professional property managers running multi-unit portfolios via channel managers (Beds24, Hostaway, Lodgify, eviivo etc.). A landlord running 5+ units through a channel manager faces trading-classification risk under Part 8ZB / Part 9A (the badges-of-trade frequency + activity factors fire). The C8 sibling decision-guide is the cross-link.

## Key questions this page must answer

1. **The reverse-charge VAT on Booking.com commission — the headline asymmetry** — Booking.com B.V. is established in the Netherlands; the commission charged to a UK host is a B2B cross-border service. Under VATA 1994 s.7A (B2B general rule: supplier is established where the customer is) + s.8 (services from outside the UK: recipient accounts for VAT), the UK host must account for output VAT on the commission as if they had supplied the service to themselves. **The host adds output VAT to their VAT return on the commission AND simultaneously claims input VAT in the same return** (the "self-supply" pattern). For a VAT-registered host making fully taxable supplies, the two cancel out — but the reverse-charge entry MUST appear on the return; failure to record it is an inaccuracy under Sch 24 FA 2007 (§27.2 lock). For a partially-exempt host, the input-VAT claim is restricted via the partial-exemption calculation (§29.6); the output-VAT side stays. The asymmetry with Airbnb (which operates partly through Airbnb Payments UK Ltd for UK-side commission) catches most hosts unaware.
2. **When does the host need to register for VAT — the £90,000 threshold + the gross-vs-net trap** — VATA 1994 Sch 1 sets the registration threshold at £90,000 (rate-by-reference; FA 2024 s.27). The threshold is computed on TAXABLE supplies — for a Booking.com host, the gross rate received from guests (excluding commission). A host with 3 properties grossing £30,000 each = £90,000 = just at threshold. Commission is an EXPENSE, not a deduction from taxable turnover for threshold purposes. Crossing the threshold triggers registration from the relevant date; reverse-charge VAT on commission then becomes payable on EVERY commission invoice going forward.
3. **The TOMS vs standard-rate decision for Booking.com hosts including breakfast / extras** — Tour Operators' Margin Scheme (VATA 1994 s.53) applies where the host buys-in identifiable components (breakfast supply from a third-party supplier, third-party cleaning, on-property third-party services) and resells the package. TOMS computes VAT on the host's MARGIN (selling price minus bought-in cost), NOT on gross. For a Booking.com host adding breakfast at £15/guest where the breakfast costs £6/guest to source, TOMS would compute VAT on £9 margin/guest, not on £15 gross. Decision is fact-sensitive; HMRC VAT Notice 709/5 sets the framework. Forward-link to `toms-vat-serviced-accommodation`.
4. **The Genius and Preferred Partner programs — gross-receipts inflation** — Booking.com's Genius (loyalty discount to repeat guests) and Preferred Partner (visibility boost in exchange for commission uplift) programs effectively reduce the host's NET rate per booking by 2-4 percentage points. The reported "Genius discount applied" or "Preferred commission" lines on Booking.com statements must be reflected in the host's accounts — the gross rate appears on the platform statement, but the discount/uplift reduces the net cash received. For HMRC reporting purposes (SA105 box 5 for individuals, CT600 for companies), the GROSS RATE is reported as income and the discount/uplift commission is reflected as an expense; the net-cash and gross-income figures DIFFER. Reconciliation discipline matters.
5. **The no-show / late-cancellation accounting line** — Booking.com strict and non-refundable rates allow the host to keep the full rate on a no-show or last-minute cancellation. Commission is still due to Booking.com on the retained rate; the host recognises BOTH income and the commission expense, even though no guest stayed. The accounting line for a £200 no-show at 15% commission = £200 gross income + £30 commission expense + £170 net cash + Booking.com VAT-reverse-charge entry on the £30 commission. Recording the gross-then-commission, not net, is the discipline.
6. **The channel-manager / professional-host pattern — and the trading-classification risk** — Hosts running 5+ units on Booking.com (and typically other OTAs simultaneously via a channel manager like Beds24, Hostaway, Lodgify, eviivo) sit close to the trading-classification line under CTA 2010 Part 8ZB / ITA 2007 Part 9A. The badges-of-trade factors (frequency, supplementary work / active management, motive at acquisition for new units, dedicated channel-manager subscription) all fire. §28.5 lock for the badges framework + forward-link to C8 sibling decision-guide. For a multi-unit host weighing whether the operation has crossed into trading territory, the C8 page is the working reference.
7. **The DAC7-equivalent platform reporting — Booking.com reports your gross receipts to HMRC** — SI 2023/817 (International Tax Compliance (Amendment) Regulations 2023) requires Booking.com B.V. to annually report UK host details + gross receipts + property identifiers to HMRC. First reports 31 January 2025 covering calendar 2024. HMRC cross-checks against host SA returns. The host's declared income MUST reconcile to Booking.com's reported gross receipts — material discrepancies trigger HMRC enquiry. The discipline: keep Booking.com's annual host statement, reconcile box-by-box to the SA105 entry, document any timing differences (e.g. December bookings paid January) on the working schedule.
8. **The 140/70-day council-tax-vs-business-rates test** — Non-Domestic Rating (Definition of Domestic Property) (England) Order 2022 (SI 2022/1265) requires both 140 days available for letting AND 70 days actually let in the year. A Booking.com host meeting both qualifies for business rates (eligible for small-business-rates relief — 100% relief for rateable values under £12,000 = zero rates bill). A host failing both stays under council tax + potential 100% second-home premium under LURA 2023 s.81 (in force from 1 April 2025 in adopting LAs). For a Booking.com host averaging 70-80 nights per year, the test is the marginal-case decider. Stage 2 verifies the test thresholds at gov.uk at write time per §16.27.
9. **The cross-OTA reconciliation — running Booking.com alongside Airbnb / Vrbo / Expedia** — Most professional short-let operators list each unit on 2-4 OTAs simultaneously. The host's gross income comprises receipts from EACH OTA; commissions differ (Booking.com 15-18%; Airbnb 3-15%; Vrbo 5-8% + 8% guest fee); VAT treatment differs (Booking.com B.V. + Vrbo Inc. + Expedia Group — non-UK-domiciled commission triggers reverse-charge; Airbnb Payments UK Ltd — UK-side commission, no reverse-charge required). The host's bookkeeping must distinguish per-OTA per-unit revenue + per-OTA commission + per-OTA VAT treatment. Channel-manager software typically exports this in a consolidated form. The SA105 entry aggregates across OTAs; the working schedule retains the per-OTA breakdown for audit.
10. **The succession + exit picture for Booking.com hosts — same as Airbnb but the cross-OTA reconciliation matters for sale** — Three exit routes: (a) **continue as investment** (CGT on transfer to family or held through retirement); (b) **convert to BTL** (lower management overhead; lower income; standard property regime); (c) **sell** — CGT at 24% residential rate on the gain; 60-day in-year reporting under TCGA 1992 Sch 2 paras 6-12; PPR unavailable. Pawson-line means no BPR for IHT (forward-link to `serviced-accommodation-bpr-eligibility-pawson-test`). Sale of a Booking.com unit as a going concern requires transfer of the Booking.com Extranet account + Genius status + property identifiers + the reverse-charge VAT recording history; the buyer's diligence routinely asks for the Booking.com platform statements + reconciliation working papers + VAT records. Forward-link to `serviced-accommodation-vs-buy-to-let-tax-comparison-2026` for the regime-choice analysis.

## Worked-example data (RUN session uses these as canvas)

### Example 1 — The reverse-charge VAT on Booking.com commission (worked numbers)

VAT-registered UK host (Ms Singh) operates 4 serviced apartments via Booking.com. April-September 2026 gross bookings via Booking.com £80,000; Booking.com commission at 17% = £13,600. Ms Singh makes fully taxable supplies (all standard-rated holiday accommodation under Sch 9 Gr 1 Item 1(a)).

- **Output VAT on host's supplies to guests:** 20% × £80,000 gross = £13,333 output VAT (gross £80,000 = £66,667 net + £13,333 VAT). Reported on the host's quarterly return.
- **Reverse-charge on Booking.com commission:** Booking.com B.V. invoices £13,600 commission with NO UK VAT charged. Under VATA 1994 s.7A + s.8, the UK host accounts for VAT on the commission as if she had supplied the service to herself.
  - **Output side (self-supply):** 20% × £13,600 = £2,720 output VAT added to Box 1.
  - **Input side (same return):** 20% × £13,600 = £2,720 input VAT added to Box 4 (and reported on net commission in Box 7).
  - **Net cash impact:** zero (output and input net to nil for a fully taxable host).
- **Critically:** the entries MUST appear on the return. Failure to record the reverse-charge entries (a) understates Box 1 output; (b) is an inaccuracy under FA 2007 Sch 24 + carries potential 0-100% penalty depending on behaviour categorisation; (c) is flagged on HMRC compliance review when the SA return + VAT return + DAC7-equivalent Booking.com data cross-reference.
- **Partially exempt host (e.g. host with separate residential lettings exempted under Sch 9 Gr 1 Item 1 default):** input VAT reverse-charge claim is RESTRICTED by the partial-exemption recovery percentage. Output side stays. Net cash impact: positive output VAT on the restricted commission element — the host effectively pays VAT on the commission.

Inline aside CTA: "The reverse-charge VAT entries are the most-missed line on first-time Booking.com host VAT returns. If you crossed the £90,000 threshold this year, talk to us about the implementation before HMRC's first review."

### Example 2 — The TOMS-vs-standard-rate decision on breakfast + extras

Host (Mr Patel) operates a B&B-style 4-room unit on Booking.com; rate £150/night including breakfast; breakfast cost to source (third-party supplier delivers) £8/guest morning. 200 paid breakfasts/quarter at £8 each = £1,600 buy-in cost. Total quarterly gross revenue (rooms + breakfast) £75,000. Mr Patel is VAT-registered (above the £90,000 threshold annually).

- **Standard-rate treatment:** all £75,000 standard-rated at 20%; output VAT = £12,500 (£75k gross = £62,500 net + £12,500 VAT). Input VAT recoverable on all related costs (cleaning, utilities, breakfast supply, Booking.com commission reverse-charge).
- **TOMS treatment (s.53):** identifiable bought-in components (breakfast supply £1,600) treated under TOMS. Margin = total selling price − bought-in cost = £75,000 − £1,600 = £73,400. VAT computed on margin: £73,400 × 1/6 = £12,233 output VAT (TOMS uses VAT-fraction of the margin). Input VAT on the buy-in component (the £1,600 breakfast supply) is NOT recoverable under TOMS — it is absorbed in the margin calculation.
- **Decision:** standard-rate is slightly higher VAT (£12,500 vs £12,233) BUT allows input VAT recovery on the £1,600 breakfast supply (20% × £1,600 = £320 input). Net standard-rate cost = £12,500 − £320 = £12,180 vs TOMS net cost £12,233. Standard-rate is approximately £50 better per quarter on these facts.
- **Where TOMS wins:** where the buy-in component is substantially larger (e.g. hosts adding spa packages, bike hire, third-party adventure activities — high-cost bought-in resold packages), TOMS margin-only VAT can save substantially. Fact-sensitive; HMRC VAT Notice 709/5 sets the framework.

### Example 3 — The Genius programme + no-show retention accounting

Booking.com host (Ms Singh) operates a 2-bed apartment listing on Booking.com with Genius level 2 (10% loyalty discount applied to qualifying repeat bookings). Quarterly bookings: 30 nights at £180/night = £5,400 gross. 12 of those nights are Genius bookings with 10% discount applied (£1,800 list, £1,620 actual paid). Commission 17% on actual rate. 2 of the 30 bookings result in no-shows under non-refundable rates; Ms Singh retains the £360 for the 2 no-show nights.

- **Income recognition (cash basis or accruals — either way; pure operational record):**
  - Gross income: £5,400 list price − £180 Genius discount = £5,220 booked + £360 no-show retention = £5,580 total received.
  - Commission expense: 17% × £5,580 = £948.60 commission to Booking.com.
  - Net cash to host: £5,580 − £948.60 = £4,631.40.
- **The reporting trap:** for SA105 box 5 (rent received) and CT600 corporate landlord, the GROSS rate is reported as income (£5,580), NOT the net cash (£4,631.40). The commission is a separate deductible expense line. Reporting net only understates income by £948.60 and creates a discrepancy with Booking.com's DAC7-equivalent gross-receipts report to HMRC (£5,580 gross).
- **The no-show line:** Ms Singh recognises the full £360 income on the 2 no-show nights AND the corresponding £61.20 commission expense — even though no guest stayed. The "I retained the rate but no service was provided" intuition is wrong for tax purposes; the income is the contractual entitlement, and the commission is the contractual obligation.

## FAQ expansion (RUN session polishes prose; 10-12 FAQs target)

1. **Q: Do I have to account for reverse-charge VAT on Booking.com commission if I'm under the £90,000 threshold?**
   A: No. The reverse-charge mechanism under VATA 1994 s.7A + s.8 applies only to VAT-registered businesses. A Booking.com host below the £90,000 registration threshold (Sch 1) is not VAT-registered, does not account for any UK VAT (output or input), and has no reverse-charge obligation. Once turnover crosses the threshold, the host must register and account for reverse-charge VAT on every subsequent Booking.com commission invoice. Crossing the threshold is computed on TAXABLE supplies (gross rate received from guests).

2. **Q: Why is Booking.com commission reverse-charge but Airbnb commission UK VAT?**
   A: Different entity-of-supply. Booking.com B.V. is established in the Netherlands; commission charged to UK hosts is a B2B cross-border service supplied from the Netherlands. Under VATA 1994 s.7A + s.8 reverse-charge mechanism, the UK host accounts for VAT (output + input self-supply). Airbnb, by contrast, operates partly through Airbnb Payments UK Ltd for UK-side host commission; the supply is UK-domestic, charged with UK VAT directly, input VAT recoverable on the invoice. Vrbo (Inc. US) and Expedia (Group EU) commission also trigger reverse-charge.

3. **Q: How do I record Booking.com no-show retention in my SA return?**
   A: Recognise the gross retained rate as income; recognise the corresponding commission as an expense, even though no guest stayed. For a £200 no-show at 17% commission: £200 gross income (SA105 box 5) + £34 commission expense (SA105 box 28) + reverse-charge VAT entry on the £34 commission (if VAT-registered). The intuition that "no service = no income" is wrong for tax purposes — the income is the contractual entitlement, not the cash flow of the service.

4. **Q: Can I use TOMS for the breakfast component of my Booking.com listing?**
   A: Yes, where the breakfast supply is bought-in from a third-party identifiable component. Under VATA 1994 s.53 + HMRC VAT Notice 709/5, the host computes VAT on the margin (selling price − bought-in cost) rather than gross. The trade-off: TOMS gives a lower output VAT figure but blocks input VAT recovery on the bought-in component. Decision is fact-sensitive; standard-rate is typically slightly better for low-buy-in operations; TOMS is better where the buy-in is substantial (spa packages, third-party activities). HMRC VAT Notice 709/5 sets the framework.

5. **Q: Does DAC7-equivalent platform reporting affect me as a Booking.com host?**
   A: Yes. Under SI 2023/817 (International Tax Compliance (Amendment) Regulations 2023), Booking.com B.V. must annually report UK host details + gross receipts + property identifiers to HMRC. First reports 31 January 2025 covering calendar 2024. HMRC cross-checks against host SA returns. Discipline: keep Booking.com's annual host statement; reconcile box-by-box to your SA105 / CT600 entries; document any timing differences. Material discrepancies trigger HMRC enquiry.

6. **Q: Do I qualify for business rates instead of council tax on a Booking.com let?**
   A: Under Non-Domestic Rating (Definition of Domestic Property) (England) Order 2022 (SI 2022/1265), a self-catering property qualifies for business rates if it is BOTH (a) available for letting for at least 140 days in the year AND (b) actually let for at least 70 days in the year. Properties meeting both qualify for business rates (often £0 under small-business-rates relief for rateable values under £12,000). Properties failing both stay under council tax + potential 100% second-home premium under LURA 2023 s.81 (in adopting LAs from 1 April 2025). Verify both day-counts annually.

7. **Q: How does the post-FHL-abolition regime affect Booking.com hosts?**
   A: The Furnished Holiday Lettings regime was abolished from 6 April 2025 (individuals) / 1 April 2025 (companies) per FA 2025 Sch 5. Booking.com host receipts now sit in the standard property-business framework — ITTOIA 2005 Part 3 (individuals) / CTA 2009 Part 4 (companies). The transitional capital-allowances pool at the FHL-abolition date is preserved per §6 + §25.7. Sessions on FHL-related questions should now framework the host as a standard short-let property business with the transitional pool legacy.

8. **Q: Am I a trader for tax purposes if I run 5+ Booking.com units with a channel manager?**
   A: Potentially yes. The badges of trade (*Marson v Morton* [1986] 1 WLR 1343) fire on frequency, supplementary work (active management, channel-manager subscription), motive at acquisition, and method of financing. CTA 2010 Part 8ZB / ITA 2007 Part 9A four-conditions test may engage particularly on Condition D (development main-purpose) where units have been substantially modified for short-let purposes. The C8 sibling page (`are-you-a-property-investor-or-developer`) is the decision-guide. For multi-unit professional hosts, the trading-classification line is the high-value forward-link.

9. **Q: Can I claim TOMS without registering for VAT?**
   A: No. TOMS is a VAT scheme; registration is required. A Booking.com host below the £90,000 VAT threshold cannot apply TOMS. The TOMS decision becomes relevant only once the host has crossed the threshold and is registering for VAT. At registration, the host elects (or defaults to) standard-rate vs TOMS treatment for the buy-in components.

10. **Q: What's the difference between a Booking.com Genius discount and a commission uplift?**
    A: Genius is a loyalty programme — Booking.com offers a discount (typically 10% at Genius level 2; 15% at level 3) to repeat guests in exchange for visibility boost in search results. The discount comes off the host's net rate per booking. Preferred Partner is a separate programme where the host pays a commission UPLIFT (2-4 percentage points) in exchange for ranking boost. Both effectively reduce the host's net cash per booking; both are reflected in the host's accounts as either discount (reducing gross income) or as additional commission expense.

11. **Q: How do I reconcile Booking.com gross receipts to my SA105 box 5?**
    A: Box 5 records gross rent received (cash basis) or gross rent receivable (accruals basis). For a Booking.com host on cash basis, box 5 = sum of guest payments received in the tax year (gross of any Booking.com fees withheld). The annual Booking.com host statement shows monthly gross + commission deduction. Working schedule: cross-reference Booking.com January-December calendar gross figures against the host's tax-year (6 April – 5 April) booking-by-booking statement; document timing differences (December bookings paid January). Retain reconciliation for 5 years per TMA 1970 s.12B.

12. **Q: What happens if I sell my Booking.com unit as a going concern?**
    A: A sale of a short-let business as a going concern may qualify for Transfer of Going Concern (TOGC) treatment under VAT Notice 700/9 — disposal outside the scope of VAT. The buyer must continue the same kind of business + be VAT-registered (or required to register) + the seller must transfer the active Booking.com Extranet account, property identifiers, and Genius status to the buyer. CGT applies to the gain on the property under TCGA 1992 — residential rates 18%/24% from 30 October 2024 per §5; 60-day in-year reporting under TCGA Sch 2 paras 6-12. PPR unavailable (commercial use). BPR unavailable (Pawson investment line — forward-link).
