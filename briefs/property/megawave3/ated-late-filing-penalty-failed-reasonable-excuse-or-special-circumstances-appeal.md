# ATED late-filing penalty: appealing on reasonable excuse (Sch 55 para 23) or special circumstances (Sch 55 para 16), the two routes and why they are not the same

## Statutory anchor
- **Primary (ATED return obligation):**
  - **FA 2013 s.159** — "Annual tax on enveloped dwellings return"; return required within 30 days of first becoming chargeable, with 90-day extended deadline for new dwellings / conversions under ss.124-125. **Verified at legislation.gov.uk on 2026-05-27.**
  - **FA 2013 s.161** — "Return to include self assessment"; ATED return must include self-assessment of tax under s.99 (rate table) plus any interim relief. **Verified at legislation.gov.uk on 2026-05-27.**
- **Primary (late-filing penalty regime):**
  - **FA 2009 Schedule 55** — late filing penalty regime. ATED falls within scope via items 11A (ATED return) and 11B (ATED adjusted chargeable amount return) added to the Sch 55 Table at para 1. The HP §18.4 verbatim-verified escalator (£100 immediate, £200 at 3 months, £300 at 6 months and 12 months, plus tax-geared penalties for deliberate / prompted / unprompted disclosure) operates through the Sch 55 paragraphs 1, 4, 5 and 6 mechanics.
- **Primary (the two appeal grounds, DO NOT COLLAPSE):**
  - **FA 2009 Sch 55 para 23 — reasonable excuse defence** (verified verbatim at legislation.gov.uk 2026-05-27): "Liability to a penalty under any paragraph of this Schedule does not arise in relation to a failure to make a return if P satisfies HMRC or (on appeal) the First-tier Tribunal or Upper Tribunal that there is a reasonable excuse for the failure." Three statutory exclusions: (i) insufficient funds unless circumstances beyond P's control, (ii) reliance on another person unless reasonable steps taken to prevent the failure, (iii) excuse ceasing, must remedy without unreasonable delay.
  - **FA 2009 Sch 55 para 16 — special reduction** (verified verbatim at legislation.gov.uk 2026-05-27): "If HMRC think it right because of special circumstances, they may reduce a penalty under any paragraph of this Schedule." Para 16(2) explicitly excludes from "special circumstances": (a) inability to pay, (b) revenue loss from one taxpayer offset by overpayments from another. Para 16(3) extends the reduction to staying a penalty or compromising proceedings.
- **Primary (appeal mechanics + 30-day clock):**
  - **TMA 1970 s.31** — right of appeal against penalties.
  - **TMA 1970 s.31A** — **30-day appeal window** from the date of the penalty notice. Non-negotiable per HP §27.9, late appeals require reasonable-excuse application under the Martland framework (FTT discretionary jurisdiction).
  - **TMA 1970 s.49** — statutory review process: appellant can request HMRC internal review OR proceed direct to FTT; review request stops the 30-day FTT clock pending HMRC's review conclusion.
- **Primary (controlling case-law on the reasonable excuse test):**
  - **Perrin v HMRC [2018] UKUT 156 (TCC)** — Upper Tribunal four-stage test for reasonable excuse (per HP §27.9 lock): (1) establish facts on the balance of probabilities; (2) decide which of those facts are relevant to the alleged "reasonable excuse"; (3) apply objective test, would a reasonable taxpayer in P's position, with P's actual knowledge and characteristics, have viewed those facts as a reasonable excuse; (4) ensure remedy without unreasonable delay once excuse ceased.
  - **Martland v HMRC [2018] UKUT 178 (TCC)** — Upper Tribunal three-stage framework for late-appeal applications (length of delay + reason for delay + balancing exercise on prejudice).
- **House position reference:** §2 (ATED bands 2026/27, verified gov.uk 2026-05-22), **§18 (full Wave 3 ATED extension)**, §18.4 (return mechanics + verified late-filing penalty schedule), §27 (HMRC enquiry + appeals architecture full lock, penalties + appeals chapters), §27.4 (the Perrin v HMRC four-stage UT framework verbatim), §27.5 (Martland late-appeal framework), §27.9 (do-not-write list for penalty / appeal architecture, multiple specific anti-patterns including "reasonable excuse is HMRC discretion" / "appeal window is 60 days" / etc.).

## Framing differentiator (anti-templating, anti-cannibalisation)
This page is the **practical-appeal-mechanics** ATED page, distinct from B9 (`ated-penalties-and-appeals`, the broader penalties-and-appeals catalogue) and B16 (`avoiding-common-mistakes-with-enveloped-dwelling-tax`, the upstream-error-prevention page). The framing differentiator is the **two-routes refusal-to-collapse**: most popular commentary blurs "reasonable excuse" and "special circumstances" into a single "appeal on grounds of hardship / misunderstanding". They are independent statutory tests with different burdens.

Specifically the page must:

1. **Refuse the collapse.** Para 23 (reasonable excuse) is an objective test the appellant carries the burden on, what would a reasonable taxpayer with the appellant's actual characteristics have viewed as excuse? Para 16 (special circumstances) is HMRC's discretionary judgment, and the FTT can only review HMRC's exercise of that discretion on Wednesbury-equivalent grounds, not substitute its own view (per the statutory architecture of Sch 55 para 22). The two routes have different burdens, different tests, and different remedies.
2. **Anchor the Perrin v HMRC four-stage framework.** Most blog posts say "you need to show you had a good reason", that is not the test. Per Perrin: (1) facts on balance of probabilities, (2) which are relevant, (3) objective-with-actual-characteristics test, (4) remedy without unreasonable delay once excuse ceased. Each stage is fact-specific evidence the appellant must adduce.
3. **Apply the three statutory exclusions.** Para 23 specifically excludes insufficient funds (unless beyond control), unconsidered reliance on others, and failure to remedy once excuse ceased. The most common ATED late-filing appeal is the "I relied on my agent" line, para 23(2) carves that out unless the appellant took reasonable steps to prevent the failure (which means oversight, not just delegation).
4. **Apply the para 16 exclusions.** Inability to pay is NOT special circumstances per para 16(2)(a). Hardship is for HMRC time-to-pay arrangements, not Sch 55 para 16 reduction. The genuine special-circumstances cases are narrow: HMRC error, third-party event beyond P's reasonable contemplation, regulatory event causing the failure (e.g. RoE-registration backlog blocking ATED filing in 2022-23 was a common para 16 angle).
5. **The 30-day clock.** TMA 1970 s.31A, non-negotiable; appeal must be lodged within 30 days of the penalty notice OR a review must be requested within that window. Late appeals require Martland-framework application to the FTT; long delays will not succeed.
6. **Note the ATED-specific operational angle.** ATED filers are typically non-natural persons (companies, partnerships with corporate member, CIS). The "reliance on agent" pattern (para 23(2)) is more frequent in ATED appeals than in personal-income-tax SA appeals because corporate filers routinely delegate. The objective test (Perrin stage 3) does NOT relax for corporate appellants, directors' oversight responsibility is part of the actual-characteristics matrix.

### Example 1 — The two-routes table (the page's first concrete artefact)

Render as semantic HTML side-by-side table. This is the structural anchor most public commentary refuses to provide:

| Feature | Reasonable excuse (Sch 55 para 23) | Special reduction (Sch 55 para 16) |
|---|---|---|
| **Statutory test** | Objective: would a reasonable taxpayer in P's position, with P's actual characteristics, have viewed those facts as reasonable excuse? | HMRC subjective: "if HMRC think it right because of special circumstances" |
| **Burden** | Appellant must satisfy HMRC or (on appeal) FTT / UT | HMRC discretion; appellant requests reduction in submissions |
| **FTT review scope** | FTT exercises full original jurisdiction on facts + objective test | FTT supervisory only (Sch 55 para 22): can review HMRC's exercise on Wednesbury-equivalent grounds; cannot substitute own view |
| **Statutory exclusions** | Three exclusions per para 23(2): insufficient funds unless beyond control; reliance on another person unless reasonable steps taken; failure to remedy once excuse ceased | Two exclusions per para 16(2): inability to pay; revenue loss offset by overpayments from another |
| **Controlling authority** | Perrin v HMRC [2018] UKUT 156 (TCC), four-stage test | None directly; para 22 + general administrative-law principles |
| **Remedy if successful** | Penalty NOT chargeable (full discharge per para 23(1)) | Penalty REDUCED (or stayed / compromised per para 16(3)); HMRC sets reduced amount |
| **Common winning angles** | Genuine illness / bereavement at filing deadline; HMRC system unavailability; third-party event beyond control with prompt remediation | HMRC mis-direction; regulatory event beyond reasonable contemplation (e.g. RoE backlog); HMRC error in calculating penalty |
| **Common losing angles** | "I relied on my accountant" without oversight evidence; "I forgot"; "I was busy with business"; insufficient funds | Inability to pay; general hardship; cross-comparison with other taxpayers' positions |

Operational anchor: the appellant should typically argue BOTH routes simultaneously, para 23 as primary defence; para 16 as fallback if para 23 fails. They are NOT mutually exclusive.

### Example 2 — Perrin v HMRC four-stage test walked through an ATED appellant

Topaz Holdings Limited (UK ltd-co holding a £2.4m London BTL; let to unconnected commercial tenants throughout; s.133 property-rental-business ATED relief claimed annually for 2020/21 to 2025/26). 2024/25 return missed: company secretary on parental leave April 2024 to October 2024; standing instruction to outsource accountant lapsed; ATED return deadline 30 April 2024 missed; £100 immediate penalty notice received 15 May 2024; £200 penalty at 3 months received 31 July 2024; £300 penalty at 6 months received 31 October 2024. Penalty notices total £600. Topaz wishes to appeal on reasonable excuse.

**Perrin v HMRC [2018] UKUT 156 (TCC) four-stage application (per HP §27.9):**

- **Stage 1: Facts on balance of probabilities.** Topaz must adduce evidence: parental-leave documentation for the company secretary; lapsed standing-instruction letter to the outsourced accountant; calendar / email evidence showing no alternative filing arrangement was made; first-action timeline showing remediation (return filed November 2024).
- **Stage 2: Which facts are relevant.** The relevant facts for the reasonable-excuse defence are: (a) the parental-leave gap creating the operational hole; (b) the lapsed agent-instruction (NOT the agent's failure, that route is foreclosed by para 23(2)); (c) whether oversight was attempted; (d) the speed of remediation once the gap was identified.
- **Stage 3: Objective test with actual characteristics.** The actual characteristics of Topaz include: it is a corporate ATED filer (had been filing claim-only returns for 4 prior years, so the deadline obligation is well-established); the director-shareholder is the natural-person principal; the company secretary is on parental leave with no automatic substitute; no calendar reminder fired for the April 2024 deadline. A reasonable corporate taxpayer in this position, with this knowledge and these characteristics, would arguably have implemented a substitute filing protocol BEFORE the company secretary's leave began. Stage 3 is the hardest stage to overcome here. The objective-with-actual-characteristics test is NOT subjective.
- **Stage 4: Remedy without unreasonable delay once excuse ceased.** The "excuse" (the parental-leave gap) ceased when the company secretary returned in October 2024. The return was filed in November 2024, prompt remediation. Stage 4 PASSES.
- **Likely FTT view:** Stages 1-2 + 4 satisfied; Stage 3 is the question. Topaz's best argument is that the absence of a substitute filing protocol was a foreseeable failure but the lapsed-agent angle is foreclosed by para 23(2). The most plausible outcome is partial success: a tribunal might discharge the £200 (3-month) and £300 (6-month) penalties on the basis that once the parental-leave gap was identified, remediation was prompt; the £100 (immediate) penalty might stand on the basis that initial-deadline filing was within the company's reasonable control via substitute protocol. Specialist representation essential.
- **Para 23(2) "reliance on another person" trap:** Topaz should NOT plead "we relied on our accountant who failed to file". That is foreclosed by para 23(2) unless Topaz can show reasonable steps were taken to prevent the failure (e.g. positive instruction with deadline reminder; confirmation request; no response triggering follow-up). Mere delegation is not reasonable steps. Stage 2 walks evidence pattern.

### Example 3 — Special circumstances (Sch 55 para 16) walked through a regulatory-event appellant

Cordoba Holdings Cayman Limited (Cayman company holding a £4.8m London penthouse; ATED relief NOT claimed because property used by ultimate beneficial owner family seasonally; full ATED 2022/23 charge of £30,650 + 30-day filing deadline). The Cayman company registered with the UK Register of Overseas Entities (RoE) under ECTEA 2022 in early March 2023 (deadline 31 January 2023; missed by 6 weeks due to Companies House RoE backlog plus delays in Cayman corporate-services agent providing the verification). RoE registration completed 14 March 2023. ATED 2022/23 return + payment of £30,650 was made on 22 March 2023 (35 days late vs the original 1 March 2023 deadline calculated from the 30-day rule running from earlier April 2022 chargeability — penalty position created). £100 immediate penalty notice received 18 March 2023.

Cordoba wishes to argue para 16 special circumstances (the para 23 reasonable-excuse route is also available but para 16 has stronger headline given the regulatory-event framing).

- **Special-circumstances framing:** the late RoE registration was caused by Companies House backlog + Cayman agent delays, both substantially beyond Cordoba's reasonable contemplation. The RoE blockage on Land Registry dispositions also created indirect blockage on certain ATED-relevant evidence (e.g. updated UBO information needed for the return). The 2022/23 transitional year saw widespread RoE backlogs; FTT awareness of the regulatory event was demonstrable.
- **Para 16(2) exclusions check:** (a) inability to pay, NOT cited (Cordoba paid the £30,650 promptly on filing); (b) cross-taxpayer balancing, not cited. Both exclusions cleared.
- **HMRC submission:** Cordoba's representation to HMRC under para 16 should: cite the specific RoE-backlog context; provide RoE timeline documentation; demonstrate prompt filing once the regulatory gap was resolved; cite (if available) HMRC's own published acknowledgement of the 2022/23 RoE transition friction; argue that the £100 penalty should be discharged or reduced to nil because the underlying late-filing event was caused by a regulatory event beyond Cordoba's reasonable contemplation.
- **FTT supervisory check:** if HMRC refuses or partially refuses, the FTT can only review HMRC's exercise of discretion on Wednesbury-equivalent grounds (irrationality, failure to consider relevant matters, considering irrelevant matters). The FTT cannot substitute its own view on whether special circumstances exist. This is the key procedural difference vs para 23 (where the FTT exercises full original jurisdiction).
- **Likely outcome:** if HMRC considered the RoE-backlog context and explicitly addressed it in the penalty-decision letter, FTT review will fail (HMRC has exercised discretion within reason). If HMRC's penalty-decision letter is templated and shows no engagement with the regulatory context, Cordoba has a procedural angle on review. Specialist representation essential.

### Example 4 — The 30-day window + statutory review timeline (operational decision-tree)

Acer Property Partners LLP (corporate partnership with corporate member; £3.1m London flat; ATED 2024/25 return missed by 4 months; £100 + £200 + £300 = £600 in penalty notices received 5 February 2025). The 30-day appeal window expires 7 March 2025.

**Decision-tree per TMA 1970 ss.31A + 49 + Martland framework:**

1. **Within 30 days of penalty notice (i.e. by 7 March 2025):** Three options. (a) Appeal direct to FTT under TMA 1970 s.31A. (b) Request HMRC statutory review under TMA 1970 s.49; review request stops the 30-day FTT clock. (c) Pay the penalties without prejudice + lodge protective appeal to preserve later remedy. **Doing nothing is NOT an option, the 30-day clock runs regardless.**
2. **Statutory review route under TMA 1970 s.49:** Acer requests internal review by 7 March 2025; HMRC issues a review conclusion notice (typically within 45 days, occasionally longer for complex cases); on receipt of the notice, a FRESH 30-day FTT appeal window opens. The review route is operationally valuable for cases where HMRC's initial penalty decision is templated and the appellant believes a fresh HMRC officer with the full submission will discharge the penalty without litigation.
3. **Direct FTT appeal:** Acer lodges the appeal via Tribunal Service (electronic or paper) by 7 March 2025; FTT acknowledges; HMRC files response; case enters case-management track; hearing scheduled (typically 6-9 months). Costs follow event under FTT default rules (low cost regime for simple ATED penalty appeals; standard regime for complex cases).
4. **Late appeal (after 30 days):** Acer must apply to FTT for permission to make a late appeal under FTT Rules + Martland v HMRC [2018] UKUT 178 (TCC) three-stage framework. (i) Length of delay, (ii) Reason for delay, (iii) Balancing exercise on prejudice. Long delays + thin reasons FAIL routinely. Specialist representation essential at the late-appeal application stage.

**Practical recommendation:** the statutory-review route via TMA 1970 s.49 is typically the right first step for ATED late-filing appeals where the underlying facts are evidentiary (e.g. parental-leave gap; agent failure with reasonable-steps angle; regulatory event). The review route is cheaper, faster, and creates a fresh appeal window if needed. Direct FTT appeal is right where HMRC has already engaged at quality and refused (e.g. post-OTM-letter penalty review already conducted internally).

### Example 5 — Misframings the page must correct (verbatim do-not-write list)

- **Misframe 1:** "Reasonable excuse is HMRC's discretion." FALSE per §27.9 — para 23 reasonable excuse is an OBJECTIVE statutory test on which the FTT exercises full original jurisdiction; HMRC discretion is para 16 (special reduction).
- **Misframe 2:** "Special circumstances is the appellant's defence on objective grounds." FALSE per §27.9 — para 16 special reduction is HMRC's subjective discretionary judgment; FTT review on supervisory Wednesbury grounds only.
- **Misframe 3:** "Reasonable excuse is whatever the taxpayer subjectively believed." FALSE per §27.9 — Perrin v HMRC [2018] UKUT 156 (TCC) Stage 3 is OBJECTIVE-with-actual-characteristics: would a reasonable taxpayer in P's position with P's actual characteristics have viewed those facts as reasonable excuse?
- **Misframe 4:** "I relied on my accountant is a reasonable excuse." FALSE per para 23(2) — reliance on another person is excluded UNLESS the appellant took reasonable steps to prevent the failure (i.e. oversight: positive instruction + deadline reminder + confirmation request + follow-up).
- **Misframe 5:** "Inability to pay is special circumstances." FALSE per para 16(2)(a) — inability to pay is statutorily EXCLUDED from special circumstances. Hardship is for HMRC time-to-pay arrangements, not Sch 55 para 16 reduction.
- **Misframe 6:** "Insufficient funds is reasonable excuse." FALSE per para 23(2)(a) — insufficient funds is EXCLUDED unless circumstances beyond P's control caused the shortage.
- **Misframe 7:** "Appeal window is 60 days." FALSE per TMA 1970 s.31A — 30 days from the penalty notice. Non-negotiable.
- **Misframe 8:** "You can appeal at any time as long as you have a good reason." FALSE per Martland framework — late appeals require formal application; FTT exercises discretion on three-stage test; long delays + thin reasons fail.
- **Misframe 9:** "FTT can overturn HMRC's special-circumstances decision on the merits." FALSE per Sch 55 para 22 — FTT supervisory only (Wednesbury-equivalent grounds); cannot substitute its own view.
- **Misframe 10:** "Reasonable excuse and special circumstances are alternative names for the same thing." FALSE — they are independent statutory routes (para 23 vs para 16) with different burdens, tests, remedies. Should typically be pleaded BOTH simultaneously.
- **Misframe 11:** "Statutory review is the same as an appeal." FALSE per TMA 1970 s.49 — statutory review is HMRC INTERNAL review; appeal is to the FTT. Review request stops the FTT clock pending conclusion; on conclusion notice, fresh 30-day FTT window opens.
- **Misframe 12:** "Claim-only returns don't carry late-filing penalty risk." FALSE per §18.4 — the £100/£200/£300 escalator applies to ANY missed ATED return, including claim-only returns where relief covers the chargeable period. The tax-geared paras 5-6 effectively don't bite at zero tax, but the escalator does.
- **Misframe 13:** "Perrin gave appellants a wider reasonable-excuse test." FALSE — Perrin LOCKED the objective-with-actual-characteristics test (Stage 3) + the four-stage framework; it did not widen the test, it disciplined it.

## FAQ expansion (RUN session polishes prose; 12-14 FAQs target)

1. **Q: I've received an ATED late-filing penalty notice, what's the very first thing to do?**
   A: Diary the 30-day appeal window. Per TMA 1970 s.31A, you have 30 days from the date of the penalty notice to either (a) appeal direct to the First-tier Tribunal, (b) request HMRC statutory review under TMA 1970 s.49 (which stops the FTT clock pending review conclusion), or (c) pay without prejudice + lodge a protective appeal. Doing nothing means the 30-day clock runs out and any later appeal requires a Martland-framework late-appeal application that is hard to win. Engage specialist tax representation within 7 days of the notice.

2. **Q: What's the difference between "reasonable excuse" and "special circumstances"?**
   A: They are TWO independent statutory routes with different tests. **Reasonable excuse** (FA 2009 Sch 55 para 23) is the appellant's defence on an OBJECTIVE test: would a reasonable taxpayer with the appellant's actual characteristics have viewed the facts as reasonable excuse? Per Perrin v HMRC [2018] UKUT 156 (TCC), four-stage test. **Special circumstances** (FA 2009 Sch 55 para 16) is HMRC's DISCRETIONARY judgment: "if HMRC think it right because of special circumstances, they may reduce the penalty". The FTT can only review HMRC's exercise of that discretion on Wednesbury-equivalent supervisory grounds; cannot substitute its own view (per Sch 55 para 22). You should typically plead BOTH routes simultaneously: para 23 as primary; para 16 as fallback.

3. **Q: What is the Perrin v HMRC four-stage test?**
   A: Per Perrin v HMRC [2018] UKUT 156 (TCC), the Upper Tribunal locked the reasonable-excuse framework: **Stage 1**, establish the facts on the balance of probabilities. **Stage 2**, decide which of those facts are relevant to the alleged "reasonable excuse". **Stage 3**, apply the objective test, would a reasonable taxpayer in the appellant's position, with the appellant's actual knowledge and characteristics, have viewed those facts as a reasonable excuse? **Stage 4**, ensure remedy without unreasonable delay once the excuse ceased. The objective-with-actual-characteristics test in Stage 3 is the most-litigated part: not subjective, not fully objective, calibrated to the appellant's actual position.

4. **Q: My accountant failed to file the return, isn't that reasonable excuse?**
   A: NOT automatically. Per FA 2009 Sch 55 para 23(2)(b), reliance on another person is EXCLUDED from reasonable excuse UNLESS the appellant took reasonable steps to prevent the failure. Reasonable steps means oversight, not just delegation. The typical evidence pattern HMRC + FTT look for: positive instruction with explicit deadline; deadline reminder communication; confirmation request to the agent; follow-up if no confirmation; substitute filing protocol if agent unresponsive. Mere "I gave it to the accountant" fails routinely. ATED corporate filers have particular exposure here because corporate delegation is routine; oversight evidence must be adduced explicitly.

5. **Q: Can I argue inability to pay as special circumstances?**
   A: NO. FA 2009 Sch 55 para 16(2)(a) explicitly EXCLUDES inability to pay from special circumstances. Hardship is for HMRC time-to-pay arrangements under TMA 1970 s.108 / FA 2008 / Statement of Practice 6/95, NOT for Sch 55 para 16 reduction. If your company genuinely cannot pay the penalty, the relief route is HMRC time-to-pay, not penalty appeal.

6. **Q: What actually counts as special circumstances?**
   A: Narrow. Examples that have succeeded: HMRC mis-direction in correspondence; genuine third-party event beyond the appellant's reasonable contemplation (regulatory backlog, system failure, supplier insolvency); HMRC error in calculating the penalty amount. The RoE-registration backlog 2022-23 was a common ATED para 16 angle because the RoE blockage prevented timely access to information needed for the ATED return. The ECCTA ID-verification rollout 2024+ may surface similar angles. Specialist representation essential because the test is HMRC's subjective judgment and submissions must be tailored.

7. **Q: My ATED return was claim-only (s.133 property-rental-business relief covers the full chargeable period), do I really still owe a late-filing penalty?**
   A: YES. The FA 2009 Sch 55 paras 1, 4, 5, 6 escalator (£100 + £200 + £300 + tax-geared) applies to ANY missed ATED return, including claim-only returns where relief fully covers the chargeable period (so no tax was actually due). The tax-geared paras 5-6 effectively don't bite at zero tax (the 5% of tax due defaults to £300 minimum), but the £100/£200/£300 escalator does. Para 23 reasonable-excuse + para 16 special-reduction routes remain available; the appeal mechanics are identical.

8. **Q: How long do I have to lodge an appeal after the penalty notice?**
   A: 30 days from the date of the penalty notice per TMA 1970 s.31A. Non-negotiable. If you cannot meet the 30-day window, you can request HMRC statutory review under TMA 1970 s.49 within the same 30 days; the review request stops the FTT clock pending HMRC's review conclusion notice, then a fresh 30-day FTT window opens. Late appeals (i.e. lodged after the 30 days without an in-window review request) require formal Martland-framework application: length of delay + reason + balancing exercise. Long delays + thin reasons fail.

9. **Q: What's the cumulative penalty exposure if I've been missing ATED returns for 5 years?**
   A: Each chargeable period generates a SEPARATE Sch 55 escalator. A 5-year non-filer for a single £2m dwelling faces 5 × (£100 + £200 + £300) = £3,000 in base penalties, plus tax-geared penalties under paras 5-6 (5% per failure of any tax due, behaviour-tiered under paras 17-18 with 100% / 70% / 30% maxima for deliberate-concealed / deliberate-not-concealed / careless respectively, mitigated by prompted / unprompted disclosure). For multi-dwelling structures the arithmetic multiplies. The HP §18.7 HMRC OTM-letter campaign typically triggers such cases; specialist disclosure + DDS (Digital Disclosure Service) is the standard remediation route.

10. **Q: Should I argue both reasonable excuse and special circumstances?**
    A: TYPICALLY YES, simultaneously. They are independent statutory routes with different tests, burdens, and remedies. Para 23 reasonable-excuse is the primary defence (full discharge if successful). Para 16 special-reduction is the fallback (penalty reduction or stay). The two are not mutually exclusive; HMRC + FTT process both. Pleading only one route foregoes the other. Specialist tax representation will plead both with appropriate fact-evidence framing for each.

11. **Q: Can the FTT overturn HMRC's special-circumstances decision on the merits?**
    A: NO. Per FA 2009 Sch 55 para 22, the FTT's jurisdiction over HMRC's special-circumstances exercise is SUPERVISORY only, equivalent to administrative-law Wednesbury grounds: irrationality, failure to consider relevant matters, considering irrelevant matters, procedural unfairness. The FTT CANNOT substitute its own view on whether special circumstances existed. This is the structural difference vs para 23 reasonable excuse, where the FTT exercises full original jurisdiction on the facts and the objective test.

12. **Q: My company secretary was on parental leave when the ATED deadline came up, is that reasonable excuse?**
    A: It depends on the facts adduced and the Perrin Stage 3 objective-with-actual-characteristics test. The parental leave itself is a relevant fact (Stage 2); the question is whether a reasonable corporate ATED filer in the same position (knowing the secretary's leave dates; with the company's actual size, resources, and filing history) would have implemented a substitute filing protocol before the leave began. If yes, the absence of substitute protocol is a foreseeable failure and the reasonable-excuse defence struggles at Stage 3. If the leave was unexpected (e.g. illness-triggered or emergency), the analysis differs. Stage 4 (remedy without unreasonable delay once excuse ceased) often saves later-month penalties even where the immediate £100 stands.

13. **Q: I missed the ATED filing because of a Companies House / RoE backlog, can I appeal?**
    A: Potentially YES under para 16 special-circumstances if you can adduce the regulatory-context evidence. The 2022-23 RoE rollout transition created widespread filing-blockage scenarios for overseas-enveloped structures; HMRC engaged with that context in some specific cases. Submissions must: cite the specific RoE / Companies House timeline; provide documentation; demonstrate prompt filing once the regulatory gap was resolved; reference any HMRC published acknowledgement of the transition. Para 23 reasonable-excuse is also plausible on the same facts (regulatory event beyond control + prompt remediation once excuse ceased). Plead both.
