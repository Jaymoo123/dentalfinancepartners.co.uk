# ATED late-filing penalty: appealing on reasonable excuse (Sch 55 para 23) or special circumstances (Sch 55 para 16) — the two routes and why they are not the same

## Statutory anchor
- **Primary (ATED return obligation):**
  - **FA 2013 s.159** — "Annual tax on enveloped dwellings return"; return required within 30 days of first becoming chargeable, with 90-day extended deadline for new dwellings / conversions under ss.124-125. **Verified at legislation.gov.uk on 2026-05-27.**
  - **FA 2013 s.161** — "Return to include self assessment"; ATED return must include self-assessment of tax under s.99 (rate table) plus any interim relief. **Verified at legislation.gov.uk on 2026-05-27.**
- **Primary (late-filing penalty regime):**
  - **FA 2009 Schedule 55** — late filing penalty regime. ATED falls within scope via items 11A (ATED return) and 11B (ATED adjusted chargeable amount return) added to the Sch 55 Table at para 1. The HP §18.4 verbatim-verified escalator (£100 immediate, £200 at 3 months, £300 at 6 months and 12 months, plus tax-geared penalties for deliberate / prompted / unprompted disclosure) operates through the Sch 55 paragraphs 1, 4, 5 and 6 mechanics.
- **Primary (the two appeal grounds — DO NOT COLLAPSE):**
  - **FA 2009 Sch 55 para 23 — reasonable excuse defence** (verified verbatim at legislation.gov.uk 2026-05-27): "Liability to a penalty under any paragraph of this Schedule does not arise in relation to a failure to make a return if P satisfies HMRC or (on appeal) the First-tier Tribunal or Upper Tribunal that there is a reasonable excuse for the failure." Three statutory exclusions: (i) insufficient funds unless circumstances beyond P's control, (ii) reliance on another person unless reasonable steps taken to prevent the failure, (iii) excuse ceasing — must remedy without unreasonable delay.
  - **FA 2009 Sch 55 para 16 — special reduction** (verified verbatim at legislation.gov.uk 2026-05-27): "If HMRC think it right because of special circumstances, they may reduce a penalty under any paragraph of this Schedule." Para 16(2) explicitly excludes from "special circumstances": (a) inability to pay, (b) revenue loss from one taxpayer offset by overpayments from another. Para 16(3) extends the reduction to staying a penalty or compromising proceedings.
- **Primary (appeal mechanics + 30-day clock):**
  - **TMA 1970 s.31** — right of appeal against penalties.
  - **TMA 1970 s.31A** — **30-day appeal window** from the date of the penalty notice. Non-negotiable per HP §27.9 — late appeals require reasonable-excuse application under the Martland framework (FTT discretionary jurisdiction).
  - **TMA 1970 s.49** — statutory review process: appellant can request HMRC internal review OR proceed direct to FTT; review request stops the 30-day FTT clock pending HMRC's review conclusion.
- **Primary (controlling case-law on the reasonable excuse test):**
  - **Perrin v HMRC [2018] UKUT 156 (TCC)** — Upper Tribunal four-stage test for reasonable excuse (per HP §27.9 lock): (1) establish facts on the balance of probabilities; (2) decide which of those facts are relevant to the alleged "reasonable excuse"; (3) apply objective test — would a reasonable taxpayer in P's position, with P's actual knowledge and characteristics, have viewed those facts as a reasonable excuse; (4) ensure remedy without unreasonable delay once excuse ceased.
  - **Martland v HMRC [2018] UKUT 178 (TCC)** — Upper Tribunal three-stage framework for late-appeal applications (length of delay + reason for delay + balancing exercise on prejudice).
- **House position reference:** §2 (ATED bands 2026/27 — verified gov.uk 2026-05-22), **§18 (full Wave 3 ATED extension)**, §18.4 (return mechanics + verified late-filing penalty schedule), §27 (HMRC enquiry + appeals architecture full lock — penalties + appeals chapters), §27.4 (the Perrin v HMRC four-stage UT framework verbatim), §27.5 (Martland late-appeal framework), §27.9 (do-not-write list for penalty / appeal architecture — multiple specific anti-patterns including "reasonable excuse is HMRC discretion" / "appeal window is 60 days" / etc.).

## Framing differentiator (anti-templating, anti-cannibalisation)
This page is the **practical-appeal-mechanics** ATED page, distinct from B9 (`ated-penalties-and-appeals`, the broader penalties-and-appeals catalogue) and B16 (`avoiding-common-mistakes-with-enveloped-dwelling-tax`, the upstream-error-prevention page). The framing differentiator is the **two-routes refusal-to-collapse**: most popular commentary blurs "reasonable excuse" and "special circumstances" into a single "appeal on grounds of hardship / misunderstanding" — they are independent statutory tests with different burdens.

Specifically the page must:

1. **Refuse the collapse.** Para 23 (reasonable excuse) is an objective test the appellant carries the burden on — what would a reasonable taxpayer with the appellant's actual characteristics have viewed as excuse? Para 16 (special circumstances) is HMRC's discretionary judgment — and the FTT can only review HMRC's exercise of that discretion on Wednesbury-equivalent grounds, not substitute its own view (per the statutory architecture of Sch 55 para 22). The two routes have different burdens, different tests, and different remedies.
2. **Anchor the Perrin v HMRC four-stage framework.** Most blog posts say "you need to show you had a good reason" — that is not the test. Per Perrin: (1) facts on balance of probabilities, (2) which are relevant, (3) objective-with-actual-characteristics test, (4) remedy without unreasonable delay once excuse ceased. Each stage is fact-specific evidence the appellant must adduce.
3. **Apply the three statutory exclusions.** Para 23 specifically excludes insufficient funds (unless beyond control), unconsidered reliance on others, and failure to remedy once excuse ceased. The most common ATED late-filing appeal is the "I relied on my agent" line — para 23(2) carves that out unless the appellant took reasonable steps to prevent the failure (which means oversight, not just delegation).
4. **Apply the para 16 exclusions.** Inability to pay is NOT special circumstances per para 16(2)(a). Hardship is for HMRC time-to-pay arrangements, not Sch 55 para 16 reduction. The genuine special-circumstances cases are narrow: HMRC error, third-party event beyond P's reasonable contemplation, regulatory event causing the failure (e.g. RoE-registration backlog blocking ATED filing in 2022-23 was a common para 16 angle).
5. **The 30-day clock.** TMA 1970 s.31A — non-negotiable; appeal must be lodged within 30 days of the penalty notice OR a review must be requested within that window. Late appeals require Martland-framework application to the FTT; long delays will not succeed.
6. **Note the ATED-specific operational angle.** ATED filers are typically non-natural persons (companies, partnerships with corporate member, CIS). The "reliance on agent" pattern (para 23(2)) is more frequent in ATED appeals than in personal-income-tax SA appeals because corporate filers routinely delegate. The objective test (Perrin stage 3) does NOT relax for corporate appellants — directors' oversight responsibility is part of the actual-characteristics matrix.

## Key questions this page must answer
1. What is the ATED late-filing penalty schedule, exactly? (Per HP §18.4 verified gov.uk 2026-05-22: £100 immediate, £200 at 3 months, £300 at 6 months and 12 months, plus tax-geared penalties under FA 2009 Sch 55 paras 5-6 for failures over 12 months. ATED falls in Sch 55 via items 11A + 11B at the Table at para 1.)
2. **The two appeal routes — what is the difference?** (Para 23 reasonable excuse is the appellant's defence on an objective test; para 16 special reduction is HMRC's discretionary judgment. Different burdens, different tests, different remedies. Sessions writing "appeal on reasonable excuse or special circumstances" must not collapse them.)
3. **What is the Perrin v HMRC four-stage test?** (Per HP §27.9: (1) facts on balance of probabilities, (2) which facts are relevant to the alleged excuse, (3) objective test with the appellant's actual knowledge and characteristics, (4) remedy without unreasonable delay once excuse ceased. The Upper Tribunal controlling authority since 2018.)
4. **What are the para 23(2) statutory exclusions?** (Insufficient funds unless beyond control; reliance on another person unless reasonable steps taken; failure to remedy once excuse ceased. The "I relied on my agent" line fails unless oversight evidence is adduced — Stage 2 walks the corporate-filer evidence pattern.)
5. **What is the special circumstances test (para 16)?** (HMRC subjective: "they may reduce a penalty if they think it right because of special circumstances"; FTT review on Wednesbury-equivalent supervisory grounds only — cannot substitute its own view. Para 16(2) excludes inability to pay + cross-taxpayer balancing.)
6. **What does count as special circumstances in practice?** (Narrow: HMRC error / mis-direction; third-party event beyond reasonable contemplation; regulatory event causing the failure. RoE-backlog 2022-23 / ECCTA ID-verification 2024+ overlapping with ATED 30-day filing window are realistic angles. Stage 2 may cite reported FTT cases.)
7. **The 30-day appeal window — non-negotiable.** (TMA 1970 s.31A; appeal OR statutory review request within 30 days of penalty notice. Late appeals require Martland-framework application; long delays rarely succeed.)
8. What is the statutory review route under TMA 1970 s.49? (Appellant can request HMRC internal review; review request stops the 30-day FTT clock pending review; FTT clock restarts on review conclusion notice with a fresh 30-day window.)
9. What about tax-geared penalties (Sch 55 paras 5-6)? (Beyond 12 months: 5% of tax due OR £300 if greater. Behaviour-tiered: deliberate-concealed 100%, deliberate not-concealed 70%, careless 30%, mitigated by prompted / unprompted disclosure per Sch 55 paras 17-18.)
10. What about claim-only ATED returns (relief covers the entire chargeable period, no tax due)? (Failing to file even a claim-only return triggers the £100/£200/£300 escalator. Para 23 reasonable-excuse defence is still available; para 16 special-reduction route also still available. The tax-geared paras 5-6 effectively don't bite where no tax was due.)
11. What is the cumulative-penalty exposure for a multi-year ATED non-filer? (Each chargeable period generates a separate Sch 55 escalator — a five-year non-filer faces five sets of £100+£200+£300+ (tax-geared) per dwelling per year. The arithmetic is harsh; HP §18.7 OTM-letter campaign context is the typical trigger.)
12. What is the planning recommendation for an appellant? (Engage specialist within 7 days of penalty notice; gather evidence to all four Perrin stages plus oversight evidence for para 23(2); review HMRC's penalty-notice statement for special-circumstances framing under para 16; lodge appeal or review request within the 30-day window without exception.)
