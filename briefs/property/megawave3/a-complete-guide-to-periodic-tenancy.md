# A Complete Guide to Periodic Tenancy (After the Renters' Rights Act 2025)

## Statutory anchor
- Primary (pre-existing periodic-tenancy law): Housing Act 1988 s.5 — "Security of tenure" — converts a fixed-term assured shorthold tenancy (AST) to a **statutory periodic tenancy** on expiry of the fixed term where the tenant remains in occupation. The mechanism predates the Renters' Rights Act 2025 and continues to operate for tenancies outside the RRA reform window.
- Primary (RRA 2025 default-periodic regime): Renters' Rights Act 2025 (2025 c. 26) — Royal Assent 27 October 2025; commencement phased. The substantive tenancy-reform Part (the periodic-from-grant default) was brought into force by SI 2026/421 (Commencement No. 2 and Transitional and Saving Provisions Regulations 2026) on 1 May 2026. **All new assured tenancies are periodic from grant; existing fixed-term ASTs convert to periodic on commencement.** §20.3 lock.
- Primary (rent period and rent-rise mechanic): RRA 2025 s.6 — "Statutory procedure for increases of rent" — restricts rent increases to the Section 13 HA 1988 route, once per 12-month period, with 2 months' written notice; tribunal-only challenge route. §20.6 lock.
- Primary (advance-rent prohibitions): RRA 2025 s.8 (inserting HA 1988 s.4B — post-tenancy advance-rent terms of no effect) + RRA 2025 s.9 (amending Tenant Fees Act 2019 — pre-tenancy advance-rent prohibited payment). §20.8 lock.
- Primary (tenant notice period): RRA 2025 s.5 (amending Protection from Eviction Act 1977 to insert PEA 1977 s.5(1ZA)) — tenants can end the tenancy with 2 months' written notice. §20.2 lock.
- Primary (section 21 abolition + reformed section 8): RRA 2025 s.2 ("Abolition of assured shorthold tenancies", omits HA 1988 Pt.1 Ch.2 + s.6A) and RRA 2025 s.3 (Sch 1 reformed possession grounds). §20.2 lock.
- Primary (rent-receipt-basis tax recognition for landlords): ITTOIA 2005 s.271A (cash basis default for individual landlords under £150,000 property + trading turnover) + s.272A-272B (finance cost restriction). §4 lock.
- Supporting (deposit protection): Housing Act 2004 ss.212-215 + Tenancy Deposit Schemes Order 2007 — three authorised schemes (DPS, TDS, mydeposits); 30-day deposit-information window; failure penalty up to 3x deposit + bar on Section 21 notices (the bar is moot post-RRA-2025 commencement because s.21 is abolished, but the 3x deposit penalty survives).
- Supporting (CT base-cost mechanics on disposal): TCGA 1992 s.222 + s.223 (PPR), s.1H (CGT residential rates). §5 lock.
- House position reference: §20.3 (periodic-tenancy default + AST phase-out) is the canonical lock; §20.2 (s.21 abolition + reformed s.8); §20.6 (rent-rise mechanics + Section 13); §20.8 (advance-rent prohibitions); §20.9 (transition for existing tenancies); §20.11 (PTP tax-side hooks); §20.12 (commencement timeline locked to SI 2025/1354 + SI 2026/421); §26.1 (RRA 2025 pointer + naming discipline). **No new HP lock needed.** F-15 (HA 1988 Sch 1 paras 3D / 3E — 21+ year carve-out) and F-14 (s.5(1ZA) statutory hook for tenant notice) corrections already in §20.

## Key questions this page must answer

1. **What is a periodic tenancy in one paragraph** — a tenancy where the tenant has the right to occupy on a recurring rent-period basis (monthly, in practice, since RRA 2025 caps the rent period at monthly), with no fixed end date set in the contract. The tenant ends it by giving notice; the landlord ends it by serving a reformed Section 8 notice on one of the statutory grounds. Distinguished from a fixed-term tenancy (where both parties are bound to the end date) and from a tenancy at will (where either party can end immediately).
2. **The four routes by which a tenancy becomes periodic** — (a) **periodic from grant** (default under RRA 2025 s.2 omitting HA 1988 Pt.1 Ch.2 + the periodic-default mechanic; in force 1 May 2026 per SI 2026/421); (b) **statutory periodic** on expiry of a pre-RRA AST fixed term under HA 1988 s.5 (still operative for tenancies outside the RRA reform window or where the fixed term has expired before the tenant continues in occupation); (c) **contractual periodic** where the tenancy agreement itself describes a rolling tenancy with no fixed term (less common post-RRA because contractual fixed-term terms over 6 months are no longer enforceable in the assured regime); (d) **common-law periodic** where there is no written tenancy agreement and the tenant has been paying rent on a recurring basis with the landlord's acceptance (the rent acceptance creates an implied periodic tenancy by conduct).
3. **What changed on 1 May 2026** — the major SI 2026/421 commencement brought the periodic-from-grant default into force. From that date, every new assured tenancy in England is periodic from the day it is granted; every existing fixed-term AST converts to periodic; six-monthly or annual rent periods are no longer permitted (RRA 2025 s.6 + §20.3 lock). Section 21 no-fault eviction is abolished by RRA 2025 s.2; possession is by reformed Section 8 grounds only.
4. **What is the standard rent period now** — monthly. RRA 2025 caps the rent period at one month; six-monthly and annual rent contracts are unenforceable. This shifts the cashflow rhythm for landlords who previously took rent quarterly or six-monthly (typical at the higher end of the market). Rent is collectible monthly; the cash-basis rental-income recognition under ITTOIA 2005 s.271A follows the actual receipt point.
5. **Tenant notice rights** — the tenant can end the tenancy at any point with 2 months' written notice (PEA 1977 s.5(1ZA) inserted by RRA 2025 s.5). No minimum-occupation lock-in; no requirement to wait until a "natural end" of a notional fixed term. Operational consequence for landlords: shorter average tenancy length, more void-period exposure, higher re-let costs across a portfolio over time.
6. **Landlord notice rights — possession via reformed Section 8** — 17 grounds restructured (§20.2 lock). Selected operative grounds: Ground 8 (8 weeks' rent arrears) + Ground 10/11 (persistent arrears) for rent-arrears possession; Ground 12 (breach of tenancy obligation); Ground 14 (anti-social behaviour); new landlord-sale ground (with 12-month re-letting restriction under HA 1988 s.16E inserted by RRA 2025 s.15); new landlord-occupation ground (same 12-month restriction). Notice periods vary by ground (2 weeks for Ground 14; 4 weeks for landlord-sale; up to 4 months for some structural grounds). Forward-link to the Section 8 grounds deep-dive page.
7. **Rent rises on a periodic tenancy** — Section 13 procedure only. One rent increase per 12-month period; 2 months' written notice on the prescribed form; tenant can challenge at the First-Tier Tribunal (Property Chamber); tribunal cannot set rent above the landlord's proposed amount. Contractual rent-review clauses are unenforceable for rent increases (§20.6 lock). Forward-link to the Section 13 deep-dive.
8. **Advance rent and the deposit-substitute trap** — RRA 2025 s.8 + s.9 abolish the practice of demanding 6 or 12 months' rent upfront as a deposit-substitute. Pre-tenancy: any pre-lease rent payment is a prohibited payment under the Tenant Fees Act 2019 (RRA 2025 s.9 amendment). Post-tenancy: terms providing for rent due in advance of the rent period are of no effect (HA 1988 s.4B inserted by RRA 2025 s.8), with narrow carve-outs for an "initial 28-day period" and the "permitted pre-tenancy period" (the gap between contract execution and the first day of the tenancy). §20.8 lock.
9. **Deposit on a periodic tenancy** — five-week-rent maximum under the Tenant Fees Act 2019 (six weeks where annual rent exceeds £50,000); must be protected in an authorised scheme (DPS, TDS, or mydeposits) within 30 days under HA 2004 ss.212-215, with the prescribed information served on the tenant in the same window. Failure exposes the landlord to a court-ordered penalty of 1x to 3x the deposit. The post-RRA-2025 bar on Section 21 notices is no longer the operative consequence (s.21 is abolished); the 3x penalty survives as the deterrent.
10. **Tax-side recognition for landlords on a periodic tenancy** — (a) rental income recognised on the cash basis (ITTOIA 2005 s.271A) at the point of receipt; monthly rent period aligns the receipt rhythm with the tax-recognition rhythm. (b) Vacant-period costs (council tax, utilities, building insurance, mortgage interest) remain deductible during void periods between tenancies as expenses of the property business — voids do not break the property-business continuity for tax purposes. (c) Mortgage interest under the s.24 restriction (§4 lock) is unaffected by tenancy type. (d) On eventual sale, CGT base cost is unaffected by tenancy type; the let-out-while-living-elsewhere position triggers the s.223 final-9-month exemption (TCGA 1992) at the end of ownership only, not over the tenancy period. (e) Section 13 rent-rise cadence interacts with cashflow modelling: where mortgage interest rises faster than the annual rent uplift permitted, the s.24 reducer may bind earlier (the §20.11 lock cross-reference). Forward-link to the s.24 cluster and the void-period deductions cluster.

## Worked-example data (RUN session uses these as canvas)

### Example 1 — The four routes to periodic, mapped to a single portfolio

A landlord has three flats let in different ways as of June 2026:

- **Flat A:** new tenant signed June 2026. **Periodic from grant** under the RRA 2025 default. Monthly rent period; no fixed-term clause enforceable; tenancy ends only by tenant's 2-month notice (PEA 1977 s.5(1ZA)) or landlord's reformed Section 8 grounds.
- **Flat B:** AST signed March 2025 for 12 months. AST converted on 1 May 2026 to a periodic tenancy by RRA commencement (substantive Part brought into force via SI 2026/421 per §20.12 lock). From 1 May 2026 forward, treated identically to Flat A.
- **Flat C:** AST signed January 2024 for 12 months; tenant remained in occupation past 31 December 2024 expiry. Statutory periodic under HA 1988 s.5 from January 2025 to 30 April 2026, then converted to RRA-default periodic on 1 May 2026.

All three are now monthly-period assured tenancies with the same operational rules.

### Example 2 — Rent-rise mechanics on a periodic tenancy

Mr Patel (landlord) wants to increase rent on Flat A from £1,400 to £1,500 per month. Last increase served June 2025 (effective August 2025).

- **Earliest valid notice date:** July 2026 (cannot serve another increase within 12 months of August 2025 increase taking effect, per new HA 1988 s.13(4A)).
- **Notice form:** prescribed Section 13 form, served 2 months before the intended start date.
- **Tenant response options:** (a) accept and pay new rent; (b) apply to First-Tier Tribunal (Property Chamber) for determination. Tribunal sets rent at market rate but **cannot exceed the landlord's proposed amount** (RRA 2025 s.6 cap).
- **Contractual rent-review clauses in the original agreement:** unenforceable post-RRA-2025 for rent increases. Section 13 is the only route.

### Example 3 — Tenant notice + landlord notice

Same flat. In November 2026, tenant gives 2 months' written notice (PEA 1977 s.5(1ZA) inserted by RRA 2025 s.5). Tenancy ends 31 January 2027. No requirement to wait for a "natural end" of the (non-existent) fixed term.

In a parallel scenario, landlord wants possession on the landlord-sale ground. Serves 4 months' notice under reformed HA 1988 Sch 1 (introduced by RRA 2025 s.3). Property cannot be re-let within 12 months of repossession (new HA 1988 s.16E inserted by RRA 2025 s.15) — re-letting within the window triggers a financial penalty under RRA 2025 s.15 + Sch.

### Example 4 — Deposit + advance-rent boundary

Tenancy granted 1 June 2026 (post-RRA commencement). Monthly rent £1,200. Deposit cap (Tenant Fees Act 2019): 5 weeks' rent = £1,384.62. Held in DPS within 30 days under HA 2004 ss.212-215.

- **Permitted pre-tenancy payment:** rent for the "permitted pre-tenancy period" (period between contract execution and first day of tenancy) + the "initial 28-day period" rent.
- **Prohibited:** demanding 6 months' rent upfront as a deposit substitute (RRA 2025 s.9 amendment to Tenant Fees Act 2019; pre-tenancy advance-rent prohibited payment).
- **Prohibited:** contractual terms providing for rent due more than one rent-period in advance, with the carve-outs above (HA 1988 inserted-s.4B by RRA 2025 s.8; terms beyond the carve-outs are "of no effect").

### Example 5 — Tax-side recognition on a periodic let

A higher-rate-band landlord lets a flat on a periodic tenancy at £1,200/month, with a 2-month void between tenancies during 2026/27.

- **Rental income recognised on cash basis** (ITTOIA 2005 s.271A default for individuals under £150k turnover). 10 months of rent received = £12,000.
- **Void-period costs deductible:** mortgage interest pro-rata over 2 months (subject to the s.24 reducer per §4 lock); council tax during void (if landlord-responsible per the council-tax framework); building insurance; service charge. All deductible because the property business has not ceased — voids do not break business continuity for tax purposes.
- **Section 13 rent-rise interaction with the s.24 reducer:** if mortgage interest rises 6% but the landlord can only raise rent under Section 13 once per 12 months at market rate (and the tribunal caps the increase at the landlord's proposed amount), the cashflow squeeze becomes a tax-reducer-binding squeeze earlier than under a free contractual rent-review regime. §20.11 lock cross-reference.

## FAQ expansion (RUN session polishes prose; 10-12 FAQs target)

1. **Q: What is a periodic tenancy in one paragraph?**
   A: A tenancy where the tenant has the right to occupy on a recurring rent-period basis, with no fixed end date in the contract. Post-RRA-2025 the rent period is capped at monthly for new assured tenancies. The tenant ends it with 2 months' written notice (PEA 1977 s.5(1ZA)); the landlord ends it by serving a reformed Section 8 notice on one of the statutory grounds. Section 21 no-fault eviction is abolished by RRA 2025 s.2.

2. **Q: What changed on 1 May 2026?**
   A: The Renters' Rights Act 2025 substantive Part was brought into force via SI 2026/421 (Commencement No. 2 and Transitional and Saving Provisions Regulations 2026). From that date every new assured tenancy in England is periodic from grant; every existing fixed-term AST converts to periodic on commencement. Section 21 no-fault eviction is abolished. Six-monthly or annual rent periods are no longer permitted. RRA 2025 s.2 omits HA 1988 Part 1 Chapter 2 (assured shorthold tenancies) plus s.6A (demotion).

3. **Q: How does a tenancy become periodic?**
   A: Four routes. (a) Periodic from grant — the post-RRA default for new assured tenancies. (b) Statutory periodic under HA 1988 s.5 — when a pre-RRA AST fixed term expires and the tenant remains in occupation; still relevant for transitional cases and tenancies outside the assured regime. (c) Contractual periodic — where the tenancy itself describes a rolling tenancy with no fixed term. (d) Common-law periodic — implied by conduct, where a landlord accepts rent on a recurring basis without a written agreement.

4. **Q: What is the rent period now?**
   A: Monthly. RRA 2025 caps the rent period at one month for new assured tenancies. Six-monthly and annual rent contracts are unenforceable. This shifts cashflow rhythm for landlords who previously took rent quarterly or six-monthly. Rental income recognition under the cash basis (ITTOIA 2005 s.271A) follows the actual receipt point.

5. **Q: How does the tenant end the tenancy?**
   A: Two months' written notice (PEA 1977 s.5(1ZA), inserted by RRA 2025 s.5). No minimum-occupation lock-in. No requirement to wait until a "natural end" of a notional fixed term. The tenant cannot be charged for early departure beyond rent for the notice period.

6. **Q: How does the landlord end the tenancy?**
   A: Via reformed Section 8 possession grounds only — Section 21 is abolished. Grounds include Ground 8 (8 weeks' rent arrears); Grounds 10 and 11 (persistent arrears); Ground 12 (breach of obligation); Ground 14 (anti-social behaviour, 2 weeks' notice); new landlord-sale ground (4 months' notice, 12-month re-letting restriction under HA 1988 s.16E inserted by RRA 2025 s.15); new landlord-occupation ground (same 12-month restriction). Notice periods range 2 weeks to 4 months depending on the ground.

7. **Q: Can I raise the rent on a periodic tenancy?**
   A: Yes, but only via Section 13 of the Housing Act 1988 as amended by RRA 2025 s.6. One increase per 12-month period; two months' written notice on the prescribed Section 13 form; tenant can apply to the First-Tier Tribunal (Property Chamber) for determination. The tribunal can set rent at market level but cannot exceed the landlord's proposed amount. Contractual rent-review clauses are unenforceable for rent increases under assured tenancies.

8. **Q: Can I still demand 6 months' rent upfront?**
   A: No. RRA 2025 s.9 amends the Tenant Fees Act 2019 to make pre-tenancy advance-rent a prohibited payment. RRA 2025 s.8 inserts HA 1988 s.4B making post-tenancy terms requiring advance rent "of no effect", with narrow carve-outs for the "initial 28-day period" and the "permitted pre-tenancy period". The five-week deposit cap (six-week if annual rent exceeds £50,000) under TFA 2019 remains the operative deposit ceiling.

9. **Q: What happens to the deposit?**
   A: Same five-week rent cap (six weeks for annual rent over £50,000) under the Tenant Fees Act 2019. Must be protected in an authorised scheme (DPS, TDS, or mydeposits) within 30 days under HA 2004 ss.212-215, with the prescribed information served on the tenant in the same window. Failure exposes the landlord to a court-ordered penalty of 1x to 3x the deposit. The bar on Section 21 notices for non-compliance is no longer the operative consequence (s.21 is abolished); the 3x penalty survives.

10. **Q: How does this affect my tax position?**
    A: Several touch-points. Rental income recognised on cash basis at point of receipt (ITTOIA 2005 s.271A). Mortgage interest under the s.24 restriction (§4 lock) is unchanged by tenancy type — converted to a 20% basic-rate reducer regardless. Void-period costs (council tax, utilities, insurance, interest) remain deductible as expenses of the property business during voids. On eventual sale, CGT base cost is unaffected by tenancy type. The Section 13 rent-rise cadence may interact with the s.24 reducer in tight cashflow scenarios. Sibling pages cover the s.24 cluster and void-period deductions in depth.

11. **Q: Does this apply in Wales or Scotland?**
    A: No. The RRA 2025 substantive reform applies to England only (PTP advises England-only per §20.12). Wales operates under the Renting Homes (Wales) Act 2016 (occupation contracts; different terminology); Scotland under the Private Housing (Tenancies) (Scotland) Act 2016 (private residential tenancies). Each devolved regime uses different statutory language and different tenant-notice and rent-increase mechanics; do not collapse the three positions.

12. **Q: How does this guide differ from `renters-rights-act-periodic-tenancy-switch-landlord-obligations`?**
    A: That page is the landlord-obligations bundle that accompanies the periodic switch — deposit re-protection, gas safety, EICR, EPC, Right to Rent re-check, Section 8 re-papering of notice templates. This page sits upstream: the definitional / orientation guide for the periodic-tenancy concept itself. Read this for the definitional ground and the four routes by which a tenancy becomes periodic; read the obligations page for the day-of-conversion compliance work.
