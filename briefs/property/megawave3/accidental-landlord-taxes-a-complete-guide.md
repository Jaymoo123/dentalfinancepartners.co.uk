# Accidental Landlord Taxes: The Complete UK Guide

## Statutory anchor
- Primary (income tax architecture): ITTOIA 2005 Part 3 (property income) — s.264 (charge to tax on profits of property business), s.270 (deductions allowable on the same basis as a trade), s.272 (capital expenditure exclusion), s.272A-272B (finance cost restriction, inserted by FA 2015 s.24); §4 lock.
- Primary (replacement of domestic items): ITTOIA 2005 s.311A — "Replacement domestic items relief" — verified verbatim at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2005/5/section/311A on 2026-05-27. Conditions A-D; replaces the abolished wear-and-tear allowance from April 2016.
- Primary (CGT on disposal): TCGA 1992 s.222 (relief on disposal of private residence), s.223 (amount of relief — final-period exemption 9 months, verified verbatim at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12/section/223 on 2026-05-27), s.223B (lettings relief — narrowed by FA 2020 s.27 to shared-occupation cases only); §5 lock for CGT residential rates.
- Primary (60-day reporting): TCGA 1992 Sch 2 paras 6-12 (residents' UK residential property return) — Stage 2 sub-agent re-verifies the schedule reference at write time; framing is "the in-year 60-day CGT return" not "30-day" (the deadline moved from 30 to 60 days on 27 October 2021 per the Autumn Budget; codified in FA 2022 s.23).
- Primary (HMRC notification): TMA 1970 s.7 (notice of chargeability — duty to notify within 6 months after the end of the tax year if chargeable to income tax or CGT and no notice to file has been issued); Sch 41 FA 2008 failure-to-notify penalty regime (§27.3 lock).
- Primary (MTD for ITSA threshold + timing): ITTOIA 2005 Part 3 + FA 2017 Sch 14 mandate framework; current threshold £50,000 gross property + trading turnover from April 2026, £30,000 from April 2027 (Stage 2 verifies thresholds at gov.uk/MTD at write time); §3 + §19.1 lock.
- Primary (Let Property Campaign route): HMRC voluntary-disclosure facility under TMA 1970 s.7 + Sch 24 FA 2007 penalty discount framework; §27.6 lock.
- Supporting: §7 (April 2027 property income tax surcharge — house framing); §17.4 (NRCGT if the accidental landlord becomes non-resident); §6 (FHL abolition transition — relevant if the let was structured as an FHL pre-April-2025).
- House position reference: §4 (Section 24 mechanics), §5 (CGT 2026/27 rates + 60-day reporting), §3 (MTD overview) + §19 (MTD operational depth), §27.6 (LPC catch-up route), §7 (2027 surcharge framing). All anchors locked; no new HP lock needed.

## Key questions this page must answer

1. **Who is an accidental landlord** — the five canonical routes: inherited a property and decided to let rather than sell; let-to-buy (kept the old home when moving); job-driven temporary relocation (working overseas, sometimes called "consent-to-let" where lender approval was needed); blended-family or cohabitation merger; divorce / separation interim arrangement. Each route triggers slightly different sub-questions (PPR sub-clock, consent-to-let lender duties, CGT base-cost-from-probate-value).
2. **The HMRC notification reflex** — TMA 1970 s.7. You have six months from the end of the tax year (ie. 5 October following the tax year in which letting began) to notify HMRC that you are chargeable to income tax on rental profits. The notification triggers either a Self-Assessment registration (if you're not already in SA) or an SA100 amendment route (if you are). Missing the notification window exposes you to a failure-to-notify penalty under Sch 41 FA 2008 — capped at 30% of potential lost revenue for non-deliberate, rising to 100% for deliberate-concealed (§27.3 lock).
3. **What counts as taxable rental income** — gross rent received in the tax year (cash basis from April 2017 default for individual landlords under £150k turnover; ITTOIA 2005 s.271A); not rent receivable. Includes amounts kept from deposit at end of let (compensation for damage / unpaid rent); excludes amounts later refunded. Repair costs, agent fees, ground rent, service charges, building insurance, mortgage *interest* (now under s.24 restriction not direct deduction — see Q5).
4. **What can you deduct against rental income** — repairs (not improvements: capital vs revenue line); replacement of domestic items under ITTOIA 2005 s.311A (sofa, fridge, washing machine, carpets when replacing like-for-like or as close as commercially available); agent and management fees; ground rent and service charges; building and contents insurance; legal fees for short lets and renewals; council tax and utilities during void periods; mileage at HMRC approved rates for trips to inspect the property. The pre-let expenses point (the seven-year pre-letting-business test under ITTOIA 2005 s.271 / s.57 trading-business analogy) is parked here at one-paragraph depth and forward-linked to the pre-letting deep-dive page.
5. **Section 24 — the finance-cost restriction** — mortgage interest is no longer a deduction against rental profit; it is converted to a 20% basic-rate tax reducer. The full restriction has been in force since 6 April 2020. For higher-rate or additional-rate taxpayers, this can flip a cashflow-profitable letting into an income-tax loss after the reducer (the headline tax bill exceeds the cash profit). Worked-example placeholder for Stage 2 with rate-by-reference framing per §16.27. Forward-link to the Section 24 cluster pillar.
6. **MTD for ITSA — does it apply to a single accidental landlord** — depends on gross rental + self-employment turnover. From April 2026, mandatory at £50,000+; from April 2027, £30,000+. Below £30,000, the SA100 paper-or-online return remains the route. Forward-link to `mtd-itsa-accidental-landlords-do-i-need-to-file-digitally` for the threshold deep-dive and to `mtd-made-simple-for-landlords-with-jointly-owned-properties` for joint-ownership mechanics.
7. **Capital gains tax when you eventually sell** — TCGA 1992 s.1H residential property rate (18% basic-rate-band gain, 24% above; rates verified per §5 lock at write time). Final-9-month exemption under s.223(1) — the last 9 months of ownership count as deemed-residence even if the property was let throughout that window. PPR fraction-of-ownership relief if you lived in the property as your main residence at some point. Lettings relief now narrow under s.223B (FA 2020 s.27 — only available where you shared occupancy with the tenant, ie. lodger-style; pure let-out-while-you-lived-elsewhere does NOT qualify).
8. **60-day in-year CGT reporting** — TCGA 1992 Sch 2 in-year UK residential property return — required within 60 days of completion (extended from 30 days on 27 October 2021 per FA 2022 s.23). Payment-on-account of estimated CGT due at the same point. Final reconciliation through SA at year-end. Missing the 60-day deadline triggers late-filing penalties even where the final CGT bill is nil. Forward-link to the 60-day reporting deep-dive page (cluster sibling).
9. **What if you let for years without telling HMRC** — the Let Property Campaign route. Voluntary disclosure under HMRC's published terms; penalty discount versus enforced enquiry (often single-figure-percent versus 30%-100% under Sch 41 / Sch 24). Forward-link to the LPC cluster (A12 `know-about-let-property-campaign`, A3 `benefits-of-participating-in-the-let-property-campaign`, A15 `let-property-campaign-why-voluntary-disclosure-makes-sense`, A17 `missed-taxes-let-property-campaign-to-the-rescue`). Do NOT re-walk LPC mechanics here; surface the route at one-paragraph depth + forward-link.
10. **The five most-missed tax points for accidental landlords** — closing checklist: (i) you must notify HMRC even if the net rental profit after expenses is below the personal allowance; (ii) consent-to-let from your lender is a contractual matter separate from HMRC compliance, but failure exposes you to lender enforcement; (iii) inherited property base cost is probate value (TCGA 1992 s.62), not original-purchaser cost; (iv) PPR election where you have two residences is a written election to HMRC within two years of acquiring the second residence (TCGA 1992 s.222(5)); (v) the 60-day CGT return clock starts at completion of sale, not exchange — diaries set accordingly.

## Worked-example data (RUN session uses these as canvas)

### Example 1 — Patel inherited flat (full journey)

Mrs Patel inherits her late mother's flat in February 2024. Probate value £325,000 (the CGT base cost under TCGA 1992 s.62). She decides to let rather than sell; first tenant in from 1 May 2024.

- **Notify HMRC by 5 October 2025** under TMA 1970 s.7 (within 6 months of the end of the 2024/25 tax year). Registers for Self Assessment.
- **2024/25 tax year:** rent £14,400 (11 months × £1,200 + 1 letting void), allowable expenses (letting-agent fee £1,440, building insurance £320, gas safety certificate £85, ground rent + service charge £1,200, repair to boiler £480) = £3,525. Net rental profit £10,875.
- **Section 24 reducer:** mortgage interest £4,200 over the year. NOT deducted from the £14,400; instead converted to a basic-rate reducer of 20% × £4,200 = £840 against tax due. As a higher-rate taxpayer (£60k salary), her tax on rental profit is 40% × £10,875 = £4,350; reducer £840; net rental tax £3,510.
- **2026/27 onward:** if total combined property + trading turnover > £50,000 she comes within MTD ITSA from April 2026 (gov.uk MTD collection confirms threshold). Below the threshold, SA route continues.
- **Sale in 2029:** sells for £390,000. Base cost £325,000 (probate value, not original purchase). Gross gain £65,000 less annual exempt amount (RUN session verifies the current AEA at write). No PPR fraction (she never lived there). No s.223(1) final-9-month exemption (the property was never her main residence). No s.223B lettings relief (she did not share occupation). 60-day NRCGT-equivalent return within 60 days of completion under TCGA 1992 Sch 2 (TCGA Sch 2 paras 6-12; framework confirmed at `gov.uk/tax-sell-property`).

### Example 2 — Singh let-to-buy (PPR fraction matters)

Mr Singh bought his flat in March 2015 for £180,000. Lived in it as his main residence until July 2022 (7 years 4 months), then moved in with his partner and let the flat from August 2022. Sells in March 2027 for £290,000.

- **Period of ownership:** 12 years (March 2015 to March 2027).
- **PPR period:** 7 years 4 months (March 2015 to July 2022).
- **PLUS final-9-month exemption** under TCGA 1992 s.223(1): the last 9 months of ownership count as deemed-residence even though the flat was let throughout.
- **Effective PPR fraction:** 7 years 4 months + 9 months = 8 years 1 month, out of 12 years total = 67.4%.
- **Gross gain:** £110,000. PPR-exempt portion: 67.4% × £110,000 = £74,140. Non-exempt residue: £35,860.
- **Lettings relief under s.223B:** Mr Singh did NOT share occupation with the tenant. No s.223B relief. (Pre-FA-2020 the let-out-old-home would have qualified for up to £40,000 lettings relief; post-FA-2020 it does not.)
- **Annual exempt amount + 24% residential CGT rate** apply to the £35,860 non-exempt residue. 60-day in-year return within 60 days of completion.

### Example 3 — Mawell-Estate divorce interim let (consent-to-let)

Mrs Mawell moves out of the marital home in October 2024 pending divorce; the home is let from January 2025 under a consent-to-let granted by her lender. Couple owned jointly; Mrs Mawell holds 50%. Decree absolute July 2026; the home is sold in November 2026 for £495,000 (joint purchase price February 2018 £380,000).

- **Period of ownership for Mrs Mawell:** February 2018 to November 2026 = 8 years 9 months.
- **Period of occupation as main residence:** February 2018 to October 2024 = 6 years 8 months.
- **Final-9-month exemption** under s.223(1): from February 2026 to November 2026 deemed residence.
- **Effective PPR fraction:** 6 years 8 months + 9 months = 7 years 5 months, out of 8 years 9 months = 84.8%.
- **Gross 50% gain:** (495 - 380) / 2 = £57,500. PPR-exempt portion: 84.8% × £57,500 = £48,760. Non-exempt residue: £8,740 (her 50% share).
- **s.223B does not apply** (no shared occupation).
- **CGT:** 24% × (£8,740 less the relevant 50% share of annual exempt amount) on the non-exempt residue. 60-day in-year return at completion.

### Example 4 — Pre-letting expenses (the seven-year window)

Mr Patel inherits a tenanted commercial-unit-with-flat-above in November 2025. The flat tenant moves out December 2025; Mr Patel spends £14,500 between January and April 2026 on works to make the flat habitable for a new tenant — kitchen replacement (£6,200), repaint and re-carpet (£2,800), boiler service and Gas Safe certificate (£480), electrical wiring renewal in two rooms (£3,400), minor plastering (£1,620). First new tenant takes occupancy 1 May 2026.

- **Capital vs revenue line:** kitchen replacement is **capital** (single asset materially different from what was there if upgrade; deductibility against rental income blocked). Repaint and re-carpet are **revenue** (restoring to lettable state; deductible). Boiler service is **revenue**. Electrical re-wiring of two rooms is **capital** (improvement to the building's electrical infrastructure). Minor plastering is **revenue**.
- **Pre-letting "trade-equivalent" deduction:** under ITTOIA 2005 s.57 (trade analogy via s.270-271 for property income), allowable revenue expenses incurred up to 7 years before the property business commences are deductible in the first period of the business — provided the expenditure would have been allowable had the business already been running. So the £2,800 repaint/re-carpet + £480 gas-safe + £1,620 plastering = £4,900 revenue deductible against May 2026 rent.
- **The £6,200 + £3,400 capital items** go into base cost for CGT on eventual disposal, not against rental income.

## FAQ expansion (RUN session polishes prose; 10-12 FAQs target)

1. **Q: Who is an accidental landlord?**
   A: Five canonical routes: (i) inherited a property and chose to let rather than sell; (ii) let-to-buy (kept the old home when moving in with a partner or moving for work); (iii) consent-to-let on a residential mortgage during a job-driven temporary relocation; (iv) blended-family or cohabitation merger where two households kept one property and let the other; (v) divorce / separation interim arrangement before a sale completes. Each route shares the same HMRC compliance machine that applies to portfolio investors. From the day of the first month's rent, you are a UK landlord.

2. **Q: How quickly must I tell HMRC?**
   A: Within 6 months of the end of the tax year in which letting began — so for a let starting in tax year 2025/26, notify by 5 October 2026. The statutory authority is TMA 1970 s.7. Notification either triggers Self Assessment registration (if you are not already in SA) or an SA100 amendment route (if you are). Missing the window exposes you to a failure-to-notify penalty under Schedule 41 FA 2008 — capped at 30% of potential lost revenue for non-deliberate, rising to 100% for deliberate-and-concealed conduct.

3. **Q: What counts as taxable rental income?**
   A: Gross rent received in the tax year. Cash basis is the default for individual landlords with property + trading turnover under £150,000 (ITTOIA 2005 s.271A). Receipt rather than receivable basis. Includes amounts kept from deposit at end of tenancy for damage or unpaid rent. Excludes amounts later refunded. The figure entered in box 5 SA105 is gross-of-letting-agent-fees and gross-of-mortgage-interest; deductions come later in the return.

4. **Q: What can I deduct against rental income?**
   A: Repairs (not improvements — capital vs revenue line bites); replacement of domestic items under ITTOIA 2005 s.311A (sofa, fridge, washing machine, carpet replacing like-for-like); letting-agent and management fees; ground rent and service charges; building and contents insurance; legal fees for short lets and renewals; council tax and utilities during void periods; mileage at HMRC approved rates for trips to inspect the property. Pre-letting revenue expenses incurred up to 7 years before the property business commences are deductible in the first period of business under ITTOIA 2005 s.57 (trade analogy).

5. **Q: What is the Section 24 mortgage-interest restriction?**
   A: Since 6 April 2020, mortgage interest is no longer deducted from rental profit; it is converted to a 20% basic-rate tax reducer. For higher-rate or additional-rate taxpayers, the change can flip a cashflow-profitable letting into an income-tax loss after the reducer. Worked example: £20,000 interest produces a £4,000 reducer regardless of marginal rate. The cluster pillar `landlord-tax-section-24-mortgage-interest-restriction-complete-guide` walks the 8-year phase-out and the reducer mechanics in depth.

6. **Q: Will I need to file under MTD for ITSA?**
   A: From 6 April 2026, mandatory at gross property + trading turnover at or above £50,000. From 6 April 2027, mandatory at £30,000. From 6 April 2028, mandatory at £20,000 (announced in Spring Statement 2025; RUN session re-verifies at write). Below £20,000, the SA100 paper-or-online route continues. The threshold uses *gross* turnover, not net rental profit. Sibling page `mtd-itsa-accidental-landlords-do-i-need-to-file-digitally` walks the threshold-decision tree.

7. **Q: What is my CGT position when I sell?**
   A: Residential gain is taxed at 18% in the basic-rate band and 24% above (rates per §5 lock; RUN session verifies at write). PPR fraction-of-ownership relief is available if you lived in the property as your main residence at any point. Final 9 months of ownership are deemed-residence under TCGA 1992 s.223(1), even if the property was let throughout. Lettings relief under s.223B is narrow post-FA-2020 — only available where you shared occupancy with the tenant. Inherited properties take probate value as base cost under TCGA 1992 s.62.

8. **Q: What is the 60-day CGT reporting clock?**
   A: For UK residential property disposals, you must file an in-year CGT return and pay any estimated CGT due within 60 days of completion of sale (NOT exchange). The 60-day window was extended from 30 days on 27 October 2021 (FA 2022 s.23). Missing the 60-day deadline triggers late-filing penalties even where the final CGT bill is nil. Final reconciliation runs through SA at year-end. Sibling page on 60-day reporting walks the mechanics.

9. **Q: I have been letting for years and never told HMRC. What now?**
   A: The Let Property Campaign is HMRC's voluntary-disclosure facility for unpaid tax on residential rental income. Penalty discount versus enforced-enquiry track is meaningful (often single-figure-percent under the LPC offer versus 30%-100% under Sch 41 / Sch 24). Disclosure covers the past 6 years (or 20 years if HMRC alleges deliberate concealment). Sibling LPC pages `know-about-let-property-campaign` and `benefits-of-participating-in-the-let-property-campaign` walk the route in depth. For more serious historic exposure including suspected fraud, the Contractual Disclosure Facility under HMRC Code of Practice 9 is the pre-prosecution route.

10. **Q: I inherited the property — what is my CGT base cost?**
    A: Probate value at the date of death of the person you inherited from (TCGA 1992 s.62 deems acquisition at market value on death). Not the original purchaser's cost. So a flat bought by the deceased in 1980 for £25,000 and worth £325,000 at death gives the inheriting landlord a £325,000 base cost. The historic uplift is locked in at inheritance; subsequent appreciation is taxable to the heir.

11. **Q: How does this guide differ from `mtd-itsa-accidental-landlords-do-i-need-to-file-digitally`?**
    A: That page is the MTD-specific threshold-decision page (does this landlord need to file digitally given turnover, exemptions, and software). This page is the wider journey-map: every tax touchpoint an accidental landlord hits, in the order they hit them, with one forward-link per touchpoint to the deeper page. Read this guide for orientation; the MTD page for the digital-filing decision.

12. **Q: I have two homes now (let-to-buy). What is the PPR election?**
    A: Under TCGA 1992 s.222(5) a written election to HMRC within two years of acquiring the second residence nominates which of two qualifying residences is treated as your PPR for CGT purposes. Without an election, HMRC determines factually which is your main residence — usually the one you actually live in. The election is reversible by further notice and useful where a temporary second residence (the new home) has weaker capital appreciation than the original (the let property). Property advisers commonly review the election at refinance or sale events.
