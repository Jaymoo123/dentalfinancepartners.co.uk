# Do I have to pay UK tax? The short-term departure trap, the 5-year recapture rule, and what UK landlords actually owe when they leave for a year or three

## Statutory anchor
- **Primary (the recapture rule — load-bearing for this page):**
  - **TCGA 1992 s.10A** "Temporary non-residents" — substituted by Finance Act 2019; section heading verified against legislation.gov.uk at write time (`https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12/section/10A`). Per HP §17.3 (verified against HMRC CG26540 on 2026-05-22): individual is "temporary non-resident" where **period of non-UK residence is 5 years or less**, having previously been UK-resident in **4 or more of the 7 tax years before departure**. Gains realised during non-residence are deemed to arise in the year of return.
  - **ITA 2007 s.812** — the income-tax parallel for certain income (dividends, pension lump sums) received during temporary non-residence; taxed in the year of return on the same 5-year basis.
- **Primary (residence determination — yes, you may still be UK-resident):**
  - **FA 2013 Schedule 45** — Statutory Residence Test (SRT). Part 1 sets out the cascade (automatic overseas → automatic UK → sufficient ties); Part 3 contains split-year Cases 1-8. Verified at HP §17.1 against gov.uk SRT guidance.
- **Primary (UK property income — UK-taxable regardless of residence):**
  - **ITTOIA 2005 Part 3** — UK property income for individuals (s.260 onwards).
  - **CTA 2009 Part 4** — UK property income for companies.
  - **FA 1995 Sch 23 + SI 1995/2902** — Non-Resident Landlord scheme. Tenants / letting agents withhold 20% basic rate from rent paid to a non-resident landlord unless the landlord holds NRL1 / NRL2 / NRL3 approval to receive rent gross. NRL is **statutory, not treaty** per HP §16.6.
- **Primary (UK property disposals by non-residents):**
  - **TCGA 1992 s.1A + Sch 1A / 1B / 4AA** — NRCGT (FA 2019 rewrite of the older ss.14B-14H structure). 60-day return required for every UK land disposal by a non-resident, including indirect disposals of property-rich entity shares, regardless of whether tax is due. Per HP §17.4.
- **Primary (IHT — the long tail you cannot outrun in a short trip):**
  - **IHTA 1984 ss.6A-6C + ss.267ZC-267ZF** (FA 2025 architecture) — Long-Term Resident (LTR) test. **10-of-20** tax years gateway. A short-term emigrant typically remains a long-term resident throughout the overseas stint, and the tail period continues to expose them after they return (or even during).
- **Supporting (where treaty residence might also resolve):**
  - **OECD Model 2017** Art 4 tie-breaker cascade (permanent home → centre of vital interests → habitual abode → nationality → mutual agreement). Per HP §16.4.
  - **TIOPA 2010 s.18 + s.130** — foreign tax credit relief framework where the destination state also taxes UK-source income / gains. Section 18 heading verified at write time against legislation.gov.uk (`https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2010/8/section/18`) as "Entitlement to credit for foreign tax reduces UK tax by amount of the credit", Part 2 Chapter 2.
- **House position reference:** §17 in full, **§17.1 (SRT cascade), §17.2 (split-year Cases 1-3 leaving), §17.3 (s.10A 5-year-or-less + 4-of-7-preceding gateway — load-bearing for this page), §17.4 (NRCGT), §17.5 (NRL operational mechanics), §22.X (IHT LTR 10-of-20 tail period)**; §10 + §16 + §16.4 + §16.6 (DTA framing, Art 4 tie-breaker, NRL is statutory).

## Framing differentiator (anti-templating, anti-cannibalisation)
This is the **short-term-departure variant** — the "I'm going abroad for a year, maybe three, possibly five if it goes well" landlord asking whether they still owe UK tax. It is **not** the clean-break permanent-emigration case (B6 `are-you-leaving-the-uk-permanently` covers that, with its >5-tax-years exit-from-s.10A framing). It is **not** the inverse arriving-in-UK case (B7 `arriving-in-the-uk`). It is **not** a generic SRT explainer.

The framing differentiator is the **honest "you can't escape as easily as you think"** opener: most short-term emigrants assume that physically leaving the UK switches off UK tax. Wrong on four counts that compound:

1. **SRT may not even make you non-resident.** Keeping a UK home, a UK-resident spouse, UK workdays during visits home, and the country tie can leave a "year abroad" landlord still UK-resident under the sufficient-ties test — meaning their worldwide income remains UK-taxable per ITTOIA 2005 / TCGA 1992 throughout the stint.
2. **Even if you do become non-resident, s.10A 5-year-or-less recaptures gains on return.** This catches the landlord who realises a substantial non-UK gain (selling shares, a foreign holiday home, a crypto disposal) while abroad and returns within 5 complete tax years. The gain is deemed to arise in the year of return. Per HP §17.3.
3. **UK rental income stays UK-taxable regardless.** ITTOIA 2005 Part 3 (or CTA 2009 Part 4) charges UK-source property income on every owner — UK-resident, non-UK-resident, treaty-resolved-non-UK-resident, anywhere on the planet. The NRL withholding bites the moment you become non-resident; tenants / agents withhold 20% from gross rent unless NRL1 / NRL2 / NRL3 approval is in hand.
4. **UK property disposals trigger 60-day NRCGT regardless.** Selling a UK BTL while abroad triggers the 60-day return + payment obligation per TCGA 1992 s.1A; no treaty displaces this; even nil-gain disposals require the return.

Then there's the IHT LTR tail: a short-term emigrant remains a UK long-term resident throughout the overseas stint (because the 10-of-20 test almost certainly catches them) and continues to be exposed for the tail period after return. The HP §22.X tail-period table governs.

## Key questions this page must answer
1. **If I'm leaving the UK for a year or two, will I still pay UK tax?** (Honest answer: almost certainly yes, often on more than you expect. SRT may keep you UK-resident; even if not, UK rental income and UK property disposals stay UK-taxable; and s.10A recaptures non-UK gains realised during a short stint if you return within 5 years.)
2. **How does the SRT decide whether I'm still UK-resident during a short trip abroad?** (FA 2013 Sch 45 cascade. The "sufficient ties" test catches most short-term emigrants who keep a UK home, UK family, or work in the UK during visits home. Day-count thresholds for "leavers" (UK-resident in any of the preceding 3 years): 16 days max for automatic overseas test; 91+ days plus ties usually means still UK-resident.)
3. **What is split-year treatment in the departure year, and does it always apply?** (FA 2013 Sch 45 Part 3 Cases 1-3 for leaving. Case 1 = starting full-time overseas work; Case 2 = partner of Case-1 individual; Case 3 = ceasing to have any UK home. Split-year is **not optional** — statute determines whether it applies. Many short-term emigrants do not qualify because they retain a UK home throughout.)
4. **What is the s.10A "5-year trap" and who does it catch?** (TCGA 1992 s.10A: individual is a "temporary non-resident" where their period of non-UK residence is **5 years or less** AND they were UK-resident in **4 or more of the 7 tax years before departure**. Gains on non-UK situs assets realised during non-residence are deemed to arise in the year of return. A landlord who sells foreign shares during a 3-year posting and then returns to the UK is squarely caught.)
5. **Does s.10A catch gains on UK property sold during the overseas stint?** (UK land disposals during non-residence are already taxed under TCGA 1992 s.1A (NRCGT) at the time of disposal — see §17.4. s.10A primarily matters for non-UK situs assets and for pre-NRCGT UK base-cost issues.)
6. **My UK rental — do tenants / agents withhold 20% the moment I leave?** (Yes, the NRL scheme bites the moment you become non-UK-resident, regardless of treaty residence. Apply for NRL1 (individual) / NRL2 (jointly-held) / NRL3 (corporate) approval to receive rent gross — but you still self-assess UK tax annually. Per HP §16.6 + §17.5.)
7. **What about the 60-day NRCGT return — even if I'm only abroad for 18 months?** (Required for every UK land disposal by a non-resident from the date you become non-resident, regardless of treaty residence, regardless of whether tax is due. Verbatim from §17.4: "60-day return required for every UK land disposal by non-residents, including indirect disposals of property-rich entity shares, regardless of whether tax is due".)
8. **Does s.10A also catch income? Dividends, pension lump sums?** (Yes, in part — **ITA 2007 s.812** is the income-tax parallel. Certain types of income received during temporary non-residence (dividends from close-controlled companies, pension lump sums, share-scheme exercises) are taxed in the year of return.)
9. **What about IHT — am I exposed during the overseas stint?** (Almost certainly yes. The new FA 2025 long-term-resident test at IHTA 1984 ss.6A-6C uses 10-of-20 tax years. A short-term emigrant who was UK-resident in 10 of the prior 20 tax years remains a long-term resident throughout the stint and into the tail period after return. Per HP §22.X.)
10. **If the destination country also taxes my UK rent, do I pay twice?** (No — relief is available via the foreign tax credit framework at **TIOPA 2010 s.18** ("Entitlement to credit for foreign tax reduces UK tax by amount of the credit", Part 2 Chapter 2, verified at write time) and the relevant bilateral DTA. Whether UK gives credit or destination state gives credit depends on the treaty's elimination article (Art 23) — most DTAs make the destination state credit UK source taxation on UK property.)
11. **When does a "short trip" become "permanently leaving" for tax purposes?** (More than 5 complete tax years non-UK-resident escapes the s.10A 5-year recapture window. Beyond that, see B6 sibling page for the clean-break framework. Note: the IHT LTR tail period is separate and may persist for years after the 5-year CGT window has closed.)
12. **Practical worked example for the user to recognise themselves in.** (Stage 2 to develop: a UK landlord with two BTLs takes a 3-year secondment to Singapore. Walk through: SRT analysis Year 1 (probably non-resident if they hit Case 1 / 3); NRL approval for the rents; ongoing self-assessment for rental profits; what happens if they sell a property in Year 2 (60-day NRCGT); what happens if they sell foreign-held shares in Year 2 and return in Year 4 (s.10A recapture catches the gain in the return year); IHT exposure throughout.)

### Example 1 — The classic 3-year Singapore secondment scenario (the page's first concrete artefact)

Mr Penrose (UK-resident in all 7 preceding tax years; UK BTL portfolio of two flats in Manchester and Leeds; offered a 3-year Singapore secondment starting 6 April 2026; UK family home retained but tenanted out during the stint; UK spouse and children relocating with him).

**SRT analysis (Year 1, 2026/27):**

- Automatic overseas test (FA 2013 Sch 45 Part 1): works full-time overseas + <91 days in UK during the tax year + works in UK on no more than 30 days. Likely meets automatic overseas test → non-UK-resident for 2026/27.
- Split-year Case 1 (starts full-time overseas work) likely applies → 2026/27 is a split year with UK part ending on the departure date and overseas part running to 5 April 2027.

**NRL scheme (operational from departure date):**

- NRL approval (NRL1) sought from HMRC to receive rent gross. Grant depends on UK self-assessment compliance history.
- If NRL1 granted: no 20% withholding by letting agent; rent flows gross.
- If NRL1 not granted: letting agent withholds 20% basic rate from gross rent monthly; remits quarterly under NRL6; credit claimable on annual self-assessment.
- Self-assessment continues annually under ITTOIA 2005 Part 3. SA100 + SA105 (UK property pages) for each tax year of non-residence; UK property profits taxable at UK rates regardless of Singapore residence.

**Year 2 (2027/28) — sells Leeds flat:**

- Disposal triggers 60-day NRCGT return under TCGA 1992 s.1A + Sch 1A.
- Gain £85,000 (acquisition 2015 £180k; disposal £290k; allowable expenses £25k). NRCGT rate 24% residential (post-30 October 2024) on portion above AEA £3,000 = £82,000 × 24% = £19,680.
- 60-day return filed at gov.uk; payment within 60 days of completion.
- Singapore-UK DTA Art 13 + Art 23 elimination: UK retains source-state taxing rights on UK property gain; Singapore credit allowed if Singapore also taxes (Singapore generally does not levy CGT on individuals).

**Year 2 (2027/28) — sells Singapore-listed equity portfolio (non-UK situs):**

- Gain £180,000 realised while non-UK-resident. Falls OUTSIDE NRCGT (no UK land disposal).
- BUT: s.10A 5-year-or-less window potentially live. Mr Penrose has been non-UK-resident for 1 complete tax year (2026/27); 4-of-7-preceding (2019/20 to 2025/26) UK-residence gateway met (UK-resident in all 7).
- IF Mr Penrose returns to UK before 6 April 2031 (within 5 complete tax years of departure 6 April 2026): the Singapore equity gain is deemed to arise in the year of return per TCGA 1992 s.10A subsection (3). UK CGT at the rate applicable to non-property gains in the return year on £177k (post-AEA) — illustrative ~£35-£42k deemed UK tax depending on bracket.
- Singapore equity gain attracts no Singapore CGT (Singapore does not levy CGT on individuals); no foreign tax credit available to mitigate the UK deemed tax.

**Year 4 (2030/31) — Singapore secondment extended to 5 years; Mr Penrose returns 6 April 2031:**

- Period of non-UK residence: 5 complete tax years (2026/27 to 2030/31).
- Per HP §17.3 verbatim (verified against HMRC CG26540): s.10A "5 years or less" measured by COMPLETE tax years of non-UK residence. 5 complete years = still ≤5 → s.10A still bites.
- Borderline rule: must be MORE THAN 5 complete tax years of non-UK residence to escape s.10A.

**IHT exposure throughout:**

- Mr Penrose was UK-resident in 18+ of the preceding 20 tax years → IHTA 1984 s.267ZC LTR gateway met at departure.
- Per FA 2025 LTR architecture (HP §22.X), Mr Penrose remains a long-term resident throughout the 5-year stint. Worldwide IHT exposure persists during the stint AND into the tail period after return.

**Practical lesson:** the "I'm leaving for 3 years" landlord assumption that UK tax switches off at departure is wrong on four fronts: (i) UK rental income remains UK-taxable; (ii) UK property disposals trigger 60-day NRCGT; (iii) non-UK-situs gains realised during the stint may be recaptured by s.10A on return; (iv) IHT LTR exposure persists. Specialist cross-border advice essential before departure.

### Example 2 — The "sufficient ties" SRT trap for a landlord who keeps a UK home

Ms Halloway (UK-resident in all 7 preceding tax years; UK BTL portfolio of three London flats; takes a 14-month sabbatical to Lisbon starting May 2026 to write a book; UK home retained; UK spouse and adult children remain in UK; Ms Halloway visits home for 3-4 weeks per quarter to see family — typically 90-95 days per UK tax year).

**SRT analysis (2026/27) per FA 2013 Sch 45 Part 1:**

- Automatic overseas test: NOT met. Spends >16 days in UK (90+ days); not working full-time overseas in any meaningful sense (writing a book is not "work" for SRT purposes unless paid as employment / trade).
- Automatic UK test: NOT met. UK home retained but not "only home" for the 30-day-only-home test.
- Sufficient-ties test (for "leaver" — UK-resident in any of the 3 preceding tax years):
  - Family tie (UK-resident spouse): YES.
  - Accommodation tie (UK home available for 91+ days, used for at least one night): YES.
  - Work tie (40+ UK workdays): probably NO (writing in Lisbon).
  - 90-day tie (90+ days in either of the 2 preceding tax years): YES (was UK-resident in all preceding years with full UK presence).
  - Country tie (UK is country of most days): probably YES given 90-95 UK days vs Lisbon days.
- Day count 91-120 days + 3 ties (family + accommodation + 90-day + country = 4) → SRT verdict: **STILL UK-RESIDENT** for 2026/27.

**Tax consequences:**

- Ms Halloway is UK-resident for 2026/27 (and likely 2027/28 with same pattern).
- All UK + worldwide income remains UK-taxable on worldwide-residence basis.
- Portuguese tax paid on any Portuguese-source income claimable as foreign tax credit under TIOPA 2010 s.18 + UK-Portugal DTA Art 23.
- NRL scheme does NOT apply (she remains UK-resident; no withholding by agents).

**Practical lesson:** the "year-or-two abroad" landlord who retains a UK home + UK family + visits home for ~quarter of the year is very likely still UK-resident under the sufficient-ties test. The SRT is unforgiving here. Many short-term emigrants assume physical departure = non-residence; this is wrong on the most common ties pattern.

### Example 3 — Split-year treatment under FA 2013 Sch 45 Part 3 Cases 1-3

Mr Carrington (departing UK on 1 August 2026 to take up a permanent overseas role in Dubai; UK home sold prior to departure; UK family relocating with him; UK BTL portfolio retained for income).

**Split-year analysis (2026/27):**

- Case 1 (starting full-time work overseas): MET — Mr Carrington starts a contractual overseas role 1 August 2026; works overseas full-time; spends <91 days in UK during overseas part of the year; works in UK <30 days during overseas part.
- Case 3 (ceasing to have any UK home): MET — UK home sold prior to departure; no UK home from 1 August 2026.

**Split-year outcome:**

- 2026/27 is split: UK part 6 April 2026 - 31 July 2026 (UK-resident); overseas part 1 August 2026 - 5 April 2027 (non-UK-resident).
- Split-year is NOT optional — applies automatically if Sch 45 Part 3 Case 1-3 conditions met (or 4-8 for arrivers).
- UK income + gains in UK part: taxed on worldwide-residence basis.
- UK income + gains in overseas part: taxed on UK-source basis only (non-UK-resident); NRL bites on UK rental from 1 August; NRCGT applies to any UK land disposal from 1 August.

**Cases 1, 2, 3 walked (departures):**

- **Case 1 starting full-time work overseas:** contract for full-time overseas work + works overseas for at least a year + <91 UK days during overseas part + works in UK on <30 days during overseas part. Most common case for departing professionals.
- **Case 2 partner of Case 1 individual:** where spouse / civil partner meets Case 1 and the individual moves overseas to be with them.
- **Case 3 ceasing to have any UK home:** individual ceases to have any home in the UK + remains overseas for the rest of the tax year + has sufficient connection with the overseas territory.

**Cases 4-8 (for completeness — page may reference Cases 4-8 in a footnote):**

- Case 4: starting to have UK home only (arrivers).
- Case 5: starting full-time UK work (arrivers).
- Case 6: ceasing full-time overseas work (return).
- Case 7: partner of Case 6 individual.
- Case 8: starting to have UK home (arrivers).

**Practical lesson:** split-year treatment turns on whether one of Cases 1-8 conditions is met. Many short-term emigrants do not qualify in the departure year because they retain a UK home (Case 3 fails) and / or do not take up full-time overseas work (Case 1 fails). The result: they remain UK-resident for the full departure tax year, and only become non-resident from 6 April of the following tax year if SRT conditions then permit.

### Example 4 — The s.10A recapture trap for a non-UK situs gain (the highest-value planning surface)

Mrs Edenfield (UK-resident in all 7 preceding tax years; 4-year posting to Switzerland starting 6 April 2026; sells Swiss-listed pharmaceutical equity portfolio in November 2028 (Year 3 of stint) realising £340,000 gain; returns to UK 6 April 2030, i.e., after 4 complete tax years of non-UK residence).

**s.10A analysis (TCGA 1992 s.10A — substituted by FA 2019):**

- Subsection (1) gateway: UK-resident in 4 or more of the 7 tax years immediately preceding the year of departure (2019/20 - 2025/26 — UK-resident in all 7 → gateway MET).
- Subsection (5) duration test: period of non-UK residence is 5 years or less (4 complete tax years 2026/27 - 2029/30 → ≤5 years → MET).
- Subsection (3) operative rule: gain on non-UK situs asset realised during temporary non-residence is "treated as accruing in the year of return" → deemed UK CGT in 2030/31.

**UK tax consequence:**

- Swiss equity gain £340,000 (no UK CGT during non-residence — non-UK situs).
- On return in 2030/31: gain deemed to arise. UK CGT at the rate applicable to non-property gains in 2030/31 (verify current bracket structure at write — for non-property gains: 18% basic / 24% higher from 30 October 2024; rate may shift in future Budgets) on £337k (post-AEA £3k) = illustrative ~£60-£81k deemed UK tax in 2030/31 depending on bracket fill.
- Mrs Edenfield's UK self-assessment return for 2030/31 must declare the deemed Year-of-Return gain + pay the deemed CGT.
- Swiss tax may have applied to the gain at realisation (some Swiss cantons levy CGT on equity gains; most do not for individuals — verify per facts). Foreign tax credit claimable on UK return per TIOPA 2010 s.18 if Swiss tax actually paid.

**Counterfactual A — same facts but Mrs Edenfield returns 6 April 2031 (5 complete tax years of non-residence):**

- Period of non-UK residence: exactly 5 complete tax years → still ≤5 years → s.10A still bites.
- Same recapture: gain deemed to arise in 2031/32 return year.

**Counterfactual B — same facts but Mrs Edenfield returns 6 April 2032 (6 complete tax years of non-residence):**

- Period of non-UK residence: 6 complete tax years → >5 years → s.10A does NOT bite.
- Swiss equity gain stays untaxed by UK (realised by non-resident on non-UK situs asset during qualifying period).

**Counterfactual C — same facts but the gateway test fails:**

- Suppose Mrs Edenfield was UK-resident in only 3 of the 7 preceding tax years (rest non-resident under SRT). Subsection (1) gateway test 4-of-7 NOT met. s.10A does not apply regardless of return date.

**Practical lesson:** the 5-year window is the single most catchable trap for departing short-term emigrants who realise substantial non-UK gains during the stint. Plan returns to fall in the 6th complete tax year (or later) if the gain is large enough to justify timing flexibility. Specialist advice required before realising any large non-UK gain during a sub-5-year stint.

### Example 5 — NRL withholding + ongoing self-assessment during the stint

Dr Westerby (medical consultant; UK-resident for past 20 years; 2-year overseas teaching post starting 6 April 2026; one UK BTL flat let to long-term tenant at £1,800/month = £21,600 gross annual rent; managed by UK letting agent).

**From departure date (6 April 2026):**

- Dr Westerby becomes non-UK-resident under SRT (automatic overseas test met: full-time overseas work + <91 UK days + <30 UK work days).
- NRL scheme bites: UK letting agent withholds 20% basic rate from gross rent monthly.

**Operational mechanics (no NRL1 approval scenario):**

- Monthly rent £1,800 → withholding £360 → tenant pays £1,440 net to landlord (after agent's commission, separately).
- Agent files quarterly NRL6 return to HMRC remitting £360 × 3 = £1,080 withheld per quarter.
- Annual NRL withholding: £21,600 × 20% = £4,320.

**NRL1 approval — alternative operational route:**

- Dr Westerby applies for NRL1 at departure (form NRL1 online + UK self-assessment compliance history confirmation). Approval typically granted within 6-8 weeks given a clean record.
- Once approved, letting agent pays rent gross from approval date.
- Dr Westerby still self-assesses annually on UK rental profits under ITTOIA 2005 Part 3.

**Annual self-assessment (SA100 + SA105):**

- Gross rental income: £21,600.
- Allowable expenses: £4,800 (letting fees, repairs, insurance; mortgage interest restricted to 20% basic-rate tax reducer under ITTOIA 2005 s.272A).
- Taxable property profit: ~£16,800.
- UK tax at 20% basic rate (assuming no other UK income that takes Dr Westerby into higher rate band; non-resident tax-free personal allowance available per ITA 2007 s.56 for UK / EU / qualifying-jurisdiction nationals): ~£3,360 ignoring personal allowance, less if personal allowance allowable.
- NRL withholding credit: £4,320 → credit fully offsets the £3,360 due → £960+ NRL OVERPAYMENT → repayment claim on return.

**Practical lesson:** NRL withholding at 20% basic rate may over-withhold for landlords with modest UK property profits, generating annual repayment claims. NRL1 approval (gross-rent receipt) is the operational improvement; landlord still pays the underlying UK tax via self-assessment but cash flow improves. Per HP §16.6 verbatim: treaty residence does NOT displace NRL — NRL is statutory.

### Example 6 — IHT LTR exposure during a short-term overseas stint

Mr Tallaght (UK-resident in 18 of the preceding 20 tax years; takes a 3-year overseas posting starting 6 April 2026; remains overseas for full 3 years; returns 6 April 2029 to UK residence).

**LTR analysis (IHTA 1984 ss.6A-6C + s.267ZC — FA 2025 architecture):**

- 10-of-20 LTR gateway: 18 of preceding 20 UK-resident → gateway MET. Mr Tallaght is a long-term resident at departure.

**LTR status during the 3-year stint:**

- LTR status persists throughout because the 10-of-20 test continues to be met (the count includes the relevant prior UK-residence years as years roll forward; a 3-year break does not unwind 18 years of preceding UK residence).
- Worldwide IHT exposure persists during the stint: any death during the stint exposes worldwide estate to UK IHT at 40% above NRB £325,000 (NRB frozen to 5 April 2031 per HP §22.X Wave 6 enacted-Act lock; verify at write).

**LTR tail period after return:**

- Per FA 2025 LTR architecture, the tail period after final cessation of UK residence depends on length of pre-departure UK residence. For someone with 18+ of 20 years, tail period runs for c. 10 years after final departure-from-UK-residence date.
- For Mr Tallaght returning after a 3-year stint, the tail period is essentially irrelevant — he is back in the UK + UK-resident again from 6 April 2029, so worldwide IHT exposure continues without interruption.

**Practical lesson:** short-term emigrants with long UK residence histories cannot outrun the IHT LTR test during a 3-year (or shorter) stint. Worldwide IHT exposure persists throughout. IHT planning (lifetime gifts within NRB, BPR / APR holdings, trust structures) should not be paused during the stint — the LTR test is unforgiving. Pre-2025 "non-dom" framing is stale per HP §17.6 Wave 5 domicile reform lock.

### Example 7 — Misframings the page must correct (verbatim do-not-write list)

- **Misframe 1:** "If I'm non-UK-resident, I owe no UK tax." FALSE. ITTOIA 2005 Part 3 charges UK source property income on non-UK-resident landlords; TCGA 1992 s.1A imposes NRCGT on UK land disposals; FA 1995 Sch 23 imposes NRL withholding; IHTA 1984 ss.6A-6C imposes LTR exposure. Treaty residence does not displace these (per HP §16.6).
- **Misframe 2:** "Physically leaving the UK makes me non-UK-resident." FALSE — SRT determines residence per FA 2013 Sch 45. Sufficient-ties test catches most short-term emigrants who retain a UK home + UK family + visit home for ~90 days per year.
- **Misframe 3:** "Split-year treatment is something I can choose to apply." FALSE — split-year is statutory under FA 2013 Sch 45 Part 3. Cases 1-8 conditions either are or are not met. Not optional.
- **Misframe 4:** "The 5-year temporary non-residence rule starts from the date I leave." MISLEADING — measured by COMPLETE tax years of non-UK residence per HP §17.3 verbatim. Departure mid-year may give a split-year, but the s.10A 5-year clock runs in tax years of full non-residence.
- **Misframe 5:** "s.10A only catches gains made just before I leave." FALSE — s.10A catches non-UK situs gains realised DURING the period of non-residence + before return. The gain is deemed to arise in the year of return.
- **Misframe 6:** "If I sell a UK property while abroad, I just file a return at year-end." FALSE — 60-day NRCGT return + payment required per TCGA 1992 s.1A. Treaty residence does not delay the 60-day window.
- **Misframe 7:** "The NRL scheme doesn't apply if my destination country's DTA assigns taxing rights to that country." FALSE — NRL is STATUTORY per FA 1995 Sch 23 + HP §16.6 ("treaty residence does not displace NRL"). Tenants and letting agents continue to withhold 20% unless NRL1 approval is held.
- **Misframe 8:** "I can avoid s.10A by remaining non-UK-resident for exactly 5 years." MISLEADING — 5 complete tax years still triggers s.10A. Must be MORE THAN 5 complete tax years to escape. Plan returns to fall in the 6th complete tax year (or later).
- **Misframe 9:** "Once I'm non-UK-resident, my UK rental income is taxed in my destination country only." FALSE — UK rental income is UK-source under ITTOIA 2005 Part 3 + UK taxes it regardless of destination-country residence. DTA Article 6 typically assigns source-state primary taxing rights; destination country may also tax, with foreign tax credit relief flowing the appropriate direction per Article 23.
- **Misframe 10:** "IHT only matters when I die — I don't need to think about it during a 3-year stint." MISLEADING — IHTA 1984 ss.6A-6C LTR test continues to expose worldwide estate during the stint. Lifetime gifts during the stint may trigger LTR-period IHT on the donor's worldwide estate if death occurs within 7 years (PET clock unaffected by residence).
- **Misframe 11:** "Domicile is the test — if I change domicile, UK tax stops." LARGELY OBSOLETE post-6 April 2025. FA 2025 replaced the domicile-based remittance basis with the Foreign Income and Gains (FIG) regime + Long-Term Resident (LTR) test per HP §17.6. Domicile is no longer the operative connecting factor for income tax / CGT / IHT exposure for most landlords. Pre-2025 "non-dom" framings are stale.
- **Misframe 12:** "The 16-day automatic overseas threshold catches all short-term emigrants." MISLEADING — the 16-day threshold applies to "leavers" (UK-resident in any of the 3 preceding tax years) AND requires <16 days in UK in the relevant tax year. Most short-term emigrants will spend >16 UK days during the year (visits home for family), so the 16-day automatic test does not apply; they fall into the sufficient-ties test instead.
- **Misframe 13:** "Foreign tax credit is automatic — I don't need to do anything." FALSE — foreign tax credit relief under TIOPA 2010 s.18 ("Entitlement to credit for foreign tax reduces UK tax by amount of the credit", Part 2 Chapter 2, verified at write time) + the applicable DTA must be CLAIMED on the relevant tax return with evidence of foreign tax paid. Not automatic.

## FAQ expansion (RUN session polishes prose; 12-14 FAQs target)

1. **Q: I'm leaving the UK for 2 years. Do I still pay UK tax?**
   A: Almost certainly yes, on more than you expect. Four overlapping reasons: (i) the SRT may keep you UK-resident if you retain UK family + UK home + visit home for ~90 days; (ii) even if non-UK-resident, UK rental income remains UK-taxable under ITTOIA 2005 Part 3 + NRL withholding bites; (iii) UK property disposals during the stint trigger 60-day NRCGT regardless of residence under TCGA 1992 s.1A; (iv) non-UK gains realised during the stint may be recaptured by TCGA 1992 s.10A on return within 5 complete tax years. Plus IHT LTR exposure throughout per IHTA 1984 ss.6A-6C.

2. **Q: How does the SRT decide whether I'm UK-resident during a short trip abroad?**
   A: FA 2013 Sch 45 Part 1 cascade. Automatic overseas test first (e.g., <16 days in UK for leavers; or full-time overseas work + <91 UK days + <30 UK work days). If not met, automatic UK test (rare for leavers). If not met, sufficient-ties test — counts UK family, UK accommodation, UK work, 90+ days, country tie against day-count brackets (16-45, 46-90, 91-120, 121-182 for leavers). Most short-term emigrants with UK family + UK home fall into sufficient-ties and remain UK-resident.

3. **Q: Is split-year treatment optional, and when does it apply?**
   A: Not optional. Statutory under FA 2013 Sch 45 Part 3 Cases 1-8 (for departures: Cases 1-3). Case 1 = starting full-time overseas work + <91 UK days + <30 UK work days in overseas part. Case 2 = partner of Case-1 individual. Case 3 = ceasing to have any UK home + sufficient overseas connection. If conditions met, split-year applies automatically. Many short-term emigrants do NOT qualify because they retain a UK home throughout.

4. **Q: What is the s.10A "5-year trap" and who does it catch?**
   A: TCGA 1992 s.10A (substituted by FA 2019). An individual is a "temporary non-resident" where (a) period of non-UK residence is 5 years or less, AND (b) UK-resident in 4 or more of the 7 tax years before departure. Gains on non-UK situs assets realised during non-residence are deemed to arise in the year of return per subsection (3). Catches the short-term emigrant who realises a substantial non-UK gain during a sub-5-year stint and then returns to UK residence.

5. **Q: Does s.10A also catch UK property gains?**
   A: UK land disposals during non-residence are already taxed under TCGA 1992 s.1A (NRCGT) AT THE TIME OF DISPOSAL — not under s.10A. The 60-day NRCGT return + payment regime applies regardless. s.10A primarily catches non-UK situs gains (foreign shares, foreign property, crypto disposals).

6. **Q: My UK rental — do tenants and letting agents withhold 20% the moment I leave?**
   A: YES. NRL scheme under FA 1995 Sch 23 + SI 1995/2902 bites from the date you become non-UK-resident. Tenants and letting agents withhold 20% basic rate from gross rent monthly + remit quarterly to HMRC under NRL6 returns. To receive rent gross: apply for NRL1 (individuals) / NRL2 (jointly-held) / NRL3 (corporate). Approval depends on UK tax compliance history. Per HP §16.6: NRL is statutory, NOT treaty — treaty residence does not displace NRL.

7. **Q: What about the 60-day NRCGT return if I sell a UK BTL while abroad?**
   A: Required for every UK land disposal by a non-resident, even if no tax is due. TCGA 1992 s.1A + Sch 1A architecture. 60 days from completion to file the return + pay the tax. Per HP §17.4 verbatim: "60-day return required for every UK land disposal by non-residents, including indirect disposals of property-rich entity shares, regardless of whether tax is due". Residential rate 18% / 24% (from 30 October 2024) on gain above AEA £3,000.

8. **Q: Does s.10A catch dividend income, pension lump sums, share-scheme exercises?**
   A: In part — ITA 2007 s.812 is the income-tax parallel. Certain types of income received during temporary non-residence — close-company dividends, certain pension lump sums, employment-related-securities events — are taxed in the year of return on the same 5-year-or-less + 4-of-7-preceding basis. Routine dividends + interest + employment income from non-UK sources during non-residence are NOT generally caught by s.812.

9. **Q: What about IHT — am I exposed during the overseas stint?**
   A: Almost certainly yes. Per IHTA 1984 ss.6A-6C + ss.267ZC-267ZF (FA 2025 LTR architecture), the 10-of-20 test catches anyone with substantial UK residence history. A 3-year stint does not displace LTR status. Worldwide estate exposure continues throughout the stint + into the tail period after return. Per HP §22.X (Wave 5 IHT LTR lock).

10. **Q: If the destination country also taxes my UK rental income, do I pay twice?**
    A: No — foreign tax credit relief under TIOPA 2010 s.18 ("Entitlement to credit for foreign tax reduces UK tax by amount of the credit", Part 2 Chapter 2, verified verbatim at write time) + the applicable bilateral DTA Article 23. Whether UK gives the credit or destination state gives it depends on the treaty's Art 23 elimination method. Most modern UK DTAs use the credit method on the destination-state side for UK-source property income — destination state taxes the income but credits the UK tax paid against destination tax.

11. **Q: When does a "short trip" become "permanently leaving" for tax purposes?**
    A: More than 5 complete tax years of non-UK residence escapes the s.10A 5-year recapture window. Beyond that, see sibling page on permanently leaving the UK. Note: the IHT LTR tail period is SEPARATE from s.10A and persists for years after the 5-year CGT window has closed; depends on years of UK residence prior to departure.

12. **Q: Is the old "non-dom" framework still relevant?**
    A: LARGELY OBSOLETE post-6 April 2025. FA 2025 replaced the domicile-based remittance basis with the Foreign Income and Gains (FIG) regime (4-year exemption for arrivers after 10 years of non-UK residence) + Temporary Repatriation Facility (TRF) for legacy unremitted income + Long-Term Resident (LTR) test for IHT (per HP §17.6 Wave 5 domicile reform lock). Domicile is no longer the operative connecting factor for income tax / CGT / IHT exposure for most landlords. Pre-2025 "non-dom" framings are stale; consult specialist for current FIG / TRF / LTR positioning.

13. **Q: Can I plan to escape s.10A by realising the gain before I leave?**
    A: If the gain is on a non-UK situs asset and the disposal occurs before departure: yes — disposal is by a UK-resident at the time of realisation, taxed under normal CGT rules. If realisation is AFTER departure: s.10A bites if the return is within 5 complete tax years and the 4-of-7-preceding gateway is met. Pre-departure realisation is the cleanest planning route if the asset is genuinely close to disposal.

14. **Q: Should I get specialist advice before leaving?**
    A: Yes, strongly recommended. The combination of SRT analysis (residence determination), split-year application (Cases 1-3), NRL approval (gross-rent receipt mechanics), NRCGT planning (if any UK property sale anticipated), s.10A planning (timing of any large non-UK gain), foreign tax credit positioning (under the destination DTA), and IHT LTR review is structurally complex and high-stakes. Mistakes are expensive and often only surface years later under HMRC enquiry.
