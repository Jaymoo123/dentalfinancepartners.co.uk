# Labour's Plans to Increase Stamp Duty for Overseas Buyers: From the 2024 Manifesto Commitment to the Current Statutory Position and the Practical Implications for Non-Resident Purchases

## Statutory anchor
- Primary (current non-resident SDLT surcharge): FA 2003 Schedule 9A — inserted by FA 2021 s.86 + Sch 16, in force from 1 April 2021. Sets a 2% surcharge on top of the otherwise-applicable rates (residential Table A or higher-rate Sch 4ZA) where the buyer is not UK-resident under the para 4 residence test (individual: present in the UK for fewer than 183 days in any 365-day period that includes the effective date). Verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2003/14/schedule/9A on 2026-05-26.
- Primary (potential rate change — verify at Stage 2): the Labour 2024 General Election manifesto stated a commitment to increase the non-resident SDLT surcharge from 2% to 3% as a flagship housing-affordability policy. Whether the increase has been legislated, the commencement date, and the current rate at the page-write date must be verified at Stage 2 against the latest Finance Act (FA 2025 c. 8 was enacted; subsequent Finance Bill / Act for 2026-27 may or may not contain the increase) AND against HMRC's published guidance at gov.uk. **Stage 1b drift watchpoint:** the page tracks a live policy and must not assert a rate without verification. If the increase has not been legislated as of the write date, the page is written as policy-analysis with the current statutory position noted; if the increase has been legislated, the page is written as a current-position explainer with the historical-policy context as background.
- Primary (rate consequence at current 2% surcharge): for an example £1m purchase by an individual non-resident buyer with no other UK or overseas property: standard residential Table A SDLT on £1m = £43,750 (verify at write); 2% non-resident surcharge = +£20,000; total £63,750. At a hypothetical 3% rate, the surcharge would be +£30,000; total £73,750. Differential per million of consideration is £10,000 between the 2% and 3% rates.
- Supporting (interaction with the 5% additional dwellings surcharge under Sch 4ZA — increased from 3% from 31 October 2024 by FA 2025 c. 8 s.51): non-resident buyers who already own a dwelling anywhere in the world face BOTH the Sch 4ZA 5% surcharge AND the Sch 9A 2% (or 3% if increased) surcharge — additive stacking. On a £1m purchase with the additional dwellings position, this is 5% + 2% = 7% surcharge total, or 5% + 3% = 8% if the increase is legislated.
- Supporting (FTB-relief disapplication under Sch 6ZA): non-resident first-time UK buyers can claim FTB relief IF they meet the Sch 6ZA tests (relief is not residence-conditional), but the Sch 9A 2% non-resident surcharge still applies even on FTB-relieved transactions — confirm at Stage 2; the interaction is subtle and Stage 2 verifies whether the surcharge is computed on the FTB-relieved liability or as a separate addition.

3. What is the current non-resident SDLT surcharge rate? (At time of writing — Stage 2 confirms — 2% under FA 2003 Sch 9A as inserted by FA 2021 s.86. The page must state the rate with the verification date.)
4. Who is a "non-resident" for the surcharge? (FA 2003 Sch 9A para 4 — individual: present in the UK for fewer than 183 days in any 365-day period that includes the effective date of the transaction (NOT a calendar year; NOT the SRT residence test under FA 2013 Sch 45 — Sch 9A has its own SDLT-specific residence definition). Para 5: companies that are non-UK-resident or are UK-resident but close-controlled by non-UK-resident participators.)
5. How is the surcharge calculated? (FA 2003 s.55C — added by FA 2021 Sch 16. The surcharge is 2% (or 3% if legislated) added to each band of the rates otherwise applicable. So for a non-resident buyer also liable for the 5% additional dwellings surcharge, total rate at the top band would be 12% standard + 5% additional + 2% non-resident = 19% on consideration above £1.5m. At a hypothetical 3% non-resident rate, this would be 20% on the top band.)
6. What is the practical SDLT impact on a typical non-resident purchase? (Worked examples for £500k, £1m, and £2m purchases — sole non-resident buyer with no other property; sole non-resident buyer with additional dwellings; non-resident first-time UK buyer claiming FTB relief. Each worked example shows the SDLT at 2% surcharge and at a hypothetical 3% surcharge for comparison. Verify rate calculations at write per §16.35.)
7. Does the surcharge apply to non-resident BTL purchases? (Yes — the surcharge applies to all residential transactions by non-resident buyers, including BTL acquisitions. Combined with the 5% additional dwellings surcharge (which applies to BTL acquisitions), non-resident BTL buyers face the highest SDLT load on residential property in the UK regime.)
8. What is the policy rationale for the surcharge and the proposed increase? (Housing affordability for UK residents; reducing the competitive disadvantage of UK-resident buyers competing with cash-rich overseas investors in prime markets; raising tax revenue. Critics argue: the surcharge has limited supply-side effect because non-resident buyers are a small share of the wider market; high-end prime markets are most affected and least sensitive to surcharge increases; possible deterrent to overseas-investor flows into UK housing development. Page presents both sides briefly and factually.)
9. What is the commencement-date discipline for transactions in flight when the surcharge increases? (Typical pattern in past SDLT rate changes: contracts exchanged before the commencement date but completing after may be at the old rate under transitional / grandfathering rules; contracts exchanged after the commencement date are at the new rate. Stage 2 confirms whether the legislated increase (if any) includes specific transitional provisions and reports them.)
10. What should a non-resident buyer planning a UK purchase do now? (Three steps: (i) confirm UK residence status under the Sch 9A 183-day test — many buyers think they are UK-resident under SRT but are non-resident for Sch 9A purposes; (ii) model SDLT at both 2% and 3% surcharge rates; (iii) engage UK-side conveyancing and tax advice early to manage the surcharge calculation and confirm any spouse-aggregation interactions with Sch 4ZA. Cross-link to A17 worldwide-property mechanics and to existing Sch 9A pages for full mechanics.)
