## Statutory anchor

- Primary (houses — extension OR freehold acquisition under one Act): Leasehold Reform Act 1967 c. 88 (LRA 1967). The 1967 Act gives the qualifying long-leaseholder of a house both an enfranchisement right (acquire the freehold) AND an extension right (50-year extension at modern ground rent). Key sections: s.1 (tenants entitled to enfranchisement or extension); s.2 (definition of "house"); s.3 (definition of "long tenancy"); s.5 (general claim provisions); s.9 (purchase price for enfranchisement, valuation formula, costs, tenant's right to withdraw); s.14 (obligation to grant extended lease); s.15 (terms of tenancy on extension). Verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1967/88/contents on 2026-05-26. The 1967 Act extension regime is the LESS-USED option for houses (because the extension is at a modern ground rent rather than peppercorn) and is largely displaced in practice by enfranchisement under the same Act; the page notes this asymmetry and explains why most house-leaseholders pick enfranchisement under LRA 1967 over the 1967 extension route.
- Primary (flats — collective enfranchisement OR individual lease extension under one Act): Leasehold Reform, Housing and Urban Development Act 1993 c. 28 (LRHUDA 1993) Part 1. The 1993 Act gives qualifying flat-leaseholders two parallel routes: **collective enfranchisement** under Chapter I (groups of qualifying tenants in a block buy the freehold of the building) and **individual lease extension** under Chapter II (a single qualifying tenant extends their lease at peppercorn ground rent against payment of a statutory premium). Key Chapter I sections: s.1 (the right to collective enfranchisement); s.13 (initial notice by qualifying tenants); s.32 (price determination + Schedule 6 valuation formula). Key Chapter II sections: s.39 (right of qualifying tenant of flat to acquire new lease); s.42 (initial notice); s.56 (obligation to grant new lease — pre-2024 term was existing-lease + 90 years at peppercorn, now extended by LFRA 2024 to 990 years). Verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1993/28/contents on 2026-05-26.
- Primary (reform regime — alters BOTH extension and enfranchisement mechanics from 2024 onwards): Leasehold and Freehold Reform Act 2024 c. 22 (LFRA 2024) Part 2 "Leasehold enfranchisement and extension". The 2024 Act makes four headline changes that recalibrate the extension-vs-freehold decision: (a) abolishes the 2-year qualifying-ownership prerequisite for both lease extension and collective enfranchisement; (b) extends the statutory new-lease term under LRHUDA 1993 from existing-lease + 90 years to a fresh **990-year** term; (c) abolishes "marriage value" (the valuation uplift triggered at the 80-year unexpired-term boundary), materially reducing premiums on short-lease enfranchisements; (d) introduces a regulated cap on the deferment / capitalisation rates used in the statutory valuation. Verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2024/22/contents on 2026-05-26. **Critical write-time verification:** the LFRA 2024 commencement programme is phased through 2026-2027; some Part 2 provisions are commenced, some require secondary legislation and are not yet in force. The page write must verify the in-force position on the write date against the gov.uk commencement-orders register and any consolidated text for LRHUDA 1993 + LRA 1967 reflecting current LFRA amendments. Pre-LFRA-2024 valuation rules continue to govern claims notified before the relevant commencement date. See HP §31.3 for the current-state framing and the do-not-write list at §31.9.
- Primary (SDLT — statutory extension route, surrender-and-regrant): FA 2003 c. 14 Schedule 17A para 9 — variation of a lease term or rent is treated as the surrender of the existing lease and the grant of a new lease for SDLT purposes. The chargeable consideration for the new lease is the premium paid plus the net present value of any rent (peppercorn = nil NPV in a statutory extension). Verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2003/14/schedule/17A on 2026-05-26. The £40,000 chargeable-consideration threshold for the 5% additional-dwellings surcharge (Sch 4ZA — HRAD, post-Autumn 2024 Budget rate locked at §1.I) catches a meaningful share of London / South-East extension premiums.
- Primary (SDLT — collective enfranchisement special calculation): FA 2003 s.74 "Exercise of collective rights by tenants of flats". On a collective enfranchisement of a block of flats under LRHUDA 1993, the standard SDLT calculation is modified: the total consideration is divided by the number of qualifying flats to derive a per-flat fraction; the standard SDLT rates are applied to that per-flat fraction; the resulting tax is then multiplied back up by the number of flats. This typically delivers a materially lower SDLT charge than applying full rates to the aggregate price — the per-flat fraction tends to fall below the £250,000 nil-rate band for residential rates. Where the per-flat fraction exceeds £500,000 and the conditions in FA 2003 Schedule 4A (high-value-dwelling 17% flat rate via corporate envelope) are met, the 17% rate applies. Verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2003/14/section/74 on 2026-05-26.
- Supporting: FA 2003 Sch 4ZA paras 1-9 (5% HRAD surcharge — applies to chargeable consideration ≥ £40,000 where the buyer already owns another residential property — see §1.I HP-lock); FA 2003 s.43 (chargeable land transaction); LRA 1967 s.9 (purchase price for house enfranchisement — separate statutory valuation from the LRHUDA 1993 flat regime); CGT regime under TCGA 1992 (the leaseholder pays no CGT on a statutory extension because the consideration moves outward, not inward; the freeholder may have a chargeable disposal — see Q7 and RUN-session verify for the freeholder's-side CGT and the lessor's PPR-availability question on enfranchised houses).

## Framing differentiator (anti-templating, anti-cannibalisation)

The site has one existing lease-extension page (`sdlt-leasehold-extension-vs-fresh-purchase`, in `landlord-tax-essentials`) and one existing case-analysis page on a Scottish lease-extension transitional question (the M1-A-B2 Archer UK Limited seed in this MegaWave). Neither covers the underlying **statutory choice between extending the lease and acquiring the freehold**. The existing page is single-route (statutory extension under LRHUDA 1993 Chapter II, English-law-centric, narrowly focused on SDLT on the extension premium); the Archer seed is single-case (LBTT transitional). This page is the **decision-architecture page** sitting upstream of both: it covers BOTH routes (extension AND enfranchisement) under BOTH statutes (LRA 1967 for houses, LRHUDA 1993 for flats), positions them post-LFRA-2024 reform, and walks the reader through the choice mechanics.

This page covers seven things competitor coverage routinely under-serves:

1. The **statutory parallelism between LRA 1967 (houses) and LRHUDA 1993 (flats)** — the rights are different in name and mechanic but the strategic question (extend vs acquire) is symmetric across both regimes; competitor pages routinely treat the two regimes in isolation without the strategic comparison.
2. The **LFRA 2024 reform package as a recalibration of the decision** — abolition of marriage value collapses the historic "extend at 81 years before the cliff" advice; abolition of the 2-year qualifying period removes the buy-and-wait friction; 990-year extension term materially closes the value-gap between extension and freehold for flats; competitor pages updated mid-2024 routinely under-cover or over-state these changes.
3. The **FA 2003 s.74 SDLT special calculation for collective enfranchisement** — the per-flat-fraction-and-multiply mechanic is non-obvious and is missed by general-conveyancing SDLT calculators.
4. The **valuation-formula structure** — both the LRA 1967 s.9 formula (for houses) and the LRHUDA 1993 Sch 6 formula (for flats) operate on three components (term, reversion, hope value where applicable) discounted at the statutory deferment rate; explaining the structural similarity helps readers anticipate the surveyor's working.
5. The **practical control / ground-rent / lender / disposal consequences** — owning the freehold ends the ground-rent liability outright (and removes the lender-friction associated with very short residual leases); the 990-year extension achieves much the same in lender terms but leaves the freehold reversion in third-party hands and preserves the service-charge / management framework; the choice is not purely a numbers calculation.
6. **Collective vs individual enfranchisement structure for flats** — collective enfranchisement requires at least 50% of qualifying tenants to participate (a coordination problem that often defeats the route in practice); individual lease extension is unilateral and does not require neighbour participation; the page explains why most flat-leaseholders default to individual extension despite the headline appeal of collective enfranchisement.
7. The **costs side of each route** — leaseholder pays the freeholder's reasonable legal and valuation costs under both regimes (LRA 1967 s.9(4) + LRHUDA 1993 ss.33 + 60), capped by the post-LFRA-2024 cost-cap framework; the all-in cost of enfranchisement is typically 1.3-1.8x the headline premium once costs are loaded.

## Key questions this page must answer

1. What are the two statutory routes available to a leaseholder, and which Act governs which route? (For houses: LRA 1967 — both extension and enfranchisement available under the same Act; in practice enfranchisement under LRA 1967 dominates because the LRA 1967 extension is at a modern ground rent rather than peppercorn. For flats: LRHUDA 1993 — collective enfranchisement under Chapter I (requires 50%+ neighbour participation; acquires the freehold of the block); individual lease extension under Chapter II (unilateral; produces a peppercorn-ground-rent lease at a statutory premium). Post-LFRA-2024, the Chapter II extension is for 990 years from existing expiry.)
2. Who is a "qualifying tenant" and what are the eligibility requirements? (Pre-LFRA-2024: a long lease (originally granted for more than 21 years), 2 years of ownership of the lease. Post-LFRA-2024: the 2-year ownership requirement is abolished, so a new buyer can extend or join an enfranchisement immediately. Other technical qualifications: the demised premises must be a flat for LRHUDA 1993 or a house meeting the LRA 1967 definition; the lease must not have already been statutorily extended; the building must not be exempt (charity-owned, National Trust, etc.).)
3. How is the premium calculated under each regime? (LRA 1967 s.9 for houses + LRHUDA 1993 Schedules 6 (collective) and 13 (individual) for flats. Three valuation components: the term (value of the freeholder's right to receive ground rent for the remaining term), the reversion (deferred value of the freeholder's right to repossess on lease expiry), and historically the marriage value (the uplift in combined value at the 80-year cliff — ABOLISHED post-LFRA-2024 commencement). Components discounted at the statutory deferment rate (5% under post-Sportelli case-law; LFRA 2024 enables Secretary-of-State regulation of the rate). Hope value (the uplift achievable by combining the freehold with adjoining leases) ABOLISHED for collective enfranchisement post-LFRA-2024.)
4. What did the Leasehold and Freehold Reform Act 2024 change for the decision? (Four headline changes: (a) 2-year qualifying ownership period abolished; (b) statutory extension term increases from existing-lease + 90 years to a fresh 990 years for flats under LRHUDA 1993; (c) marriage value abolished (large premium reduction for sub-80-year leases); (d) deferment / capitalisation rates may be regulated by Secretary of State. Commencement: phased through 2026-2027 with some Part 2 provisions still pending secondary legislation as of the page write date. RUN session verifies in-force position.)
5. What is the SDLT treatment of each route? (Statutory lease extension: FA 2003 Sch 17A para 9 — surrender-and-regrant — SDLT on the premium paid plus NPV of any rent (peppercorn = nil NPV). 5% HRAD surcharge under Sch 4ZA catches premiums ≥ £40,000 where the leaseholder already owns another residential property. Individual flat enfranchisement does not technically exist under LRHUDA 1993 — flat enfranchisement is collective. House enfranchisement under LRA 1967: standard SDLT on the freehold consideration. Collective enfranchisement of a block of flats under LRHUDA 1993: FA 2003 s.74 special calculation — total consideration divided by number of qualifying flats, standard rates applied to per-flat fraction, then multiplied back up — typically a much lower SDLT charge than applying full rates to the aggregate consideration.)
6. What are the practical control, ground-rent, lender, and disposal differences? (Freehold acquisition extinguishes the ground rent permanently and removes the freeholder from the management chain; service charges become a leaseholder-collective matter (often via a Right-to-Manage company or freehold-owning nominee entity); the leaseholder gains hope-value capture on any future scheme variations. 990-year extension under LRHUDA 1993 reduces ground rent to peppercorn for the new term and resolves the lender-friction associated with very short residual leases, but leaves the freehold reversion with the original landlord and preserves the existing service-charge / management framework. Lenders treat both routes as resolving short-lease concerns; some lenders prefer share-of-freehold (collective enfranchisement product) for premium-market flats.)
7. What about CGT, IHT, and ATED implications? (CGT: the leaseholder pays no CGT on extension or enfranchisement because the consideration is outgoing, not incoming; the freeholder has a chargeable disposal — if a corporate freeholder, corporation tax on the chargeable gain; if an individual freeholder, CGT under TCGA 1992 (residential rates: 18% / 24% from 2024 — verify against §1 + §5). IHT: lease extension or enfranchisement converts cash to interest in land — no IHT consequence on the leaseholder; if the lease was a "wasting asset" being depleted, the post-extension lease may have a higher reversionary value held outside the estate (transfer-of-value planning point for IHT-mitigation strategies — cross-link to §15 / §22 / §22.A IHT clusters). ATED (if a corporate-held leasehold residential property ≥ £500k): the post-extension or post-enfranchisement valuation may push the property into a higher ATED band — verify next valuation date under §18.)
8. What is the cost-and-process timeline for each route? (Both routes start with a formal initial notice (LRA 1967 s.5 or LRHUDA 1993 s.13 / s.42), a landlord counter-notice, valuation negotiation, and a tribunal-determined price if the parties do not agree. Typical timeline: 6-12 months for individual lease extension under LRHUDA 1993 Chapter II; 9-18 months for collective enfranchisement under Chapter I; 6-12 months for house enfranchisement under LRA 1967. All-in costs: leaseholder pays freeholder's reasonable legal and valuation costs in addition to the premium (capped post-LFRA-2024 by the costs-cap framework); leaseholder also pays own legal and valuation costs. Typical all-in cost loading: 1.3-1.8x of the headline premium.)
9. How does the decision differ between a flat-leaseholder and a house-leaseholder? (For flats, the choice is meaningful: individual extension under LRHUDA 1993 Chapter II is unilateral and quick; collective enfranchisement under Chapter I requires neighbour coordination (50%+ qualifying-tenant participation, often defeated in mixed-tenure blocks) and produces a different ownership architecture (share-of-freehold via collective vehicle). For houses, the choice is asymmetric: the LRA 1967 extension at modern ground rent is rarely chosen against the LRA 1967 enfranchisement which extinguishes the leasehold structure altogether at typically modest premium; the page explains why most house-leaseholders default to enfranchisement under LRA 1967.)
10. When does extension beat freehold acquisition, and vice versa? (Extension typically beats freehold acquisition when: (a) the leaseholder cannot coordinate 50%+ of neighbours for collective enfranchisement; (b) the leaseholder's planning horizon is the next 20-50 years rather than indefinite tenure; (c) the management structure of the block is satisfactory and the leaseholder does not want governance responsibility; (d) the premium for enfranchisement exceeds the leaseholder's risk budget. Freehold acquisition typically beats extension when: (a) collective enfranchisement is achievable and the per-flat fraction under FA 2003 s.74 keeps SDLT modest; (b) the leaseholder wants to extinguish ground rent and management friction permanently; (c) hope-value capture (development upside on the freehold) is achievable; (d) the residual lease was already very short and the post-extension lease still leaves residual reversion at a future date. RUN session builds a worked decision-grid with three fact patterns: a London flat, a regional house, and a portfolio of leasehold flats.)
