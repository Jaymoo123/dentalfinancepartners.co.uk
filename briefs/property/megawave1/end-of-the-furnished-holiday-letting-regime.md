## FAQ draft (Stage 2 — RUN polishes prose; no further research needed)

1. **Q: What was the Furnished Holiday Lettings regime?** A: A separate property-business category for UK and EEA properties let on a short-term commercial basis to the public, meeting qualifying-day tests at ITTOIA 2005 ss.325-328A. Five FHL-specific tax overlays: capital allowances under CAA 2001 s.15(1)(c)/(da) as a separate qualifying activity; BADR availability on disposal under TCGA 1992 s.241 trading-treatment; rollover relief under TCGA 1992 s.152 on business-asset reinvestment; sideways loss relief against general income under ITA 2007 s.127; FHL profit counting as relevant UK earnings for pension contributions under FA 2004 s.189(2)(ba)+(bb). The regime overlaid trading treatment onto activity that absent the overlay would generally fail the investment-versus-trading test (Pawson v HMRC [2013]).

2. **Q: When did the regime exist?** A: Originally introduced in Finance Act 1984 sections 45-49. Codified in Income and Corporation Taxes Act 1988 sections 503-504 (with substantial amendments by Finance Act 1998). Rewritten into Income Tax (Trading and Other Income) Act 2005 Part 3 Chapter 6 (ss.322-328A) with effect from 6 April 2005 for income tax purposes. Restricted from UK-only to UK+EEA from 6 April 2009 following the European Court of Justice's pressure on the UK-only restriction in similar EU-law-driven expansions. Abolished by Finance Act 2025 Schedule 5 with effect from 1 April 2025 (corporation tax) and 6 April 2025 (income tax and capital gains tax). Total regime lifespan: approximately 41 years from 1984 origin to 2025 abolition.

3. **Q: When was the abolition announced?** A: Spring Budget 2024 on 6 March 2024 — the Conservative government's final pre-election fiscal event. Confirmed in the 2024-25 Budget red book; a technical consultation document was published the same week. Draft legislation was prepared in Finance (No.2) Act 2024 but the FHL provisions were ultimately NOT included in that Act — see next FAQ.

4. **Q: Why was the F(No.2)A 2024 draft superseded by FA 2025?** A: The UK general election of July 2024 returned a Labour government. Finance (No.2) Act 2024 was enacted post-election, but the FHL abolition draft was NOT included in that Act. The new government preferred to consolidate FHL abolition in Finance Act 2025 Schedule 5, which is the enacted result. Practical implication: any adviser briefing dated 6 March 2024 to approximately October 2024 cites "FA 2024 Sch 5" for FHL abolition; that citation is now wrong on the statute book. The correct citation is FA 2025 Sch 5. The drift is captured in HP §25.7 + §25.10 do-not-write list. (Note: FA 2024 Schedule 5 exists but is a separate Schedule on museums-and-galleries exhibitions, not FHL.)

5. **Q: Why was FHL abolished?** A: The Spring 2024 consultation document set out four arguments. (i) **Exchequer cost:** the regime's tax-relief value was estimated at significant annual cost (HMRC analysis; figures published in the impact assessment — verify the official figure at write time as the published total varies between sources). (ii) **Distortion:** the regime created a tax-driven tilt toward short-term holiday letting versus long-term private-rented housing at a time when local communities — particularly in Cornwall, the Lake District, Pembrokeshire, the Scottish Highlands — were lobbying for action on the housing-supply effects of short-term let proliferation. (iii) **Compliance complexity:** the qualifying-day tests under ITTOIA 2005 s.325 + s.326 + s.327 + s.328 + s.328A (the availability test, the actual-letting test, the period-of-grace test, the averaging election, the longer-stay carve-out) were a frequent source of enquiry disputes. (iv) **Policy alternative:** serviced-accommodation operators with genuine trading activity (managed kitchen, daily housekeeping, breakfast service) qualified for trading treatment without needing the FHL overlay — providing a policy-aligned alternative for the operators the regime was originally designed to support.

6. **Q: What survives from the regime post-abolition?** A: Three things. (i) **Grandfathered capital allowances pool** under Finance Act 2025 Schedule 5 Part 5 paragraph 18 + HMRC CA20025 transitional manual — pre-1-April-2025 / 6-April-2025 plant claims continue writing down under CAA 2001 mainline; pool balances transfer into the corresponding ordinary property business pool. (ii) **In-flight CGT positions** where disposal completed on or before 5 April 2025 — pre-abolition BADR + rollover availability stands for those disposals subject to anti-forestalling at FA 2025 Sch 5 paragraph 14 (per HMRC CG73505). (iii) **Loss-relief carry-forward** — pre-abolition FHL losses brought forward into the post-abolition residential property business continue to be available against future property profits, although stripped of the trading-treatment sideways-relief overlay.

7. **Q: Did anyone challenge the abolition?** A: No. Abolition is a primary-legislation policy decision and not amenable to judicial challenge in the way that, for example, anti-avoidance enquiries can be challenged. The technical consultation in Spring 2024 received responses from CIOT, ICAEW Tax Faculty, RICS, the Professional Association of Self-Caterers (PASC), and individual operator submissions. The consultation responses focused on transitional design — grandfathering, anti-forestalling boundaries, loss-relief preservation — rather than on challenging the substantive policy decision.

8. **Q: What is the long view of FHL as a tax category?** A: Historically the regime was a creature of an era — early 1980s — when UK domestic tourism was state-supported policy and short-term holiday letting was a small-scale activity. The regime was extended to EEA properties in 2009 under EU-law pressure. The 2010s growth of online platforms (Airbnb founded 2008, scaled globally through the 2010s) industrialised short-term letting in ways the 1984 legislation never contemplated. The 2024-25 abolition is best understood as a regime that outlived its policy-justification context, not as a punitive measure against holiday-let operators. The grandfathered transitional preservation in FA 2025 Sch 5 reflects this — the policy intention was to end the overlay, not to punish operators who structured their affairs on the existing law.

9. **Q: How does this page differ from the broad FHL rules-overview page?** A: The rules-overview page (`furnished-holiday-let-tax-rules-exemptions`) is operator-action-oriented — it covers what the post-abolition rules are and what operators need to do under them. This page is history-and-policy-oriented — it covers what the regime was, when and why it existed, and what survives. The two pages are an intentional pair: rules-overview for the operator looking up "what do I do now"; this page for the searcher trying to understand the context of abolition. Different lanes; cross-link as canonical companion.

10. **Q: Where can I read the official source material?** A: Primary statutory source: Finance Act 2025 Schedule 5 on legislation.gov.uk. HMRC guidance: PIM4160 contents page links to PIM4165 (overview), PIM4170 (commencement and qualifying tests), PIM4180 (capital allowances post-abolition), PIM4185 (CGT reliefs), PIM4190 (jointly let property). Capital allowances transitional: CA20025. CGT-side detail: CG73505. Original policy material: gov.uk policy paper "Changes to the furnished holiday lettings tax regime" (URL has moved; search gov.uk for the current location). Spring Budget 2024 red book and the technical consultation document carry the original policy reasoning.
