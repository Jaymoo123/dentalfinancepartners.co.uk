# End of the Furnished Holiday Letting Regime

## Statutory anchor
- Primary: Finance Act 2025 Schedule 5 ("Furnished holiday lettings"), verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2025/8/schedule/5 on 2026-05-26. The five-Part architecture (income tax / corporation tax / capital allowances / chargeable gains / commencement) is the legislative end-point of the regime. Part 5 paragraph 12 commencement.
- Supporting: F(No.2)A 2024 (the earlier "abolition vehicle" that was overtaken by the change of government and reissued in FA 2025 — important historically: the original draft sat in F(No.2)A 2024 Sch 5 before being moved into FA 2025 Sch 5; the change in vehicle is the source of the "abolition is in FA 2024" / "abolition is in FA 2025" confusion flagged in §25.7 HP); ITTOIA 2005 ss.322-328A (the now-omitted FHL definition + qualifying-day mechanics — the substantive regime that lived for 19 tax years from 6 April 2006 until 5 April 2025); CTA 2009 ss.264-269A (the company-side mirror, omitted by FA 2025 Sch 5 Part 2); CAA 2001 s.15(1)(c) and s.15(1)(da) (the capital allowances "qualifying activity" entries omitted by FA 2025 Sch 5 Part 3); TCGA 1992 s.241 (the historic "FHL trade-treated-as-trade" CGT lever — the genesis of BADR / rollover availability — omitted by FA 2025 Sch 5 Part 4); the pre-ITTOIA framework: ICTA 1988 ss.503-504 (originally introduced by FA 1984 ss.45-49) — the historic statutory base of FHL as a separate property-business category, in force from 6 April 1982 until rewrite into ITTOIA 2005 on 6 April 2006.
- House position reference: §6 "FHL — abolition transition" (the macro lock); §25.7 "FHL transitional under FA 2025 Sch 5" (the FA 2024-versus-FA 2025 vehicle distinction explicitly captured); §25.10 (do-not-write list including "FHL abolition is in Finance Act 2024 Schedule 5" — a frequent drift trap).

## Key questions this page must answer

1. What was the Furnished Holiday Lettings regime? (A separate property-business category for UK and EEA properties let on a short-term commercial basis to the public, meeting qualifying-day tests. Five FHL-specific tax overlays: (a) capital allowances under CAA 2001 s.15(1)(c)/(da) as a separate qualifying activity, (b) BADR availability on disposal under TCGA 1992 s.241 trading-treatment, (c) rollover relief under TCGA 1992 s.152 on business-asset reinvestment, (d) sideways loss relief against general income for active loss-makers under ITA 2007 s.127, (e) FHL profit counting as "relevant UK earnings" for pension-input purposes under FA 2004 s.189(2)(ba) extending into the ITTOIA trade-equivalence override.)
2. When did the regime exist? (Originally introduced in FA 1984 ss.45-49, then re-enacted in ICTA 1988 ss.503-504. Rewritten into ITTOIA 2005 ss.322-328A on 6 April 2006 as part of the income-tax statute rewrite. Restricted from UK-only to UK+EEA from 6 April 2009 following the European Court of Justice decision in Commission v Belgium and similar EU-law-driven expansions. Abolished by FA 2025 Sch 5 with effect from 1 April 2025 (CT) / 6 April 2025 (IT and CGT). A 41-year run from 1984 to 2025.)
3. When was abolition announced? (Spring Budget 6 March 2024 — the Conservative government's final pre-election fiscal event. Confirmed in the 2024-25 Budget red book; technical consultation document published the same week; draft legislation in F(No.2)A 2024.)
4. Why was the F(No.2)A 2024 draft superseded by FA 2025? (The general election of July 2024 returned a Labour government. F(No.2)A 2024 was enacted but the FHL abolition draft was not included — the new government preferred to consolidate FHL abolition in FA 2025 Sch 5, the enacted result. Practical implication: any adviser briefing dated 6 March 2024 to ~October 2024 cites "FA 2024 Sch 5" for FHL abolition; that citation is now wrong on the statute book. The correct citation is FA 2025 Sch 5. The drift is captured in §25.10 do-not-write list.)
5. Why was FHL abolished? (The Spring 2024 consultation document set out four arguments. (1) Cost: the regime's tax-relief value was estimated at over £300 million per year (HMRC analysis). (2) Distortion: the regime created a tax-driven tilt toward short-term holiday letting versus long-term private-rented housing at a time when local communities — particularly in Cornwall, the Lake District, Pembrokeshire, the Scottish Highlands — were lobbying for action on housing-supply effects of short-term let proliferation. (3) Compliance complexity: the qualifying-day tests under ITTOIA s.325 + s.326 + s.327 + s.328 + s.328A were a frequent source of enquiry disputes. (4) Policy alternative: serviced-accommodation operators with genuine trading activity (managed kitchen, daily housekeeping, breakfast service) qualified for trading treatment without needing the FHL overlay, providing a policy-aligned alternative for the operators the regime was originally designed to support.)
6. What survives from the regime? (Three things. (a) The grandfathered capital allowances pool under FA 2025 Sch 5 Part 5 para 18 — pre-1-April-2025 plant claims continue writing down under CAA 2001 mainline. (b) In-flight CGT positions where disposal completed on or before 5 April 2025 — pre-abolition BADR + rollover availability stands for those disposals subject to anti-forestalling. (c) Loss-relief carry-forward — FHL losses brought forward into the post-abolition residential property business continue to be available against future property profits, although stripped of the trading-treatment sideways-relief overlay.)
7. Did anyone challenge the abolition? (No — abolition is a primary-legislation policy decision and not amenable to challenge. The technical consultation in Spring 2024 received responses from CIOT, ICAEW Tax Faculty, RICS, the Professional Association of Self-Caterers (PASC), and individual operator submissions; the consultation responses focused on transitional design — grandfathering, anti-forestalling boundaries, loss-relief preservation — rather than on the substantive policy decision.)
8. What is the long view of FHL as a tax category? (Historically the regime was a creature of an era — early 1980s — when UK domestic tourism was state-supported policy and short-term holiday letting was a small-scale activity. The regime was extended to EEA properties in 2009 under EU-law pressure. The 2010s growth of online platforms (Airbnb founded 2008, scaled globally through 2010s) industrialised short-term letting in ways the 1984 legislation never contemplated. The 2024-25 abolition is best understood as a regime that outlived its policy-justification context, not as a punitive measure against holiday-let operators.)
