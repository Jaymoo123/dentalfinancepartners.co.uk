## Worked-example data (RUN-phase to render in prose + table)

**Worked example 1 — pre-abolition versus post-abolition headroom collapse:**

- *Profile:* Higher-rate-taxpayer former-FHL operator. Sole earned income was FHL profit of £55,000 per year. No employment income; no other trade income.
- *Pre-abolition (2024-25):* FHL profit £55,000 counted as relevant UK earnings under FA 2004 s.189(2)(ba) by virtue of ITTOIA 2005 ss.322-328A trade-equivalence. Tax-relieved gross personal pension contribution = lower of (a) £55,000 relevant UK earnings or (b) £60,000 annual allowance per s.227 = £55,000. Operator paid £44,000 net (£11,000 basic rate relief at source) + £11,000 further higher-rate relief via SA100 = £22,000 total tax relief on £55,000 gross contribution.
- *Post-abolition (2025-26 onwards):* FHL profit £55,000 is now UK property business income (ITTOIA Part 3), NOT relevant UK earnings. Per FA 2004 s.190 the tax-relieved contribution limit is now the FLOOR of £3,600 gross (the "relevant UK individual" floor available regardless of earnings). Operator may contribute £2,880 net (£720 basic rate relief at source) + higher-rate relief on the £3,600 gross via SA100. **Net headroom loss: £51,400 of previously-relieved pension contribution capacity per year.** Annual tax-relief loss for a 40% taxpayer = ~£20,560 per year.

**Worked example 2 — incorporation route restores headroom:**

- *Profile:* Same operator post-abolition. Incorporates the property activity into a UK Ltd company (separate B15 mechanics; this brief assumes incorporation has happened).
- *Salary route:* Company pays director salary £15,000 per year (above NI secondary threshold; salary deductible by company under CTA 2009 s.1289 wholly-and-exclusively gateway). Salary is relevant UK earnings under s.189(2)(a) employment income. Director may make tax-relieved personal contributions up to £15,000 gross (£12,000 net + £3,000 basic rate at source).
- *Employer-contribution route:* Company makes employer pension contribution £40,000 per year. Not subject to s.190 relevant-UK-earnings limit (s.190 only restricts personal contributions). Subject to wholly-and-exclusively gateway per CTA 2009 s.54 — generally accepted where contribution is part of a commercially-reasonable remuneration package. Annual allowance under s.227 still binds at individual level: £60,000 total (personal £15,000 + employer £40,000 = £55,000; £5,000 carry-forward room remains).
- *Combined headroom restored:* £55,000 per year via the company route, versus £3,600 personal-only post-abolition. The route requires incorporation (B15 mechanics), modest salary cost (employer NI 15% on salary above secondary threshold per FA 2025 EmNI uplift — verify at write time per F-19), and disciplined wholly-and-exclusively documentation.

**Worked example 3 — tapered annual allowance edge case:**

- *Profile:* Higher-net-worth former-FHL operator with £210,000 employment income + £80,000 pre-abolition FHL profit.
- *Pre-abolition (2024-25):* Adjusted income includes both employment and FHL = £290,000 (>£260,000 taper threshold). Threshold income £210,000 (>£200,000). Tapered allowance: £60,000 − ((£290,000 − £260,000) ÷ 2) = £60,000 − £15,000 = £45,000 annual allowance.
- *Post-abolition (2025-26 onwards):* FHL profit no longer counted in "adjusted income" computation for taper purposes (FHL profit is now UK property income, not trade income). Adjusted income = £210,000 (<£260,000 taper threshold). **Taper does not apply; full £60,000 annual allowance restored.** Net benefit for this profile: £15,000 of additional headroom per year, despite the loss of trade-treatment overlay.
- *Sessions: do NOT generalise the taper as "abolition helps everyone" — for the operator in Worked Example 1 the loss of relevant-UK-earnings dominates the small taper benefit.*

**Worked example 4 — £3,600 floor as last resort:**

- *Profile:* Retired-from-employment former-FHL operator, age 62, taking only pension drawdown and FHL profit. No employment income.
- *Post-abolition (2025-26 onwards):* Pension drawdown is NOT relevant UK earnings ("pension is not classed as earnings" — PTM044100 verbatim). FHL profit is no longer relevant UK earnings. Operator is a "relevant UK individual" (UK resident, under 75) and qualifies for £3,600 gross floor regardless of earnings. Contribute £2,880 net to a personal pension; provider reclaims £720 basic rate at source; further higher-rate relief through SA100 if applicable.

## FAQ draft (Stage 2 — RUN polishes prose; no further research needed)

1. **Q: Why did FHL profit count as relevant UK earnings for pension contributions before April 2025?** A: Pre-abolition, ITTOIA 2005 ss.322-328A treated qualifying Furnished Holiday Lettings as a separate property business with trade-equivalence overrides. Those overrides extended into FA 2004 s.189(2)(ba) and s.189(2)(bb), which defined "relevant UK earnings" for pension-input purposes. Profit from a qualifying FHL therefore counted alongside employment income and genuine self-employed trade income as a basis for tax-relieved pension contributions above the £3,600 floor.

2. **Q: What changed at 6 April 2025?** A: Finance Act 2025 Schedule 5 Part 1 omitted both s.189(2)(ba) and s.189(2)(bb) for income-tax purposes from tax year 2025-26 onwards. Former FHL profit is now ordinary UK property business income (or overseas property business income for EEA former FHLs). It is no longer relevant UK earnings, so it no longer supports tax-relieved personal pension contributions above the £3,600 floor.

3. **Q: How much tax relief am I losing?** A: For a 40% higher-rate taxpayer whose only earned income was £55,000 of FHL profit, the previously-available £55,000 gross pension contribution drops to £3,600 gross. That is a loss of approximately £20,560 per year in tax relief if the operator was contributing to the full earnings-limit (£22,000 relief on £55,000 contribution becomes £1,440 relief on £3,600 contribution).

4. **Q: What is the £3,600 gross floor and how does it work?** A: FA 2004 s.190 gives any "relevant UK individual" the right to make tax-relieved pension contributions up to £3,600 gross per tax year, regardless of earnings. A "relevant UK individual" is broadly someone who is UK resident and under age 75. You contribute £2,880 net to your personal pension; the provider reclaims £720 basic rate from HMRC at source. If you are a higher-rate or additional-rate taxpayer, you reclaim the additional 20% or 25% through Self Assessment. The floor survives FHL abolition entirely.

5. **Q: What still counts as relevant UK earnings now?** A: After abolition, the live categories under FA 2004 s.189(2) are: (a) employment income (PAYE salary, bonus, taxable benefits), (b) trading income from a genuine trade, profession or vocation under ITTOIA 2005 Part 2 (not ordinary property letting), and (c) certain patent royalties under s.189(2A). Property rental income — including former-FHL rental income — does NOT count. Pension drawdown does NOT count (HMRC PTM044100: "a pension is not classed as earnings").

6. **Q: If I incorporate my former FHL portfolio, does pension headroom come back?** A: Yes, via two routes. (i) Personal contributions from salary: if the company pays you a director salary (say £15,000 per year above the NI secondary threshold), that salary is employment income under s.189(2)(a) and supports personal tax-relieved contributions up to that salary amount. (ii) Employer contributions from the company: the company pays directly into your pension scheme. Employer contributions are NOT subject to the s.190 relevant-UK-earnings limit. They are deductible by the company under the CTA 2009 s.54 wholly-and-exclusively gateway, provided the contribution forms part of a commercially-reasonable remuneration package. The annual allowance under FA 2004 s.227 still binds at the individual level (£60,000 from 6 April 2023, subject to tapering). Combined headroom can return to £55,000+ per year. See B15 (`understanding-the-taxation-of-fhls-in-a-company`) for the company architecture and `transferring-fhl-portfolio-to-limited-company` for the incorporation route mechanics.

7. **Q: Can I use my SIPP to buy my holiday cottage and recover the pension advantage that way?** A: No. A SIPP (self-invested personal pension) cannot purchase residential property without triggering an unauthorised payment charge under FA 2004 s.208 plus a scheme sanction charge under s.239. The unauthorised payment charge is 40% of the property value with an additional 15% surcharge in many cases. A holiday cottage is residential. SIPPs can hold commercial property (offices, warehouses, retail units let to an unconnected trading tenant), and a SIPP commercial-property purchase is a separate post-abolition strategy worth considering for operators with accumulated pension wealth, but it is NOT a same-asset substitute mechanism for the lost FHL relevant-UK-earnings treatment.

8. **Q: I have unused annual allowance from earlier years — can I carry it forward to make a big contribution now?** A: FA 2004 s.230 carry-forward of unused annual allowance from the previous three tax years survives, but it is still gated by current-year relevant UK earnings under s.190. If you have £180,000 of unused annual allowance carried forward but only £3,600 of relevant UK earnings in the current year, your tax-relieved contribution is capped at £3,600. Employer contributions can absorb the carried-forward allowance because employer contributions are not subject to s.190, but you need an employer (so the incorporation route is the practical mechanism here).

9. **Q: Did the tapered annual allowance ever help anyone losing FHL relevant-UK-earnings treatment?** A: Marginally, for some operators. FA 2004 s.228ZA tapers the annual allowance for individuals whose "adjusted income" exceeds £260,000 and "threshold income" exceeds £200,000 (verify thresholds at write time). Both computations include trading income pre-abolition; FHL profit therefore inflated both. Post-abolition, FHL is property income and drops out of adjusted income for taper purposes. A higher-net-worth operator with £210,000 employment income plus £80,000 FHL profit might have had a tapered allowance of around £45,000 in 2024-25 and a restored full £60,000 allowance in 2025-26. Net benefit: £15,000 of additional headroom per year. The taper benefit does not compensate for the loss of relevant-UK-earnings treatment for an operator whose only earned income was the FHL.

10. **Q: My accountant told me to accelerate contributions into 2024-25 before abolition. Did that work?** A: Pre-5-April-2025 contributions paid out of 2024-25 relevant UK earnings (still including FHL profit at that point) received the full tax relief on the higher earnings-limit. The mechanic worked. From 6 April 2025 the relevant-UK-earnings limit collapses for FHL-only operators, so accelerated contributions are no longer an option. Anti-forestalling rules at FA 2025 Sch 5 paragraph 14 targeted CGT-side accelerated disposals, not pension contributions — the pension acceleration in 2024-25 was not anti-forestalled.

11. **Q: What should I actually do in 2025-26 as a former-FHL owner?** A: Decision tree: (i) continue the £3,600 floor regardless of other earnings, that is unconditional; (ii) check whether you have employment, self-employment, or other relevant UK earnings that give you additional headroom; (iii) if you have a leveraged portfolio and are higher-rate, model the incorporation route (B15) — the salary plus employer-contribution combination typically restores £55,000+ of annual headroom; (iv) consider a SIPP commercial-property purchase if you have accumulated pension wealth and the right commercial tenant opportunity (separate strategy, not same-asset). Speak to a regulated pension adviser for the incorporation modelling — the interaction of salary, NI, corporation tax, and annual allowance carry-forward is fact-specific.

12. **Q: How does this page differ from the broad FHL rules-overview page?** A: The rules-overview page (`furnished-holiday-let-tax-rules-exemptions`) carries a single FAQ paragraph noting that FHL profit no longer counts as relevant UK earnings post-2025. This page is the standalone deep treatment that the FAQ links into. The rules-overview retains the one-paragraph summary for searchers landing on the broad page; this page is for the searcher specifically working out their pension strategy. The two pages are an intentional pair: rules-overview for the survey, this page for the depth.
