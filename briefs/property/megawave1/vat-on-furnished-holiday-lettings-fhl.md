# VAT on Furnished Holiday Lettings (FHL)

## Statutory anchor
- Primary: Value Added Tax Act 1994 Schedule 9 Group 1 Item 1 (the exempt grant-of-an-interest-in-land carve-outs), with **paragraph (a)** "the grant of any interest in, right over or licence to occupy holiday accommodation" carved OUT of exemption (i.e. standard-rated). Group 1 Note 13 defines "holiday accommodation". Verified via VATA 1994 s.31(1) at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1994/23/section/31 on 2026-05-26 (s.31 confirms Schedule 9 Part 2 supplies are exempt subject to Part 3 exclusions; the Item 1 carve-outs are the gateway to the standard-rated treatment of holiday accommodation).
- Supporting: VATA 1994 s.4 (scope of VAT — taxable supplies in the course or furtherance of business); s.5 (meaning of supply, including the deemed-supply rules); s.7 (place of supply for land — UK if the property is in the UK regardless of provider residence); Schedule 9 Group 1 paragraph (d) (carve-out of "sleeping accommodation in an hotel, inn, boarding house or similar establishment" — important for the FHL-versus-serviced-accommodation boundary that runs through the post-2025 trading-versus-property-income split); VATA 1994 Sch 11 paragraph 1 (registration threshold and timing — the £90,000 12-month rolling test from 1 April 2024 per the SI 2024/239 uplift, verified at write time); SI 1995/2518 reg 25 (partial exemption framework — standard method, the "de minimis" tests, the annual adjustment); the Tour Operators' Margin Scheme (TOMS) at VAT (Tour Operators) Order 1987 SI 1987/1806 — applies to operators "buying in" and reselling accommodation to consumers, with practical relevance for some serviced-accommodation operators; FA 2024 Sch 8 (the digital platforms reporting regime — DPI — imposing reporting obligations on Airbnb, Vrbo, Booking.com etc; **not** itself a VAT-collection mechanism, but changes the visibility landscape for HMRC); VATA 1994 s.43 (group registration — relevant where a former-FHL holding company is part of a broader VAT group).
- House position reference: §6 "FHL — abolition transition" (the macro FHL position, noting that VAT is unaffected by abolition); §30 "VAT — property and supplies of land" (the broader VAT-on-property framework into which holiday accommodation slots; specifically the Sch 9 Group 1 Item 1 carve-outs); §25.7 (no direct interaction but cross-noted for the post-abolition capital-allowances side).

## Key questions this page must answer

1. Did FHL abolition change the VAT treatment of holiday-let income? (No. FHL has always been a standard-rated supply under VATA 1994 Sch 9 Group 1 Item 1(a) carve-out from the otherwise-exempt grant-of-an-interest-in-land; abolition under FA 2025 Sch 5 is an income/corporation/CGT/capital-allowances reform, not a VAT reform. The mistake is common because operators conflate "FHL regime" with "FHL VAT regime" — they were always separate.)
2. When do I have to register for VAT? (When taxable turnover in the previous 12 rolling months exceeds £90,000 — the threshold from 1 April 2024 per SI 2024/239. Registration must take effect from the 1st day of the 2nd month after the test month. For holiday-let operators, holiday-accommodation receipts ARE taxable turnover; long-term residential lets ARE exempt and NOT counted; mixed-use operators count only the holiday-accommodation side.)
3. Is the £90k threshold based on Airbnb gross or net? (Gross — i.e. the amount the guest pays before Airbnb / Vrbo commission. The platform commission is a separate input-tax-deductible cost (provided the platform is VAT-registered and the operator holds a valid invoice / VAT receipt; Airbnb-EU operates on a non-UK-VAT-charging basis with a reverse-charge implication for UK operators above the registration threshold — Stage 2 deep dive).)
4. What can I reclaim if I register? (Input tax on directly-attributable taxable inputs — utilities for the holiday-let property, cleaning, maintenance, agent commissions where invoiced with UK VAT, platform fees (with reverse-charge mechanic for non-UK platforms); some recovery on overheads via the partial-exemption standard method if mixed-use; pre-registration input-tax recovery is available for goods (4 years pre-registration) and services (6 months pre-registration) under VATA 1994 s.24.)
5. Should I voluntarily register if I am below the threshold? (Common decision: input-tax recovery on a refurbishment versus output-tax obligation on guest receipts. Worth modelling on a substantial-spend year (new kitchen, new bathroom, new boiler) when input tax recovery would otherwise be locked in the asset's cost base permanently.)
6. What is the partial-exemption position if I also have a long-term residential let? (Long-term residential let is EXEMPT under Sch 9 Group 1 Item 1 (the default grant-of-an-interest-in-land position, not caught by any of the (a)-(n) carve-outs). Mixed operators apply partial exemption — the standard method under reg 101 SI 1995/2518 attributes input tax to taxable, exempt, or non-attributable buckets and recovers a percentage of the non-attributable bucket based on the taxable-supplies-versus-total-supplies ratio. The de minimis tests under reg 106 may allow full recovery if exempt input tax is below £1,875 per quarter and 50% of total input tax.)
7. Opt to tax — does it apply to my holiday let? (No. Opt to tax is the mechanism at Sch 10 paragraphs 2-3 for converting otherwise-exempt commercial-property supplies to taxable. Holiday accommodation is already standard-rated by virtue of the Sch 9 Group 1 Item 1(a) carve-out; opting to tax adds nothing. Opting becomes relevant only where the operator also holds non-holiday commercial property and wants to fold that into a taxable VAT-recovery position.)
8. What is the boundary between FHL and "hotel-like" sleeping accommodation? (Paragraph (d) of Sch 9 Group 1 Item 1 carves out "sleeping accommodation in an hotel, inn, boarding house or similar establishment" — also standard-rated. The categorisation matters for the reduced-rated meals-included supply at standard rate, but both routes lead to standard-rated VAT on the headline accommodation supply. The boundary is more important for the trading-versus-property-income income-tax split — Stage 2 sub-links to the existing `serviced-accommodation-tax-fhl-abolition-april-2025` page on that point.)
9. TOMS — does the Tour Operators' Margin Scheme apply? (Only to operators who "buy in" travel services and resell to consumers — e.g. an OTA reselling third-party-owned holiday lets, or a UK manager booking accommodation in another EU state and reselling. A typical UK individual or company owning and letting their own holiday cottage is NOT a tour operator and TOMS does not apply.)
10. What about the FA 2024 digital platforms reporting (DPI)? (FA 2024 Sch 8 imposes reporting obligations on Airbnb, Vrbo, Booking.com and similar platforms to share UK-resident-host transaction data with HMRC. This is NOT a VAT-collection mechanism — it is a visibility regime that makes it materially harder for operators to under-declare. The headline practical impact: HMRC now has platform-level data on UK holiday-let operators' gross receipts, which makes the £90k registration threshold harder to avoid in practice and surfaces under-declared income-tax positions in parallel.)
