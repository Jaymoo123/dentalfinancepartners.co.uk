# Garden Easement and the SDLT Residential / Mixed-Use Boundary: How FTT Confirmed Residential Rates Apply to a Leasehold Dwelling Benefiting From an Easement Over Adjoining Land

## Statutory anchor
- Primary (residential property definition): FA 2003 s.116 — "(1) Subject to the provisions of this section, 'residential property' means: (a) a building that is used or suitable for use as a dwelling, or is in the process of being constructed or adapted for such use, and (b) land that is or forms part of the garden or grounds of a building within paragraph (a) (including any building or structure on such land), or (c) an interest in or right over land that subsists for the benefit of a building within paragraph (a) or of land within paragraph (b)." Verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2003/14/section/116 on 2026-05-26. **Sub-paragraph (c) is operative for this page** — an easement benefiting a residential building (or its garden/grounds) is itself "residential property" for SDLT purposes by statutory definition.
- Primary (rate consequence): FA 2003 s.55 Table A (residential rates from 1 April 2025 — 0% to £125k, 2% £125k-£250k, 5% £250k-£925k, 10% £925k-£1.5m, 12% above £1.5m); FA 2003 Sch 4ZA (5% additional dwellings surcharge from 31 October 2024 — typically the live commercial question is whether residential treatment plus surcharge applies, or mixed-use Table B disapplies surcharge altogether).
- Primary (case citation): The specific FTT decision underlying the page title — **Stage 2 sub-agent verifies the precise citation at write time** via GOV.UK FTT Tax Chamber decision search and the leading tax-press summaries (Tax Journal, Tolley, BDO / Saffery / RSM tax briefings from the 2023-2025 window). Candidate cases include any FTT decision in which an appellant argued that an easement (over an adjoining garden plot, communal garden, or amenity strip) brought the leasehold transaction within mixed-use treatment, and the FTT ruled residential. If the precise citation cannot be confirmed at Stage 2, the page is written to the statutory framework with the case discussed as "the FTT decision on leasehold-with-garden-easement (citation to be confirmed)". Stage 1b drift watchpoint flagged.
- Supporting case-law (the FTT narrowing trajectory): Hyman v HMRC [2019] UKFTT 469 + [2021] UKUT 68 + [2022] EWCA Civ 185 (the "in principle suitable" test); Mudan v HMRC [2023] UKFTT 317 (state of disrepair alone does not bring property within mixed-use); MHB Ltd v HMRC (continued narrowing); Brown v HMRC [2024] UKFTT (recent FTT in same line); Suterwalla v HMRC (paddock-with-grazing-licence argument); Hortons Hall Estate v HMRC (Suterwalla-adjacent grounds argument — covered separately at A16); Averdieck v HMRC (public-rights-of-way argument — covered separately at A9).
- House position reference: §1.J Wave 9 lock (mixed-use case-law trilogy: Hyman + Suterwalla + Hortons Hall — operative HP-line on the residential / mixed-use boundary); §1.C Wave 7 lock (Bewley uninhabitable test — narrow building-side exception). **NEW HP-LOCK CANDIDATE: §1.M Stage 1b conductor decision on whether garden-easement and PROW sub-lines extend §1.J or stand as a new sub-section**. The garden-easement question is statutorily clean (s.116(1)(c) deems an easement benefiting a residential property to be itself residential property) but factually litigated.

This page covers five things competitor coverage routinely under-serves: (a) the **specific statutory pinpoint at s.116(1)(c)** — easements over land that subsist for the benefit of a residential building or its garden/grounds are themselves residential property by statutory deeming; this sub-paragraph is often missed by aggressive-claim firms framing the argument around s.116(1)(b) "garden or grounds" alone; (b) the **leasehold-specific fact pattern** — many central-London and city-centre flats are leasehold with easements over communal gardens, basement areas, or amenity strips; the easement architecture is normal for leasehold-flat conveyancing and does not bring the transaction within mixed-use treatment; (c) the **case ratio** — what the FTT specifically said about why residential rates applied (likely: the easement subsists for the benefit of the residential leasehold; s.116(1)(c) brings it within residential property; the mixed-use argument fails on the statutory wording); (d) the **distinguishing factor from successful mixed-use cases** — successful mixed-use cases involve non-residential land (commercial farmland, commercial business premises, public-amenity land used commercially), not residential-amenity easements; the page draws the distinguishing line cleanly; (e) the **planning implication** — leasehold flat buyers, particularly in mid-priced London and city-centre transactions where the SDLT figure is material, sometimes receive mis-targeted marketing from SDLT-refund-claim firms suggesting the easement / communal-garden architecture supports a mixed-use claim; the page should warn against this without naming firms.

2. What is an "easement" for SDLT purposes? (A non-possessory right over land owned by another, for the benefit of the owner of the dominant tenement. Common examples: rights of way over a driveway; rights to use a communal garden; rights to maintenance access; rights to light. In leasehold flat conveyancing, easements are commonplace — the flat owner typically holds easements over common parts, communal gardens, basement areas, and shared facilities. The easement is created at the lease grant and runs with the leasehold title.)
3. What does FA 2003 s.116(1)(c) actually say? (Verbatim quote: "an interest in or right over land that subsists for the benefit of a building within paragraph (a) or of land within paragraph (b)" — i.e., an easement benefiting a residential building (or its garden/grounds) is itself residential property for SDLT purposes. Sub-paragraph (c) is the statutory anchor — the easement does not need separate residential-property argument; it is statutorily deemed residential because it benefits residential property.)
4. How does this differ from a public right of way (PROW) over residential grounds? (PROW is a public right; easement is a private right benefiting an identified dominant tenement. The Averdieck-line cases on PROW addressed whether the public-character of the right strips the underlying garden / grounds land of residential character (FTT: no). The garden-easement case addresses whether the easement-over-adjoining-land brings additional non-residential land into the transaction (FTT: no — the easement IS the residential interest, captured by s.116(1)(c)).)
5. What is the SDLT cost of a successful versus failed mixed-use argument on a leasehold flat transaction? (Worked example for a £1.2m leasehold flat: residential SDLT (no surcharge, sole owner-occupier) = c.£61,250 — 0% £125k + 2% £125k + 5% £675k + 10% £275k. Standard residential rate. Mixed-use Table B on £1.2m = c.£53,500 — 0% £150k + 2% £100k + 5% £950k. Mixed-use treatment saves c.£7,750; with the 5% additional dwellings surcharge eliminated (where it would have applied — adding 5% × £1.2m = £60,000 to the residential figure), the saving rises sharply. Verify all rate calculations at write per §16.35.)
6. Is the leasehold structure itself relevant? (The leasehold / freehold distinction does not affect the s.116 residential / mixed-use analysis directly — both are major interests under FA 2003 s.117. What is relevant is the bundle of rights conveyed by the leasehold — including the easements forming part of the lease grant. The transaction subject is the leasehold interest as it stands, easements included.)
7. What are the boundary cases where an easement might support a mixed-use argument? (Genuinely commercial easements over substantial non-residential adjoining land — e.g., a leasehold flat with a separate commercial easement over an income-producing commercial yard, or an easement granting the lessee a separate commercial use of a non-residential building. 9. How does the FTT decision interact with the Hyman line of authority? ### Stage 2b case-citation closure — 2026-05-26 (F-2 closed)

**Verified primary citation** (delivered by Stage 2b case-verification Agent on 2026-05-26):

- **Case name:** Bonsu v HMRC
- **Citation:** [2024] UKFTT 158 (TC) (TC09084)
- **Decision date:** 26 February 2024
- **Judge:** Tribunal Judge Richard Chapman KC
- **Key holding:** FTT held the right to use the communal garden was an easement subsisting for the benefit of the leasehold flat (within FA 2003 s.116(1)(c)). Acquisition of the lease and the easement constituted a single transaction with a single main subject matter (the leasehold); the easement was "in any event residential property" because s.116(1)(c) does not require the right to be exclusively for the benefit of the dwelling. Residential SDLT rates (Table A) applied; appeal dismissed.
- **Verification URL:** https://caselaw.nationalarchives.gov.uk/ukftt/tc/2024/158
