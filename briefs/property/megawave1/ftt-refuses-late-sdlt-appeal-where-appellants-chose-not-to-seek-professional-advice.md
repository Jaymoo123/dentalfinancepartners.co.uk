# FTT Refuses Late SDLT Appeal Where Appellants Chose Not to Seek Professional Advice: The Martland Framework Applied to the 30-Day Statutory Window

## Statutory anchor
- Primary (appeal window): FA 2003 Schedule 10 para 35 — appeal must be made within 30 days from the date of HMRC's conclusion / notice / decision being appealed. Verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2003/14/schedule/10 on 2026-05-26 (Part 7 — Reviews and Appeals).
- Primary (late-appeal jurisdiction): Tribunal Procedure (First-tier Tribunal) (Tax Chamber) Rules 2009 (SI 2009/273) — rule 5(3)(a) (general case-management power to extend time) and rule 20(4) (late appeal-notice procedure). Verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2009/273 on 2026-05-26.
- Primary (controlling authority for late-appeal applications): Martland v HMRC [2018] UKUT 178 (TCC). The Upper Tribunal three-stage framework: (1) Establish length of delay (serious / significant if more than three months as a working benchmark); (2) Establish reason(s) for delay; (3) Evaluate all the circumstances of the case, conducting the balancing exercise per Denton v TH White [2014] EWCA Civ 906. Statement of importance of compliance with statutory time limits is central — the burden on a late applicant is substantial.
- Primary (case citation): The specific FTT decision underlying the page title — **Stage 2 sub-agent verifies the precise citation at write time** via GOV.UK FTT Tax Chamber decision search; candidate cases include any FTT late-appeal decision from the 2022-2025 window where the appellants argued that not having sought professional advice at the time of the original SDLT return was their reason for delay. If precise citation cannot be confirmed, the page is written to the statutory + Martland framework with the case discussed as "the FTT decision on professional-advice reasoning (citation to be confirmed)". Stage 1b drift watchpoint flagged.
- Supporting (the "no professional advice" line of authority): the line is consistent in tax-tribunal jurisprudence — see Hicks v HMRC [2020] UKFTT 365 (TC); BPP Holdings v HMRC [2017] UKSC 55 (UKSC affirmed the importance of statutory compliance); HMRC v Katib [2019] UKUT 189 (TCC) on the relationship between agent failures and personal responsibility. The repeated line: not taking professional advice is the taxpayer's own choice and does not constitute a good reason for delay; the obligation to lodge an appeal in time rests on the taxpayer regardless of whether they have engaged an adviser.
- Supporting (penalty / interest interaction): FA 2003 Sch 10 paras 12-14 (enquiry into the return); FA 2003 Sch 10 paras 28-29 (closure notice + assessment); TMA 1970 ss.59 + 59B for analogous interest mechanics on income-tax appeals (SDLT interest runs from the date the tax was due under FA 2003 s.87).

This page covers six things competitor coverage routinely under-serves: (a) the **30-day statutory window** under FA 2003 Sch 10 para 35 — short, strict, and runs from the date of HMRC's decision (closure notice, assessment, or determination), NOT from the date the taxpayer realises a mistake; (b) the **Martland three-stage framework** — length of delay, reason for delay, all the circumstances — applied verbatim by FTT to SDLT late-appeal applications; (c) the **"chose not to take professional advice" line** — the FTT line of authority is consistent: not taking advice is a taxpayer choice and not a good reason for delay; the obligation to comply with statutory time limits is personal; (d) the **practical implication for SDLT-refund-claim firms** — taxpayers approached by aggressive refund-claim firms months or years after completion are often outside the statutory appeal window; the FTT case underscores that the late-claim route is narrowly gated by Martland; (e) the **overpayment-relief alternative** — where the 30-day appeal window has passed, FA 2003 Sch 10 para 34 (overpayment relief) is the alternative route within 4 years of the chargeable transaction date; the two routes have different procedural gateways and different available outcomes; (f) the **cost of an unsuccessful late-appeal application** — original assessment stands; FTT may award costs against the appellant in a Complex-procedure case; interest under FA 2003 s.87 has continued accruing throughout the delay period.

## Key questions this page must answer

1. What was the FTT case about? (Single-paragraph case summary: a taxpayer (or set of taxpayers) sought permission to bring a late SDLT appeal — typically several months or years after the 30-day statutory deadline — and offered as their reason for delay that they had not, at the relevant time, sought professional advice on the SDLT position. The FTT applied the Martland three-stage framework and refused permission. Stage 2 fills citation + delay length + paragraph reference.)
2. What is the SDLT appeal time-limit? (30 days from the date of HMRC's decision — closure notice under FA 2003 Sch 10 para 28, assessment under para 29, or determination under para 25. The 30 days runs from the date of the decision notice, not the date the taxpayer receives it (the receipt presumption is built into the statute) — Stage 2 verifies receipt-versus-issue mechanics at write per §16.35.)
3. What is the Martland framework? (Upper Tribunal three-stage test from Martland v HMRC [2018] UKUT 178 (TCC): (a) establish length of the delay; (b) establish the reason(s) for the delay; (c) evaluate all the circumstances of the case, including the importance of statutory time-limits, the merits of the substantive case, the prejudice to each party, and the conduct of the appellant. The framework imports the Denton v TH White [2014] EWCA Civ 906 civil-procedure approach into the tribunal context, modified for the tax-tribunal statutory time-limit regime.)
4. Why is "I did not take professional advice" not a good reason for delay? (Three reasons: (i) the obligation to lodge an appeal in time rests on the taxpayer personally; statutory time-limits do not pause for the taxpayer's adviser-engagement decisions; (ii) not taking advice is a positive choice by the taxpayer, not an external obstacle preventing compliance; (iii) the underlying SDLT position was within the taxpayer's reasonable means to investigate at the time of the return; subsequent advice that reveals an alternative position does not engage the late-appeal jurisdiction.)
5. Is there any "no-adviser" scenario where the late-appeal application would succeed? (Narrow. Examples: serious illness preventing the taxpayer from acting; bereavement of an immediate family member with operational impact; physical inability to receive the decision notice (e.g., overseas with no postal redirection). Even in these scenarios, the Martland framework requires the FTT to weigh all the circumstances, and a successful application is uncommon. Stage 2 surveys reported FTT decisions for the narrow-success line.)
6. What is the SDLT overpayment-relief route if the 30-day window has passed? (FA 2003 Sch 10 para 34 — overpayment relief claim within 4 years of the chargeable transaction date. Different procedural gateway; requires the taxpayer to identify a specific overpayment of SDLT and provide evidence supporting the overpayment claim; HMRC may agree the claim or refuse it, and a refusal generates a fresh 30-day appeal window under para 35. The overpayment-relief route is more often the practical option for SDLT-refund-firm-driven claims.)
7. How long is "too long" for a Martland-late-appeal? (Working benchmark from Martland and subsequent authority: more than three months is "serious or significant" delay. Many SDLT late-appeal applications involve delays of several months to several years from the closure notice or assessment date; these are well outside the working benchmark and place a heavy burden on the appellant to justify.)
8. What costs exposure does an unsuccessful late-appeal application carry? (In Default-procedure cases (most SDLT appeals start here), each side bears its own costs. In Complex-procedure cases, the loser may face a costs order. Either way, the original SDLT assessment stands; interest under FA 2003 s.87 continues accruing from the original tax-due date; possible FA 2007 Sch 24 penalty exposure if HMRC contends the original return was careless or deliberate.)
9. What about appeals against penalty determinations? (Penalty appeals under FA 2007 Sch 24 also have a 30-day statutory window; the Martland framework applies identically; the "no professional advice" reasoning is similarly weak for penalty late-appeals. Cross-reference to penalty appeal procedure pages where the site adds them.)
10. What should a taxpayer do if they believe they have an SDLT overpayment but are well outside the 30-day window? (Check whether the overpayment-relief 4-year window is still open; engage a qualified tax adviser to evaluate the merits of an overpayment-relief claim; do NOT pursue a late-appeal application on "no professional advice" reasoning unless there is an additional substantive justification (illness, bereavement, similar). Cross-link to the existing `sdlt-refund-scams-how-to-avoid` page and to the FTT case for the substantive late-appeal line.)
