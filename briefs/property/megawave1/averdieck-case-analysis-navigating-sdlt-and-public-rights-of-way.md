# Averdieck Case Analysis: Public Rights of Way, the SDLT Residential / Mixed-Use Boundary, and the Narrowing Tribunal Line on Grounds Arguments

## Statutory anchor
- Primary (residential property definition): FA 2003 s.116 — "(1) Subject to the provisions of this section, 'residential property' means: (a) a building that is used or suitable for use as a dwelling, or is in the process of being constructed or adapted for such use, and (b) land that is or forms part of the garden or grounds of a building within paragraph (a) (including any building or structure on such land), or (c) an interest in or right over land that subsists for the benefit of a building within paragraph (a) or of land within paragraph (b)." Verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2003/14/section/116 on 2026-05-26 (confirmed in force).
- Primary (mixed-use rate consequence): FA 2003 s.55(1B) Table B (non-residential / mixed-use rates: 0% to £150,000, 2% £150,000-£250,000, 5% above £250,000 — verify at write per §16.35). Where any non-residential property is included in the transaction, the entire transaction is taxed at Table B non-residential rates (no apportionment) — this binary character of the rate-table line is what makes the s.116(1)(b) garden-or-grounds question so high-stakes.
- Primary (case citation): Averdieck v HMRC. **Stage 2 sub-agent verifies the precise citation at write time** — likely a recent FTT Tax Chamber decision in the 2022-2024 window; the case has appeared in tax-press summaries on the public-rights-of-way mixed-use argument. If citation cannot be confirmed, flag for Stage 1b drift review and write to the statutory framework with the case discussed as "Averdieck v HMRC (FTT, [date to be confirmed])". Note that "Averdieck" is sometimes seen spelt "Averdiek" or "Avderdieck" in secondary sources — Stage 2 verifies canonical spelling against the official tribunal decision.
- Supporting: Hyman v HMRC [2019] UKFTT 469 + [2021] UKUT 68 + [2022] EWCA Civ 185 (the "in principle suitable" test; minor garden / grounds features do not undermine residential character); Mudan v HMRC [2023] UKFTT 317 (unmodernised state alone does not bring property within mixed-use); MHB Ltd v HMRC (broader narrowing of mixed-use claims); Brown v HMRC [2024] UKFTT (continued narrowing trajectory); P N Bewley Ltd v HMRC [2019] UKFTT 65 (TC) TC07097 (the "substantially uninhabitable" exception under s.116(1)(a)).
- House position reference: §1.C Wave 7 lock (Bewley uninhabitable test — narrow exception only for substantially structurally dangerous / contaminated / requiring complete reconstruction properties); §1.J Wave 9 lock (mixed-use case-law trilogy: Hyman + Suterwalla + Hortons Hall — verify at Stage 2 by reading §1.J); §1 main text (residential SDLT rates + the 5% surcharge architecture that mixed-use arguments are usually attempting to escape). **NEW HP-LOCK CANDIDATE: §1.M "Public rights of way and the SDLT residential / mixed-use boundary — Averdieck principle"** for Stage 1b conductor decision — the existing §1.J mixed-use case-law trilogy lock could be EXTENDED to include the Averdieck PROW argument as a sub-line.

## Framing differentiator (anti-templating, anti-cannibalisation)

This page is a **single-case-analysis page** on the FTT decision in Averdieck v HMRC, focused on the **public-rights-of-way argument** that has recurred in residential / mixed-use SDLT disputes. The site has a deep mixed-use page (`sdlt-mixed-use-property-classification`) and the Wave 9 mixed-use case-law trilogy page (`sdlt-mixed-use-rates-vs-residential-property-tribunal-tests-landlords` — Hyman / Suterwalla / Hortons Hall coverage). It does NOT yet have a dedicated PROW-mixed-use analysis. The Averdieck case stands at the intersection of two distinct FTT lines: (i) the Hyman / Mudan / MHB / Brown narrowing of mixed-use claims based on grounds-features generally, and (ii) the specific class of cases where a public footpath, bridleway, or other PROW crosses the grounds of a residential property and the taxpayer argues that the existence of the PROW means a portion of the grounds is "non-residential land" within s.116, taking the whole transaction onto mixed-use rates.

This page covers four things competitor coverage routinely under-serves: (a) the **statutory question precisely** — whether a PROW over residential grounds is "land that is or forms part of the garden or grounds of a building" within s.116(1)(b) (the orthodox view: yes, the PROW does not strip the underlying land of its garden-or-grounds character) or whether it is non-residential land creating mixed-use treatment (the taxpayer argument, generally rejected); (b) the **Averdieck ratio and fact-pattern** — what specifically the FTT looked at when rejecting (or accepting, depending on the outcome) the PROW-mixed-use argument; (c) the **boundary cases** — when does PROW or PROW-like access argument actually move the dial (commercial usage of the path; substantial public-amenity buildings within the grounds; severance of garden into discrete public-accessible portions); and (d) the **cost of failure** — a successful mixed-use claim on a £1.5m purchase saves around £80,000-£120,000 in SDLT; a failed claim leaves the taxpayer with the original liability plus enquiry costs plus potential penalty exposure under FA 2007 Sch 24 if the claim is held to be careless or deliberate.

Distinguish from the general `sdlt-mixed-use-property-classification` page by **case-specific analysis** (Averdieck is one decision; the general page sweeps the doctrine), from the Wave 9 mixed-use case-law trilogy page by **focus on the PROW sub-line** (which the trilogy page acknowledges in passing but does not deep-dive), and from the Bewley line `sdlt-bewley-uninhabitable-property-test-non-residential-rates-landlords` by **subject matter** (PROW arguments concern grounds, not building habitability). Target length 2,400-2,800 words.

## Key questions this page must answer

1. What was the Averdieck case about? (Single-paragraph case summary: a residential property purchased at consideration triggering the higher-band residential rates; the taxpayer claimed mixed-use treatment on the basis that a public right of way crossed the grounds, making a portion of the land non-residential. The FTT ruled [outcome — verify at Stage 2] on the PROW-mixed-use argument.)
2. What is a "public right of way" for SDLT purposes? (A public footpath, bridleway, byway, or restricted byway over land; created by statute, dedication, or long usage; recorded on the local-authority Definitive Map or Statement; does not give the public ownership of the underlying land but does give a right of passage.)
3. What is the statutory test under FA 2003 s.116? (Section 116(1)(b) brings within "residential property" any "land that is or forms part of the garden or grounds of a building" within s.116(1)(a). The question is whether a PROW over the land removes it from the "garden or grounds" character. Tribunal line: no, the PROW is an encumbrance on the land, not a redesignation of the underlying use.)
4. How does Averdieck fit into the Hyman / Mudan / MHB / Brown narrowing trajectory? (The mixed-use boundary has narrowed substantially since 2021; PROW arguments are part of the residual class of mixed-use claims that taxpayers still try and FTT typically rejects unless the fact pattern is exceptional.)
5. Are there any fact patterns where the PROW argument has succeeded? (Rarely. Stage 2 sub-agent looks for any FTT decisions where PROW or analogous public-access feature has moved the residential / mixed-use line; if none, the page makes the point that the argument is generally a losing one and the taxpayer should not pursue it speculatively.)
6. What about commercial usage of the path? (If the PROW is used commercially — for example, a public car park serving the path with consideration paid — the analysis may shift. The same applies where the PROW is associated with substantial public-amenity buildings within the grounds, severing the garden into discrete portions some of which serve a public-amenity purpose.)
7. What is the SDLT cost of pursuing and losing a PROW-mixed-use claim? (Original SDLT liability stands; enquiry costs (HMRC and adviser); potential FA 2007 Sch 24 penalty if the claim is held careless or deliberate. Aggressive mixed-use claim firms occasionally take fees on contingent-success basis; the residual exposure if the claim fails sits with the taxpayer.)
8. How does this interact with the additional dwellings surcharge? (Mixed-use treatment, if successful, disapplies the 5% additional dwellings surcharge because the surcharge applies only to residential property — Sch 4ZA's reach is residential-property-only. A successful mixed-use claim therefore saves both the residential / mixed-use rate differential AND the 5% surcharge — a double-win that makes the claim attractive to second-home buyers but does not change the FTT's narrow reading of the s.116 boundary.)
9. What is HMRC's enquiry posture on PROW-mixed-use claims? (High. PROW-mixed-use claims are a known SDLT-relief-firm pattern; HMRC opens enquiries routinely and FTT decisions have consistently supported HMRC on the fact patterns examined.)
