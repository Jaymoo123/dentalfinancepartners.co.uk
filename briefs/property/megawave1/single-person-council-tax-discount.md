# Single-Person Council Tax Discount: How the 25% Discount Under LGFA 1992 s.11(1)(a) Actually Works, Who Counts, Who Gets Disregarded, and How to Apply Without Triggering a Council Tax Overpayment Review

## Statutory anchor

- Primary: **Local Government Finance Act 1992 s.11(1)(a)** — the single-occupant 25% discount. Where "on a particular day, only one such person is so resident" (i.e. only one non-disregarded adult is resident in the dwelling), the council tax is reduced by the "appropriate percentage" (fixed at 25% by s.11(3)). The discount is statutory; a billing authority cannot refuse a properly-evidenced claim. The discount applies day-by-day; status changes mid-month / mid-year trigger pro-rata recalculation. Verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/14/section/11 on 2026-05-26.
- Primary: **LGFA 1992 Schedule 1 — Persons disregarded for purposes of discount.** Eleven principal disregards (numbered paragraphs 1-11): persons in detention (para 1); severely mentally impaired (para 2); persons for whom child benefit is payable (para 3 — the 18 and 19 year-old child-benefit-recipient route); students (para 4); apprentices and youth-training trainees (para 5); patients in hospitals (para 6); persons in care homes (para 7); religious-community members (para 8); persons resident in hostels / night-shelters (para 8A); persons providing care (paras 9-10); members of visiting forces and diplomats (paras 11-12). Each disregard is operatively defined and verifiable against the Schedule 1 text — Stage 2 re-verifies the current paragraph numbering and definitions against legislation.gov.uk consolidated text.
- Primary: **LGFA 1992 s.11(5) — definition of "resident":** an individual is "resident" in a dwelling on a day "if it is his sole or main residence" — the "sole or main residence" test is a question of fact and is the most-litigated single concept across the council-tax statute. The Williams v Horsham DC line and the Bennett v Copeland BC line (the leading FTT / High Court cases on the "sole or main residence" test) ground the practical fact-pattern analysis. Stage 2 sources current authority.
- Supporting: **SI 1992/558 Class N (all occupants full-time students)** — the Class N exemption removes council tax entirely (not a discount; an exemption); critical interaction with the single-person discount because (i) a household of one student + one non-student gets the single-person discount (student is disregarded under Sch 1 para 4) — NOT Class N (Class N requires ALL occupants to be students); (ii) a household of two students gets Class N exemption (£0); (iii) a household of three students + one non-student gets the single-person discount (one non-student, three disregarded students). The misclassification can cost or save the entire annual bill. Cross-reference HP §30 proposed (council-tax framework lock — see Framing differentiator on HP numbering).
- Supporting: **SI 1992/558 Class W (annex occupied by dependent relative)** — interacts with the single-person discount for households containing a granny-annex; an annex used by a dependent relative aged 65+ / severely mentally impaired / substantially disabled is fully exempt under Class W. If the main dwelling has one non-disregarded resident, the main dwelling gets the single-person discount and the annex gets the Class W exemption.
- Supporting: **LGFA 1992 ss.11A-11C** — empty-property premium (s.11B) and second-home premium (s.11C). These INCREASE the bill; they are not discounts. But they interact with the single-person discount where a property has empty periods (e.g. a single-occupant who travels for extended periods) — the property may move from single-person-discount to empty-property-premium if the LA's definition of "no resident" applies (typically 6 weeks unoccupied + furnishings present, but LA-by-LA variation).

## Key questions this page must answer

1. What is the single-person council tax discount? (LGFA 1992 s.11(1)(a) — 25% off the council-tax bill where only one "resident" lives in the dwelling. "Resident" defined by s.11(5) as the dwelling being the person's "sole or main residence." Statutory; not means-tested; applies day-by-day; pro-rated on mid-year status changes.)
2. Who counts as a "resident" and who gets disregarded? (Resident = adult (18+) whose sole or main residence is the dwelling. Sch 1 disregards: full-time students; student nurses; apprentices; severely mentally impaired persons; persons in detention; persons resident in hospitals + care homes; 18 + 19 year olds for whom child benefit is payable; members of religious communities; foreign-language assistants; members of visiting forces; diplomats. A two-adult household where one adult is a Sch 1-disregarded person = single-person discount applies (because only ONE non-disregarded adult).)
3. How do I apply? (Three routes: (a) most LAs have an online application form on their council-tax page — fill in the form + upload supporting evidence; (b) phone application via the LA's council-tax team; (c) postal application via the LA's downloadable form. Application is FREE and direct to the LA. Do NOT use third-party "discount recovery" firms that charge a fee — the route is free.)
4. What evidence do I need? (For a straightforward single-occupant claim: proof of residency (council-tax bill or utility bill in your name; tenancy agreement or title deeds if you own); declaration that no other adult resides at the property; evidence of any Sch 1 disregard claimed for any other adult resident (student certificate from institution; medical evidence for severe mental impairment; care-home admission letter; etc.). The LA may ask for additional evidence + may carry out a verification visit.)
5. How far can the discount be backdated? (Typically to the date the entitlement arose, subject to (a) the LA's evidential satisfaction and (b) the LGFA 1992 Sch 4 collection limitation (typically 6 years). Apply promptly to avoid losing entitlement; an LA may decline backdate beyond a certain period (commonly 1 year for cooperative cases) without strong evidence.)
6. What happens if circumstances change — do I have to tell the LA? (Yes — within 21 days of any "relevant change of circumstances" per LGFA 1992 s.16(6). Examples: another adult moves in; the disregarded adult ceases to be a student / apprentice / disregarded; you move to a new property; civil partnership formation / dissolution. Failure to notify is a criminal offence + £70 penalty + LA recovery action. The 21-day clock starts the day the change occurs, not the day you notice.)
7. What is the "sole or main residence" test and how is it applied? (Question of fact, not a bright-line rule. Williams v Horsham DC + Bennett v Copeland BC line: factors include where the person sleeps most nights; where personal possessions are; where the person's family / partner / dependants are; where the person is registered to vote; where the person receives mail; where the person works (if local); the person's intentions. No single factor is determinative; the test is holistic. A person can only have ONE "sole or main residence" at a time — even if they have multiple homes. Stage 2 sources current FTT / High Court authority.)
8. What if I have two properties — can I claim the discount on both? (No. The single-person discount applies only to the property that is your "sole or main residence." The other property is your "second home" — typically subject to the s.11C second-home premium (LA-set, up to 100% under LURA 2023 reforms), NOT the single-person discount. Some LAs no longer offer ANY discount on second homes; many LAs charge a premium.)
9. What about co-habiting partners — does that affect the discount? (Yes — a co-habiting couple (married, civil-partner, or unmarried cohabiting) = two adult residents = no single-person discount UNLESS one of them is Sch 1 disregarded. Common scenarios where one partner is disregarded: one partner is a full-time student; one partner is severely mentally impaired with appropriate medical certification; one partner is a foreign-language assistant or diplomat. A separation where one partner has moved out + the LA agrees they are no longer "resident" at the property = the remaining partner qualifies for the single-person discount from the date of effective separation.)
10. What is the scam-firm risk? (Multiple firms cold-call residents offering "council tax discount recovery services" for a fee (typically 30-50% of recovered amounts). The route is FREE direct to the LA. Citizens Advice has a published warning. The legitimate route: apply directly to the LA via the LA's council-tax page. No third-party intermediary required.)
11. What happens if the LA later reviews and decides I was not entitled? (LA can recover up to 6 years of mis-applied discount under LGFA 1992 Sch 4 collection limitation. Recovery letters are rare but devastating — typically follow a tip-off from a neighbour or a council-tax review program. Defence: evidence of genuine sole-or-main-residence at the property; evidence of the Sch 1 disregard of any other resident; evidence of timely notification of any changes. If recovery proceeds, appeal to the Valuation Tribunal under LGFA 1992 s.16(1).)
12. How does the single-person discount interact with Council Tax Reduction (CTR)? (Separately — apply for the discount AND the CTR if eligible. CTR is means-tested and operated by the LA under LGFA 1992 Sch 1A (England) / Sch 1B (Wales); pensioner CTR is centrally-prescribed (SI 2012/2885); working-age CTR is locally-determined. CTR can reduce the post-discount bill by up to 100% depending on income. The single-person discount applies first; CTR applies to the residual bill.)
