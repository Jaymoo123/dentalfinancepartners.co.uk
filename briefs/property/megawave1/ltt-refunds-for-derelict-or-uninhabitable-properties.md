# Welsh LTT Refunds for Derelict and Uninhabitable Properties: The Dwelling-Suitability Test and the Two Routes to a Refund

## Statutory anchor
- Primary: **Land Transaction Tax and Anti-avoidance of Devolved Taxes (Wales) Act 2017, section 72** (meaning of "residential property"), verified verbatim at `https://www.legislation.gov.uk/anaw/2017/1/section/72` on 2026-05-26. s.72(1)(a) defines residential property as a building "used or suitable for use as a dwelling" or "in the process of being constructed or adapted for such use". The "suitable for use as a dwelling" test is the operative threshold for whether a derelict or uninhabitable building falls inside or outside the residential rate table.
- Supporting: **LTTA 2017 s.24** ("Regulations specifying tax bands and tax rates") — residential rate-setting basis; **LTTA 2017 s.25** — non-residential rate-setting basis (the alternative rate table that applies where s.72 dwelling-suitability fails). **Tax Collection and Management (Wales) Act 2016 s.41** ("Amendment of tax return by taxpayer") — 12-month return amendment route from filing date. **TCMA 2016 s.63** ("Claim for relief in respect of double assessment to devolved tax") and **TCMA 2016 s.78** (general 4-year overpayment-claim limit) — the longer-stop overpayment-claim route from filing date. **TCMA 2016 ss.172-184** — Welsh Revenue Authority review and Welsh tribunal (Tax Chamber) appeal route. **LTTA 2017 Sch 5 para 1(2)** — the dwelling-suitability question feeds into whether higher rates can engage at all (no dwelling = no higher rates, regardless of buyer's other property holdings).
- Cross-jurisdictional comparators (cited but distinguished, not relied on as Welsh authority): FA 2003 s.116 SDLT dwelling-suitability test; Bewley v HMRC [2019] UKFTT 65 (TC) (SDLT — narrow exception for substantially uninhabitable / contaminated / structurally dangerous properties); Hyman v HMRC [2022] EWCA Civ 185 (SDLT — mixed-use rejection); Mudan v HMRC [2023] UKUT (TCC) and MHB Properties v HMRC (SDLT — dwelling-suitability test narrowed post-Bewley). Welsh tribunals applying LTTA 2017 s.72 may draw on these cases but are not bound by them; the Welsh test is a Welsh-statutory question.
- House position reference: **§23.1-23.3** (Welsh LTT framework, residential rate bands, non-residential rate bands), **§1.C** (SDLT Bewley exception — Wave 7 lock; the narrowness of the Bewley line is the load-bearing cross-jurisdictional position). **§23.11** do-not-write (the Welsh test is not identical to SDLT; don't conflate). **§1.H** (the post-Bewley narrowing: Mudan, MHB, Brown all caution against overstating Bewley availability — the same caution applies to LTT).

## Key questions this page must answer

1. What does "suitable for use as a dwelling" mean under LTTA 2017 s.72(1)(a), and what is the dwelling-suitability test applied at the effective date of the transaction? (Buildings substantially constructed for the purpose, retaining essential dwelling characteristics: shelter (sound roof, weather-tight walls), the basic functional rooms (kitchen, bathroom), structural integrity, and habitable services (electricity, water, sewerage). The test is objective and not buyer-intention-dependent.)
2. What defects DO take a property outside the dwelling definition under s.72? (Collapsed roof, structural movement requiring substantial repair, removed kitchens and bathrooms, asbestos contamination requiring removal, condemnation notices, substantial damp / rot affecting the structural integrity, fire damage requiring rebuild rather than refurbishment.)
3. What defects do NOT take a property outside the dwelling definition? (Routine renovation, dated decor, absent furnishings, kitchens and bathrooms requiring replacement but in place, missing white goods, broken windows, paintwork in poor condition. The Bewley test as narrowed by Mudan / MHB / Brown — Welsh tribunals likely to follow this line absent contrary Welsh authority.)
4. What is the difference between "in disrepair" and "not a dwelling"? (Disrepair is a renovation question; not-a-dwelling is a question of whether the property could be occupied AT ALL without first being put through substantial structural reconstruction. The two lines sit very close together in the case law and most contested cases hinge on the boundary.)
5. What evidence should the buyer commission BEFORE completion to support either a non-residential return or a future refund claim? (Properly-scoped RICS Building Survey, EICR / gas safety reports showing services condition, photographs at completion date, surveyor's specific reasoning on dwelling-suitability rather than just "in poor condition", any local authority condemnation or prohibition orders, asbestos surveys if relevant.)
6. Should the buyer file non-residential at the outset or file residential and reclaim? (Strategic-choice analysis: filing non-residential is faster for cash flow but means defending the position against likely WRA enquiry; filing residential and reclaiming under TCMA s.41 / s.63 means cash outlay but typically less enquiry friction. The right answer depends on (a) strength of evidence at completion, (b) WRA's known position on similar properties, (c) buyer's cash-flow tolerance, (d) intended use post-renovation.)
7. What is the non-residential rate table under LTTA 2017 s.25 and what is the cash difference for a typical derelict-property purchase? (Non-residential rates: 0% to £225,000; 1% £225,001 to £250,000; 5% £250,001 to £1m; 6% above. A £350,000 derelict farmhouse purchase, residential rates would be roughly £7,750 at standard or substantially more at higher rates; non-residential rates would be £6,250. The worked-example saving is concrete but not always dramatic; verify rates at write time per §16.35.)
8. How does dwelling-suitability interact with the higher-rates regime under Sch 5? (If the property is non-residential, Sch 5 does not engage at all — higher rates do not apply to non-residential transactions. For a buyer who already owns a dwelling, this can be the determinative point: non-residential classification removes both the residential rate and the higher-rates surcharge.)
9. What is the Welsh tribunal (Tax Chamber) line on s.72 dwelling-suitability? (Stage 2 must search the Welsh tribunal decisions database — likely at `https://www.gov.wales/welsh-revenue-authority-tribunal-decisions` or `https://www.gov.wales/welsh-tribunals` — for s.72 dwelling-suitability cases since 2018 and name any decisions that have set the Welsh line. Where the Welsh line is sparse, the page should say so and note the heavy reliance on SDLT comparator case law.)
10. What are the time limits for action? (Non-residential return at outset: file within 30 days of the effective date per LTTA 2017 s.41. Amendment of residential return to non-residential: within 12 months of filing per TCMA 2016 s.41. Overpayment claim where amendment window has closed: within 4 years of filing per TCMA 2016 s.78. Welsh Revenue Authority review of refusal: 30 days from refusal notice; onward Welsh tribunal appeal: 30 days from review conclusion.)
