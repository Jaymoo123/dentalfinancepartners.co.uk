## Worked-example data (RUN-phase to render in prose + table)

**Cumulative-effect longitudinal worked example — typical higher-rate former-FHL operator:**

- *Profile:* Individual operator, two former-FHL properties in the Lake District. Tax-year-2024-25 gross rent £75,000. Mortgage interest £25,000. Operating costs (cleaning, agent, repairs) £20,000. Capital allowances WDA on £30,000 main pool at 18% = £5,400. Higher-rate taxpayer (other income above £50,270 threshold).

- *2024/25 (pre-abolition, pre-Section 24 ingress for FHL):*
  - Gross rent £75,000 − interest £25,000 − operating £20,000 − WDA £5,400 = profit £24,600.
  - Income tax at 40% = £9,840.
  - FHL trade-equivalence: BADR-eligible on sale (subject to qualifying-day tests); pension-relevant-UK-earnings of £24,600.
  - Effective tax: ~£9,840 on £24,600 profit = 40%.

- *2025/26 (post-abolition; Section 24 ingressed; BADR lost; pension RUE collapsed):*
  - Section 24 mechanic: full rent £75,000 less operating £20,000 less WDA (grandfathered) £4,428 = property profit £50,572 (interest now ADDED BACK to taxable profit).
  - Tax at 40% on £50,572 = £20,229. LESS Section 24 tax credit = 20% × £25,000 interest = £5,000 credit. NET tax = £20,229 − £5,000 = £15,229.
  - **Net additional tax 2025/26 vs 2024/25 = £15,229 − £9,840 = £5,389 increase.**
  - Plus pension-relevant-UK-earnings collapse (separate effect — see B14 worked example): up to £20,000 of additional pension-tax-relief lost.
  - Plus BADR no longer available on sale (planning effect for 2026/27 disposal scenario).

- *2026/27 (steady-state):*
  - Same Section 24 mechanic. Position broadly unchanged from 2025/26 (subject to any rent increase or rate-band-shift effects).
  - Plus MTD-ITSA at £50,000 threshold: this operator (£75,000 gross rent) is REQUIRED to use MTD-compatible software, file quarterly updates, submit year-end finalisation from 6 April 2026.

- *2027/28 (property-income surcharge per draft FA 2026 — treat per HP §7 as scheduled not crystallised):*
  - IF 2027 surcharge enacted: 2% surcharge on UK property income produces effective higher-rate at 42% on the £50,572 profit.
  - Tax at 42% on £50,572 = £21,240. Less £5,000 Section 24 credit = £16,240.
  - **Net additional tax 2027/28 vs 2025/26 = £16,240 − £15,229 = £1,011 incremental annual cost from the surcharge alone.**
  - Plus pensions-in-IHT commencement (separate effect — affects succession planning, not annual tax cost).

- *2028/29 (MTD-ITSA threshold drops to £20,000):*
  - This operator already inside MTD-ITSA at 2026/27 (£75k > £50k threshold). The 2028/29 cut-down to £20,000 does not change this operator's position; affects operators with combined trading + property gross qualifying income in the £20,000-£30,000 range who were previously sub-MTD.

- *Cumulative effect (2024/25 → 2027/28):* Net annual tax cost rises from £9,840 to £16,240 = £6,400 incremental. Plus pension headroom loss (£20k tax relief lost = up to £8,000 cash impact). Plus IHT planning shifts (pension freedom retracted; succession planning needs re-modelling). Total annual cash impact: ~£14,400 negative on a £24,600 pre-abolition net profit profile. **The cumulative effect on a typical higher-rate two-property former-FHL operator is approximately 60% reduction in net post-tax cash position across the window.**

**Sub-worked example — comparison cell for someone who incorporated:**

- *Same profile, incorporated to UK Ltd company on 6 April 2025:* CT at marginal rate 26.5% on £50,572 = £13,402 vs £15,229 personal-side post-abolition. £1,827 annual saving. Plus pension headroom restored via salary + employer-contribution route (B14 mechanism). Plus 5% additional dwellings SDLT surcharge on any NEW acquisitions via the company (£500k acquisition triggers £25,000 surcharge). The incorporation decision is materially favourable for this leverage profile.

## FAQ draft (Stage 2 — RUN polishes prose; no further research needed)

1. **Q: What is the single largest change to hit FHL operators in the 2024-2028 window?** A: Finance Act 2025 Schedule 5 — the regime abolition itself. From 6 April 2025 (income tax) and 1 April 2025 (corporation tax), former FHL operators lost: full mortgage interest deductibility (Section 24 ingress for individuals); Business Asset Disposal Relief on sale (10% rate cliff); capital allowances on NEW plant spend (CAA 2001 s.35 dwelling-house restriction bites); pension-relevant-UK-earnings treatment of FHL profit (FA 2004 s.189 omissions); sideways loss relief against general income (ITA 2007 s.127 amendment). Capital allowances grandfathered pool continues writing down under FA 2025 Sch 5 Part 3 transitional.

2. **Q: What changed on 31 October 2024 and how does it interact with FHL operators buying additional stock?** A: The additional dwellings SDLT surcharge rose from 3% to 5% via Finance Act 2025 section 51(1)-(2) (NOT F(No.2) 2024 as commonly mis-cited; the Autumn 2024 Budget on 30 October 2024 was enacted in FA 2025 c.8 in the following parliamentary session). The surcharge applies to FHL acquisitions by any individual or company that already owns another residential property at completion. For a £400,000 FHL purchase, the increase is £8,000 of additional SDLT versus pre-31-October-2024.

3. **Q: What changed on 30 October 2024 for CGT on FHL disposals?** A: Residential CGT rates rose to 18% basic and 24% higher from the previous 18% / 28% (announced and effective from Autumn Budget 2024 — TCGA 1992 s.4 amendment). The spread between basic and higher rate narrowed. For pre-abolition FHL disposals where BADR was still available (10% rising to 14% from 6 April 2025), the rate change interacted with BADR planning. For post-abolition disposals the residential rate stands without BADR.

4. **Q: The 6 March 2024 announcement — what locked in?** A: The Spring Budget 2024 announcement set FHL abolition for tax-year-2025-26 onwards. Anti-forestalling provisions applied to disposals between announcement (6 March 2024) and abolition (5 April 2025): HMRC retained the right to apply post-abolition CGT rates to artificially-accelerated disposals where the seller's primary motive was to lock in BADR before the regime ended. The anti-forestalling rule (now codified at FA 2025 Sch 5 paragraph 14 per HMRC CG73505) blocks BADR relief on pre-abolition disposals via unconditional contract where the contract had no genuine commercial purpose and connected parties were involved.

5. **Q: 1 April 2025 versus 6 April 2025 — why two commencement dates?** A: FA 2025 Sch 5 Part 5 paragraph 12 splits the commencement by tax type. Corporation tax amendments take effect for accounting periods beginning on or after 1 April 2025. Income tax and capital gains tax amendments take effect for tax year 2025-26 onwards (6 April 2025 for individuals). The dual-date pattern reflects the standard split between the corporation-tax fiscal year start (1 April) and the income-tax fiscal year start (6 April).

6. **Q: 6 April 2026 — what hits former-FHL operators?** A: Two material changes. (i) **MTD-ITSA staging at £50,000 threshold:** individual former-FHL operators with combined trading plus property-business gross qualifying income above £50,000 must use MTD-compatible software, file quarterly updates, and submit a year-end finalisation. Many former-FHL operators sit above this threshold. (ii) **IHT combined £1m BPR + APR cap commencement** per HP §9: the previously-unlimited 100% relief on qualifying agricultural and business property is capped at £1m combined per estate; excess gets 50% relief (effective 20% IHT). Standard residential BTL never qualified for BPR (Pawson). Former FHL never categorically qualified post-abolition. Affects landed estates and trading-business owners; rarely affects standard FHL operators directly but worth noting for landed-FHL portfolios.

7. **Q: 6 April 2027 — the property-income surcharge.** A: Announced Autumn Budget 2024; scheduled in draft Finance Act 2026; a 2% surcharge on UK property income on top of standard income tax bands produces effective rates of 22% basic, 42% higher, 47% additional. Former FHL income is UK property income for this purpose. Per HP §7 framing rule, treat as SCHEDULED, not crystallised — plan against the rates but do NOT assert as confirmed law until Royal Assent. The page's worked example shows the incremental cost (~£1,000 per year on a higher-rate operator with £50k property profit).

8. **Q: 6 April 2027 — pensions-in-IHT commencement.** A: Per HP §9, unused defined-contribution pension funds become IHT-relevant from this date (announced Autumn Budget 2024). Affects former-FHL operators whose retirement plan was "save into the pension via FHL relevant-UK-earnings, draw down in retirement and leave the residual to children IHT-free". That plan was already broken at 5 April 2025 (B14 cross-link: relevant-UK-earnings collapse); the IHT side compounds the problem from 2027.

9. **Q: 6 April 2028 — MTD-ITSA threshold drops to £20,000.** A: Catches lower-turnover former-FHL operators previously sitting below the staged thresholds. A single former-FHL operator with £25,000 gross rent plus £12,000 pension drawdown would not be inside MTD-ITSA at 6 April 2026 (under £50k) but is inside it at 6 April 2027 (over £30k) and stays inside it at 6 April 2028 (over £20k). Verify exact 2028 threshold against current SI at write time; staging dates have shifted before. Pension drawdown is NOT itself qualifying income but combines with rental for the threshold test.

10. **Q: What is the cumulative effect on a typical higher-rate-taxpayer former-FHL owner?** A: For a two-property operator with £75,000 gross rent, £25,000 interest, higher-rate taxpayer profile: 2024/25 net post-tax cash ~£14,760 (profit £24,600 minus tax £9,840). 2025/26 net cash drops to ~£35,343 net of tax on the inflated Section-24 profit base (£50,572 profit minus £15,229 tax = £35,343 — but the GROSS-of-interest profile shifts) — the operator effectively pays tax on interest cost. By 2027/28 with the property-income surcharge layered on top, cumulative annual tax cost rises by ~£6,400 versus pre-abolition. Plus loss of pension headroom (~£8,000 cash impact) and IHT planning disruption. Plus loss of BADR on any planned disposal. Cumulative net-cash deterioration is material — roughly 60% reduction in net post-tax position for a typical leveraged two-property operator.

11. **Q: Does anything help offset the cumulative impact?** A: Incorporation is the standard offset (B15 cross-link). Inside a UK Ltd company, Section 24 does not bite (full mortgage interest deductibility under CTA 2009 s.272), CT applies at marginal rate (26.5%) rather than personal-side blended Section-24 effective rate, and pension headroom can be restored via the salary + employer-contribution route (B14 cross-link). The 5% SDLT surcharge on any NEW company acquisitions and ATED for £500k+ properties (B15) are the costs to weigh. Existing personal-side stock incurs an incorporation-transfer cost (s.162 incorporation relief if available — see `transferring-fhl-portfolio-to-limited-company`).

12. **Q: How does this page differ from the existing FHL rules-overview page?** A: The rules-overview page (`furnished-holiday-let-tax-rules-exemptions`) is TOPIC-led — it covers the rules thematically (what counts as FHL, what changed, what reliefs apply). This page is DATE-led — it surveys the eight individual changes in chronological order across the 2024-2028 window. Different organising spine; cross-link as canonical companion. The rules-overview is the topic-led survey; this page is the date-led survey.
