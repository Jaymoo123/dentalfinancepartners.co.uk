# Selling a House Below Market Value: The Partial-Sale-Partial-Gift Analytical Framework Across CGT, IHT, SDLT, and the RICS Red Book Valuation Evidence Required for HMRC Defence

## Statutory anchor

- Primary (CGT — deemed market value for non-arm's-length disposals): **TCGA 1992 s.17 — Disposals and acquisitions treated as made at market value.** Section heading verified. Subsection (1): a person's acquisition or disposal of an asset is deemed to be for consideration equal to market value where the transaction is not an arm's-length bargain — specifically including (a) gifts; (b) transactions between connected persons; (c) certain other categories (distributions in specie; transfers on death). Verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12/section/17 on 2026-05-26.
- Primary (CGT — connected persons): **TCGA 1992 s.18 — Transactions between connected persons.** Specifies that disposals between connected persons are deemed at market value (regardless of actual consideration); losses on disposals to connected persons are only allowable against gains on disposals to the same connected person (the "clogged loss" rule). Verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12/section/18 on 2026-05-26.
- Primary (CGT — connected persons definition): **TCGA 1992 s.286 — Connected persons.** Six operative categories: (1) individual + spouse / civil partner; (2) individual + relative (defined as brother, sister, ancestor, lineal descendant — note: NOT including aunt, uncle, niece, nephew, cousin); (3) individual + relative's spouse / civil partner; (4) individual + spouse's relative; (5) trustees + settlor (and certain beneficiaries); (6) company + person controlling it. Note: an UNCLE is NOT a "connected person" for CGT purposes under s.286(8). This is a frequently-misunderstood corner. Verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12/section/286 on 2026-05-26.
- Primary (IHT — gift element as a PET): **IHTA 1984 s.3A — Potentially Exempt Transfers.** A gift between individuals is a PET. The PET ceases to be chargeable if the donor survives 7 years from the date of the gift; if the donor dies within 7 years, the PET becomes a chargeable transfer at the date of gift value, taxed at the appropriate IHT rate with taper relief from 4 years post-gift. The gift element in a below-market-value sale is a PET to the extent of the differential (market value minus actual consideration received). Cross-reference HP §15.
- Primary (IHT — escape route for non-gratuitous transactions): **IHTA 1984 s.10 — Transactions not intended to confer gratuitous benefit.** A disposition that (a) was NOT intended to confer any gratuitous benefit AND (b) was made between unconnected persons OR was such as might be expected to be made in a transaction between unconnected persons (arm's-length comparable) is NOT a "transfer of value" for IHT purposes. This is the critical IHT escape: a transaction at below market value but for genuine commercial reasons (e.g. quick sale, distressed sale) can fall outside IHT entirely. Verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1984/51/section/10 on 2026-05-26.
- Primary (SDLT — deemed market value for sales to connected company purchasers): **FA 2003 s.53 — Deemed market value where transaction involves connected company.** Where the purchaser is a company AND either (a) the vendor is connected with the purchaser or (b) the consideration consists of an issue of shares in a connected company, the chargeable consideration is deemed to be NOT LESS than the market value of the subject-matter at the effective date. The deeming rule applies even where the actual consideration paid is below market. Note: the rule applies ONLY to purchases by a connected COMPANY; sales to connected individuals at undervalue are NOT subject to a deemed-market-value rule for SDLT (the chargeable consideration is the actual amount paid). Verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2003/14/section/53 on 2026-05-26.
- Primary (SDLT — normal chargeable consideration): **FA 2003 Sch 4 paragraphs 1-7** — chargeable consideration for SDLT is the actual consideration paid for the property (not deemed market value, except per FA 2003 s.53 connected-company rule). A sale at undervalue between individuals (e.g. parent to child) has SDLT only on the actual consideration paid — the gift element does NOT attract SDLT.
- Primary (PPR): **TCGA 1992 ss.222-225 — Private Residence Relief.** PPR exempts gains on the disposal of a dwelling that has been the taxpayer's only or main residence throughout the period of ownership (with certain final-period exemptions + ancillary periods of absence). The PPR exemption applies to the deemed market value disposal in a s.17 below-market sale. If the seller's home is PPR-exempt, the gift element does not generate CGT even though the deemed market value rule applies. Cross-reference HP §5.
- Primary (spouse / civil partner exemption): **TCGA 1992 s.58 — Spouses and civil partners.** Disposals between spouses / civil partners living together are at no-gain-no-loss (the transferee inherits the transferor's base cost; no chargeable gain). The spouse exemption applies BEFORE the s.17 deemed-market-value rule (which would otherwise apply to connected persons). IHT spouse exemption under IHTA 1984 s.18 also applies — transfers between UK-resident spouses / civil partners (or where the recipient is UK-resident under the LTR test) are wholly exempt from IHT. Cross-reference HP §15 + §22.5.
- Supporting (CGT — gifts holdover relief): **TCGA 1992 s.165 — Holdover relief for gifts of business assets.** Available for gifts of business assets (commercial property used in a trade) — NOT typically available for gifts of residential investment property. Stage 2 verifies the precise scope at write date.
- Supporting (CGT — gift relief for chargeable lifetime transfers into trust): **TCGA 1992 s.260 — Holdover relief on gifts which are chargeable lifetime transfers.** Available where the gift element triggers a chargeable lifetime transfer (e.g. gift into a discretionary trust). The gift element is then held over (no immediate CGT) but the donee inherits the lower base cost.
- House position reference: cross-reference HP §1 (SDLT) + HP §5 (CGT) + HP §15 (IHT) + HP §22.5 (spouse exemption) + proposed §30 (council-tax framework, indirectly relevant via discount eligibility on transferred property). No NEW HP LOCK NEEDED for this page.

## Framing differentiator (anti-templating, anti-cannibalisation)

This is the **partial-sale-partial-gift decision primer** — the analytical framework for the scenario where a property changes hands at less than open market value but for some actual consideration paid (i.e. NOT a pure gift; NOT an arm's-length sale).

This page deliberately covers the **hybrid case** the existing pages do not: a partial sale + partial gift (e.g. "I want to sell my £350k flat to my son for £200k"). The hybrid case is analytically distinct because:

(a) CGT s.17 deems the disposal at £350k market value, but the seller has actually received only £200k cash — so the CGT liability must be funded from elsewhere; (b) the gift element (£150k differential) is a PET under IHT s.3A; (c) SDLT applies to the actual £200k paid (NOT the deemed market value, because the buyer is an individual not a connected company); (d) the parent + child are connected persons under TCGA 1992 s.286, so the deeming rule bites; (e) any CGT loss on a lateral disposal to the same child can only be set against later gains to that same child (the "clogged loss" rule).

This page covers eight things competitor coverage routinely under-serves or gets wrong: (a) the **partial-sale-partial-gift case is NOT the same as a pure gift** — competitor pages routinely conflate them; the analytical framework differs because actual consideration is paid (so SDLT is in play for the buyer, and the seller has cash to fund CGT); (b) the **CGT s.17 deemed-market-value applies REGARDLESS of the actual consideration** — many family members assume "if I pay something, the gift is just the differential" — true for IHT purposes but NOT for CGT, where the WHOLE disposal is at deemed market value; (c) the **SDLT s.53 connected-company deeming is a TRAP** — readers planning to sell to their own SPV at below-market-value pay full-market-value SDLT; (d) the **SDLT s.53 deeming does NOT apply to sales between connected individuals** — the SDLT on a parent-to-child sale at £200k is on the £200k, not on the deemed £350k market value (only the IHT PET and the CGT s.17 deeming apply); (e) the **uncle / aunt / niece / nephew / cousin scenario** — these relationships are NOT "connected persons" under TCGA 1992 s.286(8), so a sale to a niece at below market value MAY be at the actual consideration (no s.17 deeming) — though HMRC may challenge if the price is materially below market; (f) the **PPR + spouse exemption stack** — a PPR-exempt main residence sold to a spouse at any consideration is doubly-protected (spouse exemption AT no-gain-no-loss + PPR exempts gain on later third-party sale); (g) the **IHT s.10 "non-gratuitous" escape** — a genuine arm's-length-equivalent sale (e.g. quick distressed sale to an unrelated party) can fall outside IHT entirely even at below market value; (h) the **valuation evidence requirement** — for any partial-sale-partial-gift scenario, a contemporaneous RICS Red Book valuation supporting the market value is essential to defend the position to HMRC + the District Valuer (cross-reference C11 brief on free rental valuation vs Red Book valuation).

## Key questions this page must answer

1. What is a "below-market-value" sale and how is it taxed differently from a pure gift? (A sale at less than open market value where actual consideration is paid. Analytically a partial sale + partial gift: actual price paid = sale element; differential to market value = gift element. CGT s.17 deems the WHOLE disposal at market value (not just the gift element). IHT s.3A PET applies only to the gift element (the differential). SDLT applies only to the actual consideration paid (unless s.53 connected-company deeming applies). Each tax is analysed separately with its own rules.)
2. What is the CGT position when I sell my house to my son for less than it is worth? (TCGA 1992 s.17 — deemed market value. The disposal is treated as made at market value for CGT, regardless of the actual price paid. So if the market value is £350k and you sell to your son for £200k, the deemed disposal consideration is £350k. CGT applies to the gain calculated by reference to the deemed £350k disposal proceeds (less base cost). The son acquires at deemed £350k base cost — i.e. his future CGT is calculated from £350k, not from the £200k he paid. Connected persons rule under TCGA 1992 ss.18 + 286 applies.)
3. What is the IHT position on a partial gift / partial sale? (IHTA 1984 s.3A — the gift element (market value minus actual consideration) is a Potentially Exempt Transfer (PET). 7-year clock starts at the date of the transaction. If the donor survives 7 years, the PET ceases to be chargeable. If the donor dies within 7 years, the gift element is brought into the death estate at the date-of-gift value, taxed at the appropriate IHT rate with taper relief from 4 years post-gift. The actual consideration received does NOT enter the donor's estate (it's been paid out).)
4. What is the SDLT position when I sell to my son? (FA 2003 Sch 4 — the SDLT chargeable consideration is the ACTUAL consideration paid (£200k in the example). The gift element is NOT chargeable for SDLT. So SDLT is calculated on £200k as a normal residential purchase (with the 5% additional-dwellings surcharge applying if the son already owns another dwelling). NOTE: this is different from the SDLT-connected-company position — see Q5.)
5. What about selling to my own limited company / SPV at below market value? (FA 2003 s.53 — the SDLT chargeable consideration is deemed to be NOT LESS than market value. So selling your £350k home to your SPV for £200k = SDLT on £350k (NOT £200k). The "I'll save SDLT by undervaluing the sale to my company" plan FAILS under s.53. The CGT position is also at deemed market value under TCGA s.17 (companies are connected with persons controlling them under s.286(6)). The cash actually paid (£200k) is what the SPV pays — the SDLT + CGT liabilities are based on the deemed £350k. This is a frequently-walked-into trap for landlord-incorporation plans.)
6. Are aunts / uncles / nieces / nephews / cousins "connected persons" for these rules? (For CGT — NO. TCGA 1992 s.286(8) defines "relative" as brother, sister, ancestor, lineal descendant — NOT aunt, uncle, niece, nephew, or cousin. So a sale to a niece at below market value may be at the actual consideration (no s.17 deeming) for CGT purposes. For IHT, the relationship matters less — any below-market disposition is a transfer of value under IHTA 1984 s.3A unless s.10 "non-gratuitous" applies. The asymmetry between CGT and IHT in defining "family" is one of the most-overlooked corners of the rules.)
7. What if I sell my main residence to my son at below market value — does PPR protect me? (Yes. TCGA 1992 ss.222-225 — PPR exempts gains on a dwelling that has been the seller's only or main residence throughout the period of ownership. The PPR exemption applies to the deemed market value disposal under s.17. So if the home is fully PPR-exempt, the deemed market value disposal generates zero CGT. The IHT PET position is unaffected — the gift element is still a PET. The SDLT position on the son's purchase is on actual £200k.)
8. What about a sale at below market value between spouses / civil partners? (TCGA 1992 s.58 — disposals between spouses / civil partners living together are at no-gain-no-loss (the transferee inherits the transferor's base cost; no chargeable gain). The s.58 spouse exemption applies BEFORE the s.17 deemed-market-value rule. IHT spouse exemption under IHTA 1984 s.18 also applies (the transfer is wholly exempt — no PET). SDLT exemption under FA 2003 Sch 3 para 3 applies for transfers on divorce / separation per court order; otherwise the actual consideration is the SDLT chargeable consideration. So spouse-to-spouse below-market sale = no CGT (s.58) + no IHT (s.18) + SDLT on actual consideration (FA 2003 Sch 4) or full exemption per Sch 3 para 3 if on divorce.)
9. Can I escape the IHT consequences by claiming the sale was "not intended to confer gratuitous benefit"? (IHTA 1984 s.10. Two cumulative conditions: (a) the disposition was not intended to confer any gratuitous benefit AND (b) the disposition was made between unconnected persons OR was such as might be expected to be made in a transaction between unconnected persons. The s.10 escape is high-bar. A parent-to-child sale at below market value will almost always FAIL s.10 (intention to confer benefit is presumed from the family relationship). A genuine quick / distressed sale to an unrelated party at below valuation may succeed under s.10 — HMRC will review the facts. RICS Red Book valuation + contemporaneous commercial-evidence file (broker offers, time on market, etc.) supports the s.10 claim.)
10. What valuation evidence do I need to defend the position? (Two evidential pillars: (a) a RICS Red Book valuation (Market Value, signed by an MRICS / FRICS chartered surveyor under the Red Book methodology) — supports the "deemed market value" figure for CGT s.17 + the "gift value" for IHT s.3A; (b) for s.10 IHT-escape claims, a contemporaneous commercial-evidence file (offers received from the open market, time on market, distressed-sale evidence, broker correspondence). Cross-link to C11 brief (`free-rental-valuation`) for the Red Book / estate-agent-appraisal distinction. HMRC's District Valuer (DV) will challenge weak valuation evidence.)
11. What happens if HMRC challenges the valuation? (HMRC's District Valuer (DV) reviews valuation positions in significant cases. The DV may propose an alternative valuation; if the taxpayer disagrees, the dispute moves to the Lands Tribunal / Upper Tribunal (Tax and Chancery Chamber). Outcome turns on the strength of the contemporaneous evidence. The cost of dispute can exceed the tax at stake. Prevention: instruct a Red Book valuation at the date of the transaction; retain the evidence file.)
12. What about CGT holdover relief — can I avoid CGT on the gift element? (TCGA 1992 s.165 — holdover relief for gifts of business assets — NOT available for residential investment property (the asset is not a "business asset"). TCGA 1992 s.260 — holdover relief on gifts which are chargeable lifetime transfers — available where the gift is into a discretionary trust (which is a chargeable lifetime transfer triggering s.260 eligibility). Holdover relief is therefore NOT typically available for a partial-sale-partial-gift to an individual; the CGT on the deemed market value disposal must be paid. Specialist trust-route structuring may use s.260 — Stage 2 covers the niche scenarios.)
