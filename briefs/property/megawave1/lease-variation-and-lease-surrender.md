## Statutory anchor

- Primary (the chargeable-interest treatment of surrender and variation): FA 2003 c. 14 section 43 "Land transactions". Two sub-paragraphs are operative:
  - **s.43(3)(b)** — verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2003/14/section/43 on 2026-05-26 — treats the surrender or release of a chargeable interest as both an acquisition by any person whose interest or right is benefitted or enlarged by the transaction (typically the landlord, whose freehold reversion is enlarged on surrender of the tenant's lease) AND a disposal by the person ceasing to be entitled to the interest (the tenant). The SDLT consequence is symmetric: the landlord acquires the merged interest as a chargeable transaction; the tenant disposes for CGT purposes.
  - **s.43(3)(d)** — verified at the same URL on 2026-05-26 — treats the variation of a lease as an acquisition and disposal of a chargeable interest only where the variation takes effect (or is treated for the purposes of this Part) as the grant of a new lease. This is the statutory pickup of the common-law surrender-and-regrant doctrine (see C14 companion seed for the doctrinal analysis); FA 2003 Sch 17A paras 9, 13, 15A operate as the statutory variation-treatment framework. Verified at the section 43 URL above on 2026-05-26.
- Primary (the surrender-and-regrant overlap-relief mechanic when it does apply): FA 2003 Sch 17A para 9 "Rent for overlap period in case of grant of further lease" — applies when a variation amounts to surrender-and-regrant under either the common-law doctrine or s.43(3)(d); the rent under the new lease for the overlap period is reduced by the rent that would have been payable under the old lease (see C14 companion seed for full mechanic). Verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2003/14/schedule/17A on 2026-05-26.
- Primary (variation treatment paragraphs): FA 2003 Sch 17A para 13 (rent increase before fifth-year point treated as grant of new lease — carved out for rent-review-clause increases by para 13(2) + para 7(4A)); FA 2003 Sch 17A para 15A (variation reducing rent or term, or supported by money consideration other than rent/term variation, treated as acquisition of chargeable interest by lessee (lessor for term reduction)). Para 14 — "abnormal increase after fifth year" — was REPEALED by FA 2013 Sch 41 para 7(1). Verified at the Sch 17A URL above on 2026-05-26.
- Primary (reverse-premium / surrender-consideration treatment): FA 2003 c. 14 Schedule 4 paragraph 18 + Schedule 17A paragraph 18 — **RUN session verifies paragraph numbering against current consolidated text** (Stage 1 flagged ambiguity here; Sch 4 reverse-premium was historically at para 18; Sch 17A reverse-premium-on-surrender at para 18 — RUN must re-confirm against legislation.gov.uk). The "reverse premium" framework operates where the landlord pays the tenant to take a lease, or to surrender or vary a lease; the consideration is generally not chargeable on the tenant's side under SDLT (the tenant is receiving consideration, not paying it), but the landlord may face SDLT consequences depending on the surrender mechanic. The historical exclusion of certain reverse-premium consideration from SDLT chargeable amounts is a non-obvious carve-out that competitor pages routinely miss.
- Primary (corresponding LBTT framework — Scotland): LBTT(S)A 2013 c. 11 (asp) Schedule 19 "Leases" — Scottish Parliament Act. The variation and surrender treatment under LBTT broadly mirrors SDLT Sch 17A paragraphs 9, 13, 15A; surrender under LBTT(S)A 2013 s.5 (chargeable interest) and Sch 19 mechanics follow the Scottish-tax framework. Verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/asp/2013/11/schedule/19 on 2026-05-26 (cross-referenced via the M1-A-B2 archer-uk-limited brief seed and the C14 companion seed in this batch).
- Primary (corresponding LTT framework — Wales): LTTA 2017 (anaw 1) Schedule 6 "Leases". Variation and surrender treatment under LTT broadly mirrors SDLT; Welsh paragraph numbering differs and was renumbered in early commencement amendments — RUN session verifies against https://www.legislation.gov.uk/anaw/2017/1/schedule/6 at write time.
- Primary (CGT on surrender — tenant side): TCGA 1992 c. 12 section 22 (capital sums derived from assets — surrender consideration is a capital sum derived from the lease), with the lease being a wasting asset under TCGA 1992 Schedule 8 where the residual lease term is 50 years or less. The wasting-asset rules at TCGA 1992 Sch 8 paras 1-7 produce a depreciation of allowable expenditure over the remaining term using a curve set in Schedule 8 Part II (the "Schedule 8 percentages" used in property practice). RUN session verifies the current Schedule 8 percentages at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12/schedule/8 and provides a worked example. The CGT consequence of a voluntary surrender against a reverse premium is therefore a chargeable gain on the tenant calculated on the surrender consideration minus the lease's depreciated allowable expenditure.
- Primary (CGT on variation — tenant side): TCGA 1992 s.21 (assets and disposals); s.22 (capital sums derived from assets — a variation supported by consideration paid by the landlord is a capital sum derived from the lease asset and constitutes a part-disposal). The wasting-asset rules at Sch 8 again bite where the lease term is 50 years or less.
- Supporting (deemed surrender by operation of law): the common-law principle that conduct inconsistent with the continuation of the lease — surrender by operation of law — is a property-law doctrine recognised by SDLT under s.43(3)(b). Authorities: Allen v Rochdale Borough Council [2000] Ch 221; Tarjomani v Panther Securities Ltd (1983) 46 P&CR 32. The SDLT charge follows the common-law surrender — if the conduct deems a surrender, s.43(3)(b) treats it as an acquisition by the landlord and disposal by the tenant. RUN session must source 2-3 deemed-surrender cases to ground the analysis.
- Supporting (commercial-lease security-of-tenure interactions): Landlord and Tenant Act 1954 (c. 56) Part 2 ss.24-46 — Part 2 gives qualifying business tenants security of tenure on a renewal at the end of the contractual term; the renewal is a new lease for SDLT purposes; para 9 overlap-relief applies. Surrender during the contractual term may forfeit Part 2 rights and triggers the SDLT surrender analysis under s.43(3)(b). RUN session verifies the current Part 2 status and any post-Renters' Rights Act 2026 amendments affecting residential security of tenure (the 2026 Act is residential-only, but the page must avoid any LTA 1954 confusion in residential context — LTA 1954 Part 2 is commercial, not residential).

This page covers seven things competitor coverage routinely under-serves or gets wrong:

1. The **express FA 2003 s.43(3)(b) treatment of surrender** — competitor pages routinely state "surrender of a lease has no SDLT consequence" without addressing that s.43(3)(b) treats the surrender as an acquisition by the landlord whose interest is "benefitted or enlarged"; the SDLT liability on a surrender for consideration falls on the LANDLORD, not the tenant; the tenant's side is a CGT event, not an SDLT event.
2. The **deemed-surrender-by-operation-of-law doctrine** — conduct inconsistent with the continuation of the lease (Allen v Rochdale; Tarjomani v Panther) can produce a deemed surrender without a formal deed; the SDLT consequences follow automatically; competitor pages routinely miss this.
3. The **reverse-premium framework** — landlord pays tenant to surrender or vary; the consideration is generally outside the tenant's SDLT-chargeable-amount but inside the tenant's CGT computation; the landlord may face SDLT on the merged interest; FA 2003 Sch 4 + Sch 17A define the boundaries.
4. The **boundary between variation that triggers surrender-and-regrant and variation that does not** — covenant restructuring, demised-premises addition, term extension TRIGGER; pure rent variation usually does not at common law (although caught separately by para 13 in the first five years); pure covenant variation supported by money consideration is caught by para 15A(1A); the page builds a decision-tree.
5. The **CGT consequences on the tenant of surrender or variation for consideration** — capital sum derived from asset under TCGA 1992 s.22; wasting-asset rules under Sch 8 for leases ≤ 50 years; depreciated-expenditure formula; the tenant's chargeable gain is calculated on the consideration received minus the lease's adjusted base cost.
6. The **deed-of-variation name collision with IHTA 1984 s.142** — addressed in opening orientation.
7. The **commercial vs residential variation patterns** — commercial-lease variations (LTA 1954 Part 2 context) typically involve covenant restructuring and rent variation; residential-lease variations typically involve demised-premises adjustment or service-charge variation; the SDLT analysis differs in worked-example details though the underlying framework is the same.

## Key questions this page must answer

1. What is a lease variation in the SDLT sense, and how does the FA 2003 framework treat it? (A lease variation is a change to the terms of a continuing lease — to the demised premises, the term, the rent, or any covenant. The FA 2003 framework treats a variation as a chargeable land transaction only where it takes effect, or is treated, as the grant of a new lease — FA 2003 s.43(3)(d). The Sch 17A statutory framework then applies: para 9 overlap-relief if the variation amounts to surrender-and-regrant; para 13 if the variation increases rent in the first five years of the term; para 15A if the variation reduces rent or term, or is supported by money consideration other than rent/term variation. Para 14 was REPEALED by FA 2013.)
2. What is a lease surrender, and how does s.43(3)(b) treat it? (A surrender is the giving-up of a lease by the tenant back to the landlord, extinguishing the leasehold interest. FA 2003 s.43(3)(b) treats the surrender as an acquisition by the person whose interest is "benefitted or enlarged" — the landlord, whose freehold reversion is enlarged by the merger. The landlord's acquisition is a chargeable transaction. The tenant's side is a disposal for CGT purposes — see Q5. The mechanics of the landlord's SDLT calculation: chargeable consideration is whatever the landlord pays (or is deemed to pay, including non-monetary benefit) for the surrender; commonly nil if the tenant surrenders voluntarily without reverse premium.)
3. What is a reverse premium, and how is it treated? (A reverse premium is consideration paid by the landlord to the tenant — either to take a lease in the first place, or to surrender or vary an existing lease. FA 2003 Schedule 4 + the corresponding Sch 17A treatment for surrender/variation reverse premiums define the framework — RUN session verifies paragraph numbering. The general rule: a reverse premium is NOT chargeable consideration for the tenant's SDLT purposes (the tenant is receiving, not paying), but is a capital sum derived from the asset for the tenant's CGT purposes. The landlord's SDLT consequences depend on the surrender or variation mechanic — a surrender for reverse premium gives the landlord a chargeable interest (the merged freehold reversion plus tenant's lease) at consideration equal to the reverse premium paid.)
4. What is deemed surrender by operation of law? (Common-law doctrine: conduct by the parties inconsistent with the continuation of the lease — typically the tenant giving up possession and the landlord accepting it back without protest — amounts at law to a surrender, without need for a formal deed. Authorities: Allen v Rochdale Borough Council [2000] Ch 221; Tarjomani v Panther Securities Ltd (1983) 46 P&CR 32. SDLT consequences follow under s.43(3)(b) as if a formal surrender had occurred — the landlord's interest is treated as benefitted/enlarged. Practical implication: parties who informally walk away from a commercial-lease arrangement should formalise the surrender by deed to obtain certainty on the SDLT effective date and consideration; otherwise the deemed surrender's effective date is the date of the inconsistent conduct, which may be litigated.)
5. What are the CGT consequences for the tenant on surrender or variation for consideration? (TCGA 1992 s.22 — capital sum derived from an asset — the surrender consideration or variation consideration is treated as a part-disposal (variation) or full disposal (surrender) of the lease for CGT purposes. The lease's allowable expenditure is calculated under TCGA 1992 s.38 (acquisition cost) and depreciated under Schedule 8 if the lease term remaining at acquisition was 50 years or less (the wasting-asset rules). Sch 8 percentages produce a depreciation curve — the deduction is the acquisition cost multiplied by (Sch 8 percentage for years held / Sch 8 percentage for years remaining at acquisition). The chargeable gain is the surrender or variation consideration minus the depreciated allowable expenditure minus incidental costs. RUN session builds a worked example using current Sch 8 percentages.)
6. What variations trigger surrender-and-regrant and which do not? (Triggering variations — covered in detail in the C14 companion seed: term extension; addition of demised premises; fundamental covenant restructuring. Non-triggering variations — covered here in more depth: rent-only variation under a rent-review clause (carved out by Sch 17A para 13(2) + para 7(4A) for SDLT purposes; non-trigger at common law); minor covenant variations not affecting the substantive bargain; service-charge formula adjustments; rent-deposit-deed variations. Borderline variations — require fact-specific analysis: covenant restructuring affecting break clauses or assignment rights; addition of substantial covenants supported by money consideration (potentially within para 15A(1A)).)
7. What is the SDLT treatment of a variation that DOES NOT amount to surrender-and-regrant? (Three rules apply in the alternative. Para 13: first-five-years rent increase outside a rent-review clause = deemed grant of new lease in consideration of the additional rent. Para 15A(1): rent reduction = acquisition by lessee of a chargeable interest at consideration equal to whatever the lessee paid for the reduction. Para 15A(1A): any other variation supported by money or money's worth consideration (other than rent/term variation) = acquisition by lessee at consideration equal to the variation consideration. Para 15A(2): term reduction = acquisition by lessor. Each of these is a partial chargeable transaction — not the full new-lease analysis under s.43(3)(d) — and the chargeable consideration is the variation consideration, not the underlying lease premium.)
8. How does the IHT deed-of-variation under IHTA 1984 s.142 differ from a lease deed-of-variation? (IHTA 1984 s.142 deed-of-variation: redirects the bequests of a deceased's estate to different beneficiaries within two years of death, with retrospective effect for IHT purposes. Lease deed-of-variation: alters the terms of a continuing lease between landlord and tenant during the lease's currency. Entirely separate statutory regimes and entirely separate practical effects. The shared "deed of variation" terminology causes SERP confusion; the page disambiguates in the opening paragraph and includes a cross-link to the existing site IHT deed-of-variation page for readers who arrived at the wrong topic.)
9. How do LBTT and LTT treat the same fact patterns? (LBTT(S)A 2013 Sch 19 paras 13-14 (variation) and the LBTT(S)A 2013 s.5 chargeable-interest definition (surrender) apply in Scotland. LTTA 2017 Sch 6 paras 19-22 apply in Wales. Both regimes broadly mirror SDLT with jurisdiction-specific rate and threshold differences. Cross-border variations — e.g. an English lease varied in a Scottish-domiciled landlord's portfolio — are SDLT (the situs of the land controls, not the parties' domicile). RUN session verifies the precise paragraph numbering and any post-2023 LBTT or LTT amendments affecting variation or surrender.)
10. What practical advice follows for advisers? (For surrenders: formalise by deed to obtain certainty on the SDLT effective date and consideration; calculate the tenant's CGT under TCGA 1992 s.22 with the Sch 8 wasting-asset depreciation if the residual term was ≤ 50 years; verify whether a reverse premium is being paid and confirm its SDLT treatment for both sides. For variations: classify the variation as trigger or non-trigger under the surrender-and-regrant doctrine; if trigger, apply the full new-lease SDLT analysis with para 9 overlap-relief; if non-trigger, apply paras 13 / 15A as appropriate; confirm whether the variation falls within a rent-review carve-out under para 13(2) + para 7(4A); cross-check HRAD applicability under Sch 4ZA if the variation involves residential dwellings. For deemed surrender: document the parties' intentions and the chronology to avoid disputes about the effective date.)
