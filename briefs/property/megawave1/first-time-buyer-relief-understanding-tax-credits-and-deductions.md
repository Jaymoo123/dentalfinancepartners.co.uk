The page covers six things competitor SERP coverage routinely under-serves: (a) the **terminology clarification** — UK SDLT does not have "tax credits" or "deductions" in the US §25 / Schedule A sense; FTB relief operates as a chargeable-rate reduction through Sch 6ZA Table A, applied at the moment of the SDLT return rather than as an annual income-tax credit; (b) the **conceptual mapping** — the 0% nil band on the first £300,000 of consideration is functionally equivalent to a "deduction" (the first £300,000 of price is not subject to SDLT); the 5% reduced rate on £300,001-£500,000 versus the standard 5% rate on £250,001-£925,000 is functionally equivalent to a "rate-reduction credit" on the £250,000-£300,000 portion (where standard residential rates would apply 2% on £125-£250k then 5% above, the FTB-relief structure folds that into a single 0% band up to £300k); (c) the **operational answer** — the relief is claimed on the SDLT return at completion via the conveyancer; not annual; not refundable; not applicable retrospectively to prior purchases; not a US-style credit carrying forward against future tax years; (d) the **US-buyer interaction** — US persons buying UK property as a first-time UK buyer can still claim UK FTB relief if eligible; UK FTB relief is not coordinated with US tax credits and creates no US-side implication; UK-US tax treaty does not engage with SDLT (SDLT is not a covered tax under the treaty); (e) the **what UK does have instead** — Lifetime ISA government bonus (deposit-side, not transaction-side; functionally similar to a partial "matching credit" on first-home deposits); Help to Buy ISA for legacy holders; FTB relief itself; council tax discounts for low-occupancy properties (annual, not transaction); none of these are "tax credits" in the IRS sense; (f) the **what UK does not have** — no annual mortgage interest deduction for owner-occupiers (since Schedule A in UK income tax does not include deductions of the US itemised-deduction type; the UK has not allowed mortgage interest relief on owner-occupier homes since MIRAS was withdrawn from April 2000); no first-time buyer income tax credit; no carry-forward homebuyer credit.

## Key questions this page must answer

1. Is the UK SDLT first-time buyer relief a "tax credit" or a "tax deduction"? (Neither in the US tax-code sense. UK SDLT FTB relief is a chargeable-rate reduction — it changes the rates and bands applied to the purchase price calculation, rather than crediting an amount against tax owed or deducting an amount from taxable income. The functional effect is to reduce SDLT payable at completion; operationally it sits on the SDLT return, not the annual income tax return.)
2. What is the closest US-tax-code analogue? (Closest analogue is a *transactional sales-tax exemption with a phased rate-reduction band*. The first £300,000 of consideration is fully exempt (0% rate) — analogous to a "deduction" on the first slice; the £300,001-£500,000 portion sits at a flat 5% rate compared with standard residential bands of 2% to 12% on similar consideration — analogous to a "credit" on the bands the standard rate would otherwise apply. There is no US-style refundable credit; no carry-forward; no annual claim cycle.)
3. How is the relief actually claimed? (On the SDLT return submitted by the buyer's conveyancer at completion, within 14 days of the effective date of the transaction. Relief code box on the return signals FTB-relief claim. No separate application form; no annual filing. The conveyancer is responsible for the claim mechanics; the buyer signs the SDLT return declaring eligibility.)
4. Is the relief refundable if I overpay SDLT and then learn I qualified? (Yes, by amendment to the SDLT return under FA 2003 Sch 10 para 6 within 12 months of the filing date, or by overpayment relief claim under Sch 10 para 34 within 4 years of the chargeable transaction date for the wider time limit. The relief is not a "refundable credit" in the US sense; it is recoverable through the standard SDLT amendment / overpayment relief route.)
5. Does claiming this relief affect my UK income tax position? (No. SDLT and income tax are entirely separate. Claiming FTB relief does not generate income, does not consume any income-tax allowance, does not affect the Personal Allowance, and does not need to be reported on the Self Assessment return.)
6. I am a US person buying UK property as my first UK home. Can I still claim UK FTB relief? (Yes, if you meet the Sch 6ZA eligibility tests. UK FTB relief eligibility does not depend on UK tax residence or UK domicile; it depends on the worldwide-property test under Sch 6ZA — you must not have previously acquired a major interest in a dwelling anywhere in the world. The relief is not coordinated with US §25 credits or any US first-time homebuyer architecture; the two systems do not interact.)
7. Does the UK have any annual "first-time buyer tax credit" similar to the IRS Form 5405 architecture from 2008-2010? (No. The US §25 first-time homebuyer credit expired in 2010 and has not been replicated in UK income tax. The closest UK programmes are: (a) Lifetime ISA, paying a 25% government bonus on contributions up to £4,000 per year, usable for first home purchase up to £450,000 (Savings (Government Contributions) Act 2017 — verify current parameters at gov.uk/lifetime-isa per §16.35); (b) Help to Buy ISA for legacy holders only (closed to new accounts from 30 November 2019; existing holders can claim 25% bonus until 30 November 2029).)
8. Are mortgage interest payments deductible against my UK income tax? (No, for owner-occupier homes. UK Mortgage Interest Relief at Source (MIRAS) was phased out fully by 6 April 2000. For buy-to-let landlords, mortgage interest is restricted to a 20% basic-rate-only tax credit under ITTOIA 2005 s.272A — finance cost restriction — which IS a UK income-tax credit, but it applies only to buy-to-let rental businesses, not to owner-occupied first homes. First-time owner-occupier buyers do not get any income-tax deduction or credit for mortgage interest.)
9. Are there any council tax credits or deductions for first-time buyers? (No specific FTB-targeted council tax discount exists. Council tax can be reduced by occupancy-related discounts — single-person 25%, second-property surcharge for owners, council tax reduction scheme for low-income — but none of these target first-time buyer status. Council tax is annual and unrelated to the SDLT FTB relief.)
10. What documentation should I keep to support the FTB-relief claim? (The conveyancer holds the primary SDLT-return file. Buyers should retain: (a) the SDLT5 submission receipt; (b) any declarations made about prior property ownership worldwide; (c) the completion statement showing the SDLT figure claimed; (d) the conveyancer's confirmation of the FTB-relief claim. HMRC may enquire under FA 2003 Sch 10 para 12 within 9 months of the filing date; keep documentation for at least 6 years.)
