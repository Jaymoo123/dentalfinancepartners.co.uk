# Is Increasing Council Tax Damaging the UK Housing Market? A Policy-and-Mechanics Analysis of the LGFA 1992 Framework, the Localism Act 2011 Referendum Cap, the LURA 2023 Second-Homes and Empty-Homes Premiums, and the Real Impact on Landlord Economics, Buyer Affordability and Second-Home Markets

## Statutory anchor

- Primary (the charging framework): **Local Government Finance Act 1992 (LGFA 1992) Part I** — the council-tax statute. s.30 governs the billing authority's setting of council-tax amounts under a multi-precepting structure (district / borough + county / Greater London Authority + police + fire authority precepts aggregated into a single bill). s.31A + s.31B govern the calculation of the basic amount of council tax. s.32-37 govern the calculation of substitute amounts and the precepting hierarchy. Verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/14/section/3 on 2026-05-26.
- Primary (the referendum-cap regime): **LGFA 1992 ss.52ZA-52ZY** (inserted by Localism Act 2011 Sch 5) — the council-tax referendum framework. Each year the Secretary of State sets a "principle" defining what constitutes an "excessive" council-tax increase; any LA proposing an increase exceeding the principle must hold a local referendum. The current principles (verify at Stage 2 against the most recent Council Tax (Excessive Increase) (England) Determination): typically 5% for top-tier authorities (county / unitary / London borough) — broken into a 3% general increase + a 2% adult-social-care precept; 3% for shire districts; capped sub-£5 or 2% for police; specific bespoke principles for fire authorities. **The 5% cap is NOT statutory — it is set by annual determination under the s.52ZB principle-setting power and the Secretary of State can change it.** Verify the principle currency at Stage 2.
- Primary (the LURA 2023 premiums — operative current position):
  - **LGFA 1992 s.11B (long-term empty dwellings premium)** as amended by LURA 2023 s.79 — premium on dwellings unoccupied + substantially unfurnished for a continuous period of 1 year (reduced from 2 years by LURA 2023). Maximum premium percentages: up to **100%** in the first 5 years of vacancy (so council tax = 200% of the standard band); up to **200%** in the 5-10 year band (council tax = 300%); up to **300%** for 10+ years (council tax = 400%). The 12-month trigger commenced **1 April 2024**. Verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/14/section/11B on 2026-05-26.
  - **LGFA 1992 s.11C (second-homes premium)** inserted by LURA 2023 s.80 in force from **26 October 2023** (insertion date) — billing authorities in England may apply a premium of up to **100%** on dwellings that are "unoccupied and substantially furnished" (the second-homes definition). Operative effect: each LA must determine the premium at least one year before the start of the financial year to which it applies, so the earliest practical commencement was **1 April 2025** for LAs that determined in 2024-25 financial year. Verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/14/section/11C on 2026-05-26.
- Supporting (revaluation history + ongoing policy debate): the **2003 Council Tax (Valuation Bands) (England) Order** revaluation was abandoned in 2007; the 1991 valuation reference date for England has now been in force for **35 years**. Wales completed its 2003 revaluation. Scotland and Northern Ireland operate different regimes. The 35-year-old valuation base creates well-documented distributional distortions (over-banding of pre-1991 properties that have not gained relative value; under-banding of newly-developed prime areas; postcode-by-postcode inequities). The case for revaluation is widely made by the IFS, the Resolution Foundation, and the LGA but **as of the page write date there is no commenced revaluation programme**. Stage 2 verifies.
- Supporting (CTR scheme — local discretion since 2013): the Council Tax Benefit (centrally-administered means-tested rebate) was abolished in 2013 by the Welfare Reform Act 2012 and replaced by Council Tax Reduction (CTR) schemes operated by each LA under LGFA 1992 Sch 1A + Sch 1B (inserted by the LGFA 2012). Each LA designs its own scheme; pensioner CTR is centrally-prescribed (the Council Tax Reduction Schemes (Prescribed Requirements) (England) Regulations 2012, SI 2012/2885); working-age CTR is locally-determined. **This is the principal mechanism for low-income relief from council-tax increases** — see C6 for the dedicated reduction-routes page.
- Supporting (broader-than-LGFA-1992 framework): the **Council Tax (Chargeable Dwellings) Order 1992 (SI 1992/549)** + **Council Tax (Liability for Owners) Regulations 1992 (SI 1992/551)** + **Council Tax (Exempt Dwellings) Order 1992 (SI 1992/558)** + **Council Tax (Reductions for Disabilities) Regulations 1992 (SI 1992/554)** form the SI-level architecture underpinning the LGFA 1992 framework. Page references these collectively rather than parsing each — they are detailed in the sister-pages.

## Key questions this page must answer

1. How much have council-tax bills risen and how does that compare to wages and house prices? (Stage 2 sources current ONS / LGA / IFS data: typical Band D council tax in England 2024-25 ≈ £2,170; year-on-year increase typically 5% (the cap); 10-year increase ≈ 60-80% cumulative against wage growth ≈ 30-40% and house-price growth ≈ 50% over the same period. Page presents headline numbers without partisanship.)
2. What is the referendum-cap regime and why is the cap typically 5%? (LGFA 1992 ss.52ZA-52ZY introduced by Localism Act 2011 Sch 5; the Secretary of State sets an annual "excessive increase" principle; LAs proposing increases above the principle must hold a local referendum. The 5% cap (3% general + 2% adult-social-care) is the typical top-tier principle and is set by annual determination, not by primary legislation. The Secretary of State can change the principle each year.)
3. What is the adult-social-care precept and why is it separate? (A restricted-use addition to the council-tax bill ring-fenced for adult social-care funding; the precept is layered on top of the general principle and the combined figure is typically 5%. Designed to address adult-social-care funding gaps without breaching the general 3% principle. Restricted-use revenue: cannot be repurposed to general LA services.)
4. What did LURA 2023 do to the empty-homes premium? (LURA 2023 s.79 in force 1 April 2024 reduced the s.11B empty-homes trigger from 2 years to 1 year. Premium of up to 100% in the first 5 years, 200% for 5-10 years, 300% for 10+ years (LA discretion within these maxima). Aggregate effect: a Band D dwelling left empty 1+ years could carry council tax of up to £4,340 instead of £2,170 — material impact on landlords with between-tenancy voids that extend past 12 months.)
5. What did LURA 2023 do to second homes? (LURA 2023 s.80 inserted LGFA 1992 s.11C in force 26 October 2023 enabling LAs to charge a second-homes premium of up to 100% on dwellings that are unoccupied and substantially furnished. Each LA must determine the premium at least one year before the financial year to which it applies — earliest practical commencement was 1 April 2025. Many LAs have determined adoption; coverage is patchy and Stage 2 lists representative LA-by-LA adoption position.)
6. What is the impact on landlord economics? (Three principal effects: (i) empty-homes premium materially increases void-period cost — 12-month trigger means a between-tenancy or renovation void over 12 months kicks in 100% premium; (ii) second-homes premium can apply to landlord-held property that is "substantially furnished and unoccupied" — overlap with holiday-let / serviced-accommodation models; (iii) general council-tax inflation eats into yield on lower-yield properties. Worked examples in Manager pre-decisions.)
7. What is the impact on buyer affordability? (Annual council tax is approximately 3-7% of mortgage interest on a £300k-£500k purchase at current rates. Rising council tax compresses the affordability calculation: stricter mortgage-affordability tests under PRA / FCA rules look at "total cost of ownership" including council tax; rising council tax reduces the maximum mortgage amount that fits the affordability test. Material at the margin for first-time buyers.)
8. What is the impact on second-home and holiday-let markets? (Material — LURA 2023 s.80 + s.11C premium combined with the FA 2024 abolition of FHL income-tax benefits + the increased SDLT additional-dwellings surcharge (FA 2025 c.8 s.51 — increased from 3% to 5% from 31 October 2024) constitutes a coordinated package of disincentives against the second-home / holiday-let model. Some operators have exited; others have switched to long-let. Page presents the disincentive package neutrally.)
9. What is the regional-distribution impact of rising council tax? (The 1991 valuation reference date — 35 years old as of the page write date — creates well-documented distortions. High-band properties in low-value postcodes (Northern industrial towns; parts of Wales) bear disproportionate weight relative to current market value; high-value-low-band properties in prime areas (parts of central London) under-bear. Rising council tax exacerbates the regional distortion in absolute-value terms.)
10. What policy options have been proposed? (IFS proportional-property-tax model; Resolution Foundation revaluation proposals; LGA full-funding model; Treasury options under successive Spending Reviews. Page surveys neutrally without advocacy. As of the page write date there is no commenced revaluation programme — every recent government has acknowledged the case but declined to act due to distributional implementation costs.)
11. Is increasing council tax actually damaging the housing market? (Balanced answer: it is one of many cost drivers; the second-homes premium and empty-homes premium are deliberately targeted at specific market segments and have been priced into market response (second-home transactions down ~20% in Cornwall + Lake District + Wales post-premium-adoption — Stage 2 verifies); the broader general-increase is one cost line among many (mortgage rates, energy costs, building-safety remediation costs); attributing causation cleanly to council tax alone is not statistically supported. The page presents the evidence without claiming a clean causal answer.)
12. What should a landlord, second-homeowner or buyer do in response? (Three practical responses: (i) factor the empty-homes + second-homes premiums into acquisition models and held-vacancy budgets; (ii) explore CTR-scheme eligibility for cash-flow-constrained owners; (iii) consider planning-side options where the second-homes premium materially erodes yield — long-let alternative; rural-business-rates alternative for substantial commercial use; relief / exemption applications. Cross-link to C6 reduction-routes page for the operational mechanics.)
