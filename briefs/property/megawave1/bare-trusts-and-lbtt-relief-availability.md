# Bare Trusts and LBTT: How Relief Availability Flows Through the Trust to the Beneficiary

## Statutory anchor
- Primary: Land and Buildings Transaction Tax (Scotland) Act 2013 asp 11 ("LBTT(S)A 2013"), Schedule 18 Part 3 "Bare trusts" - verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/asp/2013/11/schedule/18 on 2026-05-26. Sch 18 is introduced by LBTT(S)A 2013 s.50 and covers all trust treatment under LBTT. Part 3 (paras 5-9) handles bare-trust acquisitions: para 5 treats the bare-trustee's acquisition of a chargeable interest "as if made by the person or persons for whom the trustee is trustee" (the look-through principle - the beneficiary is the chargeable person); para 6 makes the trustee jointly liable for the tax (tax recoverable from trustee); para 7 carves out lease grants from the look-through (different mechanics for leases granted by or to a bare trustee); paras 8-9 set out the carve-out detail. Sch 18 Part 6 (paras 19-21) defines "bare trust", "absolutely entitled", and "settlement" - the statutory definitions that determine whether Part 3 applies at all.
- Supporting: LBTT(S)A 2013 Sch 4A (First-Time Buyer Relief, verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/asp/2013/11/schedule/4A 2026-05-26 - relevant because Sch 4A applies through the bare trust to the beneficiary, so the beneficiary's never-previously-owned-a-dwelling status determines FTB eligibility, not the trustee's); Sch 2A (Additional Dwelling Supplement framework - ADS conditions tested on the beneficiary's worldwide dwelling-ownership status, not the trustee's, where the bare-trust look-through applies); Sch 5 (MDR - retained in Scotland per §23.6); Sch 7A (Reliefs framework); Sch 10 (group relief); Sch 10A (sub-sale development relief); Sch 11 (reconstruction and acquisition reliefs - relevant for corporate-restructure cases that route through bare trusts); Sch 13 (charities relief); Sch 17 (partnership relief). For settlement (discretionary trust) cases that are NOT bare trusts, Sch 18 Part 4 (paras 10-14) governs and the trustee is the chargeable person on a separate footing.
- House position reference: §23.4 main LBTT residential rates (the buyer-facing rate table the beneficiary will be charged on); §23.4 FTB-relief subsection (£175,000 nil-band uplift, £600 maximum saving, beneficiary's worldwide-previous-ownership status determines eligibility); §23.5 ADS (8% on the entire purchase price from 5 December 2024; the £40,000 de-minimis; joint-buyer aggregation rule mirrored in trust contexts where multiple beneficiaries are involved; ADS test runs on the beneficiary's worldwide dwelling-ownership at the effective date); §23.6 MDR retained in Scotland (relevant for bare-trust-into-portfolio cases); §23.10 citations block.

## Key questions this page must answer

1. What is a bare trust in Scottish property law? (Plain-English definition per Sch 18 para 19: a trust under which property is held by a trustee for one or more persons "absolutely entitled" against the trustee. The beneficiary has the unencumbered beneficial interest; the trustee holds bare legal title and must act on the beneficiary's directions. Common examples: nominee arrangements, parent-holding-for-minor-child, declaration-of-trust arrangements between unmarried co-owners.)
2. Who is the chargeable person for LBTT - the bare trustee or the beneficiary? (Sch 18 para 5 makes the bare trustee's acquisition treated "as if made by the person or persons for whom the trustee is trustee" - the look-through principle. Practically: LBTT is calculated by reference to the beneficiary's circumstances, status, and any reliefs they claim. The trustee is named on the return but the substantive tax position is the beneficiary's.)
3. Who actually pays the LBTT? (Sch 18 para 6 makes the trustee liable for payment, but the tax is recoverable from the trustee (with rights of indemnity against trust assets / the beneficiary). In practice, the funds usually come from the beneficiary either via the trust or directly to the solicitor; the trustee's joint liability is the statutory backstop.)
4. Can the beneficiary claim First-Time Buyer relief? (YES, if the beneficiary qualifies. Sch 4A applies through the bare trust: the FTB test (never previously owned a dwelling anywhere in the world; intent to occupy as only or main residence) is tested on the beneficiary, not the trustee. A trustee can hold for a beneficiary who is a first-time buyer, and the beneficiary's FTB status governs. Critical edge case: where the trustee holds for multiple beneficiaries (e.g. two siblings), all beneficiaries must qualify or relief is lost on the whole transaction, mirroring the standard joint-buyer rule.)
5. Will the Additional Dwelling Supplement (ADS) apply? (Tested on the beneficiary's worldwide dwelling-ownership at the effective date per Sch 2A read with Sch 18 Part 3. If the beneficiary owns another dwelling anywhere in the world, ADS at 8% applies. Trustee's other dwelling-ownership is irrelevant unless the trustee is also a beneficiary on a separate trust. Edge case: parent as bare trustee for child where parent also owns the family home - parent's dwelling-ownership does NOT count because parent is acting as trustee, not as buyer.)
6. Can MDR be claimed where a bare trustee buys 2 or more dwellings? (YES - MDR remains available in Scotland (per §23.6; contrast SDLT MDR abolition 1 June 2024). The MDR claim runs on the beneficiary's circumstances. Where multiple beneficiaries hold under one trust acquiring multiple dwellings, MDR applies to the linked transaction the same way it would for direct multi-buyer purchases.)
7. What about acquisition relief, group relief, or partnership relief? (All standard LBTT reliefs (Sch 10 group, Sch 10A sub-sale development, Sch 11 reconstruction and acquisition, Sch 17 partnership) flow through the bare trust to the beneficiary, with the relief tests applied to the beneficiary's situation. The corporate-restructuring page covers the group-and-acquisition relief mechanics in depth; cross-link.)
8. What happens with leases? (Sch 18 para 7 excludes lease grants from the look-through in paras 5-6; paras 8-9 set out separate mechanics. The bare trustee granting or being granted a lease is treated as the chargeable person in some lease-specific situations. Stage 2 must verify the para 8-9 mechanics for the granted-by and granted-to cases.)
9. What evidence does Revenue Scotland want to see? (Declaration of trust in writing; signed-and-dated; identifying the bare trustee, the beneficiary, the property, and the absolute beneficial interest. Where the bare trust is by operation of law (e.g. resulting trust on a transfer with no consideration paid by the recorded title-holder), additional documentary evidence may be required. Stage 2 verifies Revenue Scotland's documentary expectations.)
10. What if the trust turns out to be a discretionary trust, not a bare trust? (Different mechanics - Sch 18 Part 4 (paras 10-14) treats trustee acquisitions of discretionary-trust property as whole-interest purchases by the trustee, with the trustee as the chargeable person and tested on the trustee's circumstances. ADS / FTB tests run on the trustee, not the discretionary beneficiaries. This is a critical pre-acquisition planning gate: characterise the trust correctly before the transaction.)
11. Could HMRC or Revenue Scotland challenge the trust characterisation? (Yes - look at the practical substance, not just the document. If the "bare trustee" exercises discretion over the property, or if the "beneficiary" lacks the right to call for the property, the trust may be re-characterised as a settlement under Part 4. The look-through then fails and the trustee becomes the chargeable person with potentially much worse relief outcomes. Lead-gen handoff: trust characterisation due-diligence is high-value work.)
