# A Complete Guide to Stamp Duty Reliefs for Probate Properties: Exemptions, Reliefs and the Sch 4ZA Inherited-Interest Carve-Out

## Statutory anchor
- Primary (exemption — assents and distributions): FA 2003 Schedule 3 paragraph 3A (transactions in connection with divorce / dissolution / personal-representative administration — the "no chargeable consideration" deeming for genuine assents to beneficiaries entitled under will or intestacy). Stage 2 verify exact paragraph at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2003/14/schedule/3 on write — historical practice cites both Sch 3 para 3A (administration of estate) and the general "no chargeable consideration" architecture under FA 2003 s.49. **Stage 1b drift watchpoint: existing site page cites s.43 / Sch 4 mechanics; A6 must align with the same statutory architecture to avoid intra-site contradiction.**
- Primary (exemption — deed of variation): FA 2003 Schedule 4 paragraph 4 (deeds of variation within 2 years of death — IHTA 1984 s.142 read-across). Cross-link to the existing site page which covers the mechanics.
- Primary (relief — PR property-trader): FA 2003 Schedule 6A (relief for certain acquisitions of dwellings) — paragraph 3 covers acquisition by a property trader from the personal representatives of a deceased person (PR-buyback for resale; relief from full SDLT subject to onward sale within 3 years and other conditions). Stage 2 verify at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2003/14/schedule/6A on write.
- Primary (additional dwellings surcharge carve-out): FA 2003 Schedule 4ZA paragraph 16 (inherited interest with ≤50% share AND ≤£40,000 value AND inherited within 3 years before the new purchase — excluded from counting as an "owned" property for the purposes of the additional dwellings surcharge under Sch 4ZA para 3(6)). Stage 2 verify the exact triple-condition (50% / £40k / 3-year window) at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2003/14/schedule/4ZA on write.
- Supporting: FA 2003 s.43 (chargeable consideration — load-bearing for beneficiary buy-out scenarios where one beneficiary acquires others' shares for equality money); FA 2003 Schedule 16 (trustees and PRs); IHTA 1984 s.142 (deed-of-variation read-across); IHTA 1984 s.49 (life interest trusts — interaction with probate-vesting where life tenant exists).
- House position reference: §1 main text (SDLT rates + 5% additional dwellings surcharge from 31 October 2024 — the rate-architecture context for surcharge-on-probate-acquisition scenarios); §1.D Wave 7 lock (Sch 3 spouse/civil-partner exempt transfers — analogous architecture, useful cross-reference for the "no chargeable consideration" deeming mechanic). ## Framing differentiator (anti-templating, anti-cannibalisation)

This page is the **reliefs-and-exemptions companion** to the existing site page `sdlt-on-probate-property-transfers` (which covers the **five transfer-type taxonomy** — assents, deeds of variation, transfers in discharge of legacies, sales by PRs, beneficiary buy-outs — with the additional dwellings surcharge interaction and the Sch 6A property-trader relief). This A6 page sits in a **complementary lane**: where the existing page is **transfer-type-led** (when does SDLT apply at all?), this A6 page is **relief-led** (when does SDLT apply but a relief reduces or eliminates it; when does an exemption deem no chargeable consideration; how does the Sch 4ZA inherited-interest carve-out work for someone inheriting a property and then buying their main residence soon after?). The page covers six relief / exemption / carve-out architectures:

1. **Sch 3 para 3A administration-of-estate exemption** — the "no chargeable consideration" deeming for genuine assents to beneficiaries entitled under will or intestacy. Already covered in existing site page; this A6 page provides the statutory-architecture cross-reference and any post-2023 enquiry-practice updates.
2. **Sch 4 para 4 deed-of-variation exemption** — within 2 years of death; IHTA 1984 s.142 read-across; not a "relief" in the technical sense but treated as no chargeable consideration. Cross-link to existing site page.
3. **Sch 6A property-trader / PR-buyback relief** — narrow relief for genuine property-trader buying the deceased's home from PRs for onward sale; conditions including the 3-year onward-sale requirement, the price-cap relative to market value, the "in connection with the deceased's job-related residential transfer" original-Sch-6A scenario (relocation / job-related transfer relief was the original 2003 Sch 6A scope; PR-buyback is one of several scenarios within Sch 6A — verify the para 3 / para 4 / etc allocation at write).
4. **Sch 4ZA para 16 inherited-interest carve-out** — the **operationally most important relief in this cluster**. Someone inheriting a property and then buying their main residence within 3 years is exposed to the 5% additional dwellings surcharge on the new main residence unless the inherited interest qualifies for the para 16 carve-out (≤50% share AND ≤£40k value AND inherited within 3 years). Failure cases: inheriting a 100% interest (excluded — share >50%); inheriting a high-value share (excluded — value >£40k); inheriting >3 years before the new purchase (excluded — outside the temporal window). The carve-out is narrower than commonly understood; many newly-inherited buyers find themselves paying the 5% surcharge they assumed would be relieved.
5. **Beneficiary buy-out chargeable-consideration analysis** — where one beneficiary acquires another's share of the inherited property by paying equality money or assuming the other's share of debt, the chargeable consideration for SDLT is the equality money + debt-assumption (not the property value). Common pattern in sibling buy-outs of inherited family homes; the calculation drives whether SDLT is payable at all and whether the surcharge applies.
6. **PR-as-non-natural-person Sch 4A interaction** — where the deceased's PRs hold property and a corporate or non-natural-person acquirer takes it, the Sch 4A 15% flat rate may apply unless within the genuine-property-investment carve-out. Narrow edge-case but material for HNW estate planning.
