# LBTT Review in Scotland: Where the Tax Stands for 2026/27 After Four ADS Upratings and the SDLT MDR Abolition

## Statutory anchor
- Primary: Land and Buildings Transaction Tax (Scotland) Act 2013 asp 11 ("LBTT(S)A 2013"), enacted 31 July 2013, in force from 1 April 2015 (commencement of devolved property-transfer tax following Scotland Act 2012 Part 3) - verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/asp/2013/11/contents on 2026-05-26. Key sections: s.10 (effective date - substantial-performance test); s.24 (residential rate-setting power, used annually by Scottish Statutory Instrument); s.29 (return + 30-day filing window); s.59(8) (six-or-more-dwellings non-residential treatment). Key schedules: Sch 2A (ADS framework); Sch 4A (First-Time Buyer Relief); Sch 5 (MDR - retained in Scotland); Sch 10 (group relief); Sch 10A (sub-sale development relief); Sch 11 (reconstruction and acquisition reliefs); Sch 13 (charities relief); Sch 17 (partnership relief); Sch 18 (trusts).
- Supporting: LBTT (Amendment) (Scotland) Act 2016 (ADS introduction at 3% from 1 April 2016); Coronavirus (Scotland) (No.2) Act 2020 (ADS repayment window extended from 18 to 36 months, made permanent); Scottish Budget 2025/26 announcement of 4 December 2024 raising ADS from 6% to 8% for contracts entered into on or after 5 December 2024 (verified at https://revenue.scot/news-publications/news/scottish-budget-2025-2026-changes-land-buildings on 2026-05-26); Scottish Budget 2026/27 confirming no further changes to LBTT rates or bands (verified at https://www.gov.scot/publications/scottish-budget-2026-2027/pages/4/).
- House position reference: §23.4 main LBTT residential rates 2026/27 (rates frozen: 0%/2%/5%/10%/12% with band thresholds at £145k/£250k/£325k/£750k; Scottish FTB relief £175k nil-band uplift, £600 maximum saving, no upper property-value cap); §23.5 ADS 8% from 5 December 2024 with full rate history (3% from 1 April 2016, 4% from 25 January 2019, 6% from 16 December 2022, 8% from 5 December 2024); §23.6 LBTT MDR retained in Scotland, contrast SDLT MDR abolition 1 June 2024 (F(No.2)A 2024 s.7); §23.6 non-residential bands 0%/1%/5%; §23.8 cross-jurisdictional comparison table (the headline cross-border position); §23.9 Property Tax Partners angle (cross-border portfolio planning, ADS-as-the-highest-UK-surcharge); §23.10 citations; §23.11 do-not-write list (ADS 6%, ADS 4%, ADS 18-month window, "MDR abolished UK-wide", "Wales has FTB relief").

## Key questions this page must answer

1. What is LBTT and when did it start? (Devolved Scottish property-transfer tax that replaced SDLT in Scotland on 1 April 2015 under the Scotland Act 2012 Part 3 devolution. Administered by Revenue Scotland. Statutory basis: LBTT(S)A 2013. The first wholly-devolved Scottish tax administered by a Scottish body.)
2. What are the current LBTT residential rates for 2026/27? (Table: 0% to £145k, 2% to £250k, 5% to £325k, 10% to £750k, 12% above. Confirmed frozen for 2026/27 by Scottish Budget. Scottish FTB relief: £175k nil-band uplift, £600 maximum saving, no value cap.)
3. What has changed in the 11 years since LBTT launched? (Chronological history: 1 April 2015 LBTT replaces SDLT; 1 April 2016 ADS introduced at 3%; 30 June 2018 Scottish FTB relief introduced (Sch 4A); 25 January 2019 ADS raised to 4%; 18-to-36 month ADS repayment window extension (Coronavirus (Scotland) (No.2) Act 2020, made permanent); 16 December 2022 ADS raised to 6%; 5 December 2024 ADS raised to 8%; LTT MDR modified by 2025 and 2026 Welsh regulations; SDLT MDR abolished 1 June 2024.)
4. Why has ADS risen so sharply? (Policy arc - Scottish Government revenue-raising posture combined with housing-policy pressure on second-home concentration in tourist areas (Highlands, Edinburgh fringe, Skye). Each uprating cited revenue-and-housing-policy dual rationale in the Scottish Budget documentation. The 8% rate is now the highest dwelling-surcharge rate in the UK by a material margin - SDLT 5% surcharge and Welsh higher-rate band structure are both lower in equivalent-band comparison.)
5. What about MDR - did Scotland keep it when England abolished? (YES. SDLT MDR abolished for transactions with effective dates on or after 1 June 2024 (Finance (No.2) Act 2024 s.7). LBTT MDR retained without modification under LBTT(S)A 2013 Sch 5. This creates a cross-border planning angle: portfolio acquisitions in Scotland can still claim MDR; equivalent acquisitions in England cannot. Welsh LTT MDR retained-but-modified (subsidiary-dwelling carve-out from 7 February 2025; minimum rate raised to 3% from 13 February 2026). The three-nation MDR landscape is now genuinely divergent.)
6. What is the cross-jurisdictional comparison? (England + NI SDLT vs Wales LTT vs Scotland LBTT in headline form: nil bands £125k / £225k / £145k; additional-dwellings surcharge 5% flat / band-structure 5%-17% / 8% on entire price; non-resident surcharge 2% / none / none; FTB relief £300k nil to £500k cap / none / £175k nil-band uplift no cap; MDR abolished / retained-modified / retained-unmodified; corporate flat 15% (Sch 4A FA 2003) / none / none. The contrasts matter for cross-border landlords and investors.)
7. Has the policy direction stabilised, or is more change coming? (Policy outlook: Scottish Fiscal Commission and Scottish Government budget documentation suggest LBTT rate stability for the medium term, with ADS as the active policy lever rather than main rates. Watch points: (a) any signal in successive Scottish Budgets of further ADS uprating; (b) any reaction to UK-wide reform of the SDLT replacement; (c) housing-policy interaction with the Visitor Levy (Scotland) Act 2024 and the Short-Term Lets Licensing regime. Stage 2 must surface the current Scottish Fiscal Commission LBTT forecast at write time.)
8. What does this mean for landlords and property investors in Scotland? (Practical implications: (a) 8% ADS on every additional-property purchase, on the entire price, no marginal-relief, is now the dominant tax variable for Scottish BTL; (b) the 36-month replacement-of-main-residence window mitigates the ADS hit on genuine main-residence moves; (c) MDR retention preserves portfolio acquisition relief routes that have closed in England; (d) Scottish FTB relief at £175k nil-band uplift with no value cap is still operationally useful for owner-occupier buyers.)
9. What does this mean for the four-nation cross-border property investor? (The arbitrage angle: Scottish-LBTT portfolio acquisitions still benefit from MDR; English-SDLT portfolio acquisitions must use s.116(7) six-dwellings non-residential or Sch 15 partnership routes; Welsh-LTT acquisitions face the modified MDR with 3% minimum rate. The choice of jurisdiction for a portfolio investor is now a tax-driven decision, not just an asset-quality decision.)
10. What about the LBTT mechanics that did not change? (Stable mechanics: 30-day return window (LBTT(S)A 2013 s.29); £40,000 ADS de-minimis (Sch 2A); six-dwellings automatic non-residential treatment (s.59(8)); group relief (Sch 10); acquisition-and-reconstruction relief (Sch 11); partnership relief (Sch 17). The Scottish framework remains structurally aligned with the FA 2003 SDLT framework even as headline rates diverge.)
11. What questions should LBTT raise for the next Scottish parliament? (Forward-looking policy: should the nil-rate band rise to match Welsh £225k; should the 8% ADS be marginal-rate rather than flat-on-entire-price; should a non-resident surcharge be considered as it has in England; should FTB relief be extended above the £175k nil-band uplift. The page is editorial here - signpost the questions without advocating a position.)
