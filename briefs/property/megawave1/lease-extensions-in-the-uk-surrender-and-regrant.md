# Lease Extensions in the UK: How the Surrender-and-Regrant Doctrine Works at Law and Why It Drives the SDLT, LBTT and LTT Treatment of Every Lease Extension

## Statutory anchor

- Primary (the deemed-surrender-and-regrant principle for SDLT — the operative paragraph framework): FA 2003 c. 14 Schedule 17A "Further provisions relating to leases". Three paragraphs in Sch 17A operate together to produce the surrender-and-regrant analytical framework for SDLT purposes:
  - **Para 9** "Rent for overlap period in case of grant of further lease" — verified verbatim at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2003/14/schedule/17A on 2026-05-26. Para 9(1) sets out the situations to which the overlap-relief applies: (a) a direct surrender of an existing lease and grant of a new lease over the same or substantially the same premises between the same parties; (b) a new lease granted on the exercise of statutory rights under the landlord-tenant legislation (LRHUDA 1993 individual extension under Chapter II falls here; commercial security-of-tenure renewals under Part 2 of the Landlord and Tenant Act 1954 fall here); (c) a new lease granted in court-ordered relief from forfeiture; (d) a new lease granted pursuant to a guarantee. Para 9(2): "For the purposes of this Part the rent payable under the new lease in respect of any period falling within the overlap period is treated as reduced by the amount of the rent that would have been payable in respect of that period under the old lease." Para 9(3) defines the overlap period: the period running from the new-lease grant date to what would have been the original-lease termination date. The overlap-relief credit prevents double-counting of rent NPV consideration during the period when both leases would have run in parallel.
  - **Para 13** "Increase of rent treated as grant of new lease; variation of lease" — verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2003/14/schedule/17A on 2026-05-26. Para 13(1): a variation that increases rent as from a date before the end of the fifth year of the lease term is treated as the grant of a new lease in consideration of the additional rent. Para 13(2) carves out increases pursuant to a provision contained in the lease (rent-review clauses) or under para 7(4A) (turnover rent or RPI uplifts pre-mapped into the lease).
  - **Para 15A** "Reduction of rent or term" — verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2003/14/schedule/17A on 2026-05-26. Para 15A(1): a variation that reduces rent is treated as the acquisition of a chargeable interest by the lessee. Para 15A(1A): any other variation supported by money or money's worth consideration (other than rent variation or term variation) is treated as the acquisition of a chargeable interest by the lessee. Para 15A(2): a variation that reduces the term is treated as the acquisition of a chargeable interest by the lessor.
  - **Para 14 (REPEALED)** — formerly "Increase of rent treated as grant of new lease — abnormal increase after fifth year"; omitted by Finance Act 2013 Schedule 41 paragraph 7(1) with effect in accordance with Sch 41 para 8(5) (verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2003/14/schedule/17A on 2026-05-26 — the legislation.gov.uk page records the omission note). The repeal is significant: post-FA 2013, abnormal rent increases after the fifth year do not trigger the deemed-new-lease analysis; only para 13's first-five-years rule applies. **Stage 1b drift watchpoint:** any competitor page or HMRC manual cross-reference predating FA 2013 must NOT be cited without confirming the post-2013 position.
- Primary (the surrender-and-regrant principle at general law — the doctrine that triggers the framework above): the common-law rule that any variation extending the term of a lease, or otherwise altering its essential characteristics, operates as the surrender of the existing lease and the grant of a new lease — see Friends Provident Life Office v British Railways Board [1996] 1 All ER 336 (Court of Appeal); Jenkin R Lewis & Son Ltd v Kerman [1971] Ch 477 (Court of Appeal). The SDLT framework above gives statutory recognition to this common-law analysis: a lease extension is treated for SDLT purposes as a new chargeable land transaction under FA 2003 s.43 (the new lease being the chargeable transaction; the existing lease being deemed surrendered). The chargeable consideration is the premium paid plus the NPV of rent under the new lease, with the para 9 overlap-relief reducing the NPV of rent during the overlap period. Stage 2 must verify the most current Court of Appeal authority on the surrender-and-regrant doctrine — the principle is stable but the case-law on what constitutes an essential alteration sufficient to trigger the doctrine has evolved (covenant additions, demised-premises additions, term extension all clearly trigger; rent-only variations within rent-review clauses clearly do not; the boundary cases turn on whether the alteration is "fundamental" to the lease).
- Primary (corresponding LBTT framework): LBTT(S)A 2013 c. 11 (asp) Schedule 19 "Leases" — Scottish Parliament Act. Schedule 19 paragraphs 13-14 contain the equivalent variation provisions; the surrender-and-regrant analysis carries across. Verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/asp/2013/11/schedule/19 on 2026-05-26 (cross-referenced via the M1-A-B2 archer-uk-limited brief seed). The Scottish FTT decision in Archer UK Limited v Revenue Scotland (M1-A-B2 seed; FTT citation Stage 2 verifies) addresses a transitional fact-pattern at the SDLT-to-LBTT cutover; the underlying surrender-and-regrant doctrine analysis is the same on both sides of the border.
- Primary (corresponding LTT framework): Land Transaction Tax and Anti-avoidance of Devolved Taxes (Wales) Act 2017 (anaw 1) (LTTA 2017) Schedule 6 "Leases". Schedule 6 paragraphs 19-22 contain the Welsh equivalent variation provisions. Verified by Stage 2 against https://www.legislation.gov.uk/anaw/2017/1/schedule/6 (Stage 2 to confirm paragraph numbering at write time — schedule paragraphs in the LTTA were renumbered in early commencement amendments). The surrender-and-regrant doctrine applies equally; the LTT variation framework parallels FA 2003 Sch 17A with Welsh-specific rate and threshold differences.
- Supporting (the new lease as chargeable land transaction): FA 2003 s.43 "Land transactions" — every grant of a new lease is a chargeable land transaction, including a deemed grant arising under the surrender-and-regrant doctrine. Verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2003/14/section/43 on 2026-05-26. FA 2003 s.49 (general rules on consideration); FA 2003 Sch 5 (NPV of rent calculation for leases); FA 2003 Sch 4ZA (5% HRAD surcharge on additional residential dwellings — applies to premium on extension where the leaseholder already owns another residential property — §1.I Wave 9 mini-lock).
- Supporting (statutory lease extensions trigger the para 9 overlap relief explicitly): LRHUDA 1993 Chapter II individual lease extension for flats and LRA 1967 ss.14-15 extension for houses both produce a new lease in substitution for the existing lease; the new lease is a chargeable land transaction; the para 9 overlap-relief applies to prevent double-counting of rent during the overlap period.
- House position reference: §1 main text (SDLT general); §1.I Wave 9 mini-lock (5% HRAD surcharge — applies to premium on extensions where the leaseholder already owns another residential property); §1.J Wave 9 mini-lock (residential-vs-mixed-use line — relevant if the extending lease is mixed-use, although mixed-use extensions usually arise in commercial context). **NEW HP-LOCK CANDIDATE proposed for Stage 1b: §1.L "Lease variation surrender-and-regrant doctrine — FA 2003 Sch 17A paras 9 + 13 + 15A + LBTT(S)A 2013 Sch 19 + LTTA 2017 Sch 6"** — this seed proposes the same §1.L lock raised in the F-101 flag from C13. ## Key questions this page must answer

1. What is the surrender-and-regrant doctrine and where does it come from? (Common-law principle that any variation extending the term of a lease, or otherwise fundamentally altering its character, operates as the surrender of the existing lease and the grant of a new lease in substitution. Originates in the property-law jurisprudence — Friends Provident Life Office v British Railways Board [1996] 1 All ER 336; Jenkin R Lewis & Son Ltd v Kerman [1971] Ch 477. Property-law analysis: the parties are deemed at law to have done what they would have had to do to achieve the same economic outcome, namely surrender and regrant. The doctrine pre-dates the SDLT framework by decades and operates as background law.)
2. What is the SDLT statutory framework for variations? (FA 2003 Sch 17A paras 9, 13, 15A operate together. Para 9: overlap-relief credit on rent NPV during the overlap period (new-lease grant date to original-lease termination date). Para 13: rent increase before the fifth-year point is treated as the grant of a new lease in consideration of the additional rent. Para 15A: reduction of rent or term, or other consideration for variation, is treated as the acquisition of a chargeable interest by the lessee (or lessor for term reduction). Para 14 — "abnormal increase after the fifth year" — was REPEALED by FA 2013 Sch 41 para 7(1).)
3. How does the doctrine apply to a statutory lease extension under LRHUDA 1993 Chapter II? (The new lease granted on a statutory extension is a chargeable land transaction under FA 2003 s.43. The chargeable consideration is the premium plus NPV of rent. Statutory extensions are typically at a peppercorn rent producing nil NPV; the premium is the SDLT base. Para 9 overlap-relief mechanically applies, but because peppercorn rent under the new lease equals nil NPV the credit reduces nil — operationally invisible. The 5% HRAD surcharge under Sch 4ZA applies on premiums ≥ £40,000 where the leaseholder already owns another residential property.)
4. How does the doctrine apply to a statutory lease extension under LRA 1967 (houses)? (LRA 1967 extension is at a modern ground rent rather than peppercorn — see C13 batch-mate seed for the strategic-choice context. The new lease is a chargeable transaction; chargeable consideration is the premium plus NPV of the modern ground rent; the para 9 overlap-relief credits the rent that would have been payable under the old lease for the overlap period. Because LRA 1967 extension at modern ground rent has a meaningful NPV-of-rent component, the overlap-relief credit is more operationally significant than under LRHUDA 1993 peppercorn extensions.)
5. How does the doctrine apply to a voluntary (non-statutory) lease extension? (Same general analysis: the variation operates at common law as surrender and regrant; the new lease is a chargeable land transaction; chargeable consideration is the premium plus NPV of rent under the new lease; the para 9 overlap-relief applies. The leaseholder cannot rely on the LFRA 2024 peppercorn-rent presumption or the statutory premium formula — the parties negotiate freely — but the SDLT analysis is identical.)
6. Does the doctrine apply to a rent-only variation? (No, in the typical case. A variation that only adjusts rent — typically pursuant to a rent-review clause already in the lease — does not at common law amount to surrender and regrant; the lease continues. Para 13 separately catches rent increases in the first five years of the term as deemed new leases for SDLT purposes (but not the underlying lease at common law). Rent-review-clause increases are carved out of para 13 by para 13(2) cross-referencing para 7(4A).)
7. What is the boundary between an alteration that triggers the doctrine and one that does not? (Term extension: TRIGGERS (Friends Provident). Addition of demised premises: TRIGGERS. Substantial covenant restructuring: typically TRIGGERS (depends on whether the change is "fundamental"). Pure rent variation: does NOT trigger at common law (although caught by paras 13 / 15A separately for SDLT purposes). Pure covenant variation that does not affect the term: typically does NOT trigger but may be caught by para 15A(1A) if supported by money consideration. Stage 2 verifies current case-law: there is ongoing case-law evolution on what counts as "fundamental".)
8. What is the para 9 overlap-relief calculation mechanic? (Step 1: identify the overlap period — runs from new-lease grant date to what would have been original-lease termination date. Step 2: identify the rent that would have been payable under the old lease for each period within the overlap. Step 3: identify the rent payable under the new lease for the same periods. Step 4: treat the new-lease rent for the overlap period as reduced by the old-lease rent for that period. Step 5: feed the reduced rent into the FA 2003 Sch 5 NPV-of-rent calculation. Worked example at Stage 2: 25-year original lease with 5 years remaining (rent £30k pa), extended by 99 years (new rent £35k pa); overlap period = 5 years; new-lease rent for overlap reduced from £35k to £5k pa for SDLT NPV purposes; post-overlap rent (years 6-99) = £35k pa unreduced.)
9. How do LBTT and LTT treat the same fact pattern? (LBTT(S)A 2013 Sch 19 paras 13-14 contain the equivalent variation provisions; the surrender-and-regrant analysis carries across. LTTA 2017 Sch 6 paras 19-22 contain the Welsh equivalent. Cross-jurisdictional transitional issues — e.g. a pre-1-April-2015 SDLT lease varied post-LBTT-commencement in Scotland — are addressed by the M1-A-B2 Archer brief and the broader transitional framework under LBTT(S)A 2013 Sch 19 para 30.)
10. What practical advice follows for advisers? (Always verify whether a proposed lease variation triggers surrender-and-regrant — if yes, the new lease is a chargeable land transaction and SDLT must be calculated on the premium plus rent NPV with para 9 overlap-relief; if no, the variation may be caught separately by para 13 or para 15A but does not produce a new chargeable lease. Always check the statutory residual-term clock: a lease coming up to a statutory extension or commercial renewal should be scoped for SDLT impact on the renewal; statutory renewals are within para 9. Always cross-check HRAD applicability under Sch 4ZA on premiums where the leaseholder owns another residential property.)
