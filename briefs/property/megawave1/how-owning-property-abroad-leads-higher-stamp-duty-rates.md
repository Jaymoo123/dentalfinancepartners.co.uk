# How Owning Property Abroad Leads to Higher SDLT Rates: The 5% Additional Dwellings Surcharge, the 2% Non-Resident Surcharge, and the Loss of First-Time Buyer Relief

## Statutory anchor
- Primary (5% additional dwellings surcharge worldwide-property reach): FA 2003 Schedule 4ZA "Higher rates for additional dwellings" — para 2 + para 6 + para 7. Para 2 sets the higher-rate threshold; paras 6-7 set the "Conditions A-D" for an individual purchaser, including the worldwide-property test (the individual is treated as having a "major interest" in a "dwelling" if the major interest is in any dwelling anywhere in the world, with value over £40,000 — see para 17 for the £40,000 exclusion). The surcharge rate increased from 3% to 5% with effect from 31 October 2024 (FA 2025 c. 8 s.51). Verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2003/14/schedule/4ZA on 2026-05-26 (page shows "up to date with all changes known to be in force on or before 26 May 2026").
- Primary (2% non-resident SDLT surcharge): FA 2003 Schedule 9A "Higher rates for non-resident transactions" — inserted by FA 2021 s.86, in force from 1 April 2021. Adds 2% to the rates otherwise payable (residential or higher-rate residential) where the buyer is not UK-resident under the SDLT residence test in Sch 9A para 4 (individual: 183 days in any 365-day period spanning the effective date). Verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2003/14/schedule/9A on 2026-05-26.
- Primary (FTB relief worldwide-property test): FA 2003 Schedule 6ZA — para 6(1)(b) requires the purchaser to be a first-time buyer, defined in para 6(2) as "an individual who has not previously been a purchaser in relation to a land transaction the main subject-matter of which was a major interest in a dwelling situated anywhere in the world". Worldwide-property ownership therefore disqualifies FTB relief entirely. Verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2003/14/schedule/6ZA on 2026-05-26.
- Primary (rate consequence): FA 2003 s.55 Table A (current residential rates from 1 April 2025); FA 2003 s.55B (the additional-dwellings higher-rate computation); FA 2003 s.55C (the non-resident higher-rate computation — added by FA 2021 Sch 16).
- House position reference: §1.I Wave 9 lock (5% additional dwellings surcharge from 31 October 2024, FA 2025 s.51 — operative); §1 main text (residential SDLT rate-table architecture); **NEW HP-LOCK CANDIDATE: §1.O "Worldwide-property ownership and UK SDLT — the three statutory routes (Sch 4ZA 5% surcharge + Sch 9A 2% non-resident surcharge + Sch 6ZA FTB-relief disapplication)"** — Stage 1b conductor decision; existing §1.I covers the surcharge architecture but does not explicitly cover the worldwide-property reach as a standalone HP-line.

This page covers six things competitor coverage routinely under-serves: (a) the **three distinct statutory routes** through which overseas property ownership raises a UK buyer's SDLT — (i) Sch 4ZA 5% additional dwellings surcharge if the buyer (or their spouse / civil partner under spouse-aggregation rules) owns any major interest in any dwelling anywhere in the world over £40,000, (ii) Sch 9A 2% non-resident surcharge if the buyer is also not UK-resident under the 183-day test, (iii) Sch 6ZA FTB-relief disapplication if the buyer has previously been a purchaser of a major interest in any dwelling anywhere in the world; (b) the **stacking effect** — the surcharges stack additively (Sch 4ZA 5% + Sch 9A 2% = 7% on top of standard residential rates, plus the loss of FTB relief if applicable); (c) the **practical worldwide examples** — overseas family home inherited from parents, holiday home in Spain, share in a Mumbai apartment, off-plan investment in Dubai, equity-tracking trust interest in a US dwelling (depending on trustee residence and beneficial ownership analysis); (d) the **£40,000 exclusion** under Sch 4ZA para 17 — overseas property with market value under £40,000 does NOT trigger the additional dwellings surcharge; useful for very low-value family-village properties in some jurisdictions; (e) the **spouse / civil partner aggregation under Sch 4ZA para 9** — for married couples or civil partners, the worldwide-property ownership of either spouse / partner triggers the surcharge on a UK purchase by the other; this is the single most-common trap for UK-resident individuals with overseas-born / foreign-citizen spouses; (f) the **replacement-of-main-residence relief at Sch 4ZA para 3** — narrow route to disapply the 5% surcharge where the overseas property was previously the buyer's main residence AND the UK purchase replaces that overseas main residence within the 3-year statutory window; mechanics specific and frequently mis-claimed.

## Key questions this page must answer

1. Does owning a property abroad increase my UK stamp duty? (Yes, in up to three ways — surcharge (Sch 4ZA 5% if you own any dwelling anywhere in the world over £40,000); non-resident surcharge (Sch 9A 2% if you are also not UK-resident under the 183-day test); loss of first-time buyer relief (Sch 6ZA 0% to £300k nil band lost if you have ever previously been a purchaser of any dwelling anywhere in the world).)
2. What counts as "owning a property abroad" for the additional dwellings surcharge? (Any "major interest" in a "dwelling" located anywhere in the world, where the value exceeds £40,000. "Major interest" means freehold equivalent or leasehold of more than 7 years; sole or joint ownership; outright ownership or beneficial ownership held through a trust; legal title or equitable title. The £40,000 floor excludes very-low-value properties. Beneficial-ownership analysis matters — interests held through nominee or trust structures may still count if the buyer is the beneficial owner.)
3. How much extra do I pay because of overseas property ownership? (Worked example: £400k UK purchase by an individual already owning a £200k holiday home in France. Standard residential SDLT = £7,500. With 5% additional dwellings surcharge under Sch 4ZA = £27,500. Difference: £20,000 attributable to the overseas property. If the buyer is also non-UK-resident under the 183-day test, add 2% surcharge under Sch 9A = £35,500 total. Difference from baseline: £28,000. Verify all rate calculations at write per §16.35.)
4. Does the rule apply to inherited overseas property? (Yes. Inherited property is treated identically to property acquired by purchase — the legal mechanism of acquisition does not change the result. An inherited share in an overseas family home counts as a "major interest in a dwelling" if the share has a value over £40,000 and meets the major-interest definition. Three-year "inherited interests" carve-out at Sch 4ZA para 9A applies where a person inherits a major interest (50% or less) and is treated as not holding that interest for the first 3 years from the date of inheritance — narrow window.)
5. What is the £40,000 exclusion? (Sch 4ZA para 17 — a major interest with a value of £40,000 or less is disregarded for the surcharge test. Useful for low-value rural family properties in some jurisdictions where the local market value is genuinely below £40,000. Stage 2 confirms whether £40,000 is the value of the buyer's share or the value of the whole property — the statutory wording is value of the major interest, so for a joint interest the £40,000 is measured on the share.)
6. How does the spouse / civil partner aggregation rule work? (Sch 4ZA para 9 — for a married couple or civil partnership living together, the worldwide-property ownership of either spouse / partner is attributed to the purchasing spouse / partner for the purposes of the surcharge test. The most common trap: a UK-resident buyer with a UK-born / UK-residency-only background marries an overseas spouse who owns a family home in their country of origin; the UK buyer's subsequent UK home purchase attracts the 5% surcharge on the basis of the spouse's overseas property. Separated couples are treated differently — Sch 4ZA para 9(3) — Stage 2 explains separation mechanics.)
7. Can I avoid the surcharge by selling the overseas property first? (Yes, in principle — if the overseas property is disposed of before the effective date of the UK purchase, the buyer no longer owns it and the surcharge does not apply. Timing matters; the disposal must be completed before completion of the UK purchase. Two practical traps: (i) overseas conveyancing systems often have longer completion timelines than UK; (ii) the disposal must be a genuine arm's-length sale, not a transfer to a related party that retains beneficial ownership. The narrower replacement-of-main-residence relief at Sch 4ZA para 3 also operates where the overseas property was the buyer's main residence and is being replaced.)
8. What is the replacement-of-main-residence relief? (Sch 4ZA para 3 — the 5% surcharge disapplies where the UK dwelling being purchased replaces the buyer's only or main residence AND the previous main residence has been disposed of within the 3-year window. Operates symmetrically — if the previous main residence (overseas or UK) was sold within the prior 3 years and the UK purchase is the replacement, no surcharge. Useful for overseas-relocator buyers selling an overseas main residence and buying a UK main residence. Strict requirements on "only or main residence" status of the previous property — Stage 2 expands.)
9. How does this interact with first-time buyer relief? (Sch 6ZA defines "first-time buyer" by reference to "any dwelling situated anywhere in the world". An individual who has ever previously been a purchaser of any dwelling anywhere in the world is NOT a first-time buyer for SDLT purposes. The disqualification is absolute and not subject to the £40,000 floor — even a sub-£40k overseas property previously owned defeats FTB-relief eligibility. The Sch 6ZA test is stricter than the Sch 4ZA test in this respect.)
10. Are there any UK-domestic carve-outs that overseas-property owners can use? (Narrow. The main carve-outs operate on the same statutory wording for both UK and overseas property: replacement-of-main-residence (Sch 4ZA para 3); inherited interests three-year window (Sch 4ZA para 9A); under-£40,000 exclusion for Sch 4ZA only (not Sch 6ZA). No "overseas-specific" carve-out exists; HMRC applies the surcharge and FTB-disqualification rules uniformly.)
