# Hortons Hall v HMRC: How the FTT Drew the Residential / Non-Residential Boundary on a Large Country-House Estate and What It Means for SDLT Mixed-Use Claims

## Statutory anchor
- Primary (residential property definition): FA 2003 s.116 — "(1) Subject to the provisions of this section, 'residential property' means: (a) a building that is used or suitable for use as a dwelling, or is in the process of being constructed or adapted for such use, and (b) land that is or forms part of the garden or grounds of a building within paragraph (a) (including any building or structure on such land), or (c) an interest in or right over land that subsists for the benefit of a building within paragraph (a) or of land within paragraph (b)." Verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2003/14/section/116 on 2026-05-26. The Hortons Hall dispute centred on the s.116(1)(b) "garden or grounds" question — specifically, how much of a large estate's land qualifies as "grounds" of the main dwelling.
- Primary (rate consequence): FA 2003 s.55 Table A (residential rates) versus Table B (non-residential / mixed-use rates). For high-value transactions, the rate differential is substantial — a £3m purchase at residential rates with 5% surcharge versus mixed-use Table B can produce a difference of £200,000+ in SDLT.
- Primary (case citation): **Hortons Hall Estate v HMRC** (sometimes cited as "Horton Hall" in tax-press summaries — the official FTT citation should be confirmed at Stage 2 via GOV.UK Tax Chamber decisions database). Likely cited as Hortons Hall Estate v HMRC [202X] UKFTT [XXX] (TC). **Stage 2 sub-agent verifies citation, decision date, judge, and amount at stake.** The case is referenced in HP §1.J Wave 9 case-law trilogy lock alongside Hyman and Suterwalla, indicating it is a known FTT decision in the post-2020 narrowing-trajectory line.
- Supporting case-law (the FTT narrowing trajectory): Hyman v HMRC [2019] UKFTT 469 + [2021] UKUT 68 + [2022] EWCA Civ 185 (the "in principle suitable" test; the operative "land that the dwelling owner has a reasonable need for" framing for grounds); Suterwalla v HMRC [2024] UKUT 188 (TCC) — paddock-with-grazing-licence: UT held the FTT had misapplied the test, allowing the appellant's mixed-use claim (Suterwalla is an OUTLIER in an otherwise narrowing line; Hortons Hall sits more squarely in the narrowing line); Mudan v HMRC [2023] UKFTT 317 (state of disrepair alone); MHB Ltd v HMRC; Brown v HMRC [2024] UKFTT.
- House position reference: **§1.J Wave 9 lock — mixed-use case-law trilogy: Hyman + Suterwalla + Hortons Hall**. ## Key questions this page must answer

1. What was the Hortons Hall case about? (Paragraph-length case summary: large country-house estate purchased by the taxpayer; the taxpayer claimed mixed-use SDLT treatment on the basis that parts of the estate — outbuildings, paddocks, woodland, or commercial-use land — were non-residential and brought the whole transaction within FA 2003 s.55 Table B rates. HMRC argued residential rates with the 5% additional dwellings surcharge applied. FTT confirmed residential treatment, refusing the mixed-use argument. Stage 2 fills citation + amount + judge + key facts + paragraph references.)
2. What is the key statutory question? (Whether the disputed land "is or forms part of the garden or grounds of a building" used as a dwelling, within FA 2003 s.116(1)(b). The Hyman line interprets "grounds" purposively — land that has the character of being attached to and supporting the residential use of the dwelling, viewed in the round. Hortons Hall applied this test to a country-house estate fact pattern.)
3. What did the FTT find about the specific land at Hortons Hall? (Stage 2 fills the specific findings. Likely points: the disputed land was found to have the character of residential grounds notwithstanding its size; the taxpayer's argument that the land was commercially used / non-residential in character was rejected on the evidence; the FTT applied Hyman's purposive reading.)
4. How does Hortons Hall fit within the Hyman / Suterwalla / Mudan / MHB / Brown trajectory? (Hortons Hall is in the orthodox post-Hyman narrowing line. The FTT applied the established test and reached a residential outcome consistent with the doctrinal direction. Suterwalla is the UT-level outlier where a paddock-with-grazing-licence fact pattern allowed mixed-use; Hortons Hall does not undermine Suterwalla but rather demonstrates that the Suterwalla outcome depended on its specific facts — grazing-licence creating a measurable commercial use of the paddock — and that more general "this is a large estate" arguments fall on the residential side.)
5. What is the SDLT cost differential in a Hortons Hall-like fact pattern? (Worked example for a £5m country-house estate purchase by a non-FTB buyer who already owns one other dwelling, with the 5% additional dwellings surcharge applying: residential SDLT = c.£763,750 (current Table A bands plus 5% surcharge); mixed-use SDLT (Table B, no surcharge — surcharge applies only to residential) = c.£239,500. The difference is c.£524,000 — material enough to make taxpayer pursuit of mixed-use arguments a recurring fact pattern, even against the narrowing FTT line. Verify rate calculations at write per §16.35.)
6. What features of the land would have helped a mixed-use argument? (Genuinely commercial uses: agricultural commercial production with contracted output; let commercial offices or workshops in outbuildings; commercial woodland producing harvested timber under a forestry agreement; let paddocks with formal grazing licences and rent (the Suterwalla fact pattern). Mere ownership of large land area does not move the dial; mere presence of paddocks or outbuildings does not move the dial; mere absence of intensive residential use does not move the dial.)
7. What features of the land hurt the mixed-use argument? (Domestic outbuildings (garages, sheds, gym buildings); ornamental gardens and lawns regardless of size; private woodland not commercially harvested; paddocks held for the owner's own equestrian use; ponds and streams ornamental in character. The Hortons Hall fact pattern likely included substantial domestic-character land of this type.)
8. How does this affect the additional dwellings surcharge analysis? (Residential treatment means the 5% surcharge applies if the buyer already owns another dwelling. Mixed-use treatment disapplies the surcharge entirely (the surcharge applies to residential property only). The surcharge is a substantial element of the rate-comparison; in the £5m worked example above, the surcharge alone is £250,000. A successful mixed-use claim therefore eliminates both the rate-differential and the surcharge — a double-saving — explaining why country-house buyers continue to pursue these claims despite the narrowing FTT line.)
9. What was HMRC's enquiry strategy in Hortons Hall? (Stage 2 fills. Likely points: HMRC opened an enquiry under FA 2003 Sch 10 para 12; closure notice or assessment under paras 28-29 maintaining residential treatment; taxpayer appealed under para 35; FTT confirmed HMRC's position. HMRC enquiry strategy across the country-house estate fact pattern has been consistent post-Hyman.)
10. What is the practical lesson for buyers of country-house estates? (Three lessons: (i) model SDLT on the residential basis at the purchasing-decision stage — do not assume mixed-use will succeed; (ii) if mixed-use arguments are pursued, do so with a careful evidential foundation on the commercial use of specific parts of the land — generic large-estate arguments are losing arguments; (iii) seek independent professional advice from a tax adviser engaged directly by the buyer, not by an SDLT-refund-claim firm whose commercial incentive is to maximise claim volume rather than win rate.)
