# Sole Trader vs Partnership: The Honest Entity-Choice Comparison for UK Property Landlords (2026/27)

## Statutory anchor

- **Primary (sole-trader tax architecture):** ITTOIA 2005 Part 3 (Property Income) ss.260-364 — taxation of UK property income for individuals. **Operative for sole-trader landlord:** rental profits taxed at individual marginal income tax rates (20% / 40% / 45% bands + 2026/27 thresholds per FA 2026); finance-cost restriction (ITTOIA 2005 s.272A) applies to residential property finance costs (Section 24); CGT applied per TCGA 1992 on disposals at individual level with annual exempt amount (£3k FY26 per individual; Stage 2 verifies current). NIC: rental income generally NOT trading income for Class 2 / Class 4 NIC purposes (per long-standing HMRC position — investment activity not trading); commercial property letting can be in scope of Class 4 NIC where carried on as a trade (rare).
- **Primary (partnership tax architecture):** ITTOIA 2005 Part 9 ss.846-863 — partnership tax-transparency. ITA 2007 s.852 (notional trade for each partner). **Operative for partnership landlord:** LLP / general partnership / LP transparent for income tax; each partner taxed individually on their share of partnership profit per the LLP / partnership agreement; profit allocation under PA 1890 s.24(1) default is equal in absence of agreement; ITTOIA 2005 s.272A applies AT PARTNER LEVEL on their share of residential property finance costs (transparency); CGT under TCGA 1992 s.59 (general partnership) + s.59A (LLP) with member AEA available; settlements legislation ITTOIA 2005 ss.624-628 challenges non-bona-fide profit allocations to spouses / minor children. Cross-reference A7 M2-A-B2 `does-your-business-qualify-as-a-partnership` for the partnership-existence definitional test + sibling `partnership-partnership-agreement-roles-types-benefits` (this batch) for partnership-agreement architecture.
- **Primary (the s.2(1) PA 1890 negative — load-bearing for property audiences):** Partnership Act 1890 s.2(1) — "Joint tenancy, tenancy in common, joint property, common property, or part ownership does not of itself create a partnership as to anything so held or owned, whether the tenants or owners do or do not share any profits made by the use thereof." **Critical for sole-trader-vs-partnership decision:** two or more individuals co-owning residential rental property and sharing profits per beneficial-ownership shares ARE NOT a partnership for PA 1890 / income-tax / SDLT-Sch-15 purposes by default — they are CO-OWNERS each individually treated for tax purposes (each filing their own SA Self Assessment with their share of rental income per PIM1030). The decision to OPERATE AS a partnership (rather than passive co-ownership) requires positive partnership-formation elements (active joint conduct of a business beyond mere co-ownership). Cross-reference A7 M2-A-B2 for full definitional analysis.
- **Primary (the Form 17 election — spouses / civil partners specific):** ITA 2007 s.836 + Form 17 — married couples and civil partners jointly owning residential property are by default taxed 50/50 regardless of actual beneficial ownership shares (TCGA 1992 s.282 + ITA 2007 s.836); Form 17 election can be made to be taxed on actual beneficial shares (e.g., 90/10 if that reflects actual ownership). Form 17 election requires actual beneficial ownership in those shares (Land Registry + deed of trust + financial reality). **Operative for sole-trader-vs-partnership analysis:** the Form 17 election is NOT a partnership formation — it is an election applying to JOINT OWNERSHIP (passive co-ownership under s.2(1) PA 1890 negative). The election + partnership formation are alternative routes for landlord-couple tax planning, not the same thing.
- **Primary (joint and several liability — sole-trader vs general-partnership operational difference):** sole trader bears all liability on their own; general partnership under PA 1890 s.9 imposes joint AND several liability on every partner for partnership debts. **Operative consequence:** in a general partnership, any individual partner can be pursued by a partnership creditor for the FULL amount of partnership debts; the partner then has to seek contribution from other partners. **Practical landlord context:** general partnership form is risky for property landlords with significant assets — a partnership creditor (defaulted-mortgage lender; tenant litigation; tradesperson with unpaid invoice escalating) can pursue a wealthier partner for the entire debt regardless of their share. LLP form (LLPA 2000) and LtdCo form provide the limited-liability alternative; the LP form (LPA 1907) provides limited liability for the limited partner only.
- **Primary (the introducing-a-partner-into-an-existing-sole-trader-portfolio — the OPERATIONAL CGT + SDLT consequence):** ITTOIA 2005 Part 9 + TCGA 1992 s.59 + FA 2003 Sch 15.
  - **CGT side:** sole trader transferring a fractional interest in property to an incoming partner triggers a part-disposal at market value (TCGA 1992 s.42 part-disposal mechanic); CGT chargeable on the part-disposal at residential CGT rates (24% higher / 28% surcharge — Stage 2 verifies current FA 2024 + FA 2025 + FA 2026 CGT rate calibration); annual exempt amount applies; holdover relief (TCGA 1992 s.165) UNAVAILABLE for investment property (only available for business assets / trading businesses).
  - **SDLT side:** transfer of fractional interest to incoming partner is a land transaction; SDLT chargeable on consideration provided (cash + assumed mortgage share); Sch 15 partnership-regime mechanics apply when establishing a partnership (FA 2003 Sch 15 paras 10 + 14 — transferring property into a partnership at market value with SLP-relief mechanic for the part attributable to existing partners). **Critical:** Sch 15 partnership regime applies ONLY IF the resulting structure qualifies as a partnership under para 1 business test — passive co-ownership does not qualify. Cross-reference existing `partnership-sdlt-relief-schedule-15-fa-2003-incorporation-sum-lower-proportions`.
- **Primary (the GAAR + settlements + Ramsay overlay):** GAAR (FA 2013) + Ramsay anti-avoidance principle + settlements legislation ITTOIA 2005 ss.624-628. **Operative for sole-trader-vs-partnership decision:** partnership formation that lacks bona-fide commercial substance and is motivated solely by tax-planning (e.g., spouse-partner with no capital + no skill + no time commitment, allocated 50% of profits) is vulnerable to settlements legislation challenge (income reattributed to the originating spouse). HMRC operative position via PM132000+ partnership manual sections. **Practical landlord context:** introducing a family member as partner solely to use their lower income-tax band is risky; commercial-substance gating must be respected.
- **Primary (the SA returns + filing rhythm):**
  - **Sole trader:** SA100 Self Assessment with property pages (SA105 for UK property income) annually; 31 January (online) / 31 October (paper) filing deadline; 31 January payment deadline + 31 July payment-on-account for following year.
  - **Partnership:** SA800 partnership return (TMA 1970 s.12AA — operative under PA 1890 partnership existence); annually; 31 January (online) / 31 October (paper) deadline; PLUS each partner files own SA100 with partnership pages (SA104 / SA104S) showing their share of partnership income.
  - **Penalty exposure:** sole trader filing default = SA100 late-filing penalty (£100 immediate + daily after 3 months + 6-month and 12-month escalations); partnership filing default = SA800 late-filing penalty PER PARTNER (each partner shares the penalty; commonly £100 × partner count even for one filing default). The SA800 penalty-per-partner architecture is the single most underappreciated operational cost of partnership form vs sole trader.
- **Primary (the OPERATIONAL COSTS comparison):**
  - **Sole trader:** simplest form; no entity-formation cost; no annual filing cost beyond SA100; HMRC PAYE / NIC scheme not required unless employing staff.
  - **Partnership:** small entity-formation cost (partnership agreement drafting — recommended; commonly £500-£2,000 professional fee); annual SA800 filing in addition to per-partner SA100; partnership accounts preparation common; HMRC PAYE / NIC scheme not typically required (no salaried employee).
  - **LLP:** Companies House registration (£10-£40 + agent fee); annual Companies House confirmation statement (£13-£40); annual accounts to Companies House (per SI 2008/1911 — cross-reference sibling `llp-accounts` this batch); annual SA800 LLP-return; per-member SA100; designated-member statutory responsibility. Operational cost noticeably higher than partnership.
  - **LtdCo:** Companies House registration; annual Companies House compliance; CT600 + CT computation + iXBRL tagging; annual statutory accounts; confirmation statement; ECCTA-compliant registered office + email + ID verification (per §11.A). Operational cost highest of the four forms; matched by potential tax-planning benefits (retention, double-extraction structure, succession-planning via FIC).
