# Sole Trader vs Partnership: The Honest Entity-Choice Comparison for UK Property Landlords (2026/27)

## Statutory anchor

- **Primary (sole-trader tax architecture):** ITTOIA 2005 Part 3 (Property Income) ss.260-364 — taxation of UK property income for individuals. **Operative for sole-trader landlord:** rental profits taxed at individual marginal income tax rates (20% / 40% / 45% bands + 2026/27 thresholds per FA 2026 — RUN session verifies current bands at write time per §16.27); finance-cost restriction (ITTOIA 2005 s.272A) applies to residential property finance costs (Section 24); CGT applied per TCGA 1992 on disposals at individual level with annual exempt amount (£3k FY26 per individual; RUN session verifies current AEA). NIC: rental income generally NOT trading income for Class 2 / Class 4 NIC purposes (long-standing HMRC position — investment activity not trading); commercial property letting can be in scope of Class 4 NIC where carried on as a trade (rare).
- **Primary (partnership tax architecture per §11.C parent lock):** ITTOIA 2005 Part 9 ss.846-863 — partnership tax-transparency. ITA 2007 s.852 (notional trade for each partner per §11.C.6). **Operative for partnership landlord:** LLP / general partnership / LP transparent for income tax; each partner taxed individually on their share of partnership profit per the LLP / partnership agreement; profit allocation under PA 1890 s.24(1) default is equal in absence of agreement; ITTOIA 2005 s.272A applies AT PARTNER LEVEL on their share of residential property finance costs (transparency); CGT under TCGA 1992 s.59 (general partnership) + s.59A (LLP) with member AEA available per §11.C.7; settlements legislation ITTOIA 2005 ss.624-628 challenges non-bona-fide profit allocations to spouses / minor children. Cross-reference A7 M2-A-B2 `does-your-business-qualify-as-a-partnership` for the partnership-existence definitional test + sibling `partnership-partnership-agreement-roles-types-benefits` (this batch) for partnership-agreement architecture.
- **Primary (the s.2(1) PA 1890 negative — load-bearing for property audiences):** Partnership Act 1890 s.2(1) — "Joint tenancy, tenancy in common, joint property, common property, or part ownership does not of itself create a partnership as to anything so held or owned, whether the tenants or owners do or do not share any profits made by the use thereof." **Critical for sole-trader-vs-partnership decision (per §11.C.2 lock):** two or more individuals co-owning residential rental property and sharing profits per beneficial-ownership shares ARE NOT a partnership for PA 1890 / income-tax / SDLT-Sch-15 purposes by default — they are CO-OWNERS each individually treated for tax purposes (each filing their own SA Self Assessment with their share of rental income per HMRC PIM1030 + BIM72015). The decision to OPERATE AS a partnership (rather than passive co-ownership) requires positive partnership-formation elements (active joint conduct of a business beyond mere co-ownership). Cross-reference A7 M2-A-B2 for full definitional analysis.
- **Primary (the Form 17 election — spouses / civil partners specific, per §24 Wave 5 lock):** ITA 2007 s.836 + Form 17 — married couples and civil partners jointly owning residential property are by default taxed 50/50 regardless of actual beneficial ownership shares (TCGA 1992 s.282 + ITA 2007 s.836); Form 17 election can be made to be taxed on actual beneficial shares (e.g., 90/10 if that reflects actual ownership). Form 17 election requires actual beneficial ownership in those shares (Land Registry + deed of trust + financial reality). **Operative for sole-trader-vs-partnership analysis:** the Form 17 election is NOT a partnership formation — it is an election applying to JOINT OWNERSHIP (passive co-ownership under s.2(1) PA 1890 negative). The election + partnership formation are alternative routes for landlord-couple tax planning, not the same thing. Cross-reference existing `civil-partnerships-joint-property-ownership-tax-treatment-form-17-equality` for the Form 17 mechanics + §24 lock for depth.
- **Primary (joint and several liability — sole-trader vs general-partnership operational difference):** sole trader bears all liability on their own; general partnership under PA 1890 s.9 + s.10 imposes joint AND several liability on every partner for partnership debts. **Operative consequence:** in a general partnership, any individual partner can be pursued by a partnership creditor for the FULL amount of partnership debts; the partner then has to seek contribution from other partners. **Practical landlord context:** general partnership form is risky for property landlords with significant assets — a partnership creditor (defaulted-mortgage lender; tenant litigation; tradesperson with unpaid invoice escalating) can pursue a wealthier partner for the entire debt regardless of their share. LLP form (LLPA 2000 — per §11.C.Z) provides limited liability with maintained tax-transparency; LtdCo form provides limited liability with retention / extraction architecture; LP form (LPA 1907 — per §11.B) provides limited liability for the limited partner only (no management).
- **Primary (the introducing-a-partner-into-an-existing-sole-trader-portfolio — the OPERATIONAL CGT + SDLT consequence):** ITTOIA 2005 Part 9 + TCGA 1992 s.59 + FA 2003 Sch 15 (per §11.C.8 + §1.A locks).
  - **CGT side:** sole trader transferring a fractional interest in property to an incoming partner triggers a part-disposal at market value (TCGA 1992 s.42 part-disposal mechanic); CGT chargeable on the part-disposal at residential CGT rates (24% higher rate post-FA 2024 s.10; RUN session verifies current calibration per FA 2024 + FA 2025 + FA 2026 amendments); annual exempt amount applies; holdover relief (TCGA 1992 s.165) UNAVAILABLE for investment property (only available for business assets / trading businesses).
  - **SDLT side:** transfer of fractional interest to incoming partner is a land transaction; SDLT chargeable on consideration provided (cash + assumed mortgage share); Sch 15 partnership-regime mechanics apply when establishing a partnership (FA 2003 Sch 15 paras 10 + 14 — transferring property into a partnership at market value with SLP-relief mechanic for the part attributable to existing partners). **Critical:** Sch 15 partnership regime applies ONLY IF the resulting structure qualifies as a partnership under para 1 business test — passive co-ownership does not qualify (per §11.C.8 + §1.A.5 lock + A7 cross-link). Cross-reference existing `partnership-sdlt-relief-schedule-15-fa-2003-incorporation-sum-lower-proportions`.
- **Primary (the GAAR + settlements + Ramsay overlay):** GAAR (FA 2013) + Ramsay anti-avoidance principle + settlements legislation ITTOIA 2005 ss.624-628 + *Jones v Garnett (Arctic Systems)* [2007] UKHL 35 s.626 spouse-exception per §21.2 lock. **Operative for sole-trader-vs-partnership decision:** partnership formation that lacks bona-fide commercial substance and is motivated solely by tax-planning (e.g., spouse-partner with no capital + no skill + no time commitment, allocated 50% of profits) is vulnerable to settlements legislation challenge (income reattributed to the originating spouse). HMRC operative position via PM132000+ + TSEM4000+ partnership manual sections. **Practical landlord context:** introducing a family member as partner solely to use their lower income-tax band is risky; commercial-substance gating must be respected.
- **Primary (the SA returns + filing rhythm):**
  - **Sole trader:** SA100 Self Assessment with property pages (SA105 for UK property income) annually; 31 January (online) / 31 October (paper) filing deadline; 31 January payment deadline + 31 July payment-on-account for following year.
  - **Partnership:** SA800 partnership return (TMA 1970 s.12AA per §11.C.5 lock — operative under PA 1890 partnership existence); annually; 31 January (online) / 31 October (paper) deadline; PLUS each partner files own SA100 with partnership pages (SA104 / SA104S) showing their share of partnership income.
  - **Penalty exposure:** sole trader filing default = SA100 late-filing penalty (£100 immediate + daily after 3 months + 6-month and 12-month escalations); **partnership filing default = SA800 late-filing penalty PER PARTNER** (each partner shares the penalty; commonly £100 × partner count even for one filing default). The SA800 penalty-per-partner architecture is the single most underappreciated operational cost of partnership form vs sole trader.
- **Primary (the OPERATIONAL COSTS comparison):**
  - **Sole trader:** simplest form; no entity-formation cost; no annual filing cost beyond SA100; HMRC PAYE / NIC scheme not required unless employing staff.
  - **Partnership:** small entity-formation cost (partnership agreement drafting — recommended; commonly £500-£2,000 professional fee); annual SA800 filing in addition to per-partner SA100; partnership accounts preparation common.
  - **LLP:** Companies House registration; annual Companies House confirmation statement; annual accounts to Companies House (per SI 2008/1911 — cross-reference sibling `llp-accounts` this batch + §11.C.Z); annual SA800 LLP-return; per-member SA100; designated-member statutory responsibility (per §11.C.Z.6). Operational cost noticeably higher than partnership.
  - **LtdCo:** Companies House registration; annual Companies House compliance; CT600 + CT computation + iXBRL tagging; annual statutory accounts; confirmation statement; ECCTA-compliant registered office + email + ID verification (per §11.A). Operational cost highest of the four forms; matched by potential tax-planning benefits.
- **House position reference:** **§11.C (partnership statutory architecture parent — LIVE)**; §11.C.X salaried-member sub-lock; §11.C.Y mixed-membership sub-lock; §11.C.Z LLP accounts sub-lock; §11.B ECCTA Part 2 LP reforms; §11.A ECCTA Part 1; §21 (Ltd Co + FIC family — entity-choice anchor for next-tier-up step); §21.2 (settlements legislation + spouse exception); §24 (Form 17 + joint ownership — Wave 5 lock); §1.A SDLT partnership; §22 IHT. **No NEW HP lock requested** — entity-choice decision frame consumes existing locks.

## Worked-example data (RUN session uses these as canvas)

### Example 1 — Tax-outcome comparison: joint ownership vs Form 17 vs partnership

**Patel Couple** jointly own a £500,000 residential portfolio generating £30,000 rental profit before interest with £15,000 mortgage interest. Mr Patel is HRT; Mrs Patel is HRT.

- **Joint ownership 50/50 default (TCGA 1992 s.282 + ITA 2007 s.836):** each spouse taxed on £15k income at 40% = £6k each. Less 20% basic-rate credit on £7.5k interest share = £1.5k credit each. Net tax per spouse = £4.5k. **Total household tax = £9,000.**
- **Joint ownership with Form 17 election** (assume actual beneficial ownership 90/10 reflecting Mr Patel's higher capital contribution at acquisition; Mrs Patel is basic-rate after the income reallocation): higher-share spouse (Mr Patel, HRT) taxed on £27k at 40% (£10.8k) less 20% × £13.5k interest credit (£2.7k) = £8.1k. Lower-share spouse (Mrs Patel, basic-rate after personal allowance shift) taxed on £3k at 20% (£600) less 20% × £1.5k interest credit (£300) = £300. **Total household tax = £8,400.** Form 17 saves £600/year for this couple.
- **Partnership 50/50** (assuming partnership has been validly formed under PA 1890 s.1 with active business conduct, not just co-ownership): identical outcome to joint ownership 50/50 (transparency; s.272A applies at partner level). **Total tax = £9,000.**
- **Partnership 90/10 with bona-fide commercial allocation** (Mr Patel provides 90% capital + most management): identical to Form 17 outcome IF settlements legislation not triggered. **Total tax = £8,400.**
- **Operative insight:** for a property couple, the Form 17 election + joint-ownership delivers the same tax outcome as a 90/10 partnership formation, without the operational overhead of partnership architecture (PA 1890 + SA800 filing + s.14 holding-out + partnership-agreement-drafting). Partnership form is preferred only where (a) capital + management contributions justify a more complex allocation under bona-fide-commercial substance, or (b) the SDLT Sch 15 SLP relief on future LtdCo incorporation is the operational goal.

### Example 2 — CGT on introducing a partner

**Mr Singh sole-trader portfolio:** owns £500,000 residential portfolio with £200,000 base cost (acquired Year 1 at £200k; current market value £500k). Mr Singh decides to bring his adult son Aman in as a partner; agrees 50/50 partnership.

- **Part-disposal CGT (TCGA 1992 s.42):** Mr Singh transfers 50% interest to Aman. Market-value share = £250k; base cost attributable = £100k (50% of original £200k); chargeable gain = £150k.
- **CGT rate:** 24% on residential gain (per FA 2024 s.10; RUN session verifies current calibration). 24% × £150k = **£36,000 CGT** (before AEA of £3k FY26).
- **Net CGT after AEA:** £36,000 - (£3k × 24%) = £35,280.
- **Aman's base cost going forward:** £250k market value at acquisition (subject to any settlement legislation reattribution).
- **Counterfactual — Form 17 election alternative (if Singh-Patel were spouses, not father-son):** spouse-to-spouse transfers happen at no-gain-no-loss under TCGA 1992 s.58; no CGT on transferring beneficial interest. **Father-son partner introduction has no equivalent no-gain-no-loss relief.**
- **Operative insight:** introducing a non-spouse partner into an existing sole-trader portfolio is a CGT EVENT. Pre-incorporation planning may use s.162 incorporation relief for portfolio-to-LtdCo path (different from partnership formation) which deferred the CGT into the LtdCo's tax base. Partnership formation does NOT have an equivalent CGT deferral mechanic.

### Example 3 — SDLT on introducing a partner (Sch 15 partnership-regime)

**Continuing Example 2 — SDLT side:**

- **Sch 15 para 1 business test:** does the resulting Mr Singh + Aman structure qualify as a partnership? They actively co-manage the portfolio (joint decisions on tenant selection, property maintenance, rent reviews); para 1 likely MET.
- **Sch 15 paras 10 + 14 SLP mechanic:** when Mr Singh transfers property INTO the partnership at the partnership-formation event, Sch 15 applies. Lower-proportion calculations: Mr Singh held 100% pre-formation, holds 50% post-formation. SLP for the property = lower of (i) old proportion 100%, (ii) new partnership share 50% = 50%. SDLT chargeable proportion = 1 - SLP = 50%.
- **Chargeable consideration:** 50% × market value £500k = £250k. SDLT at residential rates with 3% HRAD surcharge on the £250k (Aman's incoming share via the partnership). RUN session calculates current bands at write time per §1.M lock.
- **Without Sch 15 partnership-regime (i.e., if straight transfer not into partnership):** SDLT on Aman's £250k acquisition. Same arithmetic; Sch 15 SLP doesn't help much in this scenario because Mr Singh's pre-existing share doesn't generate relief on the slice TRANSFERRED to Aman.
- **Operative insight:** Sch 15 SLP is more valuable on partnership-TO-LtdCo incorporation (where existing-partner-shares fully cover the LtdCo shares; SLP can be 100%) than on sole-trader-TO-partnership formation (where the incoming partner's slice does not get SLP cover from the existing partner). Common landlord misconception.

### Example 4 — SA800 per-partner penalty architecture

**Kapoor Family Property Partnership:** 4 partners (parents + 2 adult children). SA800 partnership return for 2025/26 due 31 January 2027. Single missed filing — return filed 4 months late on 1 June 2027.

- **Late-filing penalty per partner:** £100 fixed immediate per partner = £400 total at month 1.
- **After 3 months late (post-30 April):** daily penalties accrue per partner.
- **After 6 months late:** £300 per partner fixed or 5% of tax due per partner (whichever higher).
- **After 12 months late:** further escalation including potential penalty as percentage of liability per partner.
- **Counterfactual — sole-trader SA100 4 months late:** single £100 fixed + daily after 3 months on the individual liability.
- **Operative insight:** SA800 per-partner penalty multiplies operational risk by partner count. A 4-partner family LLP missing a single SA800 deadline can owe £400+ in fixed penalties even when actual tax owing is modest. This is the operational cost of partnership form that is most commonly underestimated at entity-choice stage.

### Example 5 — When partnership outgrows: step up to LLP or LtdCo

**Mawell-Estate Property Partnership:** 3-partner general partnership; £3m residential portfolio with £2m mortgages; £150k annual rental profit; ART household tax position aggregating to ~£40k tax bill annually.

- **Joint-and-several liability concern:** the partners are increasingly concerned that any partnership creditor (mortgage default; tenant litigation; tradesperson disputes) can pursue any one of them for full partnership debt. Mr Mawell-Estate has £2m+ personal assets at risk.
- **Step up to LLP:** LLPA 2000 limited liability; tax-transparency maintained (no income-tax rate change; partners continue at marginal); operational cost increase (Companies House + designated members + SI 2008/1911 accounts framework per §11.C.Z); ECCTA-compliant ID verification per §11.A. **Operative reason: limited liability with maintained tax-transparency.**
- **Step up to LtdCo:** Companies House registration; CT600; FRS 105 / 102 accounts; full operational overhead. **CGT on partnership-to-LtdCo incorporation:** TCGA 1992 s.162 incorporation relief if all partnership assets transferred + new LtdCo shares received as consideration; defers CGT into LtdCo base. **SDLT on partnership-to-LtdCo:** Sch 15 SLP mechanic via §11.C.8 + §1.A — if partners own LtdCo shares in same proportions as partnership shares, SLP can be 100% (zero SDLT). **Operative reason: retention vs extraction; potential CT-rate efficiency on retained profits; succession-planning via FIC overlay; cleaner exit options.**

## FAQ expansion (RUN session polishes prose; 12 FAQs target)

1. **Q: For tax, is there a meaningful difference between sole trader and partnership for property income?**
   A: The headline tax position is largely the same. Both are tax-transparent — sole trader pays income tax at individual marginal rates on rental profit; each partner pays income tax at their marginal rate on their share of partnership profit. Section 24 (ITTOIA 2005 s.272A) finance-cost restriction applies to both at the individual level for residential property. Both file Self Assessment (sole trader SA100 with property pages; partnership SA800 plus per-partner SA100 with partnership pages). The differences are operational rather than headline-tax: liability, filing rhythm, agreement architecture, CGT and SDLT on partner introduction.

2. **Q: What is the liability difference?**
   A: **Sole trader:** unlimited personal liability for all business debts; one person, one risk. **General partnership (PA 1890 s.9):** joint AND several liability for all partners — any partnership creditor can pursue any one partner for the FULL partnership debt; that partner then seeks contribution from others. For property landlords with significant personal assets, this is meaningfully risky vs sole trader because partnership debts spread the liability across the group BUT each partner is still on the hook for the full amount. **LLP (LLPA 2000):** members' liability limited to agreed amounts; the safest form with maintained tax-transparency. **LtdCo:** limited liability with the highest operational overhead.

3. **Q: What is the filing rhythm difference?**
   A: **Sole trader:** SA100 Self Assessment with UK Property pages (SA105) annually; deadline 31 January online or 31 October paper. Late-filing penalty £100 fixed + daily after 3 months + escalating tariff. **Partnership:** SA800 partnership return (per TMA 1970 s.12AA per §11.C.5) annually, PLUS each partner files their own SA100 with partnership pages (SA104 / SA104S). **The crucial operational difference:** SA800 late-filing penalty applies PER PARTNER. A 4-partner partnership missing a single SA800 deadline owes 4 × £100 = £400 fixed penalty plus escalations. This is the single most underappreciated operational cost of partnership form.

4. **Q: When is sole-trader form the right choice?**
   A: Five operative scenarios. (1) Single landlord with a small portfolio. (2) No partner introduction planned. (3) Simplest operational footprint preferred. (4) HMRC PAYE / NIC scheme not triggered (rental income alone does not trigger Class 4 NIC under long-standing HMRC investment-not-trading position). (5) Cost-sensitive setup where partnership-agreement legal fees + SA800 filing costs are material.

5. **Q: When is the Form 17 election + joint-ownership a better choice for couples than a partnership?**
   A: For most landlord couples, Form 17 + joint-ownership wins over partnership formation. Form 17 (per §24 Wave 5 lock) allows spouses and civil partners to be taxed on actual beneficial ownership shares (e.g., 90/10) rather than the default 50/50; no partnership-agreement drafting; no SA800; no per-partner penalty multiplication; no joint-and-several liability; no s.14 holding-out exposure. The Form 17 election applies to JOINT OWNERSHIP under PA 1890 s.2(1) negative — not partnership formation. **Partnership form is preferred** only where (a) bona-fide capital + management contributions justify a more complex allocation under settlements-substance test, or (b) the SDLT Sch 15 SLP relief on future LtdCo incorporation is the operational goal.

6. **Q: What is the s.2(1) PA 1890 negative test?**
   A: Per PA 1890 s.2(1) (and §11.C.2 lock), joint tenancy, tenancy in common, joint property, common property, or part ownership does NOT by itself create a partnership — even where co-owners share profits made by using the property. For two or more individuals co-owning residential rental property and sharing profits per beneficial-ownership shares, the default is co-ownership (each taxed individually on their share per ITTOIA 2005 + HMRC PIM1030 / BIM72015), NOT partnership. Partnership formation requires positive elements: active joint conduct of a business beyond mere co-ownership. Cross-reference A7 M2-A-B2 for the full definitional test.

7. **Q: What are the CGT consequences of introducing a partner?**
   A: A sole trader transferring a fractional interest in property to an incoming partner triggers a part-disposal at market value under TCGA 1992 s.42. CGT is chargeable on the gain at residential rates (24% post-FA 2024; verify current calibration at write time). The annual exempt amount is available. **Holdover relief (TCGA 1992 s.165) is UNAVAILABLE** for investment property (only trading businesses qualify). **Spouse-to-spouse transfers are different:** TCGA 1992 s.58 applies no-gain-no-loss; no CGT crystallisation. Partner-introduction with a non-spouse is a tax event that must be planned alongside the broader entity-choice.

8. **Q: What are the SDLT consequences of introducing a partner?**
   A: Transfer of a fractional interest to an incoming partner is a land transaction; SDLT is chargeable on consideration provided (cash + assumed mortgage share at market value). Where a partnership is being formed (per Sch 15 para 1 business test per §11.C.8 + §1.A locks), Sch 15 partnership-regime applies. **SLP mechanic at sole-trader-to-partnership formation:** SLP gives only partial relief because the existing sole-trader's pre-existing share does not generate cover for the incoming partner's slice. SLP is more valuable on partnership-TO-LtdCo incorporation (where existing-partner-shares fully cover LtdCo shares; SLP can be 100%). Cross-reference existing Sch 15 mechanics page.

9. **Q: How does the settlements / GAAR overlay affect family partnerships?**
   A: Settlements legislation at ITTOIA 2005 ss.624-628 challenges non-bona-fide allocations of income to a spouse, civil partner, or minor child. Per *Jones v Garnett* [2007] UKHL 35 + §21.2 lock, the s.626 outright-gift carve-out provides some protection for spouse-shareholder allocations (and by analogy spouse-partner allocations) provided the gift is of an ordinary income-and-capital interest, not a tax-driven preferential allocation. For other family-member partners (adult children, parents, siblings), no equivalent carve-out applies — allocations must reflect bona-fide commercial contributions (capital, skill, time, risk). HMRC operative position via PM132000+ and TSEM4000+. GAAR and Ramsay anti-avoidance principles backstop the analysis.

10. **Q: When does partnership form outgrow (step up to LLP or LtdCo)?**
    A: Five operative triggers. (1) **Assets making joint-and-several liability untenable** — consider LLP for limited liability with maintained tax-transparency. (2) **Multi-partner complexity** outgrowing partnership-agreement architecture — consider LLP for designated-member statutory responsibility framework per §11.C.Z. (3) **Retention vs extraction intent** favouring CT-rate retention — consider LtdCo. (4) **Section 24 erosion at HRT/ART level** — consider LtdCo for full interest deductibility under CTA 2009 Part 5 (not at partner level). (5) **Succession-planning need** — consider LtdCo with FIC overlay per §21.5 + §22.6.

11. **Q: What is the upgrade path from partnership to LLP / LtdCo?**
    A: **Partnership to LLP:** simple operationally — register new LLP at Companies House; transfer partnership business to LLP; partnership dissolves; LLP continues. Tax-transparency maintained; no CGT crystallisation at member level if no actual disposal occurs. **Partnership to LtdCo:** more complex. TCGA 1992 s.162 incorporation relief if all partnership assets transferred + new LtdCo shares received as consideration (deferred CGT into LtdCo base). SDLT Sch 15 SLP mechanic via §1.A — if partnership members own LtdCo shares in same proportions, SLP can be 100% (zero SDLT). Both upgrades require careful planning around CGT + SDLT events.

12. **Q: What are the common sole-trader-vs-partnership mistakes for landlords?**
    A: Six recurring patterns. (1) Assuming joint property ownership equals partnership when PA 1890 s.2(1) negative applies (co-owners are not partners by default). (2) Introducing a spouse-partner with no commercial substance triggering settlements legislation challenge. (3) Introducing a partner without managing the CGT part-disposal exposure. (4) Failing to file SA800 once a partnership exists, triggering per-partner penalties. (5) Assuming sole-trader-to-partnership transition has no SDLT consequence (it does — Sch 15 mechanics apply). (6) Choosing partnership form for limited-liability reasons (general partnership has unlimited joint-and-several liability per PA 1890 s.9; LLP is the limited-liability form).
