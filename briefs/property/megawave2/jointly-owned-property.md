# Jointly Owned Property: UK Tax Guide

## Statutory anchor

- Primary (the two flavours of joint ownership — the structural starting point):
  - **Law of Property Act 1925 ss.34-36** + **Trusts of Land and Appointment of Trustees Act 1996 (TOLATA)** — the framework distinguishing joint tenancy (JT) and tenancy in common (TIC) in English + Welsh law. Verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/Geo5/15-16/20 and https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1996/47/contents on 2026-05-26.
  - **Joint tenancy (JT):** all co-owners hold the property as an undivided whole; each owns 100% jointly. **Right of survivorship (ius accrescendi):** on death of one joint tenant, that interest passes automatically to the surviving joint tenant(s) outside the will and outside intestacy. JT requires the "four unities" — unity of possession, interest, title, and time (per common-law rule from *AG Securities v Vaughan* [1990] 1 AC 417 and pre-existing).
  - **Tenancy in common (TIC):** each co-owner holds a defined undivided share (e.g. 50:50, 99:1, 75:25). No right of survivorship; on death, the deceased's share passes per will (or intestacy). TIC shares can be unequal and can change over time by declaration of trust.
  - **Severance of joint tenancy** — JT can be severed unilaterally by one joint tenant via notice in writing (LPA 1925 s.36(2)); also severed by mutual conduct or by Court order. Result: TIC (equal shares unless otherwise expressed at severance).
  - **Scottish equivalent:** Conveyancing and Feudal Reform (Scotland) Act 1970 + Common Law of Joint Property — Scotland recognises joint property (analogous to JT) and common property (analogous to TIC). Survivorship in Scotland is only via a survivorship destination in the title deed; the default is common property with deceased's share passing per succession.
- Primary (the income-tax default 50:50 rule for spouses + civil partners):
  - **ITA 2007 s.836** — verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2007/3/section/836 on 2026-05-26. Income arising from property held in the names of an individual and the individual's spouse / civil partner living together is treated as arising in equal shares — UNLESS a joint declaration (Form 17) is made declaring the actual beneficial ownership is unequal AND that unequal declaration is supported by the beneficial-ownership facts.
  - **HP §24.1 lock:** the 50:50 rule applies only to spouses/civil partners (not unmarried co-owners), only where joint legal ownership exists (not sole-legal + co-beneficial), only where the couple are living together, and only where the property is held jointly with the spouse (not jointly with a third party + spouse).
- Primary (the Form 17 election to displace 50:50):
  - **HMRC Form 17** — "Declaration of beneficial interests in joint property and income". Filed under ITA 2007 s.836. Verified gov.uk publication 2026-05-23.
  - **Operational requirements (HP §24.2, locked):** both spouses must sign; 60-day filing window from last signature date; the declared split must match underlying beneficial ownership (Form 17 declares; declaration of trust changes ownership); joint tenants CANNOT use Form 17 (no divisible share to declare); effective from date of last signature, not from start of tax year; persists until qualifying change.
- Primary (the unmarried co-owner rule):
  - **HP §24.6 lock:** no Form 17 route for unmarried co-owners. Income split per actual beneficial ownership. Joint-tenancy bar applies equally (severance to TIC required for unequal split). Evidence-of-split discipline (contributions, declaration of trust). HMRC's TSEM9821 sets out the default-actual-beneficial-split position.
- Primary (the CGT no-gain-no-loss spouse rule):
  - **TCGA 1992 s.58** — verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12/section/58 on 2026-05-26. Transfers between spouses / civil partners living together are deemed to be at "no gain, no loss" — the receiving spouse acquires at the transferor's original base cost.
  - **s.58(1A)-(1D) — post-separation extension** (inserted by Finance (No. 2) Act 2023 c.30 s.41(2)(6), in force 6 April 2023): no-gain-no-loss extended to up to 3 tax years post-separation where the disposal is pursuant to a court order / formal separation agreement (HP §24.4).
  - **Critical contrast:** transfer between unmarried co-owners is at market value (s.17 TCGA 1992 — connected-persons / arm's-length); no s.58 relief.
- Primary (the SDLT consequence of restructuring beneficial ownership):
  - **FA 2003 Sch 4 para 8** — chargeable consideration for SDLT includes the assumed share of mortgage debt. Where a sole-owner spouse transfers a 50% beneficial interest to the other spouse and the receiving spouse assumes a share of the mortgage, the assumed debt is chargeable consideration. HP §24.3 lock — the most common Form 17 implementation error.
  - **FA 2003 Sch 4ZA para 2(3)** — joint-buyer higher-rates trigger. If ANY joint buyer meets the additional-dwellings conditions, the WHOLE transaction is at higher rates (HP §24.5).
- Primary (the IHT spouse-exemption + transferable allowances on joint-property death):
  - **IHTA 1984 s.18** — spouse exemption (post-FA-2025 LTR test, HP §22.5).
  - **JT death outcome:** the deceased's interest passes by survivorship to the surviving joint tenant — outside the will, outside the estate for IHT direction purposes (but the value IS included in the deceased's estate for IHT computation purposes per IHTA 1984 s.5 — joint tenancy doesn't avoid IHT, it just avoids the will).
  - **TIC death outcome:** the deceased's share passes per will (or intestacy). Subject to IHT as part of the estate; spouse exemption applies if passing to surviving spouse.
- Primary (the MTD-ITSA quarterly-filing joint-owner mechanic):
  - **HP §19.4** lock — each spouse tests their share-of-gross against the MTD threshold separately (£50,000 from 6 April 2026; £30,000 from April 2027; £20,000 from April 2028). A Form 17 75:25 split brings the 75% spouse into MTD scope earlier than the 25% spouse.
- Primary (the Section 24 finance-cost-restriction mechanic on joint property):
  - **ITTOIA 2005 ss.272A + 274A** (the Section 24 mechanic). Restriction applies to each spouse's share of finance costs separately; the 20% basic-rate tax credit operates on each spouse's restricted-finance-costs total.
- House position references: §24 (entire) — the page is the orientation/entry-tier ABOVE the §24 mechanics deeps; §22.5 (spouse exemption + transferable allowances); §19.4 (MTD joint-owner threshold); §15.3 (GROB if joint-ownership is structured as part of a gift); §4 (Section 24 finance cost restriction). The page threads §24.1 through §24.7 at orientation depth.

Pre-empts the most common amateur misconceptions:
- "Joint owners always split income 50:50" — false; only spouses + civil partners under s.836, with Form 17 to displace; unmarried co-owners split per actual beneficial share.
- "Joint tenants can declare 99:1 split" — false; severance to TIC required first (no divisible share to declare).
- "Form 17 changes the ownership" — false; Form 17 declares ownership; declaration of trust changes it.
- "Survivorship avoids IHT" — false; survivorship avoids the will, but the value is still in the deceased's estate for IHT calculation.
- "Cohabitee-to-cohabitee transfer is no-CGT" — false; s.58 applies to spouses + civil partners only; cohabitee transfer is at market value.
- "Each spouse gets their own SDLT first-time buyer relief on a joint purchase" — false; both spouses' positions are tested, and one disqualifies the whole transaction.
- "Each spouse can have their own PPR" — false; spouses + civil partners have one main residence between them (s.222(6)).
- "MTD applies on the combined £50k threshold" — false; each spouse tests their gross share separately.

The anti-templating axis vs the existing mechanics-deeps: those are the destination pages; this is the navigation page. Different depth, different reader-state.

## Key questions this page must answer

1. What does "jointly owned" mean — two or more people on the legal title to a property?
2. What are the two flavours of joint ownership — joint tenancy (JT, undivided whole, survivorship) vs tenancy in common (TIC, defined shares, no survivorship)?
3. How do I find out which type I have — Land Registry title showing "no restriction" = JT default; Land Registry title showing "Form A restriction" (or similar) = TIC; absence of restriction not always conclusive — declaration of trust may shift between them?
4. Who can be a joint owner — spouses / civil partners; unmarried partners; family members (parents + adult children); friends; business partners; SPVs; trusts?
5. What is the default rental income split for spouses + civil partners — 50:50 under ITA 2007 s.836, regardless of underlying beneficial ownership, unless Form 17 declares otherwise AND the underlying ownership is unequal?
6. What is Form 17 and when does it apply — joint declaration to HMRC of unequal beneficial interest; 60-day window; both signatures; cannot create the split, declares it?
7. What about unmarried co-owners — no Form 17 route; income split per actual beneficial ownership; evidence-of-split discipline?
8. What happens on death — JT: survivorship to surviving joint tenant outside the will; TIC: deceased's share passes per will / intestacy?
9. What is the IHT treatment — value of share included in deceased's estate either way; spouse exemption applies on transfer to spouse; transferable NRB + RNRB at second death?
10. What is the CGT treatment of inter-spouse transfer — s.58 no-gain-no-loss for spouses + civil partners living together (or in 3-year post-separation window); market value for unmarried co-owners?

**Mini-illustration vignettes (orientation pillar — light worked examples, not deep computations)**

**Spouse-couple BTL.** Married couple, joint legal owners of a BTL property purchased 2018. £24,000/year gross rent. Default 50:50 under s.836: each declares £12,000 rental income on SA. Mr Holloway is higher-rate (40%); Mrs Holloway is basic-rate (20%). Total income-tax on the rent: £12k × 40% + £12k × 20% = £7,200. If beneficial ownership is genuinely 70:30 favouring Mrs Holloway (she contributed 70% of deposit and the mortgage covenant), they can execute a declaration of trust documenting 70:30, then file Form 17 within 60 days of signature. Post-Form 17: Mrs Holloway declares £16,800 @ 20% = £3,360; Mr Holloway declares £7,200 @ 40% = £2,880. Total: £6,240. Annual saving: £960.

**Unmarried-couple BTL.** Cohabitees, joint legal owners. **No Form 17 route — applies to spouses + civil partners only.** Income split per actual beneficial ownership. If contributions and equity-build-up evidence the actual split is 60:40, that's what HMRC accepts (subject to evidence; HMRC's typical challenge is to a declared split that doesn't match the deed or contribution record). Inter-cohabitee transfer is market-value per s.17 — no spouse no-gain-no-loss relief; transferring a 50% beneficial interest crystallises CGT immediately.

**Parent + adult child joint purchase.** Father owns 100% of a BTL; transfers a 50% beneficial interest to adult son. CGT disposal at market value per s.17 + s.286 (connected persons); no s.58 relief. SDLT consequences if son assumes a share of mortgage (FA 2003 Sch 4 para 8). Future rental split per actual beneficial ownership (now 50:50). On father's death: if held as TIC, father's 50% share passes per will (in his estate for IHT); if held as JT, father's 50% passes by survivorship to son (still in father's estate for IHT calculation but outside the will direction).

**Joint tenancy survivorship trap.** Mrs Holloway holds a BTL with her sister Mrs Davies as joint tenants. Mrs Holloway's will leaves "all my property" to her two children. Mrs Holloway dies. The BTL share does NOT pass per the will — JT survivorship operates automatically, the entire BTL becomes Mrs Davies's. Mrs Holloway's children get nothing from the BTL. To preserve the children's inheritance, Mrs Holloway should have severed JT to TIC in her lifetime by serving notice under LPA 1925 s.36(2) and updating the title.

**Form 17 + connected-spouse SDLT trap (most common implementation error).** Husband sole-owns a BTL worth £400k with £200k mortgage. Husband and wife execute a declaration of trust giving wife 50% beneficial interest; wife is added to the mortgage. Wife's assumed share of mortgage debt (£100k) is chargeable consideration for SDLT (FA 2003 Sch 4 para 8). If wife already owns another dwelling, the higher-rate 5% surcharge applies on the full £100k consideration. SDLT due. The income-tax-restructure was sound; the SDLT trap caught them.

## FAQ draft (12 entries, Stage 2 populated; RUN sub-agent polishes prose)

1. **What does "jointly owned" mean for tax purposes?**
   Two or more people on the legal title of a property. For tax, the structure of ownership matters: joint tenants (JT) own the property as one undivided whole with right of survivorship; tenants in common (TIC) own defined shares with no survivorship. The income-tax, CGT, IHT, and SDLT treatment can differ materially between the two structures.

2. **What's the difference between joint tenants and tenants in common?**
   Per Law of Property Act 1925 ss.34-36, joint tenancy is undivided ownership: all joint tenants own the property as a whole; on the death of one, that interest passes automatically to the survivor(s) outside the will (the right of survivorship, *ius accrescendi*). Tenants in common hold defined undivided shares (typically equal but can be 99:1, 75:25 etc.); on death, the deceased's share passes per will or intestacy. JT can be severed unilaterally by written notice (s.36(2)) to convert to TIC.

3. **How do I find out which structure I have?**
   Check the Land Registry title. A "Form A restriction" on the title typically indicates tenants in common; absence of restriction often indicates joint tenancy but is not conclusive (a declaration of trust may have shifted the position). For older properties or where the title is unclear, ask your conveyancing solicitor or apply for a copy of the title plus any registered restrictions.

4. **What is the default rental-income split for spouses?**
   Per ITA 2007 s.836, where spouses or civil partners living together hold property in joint names, rental income is treated as arising 50:50 — regardless of any underlying unequal beneficial ownership — UNLESS the couple makes a joint declaration on Form 17 of their actual unequal beneficial interests, AND that declared split matches the underlying beneficial ownership. The 50:50 rule applies only to spouses / civil partners, only where joint legal ownership exists, only where the couple are living together, and only where the property is held jointly with the spouse (not with a third party).

5. **What is Form 17 and how do I file it?**
   Form 17 ("Declaration of beneficial interests in joint property and income") is HMRC's vehicle for declaring an unequal beneficial split to displace the 50:50 default. Both spouses must sign personally (no agent delegation); the form must reach HMRC within 60 days of the last signature (TSEM9851 strict); the declared split must match the underlying beneficial ownership (it declares, not creates); it takes effect from the date of the last signature; it persists until a qualifying change. Joint tenants cannot use Form 17 — severance to TIC is required first.

6. **Can unmarried co-owners file Form 17?**
   No. The 50:50 default rule and Form 17 election apply only to spouses and civil partners. Unmarried co-owners (cohabitees, friends, family members not in a recognised partnership) split rental income per actual beneficial ownership — no presumption of equality, no Form 17 route. HMRC enquiry pattern: a purported 70:30 declared split must be backed by evidence of underlying contributions or a declaration of trust.

7. **What happens to a jointly owned property on death?**
   Joint tenants: the deceased's interest passes by survivorship to the surviving joint tenant(s), automatically and outside the will. Tenants in common: the deceased's share passes per their will (or intestacy). **Critical caveat for IHT:** the value of the deceased's share is included in their estate for IHT calculation either way — joint tenancy avoids the will, NOT IHT.

8. **What's the CGT treatment of a transfer between spouses?**
   Per TCGA 1992 s.58, transfers between spouses or civil partners living together are deemed "no gain, no loss" — the receiving spouse acquires at the transferor's original base cost; no CGT crystallises on the transfer. The post-separation extension under s.58(1A)-(1D) (in force 6 April 2023 per Finance (No. 2) Act 2023 s.41) extends this treatment to up to 3 tax years post-separation where the disposal is pursuant to a court order or formal separation agreement. **No s.58 relief between cohabitees:** unmarried transfers are at market value per s.17.

9. **Does restructuring beneficial ownership trigger SDLT?**
   Sometimes. Creating a declaration of trust to redistribute beneficial ownership between spouses doesn't of itself trigger SDLT (no chargeable consideration). BUT if the receiving spouse assumes a share of an existing mortgage, the assumed debt is chargeable consideration for SDLT per FA 2003 Sch 4 para 8. The assumed-mortgage SDLT trap is the most common Form 17 implementation error. If the assumed amount crosses the nil-band and the receiving spouse owns another dwelling, the 5% additional-dwellings surcharge may apply.

10. **How does MTD for ITSA treat joint property owners?**
    Each spouse tests their share-of-gross rental income against the MTD threshold separately. From 6 April 2026 the threshold is £50,000; from April 2027 £30,000; from April 2028 £20,000. A spouse couple receiving £80k gross from joint BTL property: 50:50 default gives each £40k — below 2026 threshold for both. A Form 17 75:25 split gives the 75% spouse £60k (in MTD scope) and the 25% spouse £20k (below threshold) — the elections affect MTD scope, not just income tax.

11. **Can each spouse have their own main residence for PPR?**
    No. Per TCGA 1992 s.222(6), spouses and civil partners living together can have only one main residence between them. Where the couple owns two residences, a joint s.222(5) election nominates which one is the main residence (can be backdated to within 2 years of acquiring the second). Unmarried co-owners each can have their own main residence — the one-residence-per-couple bar does not apply.

12. **Where do I go next?**
    For JT-vs-TIC mechanics depth see our `joint-tenants-vs-tenants-in-common-property-tax-consequences-uk-landlords` page. For Form 17 mechanics depth see `form-17-declaration-beneficial-interest-property-mechanics-filing-revocation`. For unequal-split planning see `unequal-rental-income-split-spouses-tax-planning-form-17-vs-default-50-50-decision`. For the Section 24 interaction see `section-24-joint-property-ownership-tax-split`.
