# Inheritance Tax and the Family Home: UK Guide for Property Owners

## Statutory anchor

- Primary (the RNRB architecture — the figures most readers actually want):
  - **IHTA 1984 ss.8D-8M** (the Residence Nil-Rate Band suite). The cluster ss.8D-8M was inserted by **Finance (No. 2) Act 2015 s.9** and **Finance Act 2016 s.93 + Sch 15** with effect for deaths on or after **6 April 2017**. The cluster was amended by **Finance Act 2016** to add the downsizing addition (ss.8FA-8FE). All verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1984/51 on 2026-05-26 against the consolidated revised text.
  - **RNRB amount:** £175,000 per individual (frozen until 5 April 2031 — HP §15.1 + Wave 6 F-3).
  - **RNRB qualifying conditions (s.8E + s.8H):** the "qualifying residential interest" must be **closely inherited** by a **lineal descendant**. "Lineal descendant" defined at s.8K — children (including step-children, adopted, fostered), grandchildren, great-grandchildren of the deceased, and the spouses/civil partners of those descendants. **Nieces, nephews, siblings, and unrelated beneficiaries do NOT qualify** (verified per s.8K verbatim).
  - **RNRB taper (s.8D(5) + s.8FE):** withdrawn £1 for every £2 of net estate above £2,000,000. Fully extinguished at £2,350,000 (single individual with full £175k RNRB) or £2,700,000 (couple with full £175k each transferable, total £350k taper headroom).
  - **Downsizing addition (ss.8FA-8FE):** preserves RNRB where the deceased downsized after **8 July 2015** but the new residence has lower value, or sold and did not replace. The mechanic compares (a) the RNRB that would have been available on the prior residence with (b) the lower RNRB available on the current residence, and adds back the lost portion provided the difference is left to direct lineal descendants in some equivalent form (cash, other assets). Section 8FB sets out the calculation; s.8FC defines a "qualifying former residential interest"; s.8FD-FE the per-residence aggregation rule.
  - **Transferable RNRB (TNRB): s.8G + s.8L.** Where the first spouse / civil partner to die did not use all (or any) of their RNRB, the unused proportion transfers to the survivor. Claim via IHT436 within 2 years of second death.
- Primary (the GROB anti-avoidance rule — the trap most "gift the house to my kids" plans fall into):
  - **FA 1986 s.102** — the headline GROB rule. Verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1986/41/section/102 on 2026-05-26. Where the donor gives property but either (a) does not assume bona fide possession of the property at or before the start of the relevant period, or (b) the donor retains a benefit by contract or otherwise, the gift is "subject to a reservation". On death, the property remains in the donor's estate for IHT under s.102(3).
  - **FA 1986 s.102A — gifts of interests in land.** Specifically addresses the "land carve-out" route. Verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1986/41/section/102A. Where the donor gifts an interest in land and the donor retains a benefit (e.g. continued occupation rent-free), s.102A treats the entire interest in the land as remaining in the donor's estate — closing the historic *Ingram v IRC* [1999] STC 37 route by which a donor purported to carve out a lease-back from a gift of the freehold.
  - **FA 1986 s.102B — exception for gifts of undivided shares of land.** Verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1986/41/section/102B. The narrow shared-occupation carve-out: where the donor gifts an **undivided share** (typically 50%) of the land and (a) the donor and donee occupy the land together AND (b) the donor does not receive any benefit (other than negligible) at the donee's expense AND (c) the donor pays at least their share of running costs (per s.102B(4)) — the GROB rule does NOT apply. This is the *only* statutory route by which a parent can gift part of a jointly-occupied home to a child and start the 7-year PET clock cleanly.
- Primary (the rent-payment route that survives GROB):
  - **FA 1986 Sch 20 para 6(1)(a)** — gives a statutory carve-out from GROB where the donor pays "full consideration" for any continuing benefit, measured at market value. For a family-home gift where the donor continues to occupy, full market rent paid by the donor to the donee is the carve-out: rent must be commercial, reviewed periodically, demonstrably paid (bank-trail evidence), and the donee declares the rental income for income tax purposes. This is HMRC's published position (IHTM14000+).
- Primary (the spouse-life-interest route — IPDI):
  - **IHTA 1984 s.49A + s.49(1A) + s.151** — Immediate Post-Death Interest (IPDI). A testamentary settlement where the surviving spouse takes a life interest (the right to occupy / receive income) with capital to the children on the surviving spouse's death is treated for IHT under the qualifying-interest-in-possession rules (s.49(1A) treats the life tenant as beneficially entitled to the underlying capital). On first death: spouse exemption s.18 applies to the trust transfer; no IHT. On second death: trust value falls into the life tenant's estate for IHT (spouse exemption gone), but the capital then passes to children outside any second-death personal-estate planning by the survivor. HP §22.17 carries the full IPDI/QIIP rental-property depth.
- Primary (the spouse exemption baseline):
  - **IHTA 1984 s.18(1)** — unlimited exemption for transfers between spouses / civil partners where both are long-term UK resident (post-FA-2025 s.6A test). HP §15.6 + §22.X for the LTR architecture; HP §22.5 for the s.18(2A) limited exemption where the receiving spouse is non-LTR.
- House position references: §15.1 (NRB, RNRB, freezes); §15.3 (GROB family-home); §22.5 (spouse exemption + transferable allowances); §22.11 (GROB family-home depth — s.102B(4) shared-occupation deep); §22.17 (IPDI rental-property depth); §24.5 (PPR + Form 17 + spouse-living-together interaction). The page threads §15.1 + §15.3 + §22.5 + §22.11 + §22.17 with a §24.5 cross-reference at the "what if we already had Form 17 on the house" tangent.

**Mini-illustration: the four-route family-home decision — the Holloway-family case**

Persona: Holloway-family-widow (mother, 72). Family home in London £900,000 (acquisition cost 1988 £85,000 — gain ~£815,000). Stocks-ISA £400,000. Cash £200,000. Net estate £1,500,000. Husband died 2020; full TNRB + TRNRB available (claimed via IHT402 + IHT436); spouse exemption used on first death (no IHT paid).

**Outcome at death without any action (rely-on-RNRB route):**
- NRB £325k + TNRB £325k + RNRB £175k + TRNRB £175k = £1,000,000 combined allowance.
- Net estate £1.5m above allowances by £500k.
- IHT at 40% on £500k = £200,000.
- Two adult children inherit (lineal descendants — RNRB available).
- Both children inherit at probate value (s.62 CGT uplift wipes the £815k gain).
- **Total tax: £200,000.**

**Route A: lifetime gift of family home — GROB CAUGHT.** Mother gifts house to children March 2026, continues to live there rent-free. Dies November 2031 (5y 8m later). Per s.102(3), house treated as still in estate at death value (£900k assumed). Plus CGT crystallised on the lifetime gift: connected-party MV disposal under s.17 = £815k gain × 24% = £195,600 CGT paid by mother out of cash. IHT on death: same £200k as Route 0 PLUS the CGT cost. **Total tax: £395,600. Materially WORSE than doing nothing.**

**Route B: full-market-rent route.** Mother gifts house, continues to occupy BUT pays children full open-market rent (~£36,000/year for £900k London home). CGT crystallised on lifetime gift at £195,600 (same as Route A — no spouse relief, no holdover). After ~12 months of rent-paid + documented, gift may re-qualify as clean PET starting 7-year clock. Children declare £36k rental income each (£18k each) on SA. Mother needs ~£36k/year cash to fund the rent (sustainable with £200k cash + ISA drawdown). If mother survives 7 years from re-PET-start: house outside estate; £200k IHT saved; net = £195,600 CGT - £200k IHT saving + ~£252k rent paid - children's tax = complex but typically net-neutral-to-negative at age 72. **Total tax: ~£195,600 + children's marginal rate on rent.** Becomes attractive only with high donor-survival probability.

**Route C: s.102B(4) shared-occupation undivided-share gift.** Mother gifts undivided 50% share to one adult child who genuinely moves in with her. Both occupy as joint home. Child pays own share of bills. CGT on 50% MV disposal = £407,500 gain × 24% = £97,800. If 7-year survival: 50% of house outside estate; ~£100k IHT saved. Critical fit constraint: requires genuine cohabitation; doesn't work where child has own home and doesn't actually move in.

**Route D: testamentary IPDI to spouse-equivalent + remainder to children.** Not applicable here (mother is single after first death). For couples: first death sets up life-interest IIP for surviving spouse (s.18 spouse exemption applies; no first-death IHT); house and rental from house available to surviving spouse during life; on second death, IPDI s.49(1) brings the house back into the survivor's estate but allows full TNRB + TRNRB at second death (vs spouse-as-absolute route where survivor must own outright). Operates as defensive route where first death is the planning event.

## FAQ draft (10-12 entries, Stage 2 populated; RUN sub-agent polishes prose)

1. **How does inheritance tax work on the family home?**
   The family home is part of your estate at death and is valued at its probate market value. IHT is charged at 40% above the available nil-rate bands. The Residence Nil-Rate Band (RNRB) of £175,000 per individual (frozen to 5 April 2031) is available where the home passes to lineal descendants (children, grandchildren, their spouses) and the deceased used the home as a residence at some point. The £325,000 standard NRB stacks on top, and unused NRB / RNRB from a deceased spouse transfer to the survivor.

2. **What is the Residence Nil-Rate Band and who qualifies?**
   Per IHTA 1984 ss.8D-8M, RNRB gives an additional £175,000 nil-rate allowance where a "qualifying residential interest" (a property the deceased lived in at some point) is "closely inherited" by a "lineal descendant" (s.8K — children, step-children, adopted, fostered, grandchildren, and their spouses or civil partners). Pure rental property (never used by the deceased as a residence) does NOT qualify. Nieces, nephews, siblings, and unrelated beneficiaries are NOT lineal descendants and do NOT trigger RNRB.

3. **What is the £2m taper and when does it bite?**
   RNRB is tapered away £1 for every £2 of net estate above £2,000,000. A single estate of £2,350,000+ loses RNRB entirely; a couple's combined estate of £2,700,000+ loses both RNRBs entirely. The pinch point is estates between £2m and £2.7m: each £1 of additional estate value costs an extra £0.40 of IHT (the headline 40%) PLUS £0.20 of lost RNRB relief — effective 60% marginal cost. Property-portfolio estates near this threshold often benefit from charity-gift planning, lifetime gifting, or BPR-eligible business restructuring to bring the net estate back below £2m.

4. **What is the downsizing addition?**
   Per IHTA 1984 ss.8FA-8FE, if you sold a more valuable home on or after 8 July 2015 and either bought a smaller one or did not replace it at all, the RNRB you would have had on the larger home can be preserved on death — provided the equivalent value (cash, other assets) passes to lineal descendants. This protects downsizers from losing RNRB when they move into smaller accommodation later in life. The mechanics are at s.8FB; eligibility runs from 8 July 2015 only.

5. **What is the transferable RNRB and how do I claim it?**
   Per IHTA 1984 s.8G + s.8L, where the first spouse / civil partner to die did not use all of their RNRB, the unused percentage transfers to the survivor. Claimed on form IHT436 within 2 years of the second death. Critical detail: the transfer is by percentage of unused allowance, not by cash value — if the first spouse died before 6 April 2017 (when RNRB was introduced), 100% of their RNRB is treated as unused and transfers in full to the survivor.

6. **Why does "I'll gift the house to the children and live there for 7 years" fail?**
   Per FA 1986 s.102 (and the land-specific s.102A from 9 March 1999), where you give property but continue to enjoy a benefit from it (rent-free occupation, retained rights, informal management fees), the gift is "subject to a reservation" and the property remains in your estate at death — the seven-year PET clock never starts. The "gift the house and keep living there" plan is the textbook GROB case and one of the most common amateur estate-planning mistakes.

7. **What is the s.102B(4) shared-occupation carve-out?**
   The narrow statutory route per FA 1986 s.102B(4): where you gift an UNDIVIDED SHARE (typically 50%) of the family home to a donee, AND both you and the donee actually occupy the property together as your joint home, AND you do not receive any benefit (other than a negligible one) from the donee related to the gift, the GROB rule does NOT apply. The route works for genuine multi-generational households where an adult child genuinely lives with the parent in the same home; it does NOT work where the child has their own home and is named on the title only on paper.

8. **What is the full-market-rent route?**
   Per FA 1986 Sch 20 para 6(1)(a), GROB does not apply where the donor pays "full consideration" (full open-market rent) for any continuing benefit. After the lifetime gift, the donor pays the donees a commercial rent — reviewed periodically against local comparables, paid by bank standing order, with the donees declaring the rental income on their self-assessment returns. The route works but is expensive: a £900k London home might cost £30k-£40k/year in rent for the lifetime of the donor.

9. **What is the IPDI (Immediate Post-Death Interest) route?**
   Per IHTA 1984 s.49A, an IPDI is a life interest in settled property that arises on the testator's death by will or under intestacy. Common landlord use: first-spouse-to-die's will leaves the family home into a trust giving the surviving spouse a life interest (right to occupy + receive any rental income) with the capital passing to the children on the survivor's death. On first death: spouse exemption s.18 applies; no IHT. On second death: s.49(1) treats the surviving spouse as beneficially entitled to the underlying property — full TNRB + TRNRB available; capital passes to children outside the surviving spouse's personal-estate planning.

10. **What happens to the family home if there is no will?**
    Under the Administration of Estates Act 1925 ss.45-49 (as amended): if the deceased left a spouse and children, the spouse takes the personal chattels, a statutory legacy of £322,000 (SI 2023/758 from 26 July 2023), and half the residue absolutely; the children share the remaining half on statutory trusts. The spouse can elect (Sch 2) to take the matrimonial home in or towards satisfaction of their entitlement, paying equality money if the home exceeds their share. Without a spouse, the issue inherit; without issue, the parents, siblings, and remoter relatives in a statutory hierarchy.

11. **Does the CGT uplift on death apply to the family home?**
    Yes per TCGA 1992 s.62(1)(a) — beneficiaries inherit at probate market value, and any gain accrued during the deceased's ownership is wiped out for CGT purposes. This makes lifetime gifting of an appreciated family home expensive in CGT terms: a £600k gain crystallises at 24% (residential rate) on the lifetime gift = £144k immediate CGT, with no holdover available for non-business property. Holding to death avoids the CGT entirely. The CGT-uplift-on-death is the silent killer of many lifetime-gifting plans for appreciated family homes.

12. **Where do I go next for my specific situation?**
    For the GROB statute walkthrough deep, see our `grob-s102-family-home-shared-occupation-s102b-uk-mechanics` page. For the RNRB £2m taper deep specifically for portfolio estates, see `iht-residence-nil-rate-band-2m-taper-property-portfolios`. For the lifetime-vs-death decision framework see `inheritance-tax-lifetime-gifts-vs-transfer-at-death`. For the inheritor's perspective see `inheriting-a-house-in-the-uk`. For general IHT orientation see `inheritance-tax-a-brief-summary`. For GROB as a standalone concept see `gift-with-reservation-of-benefit`.
