## Worked-example data (RUN session uses these as canvas)

### Example 1 — s.455 charge on overdrawn DLA

- **Patel Property Ltd** has an accounting period ending 31 March 2027. During the period, director Anil Patel drew £80,000 from the company beyond his salary + dividend entitlement, creating a £80,000 debit DLA balance at year-end. He does NOT repay before 1 January 2028 (9 months + 1 day after year-end).
- **s.455 charge:** £80,000 × **35.75%** = **£28,600** corporation tax due, payable as if it were CT for the AP ending 31 March 2027. Charge is provisional — refundable on later repayment via Form L2P within a 4-year window.

### Example 2 — s.455 refund on later repayment

- Continuing Example 1: Anil repays £40,000 of the £80,000 DLA during the AP ending 31 March 2028 (genuine repayment from after-tax personal funds; NOT redrawn).
- **s.455 refund:** £40,000 × 35.75% = **£14,300 refundable** by HMRC. Refund claimable 9 months + 1 day after the END of the AP in which repayment was made (so refund payable from 1 January 2029). RUN session frames the cash-flow trap: the company pays £28,600 in Jan 2028 then waits until Jan 2029 to recover £14,300 even though the repayment happened in the same accounting year as the refund-trigger event.

### Example 3 — s.464A residual anti-avoidance (replacing ss.464C/D conceptual role)

- **Singh Property Ltd**: director Vikram Singh has £100,000 overdrawn DLA at year-end. Three days before year-end he engages an arrangement under which the company makes a £100,000 "consultancy payment" to a connected company that immediately makes a £100,000 loan back to Vikram. The Singh-side DLA is repaid; the connected-company loan is functionally identical in economic substance.
- **s.464A analysis:** HMRC argues this is an "arrangement conferring benefit on participator" within s.464A — designed to avoid the s.455 charge. The charge under s.464A is calculated by reference to the dividend upper rate at ITA 2007 s.8(2) (currently 35.75% from 6 April 2026), same as s.455. Net result: arrangement defeated, s.464A charge applied to the £100,000 "benefit".
- **Compare ss.464C/D historic position:** before 30 October 2024, the bed-and-breakfast specific rule at s.464C would have applied where repayment + redraw occurred within 30 days of each other (same close company). FA 2025 omitted s.464C + s.464D in full; the residual anti-avoidance is the broader s.464A framework. RUN session frames the historic position briefly but anchors current law on s.464A.

### Example 4 — Beneficial loan BIK (HMRC official rate)

- **Kapoor Property Ltd** lends £30,000 to director Meera Kapoor interest-free for personal use (above the £10,000 qualifying-loans exemption threshold).
- **BIK calculation:** £30,000 × HMRC official rate **3.75%** × 12/12 (full year) = **£1,125 cash-equivalent BIK**.
- **Personal tax:** Meera reports £1,125 as employment income on P11D (or via payrolling), taxed at her marginal income tax rate. If higher-rate, £1,125 × 40% = £450 income tax.
- **Employer NIC:** company pays Class 1A NIC on the £1,125 BIK at 15% (FA 2026 employer NIC rate per §21.4) = £169 employer NIC. Total tax cost to employer + Meera = £619.
- RUN session compares to the alternative: charge Meera interest at the HMRC official rate (3.75%) from the loan inception — no BIK, no employer NIC, but interest paid is taxable savings income on Meera AND CT-deductible expense for the company (depending on the source-of-funds analysis).

### Example 5 — Incorporation s.162 credit-balance origin + DLA exhaustion trap

- **Mrs Kapoor** incorporated her BTL portfolio in 2024 under TCGA 1992 s.162. The transfer of 6 properties worth £900,000 (book value at the time) created a £900,000 DLA credit balance (the value differential between properties transferred-in and shares issued).
- **Tax-free repayment route:** Mrs Kapoor draws £6,000/month rent receipts as DLA repayment (no income tax; no NIC; just repaying her own loan to the company). Annual draw = £72,000 tax-free.
- **DLA exhaustion calculation:** £900,000 / £72,000 per year = **12.5 years** until DLA exhausts (assuming no other draws or top-ups).
- **Exhaustion trap:** at exhaustion (year 12-13), Mrs Kapoor MUST shift to a dividend / salary / pension extraction route — her tax cost jumps from £0 (DLA repayment) to (e.g.) £72,000 × 35.75% effective dividend rate at higher-rate band = ~£25,000/year additional tax. RUN session frames the trap: founders who treat DLA-funded draws as permanent income do not plan for the post-exhaustion extraction cliff. Recommendation: model the exhaustion year + start blending in dividend extraction 2-3 years before exhaustion to smooth the transition.

## FAQ expansion (RUN session polishes prose; 10-12 FAQs target)

1. **Q: What is a director's loan account (DLA), and what does the credit-balance vs debit-balance distinction mean?**
   A: A DLA tracks the running balance of money owed BY the company TO the director (credit balance) or money owed BY the director TO the company (debit balance, also called "overdrawn DLA"). Credit balances often arise from founder-injected start-up capital, deferred dividend declarations, or — common in property — the s.162 incorporation relief value differential when an existing rental portfolio is transferred into a newly-formed company. Debit balances arise when a director draws money from the company beyond their salary / dividend entitlement. The two directions have distinct tax treatments: credit balances are repayable tax-free to the director; debit balances may trigger the s.455 charge and the beneficial-loan BIK.

2. **Q: How is a credit-balance DLA repaid tax-efficiently?**
   A: The repayment of a credit-balance DLA is a return of capital, not income, so it is tax-free to the director. The discipline is to repay the DLA BEFORE other extraction routes (which all have personal tax cost): DLA repayment first, then salary up to the Personal Allowance + Primary Threshold, then employer pension contributions (CT-deductible, no NIC), then dividends within and above the allowance. The order matters because every pound repaid from a credit balance saves at the marginal income tax rate the director would otherwise pay on extraction. Beware the DLA exhaustion trap — see the worked example above.

3. **Q: What is the s.455 charge on overdrawn DLAs?**
   A: CTA 2010 s.455 imposes a corporation tax charge on close companies that make loans to participators (typically directors who are also shareholders). The charge falls on amounts of the loan that remain unpaid 9 months and 1 day after the end of the accounting period in which the loan was made. The rate is set by reference to the dividend upper rate at ITA 2007 s.8(2), which is **35.75% for 2026/27** (raised from 33.75% by FA 2026 s.4(1)(b) effective 6 April 2026). The charge is payable as if it were corporation tax for the AP in which the loan was made.

4. **Q: Is s.455 a permanent tax, or refundable?**
   A: Refundable. HMRC describes s.455 as "tax cease to be due" once the loan is repaid; in practical terms the company claims a refund via Form L2P. The refund is repayable 9 months and 1 day after the end of the accounting period in which repayment was made — so there is a cash-flow gap of up to 12 months between the genuine repayment and the actual refund receipt. The refund window is 4 years from the end of the AP in which the loan was repaid; beyond that, refund is lost.

5. **Q: What is the bed-and-breakfast anti-avoidance, and is it still on the statute book?**
   A: Historically (pre-30 October 2024), CTA 2010 s.464C imposed a 30-day rule: where a participator repaid a loan AND the same close company made a further loan to the same participator within 30 days, the repayment was treated as NOT relieved against the s.455 charge. CTA 2010 s.464D extended this to arrangements over £15,000 where an intention to redraw existed. **Finance Act 2025 s.81(3)(b)-(4) omitted ss.464C and 464D in full from 30 October 2024.** They are no longer on the statute book. The residual anti-avoidance is now at CTA 2010 s.464A (charge to tax: arrangements conferring benefit on participator), a broader framework that catches any structured avoidance of s.455. The 30-day specific test no longer applies in form, but HMRC retains s.464A as the operative defensive line and the bed-and-breakfast economic pattern remains catchable.

6. **Q: Does HMRC still attack year-end-repay-and-redraw schemes after the ss.464C/D omission?**
   A: Yes, via s.464A. The "arrangements conferring benefit" framework is broader than the historic 30-day test; HMRC's position is that any scheme designed to avoid or reduce the s.455 charge — whether structured as repay-and-redraw, intra-group circular payments, or related-company conduits — is caught by s.464A if the substance is a participator benefit. The s.464A rate is also calculated by reference to the dividend upper rate at ITA 2007 s.8(2) (35.75% from 6 April 2026), giving the same numeric outcome as the s.455 charge would have been.

7. **Q: When does the beneficial-loan BIK apply to a director's loan?**
   A: Where a director (or any employee) has loans from the employer totaling more than £10,000 at any point in the tax year, AND the company charges less than the HMRC official rate of interest, the difference is a taxable benefit in kind. The HMRC official rate is **3.75% for 6 April 2026 onwards** (also 3.75% for 6 April 2025 to 5 April 2026 — no change at the boundary). The BIK is reported on P11D (or via payrolling); employer pays Class 1A NIC at 15% (FA 2026 employer NIC rate); employee pays income tax at their marginal rate. Below the £10,000 threshold, no BIK arises regardless of interest charged.

8. **Q: Is interest paid by the company to a director on a credit-balance DLA taxable?**
   A: Yes, in the director's hands. Interest received on a credit-balance DLA is savings income on the director's personal tax return, taxed in line with the savings allowance + savings starting rate band where available. Company-side, the interest paid is deductible (subject to close-company restrictions under CTA 2010 s.453 framework). Directors with credit-balance DLAs sometimes negotiate a "loan agreement" with the company providing for interest at a commercial rate — the director receives savings income; the company gets a CT-deductible expense; the structure is well-tested provided the rate is commercial and the terms are documented.

9. **Q: What is the s.464A "arrangements conferring benefit" charge?**
   A: CTA 2010 s.464A imposes a corporation tax charge on close companies that participate in arrangements designed to confer a benefit on a participator (or their associate), where the benefit doesn't already trigger an income tax charge or s.455 charge. The rate is calculated by reference to the dividend upper rate at ITA 2007 s.8(2), giving 35.75% from 6 April 2026 — same numeric position as s.455. The provision is the operative anti-avoidance line after the ss.464C/D omission, and is structured to catch the economic substance of repay-and-redraw schemes without the 30-day specific test.

10. **Q: What is the s.453 close-company benefits-to-participators framework?**
    A: CTA 2010 s.453 sits in the broader close-company anti-avoidance architecture covering benefits provided to participators. It informs HMRC's reading of the boundary between deductible commercial transactions and disguised participator benefits. In the DLA context, s.453 supports HMRC's challenge where company-side "loans" are not on commercial terms (zero interest below the official rate; no repayment schedule; no documentation) — HMRC may recharacterise the arrangement.

11. **Q: How does the DLA exhaustion trap work for incorporated portfolios?**
    A: When a landlord incorporates an existing rental portfolio under TCGA 1992 s.162, the value differential between properties transferred IN and shares issued OUT creates a tax-free DLA credit balance. The landlord can then draw rent receipts as DLA repayment with no further tax cost. But the balance is finite. Worked example: a £500,000 credit balance at £8,000/month drawn = 62 months (just over 5 years) until exhaustion. Post-exhaustion, the landlord must shift to a dividend or salary extraction route, with associated personal-tax cost (typically 27%-54% combined effective rate depending on rate band). RUN session models the exhaustion year for the reader and recommends blending in dividend extraction 2-3 years before exhaustion to smooth the transition.

12. **Q: How does this page differ from `director-loan-account-property-company-mechanics` and `btl-spv-directors-loan-repayment-strategy-tax-efficient-extraction`?**

This page is the **generic UK pillar / explainer** — what is a DLA in both directions, the four statutory anchors (s.455 + s.464A + s.453 + ITEPA BIK), the HMRC official rate framework, the s.162 credit-balance origin.
