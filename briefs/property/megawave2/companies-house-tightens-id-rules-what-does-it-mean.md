# Companies House Tightens ID Rules: What Does It Mean — A Before-and-After Interpretation for Landlord LtdCos and Their Advisers

## Statutory anchor
- Primary: Economic Crime and Corporate Transparency Act 2023 c. 56 ("ECCTA 2023"). Verified verbatim at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2023/56/contents on 2026-05-26 (sub-agent re-verifies at write time per §16.35). The "tightening" framing reads through ECCTA's two structural shifts:
  - **ECCTA s.1** — Registrar's objectives (CA 2006 inserted-s.1081A): four statutory objectives — properly-delivered documents, accurate information, complete register, prevention of unlawful-activity facilitation. This is the **active-gatekeeper objective set** that did not exist before ECCTA; the policy-tightening reads through this section.
  - **ECCTA Identity-verification crossheading (s.64-69)** — the substantive ID-rules tightening per §11.A: s.64 PSC verification (PSC-side ID verification, new), s.65 procedure (verbatim heading: "Procedure etc for verifying identity"), s.66 ACSP authorisation (Authorised Corporate Service Providers, new regime), s.67 national-security exemption (narrow carve-out), s.68 unique identifiers (Companies House personal code mechanic), s.69 publication restriction (verified-identity data largely withheld from public inspection).
  - **ECCTA ss.40-45 (Directors crossheading)** — director-side ID verification operative provisions (CA 2006 amendments inserted by ECCTA in this section range plus new CA 2006 Part 11A — Stage 2 sub-agent verifies precise section numbers at write per §16.35).
  - **ECCTA ss.2-7** — Company formation: identity verification at incorporation; disqualification checks (the incorporation-time tightening).
  - **ECCTA ss.51-52** — Abolition of local PSC registers (PSC information now lives only at Companies House central register — a tightening of disclosure-locus from "split between local + central" to "central-only").
- Supporting (pre-ECCTA baseline): CA 2006 ss.853A-G (confirmation statement obligation as it stood pre-March-2024) + Small Business, Enterprise and Employment Act 2015 (PSC regime introduction, pre-ECCTA — PSC was a register-data requirement but did NOT include identity verification). ## Key questions this page must answer

1. **What used to happen at Companies House — what was the pre-ECCTA position on identity?** (Before ECCTA 2023, the Registrar of Companies operated a passive register: documents delivered were recorded; the Registrar had no statutory objective to verify the accuracy or identity of filers. PSC information was collected (post-2015 Small Business, Enterprise and Employment Act 2015) but PSC identity was not verified — the PSC framework was disclosure-without-verification. Directors could be appointed without proving identity to Companies House; the appointment paperwork required a name + service address + date of birth (last two digits redacted on the public register) but no biometric or document-based identity check. Registered office could be any UK address acceptable for service of documents — pure PO-box addresses were widely used. Confirmation statement was an annual data-refresh filing without a lawful-purposes attestation. Registered email was not required.)
2. **What changed substantively under ECCTA 2023?** (Six structural changes that together represent the "tightening": (a) Registrar's statutory objectives at ECCTA s.1 / CA 2006 inserted-s.1081A — the Registrar now has a duty to ensure register integrity, not just record what is delivered; (b) identity verification mandatory for directors and PSCs under ECCTA ss.40-45 + s.64 + s.65 — biometric / document-based check with one Companies House personal code per natural person under s.68; (c) ACSP framework under s.66 — AML-supervised firms register with Companies House to perform verification on clients' behalf; (d) registered email address required for every UK company under ss.28-30 — a CH-to-company correspondence channel; (e) appropriate-address rule for registered office under ss.28-30 — must be capable of acknowledging service, PO boxes generally do not qualify; (f) lawful purposes statement on confirmation statement under ss.59-63 — annual positive attestation. The cumulative effect: Companies House moves from passive recorder to active gatekeeper, with verified-identity + appropriate-address + lawful-purposes-attestation as the three new gates.)
3. **Is this 'tightening' actually a tightening — or a structural shift?** (Honest interpretive framing: a "tightening" typically means a pre-existing rule made stricter. The ID verification regime is more accurately described as a NEW gate where one did not exist before — the previous position was disclosure-without-verification for PSC, no-identity-check for directors. This is structural rather than incremental. The lawful purposes statement is genuinely incremental — previously, the confirmation statement existed but did not include a lawful-purposes attestation; the attestation is the addition. The appropriate-address rule is a tightening of an existing standard — registered offices were previously required to be valid for service of documents, but the appropriate-address rule sharpens that to "capable of acknowledging service". Communicating this nuance honestly helps readers understand the regime as it is.)
4. **What does the ID verification gate mean qualitatively for directors and PSCs?** (Three qualitative shifts: (a) appointment is contingent on verification — a new director cannot be recorded until they have a verified personal code (ECCTA s.68); (b) PSC status is contingent on verification — a PSC who has not verified cannot lawfully continue in PSC status post-transition-window; (c) the verification is per natural person, not per company — one verification covers every directorship and PSC interest across every UK company. The qualitative point: the register now has an integrity floor it did not previously have, and continued status (director or PSC) is conditional on having cleared that floor.)
5. **What does the appropriate-address tightening mean for landlord LtdCos?** (Many landlord LtdCo SPVs were historically registered at low-cost virtual-office or PO-box services. Under the appropriate-address rule (effective 4 March 2024), the registered office must be capable of acknowledging service of documents. A pure mail-forwarding PO-box service generally does NOT meet this test. A staffed virtual office, an accountant's office, or a director's residential address typically does. Landlord LtdCos using PO-box-only registered offices need to reroute. The qualitative shift: registered office is now a meaningful address, not just a postal cipher.)
6. **What does the lawful purposes statement actually mean — is it a meaningful obligation?** (The lawful purposes statement (ECCTA ss.59-63, effective 5 March 2024) requires the confirmation statement to include a positive declaration that the company's intended future activities are lawful. Typically a tick-box on the online filing. The qualitative shift: the company now annually attests to a positive lawfulness state, rather than the previous passive position of just filing data. The substantive value depends on enforcement — false statements are an offence under the existing CA 2006 framework, so the attestation creates a documented record that can support fraud or false-statements proceedings if subsequent investigation discovers unlawful activity. For honest companies, it is a tick-box; for dishonest companies, it is an evidentiary artefact.)
7. **Why did Companies House move to a gatekeeper model?** (Policy driver in one paragraph: the post-Russian-sanctions + post-Pandora-Papers economic-crime policy environment surfaced the gap between the UK's open business register (low-friction, light-touch) and the demand for register integrity (counterparty due diligence, sanctions enforcement, beneficial-ownership transparency). ECTEA 2022 (RoE — overseas-entity beneficial-ownership register for UK property) addressed the overseas-entity gap; ECCTA 2023 addressed the domestic-company gap. The Registrar's new objectives at s.1 are the policy backbone: the register has to be accurate, complete, and not facilitating unlawful activity. The ID verification gate is the operational manifestation of those objectives.)
8. **What does the tightening mean for the PSC chain specifically?** (Three changes work together: (a) ID verification for PSCs under ECCTA s.64 — every PSC is now identity-verified; (b) abolition of local PSC registers under ss.51-52 — PSC information lives only at Companies House central register; (c) the verified-identity material itself is largely withheld from public inspection under s.69 (the public register shows the fact of verification, not the underlying documents). Combined effect: PSC chain is harder to obscure (no more "local register only" workaround), more discoverable (centralised), and more credible (verified). For landlord LtdCo portfolios with multi-layer PSC chains (holding company + subsidiaries), the chain transparency is a step-change.)
9. **What does the tightening mean for advisers — what are the new advice points?** (Advice-point shifts: (a) advise clients to inventory natural persons across the portfolio and book ID verification in Q1 of the transition window, not Q4 — verification queues + ACSP capacity can constrain near deadlines; (b) advise clients to audit registered offices for appropriate-address compliance and reroute where needed; (c) advise clients that the lawful purposes statement is an evidentiary artefact, not a passive tick-box, and to ensure their intended future activities are accurately stated; (d) advise clients that the ACSP route via the accountant (where the accountant is AML-supervised and ACSP-registered) often offers operational efficiency for multi-SPV portfolios; (e) advise clients that the per-natural-person mechanic means coordinated portfolio-wide verification is cheaper than fragmented per-SPV approach.)
10. **What about overseas directors of UK companies — does the tightening reach them?** (Yes — the regime applies to directors and PSCs regardless of residence. An overseas director of a UK company is subject to the same ID verification requirement, the same per-natural-person personal code under ECCTA s.68, and the same 12-month transition window. The qualitative shift for overseas directors is more acute than for UK residents because previously there was no document-based identity check at all (just name + service address + date of birth supplied at appointment); now there is a biometric / document-based verification via GOV.UK One Login app. Sibling MW2 B-page `how-us-based-directors-of-uk-companies-can-navigate-ecctas-new-identity-verification-rules` covers the overseas-director angle in depth.)
11. **Is the tightening one-off, or is more coming?** (More is coming. ECCTA Part 2 (Limited Partnership reforms) is phased rollout — separate from Part 1 commencement chain, but the same policy logic (registered office, annual confirmation, GP identity verification, striking-off powers). A later phase no earlier than November 2026 will bring filers + corporate-officer scenarios into ID verification scope. The Registrar's new objectives at s.1 are a continuing operational mandate, not a one-off legislative event; the Companies House Enforcement Performance Report will reveal the enforcement perimeter over time. Readers should expect the gatekeeper posture to be operationally exercised across years, not just at the commencement dates.)
12. **What is the qualitative point in one sentence?** (One-sentence framing: the Companies House register has moved from a low-friction recording instrument to a register-integrity gatekeeper, and the ID verification gate is the most visible operational manifestation of that move — for landlord LtdCo portfolios that means every natural person needs a personal code, every SPV needs an appropriate-address registered office and a registered email, every confirmation statement needs the lawful purposes attestation + verified personal codes for every in-scope role, and the file as a whole now reads to counterparties as a curated and verified record rather than a passive deposit.)
