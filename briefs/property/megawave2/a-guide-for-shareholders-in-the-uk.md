## Worked-example data (RUN session uses these as canvas)

### Example 1 — Single dividend declaration, basic-rate shareholder

- **Anil Patel** (basic-rate taxpayer, all other income £30,000 salary) is sole shareholder of his BTL SPV Patel Property Holdings Ltd. The company declares a **£10,000 dividend** in 2026/27.
- **Tax computation:** £500 dividend allowance covers the first £500 → £0 tax. Remaining £9,500 falls within the basic-rate band → £9,500 × **10.75%** = **£1,021.25** dividend tax. Net dividend received: £8,978.75.
- **Net-of-CT, net-of-personal-tax position:** assuming the company earned £10,000 profit, paid £1,900 SPR (19%) CT + then distributed £8,100 → wait, the £10,000 dividend draws from already-CT-paid retained earnings. RUN session frames as gross-up: company needs £12,346 pre-CT profit → £2,346 SPR CT → £10,000 distributable → £1,021 dividend tax → £8,979 net to Anil. Effective tax cost 27.3% of pre-CT profit.

### Example 2 — Higher-rate + additional-rate shareholder bands

- **Meera Patel** (higher-rate taxpayer, salary £70,000) receives a £30,000 dividend in 2026/27.
- £500 dividend allowance covers the first £500. The remaining £29,500 falls wholly within the higher-rate band (£70,000 salary already pushes her above the basic-rate threshold of £50,270). Tax = £29,500 × **35.75%** = **£10,546.25**.
- **Contrast:** had Meera been an additional-rate taxpayer (income above £125,140), the £29,500 dividend tax would be £29,500 × **39.35%** = £11,608.25. RUN session uses this contrast to show the value of alphabet-share allocation between basic-rate and higher-rate spouses where the income-shifting is genuine (not settlements-caught).

### Example 3 — Alphabet shares + Jones v Garnett discipline

- **Mr Patel** (additional-rate taxpayer) owns 100% of an SPV. He gifts 50% to **Mrs Patel** (basic-rate taxpayer, no other income). Both share classes are full ordinary shares with equal voting + dividend + capital rights.
- This is a **complete spouse gift** of substantive shareholding (capital + dividend + voting). It falls within the **s.626 spouse exception** to the s.624 settlements legislation (*Jones v Garnett* anchor): the spouse exception applies because the gift is an "outright gift between spouses" that is NOT "wholly or substantially a right to income" and NOT settlor-interested.
- **Critical:** RUN session must NOT generalise this to ANY share gift. Where the gift is preference shares (income-only, no capital growth) or non-voting income-only shares, the s.626 exception fails and s.624 attributes the dividend back to the settlor. The Arctic Systems facts pivoted on ORDINARY share gifts to a spouse who participated in the business; HMRC accepts the spouse exception applies in those facts but contests where the structure looks designed to shift only the income stream.

### Example 4 — Share disposal CGT mechanics

- **Mrs Kapoor** disposes of her 100% shareholding in her BTL SPV to her adult son for £200,000 (market value).
- Connected-party transfer under TCGA 1992 s.17: deemed disposal at market value regardless of actual consideration. £200,000 deemed proceeds; original share base cost £100 (the original IN01 issued capital).
- **Gain:** £199,900 (subject to annual exempt amount £3,000 — verify at write time). Tax at **24%** on the residential-element gain (property SPV with residential lets is treated as a residential gain for shareholder-disposal purposes — RUN session verifies via TCGA + HMRC CG note at write time).
- **Holdover under s.165 NOT available** because investment-company shares (CIHC-style) do NOT qualify; this is the §21.5 lock. Mrs Kapoor cannot defer the gain via holdover into her son's hands.

### Example 5 — IHT framework on shareholder death

- **Mr Singh** dies holding 100% of his BTL property-investment SPV (market value £1.2m at death).
- **BPR fails** because the company is "wholly or mainly" an investment business under IHTA 1984 s.105(3) (*Pawson* anchor at §22.1).
- **Full £1.2m in estate** for IHT purposes. NRB £325,000 + RNRB £175,000 (if available) = £500,000 nil-rate sum. Remaining £700,000 charged at 40% = £280,000 IHT.
- Compare a trading-company holding: had Mr Singh held shares in a trading family business of the same value, BPR at 50% or 100% would have applied (subject to the April 2026 £1m combined BPR/APR cap per §15.4). RUN session uses this contrast to frame why FIC + growth-share design exists — to transfer growth value as PETs out of the estate during life, rather than relying on BPR.

## FAQ expansion (RUN session polishes prose; 10-12 FAQs target)

1. **Q: What rights does a shareholder have in a UK property company?**
   A: Shareholder rights derive from the Companies Act 2006 + the company's articles of association. The core rights are: (a) voting at general meetings (one vote per share for ordinary shares; varies by class); (b) receiving declared dividends; (c) capital distribution on winding-up; (d) pre-emption rights on new issues (CA 2006 ss.561-577, may be disapplied); (e) information rights to annual accounts + confirmation statement extracts; (f) the right to apply to court for relief from unfair prejudice under s.994. Family property SPVs often customise these defaults via bespoke articles — for example, restricting share transfers without board consent.

2. **Q: How are dividends from a property SPV taxed for 2026/27?**
   A: Each individual shareholder gets a £500 dividend allowance. Above that, dividends are taxed at 10.75% (basic rate), 35.75% (higher rate), or 39.35% (additional rate) for 2026/27. The rates were raised by 2 percentage points at the basic and higher bands from 6 April 2026 (FA 2026). The company has already paid Corporation Tax on the profit before declaring the dividend, so the effective combined rate depends on the SPV's CT band (19% SPR / 25% main / 26.5% marginal). A basic-rate shareholder taking dividend out of small-profits-rate retained earnings sees a roughly 27% combined effective rate; an additional-rate shareholder taking dividend out of main-rate retained earnings sees a roughly 54% combined rate.

3. **Q: How do alphabet share classes work, and where do they help with family-LLP / SPV planning?**
   A: Alphabet share classes (A / B / C ordinary shares) are separate share classes with the same nominal capital but each can have its own dividend declared at a different rate. This lets the company declare £20,000 to A shares (held by Mrs A, basic-rate taxpayer) and £0 to B shares (held by Mr B, additional-rate taxpayer) in a given year, then flip the allocation the next year. The structure is lawful under CA 2006 + the company's articles. The tax effect is constrained by the settlements legislation at ITTOIA 2005 s.624: HMRC may attribute dividends back to the original settlor where the gift to the recipient is "wholly or substantially a right to income". The Arctic Systems case (*Jones v Garnett*) confirmed the spouse exception at s.626 protects outright gifts of ordinary shares between spouses, but not narrow income-only structures.

4. **Q: What is the settlements legislation, and when does it bite on a family company's dividend allocation?**
   A: The settlements legislation at ITTOIA 2005 s.624 attributes income arising under a "settlement" back to the settlor for tax purposes where the settlor retains an interest. HMRC interprets "settlement" broadly to include gifts of shares that are substantially a right to income, transfers to minor children, and arrangements where the settlor retains economic benefit. The s.626 spouse exception protects outright gifts to a spouse (per *Jones v Garnett*) provided the gift is not "wholly or substantially a right to income". Outright gifts to adult children also fall outside s.624 because the donor does not retain an interest. Gifts to minor children always trigger s.629 attribution to the parent until the child reaches 18.

5. **Q: What is the difference between a gift of shares to a spouse, an adult child, and a minor child for tax purposes?**
   A: Spouse: protected by the s.626 spouse exception (ITTOIA 2005) per *Jones v Garnett* provided the gift is of substantive shares (not just income rights). No CGT on the gift between spouses under TCGA 1992 s.58 no-gain-no-loss rule. Adult child (18+): the gift is a disposal at market value under TCGA s.17 connected-party rules, so CGT arises on the donor. The donor does not retain an interest, so s.624 does not attribute the child's future dividends. Minor child: ITTOIA s.629 attributes income to the parent until the child reaches 18, but CGT still arises on the donor at market value. Holdover relief under TCGA 1992 s.165 is not available for shares in an investment company (property-investment SPVs fail per §21.5 lock).

6. **Q: How are growth shares and preference shares different from ordinary shares in a property SPV?**
   A: Growth shares entitle the holder to a share of capital growth above a defined "hurdle" value, but no dividend or voting rights below the hurdle. They are useful in FIC estate-planning because the founder retains current value (and votes) on founder shares while gifting growth shares to children at a low PET-value (often nil at issue, growing as the property portfolio appreciates). Preference shares carry a fixed dividend (e.g., 6% of nominal value annually) but typically no capital growth and limited voting rights. They are useful for "income shares" given to retired-parent shareholders who need predictable income, while growth shares accrue to working-generation shareholders. Both classes are validly issued under CA 2006 Part 17 provided the articles permit them.

7. **Q: What CGT applies on disposing of shares in a property company?**
   A: A share disposal is a CGT event under TCGA 1992 s.4. For connected-party transfers (to family members), s.17 deems the disposal at market value regardless of actual consideration. Gains are taxed at 18% / 24% (the residential-rate stack from 6 April 2026 — though gains on company shares themselves are not residential property gains directly; the look-through rule for "residential property rich" companies applies in specific NRCGT contexts). Holdover relief under s.165 is NOT available for shares in an investment company per §21.5, which means most property-investment SPVs cannot defer the gain into the recipient's hands. The annual exempt amount applies (verify current figure at write time).

8. **Q: What IHT applies on shares in a property company held at death?**
   A: Shares in a property-investment company fail BPR per IHTA 1984 s.105(3) and the *Pawson v HMRC* [2013] UKUT 050 decision: the business is "wholly or mainly" one of making or holding investments. The full market value of the shareholding goes into the deceased's estate at the 40% IHT rate (above NRB + RNRB). This is the structural reason family-investment companies (FICs) use lifetime growth-share gifting (PETs out of estate over 7 years) rather than relying on BPR. The April 2026 £1m combined BPR/APR cap (per §15.4) further restricts trading-business BPR; investment-company shares are wholly outside BPR regardless of the cap.

9. **Q: What is a shareholder agreement, and how does it relate to the articles of association?**
   A: The articles of association are the public constitutional document of the company, registered at Companies House and binding on the company + all members. A shareholder agreement is a private contract between specified shareholders (and sometimes the company) that supplements the articles with confidential provisions: drag-along / tag-along rights, deadlock-resolution mechanisms, restricted-transfer rules, founder reserved-matters lists, exit / pre-emption mechanics. Where the two conflict on the same point, court resolution depends on each document's wording; well-drafted documents avoid conflicts. Family property SPVs often have lighter shareholder agreements than VC-funded companies, but a written agreement is still worthwhile if multiple unrelated shareholders are involved.

10. **Q: What information must a property company give its shareholders annually?**
    A: Annual accounts (CA 2006 Part 15 + ECCTA-amended filing windows), the confirmation statement filed at Companies House (ss.853A-K as amended by ECCTA Part 1 + the lawful purposes statement attested each year per §11.A), and dividend vouchers under ITTOIA 2005 for each dividend declared (showing the date, amount, and tax credit). PSC information remains current on the public register. Shareholders can request copies of the company's register of members and inspect them at the registered office on payment of a small statutory fee.

11. **Q: What protection does a minority shareholder have in a family property SPV?**
    A: CA 2006 s.994 (unfair prejudice petition) provides relief where the company's affairs are conducted in a manner unfairly prejudicial to a member or members. Typical fact patterns: a majority shareholder excludes a minority from board decisions, declares dividends only to specific share classes the minority doesn't hold, transfers company property to connected parties at undervalue. The court has broad remedial powers including share purchase orders. CA 2006 s.122(1)(g) of the Insolvency Act 1986 gives an alternative just-and-equitable winding-up petition where the relationship between shareholders has broken down. Family property SPVs frequently hit these provisions when adult-child shareholders disagree with founder-parent dividend strategies.

12. **Q: How does this page differ from `salary-vs-dividends-property-spv-2026-27-marginal-rate-analysis`?**
    A: That page is the **director-side extraction-route comparison**: it walks through whether to extract retained earnings as salary, dividend, or director's loan repayment, with worked numbers across rate bands. This page is the **shareholder-side rights + tax + governance primer**: who has what entitlements, how dividends received are taxed, what happens on share gift / death, and what the legal architecture is for differential allocations between family members. The two pages are complementary: the director-side page is about HOW MUCH to extract; this page is about who has rights to extract and how those rights are configured at IN01 + articles stage.
