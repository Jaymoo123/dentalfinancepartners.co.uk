# Limited Companies and BTL Properties: The Operational Handbook for Running a BTL LtdCo (2026/27)

## Statutory anchor

- **Primary (the company entity):** Companies Act 2006 (CA 2006) — body-corporate framework. Cross-reference A20 (M2-A-B2) `limited-companies` pillar for entity overview; this page does NOT restate CA 2006 architecture, it stands on it.
- **Primary (corporation tax on property income):** CTA 2009 Part 4 (UK property businesses) ss.202-291 — corporation-tax treatment of UK property income. **Operative for BTL LtdCo:** property rental profits taxed as a single UK property business (s.205); loan-relationship interest fully deductible under CTA 2009 Part 5 (no Section 24 restriction — that is ITTOIA 2005 s.272A for individuals only). Cross-reference existing `section-24-vs-incorporation-which-saves-more-tax`.
- **Primary (CT rate stack):** CTA 2010 s.3 + s.18 + ss.18A-J marginal relief — 19% small profits / 25% main / 26.5% marginal effective. **Critical interaction for BTL LtdCo groups:** associated-company count under s.18D-J dilutes the £50k / £250k thresholds across the group; a 5-SPV multi-property structure has thresholds of £10k / £50k per SPV (effective £50k group / £250k group divided by associated count = 5). Cross-reference A5 M2-A-B1 marginal-relief depth.
- **Primary (statutory accounts framework — the load-bearing operational layer):** CA 2006 Part 15 (ss.380-474) — accounts + reports framework. Small companies regime (ss.382-384) + micro-entity regime (s.384A-B per SI 2013/3008 implementation) — most single-SPV BTL LtdCos qualify as micro-entities or small companies. **Accounting standards fork — the operative day-one decision:**
  - **FRS 105 (Micro-entities):** simplest; no fair-value option; investment property held at historical cost less depreciation; NO deferred tax on fair-value revaluations (because no fair-value movements). Most appropriate for single-SPV BTL LtdCos prioritising filing simplicity. FRS 105 abridged accounts; minimal disclosures.
  - **FRS 102 Section 16 (Investment Property — Fair Value Model):** investment property held at fair value through profit or loss; revaluation gains/losses recognised in P&L; **CT consequences** — fair-value revaluation gains are NOT chargeable to CT (TCGA 1992 chargeable-gains framework applies only on actual disposal); but the accounting P&L includes the revaluation movement, creating a book-vs-tax timing difference; deferred tax provision required per FRS 102 Section 29 on the gain. Suitable for BTL LtdCos where stakeholders (lenders, investors, valuers) want real-time NAV.
  - **FRS 102 Section 17 (Property, Plant and Equipment):** alternative treatment treating the property as PPE held at cost less depreciation OR at revaluation through OCI. Rare for pure BTL portfolios; more common for owner-occupied or mixed-use.
- **Primary (the accounting-standards selection decision):** the company can elect either FRS 105 or FRS 102 at first accounts; switching subsequently requires comparatives restatement and is operationally costly. **The operative decision factors:** lender requirements (some commercial BTL lenders require FRS 102 with fair-value model for portfolio valuation); investor / co-investor visibility needs; growth path (FRS 102 is more flexible if the company will exceed micro thresholds in 1-2 years; FRS 105 forces a regime change at that point).
- **Primary (legal vs beneficial title — the day-one property transfer):** Land Registration Act 2002 + Companies Act 2006 + Trustee Act 2000 — the company holds **legal title** registered at HM Land Registry under its registered name + company number; **beneficial title** also vests in the company (a true conveyance to the company, not a bare-trust nomineeship, except where the company is itself a nominee — rare for BTL). The TR1 transfer + AP1 registration application names the company as transferee with company-number annotation. Cross-reference existing `how-to-transfer-property-into-limited-company-uk` for the transfer mechanics; this page covers the post-transfer holding mechanics.
- **Primary (mortgage charge registration — the lender side):** Companies Act 2006 ss.859A-859Q (registration of charges) — any mortgage granted by the company over its property MUST be registered at Companies House within 21 days of creation (s.859A); failure means the charge is void against the liquidator + creditors (s.859H). **Operative for BTL LtdCo:** Form MR01 filed within 21 days of completion; £15 paper fee or £10 online fee per charge (RUN session verifies current fee schedule at write time per §16.27 rate-by-reference discipline). **Common landlord trap:** non-bank lenders (private finance, bridging) sometimes overlook the 21-day window — the company is required to file even if the lender drops the ball. Land Registry side: Form CH1 registers the lender's charge against the title at HMLR (separate registration; both required).
- **Primary (the company-side ATED overlay):** FA 2013 ss.94-174 — Annual Tax on Enveloped Dwellings. Applies to companies (and partnerships with at least one corporate member) holding single dwellings valued > £500,000. **Operative reliefs for typical BTL LtdCo:** property-rental-business relief (s.133 — covers LtdCo letting to third parties at arm's length); property-developers relief (s.138); property-traders relief (s.139). ATED return required even when relief claimed (NIL return). Cross-reference §18 ATED Wave 3 extension + existing ATED-specific pages.
- **Primary (the directors' loan / DLA treatment for intercompany funding):** CTA 2010 ss.455-464A — loans to participators. **Critical for BTL LtdCo growth path:** common pattern is for the founder-director to lend the company the deposit + working capital from personal funds; the loan to director sits as a creditor in the company accounts (NOT an s.455 trigger because money flows FROM director TO company); subsequent repayments to director from rental cashflow are NOT taxable income (return of capital). **The s.455 trap fires the other way:** company makes loan TO participator (the director) — **35.75% s.455 charge** for loans made on or after 6 April 2026 (per F-9 §21.1 lock; rate references ITA 2007 s.8(2) dividend upper rate substituted by FA 2026 s.4(1)(b); 33.75% for loans made before 6 April 2026) on the loan balance outstanding 9 months and 1 day after year-end. Cross-reference A19 M2-A-B1 `directors-loan-accountsdla-uk-guide` depth. **NOTE on §21.1 lock:** ss.464C/D OMITTED by FA 2025 s.81(3)(b)-(4) from 30 October 2024 (per F-7 Wave 6 lock); residual anti-avoidance is s.464A only.
- **Primary (year-end + accounting reference date):** CA 2006 ss.391-392 — first accounting reference date (last day of month of incorporation anniversary by default); changing ARD (s.392 — shorter or longer with restrictions). **Operative for BTL LtdCo:** changing year-end can be used strategically for tax planning (defer profits into next CT period, accelerate losses, align with property-purchase timing for first-year capital allowances on integral features). Cross-reference existing `btl-limited-company-year-end-date-changing-tax-planning`.
- **Primary (intercompany / intra-group transfers):** TCGA 1992 s.171 (no-gain-no-loss intra-group transfers — 75% group test); CTA 2010 s.131 (basic CT group relief test per §21.8.1 lock); FA 2003 Sch 7 (SDLT group relief) — operative when re-housing a property between SPVs in a HoldCo group. Cross-reference §21.8 MW2 multi-company group operation lock + existing `multi-company-group-extraction-spv-holding-co-dividend-conduit-mechanics` + `sdlt-group-relief-schedule-7-fa-2003-claw-back-connected-party-recovery-depth`.
- **Primary (the disposal / exit framework):** TCGA 1992 ss.1-2 (CT on chargeable gains for companies — no AEA, no indexation post-2017). **Operative for BTL LtdCo exit:**
  - **Asset sale (company sells property):** chargeable gain at company level; CT at 19% / 25% / 26.5% effective; net proceeds inside company; subsequent extraction faces dividend layer (51.8% combined for HRT individual under current 25% CT + 35.75% dividend).
  - **Share sale (founder sells shares to acquirer):** CGT at individual level; BADR potentially available IF the company is a trading company (most BTL investment LtdCos are NOT trading — TCGA 1992 s.165A definition; BADR usually unavailable). Investor's relief (TCGA 1992 s.169VC+) similarly trading-only. RUN session verifies current BADR rates per FA 2024 s.10 increases at write time.
  - **Voluntary Striking-Off (Companies Act 2006 ss.1003-1011):** Form DS01 if company has not traded for 3 months + meets conditions; distributions to shareholders pre-strike-off treated as capital under CTA 2010 s.1030A if ≤ £25,000 (otherwise full distribution treatment per s.1000). Limited use for BTL LtdCo — typically requires asset sale first.
  - **Members' Voluntary Liquidation (MVL):** IA 1986 Pt IV Ch III — distributions in liquidation treated as capital (s.122 IA 1986 + Sch B1 + TCGA 1992 s.165 / s.169LA BADR by analogy where eligible). Common exit route for retained-profit BTL LtdCo. **Anti-phoenix TAAR: ITTOIA 2005 s.396B** (inserted by F(No.2)A 2016 Sch 1 para 11 — NOT ITA 2007 s.396B per Stage 1 seed; corrected at §16.40 Wave 6 pattern, see F-11 flag this batch). Caught if same/similar trade resumed within 2 years post-liquidation; operates as anti-avoidance against MVL-then-reincorporate income-tax sidestep. Cross-reference §28 transactions-in-UK-land + trading-vs-investment Wave 8 cluster for related anti-avoidance.
- **Primary (post-ECCTA Companies House overlay):** ECCTA 2023 Part 1 (per §11.A lock) — registered email, appropriate registered office address, confirmation statement, PSC register, ID verification (mandatory for new appointments from 18 November 2025). Cross-reference §11.A + A20 pillar §11.A coverage; this page does NOT restate ECCTA framework but cross-links.
- **House position reference:** §21 (Ltd Co + FIC family — DLA + share-class + rent-charging + salary-vs-dividends + FIC); §21.4 (rate stack with F-20 dividend rate correction); §21.1 (s.455 / s.464A loan-to-participator); §21.8 (multi-company group operation — MW2 mini-lock); §11 + §11.A (Companies House + ECCTA); §1.M (post-MDR-abolition SDLT); §18 (ATED Wave 3 extension); §28 (transactions in UK land — Wave 8). **No NEW HP lock requested by THIS pick** — accounts-standards-selection mechanics (FRS 105 vs FRS 102 fair-value model) are accountancy-technical rather than HP-locked tax positions; existing locks plus operative FRS guidance suffice.

## Worked-example data (RUN session uses these as canvas)

### Example 1 — FRS 105 vs FRS 102 fair-value comparative

**Singh Properties Ltd** purchased a BTL property for £300,000 in Year 1. Valued at £360,000 by Year 3.

- **FRS 105 (micro-entity):** balance sheet still shows £300,000 cost (less negligible depreciation on integral features); P&L shows no fair-value movement; no deferred tax. **Net effect:** simple presentation; CT impact only on actual disposal (TCGA 1992 chargeable-gains framework triggered at sale).
- **FRS 102 Section 16 fair-value model:** balance sheet shows £360,000; P&L shows £60,000 fair-value gain in Year 3; **deferred tax provision £15,000** (25% main rate × £60,000 gain — RUN session verifies if 19% small profits rate applies); NAV uplift £45,000 net for lender / investor visibility. **CT side:** the £60k accounting gain is NOT chargeable to CT until actual disposal under TCGA 1992. The deferred-tax provision is a book entry recognising the future CT liability that will crystallise on sale.
- **Operative insight:** FRS 102 gives the lender real-time NAV but pays for it with deferred-tax presentation overhead. FRS 105 is simpler for single-SPV BTLs but locks the company into cost-model presentation (sometimes a problem if a commercial lender later requires fair-value accounts).
- **Switching cost:** changing FRS 105 → FRS 102 in Year 5 requires retrospective restatement of comparative figures; auditor / accountant fees typically £2,000-£5,000 plus deferred-tax re-presentation work.

### Example 2 — DLA-IN to DLA-OUT flip (s.455 trap)

**Singh Properties Ltd** founder Mr Singh lends the company £50,000 (deposit + working capital) from personal funds at incorporation. The DLA shows **credit balance £50,000** = company owes Mr Singh £50k. No s.455 trigger (money flows FROM director TO company).

- **Year 2 cashflow drawdown:** rental cashflow returns £40,000 to Mr Singh as repayment of DLA — tax-free return of capital. DLA reduces to £10,000 credit.
- **Year 3 trap moment:** Mr Singh draws £40,000 from the company bank account for a personal car purchase, intending to "repay later from next year's rental". DLA balance flips: £10,000 credit + £40,000 drawdown = **£30,000 DEBIT balance** (director now owes the company).
- **s.455 trigger:** if the £30,000 debit balance is outstanding 9 months + 1 day after year-end, s.455 charge fires at **35.75%** (loan made post-6-April-2026 per F-9 §21.1 / 33.75% pre-6-April-2026) = **£10,725 payable by company to HMRC** with the next CT600.
- **Recovery:** when Mr Singh repays the £30,000 within the 4-year window per s.458, the company reclaims the £10,725 from HMRC.
- **Plus BIK trap:** the £30,000 interest-free loan exceeds £10,000 threshold under ITEPA 2003 s.175; BIK on the official rate of interest (~2-3.75% recent — RUN session verifies current HMRC official rate at write time) reportable on P11D.
- **Operational cost:** £10,725 cashflow tied up for typically 1-3 years + P11D + Class 1A NIC at 15% on the BIK. The "repay later" assumption costs real money in the interim.

### Example 3 — Mortgage charge MR01 missed window

**Kapoor Properties Ltd** refinances a £200,000 BTL mortgage on 1 June 2026 with a private bridging lender. The lender's solicitor files Form CH1 at HMLR same day but does NOT prompt Companies House for MR01.

- **The 21-day window:** CA 2006 s.859A — charge must be registered at Companies House within 21 days of creation (1 June + 21 days = 22 June 2026).
- **The trap:** Kapoor Properties Ltd is the filer, not the lender. Lender's solicitor's omission does not transfer responsibility.
- **Outside window (post-22 June):** s.859F application to court for late registration; court order required; legal fees typically £2,000-£4,000; charge re-registered with court order date as registration date (NOT loan creation date).
- **Worst case (insolvency before late-registration order):** under s.859H the unregistered charge is **void against the liquidator and other creditors**. The lender becomes an UNSECURED creditor in insolvency. The lender chases the company / director on the personal guarantee instead.
- **Operative defence:** Kapoor Properties Ltd checks the Companies House Beta + MR01 system within 3 days of completion, verifies the entry, and chases the lender's solicitor if absent. £10 online fee per charge (RUN session verifies current fee at write time).

### Example 4 — Multi-SPV marginal-relief dilution

**Patel Group Ltd** is a HoldCo with 5 SPV subsidiaries (each holding 2-3 properties). Aggregate group profit £200,000; aggregate group interest £100,000.

- **Single LtdCo counterfactual:** £200k profit; small profits threshold £50k; marginal relief band £50k-£250k. CT calculation: 19% × £50k = £9,500 + 26.5% × £150k = £39,750 = **£49,250 total**.
- **5-SPV actual structure:** each SPV is an associated company per CTA 2010 ss.18D-J. Thresholds divided by 5: each SPV gets £10k small-profits threshold + £50k upper threshold. Each SPV profit £40k → falls in marginal-relief band. Per SPV: 19% × £10k = £1,900 + 26.5% × £30k = £7,950 = £9,850. Aggregate: **5 × £9,850 = £49,250** — same as single-LtdCo counterfactual.
- **Operative insight:** group structuring does NOT escape associated-company marginal relief gating. The multi-SPV structure is for asset-protection / lender-segregation / exit-flexibility / group-relief loss-sharing, NOT for CT-rate optimisation. RUN session must hold this honestly.
- **Where the structure DOES pay off:** if one SPV makes a loss (e.g. refurb capex year, £40k loss), it can surrender the loss to the others under CTA 2010 group relief framework (cross-link to A8 M2-A-B2 + §21.8). The £40k loss × 26.5% = £10,600 CT saving for the group. Multi-SPV pays off when losses + profits can be netted across the group.

### Example 5 — Exit decision tree (DS01 vs MVL vs share sale)

**Mawell Estate Ltd** is a 4-property BTL LtdCo. Mr Mawell (sole director / shareholder) wants to retire and extract value at age 65. Three exit routes compared:

- **Route A: Asset sale + DS01 voluntary striking-off** — company sells all 4 properties; CT on chargeable gains at 25%; net proceeds inside company. If net assets ≤ £25,000 post-corporation-tax + post-creditor-settlement, DS01 + CTA 2010 s.1030A capital distribution treatment. Above £25k, full distribution treatment per s.1000 (taxed as dividend at 39.35% additional rate if Mr Mawell is ART).
- **Route B: Full MVL with capital distribution** — appoint insolvency practitioner; company sells assets; surplus distributed as capital under IA 1986 Pt IV Ch III + TCGA 1992 framework. CGT at individual level (24% post-FA 2024 for residential gains). **Anti-phoenix TAAR ITTOIA 2005 s.396B** — caught if Mr Mawell restarts property investment trade within 2 years; capital distribution recharacterised as dividend income.
- **Route C: Share sale (sell company shares to acquirer)** — CGT at individual level on share-disposal gain; BADR usually UNAVAILABLE (BTL investment LtdCo not a trading company per TCGA 1992 s.165A); no double-layer CT + distribution; cleanest exit if a buyer exists for the LtdCo + portfolio at a price reflecting the underlying property values + tax history.
- **Operative comparison:** Route A loses double-tax (CT then distribution); only viable for low-distribution scenarios. Route B is cleaner for high-distribution but needs MVL professional fees (£3,000-£5,000) and 2-year forward planning for anti-phoenix TAAR. Route C is cleanest if a buyer exists; rare in BTL but more common for HMO + commercial LtdCos.

## FAQ expansion (RUN session polishes prose; 12 FAQs target)

1. **Q: Should a BTL limited company use FRS 105 or FRS 102?**
   A: The default for most single-SPV BTL LtdCos is FRS 105 (micro-entity). It is simpler, requires less disclosure, and avoids the deferred-tax presentation overhead of FRS 102 Section 16. FRS 102 with the fair-value model is more appropriate where (a) a commercial lender requires fair-value accounts for portfolio valuation; (b) the company is growing toward the FRS 105 size thresholds and will need to switch within 1-2 years; or (c) investors / co-investors want real-time NAV visibility. Once FRS 102 fair-value is elected, fair-value gains and losses run through the P&L each year and a deferred-tax provision is required under FRS 102 Section 29 (25% main rate on each fair-value gain) — but the gain is NOT chargeable to corporation tax until actual disposal under TCGA 1992.

2. **Q: What happens if I miss the 21-day Form MR01 deadline for registering a charge?**
   A: Under CA 2006 s.859A, the company must register a charge at Companies House within 21 days of creation. Miss the deadline and the charge is **void against the liquidator and other creditors** under s.859H. The lender's mortgage exists as a contract between lender and company but is unenforceable against other creditors in insolvency — effectively making the lender unsecured. The lender will then pursue any personal guarantee. The remedy for late registration is an application to court under s.859F; the court can order late registration but the legal fees typically run to £2,000-£4,000. Best practice: check the Companies House register within a few days of completion and chase the lender's solicitor if the MR01 entry is absent. Form CH1 at HMLR is a separate registration; both must be filed.

3. **Q: How are director loans IN to a BTL LtdCo treated?**
   A: When the founder-director lends money TO the company (typical pattern at incorporation: the founder funds the deposit + working capital from personal sources), the DLA shows a credit balance (the company owes the director). This is NOT an s.455 trigger because the money flows from director to company. The credit balance can be repaid tax-free as a return of capital — the company pays the director back from rental cashflow, and the director does not declare the repayments as taxable income. **However:** if the company pays interest on the credit balance to the director, the interest is taxable income on the director (declared via SA100 savings income) and deductible by the company within the CTA 2010 s.453 close-company rules. Most BTL LtdCos run DLA credit balances interest-free to avoid the interest-side tax mechanics.

4. **Q: What is the s.455 charge, and when does it bite?**
   A: CTA 2010 s.455 is the close-company loan-to-participator charge. When the company lends money TO a participator (a shareholder, director, or associate), and the loan is outstanding 9 months and 1 day after the company's accounting reference date, the company pays a tax charge at **35.75%** (for loans made on or after 6 April 2026 per F-9 §21.1; 33.75% for loans made before that date). The rate references the dividend upper rate at ITA 2007 s.8(2) — FA 2026 s.4(1)(b) substituted the rate to 35.75% from 2026/27 onwards. The s.455 charge is recoverable under s.458 when the loan is repaid (typically claimed via CT600 within 4 years of repayment).

5. **Q: Can a BTL LtdCo avoid corporation tax by reinvesting rental profits?**
   A: No. CT is charged on the company's taxable profits as they arise, not on extraction. A BTL LtdCo with £20,000 of taxable profit (after deductible interest under CTA 2009 Part 5) pays CT on the £20,000 in that period regardless of whether profits are distributed, retained, or reinvested in another property. Reinvestment may produce capital allowances or deductible expenses in future periods, but it does not defer or reduce the current period's CT liability. The advantage of retention vs extraction is the avoidance of the dividend-tax layer (10.75%/35.75%/39.35% from 6 April 2026 per §21.4) — retained profits stay in the company at the CT rate alone; extracted profits face the additional dividend layer.

6. **Q: What is the ATED return obligation for a BTL LtdCo?**
   A: Under FA 2013 ss.94-174, a company holding a single residential dwelling valued above £500,000 must file an ATED return annually. Most BTL LtdCos qualify for one of three reliefs — property-rental-business relief (s.133, the most common), property-developers relief (s.138), or property-traders relief (s.139). **Critically, the relief must be claimed via a NIL ATED return — the obligation to file is not waived even where no ATED is payable.** Penalties for late filing apply even on NIL returns. The return period runs 1 April to 31 March; relief-claim NIL returns are due by 30 April for the year ahead.

7. **Q: How does multi-SPV group operation work for property LtdCos?**
   A: Operative drivers: asset protection (separating properties into different SPVs so a problem at one does not infect the others); lender risk-segregation (lenders sometimes prefer per-property SPVs); exit flexibility (selling one SPV does not trigger sale of others); group relief loss-sharing (CTA 2010 group relief framework lets one SPV's loss reduce another SPV's CT). What multi-SPV does NOT do: avoid associated-company marginal-relief dilution. Under CTA 2010 ss.18D-J, all associated companies share the £50k and £250k thresholds divided by the count of associated companies. A 5-SPV group has effective thresholds of £10k and £50k per SPV. Multi-SPV pays off through group relief on loss years and through asset protection / exit flexibility, not through CT-rate arbitrage.

8. **Q: What does ECCTA 2023 mean for an existing BTL LtdCo?**
   A: ECCTA 2023 Part 1 (per §11.A Wave 9 lock) brought several operational obligations: (a) appropriate registered office (no PO Box-only addresses — CA 2006 ss.86-88 as amended); (b) registered email address (new CA 2006 s.88A+); (c) annual confirmation statement (CA 2006 ss.853A-853L); (d) PSC register (CA 2006 Part 21A); (e) mandatory ID verification for directors and PSCs — in force from 18 November 2025 for new appointments, with a 12-month transition window for existing officeholders. RUN session re-verifies current rollout status at the Companies House campaign page (`https://changestoukcompanylaw.campaign.gov.uk/`) at write time. Companies House has been issuing email reminders for ID verification since 2026; ignoring those reminders risks a strike-off action.

9. **Q: What is the difference between voluntary striking-off (DS01) and members' voluntary liquidation (MVL)?**
   A: DS01 (voluntary striking-off under CA 2006 ss.1003-1011) is a simple form-filing route — the company has not traded for 3 months, has dealt with all assets and creditors, and applies to be struck off the register. Distributions before strike-off are treated as capital under CTA 2010 s.1030A only if total distributions are ≤ £25,000; above that, full distribution treatment applies (taxed as dividend). MVL (Members' Voluntary Liquidation under IA 1986 Part IV Chapter III) is the formal liquidation route. An insolvency practitioner is appointed; the company sells its assets; the surplus is distributed as capital under TCGA 1992 framework. MVL is the cleaner route where distributions exceed £25k. **Anti-phoenix TAAR (ITTOIA 2005 s.396B) applies** — if the founder restarts the same or similar trade within 2 years, the MVL capital distribution is recharacterised as income at dividend rates. Forward planning before MVL is essential.

10. **Q: Does BADR (Business Asset Disposal Relief) apply to a BTL LtdCo share sale?**
    A: Usually not. BADR (formerly Entrepreneurs' Relief) requires the company to be a trading company per TCGA 1992 s.165A. A BTL LtdCo holding properties for rental income is an investment company, not a trading company, so BADR is unavailable on a share sale. Investor's relief (TCGA 1992 s.169VC onwards) is similarly limited to trading companies. The exception: a mixed-use or development-LtdCo with a substantial trading element may qualify; the trading-vs-investment line per CTA 2010 ss.1124-1126 is fact-intensive (cross-link to §28 trading-vs-investment cluster).

11. **Q: How are intra-group transfers of property treated for tax?**
    A: Two operative regimes: (a) **CT side** — TCGA 1992 s.171 treats intra-group transfers as no-gain-no-loss disposals between members of a 75% group. The transferring SPV does not crystallise CT on the disposal; the receiving SPV inherits the transferring SPV's base cost. (b) **SDLT side** — FA 2003 Sch 7 SDLT group relief (75% test, structurally distinct from CT 75% test per §21.8.2). Relief from SDLT on intra-group transfer is available, subject to a 3-year intra-group claw-back per Sch 7 para 3 (if the transferee or the transferring group leaves the group within 3 years, the relief unwinds and SDLT becomes payable). Cross-reference `sdlt-group-relief-schedule-7-fa-2003-claw-back-connected-party-recovery-depth`.

12. **Q: What are the most common operational mistakes BTL LtdCo founders make?**
    A: Six recurring traps. (1) Missing the s.859A 21-day MR01 window after refinancing — the company is the filer, not the lender, and missing the deadline puts the lender's charge at risk. (2) Treating director-loan-OUT as a tax-free extraction — s.455 fires at 35.75% on balances outstanding 9 months + 1 day after year-end, plus BIK on the interest-free element above £10k. (3) Electing FRS 102 fair-value without understanding the deferred-tax-on-revaluation overhead under Section 29. (4) Mixing personal and company bank accounts — opens director-DLA risk + audit-trail problems + s.455 if HMRC reclassifies personal drawings as participator loans. (5) Failing to file a NIL ATED return on a relief-eligible high-value dwelling — penalties apply to the late NIL return. (6) Failing to update Companies House for ECCTA-compliant registered email + appropriate registered office + ID verification when prompted — strike-off action follows persistent non-compliance.
