# Agricultural Property Relief for Inheritance Tax: Key Benefits for Mixed Property Estates

## Statutory anchor

- Primary: **IHTA 1984 Part V Chapter II ss.115-124C** (Agricultural Property Relief). Verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1984/51/section/115 (definition of agricultural property), /section/116 (rates), /section/117 (minimum periods) on 2026-05-26.
  - **s.115(2)** — definition of "agricultural property": agricultural land or pasture; woodland and any building used in connection with the intensive rearing of livestock or fish where occupying adjacent agricultural land; **cottages, farm buildings and farmhouses, together with the land occupied with them, as are of a character appropriate to the property**.
  - **s.115(3)** — definition of "agricultural value": the value the property would have if subject to a perpetual covenant prohibiting use otherwise than as agricultural property. Critical: APR applies to the AGRICULTURAL value only; any hope value (development potential, equestrian uplift, residential conversion value) is OUTSIDE the relief and falls back into the chargeable estate.
  - **s.116** — rate: 100% relief where the transferor has the right to vacant possession or to obtain it within 12 months OR the property is let on a tenancy beginning on or after 1 September 1995; 50% otherwise (pre-1 September 1995 let property held since before 10 March 1981 special category).
  - **s.117** — minimum periods: owner-occupied 2 years ending with the transfer date; let / non-owner-occupied 7 years ending with the transfer date (occupied by him or another for agricultural purposes throughout). (Wave 2 A10 brief erratum noted: brief said "5 years let"; statute is 7 — F-101 logged for completeness, brief-level only.)
  - **ss.118-124** — replacements (s.118), succession and aggregation (ss.119-121), companies-and-shares (s.122), occupied for agriculture by company controlled by transferor (s.123), retained vs let (s.124), woodlands (s.125).
  - **Sch A1 IHTA 1984 (extended from 6 April 2017)** — look-through for UK agricultural property held through overseas structures (extended to UK agricultural land in scope per Autumn Budget 2025 announcement; see §15.4 last paragraph).
- Supporting (the April 2026 cap):
  - **FA 2025 / FA 2026 architecture** for the £1 million combined BPR + APR cap. Locked at house position §15.4 (verified by Wave 4 / Wave 5 prep against the gov.uk APR/BPR reforms summary publication): from 6 April 2026, the previously unlimited 100% rate is capped at £1m combined per estate; above the cap, 50% relief applies (giving an effective 20% IHT rate on the above-cap qualifying value).
  - Anti-forestalling rule: lifetime transfers on or after 30 October 2024 are caught if the donor dies on or after 6 April 2026 within 7 years of the gift.
  - Trust anti-fragmentation: trusts settled by the same settlor on or after 30 October 2024 share a single £1m allowance.
- Case law:
  - ***Brander v HMRC* [2010] UKUT 300 (TCC)** — agricultural and woodland estate where the holistic activity of the estate informed the BPR / APR split.
  - ***Pawson v HMRC* [2013] UKUT 050 (TCC)** — the trading-vs-investment line, principally a BPR case but cited frequently in APR boundary cases where the activity is part-farming-part-letting.
  - HMRC IHTM24000+ (Agricultural Property Relief Manual) for the operational construction of "character appropriate", "farmhouse" curtilage, equestrian use, the McKenna farmhouse construction test, etc.
- House position references: **§9** (IHT landlord-relevant positions, headline); **§15.4** (April 2026 BPR + APR cap, locked); **§22.1** (BPR investment line for landlord context — the Pawson case anchor); **§22.X.4** (excluded-property regime under LTR — Sch A1 extension to UK agricultural land confirmed unaffected by FA 2025); **§22.A.12** (cross-reference for the trust angle if APR-qualifying assets sit in a settlement).

**Worked example A (qualification-gate-focused, not operational): the fringe-holding test**

Persona: Singh-estate. Mixed rural holding: (i) 3-bedroom Cotswold farmhouse + 5 acres "pasture" let on grazing licence to neighbour for £600/year; (ii) detached 2-acre paddock used for owner's daughter's pony; (iii) converted barn let on Airbnb as serviced accommodation; (iv) 50 acres of productive arable land let on a 2017 FBT to a local farming partnership.

Qualification walk:
- 50 acres FBT-let arable: **qualifies for APR** — let on a tenancy beginning after 1 September 1995, owner held 7+ years, productively farmed (s.116(1) + s.117 limb 2). 100% relief on agricultural value (subject to the cap).
- Cotswold farmhouse + 5-acre pasture: **may qualify** if the McKenna "character appropriate" test passes (farmhouse must be ancillary to a working farm; here the working farm is on the 50-acre FBT, not the farmhouse-curtilage 5 acres). Sub-test: is the grazing licence a sustained productive use, or amenity? £600/year is the diagnostic — likely amenity classification, in which case farmhouse APR fails.
- 2-acre paddock for daughter's pony: **fails APR** — equestrian amenity use, not agricultural per IHTM24067.
- Converted barn Airbnb: **fails APR** — trading-style serviced accommodation, not agricultural; possibly BPR-eligible (Pawson line) but that's a separate analysis.

Cap interaction (HP §15.4 £1m / s.124D £2.5m): if Singh's qualifying agricultural property is valued at £1.5m agricultural value, treatment under the two readings:
- HP §15.4 reading (£1m cap): £1m at 100% relief; £500k at 50% relief → effective IHT on the £500k slice at 20% = £100k IHT due on qualifying property.
- s.124D reading (£2.5m allowance): £1.5m fully covered at 100%, no IHT due on qualifying property — but prior 7-year chargeable transfers may have consumed allowance.

RUN-phase writer must verify which reading is operative at the date of write.

## FAQ draft (10-12 entries, Stage 2 populated; RUN sub-agent polishes prose)

1. **What is Agricultural Property Relief and who actually qualifies for it?**
   APR is a relief in IHTA 1984 ss.115-124C that reduces the chargeable value of agricultural property by 100% or 50% on a transfer of value (lifetime gift or death). To qualify, the property must be "agricultural property" within s.115(2) — agricultural land or pasture, woodland or buildings used in connection with intensive livestock-rearing on adjacent agricultural land, and cottages, farm buildings or farmhouses "of a character appropriate" to the property. The transferor must have owned and used (or let) the property for the qualifying period under s.117: 2 years owner-occupied for agriculture, or 7 years let to someone who farms it.

2. **What is "agricultural value" — and why does it matter that APR only applies to agricultural value, not market value?**
   "Agricultural value" is defined in s.115(3) as the value the property would have if subject to a perpetual covenant restricting it to agricultural use. The market value of the same land may include hope value (development potential, equestrian conversion uplift, residential change-of-use prospect). APR shelters only the agricultural value; the hope value falls back into the chargeable estate. For a 10-acre paddock outside a village with planning potential, the market value might be £400k but the agricultural value £80k. APR shelters £80k; £320k stays in the estate.

3. **My farmhouse — does the farmhouse itself qualify, or just the land?**
   The farmhouse qualifies if it is "of a character appropriate to the property" under s.115(2). The leading test is *McKenna's Executors v HMRC* [2006] SpC 565: the farmhouse must be ancillary to the agricultural activity, not a stand-alone country residence with adjacent farmland. The Special Commissioners look at proportionality between house size and farm scale, history of use, contiguous occupation, and whether a reasonable purchaser of the farm would buy the house with the land. A 5-bedroom country house with 10 acres of grazing typically fails; a 3-bedroom farmhouse with 200 acres of arable typically passes.

4. **When does 100% relief apply versus 50%?**
   100% applies where (a) the transferor has the right to vacant possession or to obtain it within 12 months under s.116(2)(a); OR (b) the property is let on a tenancy beginning on or after 1 September 1995 (s.116(2)(c)). 50% applies in the residual case — historically this is mostly Agricultural Holdings Act 1986 tenancies granted before 1 September 1995, which were excluded from the 1995 statutory upgrade.

5. **What changes on 6 April 2026?**
   A combined cap on APR + BPR 100% relief is introduced. The headline policy figure announced 30 October 2024 was £1 million per estate; the gov.uk reforms summary still states this. The Finance Act 2026 as enacted introduced a new s.124D IHTA 1984 with an allowance of £2.5 million per chargeable transfer over a 7-year allowance period — Property Tax Partners flagged this potential discrepancy at Stage 2 brief verification (F-102 in the wave flags) and the writer should verify the operative figure at write date against legislation.gov.uk + the GOV.UK summary. Above the cap, relief drops to 50%, giving an effective 20% IHT rate on the above-cap slice.

6. **What is the anti-forestalling rule and how does it catch lifetime gifts?**
   Lifetime transfers of qualifying APR/BPR property made on or after 30 October 2024 are caught by the anti-forestalling rule if the donor dies on or after 6 April 2026 within 7 years of the gift. The effect is that the gift is treated as a chargeable transfer for cap purposes at the donor's death — so a £4m farm gifted in November 2024 to a child with the donor dying in 2028 still consumes the cap allowance at the 2028 death calculation. Landlords-with-farmland considering accelerated lifetime gifts to "lock in" pre-cap 100% relief should be advised that the anti-forestalling rule defeats this strategy in most cases.

7. **What's the minimum period of ownership and occupation, and how do gaps interact?**
   Under s.117, you need either (i) 2 years' owner-occupation for agriculture immediately before the transfer, or (ii) 7 years' ownership with the property occupied (by you or another) for agriculture throughout that 7-year period. Gaps in productive use are case-fact-sensitive. *HMRC v Atkinson* [2011] UKUT 506 (TCC) clarified that temporary cessation of personal occupation during the transferor's illness does not necessarily break the occupation continuity under s.117 — the test is whether the property continued to be used for agriculture by someone, not whether the transferor personally lived there.

8. **What disqualifies a fringe holding?**
   Three frequent disqualifiers: (i) equestrian use of paddocks — livery, riding-school, amenity grazing for owner's horses (not productive livestock) fails per IHTM24067; (ii) "redundant" farmhouse separated from the active farm by sale of the agricultural acreage — fails the "character appropriate" McKenna test; (iii) lapsed-let status where a tenant exits and the property sits unfarmed for years — fails the s.117(1)(b) requirement for agricultural occupation throughout the 7-year qualifying period.

9. **How does APR claim into the IHT400 process?**
   The personal representatives complete form IHT400 with the agricultural property listed and form IHT414 (Agricultural relief) supporting the claim. IHT414 asks for agricultural value, market value, tenure history, occupation history, and supporting documents (tenancy agreements, farm accounts, SFP/BPS payment records). The District Valuer reviews the agricultural-vs-market value split — disputes about the agricultural value or the McKenna test are typically resolved at this stage or escalate to the Lands Tribunal / First-tier Tribunal.

10. **How does APR interact with BPR for mixed rural businesses?**
    Where the farm operates both qualifying agricultural land AND a trading commercial element (farm shop, on-site B&B, contract farming for third parties), each element is analysed separately. Agricultural land + farmhouse claim APR; the farm shop trade may claim BPR per CTA 2010 / IHTA 1984 s.105 if it is a trading business not "wholly or mainly" investment (Pawson line). The £1m / £2.5m combined cap (per F-102) applies across both reliefs jointly, not separately to each — strategic allocation across BPR and APR within the cap matters for above-cap estates.

11. **How does APR stack with the spouse exemption, NRB, RNRB?**
    APR reduces the chargeable value of qualifying property before any of the exemptions or allowances. Order of application: (1) agricultural value isolated; (2) APR applied at 100% / 50% reducing the chargeable value; (3) spouse exemption applied to any surviving-spouse legacies; (4) NRB / transferable NRB applied; (5) RNRB / transferable RNRB applied if direct-descendant residential property qualifies; (6) IHT at 40% on the residual chargeable estate. APR-eligible property left to a surviving spouse gets the spouse exemption stacked with APR — the agricultural property is doubly sheltered.

12. **How does this page differ from your existing APR mixed-estate page?**
    Our existing page `agricultural-property-relief-mixed-estate-1m-cap` walks the operational mechanics of cap allocation with a £2.4m three-asset worked example. This page (C2) sits one click earlier in the funnel and answers the "do I qualify in the first place" question — the s.115(2) "is it agricultural property" gate, the s.115(3) agricultural-value-vs-hope-value distinction, the s.117 minimum period rules, and the fringe-holding disqualifiers. Once readers have established qualification, they go to the mixed-estate page for the cap-allocation arithmetic.
