# Artificial Separation and VAT: Key Insights from the Cases on HMRC Single-Taxable-Person Directions

## Statutory anchor
- Primary: Value Added Tax Act 1994 c. 23, Schedule 1 paragraphs 1A + 2 — verified verbatim at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1994/23/schedule/1 on 2026-05-26.
  - **Sch 1 para 1A(1)**: "Paragraph 2 below is for the purpose of preventing the maintenance or creation of any artificial separation of business activities carried on by two or more persons from resulting in an avoidance of VAT." (Purpose provision — the anti-avoidance hook.)
  - **Sch 1 para 2(1)**: "if the Commissioners make a direction under this paragraph, the persons named in the direction shall be treated as a single taxable person carrying on the activities of a business described in the direction." (Operative power — HMRC consolidates artificially separated operators into one taxable person from the direction's effective date.)
  - **Sch 1 para 2 sub-paragraphs (verify each at write time)**: conditions for making a direction (financial / economic / organisational links — the three-link test); effective date provisions (prospective only — direction cannot retro-impose registration on past supplies); the requirement that aggregating turnover would have triggered registration under Sch 1 para 1(1).
- Supporting: VATA 1994 Sch 1 para 1(1) (registration threshold £90,000 from 1 April 2024 — verify rate at write time per §16.27 rate-by-reference, given it has moved several times in recent fiscal events). VATA 1994 s.4 (charge to tax — defines "taxable supply"). VATA 1994 s.94 (meaning of "business"). VATA 1994 ss.5-6 (meaning of "supply"; time of supply).
- Supporting (for property-business overlap): VATA 1994 Sch 9 Group 1 (exempt land supplies — relevant context because residential lettings are exempt, sit outside the VAT framework, and so artificial separation typically bites in property contexts only where the separated activities involve standard-rated supplies — FHLs (8 December 2023 budget retained 20% standard rating for short-stay accommodation), serviced accommodation, commercial property management fees, mixed-use elements); VATA 1994 Sch 10 (option to tax — interaction where commercial-property landlords have opted to tax). FA 2024 / FA 2025 commencement provisions for the FHL regime abolition (cross-link to existing site pages); the artificial-separation question persists post-FHL-abolition for any short-stay accommodation business.

## Key questions this page must answer

1. **What is "artificial separation" in the VAT context — and why does HMRC care?** (One-paragraph framing: a business is split across multiple legal vehicles (partnerships, companies, sole trades, LLPs) so that no single vehicle's taxable turnover crosses the £90,000 VAT registration threshold (rate verified at write per §16.27 — has moved). Aggregated turnover would cross the threshold; the separation defeats VATA 1994 Sch 1 para 1(1)'s registration trigger. HMRC's anti-avoidance hook is Sch 1 para 1A + para 2: where the separation is artificial, HMRC issues a direction treating the named persons as a single taxable person from the direction's effective date.)
2. **What does Schedule 1 Paragraph 2 actually do?** (Power for HMRC to issue a direction. Effect: named persons treated as a single taxable person carrying on the directed business. Effective date: prospective only (Sch 1 para 2 cannot retro-impose registration on past supplies). Aggregated turnover must (would) have crossed the registration threshold. Underlying business must have been "artificially separated" — judged against the three-link test below.)
3. **What is the three-link test?** (HMRC's longstanding operational test for artificial separation, refined in successive FTT decisions: **financial links** (shared bank accounts, inter-company funding, common ownership of capital assets), **economic links** (common customers, common suppliers, common pricing, joint marketing, single brand identity, shared booking systems), **organisational links** (shared staff, shared premises, common management, identical operating hours, integrated systems). HMRC needs to show enough of all three categories to bring a successful direction; presence of one category alone (e.g. common ownership only) is insufficient. Cases differ on weighting; tribunal applies a "look at the substance not the form" overall judgement.)
4. **Which decided cases are most often cited?** (NEW LOCK CANDIDATE — Stage 1b reviewer / Stage 2 sub-agent verifies the leading case list before this section ships. Candidates to verify: *Customs and Excise Commissioners v Glassborow* [1975] QB 465 (early HC authority on disaggregation principles, pre-Sch 1 para 2 statutory codification); *Customs and Excise Commissioners v Trippitt* (1998 EWCA — VAT tribunal/Court of Appeal authority on artificial separation tests); *Belcher and others v HMRC* (FTT — husband-and-wife B&B / guesthouse separation finding); *Strawberry Fields Holiday Park v HMRC* (FTT 2018 — holiday park, separated into multiple LLPs); *Spearman Cottages v HMRC* (FTT — serviced accommodation disaggregation). Stage 2 sub-agent must WebFetch each candidate against BAILII / tribunals.judiciary.uk at write time per §16.35 + §16.36 brief-citation gate; replace any case that is dead-citation, mis-cited, or no longer leading authority. Caution: VAT artificial-separation cases churn at the FTT level; surface 2-3 lower-court vignettes for narrative, anchor authority discussion on UT or higher.)
5. **How does this apply to property businesses specifically?** (Three property-business patterns where Sch 1 para 2 commonly bites: (i) **furnished holiday lets / short-stay accommodation** — FHL standard-rated for VAT (8 December 2023 Budget retained 20% rating for short-stay accommodation post-FHL income tax abolition; verify at write per §16.27) so high-turnover hosts cross threshold; splitting across spouse-owned LLPs or separate SPVs invites a direction. Sibling page on FHL abolition `abolition-of-furnished-holiday-lettings-fhl-what-individual-owners-needs-to-know` covers the income-tax angle; the VAT registration threshold is independent and persists post-abolition. (ii) **Serviced accommodation operators** — same pattern, often split between sole-trade operator and management LLP. (iii) **Multi-SPV property management** — SPV-owned commercial property + separate property-management LLP charging fees back to the SPV; if the separation is artificial the management fees are aggregated for registration.)
6. **Does Sch 1 para 2 apply to wholly residential letting businesses?** (Generally NO. Residential letting is exempt under VATA 1994 Sch 9 Group 1 — the income is outside the VAT framework, so registration never triggers and Sch 1 para 2 has no purchase. Important edge case: where residential letting is mixed with standard-rated services (cleaning, laundry, breakfast, concierge — beyond the *Pawson* investment line for IHT BPR purposes; standalone for VAT), the standard-rated element is what counts toward registration, and Sch 1 para 2 can apply if the standard-rated element is artificially separated. Most pure-residential BTL portfolios are unaffected.)
7. **What does HMRC's enquiry pattern look like in practice?** (Stage 1: nudge letter or invitation to register, citing aggregated turnover figures and the three-link test factors. Stage 2: formal direction under Sch 1 para 2, naming the persons and the effective date. Stage 3: appeal to the FTT under VATA 1994 s.83 (appeal rights). The direction takes effect from the date specified (prospectively); the appeal does not stay the direction unless the FTT orders otherwise. Operator can be VAT-registered + filing under protest pending the appeal outcome — practical advice is to take immediate VAT compliance steps to avoid penalty exposure while the appeal runs.)
8. **What is the defence stance — what evidence helps?** (Distinct financial trails — separate bank accounts, no inter-company funding, separate borrowings; distinct economic profile — different customer bases, different pricing, separate marketing, distinct brands; distinct organisational footprint — separate staff (or genuinely shared on arms-length basis with documented allocations), separate premises (or shared on arms-length lease), separate management, separate hours, separate operating systems. Documentation discipline matters: contemporaneous records of arms-length arrangements survive HMRC challenge; retrospectively-constructed papers do not. Pre-decision tax advice paper trail also helps.)
9. **What are the penalty consequences if a direction is issued?** (Once a direction issues, the consolidated taxable person is in scope for VAT from the effective date forward. Failure to register / file from that date triggers FA 2009 / VATA late-registration penalty regime (variable behaviour-based percentages of VAT due). Output tax becomes due on supplies from the effective date; input tax recovery available subject to evidence. The pre-direction position is unchanged — past supplies are not retro-taxed — but the operator inherits full forward-looking VAT compliance burden, often inconveniently mid-tax-period.)
10. **Can I pre-clear a separated structure with HMRC before HMRC moves?** (No formal clearance route for Sch 1 para 2 specifically. The Non-Statutory Clearance route is unlikely to engage on a question that is fundamentally one of substance + future fact-finding. Best practice is taxation advice + contemporaneous documentation of the three-link factors at the design stage. Some operators apply for VAT registration voluntarily on the consolidated basis if the structure is borderline, accepting registration to avoid an enquiry-led direction.)
