# LLP Accounts: The Operational Accounts and Filing Handbook for Property LLPs (2026/27)

## Statutory anchor

- **Primary (the LLP entity statute):** Limited Liability Partnerships Act 2000 (LLPA 2000) — establishes the LLP as a body corporate (s.1(2)); members liable only to the extent agreed in the LLP agreement / LLPA 2000 framework (s.1(5)); registered at Companies House under same operational system as companies (ss.2-3). Cross-reference MW2 Bucket A LLP pillar (`llp-and-taxation-benefits` this batch + existing `llp-property-investment-worth-considering`); this page does NOT restate LLP entity framework — it stands on it.
- **Primary (the load-bearing accounts SI — the operative statutory layer):** Limited Liability Partnerships (Accounts and Audit) (Application of Companies Act 2006) Regulations 2008 (SI 2008/1911) — extends CA 2006 Part 15 accounts framework to LLPs with LLP-specific modifications (per §11.C.Z.1 lock). **Operative architecture:**
  - **Reg 3 + Sch 1:** applies CA 2006 Part 15 (ss.380-474) accounts framework to LLPs with the specific modifications in Sch 1.
  - **Reg 4:** application of CA 2006 Part 16 audit framework with modifications.
  - **Sch 1 modifications:** terminology adapted (members instead of shareholders / directors; designated members carry director-equivalent responsibilities; LLP agreement instead of articles); accounts must be prepared in accordance with relevant accounting standards (FRS 105 / FRS 102 + LLP SORP); accounts approval + signing by designated members; small-LLP + micro-LLP size thresholds mirror small-company + micro-company thresholds with adjustments.
  - **Post-2008 amendments:** SI 2016/575 + later amending SIs; RUN session verifies any post-ECCTA-2023 LLP-side amendments at write time per §16.35 (LLP-side ECCTA changes phased separately from companies-side per §11.B).
- **Primary (the LLP SORP — the load-bearing GAAP overlay):** SORP "Accounting by Limited Liability Partnerships" — published by CCAB (Consultative Committee of Accountancy Bodies), 2025 revision current (per §11.C.Z.2 lock; RUN session verifies current SORP version at write time). **Operative function:** the SORP sets out LLP-specific accounting treatments that adapt FRS 102 + FRS 105 to the LLP entity-form, particularly for members' interests. **Critical SORP topics:**
  - **Members' remuneration (per §11.C.Z.4):** the split between (a) remuneration treated as an EXPENSE of the LLP (automatic-allocation per LLP agreement; charged before profit; deducted in arriving at profit for the year) and (b) remuneration treated as an APPROPRIATION of profit (discretionary post-profit allocation; treated as distribution).
  - **Members' capital classification (per §11.C.Z.3):** between (a) EQUITY (members' interests classified as equity per FRS 102 Section 22 substance test — non-puttable, no contractual obligation to repay) and (b) LIABILITY (members' interests classified as financial liability where puttable on retirement or where contractual obligation to repay exists). Classification has cascade implications for balance-sheet presentation + for whether members' remuneration becomes interest-expense (on liability-classified balances) or appropriation (on equity-classified balances).
  - **Loans and other debts due to members (per §11.C.Z.5):** specific LLP balance-sheet line under SI 2008/1911 + LLP SORP — distinct from share-capital line on company balance sheets; separate disclosure required.
  - **Members' agreement disclosure:** key terms of the LLP agreement governing remuneration + capital + distribution arrangements disclosed in accounts; particularly relevant where members' interests are equity-classified (the contractual terms determine the equity-vs-liability test).
  - **Provisions, post-retirement benefits, deferred members' remuneration** — specific SORP treatments.
- **Primary (the FRS standards selection — the day-one accounting-standards decision):**
  - **FRS 105 (Micro-entities):** available to LLPs meeting micro thresholds (CA 2006 s.384A applied by SI 2008/1911 — turnover ≤ £632k, balance sheet total ≤ £316k, ≤ 10 employees; RUN session verifies post-FRC threshold updates per §16.27 rate-by-reference discipline). Operative for small property-investment LLPs prioritising filing simplicity; no fair-value option; investment property at historical cost less depreciation.
  - **FRS 102 (UK GAAP — Sections 1A small + full):** available to all LLPs; Section 1A "Small Entities" provides reduced disclosures for small LLPs (small thresholds per s.382-383 CA 2006 applied via SI). Section 16 (Investment Property — fair value model) is available to LLPs and is **the common election for property-investment LLPs** where investor / lender / member visibility on NAV matters (per §11.C.Z.9).
  - **FRS 102 Section 22 (Liabilities and Equity)** is load-bearing for the equity-vs-liability classification of members' interests — sets out the substance-over-form test that the LLP SORP then operationalises with LLP-specific guidance (per §11.C.Z.3).
  - **EU-adopted IFRS:** available to LLPs but rare; reserved for LLPs in PIE category or with specific reasons.
- **Primary (designated members + accounts responsibility — per §11.C.Z.6 lock):** LLPA 2000 ss.6-9 — designated members carry director-equivalent statutory accounts responsibilities. **Operative for LLP accounts:** at least 2 designated members required (LLPA 2000 s.8). The accounts-approval + accounts-signing + filing obligations sit with designated members. Failure to file on time creates designated-member personal exposure under CA 2006 ss.451-453 (applied by SI 2008/1911) — including criminal liability for repeated default + civil penalty regime. **Not honorific** (per §11.C.Z.6 do-not-write list).
- **Primary (accounts content + format under SI 2008/1911 applying CA 2006 Part 15):**
  - **Balance sheet (LLP-modified format):** specific lines for "Loans and other debts due to members" and "Members' other interests" — distinct from a company balance sheet. Members' interests can be equity or liability per the FRS 102 Section 22 + LLP SORP test.
  - **Profit + loss account:** must distinguish profit available for division among members from members' remuneration charged as an expense (the SORP-driven split).
  - **Notes to the accounts:** disclosure of LLP agreement terms relevant to members' interests classification; disclosure of members' interests by class; disclosure of remuneration policy and members' remuneration charged as an expense + as appropriation.
  - **Statement of changes in equity (or members' interests):** reconciliation of opening to closing members' equity-classified interests with movements (new admission, retirement, profit allocation, drawings, revaluation reserve, etc).
- **Primary (filing windows + Companies House submission per §11.C.Z.8 lock):** SI 2008/1911 applies CA 2006 ss.441-444 — accounts filing within 9 months of end of accounting reference period (private LLPs); first accounts within 21 months of incorporation. **Operative for small / micro LLPs:** abridged accounts (CA 2006 s.444 applied by SI) and filleted accounts (omitting P&L from public filing — s.444A applied with modifications) available; Companies House filing fees waived (free filing). **Post-ECCTA operational layer (per §11.C.Z.10):** LLP-side ECCTA implementation phased separately from companies-side per §11.B; ID verification framework + ACSP + registered email + appropriate registered office requirements extend to LLPs with LLP-specific operational rollout dates. RUN session verifies current LLP rollout state at write time per §11.A F-12 campaign-page reference.
- **Primary (audit thresholds — per §11.C.Z.7 lock):** small LLPs may take audit exemption if meeting size thresholds (turnover ≤ £10.2m, balance sheet ≤ £5.1m, ≤ 50 employees — meeting 2 of 3 in current and prior year; RUN session verifies post-uplift wording at write time per recent threshold-uplift policy). **Operative gating:** exemption denied where (a) members holding ≥10% by number or value require audit per CA 2006 s.476 (applied by SI), or (b) the LLP is part of a group where consolidation triggers audit, or (c) the LLP is in a regulated sector (financial-services LLPs, etc).
- **Primary (the property-investment LLP overlay — per §11.C.Z.9 lock):**
  - **Investment property accounts treatment under FRS 102 Section 16:** fair-value model commonly elected for property-investment LLPs; revaluation gains/losses recognised in P&L; **NO deferred tax provision required at LLP level** because the LLP itself is tax-transparent (members taxed individually; deferred tax considered at member level if at all). Contrast with LtdCo position (FRS 102 Section 29 deferred tax provision required on revaluation gains within the company) — this is one of the operational accounting advantages of LLP vs LtdCo for fair-value reporting.
  - **Members' remuneration vs property-income allocation:** members' shares of LLP property income are allocated per the LLP agreement; allocation is for tax-transparency purposes (each member taxed on their share per ITTOIA 2005 Part 9 framework + s.863 for LLPs taxed as partnerships); the accounts split between "remuneration as expense" and "remuneration as appropriation" reflects the GAAP treatment, NOT the tax treatment (which always remains transparent).

## Worked-example data (RUN session uses these as canvas)

### Example 1 — Members' interests equity-vs-liability classification

**Patel Family Property LLP** is a 4-member family property-investment LLP. The LLP agreement provides that a retiring member is paid out at NAV per agreement formula within 12 months of notice.

- **FRS 102 Section 22 substance test:** members' interests are PUTTABLE on retirement (the LLP has a contractual obligation to repay capital within 12 months of notice). This creates a financial liability under Section 22.
- **Classification:** members' capital balances are **financial liabilities**, not equity. They sit BELOW the equity line on the balance sheet under "Loans and other debts due to members" or in a separate "Members' interests classified as liability" caption.
- **Cascade implications:** (a) remuneration on the liability-classified balances may be interest-expense rather than profit appropriation; (b) disclosure of LLP agreement terms relevant to the classification is required in the notes; (c) any movement in fair value of the liability through P&L (rare for fixed-amount NAV-formulae but possible for index-linked or growth-linked formulae).
- **Counterfactual:** if the LLP agreement had no buy-out obligation (retiring member simply loses interest, or is paid only on actual disposal of the LLP property), members' interests would be EQUITY-classified.

### Example 2 — Members' remuneration accounting split

**Kapoor Property LLP** has 4 members. The LLP agreement provides: (a) £30,000 per year automatic allocation to each of 2 active members (Mr Kapoor + Mrs Kapoor — operational + management remuneration); (b) discretionary allocation of remainder by designated-member decision at year-end.

- **Year 1 results:** £200,000 profit before any remuneration.
- **Accounts presentation:**
  - **Members' remuneration charged as expense:** £60,000 (2 × £30,000 automatic allocation per LLP agreement) — deducted in arriving at profit for the year.
  - **Profit for the year before members' remuneration as appropriation:** £140,000.
  - **Members' remuneration as appropriation:** £140,000 allocated post-profit per designated-member discretionary decision (e.g. £80k to Mr Kapoor, £40k to Mrs Kapoor, £10k each to the two non-active members).
  - **P&L caption "profit available for division among members":** £0 retained.
- **Tax side (independent of accounts split per §11.C.Z.4):** all 4 members declare their share of LLP property-business profit per ITTOIA 2005 Part 9 + s.863, regardless of the accounts-side expense / appropriation split. The tax allocation follows the LLP agreement's profit-share entitlement; accounts split follows the SORP-driven expense / appropriation discipline.

### Example 3 — Side-by-side accounts comparison: LLP vs LtdCo on identical portfolio

**A £2,000,000 property portfolio is held by Patel Properties LLP vs Patel Properties Ltd.** Year-end valuation rises to £2,400,000 (£400k revaluation gain). Both elect FRS 102 Section 16 fair-value model.

- **LtdCo (FRS 102 Section 16 fair-value):**
  - £400,000 revaluation gain recognised in P&L.
  - £100,000 deferred tax provision per FRS 102 Section 29 (25% main rate × £400k).
  - Reported profit £300,000 net (after the deferred-tax provision).
  - Balance sheet: investment property £2.4m; deferred tax liability £100k.
- **LLP (FRS 102 Section 16 fair-value):**
  - £400,000 revaluation gain recognised in P&L.
  - **NO LLP-level deferred tax** (LLP is tax-transparent per §11.C.Z.9; members taxed individually only on actual disposal under TCGA 1992 + ITTOIA 2005 Part 9).
  - Reported profit £400,000 net.
  - Balance sheet: investment property £2.4m; no deferred tax line.
- **Operative insight:** the LLP accounts present the gain cleaner without the deferred-tax presentation overhead. Member-level CGT on actual disposal may be IDENTICAL OR WORSE than LtdCo (member-level CGT vs company-level CT typically with double-extraction), but the ACCOUNTS presentation in the LLP year is cleaner. Lender / investor / member visibility on NAV is direct.

### Example 4 — Audit exemption + filing window discipline

**Mawell Properties LLP** (3-member, £4m portfolio): turnover £180,000, balance sheet £4.2m, 0 employees.

- **Audit exemption per §11.C.Z.7 (CA 2006 s.477 + s.479 applied via SI):** the LLP meets the small thresholds (turnover ≤ £10.2m, balance sheet ≤ £5.1m, ≤ 50 employees in 2 of 3 in current and prior year; RUN session verifies current post-uplift thresholds). Audit exemption available, subject to two gating conditions.
- **Gating condition 1 — ≥10% members can require audit (CA 2006 s.476 applied via SI):** if any member or group of members holding ≥10% by number or value notify the designated members at least 1 month before year-end, audit becomes mandatory regardless of size.
- **Gating condition 2 — group consolidation:** if the LLP is part of an ineligible group, exemption denied.
- **Filing window per §11.C.Z.8:** accounts filed at Companies House within 9 months of accounting reference period end (CA 2006 s.441 via SI). First accounts within 21 months of incorporation.
- **Designated-member personal exposure per §11.C.Z.6:** missing the filing window triggers automatic civil penalty (escalating tariff: £150 → £1,500+ depending on lateness) and personal criminal liability for repeated default (CA 2006 ss.451-453 applied via SI).
- **Reduced-disclosure options:**
  - **Abridged accounts (s.444 applied):** simplified balance sheet + P&L formats; available where all members consent.
  - **Filleted accounts (s.444A applied):** P&L NOT filed for public inspection; only the balance sheet + notes + auditor's report (if any) filed. Full accounts still prepared for members; only filing differs. Common election for property-investment LLPs to limit competitor visibility on rental income.

### Example 5 — Common LLP accounts misclassification trap

**Singh Property LLP** newly-formed; designated-member accountant prepares the first accounts using a small-company template without LLP-specific SI 2008/1911 modifications.

- **Mistakes embedded:**
  - **Balance sheet caption "Share capital"** used for members' capital balances (WRONG — should be "Members' other interests" + "Loans and other debts due to members" per SI 2008/1911 + LLP SORP).
  - **P&L "Profit attributable to shareholders"** caption (WRONG — should distinguish profit before members' remuneration as expense from profit available for division).
  - **Members' interests entirely classified as equity** without FRS 102 Section 22 substance test analysis (WRONG — if LLP agreement creates puttable-on-retirement obligation, classification should be liability per §11.C.Z.3).
  - **No disclosure of LLP agreement terms** relevant to members' interests classification (WRONG — SORP-mandated disclosure).
  - **Filing on company template** without LLP-modified format (WRONG — Companies House rejects + designated members face refile + escalating-penalty risk).
- **Operative cost:** accounts rejected at filing; refile required within 9-month window; designated-member personal exposure for filing default if window slips. Practical remedy: engage an accountant who is familiar with LLP-specific SI 2008/1911 + SORP; do NOT use generic small-company templates.

## FAQ expansion (RUN session polishes prose; 12 FAQs target)

1. **Q: What statutory framework governs LLP accounts?**
   A: Three layers. (a) **LLPA 2000** establishes the LLP as a body corporate (s.1(2)) and requires Companies House registration. (b) **SI 2008/1911** (Limited Liability Partnerships (Accounts and Audit) (Application of Companies Act 2006) Regulations 2008) applies CA 2006 Part 15 (Accounts and reports) and Part 16 (Audit) to LLPs with LLP-specific modifications in Schedules 1 and 2 — members instead of shareholders / directors, designated members instead of board, LLP agreement instead of articles. (c) **LLP SORP** (Statement of Recommended Practice — Accounting by Limited Liability Partnerships, published by CCAB, current 2025 revision) operationalises LLP-specific accounting treatments under FRS 102 and FRS 105.

2. **Q: Which accounting standard should an LLP use?**
   A: Three operative choices. (a) **FRS 105 (Micro-entities)** — available to LLPs meeting micro thresholds (turnover ≤ £632k, balance sheet total ≤ £316k, ≤ 10 employees; verify current post-FRC threshold updates). No fair-value option; investment property at historical cost less depreciation. (b) **FRS 102 with Section 1A** — reduced disclosures for small LLPs. (c) **FRS 102 full** — common election for property-investment LLPs using Section 16 fair-value model. EU-adopted IFRS is available but rare. The choice drives presentation but not the underlying tax-transparency framework.

3. **Q: Are members' interests in an LLP classified as equity or liability?**
   A: Both are possible. The classification follows the **FRS 102 Section 22 substance-over-form test**, operationalised by the LLP SORP. If LLP agreement creates puttability on retirement (e.g. a retiring member is paid out at NAV within a contractual window) or any contractual obligation for the LLP to repay capital, members' interests are **liability**-classified. If members' interests are non-puttable and no repayment obligation exists, **equity** classification applies. Most property-investment LLPs with realistic buy-out provisions end up with liability-classified interests. The classification cascades to balance-sheet presentation, members' remuneration treatment, and disclosure obligations.

4. **Q: How is members' remuneration accounted for?**
   A: The LLP SORP requires a split between two categories. (a) **Remuneration as expense** — automatic allocation per LLP agreement (e.g. fixed annual amounts to active members for management work); charged in arriving at profit for the year. (b) **Remuneration as appropriation** — discretionary post-profit allocation; treated as distribution of profit, not as expense. The split is GAAP-driven, not tax-driven. Tax-side allocation under ITTOIA 2005 Part 9 + s.863 follows the LLP agreement's profit-share entitlement independently of the accounts split.

5. **Q: What is the "Loans and other debts due to members" balance-sheet line?**
   A: A LLP-specific balance-sheet line (under SI 2008/1911 + LLP SORP) distinct from the share-capital line on company balance sheets. It captures amounts the LLP owes to its members in their capacity as members — typically capital contributions classified as liabilities under FRS 102 Section 22, unpaid members' remuneration, and any formal loans from members to the LLP. Separate disclosure is required because the line represents a category that does not exist in company accounts.

6. **Q: Who are designated members and what do they do?**
   A: Per LLPA 2000 ss.6-9, an LLP must have at least 2 designated members. They carry director-equivalent statutory responsibilities for the LLP's accounts and filings. Specifically, designated members are responsible for: (a) preparing and approving the LLP's accounts; (b) signing the accounts; (c) filing accounts and the confirmation statement at Companies House within statutory deadlines; (d) maintaining the LLP's statutory registers; (e) appointing and dismissing auditors. **Personal exposure:** failure to file on time triggers civil penalties under CA 2006 ss.451-453 (applied via SI) and, for repeated default, criminal liability for the designated members personally — not honorific.

7. **Q: When must LLP accounts be filed?**
   A: Per CA 2006 s.441 applied by SI 2008/1911, private LLPs must file accounts at Companies House within **9 months of the end of the accounting reference period**. First accounts must be filed within **21 months of incorporation**. Late filing triggers an automatic civil penalty on an escalating tariff (currently £150 → £1,500+ depending on lateness; verify current scale at write time per §16.27). The penalty falls on the LLP itself; criminal liability for repeated default falls on the designated members personally.

8. **Q: What audit exemption thresholds apply to LLPs?**
   A: Per CA 2006 s.477 + s.479 applied via SI, small LLPs are exempt from statutory audit if they meet two of three thresholds in the current and prior year: **turnover ≤ £10.2 million, balance sheet total ≤ £5.1 million, average employees ≤ 50** (verify current post-uplift wording at write time — thresholds were uplifted for accounting periods beginning on or after 6 April 2025 per recent government announcement). **Exemption denied** where (a) members holding ≥10% by number or value require audit per CA 2006 s.476 applied via SI; (b) the LLP is part of an ineligible group requiring consolidated audit; (c) the LLP is in a regulated sector (financial services LLPs, certain professional-services LLPs).

9. **Q: How is investment property accounted for in a property-investment LLP?**
   A: Most property-investment LLPs elect FRS 102 Section 16 fair-value model. Investment property is held at fair value, with revaluation gains and losses recognised in profit or loss each year. **Critically:** unlike a company under FRS 102 Section 29, there is **no LLP-level deferred tax provision** because the LLP itself is tax-transparent — there is no LLP-level corporation tax that the deferred-tax provision would represent. Members are taxed individually only on actual disposal (capital gains tax at member level under TCGA 1992 + ITTOIA 2005 Part 9 mechanics). The accounts present the gain cleaner than a company on the same portfolio.

10. **Q: How does the LLP accounts split relate to the tax treatment?**
    A: The accounts split between remuneration-as-expense and remuneration-as-appropriation is **GAAP-driven**, following the LLP SORP discipline based on LLP agreement allocation mechanics. The tax treatment under ITTOIA 2005 Part 9 + s.863 is **independent** — each member is taxed on their share of LLP profit per the LLP agreement, regardless of whether the accounts split allocates remuneration as expense or as appropriation. The two run in parallel: accounts presentation does not change member tax liability, and member tax liability does not constrain accounts presentation.

11. **Q: What does ECCTA 2023 mean for LLP filings?**
    A: ECCTA 2023 LLP-side implementation is **phased separately from the companies-side** rollout per §11.B. The reforms — appropriate registered office, registered email, ID verification for designated members, ACSP framework for ID verification routing — apply to LLPs but on LLP-specific operational dates set by separate commencement statutory instruments. RUN session verifies current LLP rollout state at the Companies House campaign page (`https://changestoukcompanylaw.campaign.gov.uk/`) at write time. Designated members should expect to verify their identity via the GOV.UK One Login or the ACSP route within the rollout window for their LLP's first relevant filing event.

12. **Q: What are common LLP accounts mistakes?**
    A: Six recurring patterns. (1) Using a company-accounts template without LLP-specific SI 2008/1911 modifications (wrong balance-sheet captions, missing "Loans and other debts due to members" line, wrong P&L presentation). (2) Classifying all members' interests as equity without applying the FRS 102 Section 22 substance test (most LLPs with realistic buy-out provisions have liability-classified interests). (3) Treating all members' remuneration as appropriation when the LLP agreement creates automatic allocation triggering expense treatment under the SORP. (4) Failing to disclose LLP agreement terms relevant to members' interests classification (SORP-mandated disclosure). (5) Missing the 9-month filing window with designated-member personal civil + criminal exposure. (6) Treating designated members as honorific (they are not — they carry director-equivalent statutory accounts responsibilities).
