# Hybrid Limited Liability Partnership for UK Property Investors: Structure, Tax Treatment, and the Mixed-Membership Partnership Anti-Avoidance Trap

## Statutory anchor

- **Primary (the structure):** Limited Liability Partnerships Act 2000 (LLPA 2000) — primary statute creating LLP as a body corporate with members. There is NO statutory category "hybrid LLP" — the term refers to an LLP where the members include both (a) one or more individuals AND (b) one or more body corporates (typically a UK limited company). LLPA 2000 imposes no restriction on member type — any "person" can be a member, where "person" includes a body corporate per Interpretation Act 1978 Sch 1. Hybrid LLPs are therefore lawful and routinely registered with Companies House — Companies House operates no separate "hybrid" classification.
- **Primary (default tax treatment):** ITTOIA 2005 s.863 (income tax) + CTA 2009 s.1273 (corporation tax — applies LLP's profits to corporate members at CT rate) + TCGA 1992 s.59A (CGT — fractional-interest treatment for each member). **Default rule: LLP profits/losses allocated to members per LLP agreement; each member taxed on its share at its own rate.** Individual members taxed at marginal income tax rate; corporate members taxed at CT rate (19%-25% pre-25% FY26 + 27% post-26% FY27 if FA 2026 enacted — Stage 2 verifies current rate per §16.27 + §16.42 rate-by-reference discipline + cross-check with M2-A-B1 A5 corporation-tax-marginal-relief seed for current rate stack).
- **Primary (the mixed-membership partnership anti-avoidance regime — THE LOAD-BEARING POINT):** ITA 2007 ss.850C, 850D, 850E — "Mixed membership partnership rules" (introduced by FA 2014 Sch 17, in force from 6 April 2014, alongside the salaried-member rules per A11 this batch).
  - **s.850C:** Where (i) a non-individual member (e.g. the corporate member of a hybrid LLP) has been allocated profits that are "excess" — i.e. exceed what the non-individual member would have received on arms-length commercial terms — AND (ii) an individual member of the LLP has the "power to enjoy" those excess profits, the excess profits are REATTRIBUTED to the individual member for income tax purposes and taxed at the individual's marginal rate.
  - **s.850D:** Defines "power to enjoy" expansively — direct or indirect rights to receive, call for, or otherwise enjoy the profits; includes connected-party / family-member channels. The test is substance-over-form.
  - **s.850E:** Mirror rule for capital sums and assets — same reallocation principle applied to capital movements that mirror the profit-reallocation strategy.
- **Primary (HMRC operative guidance):** HMRC Partnership Manual **PM210000+ → PM213000+** — Mixed member partnerships + Mixed member rules. **STAGE 2 DRIFT CATCH (2026-05-27, §16.40 pattern — F-10 raised):** Stage 1 seed cited "PM236500+" as the entry-point for HMRC mixed-membership rules. **VERIFIED 2026-05-27 against gov.uk: the correct entry point is PM210000 (Mixed member partnerships: contents) → PM213000 (Mixed member rules: contents).** PM236500 specifically returns 404 (does not exist); PM236000 / PM237000 / PM238000 are SUB-PAGES within the PM213000+ tree (covering business transfers + LLP takeover specifically), NOT the entry-point. Framework is substantively unchanged since FA 2014 introduction but periodic refreshes occur.
- **Primary (interaction with salaried-member rules — distinct regime):** Mixed-membership rules at ITA 2007 ss.850C-E apply to NON-INDIVIDUAL members of a partnership (typically corporate members of hybrid LLPs). Salaried-member rules at ITA 2007 ss.863A-G apply to INDIVIDUAL members of an LLP (reclassifying them as deemed employees). **Different rules; different targets; different mechanics.** A hybrid LLP can be hit by BOTH regimes simultaneously — mixed-membership reallocation against the corporate member's allocated profits AND salaried-member reclassification of one or more individual members. See A11 this batch for salaried-member regime depth + post-BlueCrest narrowing.
- **Primary (interaction with disguised remuneration regime):** ITEPA 2003 Part 7A (the disguised remuneration regime — primarily targeting Employee Benefit Trust schemes but with potential reach into payment-via-LLP structures). Stage 2 confirms whether any LLP-specific paragraphs of Part 7A apply to hybrid-LLP payment flows from corporate to individual members.
- **Primary (anti-fragmentation overlay):** TIOPA 2010 ss.146-148 — anti-fragmentation rules where multiple connected entities allocate profits in ways that exploit lower rates. Less directly applicable to hybrid LLPs than the mixed-membership rules but may apply where the hybrid LLP sits within a wider group structure.
- **Supporting (the income-splitting attraction that drives demand):** Section 24 mortgage-interest restriction at ITTOIA 2005 s.272A (introduced by F(No.2)A 2015) bites only on individuals carrying on a property business — not on companies, and not on partnerships (because partnerships are looked-through to underlying partners). Where a hybrid LLP holds property and a corporate member receives most of the profit, that corporate member's share is taxable at CT rate (with full interest deductibility under CTA 2009 Part 5 loan relationships, not s.272A-restricted). This was the practical attraction. **But the mixed-membership rules counter this where the individual member has the power to enjoy the corporate's excess profits — which is the typical hybrid LLP setup.** Operative outcome for most property hybrid LLPs: the mixed-membership reallocation neutralises the income-splitting play unless the structure can demonstrate (a) the corporate has a commercial entitlement to its profit share, AND (b) the individual member does NOT have power to enjoy that corporate's profit share.
- **Supporting (residual legitimate uses):** (i) asset protection — corporate member ring-fences a portion of LLP equity from individual member's personal creditors; (ii) succession planning — corporate member shares can be passed via FIC mechanics for IHT planning (forward link to A6 `a-complete-guide-to-family-investment-companies-fics`); (iii) external-investor inclusion — corporate member representing institutional capital sits alongside founder-individual members.
- **House position reference:** §21 (Ltd Co + FIC family — LtdCo-side mechanics). §21.5 (FIC mechanics — if the corporate member is a FIC). ## Worked-example data (RUN session uses these as canvas)

### Example 1 — The classic founder + own-LtdCo hybrid LLP: mixed-membership reallocation in full

- **Mrs Singh** (a higher-rate individual landlord) holds a 14-property BTL portfolio personally. Post-Section-24 she is paying tax on rental profits without full interest deduction. Adviser recommends restructure: form **Singh Property LLP** with two members — Mrs Singh + Singh Holdings Ltd (a passive sleeve LtdCo owned 100% by Mrs Singh; no operational role; no third-party investment; no risk-bearing function).
- **Profit allocation strategy:** LLP allocates £40k to Mrs Singh + £180k to Singh Holdings Ltd each year. Mrs Singh pays tax at HRT on £40k. Singh Holdings Ltd pays CT at 25% on £180k = £45k. **Claimed savings vs personal ownership:** the £180k held at corporate level (CT 25%) avoids the personal HRT (40%) the same income would have triggered on Mrs Singh's personal SA — apparent £27k annual saving.
- **ITA 2007 s.850C analysis at year-end:**
  - **Step 1: Excess test.** Singh Holdings Ltd's arms-length commercial entitlement = NIL (passive sleeve, no risk-bearing role, no operational input, no third-party investment). Allocated £180k. Excess = £180k − £0 = **£180k**.
  - **Step 2: Power to enjoy.** Mrs Singh owns 100% of Singh Holdings Ltd. She can pay dividends to herself; she can pass company shares to her children; she controls all corporate-level decisions. **Power to enjoy is total.**
  - **Step 3: Reallocation.** £180k excess reattributed to Mrs Singh. Mrs Singh's reallocated income = £40k (direct) + £180k (reallocated) = £220k. Tax at marginal rate: HRT/ART blended ≈ £92k. Corporate CT paid on the £180k (£45k) is creditable.
- **Net outcome:** the structure is dismantled. Mrs Singh ends up paying approximately the same total tax as if she had held the portfolio personally — the income-splitting attraction was illusory.

### Example 2 — Hybrid LLP with REAL commercial-entitlement corporate member: structure works

- **Verma Property LLP** has two members: Mrs Verma (individual; founder) + Verma Asset Management Ltd (a third-party fund management company providing portfolio strategy + asset-management services to the LLP under an arms-length agreement; Mrs Verma owns 0% of Verma Asset Management). Verma Asset Management has independent commercial entitlement to a fee that on independent benchmarking ≈ £80k/year.
- **Profit allocation:** LLP allocates £60k to Mrs Verma + £80k to Verma Asset Management Ltd.
- **ITA 2007 s.850C analysis:**
  - **Step 1: Excess test.** Verma Asset Management's arms-length entitlement = £80k (matched to allocation). Excess = £0.
  - **Step 2: Power to enjoy.** Mrs Verma owns 0% of Verma Asset Management; no direct or indirect channel; no connected-party route. **Power to enjoy: NIL.**
  - **Step 3: Reallocation.** No reallocation. Structure operates as intended; corporate-level CT on £80k = £20k; Mrs Verma's personal tax on £60k at marginal rate.
- **Page surfaces this scenario** as the operative example of a residual legitimate hybrid LLP — external commercial corporate member with real commercial entitlement and no individual power-to-enjoy link.

### Example 3 — Hybrid LLP with FIC corporate member for succession planning (residual legitimate use)

- **Kapoor Family LLP** has three members: Mr & Mrs Kapoor (individuals) + Kapoor FIC Ltd (a Family Investment Company holding shares for the next-generation children with growth-share + share-class architecture per §21.5).
- **Profit allocation:** LLP allocates £60k to Mr Kapoor + £40k to Mrs Kapoor + £100k to Kapoor FIC Ltd (the FIC retains earnings at CT rate to fund value-freeze growth for the children's IHT-planning beneficial interest).
- **ITA 2007 s.850C analysis:** Mr + Mrs Kapoor have power to enjoy the FIC's profits indirectly (the FIC's shares pass IHT value to their children — a familial benefit). But the FIC has a real commercial purpose: capital investment + value-freeze + succession-planning. HMRC PM226000 (Arrangements to secure CT rather than IT) is the analytic anchor — if the LLP's allocation to the FIC reflects the FIC's real commercial-entitlement profile (capital provision + risk-bearing) and the IHT-planning purpose is independent of income-splitting (which is what s.850C targets), the structure may stand. **Fact-intensive analysis required** — RUN session frames this as a structurally cleaner setup than Example 1 but not automatically safe; advisory review is essential.
- **Page surfaces the IHT-planning route via FIC corporate member** as a residual legitimate use, with cross-reference to §21.5 / §22.6 + the B5 FIC pillar.

### Example 4 — Mixed-membership + salaried-member regimes firing simultaneously

- **Patel Family LLP** has 4 members: Mr Patel (founder) + Mr Patel's adult son + Mr Patel's adult daughter + Patel Holdings Ltd (a corporate sleeve owned 100% by Mr Patel).
- **Patel Holdings Ltd analysis (mixed-membership):** ITA 2007 s.850C — excess profits (Patel Holdings has minimal arms-length commercial entitlement) + Mr Patel's power to enjoy (100% ownership) → reallocation to Mr Patel at marginal rate.
- **Adult son analysis (salaried-member):** profit share of £30k of which £25k is guaranteed-monthly (Condition A met — 83% disguised salary). Operational role limited to managing 3 properties (Condition B fail post-BlueCrest). Capital contribution £200 (Condition C met — capital << 25% × £25k = £6.25k). **All three Conditions met → salaried member.** PAYE + secondary Class 1 NIC + Apprenticeship Levy on £30k.
- **Adult daughter analysis (salaried-member):** £80k capital + £30k disguised salary. Condition C ratio = 267% — well above 25% — Condition C "OUT" → **NOT a salaried member.**
- **Mr Patel analysis:** LLP-wide strategic + operational authority → Condition B safe → not a salaried member regardless of capital position.
- **Operational consequence:** the LLP simultaneously faces (a) mixed-membership reallocation on the £100k allocated to Patel Holdings Ltd (s.850C) AND (b) salaried-member reclassification on the £30k allocated to the adult son (s.863A–G). Two independent anti-avoidance regimes fire on the same LLP; legal and operational complexity is high.
- **Page surfaces the dual-regime risk** for property LLPs combining hybrid + multi-generational structures.

### Example 5 — Cost of getting it wrong: backward-looking enforcement

- Continuing Example 1: HMRC enquiry opens in 2027 covering tax years 2023/24, 2024/25, 2025/26. HMRC asserts s.850C reallocation on the £180k corporate allocation in each year.
- **Reallocation effect:** Mrs Singh's taxable income for each year increases by £180k. Tax-at-marginal-rate addition each year ≈ £72k (40% blended). Three-year cumulative additional tax ≈ £216k. Less corporate CT credit on the £180k each year (£45k creditable) × 3 = £135k credit. Net additional personal tax ≈ £81k.
- **Plus interest at HMRC official rate** from each year's original due date to settlement (compounding factor over 3-5 years adds 10-20% to the principal).
- **Plus penalty exposure** under FA 2007 Sch 24 (careless / deliberate framework — adviser-recommended structure typically pleads "reasonable care" if professional opinion was obtained, but s.850C is well-established post-FA-2014 and adviser-led "ignore the rules" patterns face deliberate-or-careless characterisation).
- **Realistic exposure for a typical hybrid LLP failure:** £90k–£120k for a 3-year audit window on the Example-1 quantum.

### Example 6 — Restructure options if hybrid LLP is at mixed-membership risk

- **Option A:** Wind down the hybrid LLP; transfer property back to individual; revisit Section 24 question via direct incorporation (full LtdCo route per A1 + A9 + A20 cross-references).
- **Option B:** Restructure the corporate member to evidence real commercial entitlement (introduce arms-length services + risk-bearing role) — fact-intensive; legal + commercial review required to ensure substance not just form.
- **Option C:** Restructure to remove individual power-to-enjoy — corporate member becomes external (sold or transferred to unconnected third party). Practically rare; the typical landlord audience does not want to genuinely surrender economic control.
- **Option D:** Accept reallocation outcome and use the structure as a wrapper for legitimate asset-protection / IHT-planning purposes (corporate member as FIC for succession planning) — the operational shield rather than the income-tax avoidance.
- **Page surfaces the four options** as the operational decision tree for current hybrid-LLP operators.

## FAQ expansion (RUN session polishes prose; 10-12 FAQs target)

1. **Q: What is a hybrid LLP?**
   A: An LLP combining individual members with one or more corporate members — typically a UK limited company sitting alongside founder individuals. There is no statutory category "hybrid LLP" in the Limited Liability Partnerships Act 2000 — the term describes a configuration, not a separate entity type. LLPA 2000 imposes no restriction on member type (any "person" can be a member, including a body corporate per Interpretation Act 1978 Sch 1), so hybrid LLPs are lawful and routinely registered. Companies House operates no separate hybrid classification.

2. **Q: Why has the hybrid LLP structure been advisor-promoted to landlords?**
   A: Post-Section-24, individual landlords lose deduction of mortgage interest against rental income (replaced by 20% basic-rate tax credit). Limited companies are not within scope of s.272A — they deduct interest in full under CTA 2009 Part 5. The hybrid LLP structure attempts to retain LLP tax-transparency for individual members while allowing a corporate member to hold most of the profit share at CT rate (avoiding the personal HRT/ART that direct individual ownership would attract). On paper this looks like a Section-24 workaround. The mixed-membership rules at ITA 2007 ss.850C–E systematically dismantle the attraction for typical founder-LtdCo setups — see below.

3. **Q: What are the mixed-membership partnership rules?**
   A: ITA 2007 ss.850C, 850D, 850E (introduced by FA 2014 Sch 17) target the income-splitting / profit-retention play in mixed-membership partnerships. The regime applies a three-step test at each year-end: (i) is the non-individual member (corporate member) allocated profits in EXCESS of what arms-length commercial entitlement would justify? (ii) does any individual member have the POWER TO ENJOY those excess profits — directly or indirectly through family / connected-party channels? (iii) if yes to both, the excess profits are REALLOCATED to the individual member and taxed at the individual's marginal rate, with double-tax credit for any corporate CT paid.

4. **Q: What is the "excess profits" test under s.850C?**
   A: HMRC tests whether the corporate member's profit allocation exceeds its arms-length commercial entitlement. Where the corporate member is a passive sleeve with no operational role, no risk-bearing function, no third-party investment, and no genuine commercial purpose, its arms-length entitlement is typically NIL or near-NIL — any allocation to it is "excess". Where the corporate member has real commercial responsibilities (asset management; risk-bearing; capital provision; external commercial relationships), an arms-length fee or profit share can be defended. The test is fact-intensive; HMRC PM214000+ sets out the operative analytic framework.

5. **Q: What is the "power to enjoy" test under s.850D?**
   A: Defined expansively. Direct or indirect rights to receive, call for, or otherwise enjoy the profits; includes connected-party channels (family members; close-control relationships); substance-over-form discipline. A founder owning 100% of a corporate member has total power to enjoy. A founder's family-investment-company holding corporate-member shares for the founder's children still has power to enjoy via the family / IHT channel. Only where the corporate member is genuinely independent of the individual members (third-party investor; unconnected PE / fund; truly independent commercial relationship) is power to enjoy absent.

6. **Q: What is the reallocation mechanism?**
   A: Where the excess + power-to-enjoy tests both pass, the excess profits are reattributed to the individual member for income tax purposes. The individual member's taxable income increases by the reallocated amount, taxed at the individual's marginal rate. Corporate-side CT already paid on the reallocated profits is creditable against the individual's reallocated tax liability (avoiding pure double taxation). The operational outcome is generally worse than direct individual ownership would have been — the corporate intermediate layer adds compliance cost without the income-tax saving the structure attempted.

7. **Q: How does the mixed-membership regime interact with the salaried-member regime?**
   A: Different regimes; different targets; different mechanics. The mixed-membership regime (ss.850C–E) targets the corporate member of a hybrid LLP — reallocating excess corporate-member profits to individuals with power to enjoy. The salaried-member regime (ss.863A–G — see A11 this batch) targets individual members of an LLP — reclassifying them as deemed employees subject to PAYE + secondary Class 1 NIC. Both regimes can fire on the same LLP simultaneously: a hybrid LLP can face mixed-membership reallocation against its corporate member AND salaried-member reclassification of one or more individual members. The two regimes cover different anti-avoidance ground but share the FA 2014 origin (both at FA 2014 Sch 17).

8. **Q: For a founder-individual + own-LtdCo hybrid LLP, does the structure work?**
   A: No — typically not. The classic founder + own-LtdCo configuration fails the mixed-membership test: the corporate member has no real arms-length commercial entitlement (it is a passive sleeve), so any meaningful allocation is "excess"; the founder owns 100% of the corporate member, so power to enjoy is total. Result: reallocation under s.850C in full. The structure achieves no income-tax saving relative to direct individual ownership while adding LLP compliance overhead, accounts complexity, and audit-trail visibility to HMRC. The honest framing for the page: the founder-LtdCo configuration does NOT solve Section-24.

9. **Q: What residual legitimate uses of hybrid LLPs survive the mixed-membership regime?**
   A: Three categories of structure typically survive. (i) **External-investor inclusion** — a third-party PE / fund / institutional corporate member with no individual member power-to-enjoy link; the regime does not bite because the power-to-enjoy test fails. (ii) **Asset protection** — corporate member ring-fences a portion of LLP equity from individual member's personal creditors WHERE the corporate's profit share corresponds to genuine commercial entitlement for its risk-bearing role; structurally cleaner than the passive-sleeve configuration. (iii) **Succession planning via FIC** — corporate member is a Family Investment Company holding shares for the next-generation; the FIC's IHT-planning purpose can support an arms-length-style profit share that survives the s.850C test, with cross-reference to §21.5 + §22.6 + the B5 FIC pillar. All three uses require fact-intensive substance analysis.

10. **Q: What is the s.863G-style anti-avoidance overlay in the mixed-membership area?**
    A: Mixed-membership rules sit within a wider close-control anti-avoidance framework. PM234000 (Anti-avoidance) covers HMRC's general approach to mixed-membership avoidance arrangements. Cross-reference PM226000 (Arrangements to secure CT rather than IT) for the analytic anchor where the structure's substance is income-splitting in form-only. Cross-reference also CTA 2010 s.464A (residual anti-avoidance after the ss.464C/D omission per F-7) for hybrid-LLP arrangements that interact with close-company loans-to-participators territory. PM248000 (Close companies: loans to participators and arrangements conferring benefit on participator) is the bridge page.

11. **Q: What is the cost of getting it wrong?**
    A: Backward-looking reallocation across the HMRC enquiry window (typically 4-6 years). The individual member's taxable income for each affected year increases by the reallocated amount. Marginal-rate tax difference paid in addition (typically £30k–£90k per year for typical landlord-scale hybrid LLPs depending on quantum). Less corporate CT credit on the reallocated profits. Plus HMRC interest from each year's original due date. Plus penalty exposure under FA 2007 Sch 24 (careless / deliberate). Realistic cumulative exposure for a 3-5-year audit window on a typical structure: £90k–£250k.

12. **Q: What should current hybrid LLP operators do?**
    A: Member-by-member + year-by-year review of profit allocations against arms-length commercial entitlement. Documentation pack: corporate-member commercial-purpose evidence (services agreements; risk-bearing roles; third-party investments); power-to-enjoy analysis (ownership chains; family / connected-party channels); restructure options (see Example 6 in the brief). Engage specialist advisor for borderline structures; do not rely on adviser-promoted "this is fine" assurances without substance review. Consider four restructure options: (A) wind down + revisit incorporation route; (B) introduce real arms-length corporate-member entitlement; (C) move to external corporate member; (D) repurpose as asset-protection / succession-planning structure. Periodic review every 12-18 months given evolving HMRC enforcement patterns + post-BlueCrest cross-reading.
