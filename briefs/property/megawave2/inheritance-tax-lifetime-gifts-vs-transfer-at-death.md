# Inheritance Tax: Lifetime Gifts vs Transfer at Death — UK Decision Guide

## Statutory anchor

- Primary (the lifetime-gift framework — PETs, CLTs, the 7-year clock, and taper relief):
  - **IHTA 1984 s.3A** — PETs (potentially exempt transfers). Verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1984/51/section/3A on 2026-05-26. A transfer of value made by an individual to another individual, or to certain trusts (bare trusts and disabled person's trusts), that would otherwise be a chargeable transfer is a PET. Becomes fully exempt if the transferor survives 7 years; becomes a chargeable transfer (taxable at full or tapered rate) if the transferor dies within 7 years.
  - **IHTA 1984 s.3** — definition of transfer of value (the underlying disposition mechanic). A disposition causing the value of the transferor's estate to be less than it would be but for the disposition; the amount = the difference.
  - **IHTA 1984 s.7** — rates of tax. s.7(4) sets the taper-relief table for PETs that fail (donor dies within 3-7 years of the gift):
    - 3-4 years: 80% of full rate (effective 32%)
    - 4-5 years: 60% (24%)
    - 5-6 years: 40% (16%)
    - 6-7 years: 20% (8%)
    - **Taper applies to the tax payable, NOT to the value of the gift.** Taper only applies where the gift exceeds the NRB (gifts within the NRB attract no tax to taper).
  - **IHTA 1984 s.19** — annual exemption £3,000 per donor per tax year with one-year carry-back of unused exemption.
  - **IHTA 1984 s.20** — small gifts exemption £250 per recipient per year (cannot combine with the s.19 annual exemption to the same recipient).
  - **IHTA 1984 s.21** — normal expenditure out of income exemption (unlimited, provided three statutory conditions met: (a) made as part of normal expenditure; (b) taken with all other transfers it must be made out of income; (c) leaves transferor with sufficient income to maintain usual standard of living).
  - **IHTA 1984 s.22** — gifts in consideration of marriage (£5,000 parent; £2,500 grandparent / great-grandparent / one party to the marriage; £1,000 other persons).
- Primary (the death-transfer framework):
  - **IHTA 1984 s.4** — chargeable transfer on death. The deceased is treated as making a transfer of value immediately before death equal to the value of the estate.
  - **IHTA 1984 s.5** — meaning of "estate": aggregate of all property to which the deceased was beneficially entitled, with statutory exclusions (excluded property under s.6, settled property under s.49 in certain regimes, GROB property under FA 1986 s.102 — added back to the estate as if owned).
  - **IHTA 1984 s.18** — spouse / civil partner exemption (unlimited where both are long-term UK resident per s.6A test post-FA-2025).
  - **IHTA 1984 ss.8D-8M** — RNRB suite. RNRB available **only on death**; not available on lifetime gifts. £175k per individual; transferable to surviving spouse (TRNRB); claimed on IHT436 within 2 years of second death. HP §15.1.
  - **Headline rates:** 40% on the estate above available nil-rate bands; 36% reduced rate where ≥10% of net estate passes to qualifying charity (Sch 1A IHTA 1984).
- Primary (the CGT silent killer — uplift on death vs no uplift on PET):
  - **TCGA 1992 s.62** — "Death: general provisions". Verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12/section/62 on 2026-05-26. s.62(1)(a): "the assets of which a deceased person was competent to dispose shall for the purposes of this Act be deemed to be acquired on his death by the personal representatives or other person on whom they devolve for a consideration equal to their market value at the date of the death". The "**CGT uplift on death**" mechanic: the beneficiary inherits at probate value (market value at date of death); no CGT charge on the deceased; the gain accrued during the deceased's ownership is **wiped out for CGT purposes**.
  - **TCGA 1992 s.17 + s.18** — connected-person disposals. A lifetime gift to a connected person (child, grandchild) is a disposal at market value (s.17(1)) regardless of any actual consideration paid; the lifetime-gift CGT crystallises immediately on the donor at market-value-minus-acquisition-cost.
  - **TCGA 1992 s.260** — IHT-chargeable-transfer holdover relief. Available on gifts into discretionary trust (CLTs) and certain other IHT-chargeable transfers, but **NOT on outright lifetime gifts to individuals (PETs)** — this is the key asymmetry. A direct gift of appreciated BTL to a child is a CGT disposal at market value with no holdover available (s.260 needs an IHT-chargeable transfer; PETs are not IHT-chargeable at the time of gift).
  - **TCGA 1992 s.165** — gift relief for business assets. Available on gifts of qualifying trading-business assets (s.165 + Sch 7). **Not available on pure BTL** (investment, not trading, per *Pawson*). Effectively unavailable for the standard "gift the BTL to the children" scenario.
  - **The combined arithmetic:** appreciated family asset of £800k market value with £200k acquisition cost. Lifetime gift to children — CGT on £600k gain at 24% (residential property rate) = £144k immediate CGT, plus IHT exposure if donor dies within 7 years (taper-reduced). Hold-to-death route — no CGT on death; children inherit at £800k base cost (s.62 uplift); IHT on the £800k value at 40% above NRB/RNRB. Pivot question: does the IHT-saving from PET (relative to death-charge) exceed the CGT-cost of crystallising the gain on lifetime gift? For appreciated property the answer is frequently **no** — particularly where the donor is over 80 with limited 7-year-survival horizon. The CGT cost is certain and immediate; the IHT-saving requires donor survival.
- Primary (the GROB anti-avoidance rule):
  - **FA 1986 s.102 + s.102A + s.102B + Sch 20** — GROB. HP §15.3 + §22.11. Where the donor reserves benefit, the gift is treated as remaining in the estate for IHT. Most amateur "gift the house, keep living there" plans get caught here.
- Primary (the deed-of-variation route — post-death redirection):
  - **IHTA 1984 s.142** — deed of variation (IHT read-back). Verified verbatim. A beneficiary of an estate can vary their inheritance within 2 years of death; the variation is treated for IHT as if made by the deceased (no fresh PET / chargeable transfer by the original beneficiary).
  - **TCGA 1992 s.62(6)** — DoV CGT read-back. The CGT base cost of the asset received by the redirected beneficiary is the probate value (not the original beneficiary's market value at the date of variation). This makes DoV a uniquely powerful tool — it is the **only mechanism that achieves both IHT-savings AND CGT-uplift** simultaneously (because the asset still benefits from the s.62(1)(a) death uplift, and the s.142/s.62(6) variation reads back).
- House position references: §15.1 (NRB, RNRB, freezes — RNRB only at death); §15.2 (PETs, CLTs, 7-year clock, taper); §15.3 (GROB); §15.4 (BPR/APR April 2026 cap — affects lifetime gifts of qualifying business property differently than non-qualifying); §22.1 (Pawson — BTL not BPR); §22.2 (deed of variation s.142 mechanics); §22.5 (spouse exemption + transferable allowances). The page threads §15.1 + §15.2 + §22.2 + §22.5 with §15.3 + §15.4 + §22.1 cross-references at the relevant decision points.

**Mini-illustration: the total-tax matrix — Holloway-family appreciated-rental case**

Persona: Holloway-family-widower, 78, owns £800k BTL property (acquisition cost 1992 £200k; gain £600k), plus £1.2m other estate (cash + ISA). Two adult children equal beneficiaries. Husband died 2018; full TNRB used at first death (£325k); RNRB / TRNRB not available on the BTL because never used as residence.

**Route A: hold to death.**
- CGT: nil (s.62(1)(a) uplift wipes the £600k gain).
- IHT: net estate £2m; NRB £325k + TNRB £325k = £650k allowance; chargeable £1,350k @ 40% = £540,000 IHT.
- Children inherit BTL at £800k probate value.
- **Total tax: £540,000.**

**Route B: lifetime PET 5 years before death.** Widower aged 73 at gift; dies aged 78 (5y survival, taper band 5-6y = 40% of full rate).
- CGT (immediate on gift): s.17 MV disposal £600k gain × 24% = £144,000 paid by donor.
- IHT on failed PET: £800k gift exceeds NRB; PET uses cumulative NRB first; chargeable above-NRB at full or tapered rate. Worked-step: cumulative-NRB-allocation to the £800k PET = £325k; chargeable PET = £475k; full rate IHT = £190,000; tapered 40% (band 5-6y) = £76,000 IHT on the failed PET. Remaining estate at death = £1.2m other - £144k CGT paid = £1,056k - PET-consumed-NRB-now-used-up = no NRB left at death; full estate chargeable @ 40% = £422,400. Plus TNRB still available £325k applied to death estate = chargeable death estate £731k @ 40% = £292,400.
- **Subtotal IHT (failed PET + death): £76,000 + £292,400 = £368,400. Plus CGT £144,000. Total tax: £512,400. SAVING vs Route A: £27,600.**
- **Marginal saving but with material CGT cost certainty + 5-year-survival contingency.**

**Route C: lifetime PET with full 7-year survival.** Same widower aged 71 at gift; dies aged 78 (7y survival, PET fully exempt).
- CGT (immediate on gift): £144,000.
- IHT on PET: nil (fully exempt at 7 years).
- IHT on death estate £1.2m - £144k CGT = £1,056k; NRB £325k + TNRB £325k = £650k allowance; chargeable £406k @ 40% = £162,400.
- **Total tax: £162,400 + £144,000 = £306,400. SAVING vs Route A: £233,600.**
- **Material saving but requires 7-year donor survival from gift date — actuarially uncertain at age 71.**

**Route D: hold to death + s.142 DoV by children.** Children inherit (Route A baseline £540k IHT). Within 2 years of death, children redirect half their inheritance to grandchildren via DoV per s.142 + s.62(6) — uses children's spare NRB/RNRB allocation for next-generation planning.
- s.62(6) preserves CGT uplift to grandchildren (children NOT treated as disposing at MV).
- s.142 reads back for IHT: variation is treated as the deceased's gift, NOT the children's PET — no fresh 7-year-clock for children.
- Generation-skip uses NRB/RNRB at grandchildren level; saves estimated £100k-£200k IHT depending on grandchildren count + estate structure.
- **Combines IHT-saving AND CGT-uplift simultaneously.** The unique route in the lifetime-vs-death space.

**Actuarial reality check:** at age 78, 5-year survival probability ~75%; 7-year survival probability ~62% (ONS 2022/24 UK life-tables). Expected-tax outcome across routes weighted by survival probability is the discipline most amateur content omits.

## FAQ draft (10-12 entries, Stage 2 populated; RUN sub-agent polishes prose)

1. **Should I gift property now or pass it on when I die?**
   It depends on the asset's CGT position more than the IHT position. For appreciated property (low acquisition cost, high current value), the CGT cost of lifetime gifting can exceed the IHT-saving — particularly where donor age makes 7-year survival uncertain. For low-gain assets, lifetime gifting can start the 7-year clock at much lower CGT cost. The decision needs the full CGT + IHT total-tax framework, not just the IHT side.

2. **What is a Potentially Exempt Transfer (PET)?**
   Per IHTA 1984 s.3A, a lifetime gift from one individual to another (or to certain trusts) is a PET — potentially exempt from IHT provided the donor survives 7 years from the gift. If the donor dies within 7 years, the PET fails and becomes chargeable, with taper relief applying to gifts more than 3 years before death.

3. **How does taper relief work?**
   Per IHTA 1984 s.7(4), where a donor dies between 3 and 7 years after a failed PET, the IHT rate is tapered: 80% of full rate at 3-4 years (effective 32%), 60% at 4-5 years (24%), 40% at 5-6 years (16%), 20% at 6-7 years (8%). **Critical: taper applies to the TAX, not the value of the gift.** Gifts within the NRB attract no tax — so there's nothing to taper.

4. **What is the CGT uplift on death and why does it matter?**
   Per TCGA 1992 s.62(1)(a), when a person dies, all their assets are treated as acquired by the personal representatives at market value at the date of death. No CGT is charged on the deceased; any gain accrued during their ownership is wiped out for CGT purposes. This is the "**silent killer of lifetime gifting**" — for appreciated property, holding to death avoids potentially large CGT bills that lifetime gifting would crystallise immediately at the donor.

5. **Can I use holdover relief on a lifetime gift of BTL property?**
   Generally no. TCGA 1992 s.260 holdover is available only on IHT-chargeable transfers (CLTs into discretionary trust), and PETs to individuals are not IHT-chargeable at the time of gift. TCGA 1992 s.165 holdover is available only for qualifying trading-business assets — pure BTL is investment per *Pawson v HMRC* and is excluded. The result: a direct gift of appreciated BTL to a child crystallises the full CGT on the donor at market value with no holdover available.

6. **What if I give the property to a discretionary trust instead?**
   That changes the calculus. A gift into a non-settlor-interested discretionary trust is a Chargeable Lifetime Transfer (CLT) — 20% IHT on excess over the available NRB. Because it's IHT-chargeable, TCGA 1992 s.260 holdover IS available, meaning no immediate CGT. For BPR-qualifying business property (100% BPR below the £1m cap), the IHT is also nil — making CLT-into-trust the maximally CGT-and-IHT-efficient lifetime route for qualifying assets. For non-BPR-qualifying BTL property, the 20% CLT entry charge often makes this route prohibitive.

7. **What is the deed of variation and why is it special?**
   Per IHTA 1984 s.142 + TCGA 1992 s.62(6), beneficiaries can vary an inheritance within 2 years of death; the variation reads back to the deceased for IHT, and for CGT the beneficiary's redirection does NOT count as a market-value disposal. The DoV is the **only mechanism that achieves both IHT-savings AND CGT-uplift simultaneously**: the deceased's s.62 uplift still applies; the s.142 + s.62(6) reading-back means the redirected beneficiary inherits at probate value too. Common uses: skip-a-generation to grandchildren; equalise between siblings; redirect to charity (triggering 36% reduced rate); redirect into a discretionary trust.

8. **What lifetime exemptions stack with the 7-year clock?**
   The annual exemption of £3,000 per donor per tax year (s.19), with one-year carry-back of unused; the small gifts exemption of £250 per recipient per year (s.20, cannot combine with s.19 to the same recipient); the normal-expenditure-out-of-income exemption (s.21, unlimited subject to three statutory conditions); the marriage / civil partnership gifts (s.22 — £5k parent, £2.5k grandparent, £1k other); the spouse exemption (s.18, unlimited where receiving spouse is long-term UK resident); and the charity exemption (full IHT relief plus the Sch 1A 36% reduced rate trigger).

9. **What is the normal-expenditure-out-of-income exemption?**
   Per IHTA 1984 s.21, gifts are fully IHT-exempt provided three conditions are met: (a) the gift forms part of the donor's normal (i.e. habitual) expenditure; (b) taken with all other transfers, the gift is made out of income (not capital); (c) after allowing for all such transfers, the donor is left with sufficient income to maintain their usual standard of living. The exemption is unlimited in value but requires evidenced pattern — annual income/outgoings records, persistent gifting pattern. Many estate planners overlook s.21 because it requires documentation; for high-income donors, it can move multi-£100k of value out of the estate annually.

10. **Does the family home benefit differently from lifetime vs death transfer?**
    Yes, for two reasons. First, the RNRB (£175k per individual, ss.8D-8M) is available ONLY on death (where the home is closely inherited by lineal descendants) — lifetime gifts cannot capture RNRB. Second, the GROB rule per FA 1986 s.102 catches the "gift the home and live there" plan, so most family-home lifetime gifts fail unless the s.102B(4) shared-occupation carve-out applies or the donor pays full market rent. For the family home, hold-to-death is structurally favoured for both IHT-RNRB and CGT-uplift reasons.

11. **What is the cumulative-NRB rule for failed PETs?**
    Per *Bennett v IRC* [1995] STC 54 and IHTM04101+, where a donor makes multiple PETs and dies within 7 years, the available NRB is applied to each PET in chronological order from the earliest. Later failed PETs may exhaust NRB earlier; the death estate then receives the residual NRB (if any). The cumulation runs only over the 7-year window — earlier transfers fall out of cumulation. Multiple PETs near the NRB threshold need careful arithmetic; this is one of the easiest places to miscalculate IHT exposure.

12. **Where do I go next?**
    For taper-mechanics depth, see our `iht-lifetime-gifts-7-year-rule-property-taper` page. For the strategy-pillar take on mid-life landlord lifetime-gifting, see `iht-7-year-clock-property-gifting-mid-life-landlord-strategy`. For the broader IHT decision menu including FIC and trust routes, see `iht-property-investors-decision-framework-2026-onwards`. For DoV mechanics depth, see `deed-of-variation-property-estate-redirecting-inheritance-iht-saving`. For the family-home-specific decision pillar, see `inheritance-tax-and-the-family-home`. For general IHT orientation, see `inheritance-tax-a-brief-summary`. For BPR planning for business-asset estates, see `maximising-business-relief-to-reduce-inheritance-tax`.
