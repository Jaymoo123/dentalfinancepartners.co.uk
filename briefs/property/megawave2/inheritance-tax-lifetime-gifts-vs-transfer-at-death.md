# Inheritance Tax: Lifetime Gifts vs Transfer at Death — UK Decision Guide

## Statutory anchor

- Primary (the lifetime-gift framework — PETs, CLTs, the 7-year clock, and taper relief):
  - **IHTA 1984 s.3A** — PETs (potentially exempt transfers). Verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1984/51/section/3A on 2026-05-26. A transfer of value made by an individual to another individual, or to certain trusts (bare trusts and disabled person's trusts), that would otherwise be a chargeable transfer is a PET. Becomes fully exempt if the transferor survives 7 years; becomes a chargeable transfer (taxable at full or tapered rate) if the transferor dies within 7 years.
  - **IHTA 1984 s.3** — definition of transfer of value (the underlying disposition mechanic). A disposition causing the value of the transferor's estate to be less than it would be but for the disposition; the amount = the difference.
  - **IHTA 1984 s.7** — rates of tax. s.7(4) sets the taper-relief table for PETs that fail (donor dies within 3-7 years of the gift):
    - 3-4 years: 80% of full rate (effective 32%)
    - 4-5 years: 60% (24%)
    - 5-6 years: 40% (16%)
    - 6-7 years: 20% (8%)
    - **Taper applies to the tax payable, NOT to the value of the gift.** Taper only applies where the gift exceeds the NRB (gifts within the NRB attract no tax to taper).
  - **IHTA 1984 s.19** — annual exemption £3,000 per donor per tax year with one-year carry-back of unused exemption.
  - **IHTA 1984 s.20** — small gifts exemption £250 per recipient per year (cannot combine with the s.19 annual exemption to the same recipient).
  - **IHTA 1984 s.21** — normal expenditure out of income exemption (unlimited, provided three statutory conditions met: (a) made as part of normal expenditure; (b) taken with all other transfers it must be made out of income; (c) leaves transferor with sufficient income to maintain usual standard of living).
  - **IHTA 1984 s.22** — gifts in consideration of marriage (£5,000 parent; £2,500 grandparent / great-grandparent / one party to the marriage; £1,000 other persons).
- Primary (the death-transfer framework):
  - **IHTA 1984 s.4** — chargeable transfer on death. The deceased is treated as making a transfer of value immediately before death equal to the value of the estate.
  - **IHTA 1984 s.5** — meaning of "estate": aggregate of all property to which the deceased was beneficially entitled, with statutory exclusions (excluded property under s.6, settled property under s.49 in certain regimes, GROB property under FA 1986 s.102 — added back to the estate as if owned).
  - **IHTA 1984 s.18** — spouse / civil partner exemption (unlimited where both are long-term UK resident per s.6A test post-FA-2025).
  - **IHTA 1984 ss.8D-8M** — RNRB suite. RNRB available **only on death**; not available on lifetime gifts. £175k per individual; transferable to surviving spouse (TRNRB); claimed on IHT436 within 2 years of second death. HP §15.1.
  - **Headline rates:** 40% on the estate above available nil-rate bands; 36% reduced rate where ≥10% of net estate passes to qualifying charity (Sch 1A IHTA 1984).
- Primary (the CGT silent killer — uplift on death vs no uplift on PET):
  - **TCGA 1992 s.62** — "Death: general provisions". Verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12/section/62 on 2026-05-26. s.62(1)(a): "the assets of which a deceased person was competent to dispose shall for the purposes of this Act be deemed to be acquired on his death by the personal representatives or other person on whom they devolve for a consideration equal to their market value at the date of the death". The "**CGT uplift on death**" mechanic: the beneficiary inherits at probate value (market value at date of death); no CGT charge on the deceased; the gain accrued during the deceased's ownership is **wiped out for CGT purposes**.
  - **TCGA 1992 s.17 + s.18** — connected-person disposals. A lifetime gift to a connected person (child, grandchild) is a disposal at market value (s.17(1)) regardless of any actual consideration paid; the lifetime-gift CGT crystallises immediately on the donor at market-value-minus-acquisition-cost.
  - **TCGA 1992 s.260** — IHT-chargeable-transfer holdover relief. Available on gifts into discretionary trust (CLTs) and certain other IHT-chargeable transfers, but **NOT on outright lifetime gifts to individuals (PETs)** — this is the key asymmetry. A direct gift of appreciated BTL to a child is a CGT disposal at market value with no holdover available (s.260 needs an IHT-chargeable transfer; PETs are not IHT-chargeable at the time of gift).
  - **TCGA 1992 s.165** — gift relief for business assets. Available on gifts of qualifying trading-business assets (s.165 + Sch 7). **Not available on pure BTL** (investment, not trading, per *Pawson*). Effectively unavailable for the standard "gift the BTL to the children" scenario.
  - **The combined arithmetic:** appreciated family asset of £800k market value with £200k acquisition cost. Lifetime gift to children — CGT on £600k gain at 24% (residential property rate) = £144k immediate CGT, plus IHT exposure if donor dies within 7 years (taper-reduced). Hold-to-death route — no CGT on death; children inherit at £800k base cost (s.62 uplift); IHT on the £800k value at 40% above NRB/RNRB. Pivot question: does the IHT-saving from PET (relative to death-charge) exceed the CGT-cost of crystallising the gain on lifetime gift? For appreciated property the answer is frequently **no** — particularly where the donor is over 80 with limited 7-year-survival horizon. The CGT cost is certain and immediate; the IHT-saving requires donor survival.
- Primary (the GROB anti-avoidance rule):
  - **FA 1986 s.102 + s.102A + s.102B + Sch 20** — GROB. HP §15.3 + §22.11. Where the donor reserves benefit, the gift is treated as remaining in the estate for IHT. Most amateur "gift the house, keep living there" plans get caught here.
- Primary (the deed-of-variation route — post-death redirection):
  - **IHTA 1984 s.142** — deed of variation (IHT read-back). Verified verbatim. A beneficiary of an estate can vary their inheritance within 2 years of death; the variation is treated for IHT as if made by the deceased (no fresh PET / chargeable transfer by the original beneficiary).
  - **TCGA 1992 s.62(6)** — DoV CGT read-back. The CGT base cost of the asset received by the redirected beneficiary is the probate value (not the original beneficiary's market value at the date of variation). This makes DoV a uniquely powerful tool — it is the **only mechanism that achieves both IHT-savings AND CGT-uplift** simultaneously (because the asset still benefits from the s.62(1)(a) death uplift, and the s.142/s.62(6) variation reads back).
- House position references: §15.1 (NRB, RNRB, freezes — RNRB only at death); §15.2 (PETs, CLTs, 7-year clock, taper); §15.3 (GROB); §15.4 (BPR/APR April 2026 cap — affects lifetime gifts of qualifying business property differently than non-qualifying); §22.1 (Pawson — BTL not BPR); §22.2 (deed of variation s.142 mechanics); §22.5 (spouse exemption + transferable allowances). The page threads §15.1 + §15.2 + §22.2 + §22.5 with §15.3 + §15.4 + §22.1 cross-references at the relevant decision points.
