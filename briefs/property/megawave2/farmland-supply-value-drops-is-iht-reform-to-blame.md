# Farmland Supply Is Up, Value Is Down: Is the April 2026 IHT Reform Really to Blame?

## Statutory anchor

- Primary (the reform alleged to be moving the market): **IHTA 1984 Part V Chapter II ss.115-124C as amended by Finance Act 2025 / Finance (No. 2) Bill 2025-26 / Finance Act 2026**. The structural change is the introduction of a **£1 million combined BPR + APR cap** per estate effective for transfers of value on or after **6 April 2026**.
  - **Below £1m of qualifying property:** 100% relief as historically applied under IHTA 1984 s.116 (APR) and s.104 (BPR).
  - **Above £1m:** 50% relief, producing an effective IHT rate of 20% on the above-cap qualifying value.
  - **Combined, not separate:** the cap is a single £1m envelope shared across both APR and BPR — a mixed estate cannot stack a £1m APR allowance on top of a £1m BPR allowance.
  - **Anti-forestalling (lifetime transfers):** the new rules apply to lifetime transfers made **on or after 30 October 2024** (Autumn Budget 2024 announcement date) if the donor dies **on or after 6 April 2026** and within 7 years of the gift. Pre-announcement gifts are NOT caught. HP §15.4 verbatim.
  - **Trust anti-fragmentation:** trusts settled by the same settlor **on or after 30 October 2024** share a single £1m allowance divided across them; pre-30-October-2024 trusts each retain their own £1m. HP §15.4 verbatim.
- Primary (the underlying definitions — what "agricultural property" and "agricultural value" mean, which determine what the cap actually catches): **IHTA 1984 s.115** (definition; verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1984/51/section/115 on 2026-05-26).
  - **s.115(2):** "agricultural property" = agricultural land or pasture, woodland and any building used in connection with the intensive rearing of livestock or fish where occupying adjacent agricultural land, **cottages, farm buildings and farmhouses, together with the land occupied with them, as are of a character appropriate to the property**.
  - **s.115(3):** "agricultural value" = the value the property would have if subject to a perpetual covenant prohibiting use otherwise than as agricultural property. **Hope value (development potential, equestrian uplift, residential conversion value) sits OUTSIDE the relief and falls into the chargeable estate without protection.**
- Primary (the surviving carve-outs under the cap):
  - **s.116:** 100% rate where vacant possession or post-1-September-1995 tenancy; 50% rate otherwise. The cap sits ABOVE this layer — eligible property gets to 100% below £1m and 50% above; legacy 50%-rate property gets 50% across the board (uncapped at the 50% rate; the cap only changes the rate from 100% to 50% on the >£1m slice).
  - **s.117:** minimum periods — 2 years owner-occupied; 7 years let.
  - **Sch A1 extension:** UK agricultural property look-through extended to UK agricultural land per Autumn Budget 2025 announcement; FA 2026 enactment. HP §15.4 + Wave 6 cross-reference.
- Primary (the non-reform drivers — for proportional attribution):
  - **Agriculture Act 2020 + Direct Payments to Farmers (Legislative Continuity) Act 2020** — the post-Brexit subsidy transition. Basic Payment Scheme phased reduction 2021-2027; Sustainable Farming Incentive (SFI) phased introduction; Countryside Stewardship enhanced offers. Net effect on viability of mid-sized arable farms is a meaningful adjacent cause to any 2024-2026 market move and must be foregrounded in the attribution analysis.
  - **Environment Act 2021 + Biodiversity Net Gain (BNG) mandatory from February 2024** — created a parallel demand-side market for "biodiversity units" from landholders, partially offsetting the BPS withdrawal headwind but not uniformly across regions.
  - **Bank of England Bank Rate path** — the August 2023 peak of 5.25%, with gradual cuts from August 2024 onwards. Higher debt-service costs on farm borrowing were a 2023-2024 cycle headwind for leveraged-buyer demand independent of any tax change.
- Case-law anchors (for the qualification-gate that determines who gets caught vs not):
  - **Brander v HMRC [2010] UKUT 300 (TCC)** — agricultural and woodland estate; holistic-activity analysis for the BPR/APR split.
  - **McKenna's Executors v HMRC [2006] SpC 565** — the farmhouse "character appropriate" test.
  - **HMRC v Atkinson [2011] UKUT 506 (TCC)** — occupation of farmhouse during sickness; s.117 occupation continuity.
  - **Pawson v HMRC [2013] UKUT 050 (TCC)** — trading-vs-investment line (cross-reference for the BPR boundary on lettings-with-services).
- House position references: **§15.4** (BPR + APR £1m cap, locked); **§22.X.4** (Sch A1 look-through extension to UK agricultural land); **§22.1** (BPR investment-line for landlord BTL context — Pawson). No dedicated farmland-market-commentary HP section; this seed treats the market-evidence side as Stage 2 / RUN research territory, with the statutory anchor remaining the locked HP §15.4.

**Worked example: the £1.8m mixed farm — proportional driver attribution + decision framework**

Persona: Holloway-family. 280-acre arable farm in Cambridgeshire valued (2026) at £6,500/acre agricultural value + £450/acre hope value (potential warehouse-development edge). Total farm value: 280 × £6,950 = £1,946,000. Farmhouse value £700,000 (character-appropriate, s.115(2) covered). 4 cottages let on post-1995 ASTs to farmworkers (£280,000 combined value). Plus £85,000 BTL flat in Cambridge (no APR; full IHT exposure).

Estate composition 2026:
- Agricultural value (s.115): 280 acres × £6,500 = £1,820,000.
- Hope value: 280 × £450 = £126,000 (OUTSIDE APR — chargeable at 40%).
- Farmhouse (character-appropriate, s.115(2)): £700,000 (APR-eligible).
- Cottages let to farmworkers post-1-September-1995: £280,000 (APR-eligible at 100% under s.116).
- Total APR-eligible at 100% under pre-6-April-2026 architecture: £1,820,000 + £700,000 + £280,000 = £2,800,000.

Pre-6-April-2026 IHT outcome (hypothetical death):
- APR-eligible £2,800,000 → 100% relief, no IHT.
- Hope value £126,000 → 40% IHT = £50,400 (after NRB allocation).
- BTL flat £85,000 → 40% IHT (allocated against NRB).
- Net IHT exposure: low five-figures after NRB + RNRB.

Post-6-April-2026 IHT outcome (same death):
- APR-eligible up to £1,000,000 → 100% relief.
- APR-eligible above £1,000,000: £1,800,000 (£2,800,000 - £1,000,000) → 50% relief → effective IHT 20% on £1,800,000 = £360,000.
- Hope value £126,000 → 40% IHT = £50,400.
- BTL flat £85,000 → 40% IHT.
- Net IHT exposure: ~£430,000+ after NRB + RNRB.

## FAQ draft (10-12 entries, Stage 2 populated; RUN sub-agent polishes prose)

1. **Has UK farmland value really fallen since the Autumn 2024 Budget — what do the published numbers show?**
   The trade press has reported softening farmland values and rising listings since the 30 October 2024 announcement of the £1m APR/BPR cap. The major industry-published indices (Knight Frank Rural Property Index, Savills UK Farmland Market Survey, Strutt & Parker Farmland Database) each report quarterly. RUN session must source the most-recently-published quarterly figure with a verbatim quote and named-source attribution. Range and direction varies by region and parcel size; smaller parcels (under 100 acres) historically more resilient than large arable blocks.

2. **What is the IHT reform that triggered the commentary?**
   From 6 April 2026, the long-standing 100% relief on agricultural property (APR) under IHTA 1984 s.116 and business property (BPR) under s.104 is capped at a combined £1 million per estate. Above the cap, the relief rate falls to 50% (effective IHT rate of 20% on the above-cap slice). Lifetime gifts made on or after 30 October 2024 are caught if the donor dies on or after 6 April 2026 within 7 years of the gift (anti-forestalling). Trusts settled on or after 30 October 2024 by the same settlor share a single £1m allowance (anti-fragmentation).

3. **Is the IHT reform really the only driver of the market move, or are other forces at work?**
   Other forces are material. The Basic Payment Scheme is in phased withdrawal (Agriculture Act 2020); arable margins compressed through 2022-2024 from input cost inflation; Bank Rate peaked at 5.25% in August 2023 and only began cutting in August 2024 (raising debt-service costs on leveraged purchases through that window); the Biodiversity Net Gain regime became mandatory from February 2024 (creating offsetting demand for marginal land). Honest attribution gives the £1m cap a meaningful share for £1m+ farms but not the whole story.

4. **Who is actually affected by the £1m cap?**
   Estates where the combined APR + BPR qualifying property exceeds £1m at death. For UK farmland, that means agricultural-value (s.115(2)) above £1m — roughly 150-160+ acres at average UK farmland values, varying significantly by region. Smaller farms remain at 100% relief below £1m. Hope value (development potential) was never within APR scope and is unaffected by the cap, but remains chargeable at 40% as it always has been.

5. **What is "agricultural value" and how does it differ from market value?**
   IHTA 1984 s.115(3) defines agricultural value as the value the property would have if subject to a perpetual covenant prohibiting use otherwise than as agricultural property. Market value usually includes "hope value" — the premium reflecting potential alternative uses (residential conversion, equestrian, renewable energy, development edge). Hope value sits outside APR and falls into the chargeable estate at full market value. For mixed-use parcels, the agricultural-value / hope-value split is a valuation question typically resolved between estate valuers and HMRC SVU (Shares Valuation Unit) and contested at FTT where parties disagree.

6. **If I am holding above-£1m farmland today, should I sell before 6 April 2026?**
   Probably not in most cases. A forced sale crystallises CGT at potentially adverse cycle prices and removes the holding's capacity to be planned around (early-lifetime PETs, spouse + second-death planning, s.102B(4) farmhouse shared-occupation route). The page's decision framework: identify which quadrant you sit in (£1m+ agricultural-value-heavy vs hope-value-heavy vs sub-£1m), and only quadrant-(i) (£1m+ agricultural-value-heavy without hope value dominance) plus a confident health/horizon picture justifies a rushed sale. For most readers, the planning routes available within the post-6-April-2026 regime are better than a forced realisation.

7. **What planning routes survive the £1m cap?**
   Five routes worth analysing: (a) early-lifetime PETs where the donor has confident 7-year survival probability — the gift uses pre-cap mechanics if it sits before 30 October 2024 (now historic) or is otherwise outside anti-forestalling; (b) spouse-exemption-then-second-death planning — each spouse has their own £1m allowance, doubling the effective cap on a properly sequenced two-death estate; (c) s.102B(4) shared-occupation route for family-home farmhouses where the donor genuinely co-occupies with the donee bona fide; (d) Brander-line holistic-activity restructuring — re-balancing the estate's trading/investment composition so more qualifies; (e) IHTA 1984 s.142 deed of variation post-death where pre-death planning didn't run.

8. **Does the £1m cap apply per estate, or per individual property?**
   Per estate. The combined £1m allowance is single — a mixed estate cannot stack a £1m APR allowance on top of a £1m BPR allowance. The allocation between APR-eligible and BPR-eligible property is taxpayer-controlled subject to anti-avoidance, and the mixed-allocation worked example on our `iht-1m-bpr-apr-cap-mixed-trading-investing-landlord-allocation` page covers the operational mechanics in depth.

9. **Are trusts affected differently?**
   Yes. Trusts settled on or after 30 October 2024 by the same settlor are aggregated for a single £1m allowance under the anti-fragmentation rule. Pre-30-October-2024 trusts each retain their own £1m allowance — this is a meaningful planning consideration where a multi-trust structure was set up before announcement. RUN session must verify the trust anti-fragmentation provision's commencement status against the current Finance Act 2026 enactment state at write time; if FA 2026 is not yet enacted at write, frame the trust provision in conditional language.

10. **What about hope value — does the cap make hope-value-heavy land different?**
    Hope value never received APR, even before the cap. So the cap does not change the IHT treatment of hope value — it remains chargeable at 40% as it always has been. Holdings dominated by hope value (the rural edge of a major town, equestrian-suitable land near commuter belts, paddocks adjacent to villages with housing pressure) are not the cohort the reform is targeting. The press headline about "farmland sales driven by IHT reform" tends to lump these in with genuinely-affected agricultural-value-heavy holdings, but the planning analysis is materially different.

11. **What about the s.117 minimum periods — does pre-6-April-2026 occupation count?**
    Yes. s.117 (2-year owner-occupation; 7-year-let) is a separate qualification gate from the rate / cap layer. Occupation periods before 6 April 2026 count fully toward the s.117 minimum. So a farmer who took occupation on 7 April 2024 satisfies the 2-year gate from 6 April 2026 onwards and the property remains APR-eligible — at 100% below £1m and 50% above £1m, per the new architecture.
