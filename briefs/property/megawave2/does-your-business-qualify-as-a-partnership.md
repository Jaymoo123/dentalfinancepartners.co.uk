# Does Your Property Business Qualify as a Partnership? Partnership Act 1890 vs Co-Ownership for Landlords

## Statutory anchor

- **Primary (definition):** Partnership Act 1890 (PA 1890) s.1(1) — "Partnership is the relation which subsists between persons carrying on a business in common with a view of profit." Three cumulative tests: (1) two or more persons; (2) carrying on a business (not merely owning property); (3) in common (joint conduct, not parallel solo); (4) with a view of profit (intent to make profit, even if loss-making in fact). All four tests must be satisfied for partnership to exist.
- **Primary (rules for determining existence — the negative tests):** PA 1890 s.2 — rules for determining the existence of partnership. **s.2(1) is the load-bearing rule for property:** "Joint tenancy, tenancy in common, joint property, common property, or part ownership does not of itself create a partnership as to anything so held or owned, whether the tenants or owners do or do not share any profits made by the use thereof." **Passive co-ownership of rental property is NOT a partnership** by itself, even when the co-owners share rental profits — verbatim. s.2(2): sharing gross returns alone does not create partnership. s.2(3): receipt of share of profits is **prima facie evidence** of partnership but not conclusive — **five rebuttal heads at paragraphs (a)–(e)** ((a) debt repayment out of profits; (b) servant/agent remuneration by share of profits; (c) widow/child/surviving civil partner annuity (the 2005 Civil Partnership Act inserted "surviving civil partner"); (d) advance of money by way of loan with profit-contingent interest; (e) annuity in consideration of goodwill sale). **Stage 2 correction: seed text earlier said "six rebuttal heads" — verified against `https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/Vict/53-54/39/section/2` on 2026-05-27: five paragraphs (a)–(e). Minor §16.40 housekeeping correction; no flag raised.**
- **Primary (HMRC operative guidance):** HMRC Business Income Manual BIM72005-BIM72165 (Partnerships) — operative guidance on identifying when a partnership exists for income tax purposes. **BIM72015 specifically addresses property co-ownership** and confirms the s.2(1) position — joint ownership of property "does not necessarily mean that there is a partnership". HMRC pursues the substance-over-form test: whether the parties are merely co-owners receiving their share of joint income, or whether they are carrying on a business together. Stage 2 verifies BIM72005+ at write time per §16.35.
- **Primary (HMRC operative guidance — partnership tax return obligation):** TMA 1970 s.12AA — partnership statement / return obligation. SA800 partnership tax return is required where a partnership exists — penalty for non-filing accrues per partner. **Filing the SA800 is one of the strongest objective indicators** HMRC uses to test partnership existence. The decision to file vs not file SA800 is the **operative trigger** for most landlord-side partnership questions — the wrong call has both downside risks (SA800 penalty for missed filing on real partnership) and upside risks (locked-in partnership treatment with SDLT Sch 15 + s.59 transfer-of-going-concern implications when subsequently incorporating).
- **Supporting (income tax — taxation framework):** ITTOIA 2005 Part 9 (Partnerships) ss.846-863 — taxation of partnership profits as transparent. Each partner taxed individually on their share of profits per the profit-sharing agreement (PA 1890 s.24(1) default: equal shares if no contrary agreement). ITA 2007 s.852 (notional trade for each partner). Property partnerships taxed under ITTOIA 2005 Part 3 (property income) at partner level — not as a single entity.
- **Supporting (capital gains — taxation framework):** TCGA 1992 s.59 (partnerships) + HMRC SP D12 (Partnership statement of practice on CGT) — each partner has fractional interest in each partnership chargeable asset; disposals by partnership are notional disposals by each partner of fractional interest. SP D12 sets out HMRC's operative CGT treatment of partnership asset movements (introduction / withdrawal / share rebasing / dissolution).
- **Supporting (SDLT — partnership transfers):** FA 2003 Sch 15 (the partnership SDLT regime). **A property "partnership" for SDLT Sch 15 purposes is defined more narrowly than for income tax** — para 1 Sch 15 requires the partnership to carry on a business (substance test, not mere ownership). The Sch 15 partnership-transfer reliefs (sum-of-lower-proportions on transfer-in, sum-of-lower-proportions on transfer-out, intra-partnership transfers) are unavailable to mere co-ownership structures. This is operationally **the largest single tax-saving consequence** of partnership-vs-co-ownership classification for landlords contemplating incorporation: Sch 15 para 10 incorporation relief can dramatically reduce SDLT on transferring a partnership-held portfolio to a connected LtdCo (no SDLT on the partners' own proportions transferring into a LtdCo they own in same shares), where the same transfer from co-ownership would attract full SDLT on the market-value transfer.
- **Supporting (VAT — disaggregation):** where multiple persons co-own and co-let property under separate VAT registrations to keep below threshold, HMRC may apply disaggregation under VATA 1994 Sch 1 para 1A (artificial separation). Question of partnership existence interacts with disaggregation question — separate enquiries but factually related.
- **House position reference:** §21 (Ltd Co + FIC family — partnership-vs-LtdCo entity choice). §22 (IHT — partnership BPR question — Pawson investment line in §22.1). §3 (incorporation mechanics — partnership status is the gating test for SDLT Sch 15 reliefs on incorporation; see Ltd Co incorporation mechanics depth in §3). **New HP coverage gap:** §11 + §11.A cover ECCTA Part 1 (companies-side); §11.B is being requested by F-1 (M2-A-B1) for ECCTA Part 2 (LP reforms). PA 1890 / general partnership statutory architecture is NOT currently a locked HP. ## Worked-example data (RUN session uses these as canvas)

### Example 1 — Co-ownership IS NOT partnership (the load-bearing s.2(1) demonstration)

- **Mr and Mrs Patel** jointly own three BTL flats as tenants-in-common (60/40 beneficial split via Form 17 declaration). They pool gross rental receipts in a joint bank account, agree expense allocation each year over Sunday lunch, and split net rental income 60/40 on their personal SA returns. No written agreement; no joint business name; no holding-out to lenders or tenants; no joint borrowing beyond the standard joint-and-several mortgage on each property.
- **PA 1890 s.2(1) result:** **NOT a partnership.** The four s.1 tests fail at "carrying on a business in common" — they are co-owners receiving rental income proportionate to beneficial entitlement; the s.2(1) negative explicitly covers this configuration. **No SA800 obligation under TMA 1970 s.12AA.** Each spouse files SA100 reporting their beneficial share per HMRC PIM1030.

### Example 2 — Family operation that DOES qualify as partnership

- **The Singh Family** (father, mother, two adult sons) operate a 14-property HMO portfolio. Properties are held in joint names (varying combinations). Single bank account in the name "Singh Family Properties" used for all rental receipts + supplier payments. Active joint management — father runs lender relationships; mother handles compliance + bookkeeping; sons each manage 5 properties operationally. Joint borrowing facility (£900k portfolio loan secured across all 14 properties under joint-and-several covenants). Trading-name signage on property boards.
- **PA 1890 s.1 result:** **IS a partnership.** Four tests all satisfied — two-plus persons; carrying on a business (active joint management goes far beyond passive co-ownership per *Ramsay v HMRC* [2013] UKUT 226 threshold pattern); in common (joint account, joint borrowing, joint business name); with a view of profit. **SA800 obligation under TMA 1970 s.12AA from inception** — penalty exposure if family has filed individually for past years. Profits taxed via ITTOIA 2005 Part 9 ss.846+ at each partner's marginal rate; capital movements via TCGA 1992 s.59 + SP D12.

### Example 3 — Partnership unlocks SDLT Sch 15 incorporation relief (the operative tax-saving)

- Continuing Example 2: in 2027 the Singh family decides to transfer the portfolio to a newly-formed **Singh Properties Ltd** owned in matching 25%/25%/25%/25% shares. Aggregate market value £4.2m.
- **Without partnership status** (had Example 1 framing applied — passive co-ownership): SDLT on company purchase per FA 2003 Sch 4ZA stack with 3% HRAD surcharge (and **no MDR** post-FA(No.2) 2024 s.7 abolition 1 June 2024 per §1.M) on each dwelling separately ≈ £210k-£270k SDLT depending on residential band stack across the 14 properties.
- **With partnership status** (Example 2 framing): FA 2003 Sch 15 para 10 sum-of-lower-proportions (SLP) relief. Because the four partners own the LtdCo in same 25/25/25/25 shares, the SLP calculation reduces the chargeable consideration on each partner's own proportion to nil. **SDLT cost approximately £0** on the partner-side proportion (negligible incidental SDLT on any unmatched proportion).
- **The SDLT saving (~£210k–£270k) is the largest single tax-saving consequence of partnership-vs-co-ownership classification.** RUN session frames this as the operative motivator for landlord audiences to do the partnership-existence analysis BEFORE the incorporation conversation. Forward-link to existing `partnership-sdlt-relief-schedule-15-fa-2003-incorporation-sum-lower-proportions` for the mechanics.

### Example 4 — SA800 penalty exposure where partnership exists but family has filed individually

- The Singh family from Example 2 has been operating the partnership configuration since 2022 but has filed individually on SA100s (each reporting share of joint rental income per PIM1030) every year. HMRC enquiry in 2027 reopens 2022/23 through 2025/26 (four years) and asserts partnership existence with SA800 obligation from 2022/23.
- **Penalty exposure under FA 2009 Sch 55:** late-filing penalty per partner per missed SA800 return. **£100 initial late-filing penalty × 4 partners × 4 years = £1,600** baseline; +£10 daily penalties continuing for up to 90 days where >3 months late (£900 × 4 partners × 4 years = up to £14,400); +6-month + 12-month tax-geared penalties on the partnership's tax-geared (no partnership-level tax but daily-penalty risk dominates). RUN session frames this as the operational reason landlord audiences need to know whether they are a partnership BEFORE the question becomes a penalty problem.

### Example 5 — Holding-out under PA 1890 s.14 (the third-party-protection edge)

- **Mrs Kapoor** owns 4 BTL flats in her sole name. Her cousin **Mr Verma** introduces a high-net-worth tenant to one of the flats and tells the tenant "I'm a partner in the family property business with my cousin — call me if there's a problem". The tenant pays a £30,000 deposit and shortly after suffers damage from a leak; the tenant sues Mrs Kapoor AND Mr Verma jointly.
- **PA 1890 s.14 holding-out:** Mr Verma is liable as a partner to that tenant even though no actual partnership exists between him and Mrs Kapoor; he held himself out as a partner and the tenant relied on it. The page surfaces this risk for landlord audiences using informal "partnership" language with non-co-owners (lettings managers, family helpers) without considering the third-party-protection consequence.

### Example 6 — PA 1890 s.24(1) default equal shares + the operative reason for a written partnership agreement

- **Two unrelated developers** form an oral arrangement to refurb-and-let two adjacent properties. No written agreement; Developer A contributes £400k cash; Developer B contributes operational time + the lender introduction. Profits over three years total £180k.
- **PA 1890 s.24(1) default:** equal shares (£90k each) absent contrary agreement. Developer A had assumed 80/20 split reflecting capital contribution; the absence of a written agreement defaults to equal shares.

## FAQ expansion (RUN session polishes prose; 10-12 FAQs target)

1. **Q: What is the legal definition of a partnership under UK law?**
   A: Partnership Act 1890 s.1(1) defines partnership as "the relation which subsists between persons carrying on a business in common with a view of profit". Four cumulative tests must all be satisfied: (1) two or more persons; (2) carrying on a business (not merely owning property); (3) in common (joint conduct, not parallel solo); (4) with a view of profit. Fail any one test and there is no partnership.

2. **Q: Does joint ownership of rental property make me a partnership?**
   A: No — not by itself. Partnership Act 1890 s.2(1) explicitly provides that "joint tenancy, tenancy in common, joint property, common property, or part ownership does not of itself create a partnership as to anything so held or owned, whether the tenants or owners do or do not share any profits made by the use thereof". This is the load-bearing negative test for property audiences. Passive co-ownership — even where co-owners share rental profits — is NOT a partnership. HMRC's operative guidance at BIM72015 confirms this position.

3. **Q: When does a husband-and-wife jointly-letting a property qualify as a partnership?**
   A: Typically NOT. Most husband-and-wife letting arrangements are co-ownership receiving rental income proportionate to beneficial entitlement (under PIM1030 default 50/50 unless a Form 17 declaration shifts the split to actual beneficial shares). To rise to partnership status, the couple must carry on a business — active joint management, joint borrowing, joint business name, joint marketing, holding-out to third parties as partners. The mere fact of joint legal ownership plus shared rent does not satisfy s.1.

4. **Q: When does a family operating multiple properties together qualify as a partnership?**
   A: Depends on substance. Separate properties owned individually do not aggregate into a partnership. A jointly owned + jointly managed multi-property portfolio with joint borrowing, joint marketing, single business bank account, and a recognisable trading identity increasingly looks like one. HMRC applies a substance-over-form test under BIM72015; courts (per *Khan v Miah*) look at whether the parties are carrying on a business in common rather than just sharing income from co-owned assets.

5. **Q: What is the SA800 partnership tax return, and when do we have to file it?**
   A: TMA 1970 s.12AA imposes the SA800 partnership tax return obligation on partnerships. The partnership files SA800 reporting partnership profits and the allocation between partners; each partner separately files their own SA100 reporting their share. SA800 is mandatory where a partnership exists — failure to file accrues late-filing penalties per partner per year under FA 2009 Sch 55. Filing the SA800 is one of the strongest objective indicators HMRC uses to test partnership existence — both the absence of SA800 where partnership exists (penalty exposure) and the presence of SA800 where no partnership exists (locked-in partnership treatment with downstream consequences) cut against the taxpayer.

6. **Q: What is the substance-over-form test HMRC applies under BIM72015?**
   A: BIM72015 is the load-bearing HMRC operative guidance on joint ownership of property and partnership existence. HMRC looks at the substance of the arrangement, not the labels — including joint bank account, single management agreement, joint business name, holding-out to third parties (lenders, tenants, advisors), joint borrowing under joint-and-several covenants, joint marketing, and the degree of active joint management beyond mere co-ownership. None of these factors is individually decisive; HMRC weights them collectively against the s.1 four-test gate.

7. **Q: What is the biggest tax consequence of partnership-vs-co-ownership classification for property landlords?**
   A: SDLT Sch 15 partnership-incorporation relief. Where a genuine partnership transfers a portfolio to a connected limited company owned in matching proportions, FA 2003 Sch 15 para 10 sum-of-lower-proportions (SLP) calculation can reduce the SDLT on the partner-side proportions to nil. The same transfer from a bare co-ownership configuration attracts full SDLT at the residential rate stack with 3% HRAD surcharge — typically £150,000–£300,000 on a mid-size portfolio. The partnership-versus-co-ownership classification is therefore worth six figures on a typical incorporation event, and is the operative motivator for landlord audiences to settle the question definitively before contemplating incorporation. Forward-link to the existing Sch 15 mechanics page.

8. **Q: Is a "partnership" for SDLT Sch 15 the same as a "partnership" for income tax?**
   A: Not quite. For income tax, the PA 1890 s.1 framework governs; HMRC's BIM72005+ operative guidance applies. For SDLT Sch 15, FA 2003 Sch 15 para 1 imposes an additional "business" gate — the partnership must actually carry on a business (i.e. fail the s.2(1) negative, which is the same gate). In practice the two definitions align — a real partnership for income tax purposes is also a Sch 15 partnership; a bare co-ownership for income tax purposes is also NOT a Sch 15 partnership — but the boundary cases require careful analysis. Stage 2 surfaces both gates.

9. **Q: What is "holding-out" liability under PA 1890 s.14?**
   A: PA 1890 s.14 imposes partner-equivalent liability on a person who, by words spoken or written or by conduct, represents themselves (or knowingly allows themselves to be represented) as a partner in a firm, where a third party relies on that representation. The risk applies even where no actual partnership exists. For landlord audiences: informal "I'm a partner in the family business" representations to tenants, lenders, or advisors can crystallise s.14 liability — the apparent partner becomes liable to the third party for partnership-style obligations.

10. **Q: What is the default profit-sharing rule under PA 1890 s.24(1)?**
    A: Equal shares between all partners, absent a contrary written or oral agreement. Where two partners contribute unequally (one contributes £400k capital, the other contributes time and a lender introduction), the s.24(1) default still splits profits equally unless the partners have explicitly agreed otherwise. This is the operative reason a written partnership agreement is essential for any real partnership — it overrides the s.24(1) default with the partners' actual intended split. Forward-link to future A16 partnership-agreement page.

11. **Q: What is the difference between a general partnership, a limited partnership, and an LLP?**
    A: Three distinct statutory regimes. **General partnership** (PA 1890) — two-plus persons carrying on business in common; partners jointly and severally liable for partnership debts; no separate legal personality. **Limited partnership** (LPA 1907) — at least one general partner with unlimited liability + one or more limited partners with liability capped at capital contribution; registration with Companies House; post-ECCTA Part 2 reforms bring LPs under stricter compliance per §11.B (forward-link to A3 `companies-house-changes-limited-partnership-requirements`). **Limited liability partnership** (LLPA 2000) — body corporate with limited liability for all members; separate legal personality; tax-transparent default (forward-link to A11 + A10 LLP siblings). The choice depends on liability profile, compliance burden, and reader configuration.
