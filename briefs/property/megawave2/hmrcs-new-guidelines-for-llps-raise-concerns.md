# HMRC's New Guidelines for LLPs Raise Concerns: The Post-BlueCrest Salaried Member Rules Update for Property LLPs

## Statutory anchor

- **Primary (the regime):** Income Tax Act 2007 (ITA 2007) ss.863A-863G — salaried member rules for LLPs. Introduced by FA 2014 with effect from 6 April 2014. Operative effect: where an LLP member is treated as a "salaried member" under the regime, the LLP itself becomes a deemed employer in respect of that member, the member becomes a deemed employee, profit shares are reclassified as employment income subject to PAYE, and the LLP becomes liable for secondary Class 1 NIC + Apprenticeship Levy on the member's profit share.
- **Primary (the three conditions — all must be satisfied):** ITA 2007 s.863A(2). A member is a salaried member if and only if all three of the following Conditions are met:
  - **Condition A (s.863B):** ≥80% of total amount payable by the LLP to the member is "disguised salary" (broadly: fixed or guaranteed amounts not dependent on the LLP's overall profits/losses). The 80% threshold is the gateway — fail Condition A and the member is OUT of the regime regardless of the other Conditions.
  - **Condition B (s.863C):** The member does NOT have "significant influence over the affairs of the partnership" — i.e. members WITH significant influence are OUT of the regime. **Post-BlueCrest, this Condition is the area of concern** — HMRC's narrowed reading reduces the population of members who qualify for this exclusion.
  - **Condition C (s.863D):** The member's contribution to the LLP (the "Capital Contribution") is LESS than 25% of the disguised salary in the relevant tax year. Members with capital contribution ≥25% of disguised salary are OUT of the regime. **Practically the most operative safe harbour for property LLPs** — easier to demonstrate than Condition B significant influence.
- **Primary (the BlueCrest Supreme Court decision):** *BlueCrest Capital Management (UK) LLP v HMRC* [2024] UKSC 33 (decided October 2024 — Stage 2 verifies exact date + neutral citation at write time). Supreme Court held, reversing the Upper Tribunal in part, that "significant influence over the affairs of the partnership" under Condition B (s.863C) does NOT require influence over the WHOLE LLP business — influence over a significant part of the LLP's affairs can be enough. **Practical effect:** narrower safe harbour than the FTT + UT had been applying. Members who run a single book / strategy / property within a multi-strand LLP may NOT have significant influence over the affairs of the partnership as a whole, and therefore may NOT qualify for the Condition B exclusion.
- **Primary (the HMRC guidance update):** HMRC Partnership Manual **PM250000+** — the operative salaried-member-rules guidance index. **STAGE 2 DRIFT CATCH (2026-05-27, §16.40 pattern — F-9 raised):** Stage 1 seed cited "PM276000+" as the HMRC salaried-member guidance entry point. **VERIFIED 2026-05-27 against gov.uk: PM276000 is HMRC's Construction Industry Scheme (CIS) guidance, NOT salaried-member rules.** The correct entry point is PM250000 (Salaried member rules: contents) verified at `https://www.gov.uk/hmrc-internal-manuals/partnership-manual/pm250000`. The PM250000 index lists the nine operative sub-pages: PM251000 (Salaried member guidance) → PM253000 (Who can be a salaried member?) → PM254000 (Tests to use) → PM255000 (Condition A — disguised salary) → **PM256000 (Condition B — significant influence; LOAD-BEARING for BlueCrest narrowing — 12 sub-sub-pages PM256100+ covering LLP agreements, types of influence, FCA functions, size considerations, hierarchies, delegated powers, indirect influence, group structures)** → PM258000 (Condition C — contributions) → PM259000 (Anti-avoidance) → PM260000 (Implementation) → PM261000 (Global structures). Key analytic positions Stage 1 seed expected to find: (a) narrower view of what counts as significant influence — operational team leadership over a single book / strategy / property does NOT automatically count; (b) members in matrix / silo'd LLP structures may need to demonstrate cross-LLP influence to qualify; (c) Conditions are conjunctive — falling out of ANY ONE Condition exits the regime (Condition C capital-contribution route remains operative).
- **Primary (HMRC's anti-avoidance overlay):** ITA 2007 s.863G — anti-avoidance. Disregards arrangements one of the main purposes of which is to secure that the salaried-member rules do not apply. **Operative concern:** post-BlueCrest, contrived Condition C capital-contribution arrangements (e.g. round-trip loans from LLP to member to fund the capital contribution; sham contributions; arrangements where the capital is at no real economic risk) face s.863G challenge. - **Supporting (LLP tax-transparency baseline):** ITTOIA 2005 Part 9 (partnerships — applied to LLPs by ITTOIA 2005 s.863 + CTA 2009 s.1273 + TCGA 1992 s.59A). Default treatment: LLP is tax-transparent; each member taxed individually on profit share at member's marginal rate as trading or property income depending on LLP activity. **Salaried-member regime is the EXCEPTION** to this baseline — operative only where all three Conditions met.
- **Supporting (employer NIC consequence):** Social Security Contributions and Benefits Act 1992 ss.6-10A — secondary Class 1 NIC. LLP becomes liable for secondary Class 1 NIC at the operative rate (currently 15% from 6 April 2026 per FA 2026 — Stage 2 verifies rate at write time per §16.27 + §16.42 rate-by-reference discipline) on the deemed-employment-income profit share. Plus Apprenticeship Levy at 0.5% above the £15k allowance per FA 2016 ss.99-121.
- **Supporting (LLP statutory framework):** Limited Liability Partnerships Act 2000 (LLPA 2000) — primary statute creating LLP as a body corporate with members; member's liability limited; default tax-transparent treatment for income tax + CGT.

## Worked-example data (RUN session uses these as canvas)

### Example 1 — Family property LLP: member-by-member status analysis

- **Kapoor Family LLP** holds 12 BTL properties. Four members: Member 1 (Mrs Kapoor — matriarch); Member 2 (Mr Kapoor — semi-retired); Member 3 (Adult Son — manages 4 properties operationally); Member 4 (Adult Daughter — minimal LLP involvement, drawing fixed monthly amount).
- **Member 1 (Mrs Kapoor) analysis:** decides LLP-wide strategy, hires accountant + property manager, signs all mortgages, allocates capital, sets profit-share. **Condition B (PM256000 post-BlueCrest)** — significant influence over LLP affairs as a whole, NOT just one property strand. **Out of regime via Condition B.** Status: full LLP member with profit-share taxed transparently per ITTOIA 2005 s.863.
- **Member 2 (Mr Kapoor) analysis:** contributed £400k start-up capital; receives £24k/year minimum drawing; capital contribution at year-end ≈ £450k; minimum drawing of £24k is disguised salary on the Condition A test. **Condition C (PM258000)** — capital contribution £450k vs disguised salary £24k → capital >> 25% × £24k = £6k → Condition C met (capital ≥ 25% of disguised salary takes member OUT of regime). **Out of regime via Condition C.**
- **Member 3 (Adult Son) analysis:** post-BlueCrest narrower reading puts Member 3 at risk on Condition B — operational management of 4 properties may not count as "significant influence over the affairs of the partnership" as a whole. Condition A test: of £45k drawing, £40k is fixed and £5k is variable based on overall LLP profit → 89% disguised salary → Condition A met (>80%). Condition C test: capital contribution £80k vs disguised salary £40k → capital ratio 200% × disguised salary → well above 25% → **Out of regime via Condition C.** Status: safe due to capital-contribution route.
- **Member 4 (Adult Daughter) analysis:** nominal £100 capital; receives £36k/year guaranteed monthly drawing regardless of LLP profitability. Condition A test: 100% disguised salary → met. Condition B test: no operational role; no LLP-wide influence; on the post-BlueCrest reading clearly fails. Condition C test: £100 vs £36k disguised salary → capital ratio 0.28% → fails (< 25%). **ALL THREE Conditions met → SALARIED MEMBER.** Consequences: LLP becomes deemed employer; £36k reclassified as employment income; PAYE on Member 4's drawings; secondary Class 1 NIC at LLP level on the £36k.

### Example 2 — Salaried-member cost computation (the cost of getting it wrong)

- Continuing Example 1, Member 4's £36k profit share reclassified as employment income.
- **LLP-side cost:** secondary Class 1 NIC at 15% from 6 April 2026 (per §21.4 lock) on £36k above the secondary threshold (typically £9.1k current) = (£36k − £9.1k) × 15% = **£4,035**. Apprenticeship Levy at 0.5% above the £15k allowance — applied at LLP level if total annual pay bill > £3m (most family property LLPs below this; ignore for the example).
- **Member 4 side:** income tax on £36k at applicable marginal rate (vs partnership profit share treatment at same marginal rate — neutral on income tax) + employee Class 1 primary NIC at 8% on band above primary threshold (typically £12.6k) = (£36k − £12.6k) × 8% = **£1,872**.
- **Cumulative-year retrospective exposure:** where HMRC enquiry post-BlueCrest reclassifies retrospectively across 3 prior tax years on undocumented structure: LLP-side £4,035 × 3 = £12,105 secondary NIC + Member-side £1,872 × 3 = £5,616 primary NIC + late-payment-penalty exposure under Sch 56 + interest at HMRC official rate from each year's original due date. Cash exposure to LLP often £15k–£25k per affected member depending on tax-year coverage + interest accrual.

### Example 3 — Condition C capital-contribution safe harbour (the operative property-LLP play)

- **JV Developer LLP** has 6 members; each contributed £75k start-up capital. Annual drawings vary by overall LLP profit (£20k–£60k per year). Condition C analysis at each year-end:
  - Year 1 capital balance £75k; drawing £40k of which £20k disguised salary (fixed-minimum guarantee) + £20k variable profit-share. Disguised salary = £20k. Capital ratio = £75k ÷ £20k = 375% × disguised salary. **Condition C met (capital ≥ 25%): out of regime.**
  - Year 5 capital balance after various drawings + injections = £60k; disguised salary £25k. Ratio = 240% — still well above 25%. Safe.
- **Operational discipline:** member-by-member documentation of capital balance at year-end + disguised-salary calculation each year. RUN session frames this as the practical operational safe harbour — easier to evidence than significant-influence (Condition B) for typical property-LLP members below partnership-leadership level.

### Example 4 — s.863G anti-avoidance: contrived Condition C capital contribution

- **Singh Property LLP** has 4 members. Member 4 has £200 capital + £40k disguised salary → fails Condition C and is at risk of salaried-member status. Adviser recommends: LLP makes a £15k "loan" to Member 4; Member 4 uses the £15k to inject £15k capital contribution; new capital ratio = £15.2k ÷ £40k = 38% > 25% → Condition C "met" → out of regime.
- **s.863G anti-avoidance result:** the arrangement is a round-trip — the capital is not at Member 4's real economic risk; the LLP's loan-and-contribution is one of the main purposes of which is to secure that the salaried-member rules do not apply. HMRC disregards the arrangement under s.863G; Condition C treated as not met; salaried-member status crystallises with retrospective cost.
- **Operational pattern:** PM259300 (Financing arrangements) covers this; RUN session WebFetches at write time + frames the trap for the page audience.

### Example 5 — BlueCrest narrowing: matrix-silo'd operational member

- **Verma Property LLP** is a multi-strand operator: 6 members each manage a distinct geographic region (London / SE / SW / Midlands / North / Wales). Each member runs their region operationally — hires, fires, lender relationships in-region, capex decisions in-region. No member has authority across regions; the partnership board (all 6) jointly decides LLP-wide strategy at quarterly meetings.
- **Pre-BlueCrest reading (FTT/UT view):** each member had significant influence over a significant part of LLP affairs (their region) — Condition B safe.
- **Post-BlueCrest reading (Supreme Court October 2024):** the SC held that "significant influence over the affairs of the partnership" does NOT require influence over the WHOLE LLP business — influence over a significant part can be enough — but the question is now sharper: is the operational region influence "significant" in the LLP context? HMRC's post-October-2024 PM256000-series reading favours a narrower interpretation: in-region operational leadership without cross-LLP strategic input may NOT cross the significance threshold. **Verma LLP members at risk of Condition B failure.**
- **Safe harbour:** RUN session frames the operative response: shift to Condition C capital-contribution route (verify each member's capital balance vs disguised-salary at year-end) + document cross-LLP board influence (quarterly board minutes evidencing each member's voting weight on LLP-wide decisions).

### Example 6 — Property LLP impact summary (the operative reason this page matters)

- **Mr and Mrs Patel + Mr Patel Snr** own and operate a 22-property family LLP. Drawings are smoothed to a fixed monthly amount per member. Capital balances vary (Mr Patel £600k, Mrs Patel £300k, Mr Patel Snr £20k). Disguised salary tests at year-end: Mr Patel £60k, Mrs Patel £40k, Mr Patel Snr £36k.
- **Status check post-BlueCrest:** Mr Patel out of regime via Condition C (£600k > 25% × £60k = £15k); Mrs Patel out of regime via Condition C (£300k > 25% × £40k = £10k); Mr Patel Snr at risk on Condition C (£20k < 25% × £36k = £9k → actually £20k > £9k, so out of regime). **All three safe via Condition C.**
- **Reframe if Mr Patel Snr capital balance drops below £9k (e.g. drawdown):** crosses into Condition C "fail" territory; status check needs Conditions A + B. RUN session frames the periodic-review discipline: family property LLPs need annual capital-vs-salary re-test at each year-end.

## FAQ expansion (RUN session polishes prose; 10-12 FAQs target)

1. **Q: What are the salaried member rules, and when do they apply to an LLP member?**
   A: ITA 2007 ss.863A–863G impose the salaried-member regime on LLP members where all three Conditions are met: (A) ≥80% of total amounts payable to the member are "disguised salary" (fixed or guaranteed amounts not depending on overall LLP profits/losses); (B) the member does NOT have "significant influence over the affairs of the partnership"; (C) the member's capital contribution to the LLP is < 25% of disguised salary. When the regime applies, the LLP becomes a deemed employer; the member becomes a deemed employee; profit shares are reclassified as employment income subject to PAYE; the LLP becomes liable for secondary Class 1 NIC (15% from 6 April 2026) and Apprenticeship Levy on the deemed employment income.

2. **Q: What is the BlueCrest decision, and what did the Supreme Court actually hold?**
   A: *BlueCrest Capital Management (UK) LLP v HMRC* [2024] UKSC 33 (October 2024) is the leading Supreme Court authority on Condition B "significant influence over the affairs of the partnership". The Supreme Court held — reversing the Upper Tribunal in part — that significant influence does NOT require influence over the WHOLE LLP business; influence over a significant part of the LLP's affairs can be enough. **Practical effect:** the test is now sharper, and the safe harbour is narrower than the FTT and UT had been applying. Members who run a single book / strategy / property within a multi-strand LLP may NOT have significant influence over the affairs of the partnership in the relevant statutory sense, and therefore may NOT qualify for the Condition B exclusion.

3. **Q: What is HMRC's updated 2025 guidance, and where do we find it?**
   A: HMRC's operative salaried-member guidance is in the Partnership Manual at PM250000+ (verified entry point on gov.uk). The load-bearing post-BlueCrest content is at **PM256000 — Condition B significant influence** — with 12 sub-pages covering LLP agreements, types of influence, FCA-regulated functions, size considerations, hierarchies, delegated powers, indirect influence, group structures, and change-in-circumstances. The anti-avoidance overlay sits at PM259000 with sub-pages on financing arrangements, intermediaries, and the mixed-membership interaction. RUN session WebFetches the current PM256000 and PM259000 series at write time for HMRC's verbatim post-BlueCrest reading.

4. **Q: Why have practitioners raised concerns about HMRC's narrowed reading?**
   A: The post-BlueCrest narrowing extends the practical reach of the salaried-member regime beyond what FA 2014 originally targeted. FA 2014 was directed at disguised-employment partner-promotion structures in professional services (lawyer / accountant / consultancy partner promotion paths). The narrower Condition B reading risks catching legitimate operational LLP architecture — multi-strand investment funds, matrix-silo'd property LLPs, regional-management structures — where each member has real authority over their part of the business but not necessarily over LLP-wide strategy. Sector bodies (CIOT, ICAEW, ATT) have raised the operative concern that ordinary commercial structures may now require defensive Condition C capital-contribution re-engineering.

5. **Q: What is the Condition C capital-contribution safe harbour, and why is it the operative practical route?**
   A: Condition C (ITA 2007 s.863D) requires the member's capital contribution to be LESS than 25% of disguised salary for the regime to apply. Where capital is ≥ 25% of disguised salary, the member is OUT of the regime regardless of Conditions A and B. Practically the operative route for property-LLP members: easier to demonstrate and document than significant-influence (Condition B), which depends on fact-intensive analysis of LLP-wide vs in-region authority. A member with £80k capital + £40k disguised salary has capital ratio 200% — well clear of Condition C.

6. **Q: What does the s.863G anti-avoidance overlay catch?**
   A: s.863G disregards arrangements one of the main purposes of which is to secure that the salaried-member rules do not apply. Post-BlueCrest enforcement attention focuses on contrived Condition C capital-contribution arrangements: round-trip loans where the LLP funds a member's capital injection; sham contributions where the capital is at no real economic risk; arrangements where the "capital" is recallable or refunded shortly after the Condition C test date. HMRC's PM259300 (Financing arrangements) is the operative guidance — RUN session WebFetches current at write time. The trap for property LLPs: well-intentioned advisers may recommend small capital injections to clear the 25% gate without considering whether the capital is at real economic risk; HMRC challenges the substance.

7. **Q: For a typical family property LLP, which members are at risk?**
   A: Members with: (i) ≥80% of drawings as fixed / guaranteed amounts (Condition A met); (ii) operational role limited to a single property / strand / region without LLP-wide strategic input (Condition B at risk post-BlueCrest); AND (iii) capital contribution < 25% of disguised salary (Condition C met). The triple intersection catches: junior family members with minimal capital and fixed monthly drawings; adult children added as LLP members for income-splitting but without real operational + strategic authority + with nominal capital; non-active family members drawing fixed amounts. Senior LLP members with substantial capital balances + LLP-wide authority typically clear via Condition B AND Condition C simultaneously.

8. **Q: What evidence should an LLP keep to demonstrate Condition B (significant influence)?**
   A: Board minutes documenting each member's decision-making authority over budgets, hires, strategy across the LLP — not just one property / region / strand. Cross-strand voting weight on partnership-wide decisions. Quarterly board minutes evidencing significant-influence patterns through repeated exercise. Authority delegated under the LLP agreement (cross-reference to PM256000 sub-page on LLP agreements). The post-BlueCrest reading requires evidence of LLP-wide influence; in-region or in-strand operational authority alone is no longer sufficient on its face.

9. **Q: What evidence should an LLP keep to demonstrate Condition C (capital contribution)?**
   A: Audit trail of capital injection from member's own funds (not LLP-loaned). Capital contribution agreement specifying the contribution is non-refundable and at member's economic risk. Year-end capital account balance documented in the LLP accounts (FRS 102 + LLP SORP framework — cross-reference F-6 §11.C.Z lock). Disguised-salary calculation at year-end (sum of all guaranteed / fixed amounts). The capital-vs-disguised-salary ratio re-tested at each year-end as capital balance fluctuates with drawings + injections. Adviser-prepared annual Condition C documentation pack is becoming the typical operational pattern for at-risk members.

10. **Q: How does the salaried-member regime interact with the LLP tax-transparency framework?**
    A: The default LLP treatment under ITTOIA 2005 Part 9 (via s.863) + CTA 2009 s.1273 + TCGA 1992 s.59A is tax-transparent — each member taxed individually on profit share at member's marginal rate. The salaried-member regime is the EXCEPTION — operative only where all three Conditions met. Non-salaried members continue to enjoy the transparent treatment unchanged; only members who fail all three Conditions are reclassified. The page must frame the regime as the exception, not the default.

11. **Q: What should property LLP operators do NOW (action checklist)?**
    A: (i) Member-by-member status review against the three Conditions at the upcoming year-end. (ii) Documentation pack: capital account balances + disguised-salary calculations + Condition B evidence for each member. (iii) Capital top-up consideration for members below the 25% Condition C threshold (with care about s.863G round-trip-loan substance risk). (iv) Structural review for matrix-silo'd member roles: would cross-strand involvement evidence Condition B more robustly? (v) Forward-look: review every 12-18 months given the moving HMRC guidance pattern post-BlueCrest. (vi) Consider professional opinion for borderline members where Condition B is the only safe harbour.
