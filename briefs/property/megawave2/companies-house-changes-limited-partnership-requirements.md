## Worked-example data (RUN session uses these as canvas)

### Example 1 — Established property-fund LP transition

- **Mawell Property Fund LP** has been registered under LPA 1907 since 2018, with a corporate general partner (Mawell Fund GP Ltd) and 12 institutional limited partners. Pre-ECCTA the LP's only compliance obligations were: initial Form LP5 registration; Form LP6 for changes in partners or capital; no annual filing; no public confirmation of GPs / LPs.
- **Post-ECCTA Part 2 (per §11.B.3):** Mawell Fund LP must now have a registered office in the UK at an appropriate address (no PO boxes); must provide a registered email address for Companies House notifications; must file an annual confirmation statement listing GPs + LPs + nature of business; the corporate GP's beneficial-ownership chain must comply with PSC-equivalent disclosures; and individual general partners (where any sit directly) must verify identity via ACSP per §11.B.4.
- **Transitional window:** existing LPs given a defined window after Part 2 commencement (typically 12-18 months) to bring registrations into compliance — RUN session verifies the exact transitional period at the campaign-page tracker at write time. Non-compliance triggers striking-off powers under Part 2.

### Example 2 — Family-investment LP holding tenanted property

- **Mr and Mrs Singh** established Singh Family LP in 2015 as a family-investment vehicle holding 4 BTL flats. The structure: SinghCo Ltd (corporate GP, 100% Mr & Mrs Singh shareholders) + Mr Singh, Mrs Singh, and adult son Vikram as limited partners (each with capital contribution).
- **Post-ECCTA Part 2 compliance:** the LP must designate a UK registered office (formerly the Singh family home address sufficed; now must be an appropriate address). Annual confirmation statement listing all four partners. The corporate GP's directors (Mr and Mrs Singh) must ID-verify under ECCTA Part 1 as company directors; the corporate GP itself doesn't need a separate verification (corporate entities don't verify, their directors do).
- **Tax-side unchanged (§11.B.7):** the LP remains tax-transparent. Mr Singh, Mrs Singh, and Vikram each report their share of profit on their personal SA returns per ITTOIA 2005 Part 9. No CT charge at LP level. RoE (ECTEA 2022) does NOT apply because the LP is UK-domiciled.
- **Operational consideration:** the family must adopt a more formal compliance posture than the original "set up once and forget" LPA 1907 baseline allowed. Many family LPs in this position consider converting to LLP (LLPA 2000 — different statute, different sub-regime) or to a limited company if the family-fund function can be achieved with similar tax efficiency; conversion is not automatic and triggers SDLT + CGT events.

### Example 3 — Overseas-LP holding UK property (RoE interaction)

- **Lemberg Holdings LP** is a Luxembourg-registered limited partnership owning two UK commercial properties (offices in central London). The GP is a Luxembourg holding company; the LPs are a mix of EU institutional investors.
- **Two-registration requirement (§11.B.5):** Lemberg Holdings LP must register on Companies House's **Register of Overseas Entities** (RoE) under ECTEA 2022 because it holds UK property as a non-UK entity. SEPARATELY, if the LP carries out activities that bring it within scope of ECCTA Part 2 LP reforms (e.g., establishes a UK place of business), it may also fall under the LP-side regime. Both registrations must be kept current; lapse of either has consequences (RoE-side: title-restriction by the Land Registry; LP-side: striking-off potential).
- **Why this matters for property landlords:** UK landlords holding via overseas-LP structures (often historic pre-RoE setups) now face double compliance overhead and should consider whether to onshore via UK incorporation or accept the higher ongoing-compliance cost.

## FAQ expansion (RUN session polishes prose; 10-12 FAQs target)

1. **Q: What is a limited partnership, and how is it different from an LLP or a limited company?**
   A: A limited partnership (LP) is established under the Limited Partnerships Act 1907 and consists of one or more **general partners** (who manage the business and bear unlimited liability for partnership debts) and one or more **limited partners** (who contribute capital but cannot participate in management; their liability is limited to their capital contribution). An LP is NOT a separate legal person at common law (unlike an LLP under LLPA 2000 or a limited company under CA 2006), though Scottish LPs are. Tax-wise, an LP is tax-transparent (each partner pays tax on their share of profits per ITTOIA 2005 Part 9), unlike a limited company which pays Corporation Tax.

2. **Q: What were the LP's compliance obligations before the ECCTA 2023 reforms?**
   A: Light-touch. The original LPA 1907 required initial registration (form LP5) listing the partners and the partnership's general nature, plus filing form LP6 to register changes in partners or capital. There was no annual confirmation requirement, no requirement to maintain a UK registered office (in many cases), no ID-verification regime for general partners, and no Companies House power to strike off non-compliant LPs. This historic regime made the LP attractive for fund vehicles and JV structures because the compliance overhead was minimal compared to a UK limited company.

3. **Q: What did ECCTA 2023 Part 2 change for limited partnerships?**
   A: ECCTA 2023 Part 2 substantively amended the LPA 1907 framework (per §11.B.1). The major changes: (a) the LP must now have a UK registered office at an "appropriate address" (no PO Boxes; analogous to CA 2006 s.28 + ECCTA Part 1 amendments — §11.B.3); (b) the LP must provide a registered email address for Companies House notifications; (c) the LP must file an annual confirmation statement listing general partners + limited partners + nature of business; (d) general partners must verify their identity under ECCTA Part 1's identity-verification regime, routed through the ACSP framework where the GP is non-UK-resident (§11.B.4); (e) Companies House has new striking-off powers over non-compliant LPs; (f) information-disclosure obligations have expanded. The reforms bring the LP into a CA-2006-style regime that is less compliance-light than the original 1907 Act.

4. **Q: Are ECCTA Part 2 LP reforms in force yet?**
   A: Implementation is phased and controlled by separate commencement regulations (statutory instruments) made under ECCTA Part 2's commencement powers. As of the most recent updates to the Companies House campaign page, parts of Part 2 are in force, others in transitional windows, others pending. You should verify the current commencement state at `https://changestoukcompanylaw.campaign.gov.uk/` before assuming any specific provision applies to your LP — the operational rollout is moving and the position may have changed since this guide was written.

5. **Q: Does my existing limited partnership need to do anything now?**
   A: Yes, if the LP is in operation. Once each ECCTA Part 2 provision commences, existing LPs are typically given a transitional window (commonly 6-18 months depending on the provision) to comply. For most family-investment LPs and property-fund LPs operating under the historic LPA 1907 light-touch regime, this means: (a) appointing a UK registered office at an appropriate address; (b) preparing to file the annual confirmation statement when first required; (c) checking general-partner ID-verification status (existing partners may need to verify retrospectively, not just new appointments); (d) reviewing the partnership agreement for any clauses that conflict with the new regime. Engage a fund-finance solicitor or specialist accountant if the LP is institutional in scale.

6. **Q: Does ECCTA Part 2 change how my LP is taxed?**
   A: No. ECCTA Part 2 changes the **compliance and transparency** regime; it does NOT change tax treatment. LPs remain tax-transparent under ITTOIA 2005 Part 9 for income tax and TCGA 1992 s.59 for capital gains tax. Each partner continues to be taxed on their share of partnership profits or gains, regardless of distribution. The partnership files an SA800 partnership return; individual partners report their share on their personal SA returns. The new annual confirmation statement is a separate Companies House filing from the SA800; do not conflate them.

7. **Q: How does ECCTA Part 2 interact with the Register of Overseas Entities (RoE) for overseas LPs holding UK property?**
   A: Where an overseas-domiciled LP holds UK property, two registrations may now apply: (a) the LP itself must register on the **Register of Overseas Entities** under ECTEA 2022 because it is a non-UK entity holding UK land (§11.B.5); (b) separately, if the LP's UK activities bring it within ECCTA Part 2 scope (typically yes for any LP with a UK place of business), it may also fall under the LP-side compliance regime. Both registrations must be kept current. Lapse of the RoE registration restricts the Land Registry's ability to register dealings with the property; lapse of the LP-side registration triggers Companies House striking-off powers. This dual-regime overhead is one of the operational reasons some historic overseas-LP UK-property structures are being unwound or onshored.

8. **Q: Who has to verify their identity, and how does ID verification work for a corporate general partner?**
   A: For LP-side ECCTA compliance, individual general partners must verify their identity under the ECCTA Part 1 identity-verification regime. This is done either directly via the GOV.UK One Login route (a free service for UK-resident individuals with standard ID documents) or through an Authorised Corporate Service Provider (ACSP — typically the LP's accountant or formation agent, who has registered with Companies House under the ACSP framework). Where the general partner is a corporate entity (e.g., a Fund GP Ltd), the corporate GP itself does not verify identity. Instead, the corporate GP's directors verify under the companies-side ECCTA regime, and the corporate GP's PSC disclosures are filed at Companies House.

9. **Q: What happens if my LP doesn't comply with the new regime?**
   A: ECCTA Part 2 gives Companies House new powers to strike non-compliant LPs from the register, in addition to financial penalties for late or missing filings. A struck-off LP loses its registered status, which has knock-on consequences: contracts entered into as an LP may be unenforceable; banking relationships may be terminated; lenders may treat the structure as in default. Restoring a struck-off LP requires court application and is materially more expensive than maintaining compliance.

10. **Q: For a property-fund LP, what new governance overlay does ECCTA Part 2 add?**
    A: Property-fund LPs (typical institutional-investor structure with a managing GP + multiple LP investors) faced a relatively informal governance overlay before ECCTA Part 2 — the partnership agreement plus side letters with specific investors. Post-ECCTA, the LP must now: maintain a UK registered office; file an annual confirmation statement (creating a permanent public record of partners + capital + business nature); have its general partners ID-verified; respond to Companies House information requests under the new disclosure regime. This is materially more visible and burdensome than the historic baseline. Many institutional managers are reviewing whether their existing English LP fund structures remain optimal, or whether to migrate to alternative vehicles (Scottish LP, ILP, or non-UK structures).

11. **Q: How does this page differ from `llp-property-investment-worth-considering` and existing LLP pages?**
    A: An LLP (limited liability partnership) is a different statutory entity from an LP. LLPs are governed by the **Limited Liability Partnership Act 2000** and are separate legal persons with limited liability for all members; LPs are governed by the **Limited Partnerships Act 1907** and have unlimited-liability general partners + limited-liability limited partners (the LP itself is not a separate legal person at common law in England, though it is in Scotland). The compliance regimes are also distinct: LLPs have applied a CA-2006-style compliance regime since 2000 (with SI 2008/1911 making CA 2006 apply with modifications); LPs only got their CA-2006-style overlay via ECCTA 2023 Part 2. Tax treatment is broadly similar (both transparent), but the substantive law, member-vs-partner architecture, and historic compliance overhead all differ.

12. **Q: How does this page differ from `partnership-sdlt-relief-schedule-15-fa-2003-incorporation-sum-lower-proportions`?**
    A: That existing page covers the FA 2003 Schedule 15 SDLT mechanics for partnerships generally (general partnership + LP + LLP all within scope of Sch 15). This page covers the LP-specific **compliance** changes under ECCTA Part 2 — the registered-office / annual-confirmation / ID-verification / striking-off overlay. The two pages are complementary: the Sch 15 page is tax-mechanics on partnership property transfers; this page is post-ECCTA compliance on LP existence + filings. They cross-link.
