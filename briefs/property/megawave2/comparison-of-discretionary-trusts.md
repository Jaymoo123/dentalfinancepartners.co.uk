# A Comparison of Discretionary Trust Variants for Property Estate Planning

## Statutory anchor

- Primary (relevant property regime): **IHTA 1984 Part III Chapter III ss.58-85** — settled property without an interest in possession.
  - **s.58** — "relevant property" definition (property in settlement without a qualifying interest in possession).
  - **s.64** — principal (10-year) periodic charge: "Where immediately before a ten-year anniversary all or any part of the property comprised in a settlement is relevant property, tax shall be charged at the rate applicable under sections 66 and 67 below on the value of the property or part at that time" (verified verbatim at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1984/51/section/64 on 2026-05-26). s.64(1A) extends to accumulated income arising more than 5 years before the anniversary, originating from relevant property, with no IIP holder.
  - **s.65** — exit (proportional) charge on capital leaving the settlement between 10-year anniversaries.
  - **ss.66-69** — rate calculation mechanics: effective rate scaled by the number of complete quarters in the 10-year period (s.66(2)) up to a maximum of 6% per anniversary.
  - **s.71** — accumulation and maintenance trusts (historical pre-22 March 2006 regime; mostly closed).
  - **s.71A** — trusts for bereaved minors (BMT) — concessionary regime applying where a parent leaves property on discretionary trust for the parent's own minor children.
  - **s.71D** — 18-to-25 trusts — concessionary regime allowing capital to vest by 25 with a proportionate exit charge for the years between 18 and vesting.
  - **s.89** + **s.89B** — disabled person's interest (DPI) — treated as a qualifying interest in possession; outside the s.64 / s.65 regime.
  - **s.142** + **s.144** — deed of variation (within 2 years of death) and distributions out of discretionary will trust within 2 years of death reading back to the deceased for IHT.
- Primary (income-tax attribution for settlor-interested discretionary trusts): **ITTOIA 2005 Part 5 Chapter 5 ss.620-628** — locked at HP §22.9 with verbatim citations.
- Primary (CGT holdover on entry): **TCGA 1992 s.260** (CLT holdover for non-settlor-interested trusts only, settlor-interested restriction at ss.169B-169G with **s.169E** as the settlor definition per F-4 / Wave 6 lock at HP §22.14).
- Primary (CGT for trustees on disposal): **TCGA 1992 ss.69-72 + Sch 4A** — trust-level CGT, 24% residential property rate (2026/27), no Annual Exempt Amount uplift for trust gains other than the trust-level £1,500 (half of personal AEA 2026/27).
- Primary (Trust Registration Service): **MLR 2017 reg 45** (SI 2017/692) — registration obligation; **TRSM80020** — case-by-case £5k max discretionary penalty (HP §22.16 locked, supersedes the graduated tariff misconception).
- Supporting (FA 2006 reform context):
  - **FA 2006 Sch 20** — the 22 March 2006 reform that moved most new lifetime IIP settlements into the relevant property regime. The "discretionary trust" label post-2006 covers both genuinely discretionary settlements AND lifetime IIPs that fall into the relevant property regime by default.
  - **s.49(1A) IHTA 1984** (inserted by FA 2006) — narrows the IIP regime to IPDIs, disabled persons' interests, transitional serial interests.
- Supporting (April 2026 / April 2027 reform overlays):
  - **HP §15.4** — £1m combined BPR + APR cap from 6 April 2026 + anti-fragmentation for same-settlor trusts settled on or after 30 October 2024.
  - **HP §15.5** — pensions IHT 6 April 2027.
  - **HP §22.X.4** — Sch A1 enveloped-residential look-through (unaffected) + new s.48ZA excluded-property test for offshore trusts (settlor LTR pivot, supersedes pre-FA 2025 s.48(3)).
- House position references: §15.4 (cap + anti-fragmentation); §15.6 (LTR test for trust settlors); §22.4 (CLT into discretionary trust mechanics — locked); §22.9 (settlements legislation s.624 / s.625 / s.626 income-tax attribution); §22.10 (minor-child s.629 attribution); §22.12 (s.49 + new s.48ZA — locked); §22.13 (trust-vs-FIC decision boundary — the four-axis comparison framework); §22.16 (TRS).

**Worked example: which discretionary variant for which scenario?**

Persona: Patel family. Three property scenarios:

(A) **Lifetime gift to children's trust during founder's lifetime, £2m property portfolio.**
- Variant: standard lifetime discretionary trust (relevant property regime).
- Entry: CLT, 20% on the slice above NRB. NRB £325k → CLT chargeable transfer £1.675m at 20% = £335k IHT due on entry (with 7-year cumulation lookback).
- 10-year periodic: up to 6% of value at the anniversary date (effective rate scaled by complete quarters).
- Exits: proportional charge per s.65.
- s.260 holdover available IF not settlor-interested (s.169E definition).
- s.624 settlor-interested attribution if settlor or settlor's spouse can benefit — full income attributed to settlor.
- TRS registration: 90 days from creation per HP §22.16.

(B) **Post-death gift via will trust, founder dies leaving £2m portfolio to grandchildren via discretionary will trust.**
- Variant: discretionary will trust.
- Entry: NRB available (£325k) reduces the death estate; surplus charged at 40% as normal. The trust itself is a relevant property settlement from the moment the will is read.
- s.144 read-back: if trustees distribute property out of the will trust within 2 years of death, the distribution is read back as if from the deceased — so a distribution to a surviving spouse within 2 years gets the spouse exemption applied retroactively.
- 10-year and exit charges apply from the post-2-year date if undistributed.

(C) **Minor grandchildren with deceased parent.**
- Variant: bereaved-minor trust (s.71A) — concessionary regime, no 10-year charges, capital vests at 18.
- Eligibility: only available where the deceased parent leaves property on discretionary trust for the parent's own minor children. Grandparent → grandchild discretionary trust does NOT get s.71A.
- 18-to-25 trust (s.71D) extends the vesting cliff to 25 with a proportionate exit charge for the 18-25 years.

**Allowance anti-fragmentation worked example:**

Pre-30 October 2024: founder set up 5 small pilot trusts (£325k each) during lifetime, each with its own NRB. Each trust escaped relevant property charges below NRB. *Rysaffe* strategy worked.

Post-30 October 2024: any trusts settled by the same settlor on or after this date share a single £1m allowance (HP §15.4). Founder cannot establish multiple sub-£1m trusts and expect each to enjoy its own allowance — the FA 2026 anti-fragmentation rule treats them collectively.

## FAQ draft (10-12 entries, Stage 2 populated; RUN sub-agent polishes prose)

1. **What does "discretionary trust" actually mean in the post-2006 world?**
   After 22 March 2006 (FA 2006 Sch 20), nearly all new lifetime settlements fall into the relevant property regime under IHTA 1984 ss.58-85. The "discretionary trust" label now covers both genuinely discretionary settlements AND lifetime interest-in-possession settlements that became relevant property by default. The narrow exceptions are Immediate Post-Death Interests (IPDIs), Disabled Person's Interests (DPIs), Transitional Serial Interests (TSIs), Bereaved Minors Trusts (BMTs) and 18-25 trusts — all defined within IHTA 1984.

2. **What are the seven variants of discretionary-class trust I might choose between?**
   (1) Standard lifetime discretionary trust — CLT entry, relevant property regime, 10-year + exit charges. (2) Discretionary will trust — NRB-aware post-death entry, s.144 read-back within 2 years. (3) Pilot trust strategy — historical multi-trust NRB-multiplication (now constrained by the 30 October 2024 anti-fragmentation rule). (4) Bereaved-minor trust (s.71A) — concessionary regime for the deceased parent's minor children. (5) 18-to-25 trust (s.71D) — concessionary regime with capital vesting by 25. (6) Disabled person's interest (s.89/s.89B) — outside the relevant property regime. (7) Charitable discretionary trust — full IHT exemption.

3. **How does the entry-tax profile differ across variants?**
   Standard lifetime discretionary: CLT at 20% above NRB, with 7-year cumulation. Discretionary will trust: charged at the death rate (40%) above NRB, with NRB available. BMT: no entry charge for property left to bereaved minors. 18-25 trust: no entry charge if structured as a parent-to-own-child trust on parent's death. DPI: no entry charge; treated as qualifying interest in possession. Charitable: no entry charge; full exemption.

4. **How does the 10-year periodic charge work, and which variants attract it?**
   Under IHTA 1984 s.64, relevant property in a settlement is charged at the rate calculated under ss.66-67 on each 10-year anniversary. The maximum rate is 6% (effective rate 30% on the assumed-chargeable-transfer amount, with a fraction-of-the-quarter-period scaling). Standard discretionary trust and discretionary will trust attract the charge; BMT, 18-25 (during the 18-25 window only), DPI, and charitable trust do not.

5. **What is the 30 October 2024 anti-fragmentation rule and how does it change my planning?**
   Per HP §15.4 lock, lifetime trusts settled by the same settlor on or after 30 October 2024 share a single £1m allowance (the headline figure; see F-102 for the £1m vs £2.5m s.124D reconciliation). This closes the *Rysaffe* multi-pilot-trust planning that allowed a settlor to create several small NRB-sized trusts each enjoying its own allowance. Trusts settled before 30 October 2024 retain their grandfathered allocations.

6. **How do the s.624 and s.629 settlements-legislation rules differ across variants?**
   s.624 attributes trust income back to the settlor if the settlor or the settlor's spouse retains an interest. s.629 attributes income to the settlor where the settlor is the parent and the beneficiary is the settlor's own minor child (with a £100 de minimis under s.629(3) and a sunset at the child's 18th birthday). Settlor-interested standard discretionary trust hits the s.624 trap. BMT for the deceased parent's children is not settlor-interested because the parent is dead. DPI and grandparent-route trusts avoid the s.629 trap.

7. **What's the CGT s.260 holdover position?**
   TCGA 1992 s.260 gives full CGT holdover on a CLT entry into a non-settlor-interested discretionary trust (the settlor's capital gain is rolled into the trustees' base cost). s.260 holdover is denied for settlor-interested trusts under TCGA 1992 ss.169B-169G (where s.169E defines settlor). BMT and 18-25 trust entries on death attract no CGT at all (death uplift under s.62 IHTA 1984). Charitable discretionary trust entries get full holdover.

8. **How does the s.144 read-back window work for discretionary will trusts?**
   Where a discretionary will trust distributes property out of the trust to a beneficiary within 2 years of the deceased's death, IHTA 1984 s.144 treats the distribution as if it had been made by the deceased directly. The IHT treatment then follows the distribution, not the trust — so a distribution to a surviving spouse within 2 years gets the spouse exemption applied retroactively even though the will originally settled the property into a discretionary trust.

9. **What is the trust-rate income tax stack and how does it compare to direct-holder taxation?**
   For accumulated trust income above the £1,000 standard-rate band, trustees pay 45% on non-dividend income and 39.35% on dividend income (2026/27). Compare to a higher-rate direct holder at 40% / 33.75% — the trust rate is consistently above the direct-holder rate, designed as an anti-deferral mechanism. The trust pays the income tax; when income is later distributed to beneficiaries, they receive a 45% tax credit (refundable if their marginal rate is lower).

10. **How does the new s.48ZA LTR test affect offshore discretionary trusts?**
    FA 2025 inserted s.48ZA into IHTA 1984, replacing the pre-FA-2025 s.48(3) domicile-based excluded-property test. The new test pivots on the settlor's long-term resident (LTR) status at the relevant transfer date. For landlord-emigrants whose settlor relocates abroad, the offshore discretionary trust's excluded-property status now depends on whether the settlor is LTR at the date of each chargeable event, not the date of trust creation. This is a material structural change — see our existing `excluded-property-trust-long-term-resident-settlor-pivot-landlords` page for the operational deep.

11. **How does this page differ from your existing FIC-vs-discretionary-trust page?**
    The FIC-vs-discretionary-trust page compares two STRUCTURALLY DIFFERENT vehicles (a corporate vehicle vs a trust vehicle). This page (C3) compares variants WITHIN the discretionary-trust class — the choice between lifetime/discretionary will/BMT/18-25/DPI structures once you've already decided a discretionary trust is the right vehicle in principle. If you're at the "which vehicle" stage, read the FIC-vs-discretionary page first; if you're at the "which discretionary variant" stage, this page is for you.

12. **How do I register a discretionary trust with the Trust Registration Service?**
    Per MLR 2017 reg 45 and HP §22.16, almost all express trusts are registrable. The deadline is 90 days from trust creation for new trusts (or 90 days from a chargeable event for taxable trusts). Penalty for non-registration is a case-by-case discretionary fine up to £5,000 (HP §22.16 corrected the graduated tariff misconception). Trustees register online via the HMRC Trust Registration Service. Our existing `trust-registration-service-trs-compliance-trust-owned-btl-mlr-2017` page walks the operational deep.
