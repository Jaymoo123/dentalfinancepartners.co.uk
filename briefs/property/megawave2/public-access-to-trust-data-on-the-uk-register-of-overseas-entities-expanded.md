# Public Access to Trust Data on the UK Register of Overseas Entities: Expanded Disclosure Explained

## Statutory anchor

- Primary (the ECTEA 2022 framework — the Register of Overseas Entities primary statute):
  - **Economic Crime (Transparency and Enforcement) Act 2022 (ECTEA 2022 c. 10)** — Part 1 ss.4-31 establishes the Register of Overseas Entities (RoE). Verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2022/10 on 2026-05-26.
  - **ECTEA 2022 s.4** — "Application for registration" — overseas entity applies to be entered on the register (per HP §11.A lock).
  - **ECTEA 2022 s.7** — "Updating duty" — entity must file an updating statement within 14 days after the end of each "update period" (HP §11.A lock — the operative clock is the update period defined in s.7, not "14 days from registration anniversary" — this is the F-13 / Stage 2 catch).
  - **ECTEA 2022 s.8** — "Failure to comply with updating duty" — criminal offence: entity + every officer in default; on conviction a fine + daily default fine ≥ greater of £2,500 or half of level 4 on standard scale (HP §11.A lock).
  - **ECTEA 2022 s.34** — "Power to require overseas entity to register if it owns certain land" — Secretary of State's compulsory-registration-notice power for unregistered overseas entities holding UK property.
  - **LRA 2002 Sch 4A** — HMLR disposition-block for non-compliant overseas entities (inserted by ECTEA 2022 Sch 3). The HMLR enforcement consequence: a non-compliant overseas entity cannot transfer / charge / grant leases >7 years over UK property held by it.
  - **Civil penalty SI** — quantum (£2,500 + £500/day) sits in separate Penalties Regulations SI, NOT in ECTEA itself (HP §11.A correction).
- Primary (the ECCTA 2023 amendments to ECTEA — the 2024 expansion):
  - **Economic Crime and Corporate Transparency Act 2023 (ECCTA 2023 c. 56) Part 3** — amends ECTEA 2022 in places (Stage 2 verifies precise sections per HP §11.A discipline). Verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2023/56 on 2026-05-26.
  - **Commencement of ECCTA Part 3 RoE amendments: 1 March 2024** (per ECCTA 2023 Sch 2 commencement) per HP §11.A timeline.
  - **Key 2024 changes:** broadened beneficial-ownership disclosure requirements; aligned RoE beneficial-owner definitions with persons-with-significant-control (PSC) framework for UK companies; introduced anti-avoidance provisions for nominee arrangements; tightened the trust-information disclosure architecture for entities with trust-chain beneficial owners.
- Primary (the 2025 expansion regulations broadening public access to trust information):
  - **The 2025 expansion** — Government announced (via Department for Business and Trade + Companies House consultation 2024) intent to expand public access to trust information held on RoE; statutory expansion via Statutory Instrument made under ECCTA 2023 amending-powers (Stage 2 MUST verify the precise SI number, commencement date, and exact in-force date — at write time the working understanding is that SI was made and brought into force in late 2025 or 2026, with consultation outcome published in 2025; verbatim verification needed). **F-106 candidate flag raised — this is genuine HP-extension territory not currently covered in HP §11.A or §22.16; new HP-lock section §22.X.RoE-trust-disclosure proposed for Stage 1b consideration once Stage 2 lands the verbatim SI reference.**
  - **The previous (pre-2025) architecture:** trust information on the RoE was accessible by HMRC, law enforcement, anti-money-laundering supervisors, and specifically-applying members of the public who could demonstrate a legitimate interest (a process largely opaque in practice).
  - **The expansion:** broader public access to certain categories of trust information — typically including the trustee identity, the trust name, the trust establishment date, the type of trust (discretionary / interest in possession / bare / etc.), and the beneficiaries-or-classes-of-beneficiaries names where the trust is part of the beneficial-ownership chain of an overseas entity owning UK property. **Redactions and safeguards:** the expansion preserves redaction routes for personal safety concerns (residential addresses, full dates of birth, vulnerable persons), the SoS retains discretion to restrict on application, and certain protected-person categories are excluded from publication.
- Primary (the TRS interaction — HP §22.16):
  - **MLR 2017 (SI 2017/692) reg 45** — Trust Registration Service. TRS captures trust beneficial-ownership data via a separate HMRC-administered register; TRS data is NOT public (accessible only to HMRC, law enforcement, AML supervisors, and parties with legitimate interest).
  - **Key distinction for sessions:** TRS is HMRC-admin'd, non-public; RoE is Companies-House-admin'd, public-with-restrictions (and now expanded). A trust holding a non-UK company that owns UK property will typically need to register on BOTH TRS (if it meets the trigger) AND have its trustees/beneficiaries disclosed in the RoE filing of the overseas entity. Sessions must NOT conflate the two.
- Primary (the Companies House identity-verification regime interaction):
  - **CA 2006 Part 11A** (inserted by ECCTA) — director and PSC identity verification. From operative dates (Wave 9 timeline coverage at HP §11.A), individuals named in RoE filings (registrable beneficial owners, trustees) face the same identity-verification requirements as UK-company directors and PSCs.
  - **ACSP framework** (HP §11.A): only registered Authorised Corporate Service Providers can submit identity-verification on behalf of others; landlords' accountants typically need ACSP registration to handle this for clients.
- House position references: §11.A (ECCTA + ECTEA + RoE primary architecture); §22.16 (TRS — for cross-reference and non-conflation with RoE); §22.X (LTR architecture for excluded-property-trust pivot); §22.X.4 (excluded-property regime under LTR — particularly relevant for trust-held overseas-entity-owned UK property where the trust was settled pre-6-April-2025 by a non-dom settlor); §15.6 (non-resident IHT — relevant for overseas-entity-owned UK property in the LTR architecture).

## FAQ draft (12 entries, Stage 2 populated; RUN sub-agent polishes prose)

1. **What is the UK Register of Overseas Entities?**
   Created by the Economic Crime (Transparency and Enforcement) Act 2022 (ECTEA 2022), in force since 1 August 2022, the Register of Overseas Entities (RoE) is a UK companies-registry mechanism administered by Companies House. Any non-UK ("overseas") entity that owns (or wishes to acquire) UK property must register on the RoE and disclose its registrable beneficial owners. Non-compliance triggers HMLR disposition-blocks (cannot sell, charge, or grant long leases), criminal offences, and civil penalties.

2. **What changed in 2025 about public access to trust data on the RoE?**
   The 2025 expansion regulations (made by Secretary of State under ECCTA 2023 amending powers — verify exact SI reference at write time) broadened public access to trust information that was previously available only to law enforcement, AML supervisors, and specifically-applying members of the public who could demonstrate a legitimate interest. Post-expansion, certain categories of trust information — typically the trustee identity, the trust name, the trust type, and beneficiary names or classes — are publicly accessible on the RoE in structured form (subject to the redaction architecture for personal safety and vulnerable persons).

3. **What is the difference between the RoE and the Trust Registration Service (TRS)?**
   Two entirely separate registers. The RoE is administered by Companies House under ECTEA 2022 / ECCTA 2023, captures beneficial-ownership information about overseas entities owning UK property, and is structured-public with redactions. The TRS is administered by HMRC under MLR 2017 SI 2017/692 reg 45, captures trust-side beneficial-ownership data, and is NOT public (accessible only to HMRC, law enforcement, AML supervisors, and parties with legitimate interest). A single trust may need BOTH filings — for instance a Jersey trust holding a Jersey company that owns a UK property.

4. **What information is now publicly accessible?**
   The 2025 expansion typically covers (verify exact list at write time per the SI): trustee identity (name of each trustee), the trust name, the trust establishment date, the type of trust (discretionary / interest-in-possession / bare / etc.), and the names or classes of beneficiaries where the trust is part of the beneficial-ownership chain of an overseas entity owning UK property. The exact scope is set out in the 2025 SI made under ECCTA 2023 amending powers.

5. **What redactions are available to protect personal safety?**
   The expansion preserves redaction routes for narrow safety and vulnerability grounds: personal-safety concerns; vulnerable persons (typically minors and adults at significant risk); residential addresses; full dates of birth. The Secretary of State retains discretion to restrict on application. Note: redaction is not available for preference alone — the grounds are narrow and evidence-based.

6. **Does the CJEU ruling restricting public access in the EU apply in the UK?**
   No. The Court of Justice of the European Union ruled in joined cases C-37/20 + C-601/20 (*Luxembourg Business Registers / Sovim*) in 2022 that the EU 5th Money Laundering Directive's mandate for unconditional public access to beneficial-ownership information was disproportionate; EU member states have narrowed access since. The UK left the EU pre-judgment, so CJEU rulings don't bind the UK post-Brexit. The UK regime evolved independently and the 2025 expansion EXPANDS rather than restricts public access — a deliberate divergence from the post-Sovim EU position.

7. **Who is affected by the expansion?**
   Overseas-entity owners of UK property held through trust structures; trustees in such structures (whether UK-resident or non-UK-resident); beneficiaries named in such trusts; and UK-based accountants and advisers acting for such clients. Typical structures caught: BVI / Jersey / Guernsey / Isle of Man / Luxembourg / Delaware LLC / Cayman company owning UK residential or commercial property, where beneficial ownership runs through one or more trusts.

8. **What is the practical timeline for affected entities?**
   Stage 2 working understanding (RUN session verifies): the expansion regulations were made in 2025, with operative commencement in late 2025 or 2026 (verify exact date against the SI). Affected entities should: (a) review their trust-chain architecture to understand what is now publicly accessible; (b) engage redaction routes where the narrow safety / vulnerability grounds apply; (c) align with the ACSP submitting their RoE filings to ensure next-update accuracy; (d) document understanding for audit-trail integrity.

9. **What are the compliance consequences of non-compliance with the RoE?**
   Substantial. HMLR disposition-block under LRA 2002 Sch 4A — the overseas entity cannot transfer / charge / grant leases >7 years over UK property held by it. Criminal offences under ECTEA s.8 — both the entity and every officer in default. Civil-penalty regime under the Penalties Regulations SI (£2,500 + £500/day per HP §11.A). Plus reputational exposure and increased risk of HMRC enquiry following compliance-flag triggers.

10. **How does the identity-verification regime interact with RoE filings?**
    Per Companies Act 2006 Part 11A (inserted by ECCTA), individuals named in RoE filings (registrable beneficial owners, trustees, officers) face the same identity-verification requirements as UK-company directors and PSCs. From operative dates (verify against current Companies House rollout schedule), submissions are routed through the Authorised Corporate Service Provider (ACSP) framework — landlords' accountants and advisers typically need to be ACSP-registered to handle this for clients.

11. **Does the LTR-trust-pivot interact with the RoE expansion?**
    The post-FA-2025 long-term-resident architecture (replacing the pre-2025 domicile-based excluded-property regime) is a SEPARATE compliance overlay. The RoE expansion sits alongside the LTR pivot but is procedurally distinct: RoE governs WHAT IS PUBLICLY DISCLOSED about the structure; LTR governs WHETHER THE TRUST QUALIFIES for IHT excluded-property treatment. Both apply concurrently. The 2025 RoE expansion does not change tax treatment; the post-FA-2025 LTR architecture does not change disclosure obligations. Sessions writing on offshore-trust-held UK property should thread both gates without conflating them.
