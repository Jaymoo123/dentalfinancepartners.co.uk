# Incorporating an HMO into a Limited Company: Pros and Cons for UK Landlords (2026/27 Decision Guide)

## Statutory anchor

- **Primary (the Section 24 driver — primary motivation for incorporation):** ITTOIA 2005 s.272A (introduced by F(No.2)A 2015) — mortgage-interest restriction for individuals carrying on a property business. Operative effect: individual landlords lose deduction of mortgage interest against rental income, replaced by 20% basic-rate tax credit on the interest. Higher-rate + additional-rate individuals therefore pay tax on rental income calculated WITHOUT interest deduction, then receive only 20% credit. Limited companies are NOT within scope of s.272A — they deduct interest in full under CTA 2009 Part 5 (loan relationships). **HMO-specific salience:** HMO mortgage products typically carry higher interest rates than standard BTL mortgages (specialist-lender market), so the absolute amount of interest restricted is larger for HMO portfolios — sharpening the Section 24 driver for HMO landlords specifically.
- **Primary (CGT relief on transfer):** TCGA 1992 s.162 — incorporation relief. Defers CGT on transfer of a "business" to a LtdCo in exchange for shares. Operative conditions per s.162(1): (a) business transferred as a going concern, (b) all assets (other than cash) transferred, (c) consideration consists wholly or partly of shares in the transferee company. **HMO-business-vs-investment question:** s.162 requires a "business" — generally interpreted to require active engagement beyond passive property-investment. **HMO operation is more easily characterised as a business than standard BTL** because of the active tenant-management, communal-area maintenance, licensing-compliance work, room-letting cycle, and (often) inclusive bills + services. Stage 2 surfaces case-law on the business-vs-investment line for HMOs — *Ramsay v HMRC* [2013] UKUT 226 (TC) is the leading FTT/UT authority on s.162 business test (involved 21 flats with active management; held to be a business). HMO operations typically exceed *Ramsay* threshold. Forward link to `section-162-incorporation-relief-property-landlords`.
- **Primary (SDLT cost on incorporation):** FA 2003 s.55 (residential rate stack) + para 3 Sch 4ZA (the 3% Higher Rates for Additional Dwellings surcharge — applies on every dwelling acquired by a company) + para 1(2) Sch 4A (the 15% flat rate for companies acquiring single dwellings >£500k). **Post-MDR-abolition (FA(No.2) 2024 s.7, effective 1 June 2024 per §1.M lock — Stage 2 verifies):** Multiple Dwellings Relief is NO LONGER available on transfers of multi-dwelling HMO portfolios to a LtdCo. This materially worsens the SDLT cost of HMO incorporation compared to pre-1-June-2024 transfers. (Sch 15 partnership-incorporation relief sum-of-lower-proportions may still mitigate, but requires a genuine pre-incorporation partnership per A7 this batch — bare co-ownership is insufficient.)
- **Primary (SDLT relief via partnership route):** FA 2003 Sch 15 — partnership SDLT regime. Where the HMO portfolio is held in a partnership at the date of transfer to a LtdCo owned in matching proportions, Sch 15 para 10 + the sum-of-lower-proportions (SLP) calculation can dramatically reduce SDLT (often to zero on the partners' own proportions). Cross-reference A7 this batch — the partnership must be a real partnership under PA 1890 s.1, not bare co-ownership per PA 1890 s.2(1). Forward link to existing `partnership-sdlt-relief-schedule-15-fa-2003-incorporation-sum-lower-proportions` page.
- **Primary (HMO-specific capital allowance upside):** CAA 2001 Part 2 (plant + machinery allowances). HMOs typically have substantially MORE capital allowance opportunity than single-let BTLs because of (a) communal kitchens (cookers, fridges, plumbing fittings); (b) communal areas (lounge furnishings, lighting, flooring); (c) fire-suppression + detection (mandatory under HMO licensing — fire doors, FD30s, fire alarms, emergency lighting); (d) integrated heating systems in common parts. **For LtdCo HMO owners, capital allowances offset CT.** For individual HMO owners post-Section-24, capital allowances offset taxable rental income but the s.272A interest restriction still bites. Cross-reference existing pages `hmo-capital-allowances-multi-tenant-landlords-claim` + `hmo-common-parts-capital-allowances-s35-claim-mechanics-multi-tenant-property`. Note: residential dwellings themselves are NOT plant; plant must be installed for use AND not function as part of the building (the CAA 2001 ss.21-23 + List C boundary).
- **Primary (corporation tax rate stack 2026/27):** CTA 2010 s.3 + s.18 + ss.18A-J — main rate 25% from FY24 + small profits rate 19% (profits ≤ £50k) + marginal relief 26.5% effective on profits between £50k and £250k (post-marginal-relief). Associated-company gating per s.18D-J relevant if scaling to multi-HMO group structure. Cross-reference A5 M2-A-B1 `corporation-tax-marginal-relief-uk-guide` (rates) + A8 this batch `eligible-groups-for-group-relief-under-uk-corporation-tax` (group operation).
- **Primary (extraction tax rate stack — dividend route — 2026/27):** ITTOIA 2005 Part 4 Ch 3 + ITA 2007 s.8 — dividend tax rates: £500 dividend allowance + 10.75% basic / 35.75% higher / 39.35% additional from 6 April 2026 per FA 2026 s.4(1)(b) substituting ITA 2007 s.8(2). **Effective combined CT + dividend tax for higher-rate individual extracting all profits:** 25% CT + 35.75% dividend on net of 75% = 25% + (75% × 35.75%) = 51.8% effective vs 40% higher-rate-individual direct income tax + 20% basic-rate-credit-on-interest = (typically) ~50% effective for a leveraged HMO. **The "incorporation always saves tax" intuition is wrong** — the operative comparison depends on (a) how much interest is being restricted by s.272A, (b) whether profits are retained or extracted, (c) marginal vs basic vs additional rate at extraction. Cross-reference §21.4 + A4 M2-A-B1 pillar.
- **Primary (ATED exposure for higher-value HMOs):** FA 2013 ss.94-174 — Annual Tax on Enveloped Dwellings. Applies where a company holds a single dwelling > £500k. **HMOs are generally taxed as single dwellings for ATED unless the property is structurally a multiple-dwelling block** — Stage 2 verifies ATED treatment for typical HMO configurations (3-7 bed converted house; purpose-built HMO; etc). ATED reliefs (qualifying property rental businesses per FA 2013 ss.133-141) generally apply to commercially let HMOs, removing the operative ATED charge but requiring annual return filing — extra admin burden. Cross-link existing site coverage if available.
- **Primary (HMO licensing transfer):** Housing Act 2004 Part 2 (mandatory HMO licensing — 5+ persons in 2+ households; selective licensing schemes by local authority). HMO licence is held by the LANDLORD (the owner or person managing); on transfer to a LtdCo, the LtdCo must apply for new licence in its own name OR seek transfer of the existing licence (depending on local authority practice). **Operative cost:** licence fees + administrative time + potential gap in licensed status during transfer.
- **Supporting (mortgage product narrowing):** No specific statute; market reality. LtdCo HMO mortgage products are narrower than personal HMO products, typically with higher rates (50-75 bps premium common as of 2025-26 market conditions — Stage 2 verifies current market state at write time per existing `buy-to-let-limited-company-mortgage-rates-2026-market-guide`). For HMO specifically, the specialist-lender market is narrower still, sharpening the rate premium.
- **House position reference:** §21 (Ltd Co + FIC family — operational tax). §21.4 (salary-vs-dividends + 2026/27 rate stack with F-20 correction to 10.75%/35.75%/39.35% dividend rates). §1.M (FA(No.2) 2024 s.7 SDLT MDR abolition lock). §3 (incorporation mechanics). No NEW HP lock needed — page sits on existing locks. Note: §21 currently does NOT have an HMO-specific subsection; HMO operational tax sits across §21.4 (extraction) + §1.M (SDLT residential rate stack + post-MDR-abolition) + CGT s.162 (incorporation relief) + HMO-specific operational coverage on existing pages. ## Worked-example data (RUN session uses these as canvas)

### Example 1 — 4-HMO portfolio incorporation: full PROs + CONs math

- **Mrs Patel** owns 4 HMOs personally. Gross rental £60k/year; mortgage interest £30k/year (50% LTV); operating costs £18k/year (compliance, repairs, fire-safety, utilities). Mrs Patel is a higher-rate individual.
- **Personal ownership math (current):** taxable income = £60k − £18k = £42k. Tax at 40% = £16,800. Less 20% basic-rate credit on £30k interest = £6,000. **Effective tax = £10,800/year.**
- **LtdCo math (post-incorporation, retention scenario):** taxable profit = £60k − £18k − £30k (full interest deduction per CTA 2009 Part 5) = £12k. CT at small-profits 19% = **£2,280**. Retention benefit: £9,720 more cash retained at LtdCo level per year vs personal.
- **LtdCo math (post-incorporation, full extraction scenario):** £12k profit → £2,280 CT → £9,720 distributable. Dividend tax on £9,220 (after £500 allowance) at 35.75% (per §21.4 / F-20 from 6 April 2026) = £3,296. Combined CT + dividend = £5,576/year. Saving vs personal £10,800 = **£5,224/year**.
- **One-off incorporation costs (deducted from saving):**
  - **SDLT post-MDR-abolition (per §1.M):** £600k portfolio value at residential rate stack with 3% HRAD on each property separately (no MDR linking from 1 June 2024). Per-property SDLT ≈ £8k–£12k × 4 = **£32k–£48k**.
  - **CGT (if no s.162 incorporation relief):** market-value disposal at TCGA 1992 s.17. With s.162 relief (HMO operations typically pass the *Ramsay* business test): **CGT deferred** via roll-into-share-base; tax-free DLA credit balance ≈ portfolio MV − historic cost.
  - **Legal + valuation + lender refinancing + accounting setup:** typically £5k–£15k all-in.
- **Payback period:** £32k–£48k SDLT + £10k setup = £42k–£58k one-off cost ÷ £5,224 annual saving = **8–11 years**. **The math favours incorporation only over long retention horizons.**

### Example 2 — Section 162 incorporation relief: HMO business-test depth

- Continuing Example 1: Mrs Patel applies s.162 incorporation relief on transfer.
- **s.162(1) conditions:** (a) business transferred as a going concern; (b) all assets (other than cash) transferred; (c) consideration consists wholly or partly of shares.
- **Business-vs-investment test (post-*Ramsay*):** HMO operations carry materially more business-like activity than standard BTL — active tenant management (room-letting cycle, communal-area maintenance), licensing compliance (Housing Act 2004 Part 2), often inclusive bills + services (utilities, cleaning, broadband). Mrs Patel's 4-HMO portfolio with ≈ 16-20 tenants total + active management = comfortably above the *Ramsay* 21-flat threshold. **s.162 typically available.**
- **Operational outcome:** historic-cost CGT base £200k (purchase prices); market value at transfer £600k. Indemnified gain £400k. Under s.162, gain rolled into shares of NewCo (effective £200k base in NewCo shares). NO CGT crystallised at transfer. NewCo holds property at £600k MV in its accounts; £400k tax-free DLA credit balance created by the value-differential (per F-9 §21.1 lock from M2-A-B1 batch — DLA exhaustion trap applies).
- **Operative reader insight:** for HMO landlords, s.162 typically works; the CGT-deferral benefit removes one of the largest cost barriers to incorporation. Forward-link to `section-162-incorporation-relief-property-landlords`.

### Example 3 — HMO-specific capital allowance upside (the structurally distinct advantage)

- **Mrs Kapoor**'s 4-HMO portfolio includes substantial plant + machinery in common parts: communal kitchens (cookers, fridges, microwaves, plumbing fittings) ≈ £30k; communal lounge / dining areas (furnishings, lighting, flooring) ≈ £25k; mandatory fire-suppression + detection (fire doors FD30s, fire alarms, emergency lighting) per Housing Act 2004 licensing ≈ £15k; integrated heating systems in common parts ≈ £10k. **Total qualifying plant ≈ £80k** (per CAA 2001 Part 2; HMO common-parts treatment per existing `hmo-common-parts-capital-allowances-s35-claim-mechanics-multi-tenant-property`).
- **Personal ownership:** capital allowances offset rental income but s.272A interest restriction still bites; effective marginal tax-saving on the allowance ≈ £80k × 40% (HRT on offset income) − £80k × 20% (lost basic-rate credit on the interest portion) = ≈ £16k effective saving.
- **LtdCo ownership:** capital allowances offset CT-rated profits; effective saving = £80k × 19%–25% CT rate = £15k–£20k.
- **Operative HMO-specific insight:** the capital-allowance pool is materially larger for HMOs than standard BTL because of the regulatory + operational factors. RUN session frames this as the structurally distinct HMO incorporation advantage (single-let BTL incorporation does not access this).

### Example 4 — SDLT post-MDR-abolition: the cost-side change

- **Same 4-HMO portfolio**; market value £600k (4 properties at £150k average).
- **PRE-1-June-2024 (with MDR):** Multiple Dwellings Relief allowed treating the 4-property bundle as a single transaction with rate stack applied to the average value (£150k × 4 = £600k, divided by 4 = £150k average) at residential rate stack + 3% HRAD = ≈ £18k total SDLT for the bundle.
- **POST-1-June-2024 (FA(No.2) 2024 s.7 abolition per §1.M):** each dwelling taxed separately at full residential rate stack with 3% HRAD. £150k at residential rates: 5% on first £125k + 3% surcharge on full £150k = £6,250 + £4,500 = £10,750 per property × 4 = **£43,000 total SDLT for the bundle**.
- **MDR abolition effect:** materially worsens the cost-side of HMO incorporation by approximately 2.4× vs the pre-1-Jun-2024 framing. RUN session frames this as the operative cost-side change since 1 June 2024.
- **Mitigation via Sch 15 partnership-incorporation route:** if Mrs Patel's portfolio is held via a genuine partnership (per A7 this batch + PA 1890 s.1 four-tests gate), FA 2003 Sch 15 para 10 sum-of-lower-proportions calculation can reduce SDLT to ≈ £0 on the partner-side proportions (where partners own the LtdCo in matching shares).

### Example 5 — When the math FAVOURS personal ownership

- **Mr Verma** owns a single low-yield HMO (1 property; £6k gross rental; £1.5k mortgage interest; £1.8k operating costs; market value £180k; he is a basic-rate taxpayer).
- **Personal ownership math:** taxable income = £6k − £1.8k = £4.2k. Tax at 20% basic rate = £840. 20% basic-rate credit on £1.5k interest = £300. **Effective tax = £540/year.**
- **LtdCo math (full extraction):** £6k − £1.8k − £1.5k = £2.7k profit. CT at 19% = £513. Dividend tax on £2.2k (after £500 allowance) at 10.75% = £237. Combined = £750/year. **More expensive than personal by £210/year.**
- **PLUS one-off incorporation costs:** SDLT ≈ £10.75k + setup ≈ £5k = £15.75k that never amortises (the structure is more expensive ongoing).
- **Operational conclusion:** for a single low-yield HMO with a basic-rate owner, LtdCo route loses. RUN session frames this honestly: incorporation is not universally beneficial.

### Example 6 — HMO licensing transfer operational gotcha

- **Mrs Kapoor** decides to incorporate her 3-HMO portfolio. Each HMO holds a 5-year mandatory licence under Housing Act 2004 Part 2.
- **On transfer:** the HMO licence is held by the landlord — on transfer to LtdCo, the LtdCo must apply for a NEW licence in its own name (or seek transfer of existing — local authority practice varies). Practical pattern: most local authorities require fresh application + new fees + administrative cycle (4–12 weeks).
- **Cost:** £500–£1,500 per HMO × 3 = £1,500–£4,500 + administrative time.
- **Risk during transfer:** running an unlicensed HMO is an offence under Housing Act 2004 s.72 with rent-repayment-order exposure under Housing and Planning Act 2016 ss.40–45 (tenant can recover up to 12 months' rent). Operational discipline: time the licence-transfer / re-application to overlap with the SDLT + lender + accounting transfer; do not leave a gap in licensed status.

## FAQ expansion (RUN session polishes prose; 10-12 FAQs target)

1. **Q: Why does HMO incorporation become a question post-Section 24?**
   A: ITTOIA 2005 s.272A (introduced by F(No.2)A 2015) restricts mortgage-interest deduction for individuals carrying on a property business — full deduction replaced by 20% basic-rate tax credit. Higher-rate and additional-rate individuals therefore pay tax on rental income calculated WITHOUT interest deduction. Limited companies are NOT within scope of s.272A; they deduct interest in full under CTA 2009 Part 5 loan relationships. For HMOs specifically the impact is sharper because HMO mortgages typically carry higher rates than standard BTL mortgages (specialist-lender market), so the absolute amount of interest restricted is larger.

2. **Q: What are the main pros of incorporating an HMO into a limited company?**
   A: Six structural advantages. (i) Section 24 unrestricted — full interest deductibility per CTA 2009 Part 5. (ii) Corporation tax rate (19% small profits up to £50k; 25% main rate; 26.5% marginal-relief effective £50k–£250k per CTA 2010 ss.18A–J) typically lower than HRT/ART on a leveraged HMO. (iii) HMO-specific capital allowance upside — substantially more plant + machinery than standard BTL (communal kitchens, common areas, mandatory fire-suppression + detection per Housing Act 2004 licensing). (iv) Reinvestment retention at CT rate. (v) Asset protection via separate LtdCo per HMO or per region. (vi) Succession planning via share transfer with optional FIC overlay for the next generation.

3. **Q: What are the main cons of incorporating an HMO?**
   A: Multiple cost-side items. (i) SDLT on transfer at residential rate stack with 3% HRAD surcharge per Sch 4ZA, materially worsened by F(No.2)A 2024 s.7 MDR abolition effective 1 June 2024 per §1.M lock — no longer mitigated by multiple-dwellings relief. (ii) CGT on transfer at market value unless TCGA 1992 s.162 incorporation relief or FA 2003 Sch 15 partnership-incorporation relief applies. (iii) Double-layer tax on extraction (CT + dividend at 10.75%/35.75%/39.35% per F-20 §21.4 from 6 April 2026). (iv) HMO mortgage product narrowing in LtdCo space + typical 50–75bps rate premium. (v) ATED admin even where the property-rental-business relief applies (annual return for dwellings > £500k). (vi) HMO licensing transfer under Housing Act 2004. (vii) Annual filing overhead (CT600, statutory accounts, confirmation statement, PSC register, ID verification per ECCTA Part 1 / §11.A). (viii) Loss of CGT annual exempt amount inside LtdCo. (ix) Loss of PRA / lettings relief on disposal. (x) Refinancing cost + lender consent process.

4. **Q: Does TCGA 1992 s.162 incorporation relief typically work for HMO portfolios?**
   A: Yes — typically. s.162 requires a "business" to be transferred as a going concern. HMO operations typically exceed the *Ramsay v HMRC* [2013] UKUT 226 business-test threshold by a material margin because of active tenant management (room-letting cycle, communal-area maintenance), licensing-compliance work (Housing Act 2004 Part 2), and often inclusive bills + services (utilities, cleaning, broadband). The result is CGT deferral via roll-into-share-base — no CGT crystallises at transfer; the gain rolls into the share base. A tax-free DLA credit balance is also created by the value-differential, providing tax-efficient extraction route in the early post-incorporation years (subject to the DLA exhaustion trap per §21.1).

5. **Q: How does the FA 2003 Sch 15 partnership-incorporation route work?**
   A: Where the HMO portfolio is held in a genuine partnership at the date of transfer to a LtdCo owned in matching proportions, FA 2003 Sch 15 para 10 + sum-of-lower-proportions (SLP) calculation can dramatically reduce SDLT — often to nil on the partners' own proportions. The partnership must be a real partnership under PA 1890 s.1 four-tests (per A7 this batch), not bare co-ownership per s.2(1). For multi-owner HMO portfolios, this is the operative SDLT-mitigation route post-MDR-abolition; forward-link to `partnership-sdlt-relief-schedule-15-fa-2003-incorporation-sum-lower-proportions` for mechanics.

6. **Q: What is the SDLT cost impact of MDR abolition (effective 1 June 2024)?**
   A: F(No.2)A 2024 s.7 abolished Multiple Dwellings Relief from 1 June 2024 per §1.M lock. Pre-abolition: multi-property bundles transferred in a single transaction enjoyed MDR, with SDLT computed on the average value across the bundle (materially lower stack). Post-abolition: each dwelling taxed separately at full residential rate stack with 3% HRAD surcharge for company purchasers. Practical effect on a £600k 4-HMO bundle: pre-1-Jun-2024 ≈ £18k total SDLT (with MDR); post-1-Jun-2024 ≈ £43k total SDLT — approximately 2.4× cost. Only mitigation route now is the Sch 15 partnership-incorporation relief (where partnership status genuinely exists per A7).

7. **Q: What is the HMO-specific capital allowance upside?**
   A: HMOs typically carry substantially more capital allowance opportunity than single-let BTLs because of (a) communal kitchens (cookers, fridges, microwaves, plumbing fittings); (b) communal areas (lounge furnishings, lighting, flooring); (c) mandatory fire-suppression + detection per Housing Act 2004 licensing (fire doors FD30s, fire alarms, emergency lighting); (d) integrated heating systems in common parts. The CAA 2001 Part 2 P&M allowances apply to plant installed in common parts; the residential-dwelling restriction at CAA 2001 ss.21–23 + List C does not exclude common-area plant where the property is HMO-licensed. For LtdCo HMO owners these allowances offset CT; for individual owners post-Section-24 the s.272A interest restriction still bites.

8. **Q: For typical HMO portfolio scales, at what point does the math favour incorporation?**
   A: Rough decision band: portfolio of 3+ HMOs + LTV ≥ 50% + higher-rate or additional-rate individual + retention intent → favours incorporation (typical payback 5–10 years). Single HMO + low LTV (≤ 30%) + basic-rate individual + extraction intent → typically favours personal ownership (incorporation costs never amortise). 2-HMO portfolios are borderline — depends on individual rate, LTV, and time horizon. The decision is genuinely fact-intensive; the page provides the framework, not a formulaic answer.

9. **Q: How do mortgage product narrowing + rate premium affect the decision?**
   A: HMO LtdCo mortgage products are narrower than personal HMO products; the specialist-lender market is narrower still for LtdCo HMO + 5+ bedroom configurations. Typical rate premium 50–75bps as of 2025-26 market conditions (RUN session re-verifies via `buy-to-let-limited-company-mortgage-rates-2026-market-guide` at write time). Practical effect: refinancing the portfolio into LtdCo at incorporation typically increases monthly interest cost. For a £30k/year interest portfolio, a 50bps premium ≈ £1,500/year additional interest cost — non-trivial against the tax saving. RUN session frames this honestly.

10. **Q: Does ATED apply to HMOs?**
    A: ATED (FA 2013 ss.94–174) applies to companies holding a single dwelling > £500k. HMOs are typically taxed as single dwellings for ATED — unless the property is structurally a multiple-dwelling block (separate self-contained units), in which case the dwelling test is applied per unit. For HMOs above £500k, ATED chargeable amounts apply unless the company qualifies for the property-rental-business relief at ss.133–141 (which usually covers commercially-let HMOs). Even where the relief applies, an annual return must be filed — a non-trivial admin burden. Below £500k, ATED does not apply.

11. **Q: What about the HMO licence transfer on incorporation?**
    A: The HMO licence is held by the landlord under Housing Act 2004 Part 2. On transfer to a LtdCo, the LtdCo must apply for a new licence in its own name (or seek transfer of the existing licence — local authority practice varies). Operative cost: licence fees of £500–£1,500 per HMO per 5-year cycle plus administrative time. Risk during transfer: running an unlicensed HMO is an offence under Housing Act 2004 s.72 with rent-repayment-order exposure under Housing and Planning Act 2016 ss.40–45 (tenant can recover up to 12 months' rent). Operational discipline: time the licence application to overlap with the SDLT + lender + accounting transfer to avoid an unlicensed gap.

12. **Q: How does this page differ from the existing HMO-incorporation mechanics page?**
    A: This page is the **decision-helper / pros-and-cons framing layer** — honest decision framework weighing the seven HMO-specific factors that change the standard incorporation math. The existing `incorporating-hmo-portfolio-to-limited-company` page covers the **mechanics layer** — the how-to of the SDLT + CGT + lender + accounting steps once the decision is made. Reading sequence: this page first to settle the should-you question; the mechanics page once you decide yes. The pages cross-link.
