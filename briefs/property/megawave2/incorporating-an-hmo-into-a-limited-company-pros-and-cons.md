# Incorporating an HMO into a Limited Company: Pros and Cons for UK Landlords (2026/27 Decision Guide)

## Statutory anchor

- **Primary (the Section 24 driver — primary motivation for incorporation):** ITTOIA 2005 s.272A (introduced by F(No.2)A 2015) — mortgage-interest restriction for individuals carrying on a property business. Operative effect: individual landlords lose deduction of mortgage interest against rental income, replaced by 20% basic-rate tax credit on the interest. Higher-rate + additional-rate individuals therefore pay tax on rental income calculated WITHOUT interest deduction, then receive only 20% credit. Limited companies are NOT within scope of s.272A — they deduct interest in full under CTA 2009 Part 5 (loan relationships). **HMO-specific salience:** HMO mortgage products typically carry higher interest rates than standard BTL mortgages (specialist-lender market), so the absolute amount of interest restricted is larger for HMO portfolios — sharpening the Section 24 driver for HMO landlords specifically.
- **Primary (CGT relief on transfer):** TCGA 1992 s.162 — incorporation relief. Defers CGT on transfer of a "business" to a LtdCo in exchange for shares. Operative conditions per s.162(1): (a) business transferred as a going concern, (b) all assets (other than cash) transferred, (c) consideration consists wholly or partly of shares in the transferee company. **HMO-business-vs-investment question:** s.162 requires a "business" — generally interpreted to require active engagement beyond passive property-investment. **HMO operation is more easily characterised as a business than standard BTL** because of the active tenant-management, communal-area maintenance, licensing-compliance work, room-letting cycle, and (often) inclusive bills + services. Stage 2 surfaces case-law on the business-vs-investment line for HMOs — *Ramsay v HMRC* [2013] UKUT 226 (TC) is the leading FTT/UT authority on s.162 business test (involved 21 flats with active management; held to be a business). HMO operations typically exceed *Ramsay* threshold. Forward link to `section-162-incorporation-relief-property-landlords`.
- **Primary (SDLT cost on incorporation):** FA 2003 s.55 (residential rate stack) + para 3 Sch 4ZA (the 3% Higher Rates for Additional Dwellings surcharge — applies on every dwelling acquired by a company) + para 1(2) Sch 4A (the 15% flat rate for companies acquiring single dwellings >£500k). **Post-MDR-abolition (FA(No.2) 2024 s.7, effective 1 June 2024 per §1.M lock — Stage 2 verifies):** Multiple Dwellings Relief is NO LONGER available on transfers of multi-dwelling HMO portfolios to a LtdCo. This materially worsens the SDLT cost of HMO incorporation compared to pre-1-June-2024 transfers. (Sch 15 partnership-incorporation relief sum-of-lower-proportions may still mitigate, but requires a genuine pre-incorporation partnership per A7 this batch — bare co-ownership is insufficient.)
- **Primary (SDLT relief via partnership route):** FA 2003 Sch 15 — partnership SDLT regime. Where the HMO portfolio is held in a partnership at the date of transfer to a LtdCo owned in matching proportions, Sch 15 para 10 + the sum-of-lower-proportions (SLP) calculation can dramatically reduce SDLT (often to zero on the partners' own proportions). Cross-reference A7 this batch — the partnership must be a real partnership under PA 1890 s.1, not bare co-ownership per PA 1890 s.2(1). Forward link to existing `partnership-sdlt-relief-schedule-15-fa-2003-incorporation-sum-lower-proportions` page.
- **Primary (HMO-specific capital allowance upside):** CAA 2001 Part 2 (plant + machinery allowances). HMOs typically have substantially MORE capital allowance opportunity than single-let BTLs because of (a) communal kitchens (cookers, fridges, plumbing fittings); (b) communal areas (lounge furnishings, lighting, flooring); (c) fire-suppression + detection (mandatory under HMO licensing — fire doors, FD30s, fire alarms, emergency lighting); (d) integrated heating systems in common parts. **For LtdCo HMO owners, capital allowances offset CT.** For individual HMO owners post-Section-24, capital allowances offset taxable rental income but the s.272A interest restriction still bites. Cross-reference existing pages `hmo-capital-allowances-multi-tenant-landlords-claim` + `hmo-common-parts-capital-allowances-s35-claim-mechanics-multi-tenant-property`. Note: residential dwellings themselves are NOT plant; plant must be installed for use AND not function as part of the building (the CAA 2001 ss.21-23 + List C boundary).
- **Primary (corporation tax rate stack 2026/27):** CTA 2010 s.3 + s.18 + ss.18A-J — main rate 25% from FY24 + small profits rate 19% (profits ≤ £50k) + marginal relief 26.5% effective on profits between £50k and £250k (post-marginal-relief). Associated-company gating per s.18D-J relevant if scaling to multi-HMO group structure. Cross-reference A5 M2-A-B1 `corporation-tax-marginal-relief-uk-guide` (rates) + A8 this batch `eligible-groups-for-group-relief-under-uk-corporation-tax` (group operation).
- **Primary (extraction tax rate stack — dividend route — 2026/27):** ITTOIA 2005 Part 4 Ch 3 + ITA 2007 s.8 — dividend tax rates: £500 dividend allowance + 10.75% basic / 35.75% higher / 39.35% additional from 6 April 2026 per FA 2026 s.4(1)(b) substituting ITA 2007 s.8(2). **Effective combined CT + dividend tax for higher-rate individual extracting all profits:** 25% CT + 35.75% dividend on net of 75% = 25% + (75% × 35.75%) = 51.8% effective vs 40% higher-rate-individual direct income tax + 20% basic-rate-credit-on-interest = (typically) ~50% effective for a leveraged HMO. **The "incorporation always saves tax" intuition is wrong** — the operative comparison depends on (a) how much interest is being restricted by s.272A, (b) whether profits are retained or extracted, (c) marginal vs basic vs additional rate at extraction. Cross-reference §21.4 + A4 M2-A-B1 pillar.
- **Primary (ATED exposure for higher-value HMOs):** FA 2013 ss.94-174 — Annual Tax on Enveloped Dwellings. Applies where a company holds a single dwelling > £500k. **HMOs are generally taxed as single dwellings for ATED unless the property is structurally a multiple-dwelling block** — Stage 2 verifies ATED treatment for typical HMO configurations (3-7 bed converted house; purpose-built HMO; etc). ATED reliefs (qualifying property rental businesses per FA 2013 ss.133-141) generally apply to commercially let HMOs, removing the operative ATED charge but requiring annual return filing — extra admin burden. Cross-link existing site coverage if available.
- **Primary (HMO licensing transfer):** Housing Act 2004 Part 2 (mandatory HMO licensing — 5+ persons in 2+ households; selective licensing schemes by local authority). HMO licence is held by the LANDLORD (the owner or person managing); on transfer to a LtdCo, the LtdCo must apply for new licence in its own name OR seek transfer of the existing licence (depending on local authority practice). **Operative cost:** licence fees + administrative time + potential gap in licensed status during transfer.
- **Supporting (mortgage product narrowing):** No specific statute; market reality. LtdCo HMO mortgage products are narrower than personal HMO products, typically with higher rates (50-75 bps premium common as of 2025-26 market conditions — Stage 2 verifies current market state at write time per existing `buy-to-let-limited-company-mortgage-rates-2026-market-guide`). For HMO specifically, the specialist-lender market is narrower still, sharpening the rate premium.
- **House position reference:** §21 (Ltd Co + FIC family — operational tax). §21.4 (salary-vs-dividends + 2026/27 rate stack with F-20 correction to 10.75%/35.75%/39.35% dividend rates). §1.M (FA(No.2) 2024 s.7 SDLT MDR abolition lock). §3 (incorporation mechanics). No NEW HP lock needed — page sits on existing locks. Note: §21 currently does NOT have an HMO-specific subsection; HMO operational tax sits across §21.4 (extraction) + §1.M (SDLT residential rate stack + post-MDR-abolition) + CGT s.162 (incorporation relief) + HMO-specific operational coverage on existing pages. The cross-link grid is dense.
