# A Complete Guide to UK Payroll Taxes and Deductions for Property-Business Employers

## Statutory anchor

- Primary (charge to tax on employment income): **Income Tax (Earnings and Pensions) Act 2003 ("ITEPA 2003")** — Part 2 (charge to tax on employment income, ss.3-61L), Part 3 (earnings + benefits code, ss.62-226A), Part 4 (exemptions), Part 11 (PAYE — ss.682-712). Verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2003/1/contents on 2026-05-26.
- Primary (PAYE operational mechanics): **The Income Tax (Pay As You Earn) Regulations 2003 (SI 2003/2682)** — Part 2 (codes, regs 13-20), Part 3 (deduction and repayment, regs 21-67A), Part 4 (payments, returns and information including RTI, regs 67B-97M), Part 5 (employers, regs 98-104). Verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2003/2682/contents on 2026-05-26.
  - **Reg 21** — duty to deduct PAYE by reference to employee code.
  - **Reg 67B** — Real Time Information (RTI) returns: the Full Payment Submission (FPS) is the operative on-or-before-payment-day return.
  - **Reg 67G** — payment cycle for RTI employers (typically by 22nd of the following month where paid electronically, 19th by cheque).
  - **Reg 73** — annual return obligation (legacy mechanic; RTI subsumes most of it but the historical P35/P14 references remain in the regulations).
- Primary (NIC): **Social Security Contributions and Benefits Act 1992 ("SSCBA 1992")** — Part 1, ss.1-19A; primary Class 1 contributions on employee earnings + secondary Class 1 contributions on employers; Class 1A on benefits in kind. Operative rates set annually in the Welfare Benefits Up-rating Order / National Insurance Contributions Regulations cycle.
- Primary (Apprenticeship Levy): **Finance Act 2016 ss.99-121** — 0.5% levy on employers' annual pay bill above £3 million, with £15,000 allowance.
- Supporting:
  - **Employment Rights Act 1996 s.8** — statutory itemised pay statement requirement.
  - **National Minimum Wage Act 1998** + **National Minimum Wage Regulations 2015 (SI 2015/621)** — minimum pay floor (annually uprated).
  - **Pensions Act 2008 + Automatic Enrolment Regulations** — auto-enrolment duties on UK employers with at least one worker.
  - **Education (Student Loans) (Repayment) Regulations 2009 (SI 2009/470)** + **2018 postgraduate loan regs** — student / postgraduate loan deductions via payroll.
  - **Attachment of Earnings Act 1971** + **Welfare Reform Act 2012 s.105** — court-ordered AEOs + DEAs (Direct Earnings Attachments).
- House position reference: payroll is NOT a locked HP cluster in current scope (§3 covers MTD for ITSA only; §11.A covers Companies House ECCTA; §21 covers Ltd Co + FIC operational tax but not payroll mechanics). **NEW HP LOCK CANDIDATE** for Stage 1b: a §33 (or similar) "Payroll mechanics for property-business employers" cluster anchored on ITEPA 2003 + SI 2003/2682 + SSCBA 1992. **Worked example A: £30,000 in-house bookkeeper at landlord LtdCo, 2026/27**

- Gross salary: £30,000
- Employee NIC (8% on (£30,000 - £12,570) = £17,430): £1,394.40
- Income tax (20% on £17,430): £3,486.00
- Employee net cash: £30,000 - £1,394.40 - £3,486.00 = £25,119.60
- Employer NIC (15% on (£30,000 - £5,000) = £25,000): £3,750.00
- Employment Allowance offset (if available; not for single-director LtdCo with no other employee, but available here with director + bookkeeper): up to £3,750 of the £10,500 EA reduces this to £0
- Apprenticeship Levy: £0 (pay bill below £3m)
- Total cost to employer (with EA available): £30,000 + £0 = £30,000
- Total cost to employer (without EA, single-director LtdCo before hire): £30,000 + £3,750 = £33,750
- CT deduction (CTA 2009 Part 3 wholly-and-exclusively): full £30,000 (or £33,750 with employer NIC) reduces taxable profits
- CT saving at 25% main rate: £7,500 → effective net cost £22,500 (with EA) or £25,312.50 (without EA)

**Worked example B: connected-companies Apprenticeship Levy interaction**

- Portfolio operator runs five SPVs, each £700k pay bill = combined £3.5m
- CTA 2010 connected-companies rules treat them as a single £3.5m pay-bill employer for the £3m levy threshold (FA 2016 s.101)
- Levy due: 0.5% × (£3,500,000 - £3,000,000) - £15,000 levy allowance = £2,500 - £15,000 = £0 (allowance covers it)
- Allowance must be allocated across the group at the start of the tax year

## FAQ draft (10-12 entries, Stage 2 populated; RUN sub-agent polishes prose)

1. **When does my property LtdCo become an "employer" for PAYE purposes?**
   The moment you make the first taxable payment to anyone classed as an employee. You must register with HMRC as an employer before that first payment (online via gov.uk; the registration is operative for the tax month in which it's made under SI 2003/2682 reg 9). A director paying themselves a director's salary is also an employee for PAYE purposes — the company becomes an employer the first month the director-salary runs.

2. **Does paying a single director below the NIC threshold mean no PAYE registration?**
   No. Paying any amount that counts as employment income triggers a PAYE liability even if income tax and NIC are nil for that month. The exception is where total monthly payments to all employees are below the Lower Earnings Limit (£123/week, £533/month for 2026/27) and the director has no other PAYE source — but most directors run a salary at or near the Personal Allowance, which is well above LEL and inside PAYE scope.

3. **What does a £30,000 bookkeeper actually cost my landlord LtdCo in 2026/27?**
   Gross £30,000; employer NIC of £3,750 (15% on £25,000 above the £5,000 secondary threshold); zero Apprenticeship Levy. If you qualify for Employment Allowance the £3,750 is offset, so total cost is £30,000. If you don't qualify (single-director LtdCo with no other employee) the total cost is £33,750. CT deduction at 25% main rate brings the net cost to around £22,500 to £25,300 depending on EA availability.

4. **Does my single-director LtdCo qualify for the £10,500 Employment Allowance?**
   No. The single-director exclusion (introduced April 2016 via The Employment Allowance (Excluded Companies) Regulations 2016 / SI 2016/344) bars a company whose only employee paid above the Secondary Threshold is the sole director. The moment a second employee is paid above ST (such as the in-house bookkeeper in the worked example), the exclusion lifts. Some accountants pay an additional family-member-employee a small wage specifically to lift this bar — that approach carries National Minimum Wage and settlements-legislation risk and should be advised on case-by-case.

5. **What is the Apprenticeship Levy threshold and when does my property group cross it?**
   The 0.5% levy applies to pay bills above £3 million per tax year, after a £15,000 levy allowance. For most landlord LtdCos and portfolio SPVs the threshold is irrelevant. Portfolio operators running multiple SPVs need to apply CTA 2010 connected-companies rules under FA 2016 s.101: the combined pay bill of all connected employers is tested against the £3m threshold and the £15k allowance is split across the group at the start of the tax year. A 5-SPV portfolio with £700k pay bill per SPV (combined £3.5m) crosses the threshold but the levy allowance fully covers the £2,500 liability.

6. **How does PAYE interact with corporation tax for a landlord LtdCo?**
   Salary, employer NIC and Apprenticeship Levy are wholly-and-exclusively deductible against company profits in the accounting period in which they're incurred (CTA 2009 Part 3). The 9-month rule under CTA 2009 s.1289 catches accrued but unpaid earnings: a bonus accrued at year-end but not actually paid within 9 months of the year-end loses the CT deduction for that period and is deductible only in the period of actual payment.

7. **What benefits-in-kind issues catch landlord-employers most often?**
   Three frequent ones: (i) employer-provided accommodation for a live-in property manager — ITEPA 2003 ss.97-113 charge applies unless the "necessary for proper performance" or "better performance / customary" exemption operates (s.99) and even where exempt, the property's annual value still feeds the Class 1A computation; (ii) employer-provided vehicle for a refurb foreman — standard company-car BIK rates under ss.114-148 with the CO2-driven percentage scale; (iii) employer-paid private fuel — the multiplier-based charge under s.150 which is rarely worth the BIK exposure for low-mileage drivers.

8. **What is the auto-enrolment duty trigger and the contribution stack?**
   Every UK employer with at least one qualifying worker (broadly: aged 22 to State Pension Age, earning above the £10,000 trigger, working in the UK) must auto-enrol them into a qualifying pension scheme. Minimum total contribution is 8% of qualifying earnings (£6,240 - £50,270 band for 2026/27) of which the employer must contribute at least 3%. Declaration of compliance to The Pensions Regulator is required within 5 months of the duties start date, and every 3 years on re-declaration.

9. **What's the on-boarding checklist when I first hire?**
   Six items: (i) register as employer with HMRC online before first payday; (ii) select payroll software that is RTI-compatible (most cloud accounting platforms include it); (iii) obtain a Government Gateway PAYE login and activate the PAYE for Employers service; (iv) check the prospective employee's right to work and obtain a P45 or starter checklist; (v) issue a written statement of particulars per Employment Rights Act 1996 s.1 by the start date; (vi) select an auto-enrolment provider (NEST is free for employers; alternatives include Smart, The People's Pension, Aviva).

10. **What's the difference between PAYE and CIS for landlord LtdCos that hire refurb labour?**
    PAYE applies to employees — people whose engagement passes the *Ready Mixed Concrete* employment-status tests (control, integration, mutuality of obligation). CIS applies to self-employed construction sub-contractors paid for construction operations on a property. The two regimes are distinct and a single landlord LtdCo can be both an employer (for the in-house bookkeeper) and a contractor (for the refurb foreman who is genuinely self-employed). The C5 sibling page covers CIS reporting in more detail.

11. **What happens if I get the employment-vs-self-employment classification wrong?**
    HMRC can re-categorise the worker as an employee, raise back-PAYE + back-NIC for up to 4 years (extended to 6 if carelessness or 20 if deliberate behaviour), and impose penalties under FA 2007 Sch 24 of up to 30% of the unpaid liability (careless) or 70% (deliberate). The risk concentrates around long-term in-house roles (property managers, bookkeepers) where the engagement looks substantively employed but is paper-styled as a contract for services. The CEST tool on gov.uk is HMRC's preferred classification check, though it's not legally binding.

12. **Should I run my own payroll or outsource to a payroll bureau?**
    Three factors decide: (i) headcount — under 3 employees, in-house software is workable; over 5, bureau saves time; (ii) complexity — directors-only payroll is simple; payroll with statutory payments (SSP, SMP), salary-sacrifice arrangements, or share schemes pushes toward a bureau; (iii) error tolerance — late or wrong FPS exposes the company to FA 2009 Sch 55 / Sch 56 penalties stacked monthly (see C5 sibling page). Most landlord LtdCos with one or two employees run payroll in-house via cloud accounting software; LtdCos with five-plus employees or benefits-in-kind exposure typically benefit from outsourcing.
