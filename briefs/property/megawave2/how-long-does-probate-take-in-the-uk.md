# How Long Does Probate Take in the UK? Realistic Timeline for Property-Owning Estates

## Statutory anchor

- Primary (probate as a legal process): **Senior Courts Act 1981 ss.108-127** (grants of representation regime) + **Non-Contentious Probate Rules 1987 (SI 1987/2024)** as amended. Verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1981/54/part/V on 2026-05-26. SCA 1981 s.114 + s.116 cover the grant categories (probate where there is a will, letters of administration where there is none); the NCPR govern application procedure, evidence requirements, and contentious-non-contentious routing.
- Primary (the IHT side — the inheritance-tax filing that gates the grant):
  - **IHTA 1984 s.4** — chargeable transfer on death; the IHT account triggers.
  - **IHTA 1984 s.216 + s.226** — duties on personal representatives to deliver an IHT account; payment-of-tax-before-grant rule.
  - **IHT400 + IHT421 framework:** for **non-excepted estates**, the personal representative submits IHT400 (full inheritance tax account) and any payable IHT before the grant. HMRC then issues IHT421 (probate summary / payment receipt) which the personal representative submits to HMCTS Probate Service as evidence the IHT side is settled. **HMRC self-declared 12-week target for IHT421 issuance from receipt of IHT400** per HMRC published service standards. Re-verify at Stage 2 against current gov.uk publication — the 12-week target has been periodically extended in practice.
  - **Excepted estates regime (the IHT205/IHT207 successor):** per the **Inheritance Tax (Delivery of Accounts) (Excepted Estates) (Amendment) Regulations 2021 (SI 2021/1167)** in force from 1 January 2022, most low-value or wholly-spouse-exempt estates fall outside the IHT400 obligation. Personal representatives self-certify the estate as excepted in the probate application (no separate IHT account filed; HMRC has 60 days after grant to require additional information per IHTA 1984 s.256(1A)).
- Primary (the probate side — the grant application itself):
  - **HMCTS Probate Service** (the post-2019 single online portal). **Published service standard: 16 weeks from receipt of valid application to grant**, per HMCTS Probate Service published service-level targets. Re-verify at Stage 2 against current gov.uk publication.
  - **Stop notices + requisitions:** the Probate Service can pause an application if the documentation is incomplete or inconsistent, adding 4-16 weeks. Common requisition triggers: original will not provided; executor identity inconsistencies; missing IHT421 for non-excepted estate; ambiguity over domicile or jurisdiction.
- Primary (the estate-administration income-tax + CGT regime — the tax compliance during probate):
  - **ITA 2007 s.466** — personal representatives' income tax position; **TCGA 1992 s.62** — uplifted base cost at death (acquisition cost = market value at date of death); **TCGA 1992 s.3** — personal representatives' annual exempt amount available for the tax year of death and the following two tax years (currently £3,000 per HMRC published figures from 6 April 2024; re-verify at Stage 2 against current gov.uk publication).
  - **CTA 2010 ss.34-35** — administration period income tax for personal representatives at the basic rate (20%) on rental income, with no personal allowance available (PRs are not individuals for personal allowance purposes per HMRC TSEM7320).
- Primary (the rental property during administration — the income-tax + CGT mechanics):
  - **Property income from a let asset during administration:** continues to be taxable, with PRs accountable. Rents collected from death until vesting are taxed at PR-level (20% basic rate, no personal allowance) per HMRC TSEM7000+.
  - **Disposal during administration:** uplifted base cost at death under TCGA 1992 s.62; PR's £3,000 annual exempt amount available; PR rate 24% (residential) / 20% (non-residential) per FA 2024 measures from 6 April 2024.
  - **Vesting:** when the property vests in beneficiaries, the beneficiaries acquire at the PR's deemed acquisition price (the probate value), which itself is the death market value under TCGA 1992 s.62.
- Primary (the dispute / contentious-probate routing — adds substantial time):
  - **Inheritance (Provision for Family and Dependants) Act 1975** — six-month claim window from grant. The IPFDA 1975 claim window means PRs commonly hold off final distribution until 6 months past grant + reasonable additional period for late-arriving claims. This is the single biggest "non-statutory" delay built into responsible PR practice.
  - **Caveats** under **NCPR 1987 r.44** — any person can lodge a caveat that prevents a grant being issued; valid for 6 months and renewable. Removes the grant from the timeline entirely until resolved.
- Primary (the property-context complications that lengthen probate):
  - **DST (Deeds of Variation) under IHTA 1984 s.142** — post-death variation within 2 years of death. Doesn't lengthen probate per se but commonly motivates PRs to delay final distribution while beneficiaries decide whether to vary.
  - **Section 36 LPA 1925 trustee retirement / appointment** where the will appoints trustees who decline or have predeceased.
  - **Section 27 Trustee Act 1925 statutory notices** to creditors and claimants (2 months minimum; gives PRs statutory protection against late-arriving claims; recommended best practice before final distribution).
- House position references: **§15.1** (NRB, RNRB, transferable allowances — the IHT400 boxes that determine excepted-estate routing); **§15.4** (BPR + APR £1m cap effective 6 April 2026 — affects IHT400 completion for estates with qualifying business or agricultural property dying on or after that date); **§22.2** (deed of variation s.142 IHTA 1984); **§22.17** (IPDI rental-property depth, Wave 7 lock — affects probate sequencing for IPDI-trust-tied rental property); **§15.5** (pensions in IHT from 6 April 2027 — affects estate valuation timing for deaths on or after 6 April 2027). No dedicated probate-timeline HP section; this seed treats the timeline as operational/HMCTS-evidence-led territory.

**Worked example: realistic timeline for a rental-property estate — the Holloway-family case**

Persona: Holloway-family. Mr Holloway (deceased 6 January 2026, aged 78). Estate: family home (£650k); BTL flat in Cambridge (£280k); rental flat held in single-SPV LtdCo (£195k portfolio value via shares); pension funds (£420k DC pot — pre-6-April-2027 so OUTSIDE IHT under current architecture); ISAs (£165k); savings (£28k); car (£18k). Will: residuary estate split 60% to surviving spouse Mrs Holloway, 40% to two adult children. Executors: Mrs Holloway and elder son. No contentious issues; spouse exemption applies to her 60%.

Realistic timeline:

| Stage | Activity | Duration | Cumulative |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0 | Death (6 January 2026), death certificate (within 5 days) | 1 week | 1 week |
| 1 | Asset / liability identification; valuation engagement; appointment of solicitor + accountant | 4 weeks | 5 weeks |
| 2 | Property valuations (death-date market value for IHT400 + TCGA s.62 uplift); BTL flat valuation; SPV share valuation; pension provider correspondence | 4-6 weeks | 9-11 weeks |
| 3 | IHT400 preparation + sign-off by executors; spouse exemption claim documented; calculation of IHT due (£325k NRB + £175k RNRB; spouse exempt portion deducted) | 3-4 weeks | 12-15 weeks |
| 4 | IHT400 submission to HMRC; first instalment of IHT paid (10-year instalment route under s.227 elected for property) | 1 week | 13-16 weeks |
| 5 | HMRC issues IHT421 (12-week target from IHT400 receipt; RUN verifies current target at write time) | 12 weeks | 25-28 weeks |
| 6 | Probate application submitted to HMCTS Probate Service via online portal | 1 week | 26-29 weeks |
| 7 | HMCTS Probate Service issues grant (16-week target) | 16 weeks | 42-45 weeks |
| 8 | Grant received; PRs collect assets; pay debts; manage rental property during administration; file SA900 for income arising | 8-12 weeks | 50-57 weeks |
| 9 | IPFDA 1975 6-month window observed (responsible PR practice) | 26 weeks from grant = ~12 weeks more after step 8 starts | aligned with step 8 |
| 10 | Final distribution of residuary; closing of estate accounts; final SA900 + clearance | 4-6 weeks | 54-63 weeks (12-15 months) |

**Realistic outcome: 12-15 months from death to final distribution** for this modal-case non-excepted estate without disputes. The "9-12 month" trade-press shorthand is the lower bound on this distribution; in practice ~12-15 months is closer to typical for property-holding estates with IHT to pay.

| Foreign assets requiring resealing or separate grant | +4-8 weeks |
| Multiple SPVs requiring final CT600 + dormancy filing | +4-12 weeks (parallel to main probate, sometimes serial) |
| Agricultural / business property requiring APR/BPR valuation contest | +12-26 weeks (HMRC SVU review + potential FTT) |
| Caveat lodged under NCPR 1987 r.44 | +6 months minimum; potentially years |
| IPFDA 1975 claim filed | +6-24 months litigation + settlement |
| Will dispute / contentious probate | +12-60 months |
| Deed of variation negotiated within 2-year window | +4-12 weeks (typically reduces tax exposure rather than delaying core probate timeline) |

**Operational discipline takeaway:** the property-holding modal case is 12-15 months, not the trade-press 9-12. Excepted estates (small + wholly-spouse-exempt + nil-rate) can complete in 8-10 months. Complicated estates (multiple SPVs, foreign assets, APR/BPR contest) commonly run 18-24 months. Contentious estates run 3-5 years or longer. The page should frame readers' expectations honestly.

## FAQ draft (10-12 entries, Stage 2 populated; RUN sub-agent polishes prose)

1. **How long does probate take in the UK for a property-holding estate?**
   For a modal-case non-excepted estate with one or more rental properties and no disputes, 12-15 months from death to final distribution is realistic. The 9-12 month trade-press shorthand applies to smaller / excepted estates (small + wholly-spouse-exempt + nil-rate). Complicated estates (multiple SPVs, foreign assets, APR/BPR contest) run 18-24 months. Contentious estates run 3-5 years or longer. The non-trivial element is the IHT side: HMRC's published target for IHT421 issuance is 12 weeks from IHT400 receipt, and HMCTS Probate Service's target for grant is 16 weeks from valid application receipt. Realistic slippage occurs in both — RUN verifies current published targets at write time.

2. **What is an "excepted estate" and does my parent's estate qualify?**
   Per SI 2021/1167, "excepted estates" are estates that meet specific value + composition criteria, removing the need to file a full IHT400 account. Three main routes: (a) low-value (net estate within IHT nil-rate band), (b) wholly-spouse-exempt (estate passes entirely to UK-resident spouse under IHTA 1984 s.18), (c) nil-rate (estate at or below the NRB after gifts in seven years pre-death). Most landlord estates fail one or more criteria — a £325k+ portfolio with anything outside the spouse share typically pushes outside excepted-estate status. RUN session uses the SI 2021/1167 current threshold figures (re-verify at write).

3. **What is IHT400 and when do I need to file it?**
   IHT400 is the full inheritance-tax account that personal representatives must complete and submit to HMRC before a grant of representation can be issued. IHT400 is required for non-excepted estates. The personal representatives sign and submit IHT400 with supporting forms (IHT403 gifts, IHT404 jointly-owned, IHT405 houses, IHT411 listed shares, etc) and any IHT due, then HMRC issues IHT421 (the probate summary / payment receipt). HMRC's published service target for IHT421 issuance is 12 weeks from IHT400 receipt — re-verify at write time as targets have slipped.

4. **What is the 10-year instalment option for property?**
   IHTA 1984 s.227 allows personal representatives to elect to pay IHT attributable to qualifying property (land, buildings, certain business assets) in 10 equal annual instalments. The first instalment is due 6 months from the end of the month of death; subsequent instalments at annual intervals thereafter. Interest accrues on unpaid instalments. This is operationally useful where the estate is property-heavy but cash-poor — PRs are not forced to sell the property before grant to pay the IHT bill upfront. Once a property is sold during administration, the outstanding instalment IHT becomes payable on the sold property's apportioned share.

5. **What happens to my parent's rental income during probate?**
   Rental income arising between death and final distribution is taxed at PR-level. Per CTA 2010 ss.34-35, personal representatives pay income tax at the 20% basic rate on income during administration, with NO personal allowance available (PRs are not individuals for personal allowance purposes per HMRC TSEM7320). The PRs file SA900 (trust and estate return) for the administration period. Once the property vests in a beneficiary, rental income from then on is taxed on the beneficiary at their personal marginal rates.

6. **What is the IPFDA 1975 six-month claim window and why does it delay distribution?**
   The Inheritance (Provision for Family and Dependants) Act 1975 gives a six-month window from the issuance of the grant in which certain family members and dependants can apply to the court for reasonable financial provision from the estate. Responsible PRs typically hold final distribution until 6 months past grant + a reasonable additional margin to ensure no late-arriving claims will catch them with assets already distributed. This is a non-statutory practice rather than a statutory delay, but it commonly adds 6-8 months to the modal timeline.

7. **What is the difference between probate, letters of administration, and a grant of representation?**
   "Grant of representation" is the generic term. "Grant of probate" is the specific grant where the deceased left a valid will and named executors. "Letters of administration" is the specific grant where there is no will (intestacy) or no surviving executor — the court appoints administrators instead. The administrative differences for the PRs are limited; the timeline is broadly the same; the substantive difference is in who has authority to act and how priorities are determined under the intestacy rules.

8. **Can I sell my parent's rental flat before probate is granted?**
   No, not as PR. The PRs need the grant to demonstrate authority to deal with the property. However, the property can be marketed before grant — for-sale boards, viewings, offers received, even contracts conditional on grant. Completion cannot happen pre-grant because Land Registry will not register the transfer without it. CGT-side: TCGA 1992 s.62 uplifts the base cost to death-market-value, so disposals during administration typically attract limited CGT exposure as the gain crystallised pre-death is washed out.

9. **What is the CGT position on a property I inherit?**
   Two-stage. (i) During administration: PRs hold the property at the uplifted death-market-value base cost per TCGA 1992 s.62. PR disposals attract PR CGT rates (24% residential / 20% non-residential from 6 April 2024) with a PR annual exempt amount of £3,000 in the tax year of death + the following 2 tax years. (ii) After vesting in you as beneficiary: you acquire at the same probate value (the death market value) as your base cost. Future disposals from then on attract your personal CGT rates, with your personal annual exempt amount.

10. **What is a "caveat" and how does it affect the timeline?**
    Under NCPR 1987 r.44, any person who wishes to prevent a grant being issued (because they dispute the will, the executor's appointment, or the deceased's mental capacity) can lodge a caveat at the Probate Registry. The caveat lasts 6 months and is renewable. While in force, no grant can be issued. To proceed, the executor must "warn" the caveator, who must then enter an "appearance" within 8 days. Failure to appear allows the caveat to be removed. Contested caveats commonly delay grant by 6-12 months minimum and can run for years.

11. **What is a deed of variation and when is it useful?**
    Per IHTA 1984 s.142, beneficiaries of an estate can vary their inheritance within 2 years of death; the variation reads back to the deceased for IHT (and for CGT per TCGA 1992 s.62(6) if elected). Common landlord uses: redirecting a BTL property from spouse to adult children (skipping a generation); redirecting to qualify for the Sch 1A 36% reduced rate via ≥10% charity legacy; equalising NRB/RNRB across spouses where first death wasted allowance. The variation must include the necessary statutory elections and be supported by all affected beneficiaries' consent. Doesn't fit the "delays probate" pattern but commonly motivates PRs to delay final distribution within the 2-year window.

12. **What can the family do to compress the probate timeline?**
    Five steps that meaningfully compress: (i) early appointment of a solicitor + accountant + estate valuer at the same time, not sequentially; (ii) IHT400 preparation starting immediately on death (rather than waiting for asset-valuation completion), with valuations slotting in as they arrive; (iii) clear executor-to-executor communication channels — many delays come from internal executor disagreements rather than HMRC or HMCTS; (iv) statutory s.27 Trustee Act 1925 notices placed early (allows the 2-month statutory window to run concurrent with the grant application rather than after); (v) honest disclosure on the IHT400 — late-arriving HMRC requisitions on incomplete returns add weeks per round. None of these can compress below ~8-9 months for a non-excepted estate because the HMRC + HMCTS service-level standards themselves set the floor.
