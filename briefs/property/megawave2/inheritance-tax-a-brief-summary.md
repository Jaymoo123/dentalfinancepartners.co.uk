# Inheritance Tax: A Brief Summary for UK Property Owners

## Statutory anchor

- Primary (the charging architecture): **Inheritance Tax Act 1984** as amended. Verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1984/51 on 2026-05-26 — the consolidating IHT statute since 1984, amended in every subsequent Finance Act of substance.
  - **IHTA 1984 s.1** — charge to IHT on chargeable transfers.
  - **IHTA 1984 s.2(1)** — definition of chargeable transfer: a transfer of value made by a person which is not an exempt transfer.
  - **IHTA 1984 s.3** — definition of transfer of value: a disposition of property which causes the value of the transferor's estate to be less than it would be but for the disposition; the amount = the difference.
  - **IHTA 1984 s.3A** — potentially exempt transfers (PETs) — outright lifetime gifts to individuals; no IHT at the time of gift; fully exempt if the donor survives 7 years; taxed at lifetime PET rate if the donor dies within 7 years (with taper relief for years 3-7).
  - **IHTA 1984 s.4** — chargeable transfer on death: deceased treated as making a transfer of value immediately before death of the value of the estate at that moment.
  - **IHTA 1984 s.5** — meaning of estate: aggregate of all property to which a person is beneficially entitled, with specific exclusions (excluded property under s.6, settled property in certain regimes under s.49, etc.).
- Primary (the rates and bands — the figures most readers actually want):
  - **NRB (Nil Rate Band):** £325,000 per individual. Frozen until 5 April 2031 per the additional one-year extension (HP §15.1 + Wave 6 F-3 verification 2026-05-23).
  - **RNRB (Residence Nil Rate Band):** £175,000 per individual. Frozen until 5 April 2031.
  - **RNRB taper:** withdrawn at £1 for every £2 of net estate above £2,000,000; fully extinguished at £2,350,000 (single) or £2,700,000 (with full transferable RNRB).
  - **RNRB qualifying conditions:** residence in deceased's estate; deceased used the residence at some point; passes to direct lineal descendant (children, step-children, adopted, foster, grandchildren — nieces/nephews/siblings do NOT qualify).
  - **Headline IHT rate:** 40% on the estate above the available nil-rate band(s). 36% reduced rate where at least 10% of the net estate passes to charity (IHTA 1984 Sch 1A). 20% lifetime rate on CLTs (chargeable lifetime transfers) above the NRB.
  - **Transferable NRB and RNRB:** unused portion transfers to surviving spouse / civil partner on first death; up to 100% of each. Claim made by personal representatives on IHT402 (NRB) / IHT436 (RNRB) within 2 years of second death.
- Primary (the spouse exemption — the most important single rule for most estates):
  - **IHTA 1984 s.18** — transfers between spouses or civil partners are exempt, regardless of size, with the result that no IHT is charged on a transfer to a surviving spouse.
  - **Post-FA-2025 residence-based qualification:** spouse exemption is unlimited where both spouses are UK long-term resident (the post-FA-2025 LTR test replacing the pre-FA-2025 domicile-based test from 6 April 2025); restricted to £325,000 where the receiving spouse is not UK long-term resident, unless the s.267ZA election is made for both to be treated as LTR for IHT (HP §15.6 + §22.X.6, locked Wave 6 / FA 2025 architecture).
- Primary (the lifetime gift framework — PETs and the 7-year rule):
  - **IHTA 1984 s.3A + s.7(4) + Sch 1** — PETs become fully exempt at 7-year survival; if donor dies within 7 years, tapered rates apply.
  - **Taper relief (s.7(4) IHTA 1984):** 3-4 years 80% of full rate (i.e. 32% effective); 4-5 years 60% (24%); 5-6 years 40% (16%); 6-7 years 20% (8%). Taper applies only where the gift exceeds the NRB; gifts within the NRB get no taper because there's no tax to taper.
  - **Annual exemption:** £3,000 per donor per tax year with one-year carry-back. Small gifts exemption: £250 per recipient per year (cannot combine with the £3,000).
- Primary (the gift-with-reservation-of-benefit anti-avoidance rule):
  - **FA 1986 s.102 + s.102A + s.102B** — GROB. If the donor reserves any benefit in the gifted property, the gift fails to leave the estate for IHT. Most amateur "gift the house to the kids" plans get caught here. HP §15.3 + §22.11 locked depth.
- Primary (the property-specific reliefs):
  - **APR (Agricultural Property Relief) — IHTA 1984 ss.115-124C:** 100% relief below the new £1m combined cap; 50% above. Effective 6 April 2026 (HP §15.4 locked).
  - **BPR (Business Property Relief) — IHTA 1984 ss.103-114:** 100% relief below the £1m combined cap; 50% above (plus the separate 50% AIM sub-tier outside the cap). Effective 6 April 2026 (HP §15.4).
  - **Pawson investment line:** standard BTL does NOT qualify for BPR (investment, not trading; *Pawson v HMRC* [2013] UKUT 050 (TCC)). Serviced-accommodation with substantial services may qualify on the Pawson-distinguishing facts (HP §22.1).
- Primary (the 6 April 2027 pensions-in-IHT change — the next live reform):
  - From 6 April 2027, unused defined-contribution pension funds and unused DB lump-sum death benefits will be brought into the deceased's estate for IHT. Personal representatives (not scheme administrators) will report and pay. Death-in-service benefits excluded from estate IHT. HP §15.5 locked.
- House position references: §15.1 (NRB, RNRB, freezes); §15.2 (PETs, CLTs, 7-year clock); §15.3 (GROB family-home); §15.4 (BPR/APR April 2026 cap); §15.5 (Pensions-in-IHT April 2027); §15.6 (non-resident IHT + LTR test); §22.X (long-term-resident architecture post-FA-2025); §22.1 (Pawson investment line). The page threads all of §15 + the key §22 cross-references at orientation depth.

**Mini-illustration: a £950k landlord estate — Holloway-family**

Persona: Holloway-family — Mr Holloway (widowed, age 74). Estate: family home £450k passes to two adult daughters; BTL flat £220k passes to elder daughter; ISAs + savings £180k; pension £100k DC pot (currently outside IHT; would be included from 6 April 2027 if death later than that). Wife predeceased 2024 with full NRB + RNRB unused (100% transferable).

Death in 2026:
- Available allowances: NRB £325k + transferable NRB £325k + RNRB £175k + transferable RNRB £175k = £1,000,000.
- Net estate (pension still outside) = £950,000.
- Net estate below combined allowances; no IHT due.

Same death in 2028 (after 6 April 2027 pensions-in-IHT):
- Net estate including pension: £1,050,000.
- Above combined allowances by £50,000.
- IHT due: 40% × £50,000 = £20,000.
- Position changed by the pensions-in-IHT reform; this is the most material 2027 reform impact for middle-income property owners.

## FAQ draft (10-12 entries, Stage 2 populated; RUN sub-agent polishes prose)

1. **What is inheritance tax?**
   Inheritance tax (IHT) is a tax on the value of a deceased person's estate. The legal framework is the Inheritance Tax Act 1984 as amended. The headline rate is 40% on the value of the estate above any available allowances (the £325k nil rate band per individual, plus £175k residence nil rate band where the home passes to direct lineal descendants). Lifetime gifts can also be caught — gifts made within 7 years of death are pulled back into the IHT calculation, with taper relief for years 3-7.

2. **What are the current nil rate bands?**
   The Nil Rate Band is £325,000 per individual; the Residence Nil Rate Band is £175,000 per individual where qualifying. Both are frozen until 5 April 2031 per HP §15.4 housekeeping lock (verified at Wave 6 close 2026-05-23 via gov.uk publication). Unused portions of both bands transfer to a surviving spouse on first death (100% transferable). A married couple where both die without using their allowances therefore have up to £1,000,000 combined available before IHT bites.

3. **How does the 7-year rule work for lifetime gifts?**
   Per IHTA 1984 s.3A, lifetime gifts to individuals are "potentially exempt transfers" (PETs). If the donor survives 7 years from the gift, the gift is fully exempt. If the donor dies within 7 years, the gift comes into the IHT calculation. Taper relief reduces the effective rate based on time elapsed: 3-4 years 80% of full rate (32% effective); 4-5 years 60% (24%); 5-6 years 40% (16%); 6-7 years 20% (8%). Taper only matters where the gift exceeds the NRB — gifts within the NRB get no taper because there's no tax to taper.

4. **What is the spouse exemption?**
   Under IHTA 1984 s.18, transfers between spouses and civil partners are exempt from IHT regardless of size, where both are UK long-term resident (the post-FA-2025 test from 6 April 2025 replacing the historic domicile-based test). Where the receiving spouse is NOT UK long-term resident, the exemption is limited by reference to the Sch 1 NRB upper limit (currently £325,000 cumulative lifetime). The non-LTR receiving spouse can elect under IHTA 1984 s.267ZC to be treated as UK long-term resident for IHT purposes — bringing their worldwide assets into UK IHT in exchange for unlimited spouse exemption.

5. **What is gift with reservation of benefit (GROB)?**
   Per FA 1986 s.102, if the donor reserves any benefit in the gifted property, the gift fails to leave the estate for IHT. The classic case: a parent "gives" the family home to children but continues to live there rent-free. Section 102 catches this — the property is still treated as the donor's at death, and IHT applies on the death value. Three statutory exit routes exist (full market rent, ceased occupation, narrow s.102B(4) shared-occupation carve-out). See our separate `gift-with-reservation-of-benefit` orientation page for the entry-tier deep.

6. **What is the residence nil rate band and when does it apply?**
   The RNRB gives an additional £175,000 nil rate band per individual where the deceased's home passes to direct lineal descendants (children, step-children, adopted, foster, grandchildren — nieces / nephews / siblings do not qualify). The RNRB tapers at £1 per £2 of net estate above £2,000,000, fully extinguished at £2,350,000 single (or £2,700,000 with full transferable RNRB). Most middle-income property owners receive the full RNRB; very-large-estate landlords lose it to the taper.

7. **What is changing on 6 April 2026?**
   The combined cap on agricultural property relief and business property relief at £1,000,000 per estate. Above the cap, relief drops from 100% to 50% (effective IHT rate 20% on the above-cap slice). Lifetime gifts made on or after 30 October 2024 are caught if the donor dies on or after 6 April 2026 within 7 years (anti-forestalling). Trusts settled on or after 30 October 2024 by the same settlor share a single £1m allowance. See `iht-april-2026-bpr-apr-cap-property-impact` for the reform mechanics deep.

8. **What is changing on 6 April 2027?**
   Unused defined-contribution pension funds and unused defined-benefit lump-sum death benefits will be brought into the deceased's estate for IHT. Personal representatives (not pension scheme administrators) will report and pay. Death-in-service benefits are excluded from the estate IHT charge. For middle-income landlords with a £100k-£500k DC pension pot, this is the most material 2027 reform impact — pension assets historically outside IHT will now sit alongside property and other assets in the estate calculation. See `pension-iht-april-2027-landlord-estate-planning` for the operational mechanics.

9. **Do buy-to-let portfolios qualify for business property relief?**
   No, not in the standard case. Per *Pawson v HMRC* [2013] UKUT 050 (TCC), passive rent collection from residential lettings is "mainly investment" and fails BPR. HMO landlords also fail. Property developers (genuine trading activity) can qualify on the trading element. Serviced-accommodation businesses may qualify where active services beyond passive letting are substantial (managed kitchen, daily cleaning, breakfast, concierge); the bar is high. Most amateur "my BTL portfolio is a business" claims fail the *Pawson* line.

10. **What about non-residents holding UK property?**
    UK-situs property (including UK real estate) is always within UK IHT regardless of the owner's residence or LTR status. The post-FA-2025 architecture (from 6 April 2025) means non-LTR individuals are within UK IHT only on UK-situs property; LTR individuals are within UK IHT on worldwide assets. Schedule A1 IHTA 1984 continues to bring UK residential property held via offshore structures into UK IHT (enveloped-residential look-through). See `iht-non-resident-uk-property-april-2025-residence-test` for the operational mechanics.

11. **What is the 36% reduced rate?**
    Per IHTA 1984 Sch 1A, where at least 10% of the "components of the estate" pass to qualifying charity, the IHT rate on the chargeable estate drops from 40% to 36%. For portfolios above approximately £2m, the 36% rate often makes the charity gift "self-funding" — the maths flips at the break-point where 36% on (estate minus charity) equals 40% on (estate). See `iht-charitable-legacy-property-portfolio-36-percent-reduced-rate` for the worked-example deep.
