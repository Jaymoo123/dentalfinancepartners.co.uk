# Inheritance Tax: A Brief Summary for UK Property Owners

## Statutory anchor

- Primary (the charging architecture): **Inheritance Tax Act 1984** as amended. Verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1984/51 on 2026-05-26 — the consolidating IHT statute since 1984, amended in every subsequent Finance Act of substance.
  - **IHTA 1984 s.1** — charge to IHT on chargeable transfers.
  - **IHTA 1984 s.2(1)** — definition of chargeable transfer: a transfer of value made by a person which is not an exempt transfer.
  - **IHTA 1984 s.3** — definition of transfer of value: a disposition of property which causes the value of the transferor's estate to be less than it would be but for the disposition; the amount = the difference.
  - **IHTA 1984 s.3A** — potentially exempt transfers (PETs) — outright lifetime gifts to individuals; no IHT at the time of gift; fully exempt if the donor survives 7 years; taxed at lifetime PET rate if the donor dies within 7 years (with taper relief for years 3-7).
  - **IHTA 1984 s.4** — chargeable transfer on death: deceased treated as making a transfer of value immediately before death of the value of the estate at that moment.
  - **IHTA 1984 s.5** — meaning of estate: aggregate of all property to which a person is beneficially entitled, with specific exclusions (excluded property under s.6, settled property in certain regimes under s.49, etc.).
- Primary (the rates and bands — the figures most readers actually want):
  - **NRB (Nil Rate Band):** £325,000 per individual. Frozen until 5 April 2031 per the additional one-year extension (HP §15.1 + Wave 6 F-3 verification 2026-05-23).
  - **RNRB (Residence Nil Rate Band):** £175,000 per individual. Frozen until 5 April 2031.
  - **RNRB taper:** withdrawn at £1 for every £2 of net estate above £2,000,000; fully extinguished at £2,350,000 (single) or £2,700,000 (with full transferable RNRB).
  - **RNRB qualifying conditions:** residence in deceased's estate; deceased used the residence at some point; passes to direct lineal descendant (children, step-children, adopted, foster, grandchildren — nieces/nephews/siblings do NOT qualify).
  - **Headline IHT rate:** 40% on the estate above the available nil-rate band(s). 36% reduced rate where at least 10% of the net estate passes to charity (IHTA 1984 Sch 1A). 20% lifetime rate on CLTs (chargeable lifetime transfers) above the NRB.
  - **Transferable NRB and RNRB:** unused portion transfers to surviving spouse / civil partner on first death; up to 100% of each. Claim made by personal representatives on IHT402 (NRB) / IHT436 (RNRB) within 2 years of second death.
- Primary (the spouse exemption — the most important single rule for most estates):
  - **IHTA 1984 s.18** — transfers between spouses or civil partners are exempt, regardless of size, with the result that no IHT is charged on a transfer to a surviving spouse.
  - **Post-FA-2025 residence-based qualification:** spouse exemption is unlimited where both spouses are UK long-term resident (the post-FA-2025 LTR test replacing the pre-FA-2025 domicile-based test from 6 April 2025); restricted to £325,000 where the receiving spouse is not UK long-term resident, unless the s.267ZA election is made for both to be treated as LTR for IHT (HP §15.6 + §22.X.6, locked Wave 6 / FA 2025 architecture).
- Primary (the lifetime gift framework — PETs and the 7-year rule):
  - **IHTA 1984 s.3A + s.7(4) + Sch 1** — PETs become fully exempt at 7-year survival; if donor dies within 7 years, tapered rates apply.
  - **Taper relief (s.7(4) IHTA 1984):** 3-4 years 80% of full rate (i.e. 32% effective); 4-5 years 60% (24%); 5-6 years 40% (16%); 6-7 years 20% (8%). Taper applies only where the gift exceeds the NRB; gifts within the NRB get no taper because there's no tax to taper.
  - **Annual exemption:** £3,000 per donor per tax year with one-year carry-back. Small gifts exemption: £250 per recipient per year (cannot combine with the £3,000).
- Primary (the gift-with-reservation-of-benefit anti-avoidance rule):
  - **FA 1986 s.102 + s.102A + s.102B** — GROB. If the donor reserves any benefit in the gifted property, the gift fails to leave the estate for IHT. Most amateur "gift the house to the kids" plans get caught here. HP §15.3 + §22.11 locked depth.
- Primary (the property-specific reliefs):
  - **APR (Agricultural Property Relief) — IHTA 1984 ss.115-124C:** 100% relief below the new £1m combined cap; 50% above. Effective 6 April 2026 (HP §15.4 locked).
  - **BPR (Business Property Relief) — IHTA 1984 ss.103-114:** 100% relief below the £1m combined cap; 50% above (plus the separate 50% AIM sub-tier outside the cap). Effective 6 April 2026 (HP §15.4).
  - **Pawson investment line:** standard BTL does NOT qualify for BPR (investment, not trading; *Pawson v HMRC* [2013] UKUT 050 (TCC)). Serviced-accommodation with substantial services may qualify on the Pawson-distinguishing facts (HP §22.1).
- Primary (the 6 April 2027 pensions-in-IHT change — the next live reform):
  - From 6 April 2027, unused defined-contribution pension funds and unused DB lump-sum death benefits will be brought into the deceased's estate for IHT. Personal representatives (not scheme administrators) will report and pay. Death-in-service benefits excluded from estate IHT. HP §15.5 locked.
- House position references: §15.1 (NRB, RNRB, freezes); §15.2 (PETs, CLTs, 7-year clock); §15.3 (GROB family-home); §15.4 (BPR/APR April 2026 cap); §15.5 (Pensions-in-IHT April 2027); §15.6 (non-resident IHT + LTR test); §22.X (long-term-resident architecture post-FA-2025); §22.1 (Pawson investment line). The page threads all of §15 + the key §22 cross-references at orientation depth.
