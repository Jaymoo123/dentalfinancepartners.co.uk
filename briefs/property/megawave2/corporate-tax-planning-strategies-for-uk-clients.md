# Corporation Tax Planning Strategies for UK Property Companies: A Lever Map for Landlord LtdCos (2026/27)

## Worked-example data (RUN session uses these as canvas)

### Example 1 — Single-SPV at the marginal-relief band

- **Mawell Estates 1 Ltd** (single SPV, no associated companies) earns augmented profits £150,000 in 2026/27.
- **Lever 1 outcome:** profits land in the £50k-£250k band. Marginal relief applies. Formula: F = (U − A) × (N / A) × (3/200) where U = £250,000 (upper limit), A = £150,000 (augmented profits), N = taxable total profits = £150,000 (assume no exempt distributions). F = £100,000 × 1 × 3/200 = £1,500 marginal relief.
- CT at main rate: £150,000 × 25% = £37,500. Less marginal relief £1,500 = **£36,000 CT**. Effective rate 24.0% on profits in the band.
- **Mid-band effective rate check:** at exact mid-band (£150,000), effective rate is 24%. The 26.5% effective rate appears only at the top of the band approach. RUN session shows worked numbers for £75,000 (effective ~21.8%), £150,000 (24%), £225,000 (~24.4%) to expose the curve.

### Example 2 — Multi-SPV associated-companies impact (lever 2)

- **Mawell Estates 1 Ltd** in Example 1 is now part of a 5-SPV portfolio with **4 associated companies** (Mawell Estates 2, 3, 4, 5 Ltd) plus the original. Total N + 1 = 5.
- **Lever 2 outcome:** limits divide by 5. New lower limit = £10,000; new upper limit = £50,000.
- Same £150,000 profits now FAR exceed the £50,000 new upper limit → company pays MAIN RATE 25% on the full £150,000 = **£37,500 CT**. Marginal relief disappears entirely.
- **Comparative cost:** going from single-SPV to 5-SPV cost this entity £1,500 in lost marginal relief. Across all 5 SPVs in the group earning at this profit level, the aggregate cost of fragmentation is £7,500/year vs holding the same activity in one entity. RUN session frames as decision: fragmentation has SDLT, liability isolation, and operational benefits that may outweigh £7,500/yr; the lever-2 task is making the trade explicit, not always avoiding fragmentation.

### Example 3 — CIHC denial (lever 1, family-tenant trap)

- **Singh Property Ltd** owns 3 BTL flats. 2 are let to unconnected tenants at market rent; 1 is let to the founder's adult daughter at £200/month (market rent £900/month).
- **CIHC analysis under CTA 2010 s.18N:** the connected-tenant let at sub-market rent risks failing the s.18N(2)(b) qualifying-purpose carve-out + s.18N(3) connected-tenant exclusion (§21.A drift catch). HMRC's expansive reading would catch any below-market connected let.
- **Consequence if classified as CIHC:** SPR + marginal relief denied. Singh Property Ltd pays 25% main rate on ALL profit (not just on the connected-let portion). At £80,000 augmented profits the cost is £80,000 × (25% − 19%) = £4,800/year vs the non-CIHC position.
- **Remediation:** charge the daughter market rent £900/month. The arm's-length terms restore the qualifying-purpose carve-out (let commercially under s.18N(3) interpretation). RUN session frames this as a high-ROI lever 1 action: a £200 → £900 rent change generates £4,800 CT savings.

### Example 4 — Group relief between SPVs (lever 4)

- **Mawell Group:** parent Mawell Holdings Ltd owns 100% of Mawell Estates 1 Ltd (profitable, £100k profit) and 100% of Mawell Estates 2 Ltd (loss-making, £40k loss from refurb-cost write-down).
- **Group relief surrender:** Mawell Estates 2 surrenders the £40k loss to Mawell Estates 1 under CTA 2010 Part 5 group relief (75% group test satisfied at 100% direct ownership). Mawell Estates 1's profit drops to £60k.
- **CT impact:** £60k profit at SPR 19% (now within single-company lower limit assuming no associated-companies divisor) = £11,400 CT vs without group relief: £100k profit triggers marginal-relief band, ~£23,500 CT. Net group saving ~£12,100/year.
- **Note:** the associated-companies divisor (lever 2) cuts the other way; with 2 associated companies, limits divide by 3, and Mawell Estates 1's profit £60k now exceeds the £83k upper limit divided by 3 (£83k upper limit / 3 = £27,667). RUN session shows full-grid numbers; the levers interact non-linearly.

### Example 5 — Intra-group no-gain-no-loss restructuring (lever 5 + s.179 trap)

- **Mawell Holdings Ltd** wants to sell Mawell Estates 1 Ltd (which owns 3 properties) to an external buyer. To clean the SPV for sale, the group transfers 1 property out of Mawell Estates 1 to Mawell Estates 2 at no-gain-no-loss under TCGA 1992 s.171 (intra-75%-group transfer).
- **s.179 degrouping trap:** if Mawell Estates 1 is then sold within 6 years of the s.171 transfer, the property transferred OUT triggers a degrouping charge at Mawell Estates 1 level: the deemed gain is the difference between the original cost and the s.171 transfer value, taxed at CT-on-chargeable-gains rates.
- **Lever 5 discipline:** plan the restructure timeline. If sale of Mawell Estates 1 is contemplated within 6 years, the s.171 + s.179 combo may be the wrong route. Alternative: SSE under TCGA Sch 7AC if the disposal is of the SUBSTANTIAL SHAREHOLDING in Mawell Estates 1 by Mawell Holdings (10%+ shareholding for 12+ months; trading-company test for the target).

## FAQ expansion (RUN session polishes prose; 10-12 FAQs target)

1. **Q: What are the main Corporation Tax planning levers available to a UK property limited company in 2026/27?**
   A: Seven levers organise the landscape: (1) CIHC avoidance — keeping the company on the small-profits / marginal-relief track by ensuring lets are on arms-length commercial terms; (2) associated-companies divisor management — deciding whether fragmenting into multiple SPVs is worth the SPR-band shrinkage; (3) extraction mix optimisation — salary / dividend / director's loan repayment / pension contributions; (4) group relief between SPVs under common ownership; (5) intra-group asset transfers at no-gain-no-loss (TCGA s.171) for restructuring before disposal; (6) capital allowance stacking on commercial property (SBA + AIA + FYA); (7) Corporate Interest Restriction planning where group-wide net interest exceeds £2m.

2. **Q: How does the FA 2021 three-rate CT framework shape my planning landscape?**
   A: Since 1 April 2023 the rate framework is: small profits rate 19% on augmented profits up to £50,000; main rate 25% on augmented profits over £250,000; marginal relief between with a standard fraction of 3/200 producing an effective marginal rate of about 26.5% just below the upper limit. The £50,000 and £250,000 limits are divided by 1 + N (where N is the number of associated companies in the accounting period under CTA 2010 s.18E). Multi-SPV portfolio operators must run the associated-companies divisor explicitly when planning new incorporations or considering whether to consolidate existing SPVs.

3. **Q: When is a property SPV at risk of CIHC classification, and what does that cost?**
   A: A close investment-holding company under CTA 2010 s.18N is denied access to the small profits rate and marginal relief — it pays 25% main rate regardless of profit level. The s.18N(2)(b) carve-out excludes companies whose business consists wholly or mainly of letting land to unconnected persons on commercial terms. The s.18N(3) connected-tenant exclusion is read expansively by HMRC: any below-market let to a connected person (family member, associated company, settlor of a related trust) risks pulling the WHOLE company into CIHC, not just the connected-let portion. Cost of CIHC classification: typically (25% − 19%) × augmented profits = 6 percentage points more CT than the SPR position, or higher where marginal relief would have given an even better effective rate. Remediation: ensure all lets are at arms-length commercial rent with proper tenancy documentation.

4. **Q: How do I decide the right extraction mix for my property SPV in 2026/27?**
   A: The optimal mix depends on the shareholder-director's personal tax position, the SPV's CT band, the credit balance on the director's loan account, and whether pension contributions are within the annual allowance. Default sequencing: (a) salary up to the Personal Allowance + Primary Threshold to use the income-tax allowance; (b) employer pension contributions (CT-deductible; not subject to NIC); (c) tax-free dividends within the £500 dividend allowance; (d) DLA repayments where a credit balance exists (no further tax, but balance is finite); (e) further dividends across the basic and higher rate bands depending on personal allowance utilisation. Our specialist page `salary-vs-dividends-property-spv-2026-27-marginal-rate-analysis` walks the marginal-rate maths in depth.

5. **Q: When does group relief make sense between my SPVs?**
   A: Group relief under CTA 2010 Part 5 lets a 75%-group company surrender its current-year trading or property losses to a fellow 75%-group company. The mechanic is most valuable when one SPV is loss-making (e.g., refurb-cost write-down, vacancy losses) and another is profitable. Key conditions: 75% direct or indirect ownership at the share + dividend + winding-up-rights levels under the Schedule 18 equity-holder overlay; both companies must be in CT charge; surrender must be claimed within the time limits (usually 2 years from the surrendering company's accounting period end). Surrender is restricted to TRADING and property-business losses; capital losses are not group-relievable (use TCGA s.171 transfers + offset within the gaining company instead). Single-property-LLP-investing-as-CT-payer is not typically eligible.

6. **Q: What is the s.179 degrouping charge trap I need to avoid when restructuring?**
   A: TCGA 1992 s.171 lets you transfer an asset between members of a 75% capital-gains group at no gain / no loss. The trap: if the transferee company leaves the group within 6 years of the transfer (typically because it is sold to an external buyer), s.179 deems a degrouping charge — the original transferor's gain is reactivated and taxed at CT rates. The economic effect can be substantial because the gain may have built up over many years before the s.171 transfer. Planning: if you anticipate a within-6-year disposal of one SPV, do NOT use s.171 to clean it before sale. Consider instead SSE under Sch 7AC (if the disposal is of the holding company's substantial shareholding in a trading subsidiary) or accept a chargeable disposal and plan the tax provision.

7. **Q: When does the Corporate Interest Restriction start to bite for portfolio operators?**
   A: The CIR at TIOPA 2010 Part 10 restricts group-wide net interest expense to 30% of UK tax-adjusted EBITDA (the Fixed Ratio Method), with a de minimis £2m group-wide net interest threshold. Below £2m, the CIR does not bite. Property-portfolio operators with multiple SPVs and substantial mortgage finance can cross the £2m threshold faster than they realise (e.g., a portfolio with £40m of property at average 5% interest produces £2m of interest annually — already at the threshold). Cross-link to a specialist page is appropriate if you cross the threshold; for most landlord-LtdCo operators below it, CIR is a non-issue. The Group Ratio Method can give higher headroom for groups with high external interest cost.

8. **Q: How do the levers interact — when do they conflict?**
   A: The most common conflict is between fragmentation (multi-SPV for liability isolation, individual-property SDLT planning, lender preference) and associated-companies divisor (which shrinks the marginal-relief band across ALL associated SPVs). Decision framework: tally the SDLT + liability + lender benefits per fragmentation step, compare against the CT cost (typically £1,500-£3,000/year per associated company added at mid-band profit levels). For most operators below £250k group profit, the CT cost is modest; for operators near the upper marginal-relief limit, fragmentation can be expensive enough to reconsider. The second conflict is between extraction-route optimisation and pension contributions: heavy pension contributions reduce current-year extractable cash but produce long-run-tax-relief that beats dividend extraction at higher rate bands.

11. **Q: What audit-trail discipline do I need to defend each lever if HMRC enquires?**
    A: Board minutes recording the commercial rationale for each lever decision; written valuations supporting market rent for any connected-party tenancies (lever 1 CIHC defence); written transfer-pricing analysis for any intra-group transactions above the £2m group de minimis (lever 7 + TIOPA Part 4); SDLT clearance + s.171 elections for intra-group asset transfers (lever 5); pension contribution employer-resolutions and trustee acknowledgments for the extraction-mix (lever 3). HMRC's enquiry window for CT is 12 months from the filing date (extended to 4 years for negligent under-declaration, 6 years for careless, 20 years for deliberate). Maintain the audit trail in real time, not retrospectively under enquiry.

12. **Q: I have just one BTL property SPV — which of these levers actually apply to me?**
    A: For a single-SPV operator below £50k profit, only levers 1 (CIHC avoidance), 3 (extraction mix), and 6 (capital allowances if commercial) are typically live. Levers 2, 4, 5, 7 require a multi-company group. Lever 1 still matters because a single below-market connected let can flip the WHOLE company into CIHC regardless of group size. Single-SPV operators benefit most from getting the extraction mix right (lever 3) and ensuring tenant-arrangement documentation supports the SPR position (lever 1).
