# The Companies House Identity Verification Form: What You Actually Submit, What Data Companies House Captures, and How the Personal Code Flows Into Your Filings

## Statutory anchor
- Primary (the ECCTA ID-verification architecture): Economic Crime and Corporate Transparency Act 2023 c. 56 ("ECCTA 2023"). Verified verbatim at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2023/56/contents on 2026-05-27 (Stage 2 sub-agent re-verifies at write time per §16.35). Operative anchors per §11.A Wave 9 mini-lock + F-14 Wave 9 RUN catch corrections 2026-05-25:
  - **ECCTA 2023 s.64** — "Identity verification of persons with significant control" (the PSC-side anchor that drives the data-fields question for PSC reads of the form).
  - **ECCTA 2023 s.65** — "Procedure etc for verifying identity" — **the operative how-to provision; this is the section the form-mechanic page anchors against**. Section 65 sets out (or authorises by regulations) what the verification process must do, what is captured, and what counts as successful verification.
  - **ECCTA 2023 s.66** — "Authorisation of corporate service providers" — the ACSP route the form-mechanic page must walk in parallel with the GOV.UK One Login route.
  - **ECCTA 2023 s.68** — "Allocation of unique identifiers" — the operative anchor for the personal code (the unique identifier issued to each verified natural person). The code is permanent + per-natural-person.
  - **ECCTA 2023 s.69** — "Identity verification: material unavailable for public inspection" — **the operative anchor for the public-register-visibility question that the form-mechanic page must answer accurately** (residential address + identifying document number + biometric data NOT publicly visible; verified-status flag IS publicly visible).
  - Director-side ID verification operative provisions live in CA 2006 amendments inserted by ECCTA earlier in Part 1 (ss.2-7 + ss.40-45 area) plus the new CA 2006 Part 11A — Stage 2 sub-agent verifies precise inserted-section numbers at write time per §16.35.
- Primary (the commencement chain — when the form must be submitted): per §11.A Wave 9 hardening 2026-05-25:
  - **8 April 2025:** verification opens VOLUNTARILY for directors + PSCs (form is live but not yet operatively required).
  - **18 November 2025:** verification becomes a LEGAL REQUIREMENT for newly appointed directors and PSCs (form must be submitted BEFORE the appointment can be filed at Companies House).
  - **18 November 2025 → ~November 2026 (12-month transition window):** EXISTING directors and PSCs submit the form by their next confirmation statement filed within / after the transition window. Per-company timing anchored to the confirmation statement cycle.
  - **No earlier than November 2026:** later phases bring presenter-side verification + corporate-officer-scope filers + the corporate-director regime into scope (Stage 2 sub-agent verifies current commencement state at write per §11.A F-12 against `https://changestoukcompanylaw.campaign.gov.uk/`).
- Primary (the substantive filings where the personal code is then quoted): Companies Act 2006 anchors —
  - **CA 2006 s.853A** — confirmation statement (annual obligation; the personal code is supplied here for every in-scope director + PSC linked to the company).
  - **CA 2006 s.167** — particulars of directors to be registered (AP01 director appointment).
  - CA 2006 PSC regime (CA 2006 ss.790A-790ZG + Sch 1A — operative PSC notification + change-of-particulars filings include the personal code).
  - **CA 2006 ss.7-12** — incorporation (IN01 includes personal code where founder-directors / first PSCs are in scope at the point of incorporation post-18-November-2025).
- Primary (the parallel UK MLR-2017 supervision framework — for ACSPs): Money Laundering, Terrorist Financing and Transfer of Funds (Information on the Payer) Regulations 2017 (SI 2017/692). ACSPs must be supervised under MLR 2017 by an AML supervisor (HMRC, FCA, professional body such as ICAEW, ACCA, ICAS, CIOT, Law Society). The MLR 2017 supervision is the back-end of the ACSP route's "form" — the ACSP applies its existing AML KYC standards then submits the resulting verification confirmation to Companies House under ECCTA s.66.
- Supporting (the GOV.UK One Login service architecture): GOV.UK One Login is a cross-government identity-and-authentication service operated by the Government Digital Service. The Companies House identity verification flow is delivered through GOV.UK One Login as one of its identity-proofing services. Service-level documentation lives at `https://www.sign-in.service.gov.uk/` and at `https://signin.account.gov.uk/`. Stage 2 sub-agent verifies current service architecture + screen flow at write per §16.35 against gov.uk One Login service status + Companies House published guidance.
- House position reference: **§11.A** Wave 9 mini-lock (the operative ECCTA architecture + commencement chain + ACSP framework + ECCTA s.69 publication restriction + s.68 personal code mechanic). **§13** main do-not-write applies. **§11.A do-not-write list** applies — "Each SPV needs separate ID verification" (false), "Registered email is published on the public register" (false), "ID verification is voluntary" (false now), "Schedule 12 of ECCTA 2023 amends ECTEA 2022" (false). **§16.27** rate-by-reference applies — any specified ACSP fee is verified at write time, not stated as fixed.

## Key questions this page must answer

1. **Why is there no "identity verification form" to download?** (Operational answer: ECCTA 2023 + the implementing Commencement Regulations + Companies House operational implementation deliver verification through an interactive electronic flow, not a numbered paper-or-PDF form. The historical Companies House numbered-form pattern (AP01 / CS01 / PSC01 / IN01 / MR01) was a snapshot-of-data submission against a static template. Identity verification is by contrast a real-time process: capture a current photograph, match it against a current identity document, run liveness checks, anchor against a current address-of-residence, deliver a unique identifier. None of that fits the numbered-paper-form pattern. The two routes (GOV.UK One Login + ACSP) are the operational delivery mechanisms; the personal code allocated under ECCTA s.68 is the artefact that THEN gets quoted on the numbered forms (CS01, AP01, PSC01, IN01) when the substantive filings happen.)
2. **The GOV.UK One Login route — screen-by-screen walkthrough?** (Step-by-step: (i) **Account creation or sign-in.** GOV.UK One Login at `https://signin.account.gov.uk/`. If the reader already has a One Login account (used for personal tax, Self Assessment, MOT history, Universal Credit, NHS continuous care, etc.) the same login works. If not, create with email address + 6-character security code emailed for confirmation + password + answer to security question. (ii) **Start the Companies House identity verification step.** Either initiated directly from the Companies House service ("Verify your identity for Companies House") or from a One Login account dashboard option. The reader confirms intent to verify identity for Companies House purposes. (iii) **Choose identity document.** Options: UK passport (PRIMARY recommended route), non-UK passport in date (acceptable per Companies House published acceptance list), UK photo driving licence (full or provisional), UK biometric residence permit, UK biometric residence card, UK Frontier Worker permit. Stage 2 sub-agent verifies current acceptance list at write per §16.35. (iv) **Document photograph.** Either upload a photo of the document or use the GOV.UK One Login app on a smartphone (iOS / Android) to read the document directly. The app reads the biometric chip on a passport or the photograph zone on a driving licence. The web route accepts uploaded scans where the app is not used. (v) **Face-match photograph.** Live selfie taken on smartphone camera or webcam. Service runs liveness checks (turn head, blink, look up, etc.) and matches the live face against the document photograph. (vi) **Address-of-residence + personal-details anchor.** Confirmation of current residential address + date of birth + full legal name (any former names also captured if relevant for cross-checking against the Companies House register where a prior directorship was registered under a former name). (vii) **Security questions.** Cross-check against electoral-register / credit-history sources to confirm the natural-person record. (viii) **Confirmation + submission.** Reader confirms the data + submits. (ix) **Personal code allocation.** Companies House allocates a unique alphanumeric personal code under ECCTA s.68 + sends to the reader's One Login account messages + by email. Code is permanent + covers all UK directorships + PSC interests the reader will ever hold.)
3. **The Authorised Corporate Service Provider (ACSP) route — what does the accountant submit on the reader's behalf?** (Step-by-step: (i) **ACSP onboarding.** The reader engages an AML-supervised UK firm that has registered as an ACSP at Companies House under ECCTA s.66. ACSP register searchable at Companies House. Typical ACSPs: the reader's existing accountant or company-secretarial agent (where they are AML-supervised under MLR 2017 — most are by default of taking on the client). (ii) **AML KYC.** The ACSP collects identity documents under its MLR 2017 supervision: photo ID (passport, driving licence) + address-of-residence proof (utility bill, bank statement, council tax bill — typically dated within last 3 months) + signed declaration. If the ACSP already holds AML-verified ID for the client (which it typically does as a baseline of client onboarding), the marginal effort is small. (iii) **KYC platform verification.** Most ACSPs use a third-party KYC platform (Onfido, Veriff, Yoti, Jumio, Persona, etc.) to run the document-photograph + face-match + liveness checks digitally. Some ACSPs run the verification in-person where the client visits the firm's office. (iv) **ACSP-side electronic submission to Companies House.** Once the ACSP has verified identity, it submits an electronic confirmation to Companies House under its ACSP credentials, confirming: the natural-person identity verified + the documents used + the verification timestamp + the ACSP's responsibility for the verification under ECCTA s.66 + MLR 2017 supervision. Companies House does NOT re-verify; it accepts the ACSP-side confirmation as the verification event. (v) **Personal code allocation.** Companies House allocates the unique alphanumeric personal code under ECCTA s.68 + delivers to the ACSP + to the natural person (typically by email + by post depending on the ACSP's process). (vi) **ACSP fees.** Typically £50-£200 one-off for existing clients of an accountant who already holds AML-verified ID; higher (£150-£300+) for new clients where the ACSP is verifying from scratch. Rate-by-reference per §16.27 — verify at write against current 2026-vintage ACSP rate cards.)
4. **What data does Companies House capture (and where does each field go)?** (Data captured during verification — common to both routes: (i) full legal name + any former names; (ii) date of birth; (iii) nationality; (iv) country of residence; (v) current residential address (full UK or non-UK address); (vi) identifying document type + number + expiry date (passport / driving licence / etc.); (vii) biometric photograph (captured for face-match purpose; retained per Companies House retention policy); (viii) signed declaration that information provided is accurate; (ix) verification timestamp + route (One Login vs ACSP) + (for ACSP route) ACSP identifier + AML supervisor identifier. Each field has a specific retention purpose: full legal name + DOB + nationality + verified-status flag are linked to the natural-person record at Companies House and surface on any director / PSC entry the reader subsequently holds. Residential address + identifying-document number + biometric photograph are RETAINED but NOT published — per ECCTA s.69 (publication restriction). Stage 2 sub-agent verifies precise retention period at write per §16.35 against Companies House published privacy notice + ICO-registered data processing standards.)
5. **What becomes publicly visible vs what does NOT?** (Per ECCTA s.69 publication restriction: PUBLICLY VISIBLE on the Companies House register for each natural-person who is a director or PSC of a UK company — verified-status flag (yes / no) + full legal name + date of birth (month + year ONLY, not day; this protects identity-theft exposure on a public register) + nationality + country of residence + service address (registered office of the company OR separately designated service address for the natural person) + occupation (director-side only). NOT PUBLICLY VISIBLE per ECCTA s.69 — residential address (where it differs from service address); identifying document number; biometric photograph; signed-declaration record; full date of birth (day suppressed); any data captured during identity-proofing that is not part of the existing director / PSC public-register profile. The publication restriction is the OPERATIVE protection that allows directors + PSCs to comply with the verification regime without exposing identity-theft-attractive data to the public.)
6. **How is the personal code delivered + recorded?** (Delivery: (i) For GOV.UK One Login route — code appears in the One Login account messages + emailed to the verified email address + visible in the One Login dashboard. (ii) For ACSP route — code delivered by the ACSP to the natural person, typically by email + in writing as part of the verification-complete documentation. Format: one alphanumeric code per natural person (Stage 2 sub-agent verifies current code format at write — typically 8-12 characters, alphanumeric). Permanence: the code is PERMANENT — does not expire, does not require annual renewal, does not change if the natural person moves house or changes name (the underlying verification data is updated separately; the code identifier persists). Recording: practical recommendation — the reader records the code in (a) a personal secure record (password manager, safe), (b) their accountant or company-secretarial agent's client record, (c) immediately on their next Companies House filing where required. If the code is lost, retrieval is via GOV.UK One Login account or via the ACSP (where ACSP route was used); in both cases the reader does NOT re-verify identity to retrieve the code — retrieval is via account access.)
7. **How does the personal code then flow into Companies House filings?** (Substantive filings where the code is quoted post-verification: (i) **CS01 confirmation statement** (annual) — every in-scope director + PSC linked to the company must have a personal code recorded as part of the confirmation statement filing. CA 2006 s.853A operative provision. From 4 March 2024 the confirmation statement also requires the new lawful-purposes statement + the registered email confirmation (other ECCTA reforms). (ii) **AP01 director appointment** — new director appointments post-18-November-2025 require the personal code on the appointment filing. CA 2006 s.167 operative provision. (iii) **PSC01 PSC notification** (and PSC02 / PSC03 / PSC04 / PSC05 / PSC06 / PSC07 / PSC08 / PSC09 — the family of PSC filings for first-notification, change-of-particulars, ceasing-to-be-PSC, etc.) — every new PSC notification post-18-November-2025 requires the personal code. CA 2006 ss.790A-790ZG + Sch 1A operative provisions. (iv) **IN01 incorporation** — incorporation of a new UK company with founder-directors and / or first PSCs post-18-November-2025 requires personal codes for each in-scope founder-director + first-PSC. CA 2006 ss.7-12 operative provisions. The personal code is therefore the OPERATIVE GATE: it does not replace the existing numbered-form architecture; it INTEGRATES into it as a required data-field on the existing forms. Practical implication: the existing accountant or company-secretarial agent who files CS01 / AP01 / PSC01 / IN01 picks up the personal code from the verified natural person + populates the new code-field on the existing forms.)
8. **Common submission errors + how to correct them?** (Operational errors typically reported: (i) **Document-photograph quality failure.** Document image too blurry, glare obscuring data zone, or partial-document upload. Resolution: re-upload with better lighting + flat surface; use One Login app on smartphone (more reliable than web upload). (ii) **Face-match failure.** Liveness check fails (poor lighting; reader wearing glasses or hat; reader's face partially obscured); face-photograph does not match document photograph (typically due to ageing of the document photograph — a passport from 10 years ago may not match a current selfie convincingly). Resolution: re-attempt with better conditions; if persistent failure, escalate via GOV.UK One Login support OR switch to ACSP route. (iii) **Security-questions failure.** Cross-check against electoral-register / credit-history sources returns no match (typically affects readers who have recently moved, have minimal credit footprint, or are non-UK-resident). Resolution: provide alternative cross-check answers if available; or switch to ACSP route which uses different document-based verification. (iv) **Wrong-data entry.** Reader enters wrong DOB or address or document-number. Resolution: corrections can typically be made within the One Login session; if submitted with wrong data, contact Companies House directly via published support channels (do NOT rely on the personal code if you believe the underlying data is wrong — correction may issue a new code or update the existing one). (v) **Personal code lost.** Retrieve via One Login account messages OR via ACSP. If both routes fail, contact Companies House (the natural person does NOT re-verify; the code is retrievable). (vi) **Multiple identity claims.** A natural person who has previously been registered at Companies House under a former name (married name change, deed poll change, transgender transition) must surface former names during verification to ensure cross-linking with the historical director / PSC record. Failure to surface former names can leave historical records orphaned.)
9. **What happens if the verification is refused?** (Refusal scenarios: (i) **Documentation insufficient.** Insufficient identity-proofing — re-try with better documents, or switch route (One Login → ACSP or vice versa). (ii) **Identity dispute.** Companies House or the ACSP cannot match the live verification against the natural-person record reliably — escalate via Companies House published support channel. (iii) **Sanctions / PEPs / criminal-record screening.** The MLR 2017 supervision applied by the ACSP (or the equivalent screening at GOV.UK One Login) flags the natural person against sanctions lists or politically-exposed-persons screening or adverse-record screening — this is rare but creates a legitimate hold on the verification. Where the issue is unrelated to the ID verification itself (e.g. a name-match coincidence with a sanctioned person), resolution is through the screening provider's escalation channel. Where the issue IS a positive sanctions / PEPs match, separate compliance considerations attach (the reader's lawful capacity to act as a UK director or PSC may be in question — separate legal advice required). (iv) **National security exemption.** ECCTA s.67 provides a narrow national-security exemption from verification. Practically unavailable to ordinary readers; flagged here for completeness.)
10. **Data protection + privacy summary?** (Three-layer answer: (i) **What Companies House holds and how long.** Companies House is data controller for the verification data per Data Protection Act 2018 + UK GDPR. Retention period set by Companies House published privacy notice — Stage 2 sub-agent verifies at write per §16.35. (ii) **What is published and what is not.** Per ECCTA s.69 + the Companies House published register of changes — full legal name + month + year of birth + nationality + country of residence + service address + verified-status flag are publicly visible; residential address (where different from service address) + full DOB day + identifying-document number + biometric data are NOT publicly visible. (iii) **What the reader can do under UK GDPR + Data Protection Act 2018.** Subject access requests, rectification rights, restriction-of-processing rights, complaint to ICO. Practical reality: the verification data sits within the Companies House register architecture + is subject to its statutory operating framework — UK GDPR rights apply but the statutory basis for processing under ECCTA is solid + restricts the practical scope of rectification or erasure claims to genuinely-incorrect data, not to opting out of the regime.)
11. **What if I want to switch routes mid-process?** (GOV.UK One Login → ACSP or vice versa is permitted. The natural person is not locked into a route — the operative requirement is verified-identity status + a personal code, which can be delivered via either route. If a reader starts the One Login route + abandons it before completing, the partial data is typically discarded after a session-timeout period (Stage 2 sub-agent verifies at write). If a reader starts the ACSP route + then chooses to switch to One Login (e.g. because the ACSP fee was unwelcome or the relationship lapsed), the reader can start the One Login route fresh + complete. The PERSONAL CODE allocation is route-agnostic — the reader's final allocated code is the same code regardless of route, once the underlying verification is complete.)
12. **How does this interact with corporate-directors + corporate-PSCs (the later-phase scope)?** (The current regime (18 November 2025 commencement) applies to NATURAL-PERSON directors + PSCs only. ## FAQ expansion (RUN session polishes prose; 14-16 FAQs target — form-mechanic depth + data-fields + public-vs-private visibility justifying full coverage)

1. **Q: Is there a "Companies House identity verification form" I can download and complete?**
   A: No. There is no numbered Companies House paper or PDF form for identity verification in the way historic Companies House practice produced AP01 (director appointment), CS01 (confirmation statement), PSC01 (PSC notification), IN01 (incorporation), and MR01 (mortgage particulars). The verification regime under the Economic Crime and Corporate Transparency Act 2023 (ECCTA) is delivered electronically through one of two routes: (a) the GOV.UK One Login service, an interactive web and mobile flow operated by the Government Digital Service that captures identity through document scan, biometric photograph, face-match, and security questions; or (b) the Authorised Corporate Service Provider (ACSP) route, where a UK firm authorised by Companies House and AML-supervised under MLR 2017 verifies your identity using its own KYC platform and submits an electronic confirmation to Companies House on your behalf. Both routes produce the same outcome.

2. **Q: What is the GOV.UK One Login route and how long does it take?**
   A: GOV.UK One Login is a cross-government identity-and-authentication service at `https://signin.account.gov.uk/`. The Companies House identity verification flow is delivered through One Login as one of its identity-proofing services. The screen-by-screen flow takes most readers under 30 minutes from a cold start (account creation + verification) or under 15 minutes if you already have an active One Login account from prior tax-side filings. You need an acceptable identity document (UK passport, non-UK passport in date, UK photo driving licence, UK Biometric Residence Permit, UK Biometric Residence Card, or UK Frontier Worker permit), a smartphone or webcam for the live face-match, and an address-of-residence anchor. The service runs liveness checks + face-match against the document photograph + cross-checks against electoral register and credit-history sources.

3. **Q: What is the ACSP route and how much does it cost?**
   A: Authorised Corporate Service Provider, a UK firm AML-supervised under Money Laundering Regulations 2017 (most accountants, company-secretarial agents, and solicitors qualify) that has registered with Companies House to perform identity verification on clients' behalf per ECCTA s.66. The ACSP collects your identity documents, runs document-authentication + face-match + liveness checks (typically via Onfido, Veriff, Yoti, Jumio, or Persona), and submits an electronic verification confirmation to Companies House. Typical fee: £50-£300 one-off; the lower end applies for existing accountancy clients with current AML records on file, the upper end for new clients verifying from scratch. (Verify against current 2026-vintage rate cards at write per §16.27.) The personal code that results from the verification is permanent and covers every UK directorship + PSC interest you will ever hold.

4. **Q: Which identity documents are accepted?**
   A: The current Companies House published list (verify at write per §16.35 against Companies House guidance): UK passport (PRIMARY recommended); non-UK passport in date; UK photo driving licence (full or provisional); UK Biometric Residence Permit; UK Biometric Residence Card; UK Frontier Worker permit. ACSPs operating under MLR 2017 have somewhat broader latitude — they may accept additional documents under their AML-supervised CDD policy provided the documents meet the firm's identity-proofing floor. Address anchors (utility bills, bank statements, council tax bills, formal address-verification letters) are required separately for the ACSP route; the GOV.UK One Login route runs address verification through electoral register and credit-history cross-checks rather than document uploads.

5. **Q: What data does Companies House capture during verification?**
   A: Common to both routes: full legal name + any former names; date of birth; nationality; country of residence; current residential address; identifying document type + number + expiry date; biometric photograph (captured for face-match purpose); signed declaration that information provided is accurate; verification timestamp + route used + (for ACSP route) ACSP identifier + AML supervisor identifier. Each field has a specific retention purpose driven by the Companies House published privacy notice + the statutory framework under Data Protection Act 2018 + UK GDPR.

6. **Q: What is visible on the public register and what is not?**
   A: Per ECCTA s.69 publication restriction + Companies House published display rules — PUBLICLY VISIBLE on the searchable PSC + director record: full legal name; any former names (where notified on the record); date of birth (month + year ONLY; full day suppressed); nationality; country of residence; service address (registered office of the company OR separately designated service address); verified-status flag (yes / no); date became director / PSC; nature of control (PSCs only); occupation (directors only). NOT PUBLICLY VISIBLE per ECCTA s.69: residential address (where it differs from service address); full date of birth day; identifying document number; biometric photograph; signed-declaration record. This is the operational protection that allows compliance without exposing identity-theft-attractive data on the public register.

7. **Q: How is the personal code delivered and where do I record it?**
   A: For the GOV.UK One Login route, the personal code appears in your One Login account inbox + is emailed to your verified email address typically within 5-15 minutes of submission. For the ACSP route, the code is delivered by the ACSP firm to you, typically by encrypted email + recorded on the firm's client file. The code is alphanumeric (8-12 characters at current rollout — verify format at write per §16.35); permanent (does not expire, does not renew annually, does not change when you move house or change your name); and covers every UK directorship + PSC interest you will ever hold under ECCTA s.68. Record the code in a personal secure place (password manager, safe) + provide it to the accountant or company secretary of every UK company you are a director or PSC of, who will quote it on the next required filing.

8. **Q: Where does the personal code go on my Companies House filings?**
   A: The code becomes a required data-field on the substantive numbered forms that record you as a director or PSC: CS01 (confirmation statement, annual; CA 2006 s.853A); AP01 (director appointment; CA 2006 s.167); PSC01 / PSC02 / PSC03 / PSC04 / PSC05 / PSC06 / PSC07 / PSC08 / PSC09 (PSC notification + change-of-particulars + ceasing-to-be-PSC; CA 2006 ss.790A-790ZG + Sch 1A); IN01 (incorporation; CA 2006 ss.7-12). The code does not replace the existing forms; it integrates into them as a required data-field. Practically, your accountant or company-secretarial agent will pick up the code from you + populate the new code-field on each filing.

9. **Q: What if my document photograph or face-match fails?**
   A: Common failure modes: document image too blurry or with glare obscuring the data zone (re-upload with better lighting + flat surface; use the GOV.UK One Login smartphone app rather than web upload because it reads the biometric chip directly); face-match failure due to glasses, hat, poor lighting, or significant ageing between the document photograph and the live selfie (re-attempt with better conditions, remove glasses); security-question failure where the electoral-register or credit-history cross-check returns no match, common for recent movers and readers with minimal credit footprint (provide alternative cross-check answers if available, or switch to the ACSP route which uses document-based verification rather than database cross-checks).

10. **Q: What if I lose my personal code?**
    A: Retrieve via your GOV.UK One Login account inbox + email archive (if you verified via One Login) OR via the ACSP firm that performed your verification (if you used the ACSP route). If both retrieval routes fail, contact Companies House through their published support channel; the code is retrievable from Companies House records — you do NOT re-verify identity to recover it. The natural-person verification record persists; the code is an identifier sitting alongside that record.

11. **Q: What if I want to switch from GOV.UK One Login to ACSP mid-process or vice versa?**
    A: Permitted. The natural person is not locked into a route; the operative requirement is verified-identity status + a personal code under ECCTA s.68, which can be delivered via either route. If you start GOV.UK One Login and abandon it before completing, the partial data is typically discarded after a session-timeout period; you can then engage an ACSP fresh. If you engage an ACSP and then decide to use One Login (perhaps because the fee was unwelcome or the adviser relationship lapsed), you can start the One Login route fresh. Once verification is complete the personal code is route-agnostic.

12. **Q: What if my identity is refused?**
    A: Refusal scenarios include insufficient identity-proofing (retry with better documents, or switch routes); identity dispute where Companies House or the ACSP cannot match the live verification against the natural-person record reliably (escalate via Companies House published support channel); sanctions / PEPs / criminal-record screening flag (rare; resolution is through the screening provider's escalation channel where the issue is a name-match coincidence; separate compliance considerations attach where the issue is a positive match); national security exemption under ECCTA s.67 (narrow; practically unavailable to ordinary readers).

13. **Q: What about Register of Overseas Entities (RoE) verification — is that the same thing?**
    A: No. Different registers, different verification regimes, different forms. ECCTA identity verification is the COMPANIES-SIDE register (UK private companies + LLPs + their natural-person directors + PSCs) — this page covers it. RoE is the SEPARATE OVERSEAS-ENTITIES register for overseas LEGAL ENTITIES (BVI, Jersey, Guernsey, Cayman, Delaware LLC etc.) that own UK property, governed by ECTEA 2022 — covered by B13 `consequences-for-roe-non-compliance` (M2-B-B3) and existing `register-of-overseas-entities-roe-annual-update-statement-non-resident-landlords`. RoE verification of overseas-entity beneficial owners is parallel-but-distinct: separate forms, separate UK-regulated agent verification mechanic, separate annual update obligations. A natural person who is BOTH a director of a UK company AND a beneficial owner of an overseas-entity-owned UK property faces BOTH regimes.

14. **Q: What about corporate-directors and the later phase?**
    A: The current regime (8 April 2025 voluntary + 18 November 2025 mandatory for new appointments + 12-month transition window to approximately November 2026 for existing directors and PSCs) applies to NATURAL-PERSON directors + PSCs only. Corporate-directors (where a UK company has another company as its director — restricted but not eliminated under CA 2006 + ECCTA reforms) + corporate-PSC officers (where a PSC is a corporate entity + the relevant officers of that corporate entity must verify identity) come into scope in a LATER PHASE scheduled no earlier than November 2026 per the Companies House campaign page (verify current commencement state at write per §11.A F-12).

15. **Q: What is my practical next step?**
    A: Six-step practical sequence: (i) determine whether you are in scope — every natural-person director + every 25%+ PSC of every UK company; (ii) choose your route — GOV.UK One Login (free, self-service, fast for tech-comfortable readers with active One Login + UK passport) vs ACSP (typically £50-£300 fee, accountant-mediated, useful for readers without active One Login + multi-SPV portfolio holders + non-UK-resident directors); (iii) gather documents (One Login: smartphone + UK passport or photo driving licence; ACSP: documents as required by the chosen firm); (iv) complete verification (One Login takes 5-15 minutes typical; ACSP process takes 1-5 business days typical); (v) receive + record personal code in personal records + on the accountant's client file; (vi) quote personal code on all subsequent CS01 / AP01 / PSC01 / IN01 filings.
