# Disadvantages of Family Investment Companies (FICs): The Complete UK Counter-List for Landlords + Family-Business Owners + Estate-Planning Candidates

## Statutory anchor
- Primary (corporate-side disadvantages): Companies Act 2006 c. 46 (governance + disclosure + filing obligations — every FIC is a private limited company under CA 2006 with full company-law overhead). Verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2006/46/contents on 2026-05-27 (sub-agent re-verifies at write time per §16.35). CTA 2010 ss.18N-Q (close investment-holding company framework — disadvantage where carve-out at s.18N(2)(b) fails; 25% main rate without small-profits taper); CTA 2010 s.455 (overdrawn-DLA charge at the dividend-upper-rate set by ITA 2007 s.8(2) — 35.75% from 6 April 2026 per §21.4 rate-by-reference); CTA 2009 s.54 (rent-to-own-property-company hostility per §21.3 lock).
- Primary (incorporation-cost disadvantages): TCGA 1992 s.17 (deemed disposal at market value on transfer to a connected company — CGT crystallises in full unless s.162 incorporation relief applies — §21.5 lock + §25 capital allowances cross-reference); TCGA 1992 s.162 (incorporation relief; *Ramsay v HMRC* [2013] UKUT 226 (TCC) business-test gate — passive few-property BTL portfolios usually fail; **§21.5 do-not-write: "s.162 holdover applies on FIC funding for any BTL portfolio" is false**); Finance Act 2003 (SDLT on the property transfer — standard residential rates + 5% additional dwellings surcharge per §1 lock; no MDR post-1-June-2024 per §1.H Wave 9 mini-lock).
- Primary (settlements + share-gift disadvantages): ITTOIA 2005 s.624 + s.626 (settlements legislation + spouse-exception — Arctic Systems carve-out for spouses, NO carve-out for minor children — §21.5 + Wave 4 lock + ITTOIA 2005 s.629 + s.631 minor-child attribution per §22.10 lock); TCGA 1992 s.165 + Sch 7 (holdover relief on share gifts LIMITED to trading-company shares — investment FIC share gifts NOT eligible; CGT crystallises at market value on the gift — §21.5 lock).
- Primary (IHT-side disadvantages): IHTA 1984 s.105(3) + *Pawson v HMRC* [2013] UKUT 050 (TCC) (investment-letting line — pure-investment FICs do NOT qualify for BPR; §22.1 lock); IHTA 1984 s.103 (loan-back debt anti-avoidance — affects founder-loan-to-FIC architecture); FA 1986 s.102 + s.102B (GROB risk where founder retains use of FIC-held property — §22.6 + §22.11 locks).
- Primary (disclosure + governance disadvantages — post-ECCTA-2023): CA 2006 ss.853A-853L (annual confirmation statement obligation — every FIC files annually + £5,000 max civil penalty under Sch 1B inserted by ECCTA — verify at write); ECCTA 2023 ss.40-45 / s.64 / s.65 (director + PSC identity verification — every FIC director and every FIC PSC must verify ID with Companies House or via ACSP; legal requirement from 18 November 2025 for new appointments + 12-month transition window for existing — §11.A Wave 9 mini-lock); ECCTA 2023 ss.59-63 (lawful purposes statement — annual positive declaration on confirmation statement); ECCTA 2023 ss.28-30 (registered email + appropriate-address rule).

## Key questions this page must answer

1. **What is the headline disadvantage stack for FICs — in one paragraph?** (Three categories of disadvantage operate simultaneously: (i) one-off incorporation costs — CGT on s.17 deemed disposal at market value (no s.162 holdover for passive few-property BTL per *Ramsay v HMRC* business-test), SDLT on the property transfer at standard residential rates plus 5% additional dwellings surcharge (no MDR post-1-June-2024 per §1.H), professional fees £10k-£25k setup; (ii) ongoing running costs and tax-profile costs — corporation tax at 25% main rate where CIHC carve-out fails (and even with the carve-out, the marginal-rate band starts at £50k profit / £250k profit limits-by-reference per §21.4), s.455 overdrawn-DLA charge at 35.75% (rate-by-reference per §21.4 / §21.1), no BPR on founder's death (§22.1 *Pawson*), no s.165 holdover on share gifts (§21.5), settlements legislation traps with minor-child shareholders, £4k-£8k annual maintenance, governance overhead, ECCTA-2023 ID verification + lawful purposes statement + registered email obligations; (iii) exit + horizon costs — winding-up CGT, the 7-year PET clock on growth-share gifts, articles-of-association inflexibility, family-dispute risk over multi-generation horizons.)
2. **CGT on incorporation — when does it crystallise in full, when does s.162 holdover help?** (TCGA 1992 s.17: transfer of property to a connected company is a deemed disposal at market value; CGT crystallises on the gain to date in full unless s.162 incorporation relief applies. s.162 requires the transferred portfolio to be a "business" — *Ramsay v HMRC* [2013] UKUT 226 (TCC) sets the bar: degree of activity sufficient to constitute a business beyond passive rent collection. Passive few-property BTL portfolios USUALLY FAIL the *Ramsay* business-test — CGT crystallises in full. Genuinely active portfolios (full-time portfolio manager landlord with active letting, refurbishment, regulatory compliance, tenant management) MAY pass *Ramsay* but the threshold is fact-specific and HMRC-attackable. Worked example illustrative — Stage 2 sub-agent verifies rates at write per §16.27 / §21.4 / §1.)
3. **SDLT on incorporation — does the 5% additional dwellings surcharge apply, and is MDR available?** (SDLT applies on the property transfer to the FIC at standard residential rates (£0-£125k 0%, £125k-£250k 2%, £250k-£925k 5%, £925k-£1.5m 10%, above £1.5m 12%) PLUS 5% additional dwellings surcharge (raised from 3% with effect from 31 October 2024 per §1 lock). For non-natural-person purchaser (the FIC is non-natural), the 15% flat rate may apply on dwellings over £500k where ATED conditions are not met (FA 2003 Sch 4A). **MDR ABOLISHED for transactions effective from 1 June 2024 (Finance (No.2) Act 2024 s.7 — §1.H lock) — the legacy "MDR can reduce SDLT on portfolio incorporation" framing is dead.** Six-dwellings rule under FA 2003 s.116(7) survives MDR abolition — where 6+ dwellings are transferred in a single transaction (or linked transactions), non-residential SDLT rates apply (no additional dwellings surcharge). For portfolios <6 dwellings, no surcharge-relief route remains. Genuine partnership incorporation under FA 2003 Sch 15 para 10 may achieve sum-of-lower-proportions relief but requires a real pre-existing partnership per §1.A Wave 7 lock.)
4. **Corporation tax at 25% main rate — and the CIHC carve-out is not automatic?** (Corporation tax from 1 April 2023: small profits rate 19% up to £50k, marginal relief between £50k and £250k, main rate 25% above £250k (rate-by-reference per §21.4). Close investment-holding company (CIHC) status under CTA 2010 s.18N disqualifies the FIC from the small profits rate — pure-investment FICs face 25% on all profits regardless of profit level. **The s.18N(2)(b) carve-out (commercial lettings to unconnected persons) RESCUES most BTL-let FICs from CIHC status, but the carve-out is NOT automatic — fact-specific per FIC, verified per change of asset mix.** §21.5 lock — Wave 1 lesson + drift correction (the "all BTL SPVs are CIHCs" framing is wrong; the "no BTL SPVs are CIHCs" framing is also wrong — verify each FIC against the s.18N(2)(b) qualifying-purpose test on incorporation and on each material asset-mix change). FICs holding shares + cash + connected-tenant property predominantly fall WITHIN CIHC — 25% main rate applies.)
5. **s.455 overdrawn-DLA charge — and the rate is now 35.75%?** (Director's loan account credit balance (founder lent money to FIC) is repayable to founder tax-free until exhausted — useful repayment vehicle for the founder's personal income needs. BUT if the founder withdraws MORE than the credit balance, the FIC has an OVERDRAWN DLA — CTA 2010 s.455 charge applies. **Rate: 35.75% from loans made on or after 6 April 2026 per FA 2026 s.4(1)(b) — rate-by-reference to ITA 2007 s.8(2) dividend upper rate per §21.1.** Charge is repayable to the FIC on repayment of the loan to the company within 9 months of the accounting period end — but cashflow risk is real where the founder cannot replenish the DLA promptly. Beneficial loan benefit-in-kind under ITEPA 2003 ss.173-191 separately applies where the loan exceeds £10,000 — additional Class 1A NIC + income-tax cost on the founder personally. §21.1 + §21.4 lock.)
6. **No BPR on the founder's death — *Pawson* investment line?** (IHTA 1984 s.105(3): BPR not available for businesses consisting wholly or mainly of holding investments. *Pawson v HMRC* [2013] UKUT 050 (TCC): passive rent collection from residential lettings is "mainly investment" — BPR does NOT apply. Investment FICs holding BTL portfolios FAIL BPR on the founder's death. From 6 April 2026: even where BPR applies, £1m combined BPR + APR cap per §15.4; above-cap relief drops to 50% — but FICs holding BTL property never qualify in the first place. Forward-link to existing `fic-iht-treatment-bpr-myth` deep-dive for the full BPR myth walkthrough. §22.1 + §21.5 lock.)
7. **No s.165 holdover on share gifts to next generation — investment-FIC exclusion?** (TCGA 1992 s.165 holdover relief on gifts of business assets is LIMITED to trading-company shares per Sch 7 — investment-FIC share gifts NOT eligible. CGT crystallises at market value (with minority-discount valuation per growth-share design) on the gift from founder to children. This is the principal CGT-cost gap between FIC route and direct-property route. Forward-link to existing `fic-gifting-shares-children-property-7-year-iht-mechanics` for the gift-mechanic walkthrough. §21.5 + §22.6 lock.)
8. **Settlements legislation traps — minor children + ITTOIA s.629?** (ITTOIA 2005 s.624: settlements legislation attributes income from "settled property" back to the settlor (founder) where the settlor retains an interest — defeats the wealth-transfer rationale on dividends to founder-controlled shareholdings. ITTOIA 2005 s.626 spouse-exception: income from settlements between spouses NOT attributed (Arctic Systems / *Jones v Garnett* [2007] UKHL 35) — spouse shares are SAFE. ITTOIA 2005 s.629 + s.631 minor-child attribution: income from settlements in favour of unmarried minor children of the settlor IS attributed back to the settlor — minor-child FIC shareholdings are tax-attribution-defective until the child turns 18. §22.10 lock. Adult children: attribution does NOT apply via s.629 — adult-child shares are operationally cleaner BUT the spouse-only s.626 carve-out is wider than commonly framed; sessions writing on family-shareholding architecture must distinguish.)
9. **The cost floor — what does it actually cost to set up + run an FIC?** (Setup: typically £10,000-£25,000 covering legal advice + tax planning + company formation + articles-of-association drafting + share-class design + initial verification of directors and PSCs under ECCTA. Annual maintenance: £4,000-£8,000 covering CT return + statutory accounts + Companies House filings + board governance + ID verification renewals. Cost floor scales with portfolio complexity — multi-property + multi-jurisdiction + cross-border tenanting + multi-class share structures all push setup + maintenance higher. **Cost floor is one reason the FIC route is generally uneconomic below the c. £2m portfolio mark** — the amortised cost-per-£100k-of-value-transferred over a 20-year planning horizon dominates the benefit. Sub-agent flags figures vintage — Stage 2 sub-agent re-sources from a 2026-vintage practice survey or competitor cost page or moves to "typically several thousand to low-five-figures" framing rather than precise £-bands.)
10. **Governance overhead — board meetings, minute books, statutory accounts?** (Every FIC is a private limited company under CA 2006 with full governance overhead: board meetings (typically quarterly + ad-hoc resolutions); minute book maintained + signed; written resolutions for shareholders where board cannot decide; dividend declarations dated + minuted; statutory accounts prepared under FRS 102 or FRS 105 + filed at Companies House annually; CT600 filed with HMRC annually; confirmation statement filed at Companies House annually (now with lawful purposes statement + registered email + verified-PSC + verified-director per ECCTA per §11.A). Substance-over-form risk on dividend declarations + share-class decisions — HMRC may re-characterise as settlements / sham / artificial-arrangement if governance is not real per §21.5 lock. Forward-link to existing `fic-property-corporate-governance-board-meetings-resolutions-discipline` for the governance walkthrough.)
11. **Companies House disclosure + ECCTA-2023 ID verification — loss of privacy?** (Every FIC is on the Companies House public register: directors named (with service address + DOB month-and-year + nationality + occupation + country of residence); PSCs named (with nature of control percentages); share capital structure + statement of capital; registered office; SIC codes. **Post-ECCTA-2023:** every director + PSC must verify identity with Companies House or via ACSP (legal requirement from 18 November 2025 for new appointments + 12-month transition window for existing roles per §11.A). Verified-identity data largely withheld from public inspection per ECCTA s.69 — but the FACT of verification is public + the personal code is permanent. **Public-register visibility is a NET LOSS of privacy vs holding property in personal name (no PSC-equivalent register) or via a discretionary trust (where TRS disclosure is more limited per §22.16).** Forward-link to existing `register-of-overseas-entities-roe-annual-update-statement-non-resident-landlords` for the parallel RoE-side disclosure regime. §11.A lock.)
12. **Exit complexity — winding-up CGT + minority-shareholder gridlock?** (Two exit paths: (a) winding-up (members' voluntary liquidation under IA 1986 Part IV — distribution-in-specie of property to shareholders at CGT market-value disposal; CGT on each disposal at the shareholder's rate (18% / 24% per §5) — **AND SDLT may apply on the deemed transfer from FIC to shareholder per FA 2003 s.53 connected-party rules — depending on consideration and Sch 7 group-relief availability**; substantial CGT + SDLT crystallisation event); (b) share-sale (founder + family sell FIC shares to third-party buyer — CGT on each shareholder's gain at 18% / 24%; no SBA / BADR because investment-FIC). Either path triggers material tax + transaction cost. Minority-shareholder gridlock: where one family member holds a minority growth-share and refuses to consent to winding-up or sale, the controlling shareholder cannot force exit without expensive litigation or unfair-prejudice petition under CA 2006 s.994. Articles-of-association drag-along provisions help but are not absolute.)
13. **The 7-year PET clock + horizon risk on the gift of growth shares?** (Wealth-transfer rationale of FIC depends on the FOUNDER SURVIVING 7 YEARS from the date of growth-share gift — within 7 years, the gifted value is brought back into the founder's estate per IHTA 1984 s.7 (PET fail) + tapered relief between 3-7 years per s.7(4). Founder dying within 3 years of gift = full 40% IHT on the gifted value with no taper. The 7-year clock starts on the SHARE GIFT, NOT on FIC formation. Operational consequence: founder must commit to the gift early in the planning horizon to give the 7-year clock time to run. Founders in their 70s+ are exposed to PET-fail risk; founders in their 50s with 20+ year horizons have lower exposure. §22.6 lock. Cross-link to existing `fic-gifting-shares-children-property-7-year-iht-mechanics` for the gift-mechanic walkthrough.)
14. **Articles-of-association inflexibility + family-dispute risk?** (Articles are bespoke at formation (CA 2006 s.18); reserved-matters list, share-class rights, pre-emption + drag-along + tag-along + dividend-control mechanics are locked in at formation. **Changing articles post-formation requires special resolution (75% shareholder vote per CA 2006 s.21) — minority growth-shareholders can block article changes that affect their class rights (CA 2006 s.630 variation-of-class-rights mechanic).** Family-dispute risk over multi-generation horizons: founder sets up FIC with specific intentions; children disagree at adulthood; minority growth-shareholder refuses to consent to changes; structure becomes inflexible exactly when family circumstances have evolved. Forward-link to existing `fic-articles-of-association-property-control-mechanics` for the articles-design walkthrough — same article structure can be either a strength (control retained, anti-abuse) or a weakness (rigid, dispute-prone) depending on family alignment.)
15. **HMRC FIC Campaign + heightened scrutiny on dividend extraction?** (HMRC launched a dedicated FIC review unit (the FIC Campaign / FIC Unit — see B16 `fic-campaign` deep-dive in this batch) — heightened scrutiny on FIC structures: substance-over-form on dividend declarations (sham challenge under *Furniss v Dawson*-style line), settlements re-characterisation on share-class architecture (s.624 challenge), GROB challenge on founder-occupied property held by FIC (s.102 / s.102B challenge), Ramsay general anti-avoidance attack on incorporation arrangements. Heightened HMRC scrutiny means: more enquiries, more inspections, more documentation requests, more cost on the family side over the planning horizon. Forward-link to B16 for the FIC Campaign deep-dive.)
16. **Dividend-allowance erosion + higher-rate-band exposure on extractions?** (Dividend extraction from FIC to shareholders is taxed in shareholders' hands at 8.75% basic / 33.75% higher / 39.35% additional from 6 April 2022 (rate-by-reference per §21.4 — verify rates + bands at write); £500 dividend allowance from 6 April 2024 (down from £1,000 in 2023/24 + £2,000 historic). For higher-rate + additional-rate shareholders, dividend extraction at the higher / upper rate is INEFFICIENT vs personal-name BTL with s.24-restricted finance costs at the basic-rate-tax-credit cap. Where the founder is in higher-rate or additional-rate bracket pre-FIC and would remain so post-FIC, the dividend-tax cost on extraction can EXCEED the s.24 cost saved at corporate level — particularly for low-leverage portfolios where s.24 was not biting hard pre-FIC. Worked-example discipline expected.)
17. **The opportunity cost — what would have happened if I had NOT set up the FIC?** (Counterfactual analysis: keeping properties in personal name; CGT exposure on direct gifts to children OR death-time CGT rebase under TCGA 1992 s.62(1); no SDLT incorporation cost; no annual maintenance cost; full personal CGT allowance + personal income-tax bands available on rental income; no s.24 mitigation but also no s.455 / no dividend-tax / no CIHC. **For SOME landlords — particularly basic-rate-band, single-property, sub-£2m-portfolio — the do-nothing counterfactual outperforms the FIC route over a 20-year horizon.** B14 surfaces this counterfactual; the decision pillar `family-investment-company-property-worth-it` weighs the counterfactual against the FIC benefits in depth.)
