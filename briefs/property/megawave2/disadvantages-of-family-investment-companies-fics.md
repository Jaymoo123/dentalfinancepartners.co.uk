# Disadvantages of Family Investment Companies (FICs): The Complete UK Counter-List for Landlords + Family-Business Owners + Estate-Planning Candidates

## Statutory anchor
- Primary (corporate-side disadvantages): Companies Act 2006 c. 46 (governance + disclosure + filing obligations — every FIC is a private limited company under CA 2006 with full company-law overhead). Verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2006/46/contents on 2026-05-27 (sub-agent re-verifies at write time per §16.35). CTA 2010 ss.18N-Q (close investment-holding company framework — disadvantage where carve-out at s.18N(2)(b) fails; 25% main rate without small-profits taper); CTA 2010 s.455 (overdrawn-DLA charge at the dividend-upper-rate set by ITA 2007 s.8(2) — 35.75% from 6 April 2026 per §21.4 rate-by-reference); CTA 2009 s.54 (rent-to-own-property-company hostility per §21.3 lock).
- Primary (incorporation-cost disadvantages): TCGA 1992 s.17 (deemed disposal at market value on transfer to a connected company — CGT crystallises in full unless s.162 incorporation relief applies — §21.5 lock + §25 capital allowances cross-reference); TCGA 1992 s.162 (incorporation relief; *Ramsay v HMRC* [2013] UKUT 226 (TCC) business-test gate — passive few-property BTL portfolios usually fail; **§21.5 do-not-write: "s.162 holdover applies on FIC funding for any BTL portfolio" is false**); Finance Act 2003 (SDLT on the property transfer — standard residential rates + 5% additional dwellings surcharge per §1 lock; no MDR post-1-June-2024 per §1.H Wave 9 mini-lock).
- Primary (settlements + share-gift disadvantages): ITTOIA 2005 s.624 + s.626 (settlements legislation + spouse-exception — Arctic Systems carve-out for spouses, NO carve-out for minor children — §21.5 + Wave 4 lock + ITTOIA 2005 s.629 + s.631 minor-child attribution per §22.10 lock); TCGA 1992 s.165 + Sch 7 (holdover relief on share gifts LIMITED to trading-company shares — investment FIC share gifts NOT eligible; CGT crystallises at market value on the gift — §21.5 lock).
- Primary (IHT-side disadvantages): IHTA 1984 s.105(3) + *Pawson v HMRC* [2013] UKUT 050 (TCC) (investment-letting line — pure-investment FICs do NOT qualify for BPR; §22.1 lock); IHTA 1984 s.103 (loan-back debt anti-avoidance — affects founder-loan-to-FIC architecture); FA 1986 s.102 + s.102B (GROB risk where founder retains use of FIC-held property — §22.6 + §22.11 locks).
- Primary (disclosure + governance disadvantages — post-ECCTA-2023): CA 2006 ss.853A-853L (annual confirmation statement obligation — every FIC files annually + £5,000 max civil penalty under Sch 1B inserted by ECCTA — verify at write); ECCTA 2023 ss.40-45 / s.64 / s.65 (director + PSC identity verification — every FIC director and every FIC PSC must verify ID with Companies House or via ACSP; legal requirement from 18 November 2025 for new appointments + 12-month transition window for existing — §11.A Wave 9 mini-lock); ECCTA 2023 ss.59-63 (lawful purposes statement — annual positive declaration on confirmation statement); ECCTA 2023 ss.28-30 (registered email + appropriate-address rule).

## Key questions this page must answer

1. **What is the headline disadvantage stack for FICs — in one paragraph?** (Three categories of disadvantage operate simultaneously: (i) one-off incorporation costs — CGT on s.17 deemed disposal at market value (no s.162 holdover for passive few-property BTL per *Ramsay v HMRC* business-test), SDLT on the property transfer at standard residential rates plus 5% additional dwellings surcharge (no MDR post-1-June-2024 per §1.H), professional fees £10k-£25k setup; (ii) ongoing running costs and tax-profile costs — corporation tax at 25% main rate where CIHC carve-out fails (and even with the carve-out, the marginal-rate band starts at £50k profit / £250k profit limits-by-reference per §21.4), s.455 overdrawn-DLA charge at 35.75% (rate-by-reference per §21.4 / §21.1), no BPR on founder's death (§22.1 *Pawson*), no s.165 holdover on share gifts (§21.5), settlements legislation traps with minor-child shareholders, £4k-£8k annual maintenance, governance overhead, ECCTA-2023 ID verification + lawful purposes statement + registered email obligations; (iii) exit + horizon costs — winding-up CGT, the 7-year PET clock on growth-share gifts, articles-of-association inflexibility, family-dispute risk over multi-generation horizons.)
2. **CGT on incorporation — when does it crystallise in full, when does s.162 holdover help?** (TCGA 1992 s.17: transfer of property to a connected company is a deemed disposal at market value; CGT crystallises on the gain to date in full unless s.162 incorporation relief applies. s.162 requires the transferred portfolio to be a "business" — *Ramsay v HMRC* [2013] UKUT 226 (TCC) sets the bar: degree of activity sufficient to constitute a business beyond passive rent collection. Passive few-property BTL portfolios USUALLY FAIL the *Ramsay* business-test — CGT crystallises in full. Genuinely active portfolios (full-time portfolio manager landlord with active letting, refurbishment, regulatory compliance, tenant management) MAY pass *Ramsay* but the threshold is fact-specific and HMRC-attackable. Worked example illustrative — Stage 2 sub-agent verifies rates at write per §16.27 / §21.4 / §1.)
3. **SDLT on incorporation — does the 5% additional dwellings surcharge apply, and is MDR available?** (SDLT applies on the property transfer to the FIC at standard residential rates (£0-£125k 0%, £125k-£250k 2%, £250k-£925k 5%, £925k-£1.5m 10%, above £1.5m 12%) PLUS 5% additional dwellings surcharge (raised from 3% with effect from 31 October 2024 per §1 lock). For non-natural-person purchaser (the FIC is non-natural), the 15% flat rate may apply on dwellings over £500k where ATED conditions are not met (FA 2003 Sch 4A). **MDR ABOLISHED for transactions effective from 1 June 2024 (Finance (No.2) Act 2024 s.7 — §1.H lock) — the legacy "MDR can reduce SDLT on portfolio incorporation" framing is dead.** Six-dwellings rule under FA 2003 s.116(7) survives MDR abolition — where 6+ dwellings are transferred in a single transaction (or linked transactions), non-residential SDLT rates apply (no additional dwellings surcharge). For portfolios <6 dwellings, no surcharge-relief route remains. Genuine partnership incorporation under FA 2003 Sch 15 para 10 may achieve sum-of-lower-proportions relief but requires a real pre-existing partnership per §1.A Wave 7 lock.)
4. **Corporation tax at 25% main rate — and the CIHC carve-out is not automatic?** (Corporation tax from 1 April 2023: small profits rate 19% up to £50k, marginal relief between £50k and £250k, main rate 25% above £250k (rate-by-reference per §21.4). Close investment-holding company (CIHC) status under CTA 2010 s.18N disqualifies the FIC from the small profits rate — pure-investment FICs face 25% on all profits regardless of profit level. **The s.18N(2)(b) carve-out (commercial lettings to unconnected persons) RESCUES most BTL-let FICs from CIHC status, but the carve-out is NOT automatic — fact-specific per FIC, verified per change of asset mix.** §21.5 lock — Wave 1 lesson + drift correction (the "all BTL SPVs are CIHCs" framing is wrong; the "no BTL SPVs are CIHCs" framing is also wrong — verify each FIC against the s.18N(2)(b) qualifying-purpose test on incorporation and on each material asset-mix change). FICs holding shares + cash + connected-tenant property predominantly fall WITHIN CIHC — 25% main rate applies.)
5. **s.455 overdrawn-DLA charge — and the rate is now 35.75%?** (Director's loan account credit balance (founder lent money to FIC) is repayable to founder tax-free until exhausted — useful repayment vehicle for the founder's personal income needs. BUT if the founder withdraws MORE than the credit balance, the FIC has an OVERDRAWN DLA — CTA 2010 s.455 charge applies. **Rate: 35.75% from loans made on or after 6 April 2026 per FA 2026 s.4(1)(b) — rate-by-reference to ITA 2007 s.8(2) dividend upper rate per §21.1.** Charge is repayable to the FIC on repayment of the loan to the company within 9 months of the accounting period end — but cashflow risk is real where the founder cannot replenish the DLA promptly. Beneficial loan benefit-in-kind under ITEPA 2003 ss.173-191 separately applies where the loan exceeds £10,000 — additional Class 1A NIC + income-tax cost on the founder personally. §21.1 + §21.4 lock.)
6. **No BPR on the founder's death — *Pawson* investment line?** (IHTA 1984 s.105(3): BPR not available for businesses consisting wholly or mainly of holding investments. *Pawson v HMRC* [2013] UKUT 050 (TCC): passive rent collection from residential lettings is "mainly investment" — BPR does NOT apply. Investment FICs holding BTL portfolios FAIL BPR on the founder's death. From 6 April 2026: even where BPR applies, £1m combined BPR + APR cap per §15.4; above-cap relief drops to 50% — but FICs holding BTL property never qualify in the first place. Forward-link to existing `fic-iht-treatment-bpr-myth` deep-dive for the full BPR myth walkthrough. §22.1 + §21.5 lock.)
7. **No s.165 holdover on share gifts to next generation — investment-FIC exclusion?** (TCGA 1992 s.165 holdover relief on gifts of business assets is LIMITED to trading-company shares per Sch 7 — investment-FIC share gifts NOT eligible. CGT crystallises at market value (with minority-discount valuation per growth-share design) on the gift from founder to children. This is the principal CGT-cost gap between FIC route and direct-property route. Forward-link to existing `fic-gifting-shares-children-property-7-year-iht-mechanics` for the gift-mechanic walkthrough. §21.5 + §22.6 lock.)
8. **Settlements legislation traps — minor children + ITTOIA s.629?** (ITTOIA 2005 s.624: settlements legislation attributes income from "settled property" back to the settlor (founder) where the settlor retains an interest — defeats the wealth-transfer rationale on dividends to founder-controlled shareholdings. ITTOIA 2005 s.626 spouse-exception: income from settlements between spouses NOT attributed (Arctic Systems / *Jones v Garnett* [2007] UKHL 35) — spouse shares are SAFE. ITTOIA 2005 s.629 + s.631 minor-child attribution: income from settlements in favour of unmarried minor children of the settlor IS attributed back to the settlor — minor-child FIC shareholdings are tax-attribution-defective until the child turns 18. §22.10 lock. Adult children: attribution does NOT apply via s.629 — adult-child shares are operationally cleaner BUT the spouse-only s.626 carve-out is wider than commonly framed; sessions writing on family-shareholding architecture must distinguish.)
9. **The cost floor — what does it actually cost to set up + run an FIC?** (Setup: typically £10,000-£25,000 covering legal advice + tax planning + company formation + articles-of-association drafting + share-class design + initial verification of directors and PSCs under ECCTA. Annual maintenance: £4,000-£8,000 covering CT return + statutory accounts + Companies House filings + board governance + ID verification renewals. Cost floor scales with portfolio complexity — multi-property + multi-jurisdiction + cross-border tenanting + multi-class share structures all push setup + maintenance higher. **Cost floor is one reason the FIC route is generally uneconomic below the c. £2m portfolio mark** — the amortised cost-per-£100k-of-value-transferred over a 20-year planning horizon dominates the benefit. Sub-agent flags figures vintage — Stage 2 sub-agent re-sources from a 2026-vintage practice survey or competitor cost page or moves to "typically several thousand to low-five-figures" framing rather than precise £-bands.)
10. **Governance overhead — board meetings, minute books, statutory accounts?** (Every FIC is a private limited company under CA 2006 with full governance overhead: board meetings (typically quarterly + ad-hoc resolutions); minute book maintained + signed; written resolutions for shareholders where board cannot decide; dividend declarations dated + minuted; statutory accounts prepared under FRS 102 or FRS 105 + filed at Companies House annually; CT600 filed with HMRC annually; confirmation statement filed at Companies House annually (now with lawful purposes statement + registered email + verified-PSC + verified-director per ECCTA per §11.A). Substance-over-form risk on dividend declarations + share-class decisions — HMRC may re-characterise as settlements / sham / artificial-arrangement if governance is not real per §21.5 lock. Forward-link to existing `fic-property-corporate-governance-board-meetings-resolutions-discipline` for the governance walkthrough.)
11. **Companies House disclosure + ECCTA-2023 ID verification — loss of privacy?** (Every FIC is on the Companies House public register: directors named (with service address + DOB month-and-year + nationality + occupation + country of residence); PSCs named (with nature of control percentages); share capital structure + statement of capital; registered office; SIC codes. **Post-ECCTA-2023:** every director + PSC must verify identity with Companies House or via ACSP (legal requirement from 18 November 2025 for new appointments + 12-month transition window for existing roles per §11.A). Verified-identity data largely withheld from public inspection per ECCTA s.69 — but the FACT of verification is public + the personal code is permanent. **Public-register visibility is a NET LOSS of privacy vs holding property in personal name (no PSC-equivalent register) or via a discretionary trust (where TRS disclosure is more limited per §22.16).** Forward-link to existing `register-of-overseas-entities-roe-annual-update-statement-non-resident-landlords` for the parallel RoE-side disclosure regime. §11.A lock.)
12. **Exit complexity — winding-up CGT + minority-shareholder gridlock?** (Two exit paths: (a) winding-up (members' voluntary liquidation under IA 1986 Part IV — distribution-in-specie of property to shareholders at CGT market-value disposal; CGT on each disposal at the shareholder's rate (18% / 24% per §5) — **AND SDLT may apply on the deemed transfer from FIC to shareholder per FA 2003 s.53 connected-party rules — depending on consideration and Sch 7 group-relief availability**; substantial CGT + SDLT crystallisation event); (b) share-sale (founder + family sell FIC shares to third-party buyer — CGT on each shareholder's gain at 18% / 24%; no SBA / BADR because investment-FIC). Either path triggers material tax + transaction cost. Minority-shareholder gridlock: where one family member holds a minority growth-share and refuses to consent to winding-up or sale, the controlling shareholder cannot force exit without expensive litigation or unfair-prejudice petition under CA 2006 s.994. Articles-of-association drag-along provisions help but are not absolute.)
13. **The 7-year PET clock + horizon risk on the gift of growth shares?** (Wealth-transfer rationale of FIC depends on the FOUNDER SURVIVING 7 YEARS from the date of growth-share gift — within 7 years, the gifted value is brought back into the founder's estate per IHTA 1984 s.7 (PET fail) + tapered relief between 3-7 years per s.7(4). Founder dying within 3 years of gift = full 40% IHT on the gifted value with no taper. The 7-year clock starts on the SHARE GIFT, NOT on FIC formation. Operational consequence: founder must commit to the gift early in the planning horizon to give the 7-year clock time to run. Founders in their 70s+ are exposed to PET-fail risk; founders in their 50s with 20+ year horizons have lower exposure. §22.6 lock. Cross-link to existing `fic-gifting-shares-children-property-7-year-iht-mechanics` for the gift-mechanic walkthrough.)
14. **Articles-of-association inflexibility + family-dispute risk?** (Articles are bespoke at formation (CA 2006 s.18); reserved-matters list, share-class rights, pre-emption + drag-along + tag-along + dividend-control mechanics are locked in at formation. **Changing articles post-formation requires special resolution (75% shareholder vote per CA 2006 s.21) — minority growth-shareholders can block article changes that affect their class rights (CA 2006 s.630 variation-of-class-rights mechanic).** Family-dispute risk over multi-generation horizons: founder sets up FIC with specific intentions; children disagree at adulthood; minority growth-shareholder refuses to consent to changes; structure becomes inflexible exactly when family circumstances have evolved. Forward-link to existing `fic-articles-of-association-property-control-mechanics` for the articles-design walkthrough — same article structure can be either a strength (control retained, anti-abuse) or a weakness (rigid, dispute-prone) depending on family alignment.)
15. **HMRC FIC Campaign + heightened scrutiny on dividend extraction?** (HMRC launched a dedicated FIC review unit (the FIC Campaign / FIC Unit — see B16 `fic-campaign` deep-dive in this batch) — heightened scrutiny on FIC structures: substance-over-form on dividend declarations (sham challenge under *Furniss v Dawson*-style line), settlements re-characterisation on share-class architecture (s.624 challenge), GROB challenge on founder-occupied property held by FIC (s.102 / s.102B challenge), Ramsay general anti-avoidance attack on incorporation arrangements. Heightened HMRC scrutiny means: more enquiries, more inspections, more documentation requests, more cost on the family side over the planning horizon. Forward-link to B16 for the FIC Campaign deep-dive.)
16. **Dividend-allowance erosion + higher-rate-band exposure on extractions?** (Dividend extraction from FIC to shareholders is taxed in shareholders' hands at 8.75% basic / 33.75% higher / 39.35% additional from 6 April 2022 (rate-by-reference per §21.4 — verify rates + bands at write); £500 dividend allowance from 6 April 2024 (down from £1,000 in 2023/24 + £2,000 historic). For higher-rate + additional-rate shareholders, dividend extraction at the higher / upper rate is INEFFICIENT vs personal-name BTL with s.24-restricted finance costs at the basic-rate-tax-credit cap. Where the founder is in higher-rate or additional-rate bracket pre-FIC and would remain so post-FIC, the dividend-tax cost on extraction can EXCEED the s.24 cost saved at corporate level — particularly for low-leverage portfolios where s.24 was not biting hard pre-FIC. Worked-example discipline expected.)
17. **The opportunity cost — what would have happened if I had NOT set up the FIC?** (Counterfactual analysis: keeping properties in personal name; CGT exposure on direct gifts to children OR death-time CGT rebase under TCGA 1992 s.62(1); no SDLT incorporation cost; no annual maintenance cost; full personal CGT allowance + personal income-tax bands available on rental income; no s.24 mitigation but also no s.455 / no dividend-tax / no CIHC. **For SOME landlords — particularly basic-rate-band, single-property, sub-£2m-portfolio — the do-nothing counterfactual outperforms the FIC route over a 20-year horizon.** B14 surfaces this counterfactual; the decision pillar `family-investment-company-property-worth-it` weighs the counterfactual against the FIC benefits in depth.)

## Worked-example data (RUN session uses these as canvas)

### Example 1 — The CGT-on-incorporation cost (anchor disadvantage opening hook)

Mrs Patel owns three London BTL flats acquired 2014-2018 at total cost £820k; current market value £1.42m; latent gain £600k. She is considering FIC incorporation on adviser recommendation. TCGA 1992 s.17 deems disposal at market value on transfer to a connected company; CGT crystallises in full at the £600k gain unless s.162 incorporation relief applies. The *Ramsay v HMRC* [2013] UKUT 226 (TCC) business-test gate: a three-flat passive-let portfolio managed by an external agent does NOT pass the business test. CGT at 24% (post-April-2024 residential rate — verify at write per §5) = £144k of CGT crystallising immediately on the transfer. Add SDLT on the connected-party transfer (FA 2003 deemed market-value consideration per s.53): full standard residential SDLT + 5% additional dwellings surcharge per §1 + 5% (post-31-Oct-2024) on three dwellings — typically £130k-£160k SDLT. Total incorporation cost: £274k-£304k of tax PLUS £15k-£20k of professional fees. This is paid in year 1 before any benefit accrues. The IHT savings the FIC eventually delivers must exceed this £300k cost over the founder's planning horizon — a high break-even for many family situations.

### Example 2 — The s.455 + dividend-rate stack on extraction

The FIC has earned £180k of net rental profit + £30k of fund-management income in year 3; corporation tax at 25% (assuming CIHC carve-out fails) = £52,500 leaves £157,500 distributable. The founder has been drawing on her DLA credit balance for 2 years and is now at zero; she needs £80k cash this year. Two options: (a) declare an £80k dividend — dividend tax at her marginal rate (assume higher rate 33.75% pre-FA-2026; rate may move per §21.4 rate-by-reference at write) = £27k personal tax + £80k cash drawn; total cash cost to founder £27k. (b) Take an £80k overdrawn-DLA loan from the FIC, planning to repay within 9 months of accounting period end — if repaid in time, no s.455 + no dividend tax. If NOT repaid within 9 months, s.455 charge at 35.75% (from 6 April 2026 per §21.1 / §21.4 — verify rate + commencement at write) = £28,600 charged to FIC (recoverable on repayment) PLUS beneficial-loan benefit-in-kind under ITEPA 2003 ss.173-191 (notional interest charge to founder). The s.455 architecture incentivises careful DLA management; the higher-rate dividend tax incentivises against routine extraction. Compare with personal-name BTL where rental income is taxed once at the founder's marginal rate without the dividend-stack overlay.

### Example 3 — The no-BPR + no-s.165 double IHT gap on succession

The founder dies in 2031 owning 100% of the FIC's ordinary shares (founder retained voting control + dividend-control architecture); FIC NAV at death £4.8m (BTL portfolio £4.2m + cash + fund holdings £600k). IHTA 1984 s.105(3) + *Pawson v HMRC* [2013] UKUT 050 (TCC): pure-investment FIC fails the BPR business-mainly-investment test. No BPR. The full £4.8m sits in the founder's estate at 40% IHT above the NRB + RNRB allowances per §22.16 architecture: roughly £1.92m of IHT crystallising on the founder's £4.8m FIC holding (subject to NRB / RNRB / transferable allowances). The April 2026 £1m combined BPR + APR cap per §15.4 is irrelevant because investment-FIC was never in scope for BPR in the first place. If the founder had previously gifted growth-shares to her two children, those gifts were CGT-chargeable at market value (TCGA 1992 s.165 holdover NOT available for investment-FIC share gifts per Sch 7 trading-company gate), AND the gifted value falls back into the estate per IHTA 1984 s.7 PET-fail mechanic if the founder dies within 7 years (full 40% IHT if death within 3 years; tapered relief between 3-7 years per s.7(4)). The double IHT gap is the principal architectural cost of the FIC route on the IHT side — the wealth-transfer rationale depends on the founder surviving the 7-year PET clock + the rate of value accumulation in growth shares vs in the founder's frozen original-share base.

### Example 4 — The cost floor + portfolio-economic-threshold reality

For a 5-SPV BTL portfolio with £2.4m NAV currently held in personal name: incorporation as FIC costs £18k (legal + tax + share-class drafting + company formation) + transfer-in CGT of £180k (24% on £750k aggregate gain) + transfer-in SDLT of £164k (residential SDLT + 5% surcharge on the £2.4m transfer). Year-1 incorporation cost total: £362k. Subsequent annual maintenance: £6k accountant + £2k governance + £1k ID-verification + £1k incidental = £10k/year. Over a 20-year planning horizon: £200k of maintenance + £362k of incorporation = £562k all-in cost. To break even, the FIC structure must deliver £562k+ of net IHT + CT-rate-arbitrage + intergenerational-transfer savings over 20 years. For this portfolio profile, the break-even depends heavily on the founder's planning horizon, marginal income-tax band, and family-shareholder architecture — for many similar profiles the break-even is achievable but tight; for smaller portfolios (sub-£2m NAV with similar latent gain), the break-even moves further out and the do-nothing counterfactual outperforms.

### Example 5 — Misframings the page must correct (verbatim do-not-write list)

- **Misframe 1:** "s.162 holdover applies on FIC funding for any BTL portfolio." FALSE — *Ramsay v HMRC* business-test gate; passive few-property BTL portfolios usually fail. Per §21.5 do-not-write.
- **Misframe 2:** "MDR can reduce SDLT on portfolio incorporation." FALSE — MDR abolished for transactions effective from 1 June 2024 per F(No.2)A 2024 s.7 + §1.H Wave 9 mini-lock. Sub-6-dwelling portfolio incorporations face full SDLT + 5% surcharge.
- **Misframe 3:** "All BTL SPVs are CIHCs." FALSE. **Misframe 4:** "No BTL SPVs are CIHCs." FALSE. Verify each FIC against s.18N(2)(b) qualifying-purpose carve-out test per §21.5 Wave 1 lock.
- **Misframe 5:** "FIC shares qualify for BPR." FALSE — IHTA s.105(3) + *Pawson* investment line; investment FICs fail. Even where BPR applies elsewhere, the April 2026 £1m BPR + APR cap per §15.4 caps the relief.
- **Misframe 6:** "s.165 holdover applies on share gifts to children." FALSE — investment-FIC share gifts excluded under Sch 7 trading-company gate.
- **Misframe 7:** "Spouse-shareholding under Arctic Systems is wide-open." FALSE — Arctic Systems carve-out at s.626 is narrow; verify against *Jones v Garnett* [2007] UKHL 35 framework + ongoing HMRC challenges.
- **Misframe 8:** "Minor-child shareholding works the same as adult-child shareholding." FALSE — ITTOIA s.629 + s.631 minor-child attribution bites until age 18 per §22.10 lock.

## FAQ expansion (RUN session polishes prose; 13-15 FAQs target — negative-pillar depth)

1. **Q: What is the headline list of FIC disadvantages?**
   A: Fifteen-plus operationally material disadvantages across three categories: (i) one-off incorporation costs — CGT on s.17 deemed market-value disposal (no s.162 holdover for passive BTL per *Ramsay v HMRC*); SDLT on the property transfer at standard residential rates + 5% additional dwellings surcharge (no MDR post-1-June-2024 per §1.H); professional fees £10k-£25k; (ii) ongoing running costs — corporation tax at 25% main rate where CIHC carve-out fails; s.455 overdrawn-DLA charge at the dividend-upper-rate (35.75% from 6 April 2026 per §21.1); no BPR on death (§22.1 *Pawson*); no s.165 holdover on share gifts (§21.5); settlements legislation traps with minor children; £4k-£8k annual maintenance + governance overhead + ECCTA-2023 ID verification + lawful purposes statement + registered email obligations; (iii) exit and horizon costs — winding-up CGT + SDLT exposure; the 7-year PET clock; articles-of-association inflexibility; family-dispute risk; HMRC FIC Campaign / Wealthy Team scrutiny; dividend-allowance erosion for higher-rate shareholders.

2. **Q: When does CGT crystallise on FIC incorporation and when does s.162 help?**
   A: TCGA 1992 s.17 deems a market-value disposal on transfer to a connected company; CGT crystallises in full on the gain to date unless s.162 incorporation relief applies. The s.162 relief requires the transferred portfolio to constitute a "business" per the *Ramsay v HMRC* [2013] UKUT 226 (TCC) framework — passive few-property BTL portfolios usually fail. Genuinely active full-time portfolio management with material letting, refurbishment, regulatory compliance, and tenant management may pass; the threshold is fact-specific and HMRC-attackable. CGT rate from 6 April 2024: 18% basic / 24% higher (per §5 — verify at write). On a £600k latent gain crystallising in full, the cost is roughly £108k-£144k of CGT depending on the founder's CGT band — paid in the tax year of transfer, before any FIC benefit accrues.

3. **Q: Does the 5% additional dwellings surcharge apply on FIC funding?**
   A: Yes, on residential property transfers to the FIC. Standard residential SDLT bands apply (£0-£125k 0%, £125k-£250k 2%, £250k-£925k 5%, £925k-£1.5m 10%, above £1.5m 12%) PLUS 5% additional dwellings surcharge (raised from 3% with effect from 31 October 2024 per §1 lock). For non-natural-person purchaser (the FIC), the 15% flat rate may apply on dwellings over £500k where ATED conditions are not met (FA 2003 Sch 4A). MDR has been ABOLISHED for transactions effective from 1 June 2024 (F(No.2)A 2024 s.7 + §1.H Wave 9 mini-lock) — the legacy "MDR reduces SDLT on portfolio incorporation" framing is dead. The six-dwellings rule under FA 2003 s.116(7) survives — where 6+ dwellings are transferred in a single transaction (or linked transactions), non-residential rates apply (no additional dwellings surcharge). Genuine partnership incorporation under FA 2003 Sch 15 para 10 may achieve sum-of-lower-proportions relief but requires a real pre-existing partnership per §1.A Wave 7 lock.

4. **Q: Does my FIC qualify for the small profits rate, or am I stuck at 25%?**
   A: Corporation tax from 1 April 2023: small profits rate 19% up to £50k profit; marginal relief between £50k and £250k; main rate 25% above £250k (rate-by-reference per §21.4 — verify at write). Close investment-holding company (CIHC) status under CTA 2010 s.18N disqualifies the FIC from the small profits rate — pure-investment FICs face 25% on all profits regardless of profit level. The s.18N(2)(b) carve-out (commercial lettings to unconnected persons) rescues most BTL-let FICs from CIHC status — BUT the carve-out is NOT automatic; verify each FIC against the qualifying-purpose test on incorporation and on each material asset-mix change per §21.5 Wave 1 lock. FICs holding shares + cash + connected-tenant property predominantly fall WITHIN CIHC — 25% main rate applies.

5. **Q: What is the s.455 charge and what is the current rate?**
   A: Where the founder has an OVERDRAWN director's loan account (drawn more from the FIC than the credit balance allows), CTA 2010 s.455 charges the FIC on the overdrawn amount at the dividend upper rate per ITA 2007 s.8(2) — 33.75% pre-FA-2026; **35.75% from 6 April 2026 per FA 2026 s.4(1)(b)** (rate-by-reference per §21.1 / §21.4 — verify Royal Assent + commencement at write per §16.27). Charge is repayable to the FIC on repayment of the loan within 9 months of the accounting period end. Beneficial loan benefit-in-kind under ITEPA 2003 ss.173-191 separately applies where the loan exceeds £10,000 (notional-interest income-tax cost on the founder personally + Class 1A NIC on the FIC). Cashflow risk is real where the founder cannot replenish the DLA promptly within the 9-month window.

6. **Q: Does my FIC qualify for Business Property Relief on my death?**
   A: No. IHTA 1984 s.105(3) excludes businesses consisting wholly or mainly of holding investments. *Pawson v HMRC* [2013] UKUT 050 (TCC): passive rent collection from residential lettings is "mainly investment" — BPR does NOT apply. Investment FICs holding BTL portfolios FAIL BPR on the founder's death. From 6 April 2026 the £1m combined BPR + APR cap per §15.4 caps any BPR relief above £1m at 50% — but FICs holding investment property never qualified in the first place, so the cap is irrelevant. The IHT crystallisation on the founder's FIC holding at death (subject to NRB / RNRB / transferable allowances per §22.16) is one of the largest hidden costs of the FIC route on the IHT side.

7. **Q: Can I use s.165 holdover to gift FIC shares to my children CGT-free?**
   A: No. TCGA 1992 s.165 holdover relief on gifts of business assets is LIMITED to trading-company shares per Sch 7 — investment-FIC share gifts NOT eligible. CGT crystallises at market value (with minority-discount valuation on growth-share design) on the gift from founder to children. This is the principal CGT cost on intergenerational transfer of FIC shares. Combined with the IHTA 1984 s.7 PET-fail mechanic (gift fails as a Potentially Exempt Transfer if founder dies within 7 years; full 40% IHT if death within 3 years; tapered relief between 3-7 years per s.7(4)), the share-gift architecture carries both CGT + IHT exposure.

8. **Q: What are the settlements legislation traps with FIC share architecture?**
   A: ITTOIA 2005 s.624 attributes income from "settled property" back to the settlor (founder) where the settlor retains an interest — defeats the wealth-transfer rationale on dividends to founder-controlled shareholdings. ITTOIA 2005 s.626 spouse-exception: income from settlements between spouses NOT attributed (Arctic Systems / *Jones v Garnett* [2007] UKHL 35) — spouse shares are safe within carve-out limits. ITTOIA 2005 s.629 + s.631 minor-child attribution: income from settlements in favour of unmarried minor children of the settlor IS attributed back to the settlor — minor-child FIC shareholdings are tax-attribution-defective until the child turns 18 per §22.10 lock. Adult children: attribution does NOT apply via s.629 — adult-child shares are operationally cleaner.

9. **Q: What does it actually cost to set up and run a typical FIC?**
   A: Setup: typically £10,000-£25,000 covering legal advice + tax planning + company formation + articles-of-association drafting + share-class design + initial ECCTA ID verification of directors and PSCs. Annual maintenance: £4,000-£8,000 covering CT return + statutory accounts + Companies House filings (now with lawful purposes statement + verified personal codes per ECCTA per §11.A) + board governance + ID-verification renewals. Cost floor scales with portfolio complexity. The FIC route is generally uneconomic below approximately £2m portfolio NAV — the amortised cost per £100k of value transferred over a 20-year planning horizon dominates the benefit. (Figures vintage; RUN session re-sources from 2026-vintage practice survey or shifts to range framing at write.)

10. **Q: What governance overhead does an FIC actually carry?**
    A: Every FIC is a private limited company under CA 2006 with full company-law overhead. Board meetings (typically quarterly + ad-hoc resolutions); minute book maintained + signed; written shareholder resolutions where the board cannot decide; dividend declarations dated and minuted; statutory accounts prepared under FRS 102 or FRS 105 + filed at Companies House annually; CT600 filed with HMRC annually; confirmation statement filed at Companies House annually (now with lawful purposes statement + registered email + verified PSC + verified director per ECCTA per §11.A). Substance-over-form risk on dividend declarations + share-class decisions — HMRC may re-characterise as settlements / sham / artificial-arrangement if governance is not real (see FIC Campaign / Wealthy Team enquiry vectors in companion page B16 `fic-campaign`).

11. **Q: How does ECCTA 2023 affect my FIC's privacy?**
    A: Every FIC sits on the Companies House public register: directors named (with service address + DOB month-and-year + nationality + occupation + country of residence); PSCs named (with nature of control percentages); share capital structure + statement of capital; registered office; SIC codes. Post-ECCTA-2023, every director + PSC must verify identity with Companies House or via ACSP (legal requirement from 18 November 2025 for new appointments + 12-month transition window for existing roles per §11.A). Verified-identity data is largely withheld from public inspection per ECCTA s.69 — but the FACT of verification is public + the personal code is permanent. Public-register visibility is a net loss of privacy vs personal-name holding (no PSC equivalent register) or vs a discretionary trust (where TRS disclosure is more limited per §22.16).

12. **Q: What happens when I want to wind up the FIC or sell?**
    A: Two exit paths, both expensive. (a) Members' voluntary liquidation under IA 1986 Part IV — distribution-in-specie of property to shareholders is a CGT market-value disposal; CGT at the shareholder's rate (18% / 24% per §5) PLUS SDLT may apply on the deemed transfer from FIC to shareholder per FA 2003 s.53 connected-party rules (depending on consideration and Sch 7 group-relief availability); substantial CGT + SDLT crystallisation event. (b) Share-sale to third party — CGT on each shareholder's gain at 18% / 24%; no SBA / BADR for investment FICs. Minority-shareholder gridlock: where a family member holds a minority growth-share and refuses to consent to winding-up or sale, the controlling shareholder cannot force exit without expensive litigation or unfair-prejudice petition under CA 2006 s.994. Articles drag-along provisions help but are not absolute.

13. **Q: Does HMRC scrutinise FICs more aggressively than other structures?**
    A: Yes, in practice. HMRC ran a dedicated FIC Unit between April 2019 and 2021 within the Wealthy and Mid-Sized Business Compliance Directorate; the unit's review concluded that FICs were not being used as significant tax-avoidance vehicles, but the unit's enquiry vectors (settlements re-characterisation under s.624, substance-over-form on dividend extraction, GROB challenges on founder-occupied property under FA 1986 s.102B, Ramsay + GAAR challenges on incorporation arrangements) continue under the Wealthy Team caseload. Heightened scrutiny means more enquiries, more inspections, more documentation requests, more cost on the family side over the planning horizon. See companion page B16 `fic-campaign` for the FIC Campaign deep-dive.

14. **Q: What would happen if I did NOT set up the FIC — what is the counterfactual cost?**
    A: Keeping properties in personal name carries different costs: s.24 mortgage interest restriction at the basic-rate-tax-credit cap; CGT exposure on lifetime gifts to children OR death-time CGT rebase under TCGA 1992 s.62(1); no SDLT incorporation cost; no annual maintenance cost; full personal CGT allowance + personal income-tax bands available on rental income; no s.455 + no dividend-tax stack. For some landlords — particularly basic-rate-band, single-property, sub-£2m-portfolio — the do-nothing counterfactual outperforms the FIC route over a 20-year planning horizon. For others — higher-rate-band, multi-property, multi-generation succession planning, large-scale leveraged portfolio — the FIC may still win on net. The decision pillar `family-investment-company-property-worth-it` walks the balanced cost-benefit; this page provides the negative-only counter-list to weigh against the positives.
