# Gift with Reservation of Benefit: What It Is, Why the Rule Exists, and What It Means for Property Owners

## Statutory anchor

- Primary: **Finance Act 1986 s.102** — verified verbatim at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1986/41/section/102 on 2026-05-26.
  - **s.102(1):** the section applies where an individual disposes of property by way of gift on or after **28 March 1986** and either (a) possession and enjoyment of the property is not bona fide assumed by the donee at or before the beginning of the **relevant period**, or (b) at any time in the relevant period the property is not enjoyed to the entire exclusion, or virtually to the entire exclusion, of the donor and of any benefit to him by contract or otherwise.
  - **The "relevant period":** the seven years ending with the donor's death, OR where the gift is less than seven years before death, the period from the gift to death.
  - **s.102(2):** where (a) or (b) is satisfied, the property is treated as **property subject to a reservation**.
  - **s.102(3):** if subject to a reservation immediately before the donor's death, the property is treated for IHT purposes as **property to which the donor was beneficially entitled** — i.e. it falls back into the donor's death estate at the death market value.
  - **s.102(4):** if the reservation ceases before death (e.g. donor moves out, or starts paying full market rent), the donor is treated as making a fresh PET on the date the reservation ceased; the seven-year clock restarts from that date for that fresh PET.
- Primary (the property-specific extension, in force from 9 March 1999): **Finance Act 1986 s.102A** — "Gifts with reservation: interest in land". Verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1986/41/section/102A on 2026-05-26. Treats an interest in land as subject to a reservation if the donor (or spouse / civil partner) enjoys a **"significant right or interest"** or is party to a **"significant arrangement"** in relation to the land. A right is **significant** if it entitles the donor to occupy or enjoy part of the land otherwise than for full consideration in money or money's worth. A right is **not significant** if granted or acquired more than seven years before the gift.
- Primary (the undivided-share family-home carve-out): **Finance Act 1986 s.102B** — "Gifts with reservation: share of interest in land". Verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1986/41/section/102B on 2026-05-26.
  - Applies to gifts on or after 9 March 1999 of an **undivided share** of an interest in land.
  - The share is treated as subject to a reservation EXCEPT where: (s.102B(3)) the donor does not occupy the land, or occupies it to the exclusion of the donee for full consideration; OR (s.102B(4)) **both donor and donee occupy the land AND the donor does not receive any benefit, other than a negligible one, provided by or at the expense of the donee for some reason connected with the gift**.
- Primary (the carve-out for limited family-or-charity-or-political gifts under FA 1986 Sch 20 para 6): the 1986 reservation rule carves out specific gift categories — to spouse / civil partner (already exempt under IHTA 1984 s.18); to charity (s.23); to political party (s.24); to maintenance funds for historic buildings (s.27); for national purposes (s.25). The carve-outs do not save the family-home parent-to-child case, which is the highest-volume GROB pattern.
- Primary (the back-stop where GROB does not apply but the donor still has benefit — Pre-Owned Assets Tax): **Finance Act 2004 ss.84 + Sch 15 (POAT)** — an annual income-tax-style charge based on deemed market rent, applied where a person enjoys property they previously owned (or that they funded), and where neither GROB under FA 1986 s.102 nor the normal income/CGT charges apply. POAT is the IHT-substitute revenue mechanism for arrangements that engineer their way around s.102. **IHT500 election** allows the donor to elect to be treated as making a GROB (and pay no POAT income tax) — useful where the donor expects to die within 7 years and wants the IHT outcome rather than the annual income-tax outcome.
- Supporting (the IHT framework that GROB sits within):
  - **IHTA 1984 s.4** — chargeable transfer on death.
  - **IHTA 1984 s.5** — meaning of estate.
  - **IHTA 1984 s.3A** — potentially exempt transfers (PETs); the contrast with GROB-tainted gifts is the structural point of the page.
  - **IHTA 1984 s.7 (Sch 2 + s.7(4)) taper relief** — relevant for explaining what a clean PET would have looked like, by contrast.
- House position references: **§15.2** (PETs, CLTs, 7-year clock — GROB's structural context); **§15.3** (GROB family-home, locked); **§22.11** (GROB family-home depth under FA 1986 s.102 + s.102A + s.102B, locked 2026-05-23 with the Wave 6 §22 extension); **§22.15** (do-not-write list for GROB drift catches). The page sits as the entry-tier upstream of the §22.11 statutory depth.

**Mini-illustration: the classic family-home GROB case — the Holloway-family case**

Persona: Holloway-family-parent (mother, widowed, 76). House value £500,000. Two adult children, both with own homes. Daughter approaches mother in March 2026 with a plan: "Mum, give us the house now while you're well — start the seven-year clock — and just carry on living here. The accountant friend says it works." Mother agrees, executes a deed of gift to the children jointly, continues to live in the house rent-free.

The GROB outcome (mother dies in November 2031, 5 years and 8 months later — within the 7-year relevant period):
- The "gift" satisfies FA 1986 s.102(1)(b): the property is NOT enjoyed to the entire exclusion of the donor for the relevant period (mother continued to occupy without paying full market rent).
- Per s.102(2), the property is treated as subject to a reservation.
- Per s.102(3), at death the property is treated as property to which mother was beneficially entitled — i.e. the £500,000 (death market value) falls back into her death estate as if no gift had occurred.
- IHT impact: NRB £325k + RNRB £175k = £500k exemption potentially covers the house value alone — but if she also has other assets (savings, investments, BTL), those exhaust the allowances and the £500k house value adds £200,000 IHT (40% × £500k) before other planning. The 2031 RNRB taper position (from £2m estate) becomes critical.
- The seven-year clock that the "plan" was supposed to start never started — the gift was not a clean PET because of the retained benefit.

**The three exit routes that would have worked (had mother and family planned properly):**

(a) **Full market rent route.** Mother starts paying the children full open-market rent (e.g. £18,000/year for a £500k home — verifiable against local rental comparables). Rent is paid by bank standing order, monthly. Children declare the rental income on self-assessment. Within ~12 months of execution + rent flow, mother has effectively ceased the "significant right or interest" under s.102A; the gift can re-classify as a clean PET starting the 7-year clock from the date the reservation ceased per s.102(4). Cost: ~£18,000/year cash flow, plus children's tax on rental income.

(b) **Cease occupation entirely.** Mother moves out (to live with children, into care, or into a smaller property). s.102(4) treats the cessation as a fresh PET starting from move-out date. 7-year clock starts from then. No rent required. Practical fit: works when mother is willing to move; doesn't work for the "stay in the home" objective that motivated the gift in the first place.

(c) **s.102B(4) shared-occupation undivided-share route.** Mother gifts an undivided 50% share (not the whole house) to the children. Both mother AND children must genuinely occupy as their joint home. Children must move in. Mother receives no benefit from children connected to the gift (no rent from children, no contribution to bills above their own share, no household-services provision). This route is narrow and works in genuine multi-generational living arrangements; it does NOT work for the typical case where children have their own homes and don't actually move in.

## FAQ draft (10-12 entries, Stage 2 populated; RUN sub-agent polishes prose)

1. **What is a gift with reservation of benefit?**
   A gift with reservation of benefit (GROB) is a gift where the donor keeps using or benefiting from the asset after the formal title transfer. Per FA 1986 s.102, where the donor reserves a benefit, the asset is treated for IHT as still in their estate at death — the gift is illusory for IHT purposes notwithstanding title-transfer formalities. The classic case: a parent "gives" the family home to children but continues living there rent-free.

2. **Why does the GROB rule exist?**
   Section 102 was enacted in Finance Act 1986 to close a long-running estate-planning device. Before 1986, donors routinely gifted assets they continued to enjoy (family homes, holiday cottages, art collections) and claimed the IHT saving while keeping the use. HMRC's policy response was to deem such "gifts" as not real gifts for IHT — if you keep the benefit, you keep the IHT exposure. The rule applies to all gifts made on or after 28 March 1986.

3. **What does "reservation of benefit" actually mean in practice?**
   Two tests per s.102(1): either (a) possession and enjoyment is not bona fide assumed by the donee, or (b) the property is not enjoyed to the entire exclusion (or virtually the entire exclusion) of the donor for the relevant period. The second test is the operative one for property cases — any continuing use, rent-free occupation, or material benefit (an annual stay, free management, a fee waiver) triggers the rule.

4. **What is the "relevant period"?**
   The seven years ending with the donor's death, or where the gift was less than seven years before death, the period from the gift to death. If the reservation ceases within the relevant period (e.g. donor moves out, or starts paying full market rent), s.102(4) treats the cessation as a fresh PET starting from the cessation date — the seven-year clock restarts from then for that PET to potentially become exempt.

5. **What happens at death if a gift is still subject to a reservation?**
   Per s.102(3), the property is treated as property to which the donor was beneficially entitled at death, i.e. it falls back into the death estate at the death market value. IHT is charged on the death value at the rate appropriate to the estate (40% above NRB + reliefs; 36% reduced rate where ≥10% to qualifying charity per Sch 1A). The donee's title is unaffected — they still own the asset — but the IHT charge falls on the estate (or on the donee under the secondary liability mechanics).

6. **What are the three statutory exit routes for family-home gifts?**
   (a) Donor pays full open-market rent to donees for ongoing occupation — must be commercial, paid in cash, regularly, with documentary record; informal or below-market rent re-triggers GROB. (b) Donor ceases occupation entirely — moves out and does not return as a regular occupier. (c) s.102B(4) undivided-share shared-occupation — donor gifts an undivided share (e.g. 50%); both donor and donee genuinely occupy as their joint home; donor receives no benefit from donee related to the gift. Each route is narrow; most attempted "workarounds" fail on the detail.

7. **What is Pre-Owned Assets Tax (POAT)?**
   FA 2004 Sch 15 introduced POAT as the income-tax back-stop where an arrangement engineers its way around GROB. Where a person enjoys property they previously owned (or funded), and where neither GROB nor normal income/CGT charges apply, POAT imposes an annual income-tax-style charge based on deemed market rent. Common POAT targets: sale-at-undervalue-plus-loanback structures, discretionary trust mechanisms that nominally exclude the donor, "trust then loan" schemes. The donor can elect via Form IHT500 to be treated as making a GROB instead — useful where the donor expects to die within 7 years and prefers the IHT outcome over an annual income-tax bill.

8. **Does GROB apply to BTL gifts to children?**
   Often. If the donor continues to manage the BTL, take a fee or commission, retain rights of occupation, receive rental income directly into a personal account, or benefit from the use of the property, s.102 is triggered. A pure transfer with the donor stepping completely away from the asset — children manage, children receive rent, children make all decisions — is the only clean route. Even informal "I'll keep an eye on the tenants for you" management arrangements risk creating a benefit. Document the donor's complete cessation of involvement at the gift date.

9. **What if my friend's accountant says they have a workaround?**
   Be cautious. Most claimed workarounds either (a) trigger POAT (the income-tax back-stop), (b) fail the s.102A "significant right" test that closed many pre-1999 planning structures, or (c) involve trust mechanics that bring their own settlor-interested and POAT exposures. The three statutory exit routes above are the genuinely-working routes; everything else carries material risk and should be reviewed by a tax adviser who is prepared to put their analysis in writing.

10. **How does GROB interact with the seven-year PET rule?**
    They are mutually exclusive. A clean PET (no reservation) starts the seven-year clock at gift date and becomes exempt at year-seven (with taper from year-three under s.7(4) and Sch 2). A GROB-tainted gift never starts the clock for IHT purposes — the asset stays in the death estate notwithstanding the gift. If the reservation ceases during the relevant period, a fresh PET starts from the cessation date per s.102(4) — but only that fresh PET runs the seven-year clock, not the original gift.

11. **What did the Ingram case do?**
    *Ingram v IRC* [1999] UKHL 47 was a House of Lords decision where the donor reserved a leasehold interest in a gifted freehold. The Lords held the arrangement was NOT a reservation of benefit under the s.102 wording as it then stood. Parliament's immediate response was Finance Act 1999, inserting s.102A to close the leasehold-carve-out route specifically. This is why s.102A "significant right or interest" exists — to plug the Ingram gap for land gifts on or after 9 March 1999. Many post-Ingram pre-1999 planning structures remain validly grandfathered, but no new structure can use that route.
