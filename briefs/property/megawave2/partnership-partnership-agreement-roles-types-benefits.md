# Partnership and Partnership Agreement: Roles, Types and Benefits for UK Property Partnerships (2026/27)

## Statutory anchor

- **Primary (the base partnership statute):** Partnership Act 1890 (PA 1890) — establishes the general partnership framework (per §11.C parent lock). **Critical for this page:** s.24 — "rules as to interests and duties of partners subject to special agreement". s.24 sets out the NINE statutory default rules that govern a partnership IN THE ABSENCE OF a contrary agreement; these defaults are commonly inappropriate for typical property partnership setups and are the reason a written partnership agreement is operationally essential. **The s.24 defaults are the load-bearing point of this page** — many partnerships operate without an agreement and unknowingly trigger the defaults that work against them.
- **Primary (the s.24 nine defaults — the operative content):**
  - **s.24(1):** all partners entitled to share equally in capital and profits, and must contribute equally toward losses — regardless of actual capital contributions. Operative for property partnerships where one partner contributed the deposit and another the management time — DEFAULT TREATS THEM EQUALLY despite unequal contributions.
  - **s.24(2):** partnership must indemnify partner for payments + personal liabilities incurred in ordinary and proper conduct of partnership business. Operationally sensible default; commonly retained.
  - **s.24(3):** partner who advances money beyond agreed capital is entitled to interest at 5% per annum from the date of advance. Operationally low rate; commonly varied.
  - **s.24(4):** partner is NOT entitled to interest on capital before profits are ascertained. Operationally restrictive; commonly varied.
  - **s.24(5):** every partner may take part in management of the business. **Critical for property partnership:** sleeping partner setups need s.24(5) DISPLACED — otherwise the sleeping partner has statutory management rights they may not be exercising.
  - **s.24(6):** no partner entitled to remuneration for acting in the partnership business — i.e., NO SALARY for managing partner by default. Operationally typically displaced where one partner contributes significantly more time / management.
  - **s.24(7):** no new partner may be admitted without consent of ALL existing partners. Operational gating; commonly varied to majority or supermajority decision.
  - **s.24(8):** ordinary matters connected with partnership business may be decided by majority; NO CHANGE in nature of partnership business without consent of ALL partners. Operationally rigid — single dissenting partner can veto business-type changes (e.g., expanding from residential BTL into commercial property requires unanimous consent under s.24(8) by default).
  - **s.24(9):** partnership books to be kept at the principal place of business and every partner has access. Operationally standard; commonly retained.
- **Primary (partnership-formation default duration):** PA 1890 s.32 + s.33 + s.26 — partnership at will (no fixed duration) is dissolvable by any partner's notice (s.26). **Critical:** default termination on notice means a single partner can dissolve the entire partnership unilaterally — this is the single most operationally-dangerous default for property partnerships, particularly when the partnership has long-lived assets (residential portfolio with mortgages that would need refinancing on dissolution). A written agreement specifying minimum-duration / continuation-on-death / continuation-on-retirement / buyout mechanics is essential to displace s.26.
- **Primary (partnership-formation default termination on partner events):** PA 1890 s.33(1) — partnership is dissolved by death or bankruptcy of any partner (subject to contrary agreement). Operational catastrophe for property partnerships — death of a partner triggers automatic dissolution under the default; written agreement commonly provides for continuation with deceased partner's estate paid out per buyout formula.
- **Primary (the duty of good faith — load-bearing for written-agreement context):** PA 1890 ss.28-30 — duties of partners to render true accounts and full information (s.28); to account for benefit derived from any transaction concerning the partnership without consent (s.29); not to compete with the partnership (s.30). **Critical:** these duties are NOT displaceable to the extent of fundamental good faith but CAN be modified by agreement for specific conflicts (e.g., a partner who also operates a separate property portfolio outside the partnership may want a written carve-out from s.30).
- **Primary (the holding-out liability — important for partner-type architecture):** PA 1890 s.14 — a person who represents themselves (or knowingly allows themselves to be represented) as a partner is liable to the third party as if they were a partner. **Operative for sleeping partner setups:** sleeping partner whose name appears on the partnership letterhead, website, or lease as a partner is liable for partnership debts to third parties relying on the representation, EVEN IF the agreement says they have no management role.
- **Primary (PA 1890 s.2 negative tests — partnership existence boundary):** PA 1890 s.2(1) joint property / co-ownership negative + s.2(2) sharing gross returns negative + s.2(3) profit-share prima-facie rule with six rebuttal heads (per §11.C.2 + §11.C.3 lock). Cross-reference A7 M2-A-B2 for the partnership-existence definitional layer; this page sits ABOVE A7 for setups that ARE partnerships.
- **Primary (partnership types — the entity-choice fork):**
  - **General partnership (PA 1890):** unlimited joint-and-several liability for all partners; tax-transparent; no Companies House filing; PA 1890 default applies subject to written agreement. Cross-reference A7 M2-A-B2 for the definitional layer.
  - **Limited partnership (LP — LPA 1907):** at least one general partner with unlimited liability + at least one limited partner with liability limited to capital contributed; limited partner CANNOT participate in management (otherwise loses limited-liability protection per LPA 1907 s.6(1)); Companies House registration required (post-ECCTA Part 2 expanded per §11.B lock); tax-transparent. Cross-reference A3 M2-A-B1 `companies-house-changes-limited-partnership-requirements` for the LP-specific compliance / ECCTA Part 2 reforms.
  - **Limited Liability Partnership (LLP — LLPA 2000):** body corporate at law (s.1(2)) but transparent for income tax (ITTOIA 2005 s.863); members' liability limited to agreed amounts (LLPA 2000 s.1(5)); members can participate in management (no LP s.6(1) restriction); Companies House registration + annual accounts + designated members required. Cross-reference siblings `llp-accounts` + `llp-and-taxation-benefits` (this batch) for accounts-side and tax-side handbooks; §11.C.Z LLP accounts lock + §11.C.X salaried-member + §11.C.Y mixed-membership locks for sub-regime depth.
  - **Corporate partner:** any of general partnership / LP / LLP can have a body-corporate member (LtdCo). Operationally common in property contexts (hybrid LLP with corporate member) but anti-avoidance regimes (mixed-membership rules ITA 2007 ss.850C-E per §11.C.Y) systematically dismantle the typical income-splitting motivation. Cross-reference A10 M2-A-B2 `hybrid-limited-liability-partnership` for the hybrid-LLP depth.
- **Primary (partner roles — the operative architecture):**
  - **Managing partner / senior partner:** partner with primary day-to-day management responsibility; may receive salary (s.24(6) displaced) + weighted profit share above pari passu; commonly a designated member in an LLP.
  - **Sleeping / dormant partner:** partner who contributes capital but no management; CRITICAL — needs s.24(5) management rights displaced AND s.14 holding-out exposure managed via letterhead / website / lease-naming conventions.
  - **Salaried partner:** partner who receives a salary (or salary-equivalent) regardless of profits; in an LLP, exposure to the salaried-member rules ITA 2007 ss.863A-G (per §11.C.X lock + cross-reference A11 M2-A-B2) — Conditions A/B/C analysis determines whether treated as employee for PAYE + NIC.
  - **Junior partner:** partner with fixed share or capped share; commonly entry-level partner on a path to senior partnership; commonly first to be removed under buyout mechanics.
  - **Limited partner (LP only):** capital contribution + liability limited to contributed amount; NO MANAGEMENT (LPA 1907 s.6(1)).
  - **Corporate partner / member:** body-corporate member; common for FIC-overlay setups (FIC as corporate member of family LLP) or external-investor setups.
- **Primary (partnership agreement key clauses — operational checklist):**
  - **Capital contributions** — initial and ongoing; interest on capital (displacing s.24(4)).
  - **Profit-sharing ratios** — including how profits are divided (equal / proportionate to capital / per agreement formula) (displacing s.24(1) equal default).
  - **Drawings + remuneration** — monthly drawings entitlements; partner salaries (displacing s.24(6)); year-end balancing.
  - **Loan accounts** — partner advances beyond capital and interest thereon (displacing s.24(3)).
  - **Decision-making + voting** — what requires unanimity / supermajority / simple majority (displacing s.24(7) + s.24(8)).
  - **Management roles + responsibilities** — including sleeping partner architecture (displacing s.24(5) for sleeping partners).
  - **Admission of new partners** — process + thresholds + buy-in mechanics.
  - **Retirement / death / removal of partners** — buyout formula (commonly NAV per accounts or independent valuation); continuation on partner events (displacing s.33(1) default dissolution on death/bankruptcy); notice periods; restrictive covenants post-exit.
  - **Dissolution** — circumstances triggering dissolution; mechanics; surviving-partner buyout vs sale-of-business; tax-side impact (s.59 / s.59A CGT fractional disposals on dissolution + SP D12 treatment per §11.C.7).
  - **Confidentiality + non-compete + non-solicitation** — modifying s.30 in specific bona-fide-commercial ways.
  - **Dispute resolution** — mediation / arbitration / court; commonly important for family property partnerships.
  - **Bank mandates + signing authorities** — operational delegation framework.
- **Primary (tax-transparency framework for general partnerships and LLPs):** ITTOIA 2005 Part 9 ss.846-863 — partnership tax-transparency (per §11.C.6 lock). ITA 2007 s.852 (notional trade for each partner). LLP-specific transparency under ITTOIA 2005 s.863. TCGA 1992 s.59 (general partnerships) + s.59A (LLPs) — CGT transparency (per §11.C.7). SP D12 (HMRC Statement of Practice on Partnership CGT).
- **Primary (the SDLT partnership relief gating — operative cross-link):** FA 2003 Sch 15 — partnership-SDLT regime applies to general partnerships, LPs, and LLPs alike (per §11.C.8 + §1.A lock). Sch 15 SLP relief on partnership-to-LtdCo incorporation depends on para 1 business gate being met. Cross-reference existing `partnership-sdlt-relief-schedule-15-fa-2003-incorporation-sum-lower-proportions` + A7 M2-A-B2 for para 1 analysis.
- **House position reference:** **§11.C (partnership statutory architecture parent — LIVE)**; §11.C.X salaried-member sub-lock; §11.C.Y mixed-membership sub-lock; §11.C.Z LLP accounts sub-lock; §11.B ECCTA Part 2 + LP reforms (LIVE); §11.A ECCTA Part 1; §1.A SDLT partnership; §21 (Ltd Co entity-choice anchor); §22 IHT side. **No NEW HP lock requested** — this page consumes the §11.C cluster + §11.B as its primary architecture.

## Worked-example data (RUN session uses these as canvas)

### Example 1 — s.24(1) default disaster (the load-bearing worked example)

**Mawell-Estate Sibling Partnership:** Two siblings (Mr A Mawell-Estate + Mr B Mawell-Estate) form a partnership to acquire and let a £400,000 residential BTL portfolio. Mr A contributes £200,000 deposit (mortgaged for the £200k balance). Mr B contributes £0 deposit but commits to day-to-day management (tenant relationships, maintenance, rent collection, accounts). **No written partnership agreement.**

- **What they expect:** Mr A entitled to interest / return on his £200k capital exposure; Mr B entitled to remuneration for his management time; profit split weighted to reflect capital + labour contributions.
- **What PA 1890 s.24 actually delivers:**
  - s.24(1): profits split **EQUALLY** (50/50) regardless of capital exposure.
  - s.24(4): Mr A entitled to NO interest on his £200k capital before profits ascertained.
  - s.24(6): Mr B entitled to NO salary for his management time.
- **Year-1 reality:** £30,000 net rental profit. Each sibling gets £15,000 per s.24(1). Mr A's effective return on £200k capital: 7.5% gross, before he pays HRT income tax on his £15k share. Mr B's effective return on zero capital: infinite (no capital exposure). Mr A justifiably feels the split is unfair; Mr B points to s.24(1) and says the default is the default.
- **Family dispute escalation:** without written agreement, neither can prove agreed terms; resolution requires either negotiated ex-post written agreement (expensive when relationships are strained) or litigation.
- **What a written agreement would have done:** specified capital contributions; provided interest on differential capital under s.24(3) variation; provided Mr B salary for management work under s.24(6) variation; split profits per agreed formula (e.g., 60/40 to Mr A reflecting capital, or 50/50 after salary + interest).

### Example 2 — s.26 default termination disaster

**Kapoor Property Partnership:** Three partners (Mr + Mrs Kapoor + Mr Singh-business-associate) hold a £1.5m residential BTL portfolio with £900k of mortgages across the properties. No written agreement specifying minimum duration or notice periods.

- **Year-3 event:** Mr Singh-business-associate suffers personal financial difficulty and serves notice of dissolution under PA 1890 s.26.
- **Partnership dissolves immediately.** Mortgages potentially trigger lender notification requirements (partnership-banked facilities + change of borrower's identity creates re-underwriting risk + potentially-payable fees). Property titles may need restructuring at HMLR if held as partnership property. CGT crystallisation issues on partnership-asset disposals per TCGA 1992 s.59 + SP D12.
- **The Kapoors lose control:** they cannot keep the partnership going without Mr Singh's consent post-dissolution; he can demand sale of partnership assets to realise his fractional interest in cash; their long-term portfolio plans collapse.
- **What a written agreement would have done:** specified minimum duration (e.g., 10 years); specified notice periods (e.g., 12 months written notice); specified continuation-on-dissolving-partner-departure (the remaining partners buy out the dissolving partner per agreement formula, partnership continues); preserved the mortgage facilities + property structure + family operational plans.

### Example 3 — s.33(1) death-dissolution disaster

**Patel Family Partnership:** 4 family members (parents + 2 adult children) operate a 7-property residential portfolio as a general partnership.

- **Year-5 event:** father dies unexpectedly. **PA 1890 s.33(1) automatic dissolution** triggers (subject to contrary agreement; none exists).
- **Cascade consequences:**
  - Partnership dissolves; assets must be valued + wound up under partnership-dissolution rules.
  - CGT crystallisation: each surviving member treated as disposing of their fractional share to the dissolved-partnership pool per TCGA 1992 s.59 + SP D12; gains accrue at member level immediately on dissolution event.
  - IHT exposure: father's fractional share included in his estate at market value; family's residence-nil-rate band + other reliefs interact.
  - Mortgages: lender notification + re-underwriting needed on dissolved-partnership-to-new-structure transition.
- **What a written continuation clause would have done:** father's share automatically transferred to surviving partners per agreement formula (e.g., NAV at last accounts); his estate paid out in cash or via promissory note over agreed period; partnership continues without dissolution; CGT only on actual subsequent disposals; IHT exposure still arises but operates against an undissolved business interest.

### Example 4 — s.14 holding-out disaster

**Singh Family Partnership:** Mrs Singh contributes capital but takes no management role; she is the "sleeping partner". The partnership letterhead names her: "Singh Property Partnership — Partners: Mr A Singh, Mr B Singh, Mrs Singh". The website carries the same listing. A commercial lease lists all three as landlords.

- **Year-2 event:** the partnership defaults on a £40,000 supplier invoice for property refurbishment. The supplier sues all three named partners.
- **Mrs Singh's defence:** she is a sleeping partner with no management role under the internal LLP agreement.
- **The court's response:** PA 1890 s.14 — Mrs Singh held herself out (or knowingly allowed herself to be held out) as a partner via the letterhead, website, and lease. The supplier relied on the representation. Mrs Singh is liable for the partnership debt as if she were an active partner. The internal agreement does not protect against third-party holding-out exposure.
- **What a careful sleeping-partner architecture would have done:** kept Mrs Singh's name OFF the letterhead, website, and external documents; clearly designated her as a "capital partner" not naming her in any management-implying way; ensured external counterparties did not rely on her name when extending credit; used different document conventions for active vs passive partners.

### Example 5 — The benefits of formalising via written agreement (positive case)

**Patel Property LLP:** 4-member family LLP with carefully-drafted LLP agreement covering all key clauses. Father retires.

- **Agreement-driven continuation:** father's capital + accumulated profit share calculated per NAV per agreement formula; paid out over 3 years via promissory note carrying interest at 4% per annum (agreed at agreement-execution date).
- **CGT minimisation:** because the partnership continues post-father's-departure without dissolution, no immediate CGT crystallisation under s.59A; father's CGT only on his actual fractional-share disposal value; surviving members continue holding their fractional shares with the same base costs.
- **Salaried-member analysis (§11.C.X cross-reference):** active LLP members' capital contributions checked against Condition C 25%-of-disguised-salary threshold pre-retirement; father's retirement doesn't disturb others' Condition C status because their capital balances are separate.
- **Mixed-membership analysis (§11.C.Y cross-reference):** the LLP has no corporate member; mixed-membership rules don't apply. If the family adds a FIC as corporate member later, the agreement provides for advance modelling against the §11.C.Y excess + power-to-enjoy tests.
- **Family relationships preserved:** father's exit handled per agreed terms; no litigation; no late-stage negotiation under stress; family Christmas dinner remains pleasant.
- **Operative conclusion:** the written agreement cost ~£5,000-£10,000 in legal fees at execution; it saved tens of thousands in tax-side consequences + immeasurable family-relationship value at the retirement event.

## FAQ expansion (RUN session polishes prose; 12 FAQs target)

1. **Q: Why does a property partnership need a written agreement?**
   A: Because the Partnership Act 1890 sets nine default rules in section 24 that govern partnerships in the absence of a written agreement, plus a default rule at section 26 allowing any partner to dissolve a partnership-at-will on notice and a default rule at section 33(1) automatically dissolving the partnership on death or bankruptcy of any partner. These defaults are almost always inappropriate for property partnerships with capital contributors, management contributors, long-lived assets with mortgages, and family succession plans. A written agreement displaces the defaults with operationally appropriate terms.

2. **Q: What are the PA 1890 s.24 nine defaults?**
   A: (1) equal profit + loss share regardless of capital; (2) indemnity for partner's costs in ordinary partnership business; (3) interest at 5% on partner advances beyond agreed capital; (4) no interest on capital before profits ascertained; (5) every partner may take part in management; (6) no salary for any partner; (7) unanimous consent required to admit new partners; (8) majority vote on ordinary matters but unanimity for any change in business nature; (9) partnership books at principal place of business with all-partner access. Of these, (1), (4), (5), (6), (7), and (8) are typically displaced by property partnership agreements.

3. **Q: What happens if a property partnership has no written agreement?**
   A: All nine s.24 defaults apply, plus s.26 (dissolution by any partner's notice) and s.33(1) (automatic dissolution on death / bankruptcy). For a typical property partnership this means: profit shares equal regardless of capital contributions; no salary for the managing partner; any partner can dissolve the partnership on short notice; death of any partner dissolves the partnership and triggers wind-up. Disputes are hard to resolve without a contractual baseline; CGT and IHT consequences crystallise at dissolution events that could have been avoided.

4. **Q: What is the s.14 holding-out exposure?**
   A: Per PA 1890 s.14, a person who represents themselves as a partner (or knowingly allows themselves to be represented as a partner) is liable to third parties relying on the representation as if they were a partner. **Critical for sleeping partner setups:** if a sleeping partner is named on the partnership letterhead, website, lease, or other external document, they may be liable for partnership debts to suppliers, tenants, lenders, or contractors who relied on the representation, EVEN IF the internal partnership agreement says they have no management role. The internal agreement does not protect against third-party holding-out exposure; only external naming conventions do.

5. **Q: What is the difference between a general partnership, an LP, and an LLP?**
   A: **General partnership (PA 1890):** all partners have unlimited joint-and-several liability; no Companies House filing; tax-transparent. **Limited partnership (LP — LPA 1907):** at least one general partner with unlimited liability + at least one limited partner with liability limited to capital contributed; the limited partner CANNOT take part in management or loses the limited-liability protection per LPA 1907 s.6(1); Companies House registration required (post-ECCTA Part 2 reforms per §11.B). **Limited Liability Partnership (LLP — LLPA 2000):** body corporate at law but transparent for income tax; all members have limited liability; all members can take part in management; Companies House registration + annual accounts + designated members required. For property partnerships, LLP is typically the most operationally flexible; LP is rare; general partnership is the default for small / family setups without limited-liability needs.

6. **Q: What partner roles are available?**
   A: Six common roles. **Managing partner / senior partner** — primary day-to-day management; salary + weighted profit share. **Sleeping / dormant partner** — capital contribution, no management; needs careful s.24(5) + s.14 management. **Salaried partner** — receives salary regardless of profits; in an LLP, exposed to salaried-member rules per §11.C.X. **Junior partner** — fixed or capped share; entry-level. **Limited partner (LP only)** — capital + liability limited; no management permitted under LPA 1907 s.6(1). **Corporate partner / member** — body-corporate member, common for FIC overlays + external investors; exposed to mixed-membership rules per §11.C.Y in LLP context.

7. **Q: What clauses should a property partnership agreement contain?**
   A: Twelve operational categories. (1) Capital contributions + interest on capital. (2) Profit-sharing ratios. (3) Drawings + remuneration + year-end balancing. (4) Loan accounts + interest. (5) Decision-making + voting thresholds. (6) Management roles + responsibilities (including sleeping-partner architecture). (7) Admission of new partners. (8) Retirement, death, removal + buyout formulae + continuation. (9) Dissolution circumstances and mechanics. (10) Confidentiality + non-compete + non-solicitation. (11) Dispute resolution. (12) Bank mandates + signing authorities. Plus, for LLP-specific architecture: designated member identification per §11.C.Z.6; capital contributions sized for Condition C safe harbour where salaried-member exposure is a concern.

8. **Q: How does the partnership agreement interact with SDLT Sch 15 SLP relief?**
   A: Per FA 2003 Sch 15 + §11.C.8 + §1.A lock, the Sch 15 SLP mechanic on partnership-to-LtdCo incorporation depends on (a) the para 1 business gate being met — the partnership must carry on a business, not merely hold property — and (b) the partnership shares as defined in the partnership agreement. Lower-proportion calculations under para 10 use agreement-defined shares vs LtdCo share allocations. A carefully-drafted agreement aligning partnership shares to intended LtdCo ownership maximises SLP relief; sloppy drafting can dilute or eliminate the relief.

9. **Q: How does the partnership agreement interact with the salaried-member rules?**
   A: The salaried-member rules at ITA 2007 ss.863A-G (per §11.C.X) apply to LLP members. Condition C — the most reliable safe harbour — requires capital contribution ≥ 25% of disguised salary. The LLP agreement is the operational document defining capital contributions; well-drafted agreements size capital contributions to keep active members comfortably above the Condition C threshold + flag the §11.C.X.5 s.863G anti-avoidance against contrived contributions (no round-trip loans; no contributions at no real economic risk).

10. **Q: How does the partnership agreement interact with the mixed-membership rules?**
    A: The mixed-membership rules at ITA 2007 ss.850C-E (per §11.C.Y) apply where an LLP has both individual and corporate members. The three-step test (corporate allocated profit + excess vs arm's-length + power-to-enjoy) reallocates excess profits to individual members. The LLP agreement governs profit-share allocations and is therefore the operational governance layer for managing mixed-membership exposure: contemporaneous documentation of arm's-length commercial rationale for corporate-member allocations; explicit acknowledgment of mixed-membership analysis at LLP-establishment; periodic re-modelling at material change events.

11. **Q: When should an existing partnership agreement be updated?**
    A: On every material event: (a) admission or departure of a partner; (b) significant change in capital or profit-share architecture; (c) ECCTA-related compliance updates for LP (per §11.B) or LLP designated member requirements; (d) tax-rule changes affecting profit-share architecture (dividend rate changes per F-20 §21.4; salaried-member or mixed-membership rule changes); (e) imminent SDLT Sch 15 incorporation event (align agreement shares to intended LtdCo ownership); (f) family succession planning event (death, retirement, generational transfer); (g) property portfolio re-structuring (expansion into new segments triggers s.24(8) consent question under default).

12. **Q: What are the common partnership-agreement mistakes?**
    A: Seven recurring patterns. (1) No written agreement at all — defaults govern with operationally inappropriate outcomes. (2) Generic template copied from online source without property-specific adaptation. (3) Agreement silent on s.33(1) continuation — death of a partner triggers automatic dissolution. (4) Agreement silent on buyout formula — partner-exit disputes have no contractual anchor. (5) LLP agreement that creates inadvertent salaried-member exposure — capital contributions below Condition C threshold for active members. (6) LLP agreement that creates inadvertent mixed-membership exposure — corporate-member allocations not documented against arm's-length test. (7) Agreement that fails to manage s.14 holding-out exposure for sleeping partners — sleeping partner named on external documents.
