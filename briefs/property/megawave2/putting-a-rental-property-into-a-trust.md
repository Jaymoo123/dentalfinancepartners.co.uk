# Putting a Rental Property Into a Trust: UK Landlord Orientation Guide

## Statutory anchor

- Primary (what a trust IS — the legal-mechanic backbone):
  - **Trustee Act 1925** + **Trustee Act 2000** — the principal statutes governing trustee duties, powers, and standards of care in England and Wales. Verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/Geo5/15-16/19 and https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2000/29 on 2026-05-27.
  - **Trustee Act 2000 s.1** — statutory duty of care: a trustee must exercise such care and skill as is reasonable in the circumstances, having particular regard to any special knowledge or experience the trustee has or holds himself out as having (higher standard for professional trustees).
  - **Trustee Act 2000 Part II** — general power of investment + duty to consider standard investment criteria (suitability + diversification) + duty to obtain and consider proper advice.
  - **Law of Property Act 1925 s.53(1)(b)** — verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/Geo5/15-16/20/section/53 on 2026-05-27: "a declaration of trust respecting any land or any interest therein must be manifested and proved by some writing signed by some person who is able to declare such trust or by his will." Trusts of land MUST be in writing — oral arrangements are unenforceable.
  - **TOLATA 1996** (Trusts of Land and Appointment of Trustees Act 1996) — modern framework for trusts where land is held by trustees for beneficiaries; rights of occupation (s.12-13), duties to consult beneficiaries (s.11), application to Court (s.14).
  - **Scottish equivalent:** Trusts (Scotland) Act 2024 (replacing Trusts (Scotland) Act 1921 — Stage 2 verifies commencement architecture); separate trust framework with its own formalities.
- Primary (the IHT classification — the structural fork: CLT vs PET):
  - **IHTA 1984 s.3** — verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1984/51/section/3 on 2026-05-27: a "transfer of value" is a disposition by which the value of the transferor's estate is reduced. The foundational concept.
  - **IHTA 1984 s.3A** — verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1984/51/section/3A on 2026-05-27: "potentially exempt transfer" (PET). A PET is a transfer of value made by an individual on or after 18 March 1986 which would otherwise be chargeable, where the transfer is to another individual, to a bare trust for an individual, or (in limited cases) to a disabled trust. **A PET is NOT chargeable at the time of the gift; becomes fully exempt if the donor survives 7 years; becomes chargeable (with taper relief for years 4-7) if donor dies within 7 years.**
  - **IHTA 1984 s.7(2)** — verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1984/51/section/7 on 2026-05-27: lifetime chargeable transfers (CLTs — transfers into relevant-property trusts) are charged at **half the death rate** — i.e., 20% on the excess over the nil-rate band (£325,000 frozen to 2030 per HP §15.X / FA-2025 extension).
  - **HP §15.3 lock — the structural fork:** transfers into bare trusts and (in limited cases) qualifying disabled trusts are **PETs** (not chargeable on the way in). Transfers into discretionary trusts, post-22 March 2006 interest-in-possession trusts, and most other lifetime-created trusts are **CLTs** — chargeable immediately at 20% on the excess over the NRB, with further 20% on death-within-7-years (40% total less taper).
- Primary (the relevant-property regime — 10-year periodic + exit charges):
  - **IHTA 1984 ss.58-65** — verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1984/51/part/III/chapter/III on 2026-05-27. The "relevant property regime" applies to most discretionary trusts and (post-22 March 2006) most lifetime-created interest-in-possession trusts.
  - **IHTA 1984 s.64** — 10-yearly anniversary periodic charge: trust property is reassessed every 10 years from creation; chargeable at up to 6% (effective rate after notional CLT computation against current NRB) on the value above the available NRB at the anniversary date.
  - **IHTA 1984 s.65** — exit charges: when property exits the relevant-property regime (capital distribution to beneficiary, advancement, appointment out), a proportionate charge based on time-since-last-anniversary (a fraction of the periodic-charge rate, up to 6% pro-rata).
  - **HP §22.13 lock:** the relevant-property regime is the principal IHT cost of using discretionary trusts — the up-to-6%-every-10-years is NOT a one-off; it is a permanent IHT compliance overhead for the life of the trust. The 10-yearly + exit charges interact with NRB headroom; trusts with property valued substantially over the NRB will pay the full 6% periodically; trusts within NRB pay nothing on the periodic but face the entry charge issue (s.7(2) 20% on the way in).
- Primary (the IIP post-2006 classification trap):
  - **IHTA 1984 s.49(1A)** — verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1984/51/section/49 on 2026-05-27 (inserted by FA 2006 Sch 20). Interest-in-possession trusts created on or after 22 March 2006 are NOT treated as the IIP-beneficiary's estate for IHT purposes (i.e., no estate-aggregation on death of the life tenant); they sit inside the relevant-property regime instead — so they too face the s.7(2) 20% entry charge + s.64 10-year + s.65 exit charges.
  - **HP §15.3 lock — common amateur error:** "interest in possession trusts don't have the 20% entry charge" was true PRE-22 March 2006 only. Post-22 March 2006 IIP trusts have the SAME entry charge regime as discretionary trusts (with narrow exceptions: IPDI, transitional serial interest, disabled person's interest).
- Primary (the CGT mechanic on transferring property INTO trust):
  - **TCGA 1992 s.17** — verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12/section/17 on 2026-05-27: gifts (and other transactions between connected persons under s.286) are deemed to be at **market value** for CGT — i.e., a gift of property to a trust is a disposal at market value, triggering CGT on the donor.
  - **TCGA 1992 s.260** — verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12/section/260 on 2026-05-27: holdover relief available where the disposal is **immediately chargeable to IHT** (i.e., a CLT into a relevant-property trust). **Critical structural feature:** s.260 holdover IS available on a gift of residential property into a discretionary trust because the gift is a CLT (immediately chargeable to IHT). The holdover defers CGT to the trustees' acquisition base cost.
  - **TCGA 1992 s.165** — verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12/section/165 on 2026-05-27: holdover for gifts of business assets. Residential rental property is NOT a business asset for s.165 (it is investment property, not trading); s.165 generally NOT available for BTL gifts.
  - **HP §15.3 lock — the structural asymmetry:** gift of residential property to an individual or bare trust is a PET (no IHT on entry) but the donor pays CGT at market value with NO holdover available (s.260 not in point because no immediate IHT charge; s.165 not in point because not business asset). Gift of residential property to a discretionary trust is a CLT (20% IHT entry charge over NRB) with s.260 holdover available (CGT deferred to trustees). **This is the central CGT-vs-IHT trade-off that drives the trust-route decision** — it is NOT "trusts save tax"; it is "trusts trade upfront IHT-entry-cost for CGT-deferral, with periodic IHT cost thereafter."
  - **TCGA 1992 s.62** — verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12/section/62 on 2026-05-27: CGT uplift on death (estate acquires the deceased's assets at probate value, wiping out lifetime gain). **The CGT-uplift-on-death is LOST when the property has been transferred into trust during lifetime** — the trustees' base cost (post-s.260 holdover, the donor's original base cost) carries forward unrelieved. HP §15.1 / §22.2 anchor.
- Primary (the SDLT mechanic on transferring property INTO trust):
  - **FA 2003 Sch 4 para 8** — chargeable consideration for SDLT includes the assumed share of any mortgage debt. Where rental property subject to a mortgage is transferred to a trust and the trustees assume responsibility for the debt (or the donor is released from it), the amount of the debt assumed is chargeable consideration even if no cash changes hands. **Common amateur trap:** "gifting" mortgaged property to a trust triggers SDLT on the assumed mortgage debt.
  - **FA 2003 Sch 4ZA** — additional-dwellings 3% surcharge. **Trustees of a settlement generally take the surcharge in the same way as individuals on the additional-dwellings test** (Sch 4ZA para 6 + para 10 — Stage 2 verifies the precise para for trustee classification); the surcharge is in addition to the standard SDLT rates on the chargeable consideration.
  - **FA 2003 s.55 + Sch 4A** — 15% body-corporate trap on residential property over £500,000 acquired by a non-natural-person (NNP). Trustees are not body-corporate per se, but if the trust owns shares in an SPV that holds the property, the 15% trap is in play for the SPV. HP §22.16 + §6.B anchors.
  - **HP §15.3 / §24.3 lock:** the SDLT-debt-consideration + 3% surcharge stack is the single most-overlooked friction cost when landlords contemplate putting a BTL into trust. A £500,000 BTL with a £300,000 mortgage transferred to a discretionary trust faces: 20% IHT entry charge on the equity over the NRB (or against the NRB if within), SDLT on £300,000 chargeable consideration plus 3% surcharge across the slabs, plus the s.260 deferred CGT (later crystallised on exit or sale), plus 10-yearly periodic IHT.
- Primary (the income-tax mechanic — who pays tax on rents):
  - **ITTOIA 2005 Part 3** — property income provisions; rental income arising to trustees taxed at trust rates (45% for discretionary trusts on income over standard-rate band; 7.5%/8.75%-equivalent on dividends — verify current rates Stage 2).
  - **ITA 2007 s.479-481** — trust rate of income tax for discretionary trusts (45% from 2010 onwards; small standard-rate band of £1,000 split between trusts of same settlor — HP §22.14 anchor).
  - **Bare trust / qualifying IIP:** income tax on rents arises to the beneficiary directly (transparent / look-through), not the trustees — so the beneficiary's marginal rate applies, not the trust rate. This is the principal income-tax-cost difference between bare/IIP and discretionary trusts.
- Primary (the GROB risk — the structural elephant in the room):
  - **FA 1986 s.102** — verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1986/41/section/102 on 2026-05-27: gift with reservation of benefit. If the donor (or someone connected) continues to benefit from the gifted property (e.g., continues to receive rental income, continues to occupy, retains the right to direct trustees), the gift is treated for IHT as if it had not been made — the property is in the donor's estate on death at full value.
  - **FA 2004 Sch 15** — Pre-Owned Assets Tax (POAT): income-tax charge on the donor for the benefit they retain (a fallback charge if GROB doesn't fully apply). HP §22.11 anchor.
  - **HP §22.11 lock:** the most common landlord error is "I put the BTL into a discretionary trust where I am one of the discretionary beneficiaries — so I can still receive rental distributions when I need them." This is a textbook GROB — the property remains in the donor's estate for IHT, AND the 20% CLT entry charge has been paid, AND the SDLT + CGT costs are sunk. The settlor-as-beneficiary structure is the principal GROB trap for landlord trusts.
- Primary (the TRS + RoE compliance overlay):
  - **MLR 2017 (SI 2017/692) reg 45** — Trust Registration Service (TRS). Most express trusts holding UK land must register on TRS within 90 days of becoming registrable; updates within 90 days of changes; annual review. HP §22.16 lock.
  - **ECTEA 2022 + ECCTA 2023** — Register of Overseas Entities (RoE) interaction where a trust holds the property indirectly via an overseas entity. HP §11.A lock (cross-reference to C18).
- House position references: §15.1 (IHT general architecture); §15.3 (lifetime transfers — CLT vs PET fork); §15.4 (BPR/APR — not in point for residential BTL but for completeness); §22.5 (spouse exemption); §22.11 (GROB + POAT); §22.13 (relevant property regime 10-yearly + exit charges); §22.14 (trust income tax rates); §22.16 (TRS compliance); §24 (joint ownership — for the route where the spouse is the natural pre-trust step); §22.X.4 (excluded property trust under LTR — for non-UK-domiciled-or-non-LTR settlors only); §22.2 (CGT uplift on death — lost on trust route); §11.A (RoE + ECCTA — only for overseas-entity-owned property held in trust); §6.B (15% body-corporate trap — for SPV-via-trust structures).

The most common competitor + amateur error is jumping straight into "discretionary trusts have 20% entry charges and 10-year periodic charges" without orienting the reader to what trustees + beneficiaries + relevant property regime even mean. The structural backbone is the **CGT-vs-IHT trade-off fork**: trusts are not "tax-saving vehicles"; they trade upfront IHT-entry-cost for CGT-deferral, with periodic IHT cost thereafter, for the right candidate profile. Pre-empts the most common amateur misconceptions:

- "Putting property into trust avoids inheritance tax" — false. The relevant-property regime imposes 20% lifetime CLT charge + 6% every 10 years + exit charges. Trusts manage IHT timing + jurisdiction, they don't eliminate it.
- "I can put my BTL in trust and still receive the rent — best of both worlds" — false. Textbook GROB under FA 1986 s.102. Property stays in the settlor's estate for IHT AND the entry charges + SDLT + CGT costs have been incurred.
- "Trusts are only for the very wealthy" — partial truth. Trusts ARE typically suited to substantial portfolios because the fixed compliance + advice + ongoing costs are punitive at small scale, but the reasoning is mechanical (proportional cost vs benefit) not class-based.
- "I'll use a bare trust to avoid the 20% entry charge" — partial truth. Bare trusts are PETs (no entry charge), but bare trusts give absolute beneficial ownership to the named beneficiary (typically a child or grandchild) and do NOT provide the asset-protection or distribution-flexibility features that motivate most trust planning. Bare trusts are a specific tool, not a general-purpose trust alternative.
- "Putting BTL in trust gets me the CGT uplift on death" — false. The CGT uplift on death under TCGA 1992 s.62 applies to the deceased's estate. Property transferred to trust during lifetime is NOT in the deceased's estate (that's the point of the gift). The trustees' base cost (post-s.260 holdover, the donor's original base cost) carries forward unrelieved — so the trust route LOSES the CGT uplift.
- "Putting BTL in trust avoids SDLT because there's no purchase" — false. SDLT applies to chargeable consideration, which includes assumed mortgage debt under FA 2003 Sch 4 para 8. A "gift" of mortgaged BTL to trust pays SDLT on the debt assumed plus the 3% additional-dwellings surcharge.
- "Trusts and FICs do the same job" — false. Different vehicles, different tax treatment (FIC is a company taxed at corporation tax; trust is a fiduciary structure taxed at trust rates), different control mechanics, different succession architecture. See FIC-vs-discretionary comparison deep for the side-by-side.
- "I can be the trustee of a trust I created and put my BTL into" — partial truth, with serious caveats. Settlor-as-trustee is permitted, but settlor-as-beneficiary triggers settlor-interested trust rules (income attribution under ITTOIA 2005 s.624; CGT attribution under TCGA 1992 s.169B-G); settlor-as-beneficiary + occupier triggers GROB. See settlor-interested deeps.
