# Maximising Business Relief to Reduce Inheritance Tax

## Statutory anchor

- Primary (the BPR framework):
  - **IHTA 1984 ss.103-114** — Business Property Relief. Verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1984/51 on 2026-05-26.
  - **IHTA 1984 s.104** — relief mechanic. Two rates of relief (post-FA-2025/2026-cap-architecture — see §15.4):
    - **100% relief** below the £1m combined APR+BPR cap (from 6 April 2026) — applies to (a) a business or interest in a business, (b) unquoted securities giving control, (c) unquoted shares.
    - **50% relief** for (a) quoted shares giving control, (b) land/buildings/plant used by a business / settled property in which the deceased had a life interest, (c) for AIM-traded shares (post 6 April 2026 — moved from 100% to 50% per FA 2025/2026 reforms).
    - **Above the £1m cap:** rate drops to 50% on the excess (HP §15.4 lock).
  - **IHTA 1984 s.105** — categories of "relevant business property":
    - s.105(1)(a) — a business or an interest in a business (sole-trader/partnership share).
    - s.105(1)(b) — securities of a company (under s.269 controlled) that gave control to the transferor.
    - s.105(1)(bb) — any unquoted shares in a company.
    - s.105(1)(c) — quoted shares giving control to the transferor.
    - s.105(1)(d) — land/buildings/machinery/plant used wholly or mainly for the purposes of a business carried on by a company controlled by the transferor or by a partnership of which the transferor was a partner.
    - s.105(1)(e) — land/buildings/machinery/plant settled in a trust of which the transferor was entitled to an interest in possession, where used for business purposes.
  - **IHTA 1984 s.105(3)** — the **investment exclusion**. Verified verbatim: "A business or interest in a business … shall not be relevant business property if the business … consists wholly or mainly of one or more of the following, that is to say, dealing in securities, stocks or shares, land or buildings or **making or holding investments**." This is the operative provision that excludes pure BTL portfolios from BPR — the *Pawson v HMRC* line (HP §22.1).
  - **IHTA 1984 s.106** — **minimum 2-year holding period**. Verified verbatim: "Property is not relevant business property in relation to a transfer of value unless it was owned by the transferor throughout the two years immediately preceding the transfer." The 2-year clock is the operational gatekeeper for new acquisitions; a property bought as a trading-business asset 18 months before death does NOT qualify regardless of merits.
  - **IHTA 1984 s.107** — **replacement-property reset rule** (the key planning lever). Verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1984/51/section/107 on 2026-05-26. Where qualifying business property is replaced by other qualifying business property within the prior 3 years, the original 2-year clock is preserved across the replacement — the new property doesn't need its own 2-year clock provided continuous qualification. **Critical for property-business reorganisations:** swapping one trading-business asset for another within 3 years does NOT reset the 2-year clock. Swapping a qualifying asset for a non-qualifying asset (e.g. selling a trading FHL and buying a pure BTL) does break continuity.
  - **IHTA 1984 s.108** — successive transfers. Where business property is transferred between spouses (spouse exemption applies, no chargeable transfer), the receiving spouse inherits the 2-year clock — the clock does NOT reset on inter-spouse transfer (this is a key planning point most amateur content misses).
  - **IHTA 1984 s.109** — successive transfers in 2-year period. Where transferor acquired property by an earlier transfer that itself qualified for BPR, the 2-year clock is preserved.
  - **IHTA 1984 s.110** — value of business — the value of a business means net value (assets minus liabilities). BPR applies to that net value, not gross asset value.
  - **IHTA 1984 s.111** — value of "excepted assets" — value of assets within the business not used for business purposes (cash surplus to business needs, investments held in trading company) is EXCLUDED from BPR. This is the **mixed-purpose company carve-out** — a trading company with substantial surplus cash + investment property loses BPR on the value attributable to the non-trade assets.
  - **IHTA 1984 s.112** — value of excepted assets continued. Detailed application of the carve-out.
  - **IHTA 1984 s.113 + s.113A + s.113B** — **binding contract for sale gateway**. Verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1984/51/section/113 on 2026-05-26. Where the transferor had entered into a binding contract to sell the business / shares before the transfer (typically before death), BPR is denied. This catches the "I'm selling the business; I'll die before completion; my estate inherits the sale proceeds with BPR" plan — closed.
  - **IHTA 1984 s.114** — replacement of business property by property of a different kind.
- Primary (the cross-tax CGT holdover interaction):
  - **TCGA 1992 s.165** — gift relief for business assets. Verified verbatim at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12/section/165. Available on gifts of qualifying business assets (Sch 7); the donor and donee jointly elect; CGT crystallises on the donee at the donee's later disposal at donor's base cost. **Property-context limitation:** s.165 is for trading business assets (Sch 7 para 2). Pure BTL is investment, not trading — s.165 unavailable. Trading property businesses (e.g. property developers' WIP, qualifying FHL pre-Apr-2025-cessation if relevant) may qualify.
  - **TCGA 1992 s.260** — holdover relief on IHT-chargeable transfers (CLTs into trust). Available on gifts into discretionary trust (a chargeable lifetime transfer for IHT) provided the trust is non-settlor-interested (ss.169B-G). **Strategic lever:** transfer BPR-qualifying property into a non-settlor-interested discretionary trust pre-death — 100% BPR available so no immediate IHT, AND s.260 CGT holdover applies (because the transfer is an IHT-chargeable transfer in form even though tax due is nil under BPR). Result: asset moves into trust without CGT, IHT-free under BPR, future appreciation outside the donor's estate. This is the **classic BPR-into-trust planning route** and is the principal lever for maximising BPR benefit.
- Primary (the £1m cap from 6 April 2026 — HP §15.4 lock):
  - **From 6 April 2026: £1m combined APR + BPR cap.** Below the cap: 100% relief; above the cap: 50% relief (effective 20% IHT on excess).
  - **Transferable between spouses:** but only £1m each — no doubling between spouses; the cap is per-deceased not per-married-couple. (This is the key change vs the pre-2026 architecture where allowances stacked.)
  - **AIM-shares 50% sub-tier:** AIM-traded shares now relieved at 50% AND do NOT consume the £1m allowance. Operationally a separate sub-tier.
  - **Anti-forestalling:** new rules apply to lifetime transfers on or after **30 October 2024** (Autumn Budget 2024 announcement) if donor dies on or after 6 April 2026 and within 7 years. Pre-announcement gifts are NOT caught.
  - **Trust anti-fragmentation:** multi-trust same-settlor cohort post-30-October-2024 share a single £1m allowance (Finance Bill 2025-26 published 21 July 2025; FA 2026 enactment expected with Autumn Budget 2025 cycle).
- Primary (the Pawson investment line — the property-investor's anti-claim discipline):
  - ***Pawson v HMRC* [2013] UKUT 050 (TCC)** — the leading authority on the s.105(3) investment exclusion for property businesses. Verified at https://www.bailii.org on 2026-05-26. Verified anchor at HP §22.1. Standard BTL is "wholly or mainly" investment; BPR denied. Serviced accommodation may qualify IF services are substantial — the Pawson-distinguishing factual matrix is high.
  - ***Graham v HMRC* [2018] UKFTT 306** + ***McCall v HMRC* [2009] NICA 12** — narrower SA-qualifying-for-BPR fact patterns.
  - ***Hardcastle v HMRC* [2024] UKFTT** — recent SA-qualifying case, narrowly decided in taxpayer's favour on managed-kitchen + daily-cleaning + breakfast facts.
  - ***Zetland v HMRC***, ***Henderson v HMRC***, ***Pawson*** at FTT (pre-UT appeal) — the SA-not-qualifying line.
- Primary (the FIC + BPR boundary — important non-overlap):
  - **FIC equity is NOT BPR-qualifying** typically — because most FICs are pure investment vehicles holding BTL property (Pawson). The "I'll wrap my BTL portfolio in a FIC and get BPR on the shares" plan FAILS — FIC shares are unquoted (s.105(1)(bb)), but the underlying business is investment (s.105(3) exclusion); BPR denied. HP §22.6 + §21.5.
- House position references: §15.4 (BPR/APR April 2026 cap — primary lock); §22.1 (Pawson investment line); §22.2 (deed of variation to maximise BPR post-death); §22.5 (spouse exemption + transferable allowances); §22.6 (FIC value-freeze + BPR boundary); §22.13 (trust-vs-FIC decision); §15.1 (NRB/RNRB stack). The page threads §15.4 + §22.1 + §22.13 + §15.4-anti-forestalling at the trust-route depth.

Anti-templating signature: the page leads with **the Pawson investment-line gate** — for a property audience the very first question is "does any of my property even qualify?" and the answer for pure BTL is no. The page does NOT pretend that BPR is broadly available to landlords; it surfaces the Pawson-fails reality, then walks through the cases where it MAY apply (genuine trading businesses, serviced-accommodation with substantial services, property-development WIP, mixed estates with trading businesses + property), and explains how to maximise BPR for those cases. This is the operational truth most amateur "5 ways to use BPR for landlords" content misses by pretending BPR applies broadly to BTL.

Pre-empts the most common amateur misconceptions:
- "BTL portfolios get BPR" — false; Pawson investment exclusion.
- "Wrap the BTL in a limited company and the shares get BPR" — false; unquoted-shares mechanic (s.105(1)(bb)) doesn't override the underlying investment activity (s.105(3)).
- "Wrap the BTL in a FIC and the FIC shares get BPR" — false; same Pawson reasoning.
- "BPR is unlimited at 100%" — false from 6 April 2026; £1m combined APR+BPR cap.
- "Each spouse gets their own £1m allowance, total £2m for the couple" — partial truth; £1m EACH but NOT doubled by spouse exemption (spouse exemption transfers value, not the cap).
- "I can move BPR assets between trusts to multiply the allowance" — false from 30 October 2024 (anti-fragmentation rules for same-settlor cohort).
- "AIM shares are unaffected by the cap" — partial truth; AIM rate drops to 50% AND outside the £1m cap (separate sub-tier).
- "I'll just sign the sale contract now and die before completion" — false; s.113 binding-contract-for-sale denies BPR.
- "2-year clock resets on transfer between spouses" — false; s.108 preserves the clock.
- "Replacement property always resets the 2-year clock" — partial truth; s.107 preserves where replacement is within 3 years AND remains qualifying property.

**Mini-illustration: the mixed-estate planning case — Holloway-family-portfolio**

Persona: Holloway-family-landlord (founder, 65, still active). Estate composition March 2026:
- £1,500,000 BTL portfolio held in personal name (no BPR per Pawson).
- £600,000 unquoted shares in active trading business he founded (qualifies for 100% BPR below cap; current value plus growth potential).
- £400,000 AIM-traded shares (post-2026: 50% rate, separate sub-tier outside £1m cap).
- £200,000 cash, ISA, NRB-stacking.
- Total estate value March 2026: £2,700,000.

**Without planning (status quo at death):**
- BTL portfolio £1.5m: no BPR; fully chargeable.
- Trading shares £600k: 100% BPR (below £1m cap); no IHT.
- AIM shares £400k: 50% BPR (sub-tier outside cap); 50% chargeable = £200k.
- Net estate for IHT calculation: £1.5m BTL + £200k AIM-chargeable + £200k other = £1,900,000.
- NRB £325k + TNRB (assume £325k available) = £650k; RNRB / TRNRB N/A on the BTL portfolio (no main residence in mix); chargeable £1,250k @ 40% = **£500,000 IHT**.

**Lever 1: lifetime CLT of trading shares into discretionary trust now (s.260 holdover route).**
- Transfer £600k trading shares into non-settlor-interested discretionary trust.
- IHT: CLT in form, but 100% BPR-eligible AND below £1m cap → 0% IHT immediately.
- CGT: s.260 holdover (because CLT is IHT-chargeable transfer in form) → 0% CGT immediately; trustees inherit founder's base cost.
- Founder's estate at death: trading shares gone (already in trust); growth from now to death also outside founder's estate.
- Net effect at death: estate calculation as before minus trading-shares element + future growth on shares outside estate.
- **Saving from this lever alone (assumed 5-year growth × 7% = +£236k growth excluded from estate): ~£94,000 IHT saved on future growth + clock-started on the lifetime transfer; if founder survives 7 years CLT also falls out of cumulation entirely.**

**Lever 2: AIM 50% sub-tier captured automatically at death.** No active lever required — the AIM 50% relief sub-tier operates at death; founder simply needs to retain AIM holding (subject to 2-year holding period per s.106). **Saving: 50% × £400k × 40% = £80,000 (vs no-relief baseline).**

**Lever 3: extract non-trade assets from any qualifying company (s.111-112 discipline).** Founder reviews trading business: balance sheet shows £150k surplus cash held within trading company. Per s.111, the surplus cash is an "excepted asset" — BPR is denied on its value within the company shares. Action: extract £100k surplus cash as dividends pre-death (subject to dividend tax cost) to bring company to lean trading-asset profile; remaining £500k of company value is fully qualifying. **Saving: 100% BPR on £500k clean × 40% = £200k saved relative to BPR-denied-on-surplus-cash position.**

**Lever 4: do NOT enter a binding contract for sale before death.** Founder turns down an opportunistic offer to sell the trading business in Q4 2026 (signed-then-die scenario per s.113 would deny BPR retrospectively); instead negotiates a non-binding earn-out or sells outright with the 100% BPR sheltering the lifetime transfer beforehand. **Saving: avoids potential £600k × 40% = £240k IHT exposure under s.113.**

**Lever 5: spouse-clock preservation under s.108.** Founder transferred 50% of trading-business holding to spouse 3 years ago. On founder's eventual death, the spouse's holding inherits the founder's 2-year clock per s.108 — no need to wait a new 2 years post-death-marriage. Already done; surfaces as planning education for similar fact patterns.

**Combined planning saving: ~£614,000 IHT vs the no-planning baseline of £500k.** The actual final IHT bill depends on remaining estate composition, 7-year survival on the CLT, and the £1m cap allocation between BPR + AIM + APR claims (if any).

## FAQ draft (12 entries, Stage 2 populated; RUN sub-agent polishes prose)

1. **Can my BTL portfolio get Business Property Relief?**
   No, almost always. Per IHTA 1984 s.105(3) and the leading authority *Pawson v HMRC* [2013] UKUT 050 (TCC), pure buy-to-let property businesses are "wholly or mainly investment" and excluded from BPR. The exclusion catches standard residential BTL, HMOs, and most furnished holiday lettings (FHLs). BPR applies to property businesses only where they cross into trading — typically with substantial services beyond passive lettings (serviced accommodation borderline) or where the activity is genuine property development (WIP, build-to-rent during construction).

2. **What changed in the April 2026 BPR + APR reforms?**
   From 6 April 2026, a £1,000,000 combined cap on APR + BPR was introduced per individual. Below £1m: 100% relief continues. Above £1m: relief drops to 50% (effective 20% IHT on the excess). The cap is per-deceased, not per-couple — no doubling between spouses. AIM-traded shares moved from 100% relief to 50% relief AND now sit in a separate sub-tier outside the £1m cap. Anti-forestalling applies to lifetime transfers on or after 30 October 2024 if the donor dies on or after 6 April 2026 within 7 years.

3. **What is the 2-year holding period?**
   Per IHTA 1984 s.106: "Property is not relevant business property in relation to a transfer of value unless it was owned by the transferor throughout the two years immediately preceding the transfer." The 2-year clock is the gatekeeper for new acquisitions; a qualifying asset acquired 18 months before death does NOT get BPR regardless of merits. Two key carve-outs: (a) s.108 — inter-spouse transfer preserves the clock; (b) s.107 — qualifying-to-qualifying replacement within 3 years preserves the clock.

4. **What is the replacement-property reset rule?**
   Per IHTA 1984 s.107, where qualifying business property is replaced by other qualifying business property within the prior 3 years, the original 2-year clock is preserved — the new property doesn't need its own fresh 2-year clock. Critical for property-business reorganisations: swapping one trading-business asset for another within 3 years does NOT reset the clock. But swapping a qualifying asset for a NON-qualifying asset (e.g. selling a trading FHL and buying a pure BTL) breaks continuity.

5. **What are "excepted assets"?**
   Per IHTA 1984 ss.111-112, the value of assets within an otherwise qualifying business that are NOT used for business purposes is excluded from BPR. Common examples: surplus cash held within a trading company beyond reasonable working capital needs; investment properties held within a trading company; intra-group loans not used for trade. The carve-out is critical for trading-and-investment-mixed companies — the trading element gets BPR, the investment element does not. Pre-death asset-stripping to remove non-trade elements is a standard planning lever.

6. **What is the binding-contract-for-sale gateway?**
   Per IHTA 1984 s.113, BPR is denied where the transferor had entered into a binding contract to sell the business or shares before the transfer (typically before death). This closes the "I'm selling the business; I'll die before completion; my estate inherits the cash with BPR" plan — a binding contract converts the qualifying business interest into a sale-of-proceeds receivable, which is no longer relevant business property. Action: do NOT sign before death; either sell outright (and accept the cash position) or avoid binding the deal.

7. **Can I gift BPR-qualifying property into a trust to use BPR + CGT holdover?**
   Yes — this is the classic BPR planning route. Transfer BPR-qualifying property into a non-settlor-interested discretionary trust: the transfer is a CLT (IHT-chargeable in form), but 100% BPR shelters it (no immediate IHT below £1m cap), AND because it is an IHT-chargeable transfer in form, TCGA 1992 s.260 holdover applies (no immediate CGT). Result: asset moves into trust IHT-free + CGT-free, with future growth outside the donor's estate. The trust must be non-settlor-interested (no benefit path back to settlor or spouse) per TCGA 1992 ss.169B-169G.

8. **Why doesn't FIC equity get BPR?**
   Because the underlying business in most FICs is investment (BTL property), not trading. Per *Pawson*, the s.105(3) investment exclusion bites regardless of corporate wrapper. FIC shares are unquoted (s.105(1)(bb)) and might appear to qualify on the share-class test alone, but the test runs through to the underlying business activity. A FIC holding pure BTL fails. A FIC holding a trading business (rare in property contexts) might qualify subject to the usual 2-year-hold + excepted-assets analysis.

9. **What about serviced accommodation — does that qualify?**
   Sometimes, but the bar is high. The leading authority is *Pawson v HMRC*: SA businesses MAY qualify where services beyond passive lettings are substantial (managed kitchen, daily cleaning, breakfast service, concierge — *Hardcastle v HMRC* [2024] UKFTT; *Graham v HMRC* [2018] UKFTT 306). Bare-bones SA (cleaning between stays, key handover, light-touch management) fails. The factual matrix matters; pre-flight evidence pack required (service-level documentation, average-stay-length analysis, ancillary-services revenue split). For most SA operators the safer assumption is that BPR will NOT apply.

10. **How does the AIM 50% sub-tier work post-2026?**
    AIM-traded shares (and equivalent unquoted-market shares) had 100% BPR pre-2026. From 6 April 2026, AIM relief drops to 50%. The AIM allowance is a separate sub-tier — AIM holdings do NOT consume the £1m combined APR+BPR cap. Practical implication: a £400k AIM portfolio sheltered at 50% saves £80k of IHT (50% × £400k × 40%); the £1m cap remains fully available for qualifying trading-business + APR claims.

11. **What planning levers actually save tax under the new £1m cap?**
    Four primary levers: (1) **Protect the 2-year hold (s.106)** — don't restructure within 2 years of expected death; use the s.107 replacement reset for active changes within 3 years. (2) **Extract non-trade assets (s.111-112)** — surplus cash and investment property within a trading company is BPR-disqualified; strip pre-death. (3) **Lifetime CLT into non-settlor-interested trust** — uses 100% BPR + s.260 holdover for combined IHT + CGT neutrality on transfer in; capped at £1m. (4) **Avoid binding-contract-for-sale (s.113)** — sell outright pre-death OR negotiate without binding. Secondary: inter-spouse clock preservation (s.108); post-death DoV to optimise BPR allocation across beneficiaries (s.142).

12. **Where do I go next?**
    For the BPR definitional explainer see our `business-property-relief-rental-property-iht` page. For the April 2026 cap mechanics depth see `iht-april-2026-bpr-apr-cap-property-impact`. For the serviced-accommodation Pawson-test deep see `serviced-accommodation-bpr-eligibility-pawson-test`. For the CLT-into-trust route deep see `iht-clt-property-discretionary-trust-20-percent-entry-charge`. For the FIC + BPR boundary see `fic-vs-discretionary-trust-property-comparison`. For the APR pillar sibling see `agricultural-relief-for-inheritance-tax-key-benefits`. For general IHT orientation see `inheritance-tax-a-brief-summary`. For the lifetime-vs-death decision pillar see `inheritance-tax-lifetime-gifts-vs-transfer-at-death`.
