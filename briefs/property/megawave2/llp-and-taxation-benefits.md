# LLP and Taxation Benefits: The Honest UK-Property-Context Analysis (2026/27)

## Statutory anchor

- **Primary (the LLP entity statute):** Limited Liability Partnerships Act 2000 (LLPA 2000) — body corporate (s.1(2)); members liable only as agreed in LLP agreement / LLPA 2000 framework (s.1(5)); registered at Companies House. Cross-reference sibling `llp-accounts` (this batch) for the operational accounts framework + `llp-property-investment-worth-considering` (existing entity-overview page) for the entity introduction. This page does NOT restate LLPA 2000 entity framework — it stands on it.
- **Primary (the load-bearing tax-transparency rule — the operative attraction):** ITTOIA 2005 s.863 — "treated as a partnership" for income tax purposes. The LLP is body corporate at law (LLPA 2000 s.1(2)) BUT transparent for income tax (s.863). Each member taxed individually on their share of LLP profits per the profit-sharing arrangement; LLP itself pays no income tax. **CGT equivalent:** TCGA 1992 s.59A — LLPs treated as partnerships for CGT (transparent; each member treated as holding fractional interest in each chargeable asset). **Operative consequence:** LLP profits taxed once at member marginal rates; NO double-extraction layer (cf LtdCo CT + dividend layered tax structure).
- **Primary (the s.863 disapplication on insolvency):** ITTOIA 2005 s.863(2)-(3) — s.863 disapplies on commencement of liquidation / winding-up; LLP-in-liquidation taxed as body corporate for the wind-up period. Operative for LLP exit / dissolution planning.
- **Primary (Section 24 application to LLP members — the killer of the "interest deductibility" claim for typical setups):** ITTOIA 2005 s.272A — finance-cost restriction for individuals with residential property income. **Critical:** because the LLP is transparent under s.863, each individual member is treated as receiving their share of the LLP's property income + finance costs; s.272A applies AT MEMBER LEVEL on the residential-property finance costs. **The widely-promoted "LLPs deduct interest in full" claim is FALSE for typical landlord LLPs with individual members:** the s.272A restriction applies at the individual-member level just as it would on direct personal ownership. The "interest deductibility" claim only stands where (a) members are NOT individuals (e.g., LtdCo members — but see mixed-membership rules below), or (b) the property income is NOT within the s.272A scope (commercial; FHL until abolition; non-UK; etc).
- **Primary (mixed-membership rules — the killer of the "corporate member" workaround):** ITA 2007 ss.850C-E (FA 2014 origin) — covered by F-5 (M2-A-B2) §11.C.Y proposed lock. Operative architecture: where (i) corporate member is allocated profit share, (ii) excess test passes (corporate's share > arm's-length commercial entitlement), and (iii) power-to-enjoy test passes (individual member can benefit), HMRC reallocates the excess to the individual member at marginal income tax rate (with double-tax credit for CT paid by corporate). **Practical consequence for property LLPs:** hybrid LLP setups using a corporate member to escape s.272A on the corporate's share are systematically dismantled — the rules were specifically designed against this structure. Per F-5 §11.C.Y proposed lock + sibling A10 M2-A-B2 `hybrid-limited-liability-partnership` for full depth.
- **Primary (salaried-member rules — the killer of the "limited-liability without employee NIC" claim):** ITA 2007 ss.863A-G (FA 2014 origin) — covered by F-4 (M2-A-B2) §11.C.X proposed lock. Operative architecture: three conjunctive Conditions (A: 80% disguised salary; B: lack of significant influence per BlueCrest narrowed reading [2024] UKSC 33; C: capital < 25% of disguised salary). Where ALL three met, member treated as employee for PAYE + secondary Class 1 NIC at LLP level (15% from 6 April 2026 per FA 2026 — verify rate) + Apprenticeship Levy. **Practical consequence for property LLPs:** the claimed benefit "LLP members are self-employed, save employer NIC" depends on at least one Condition failing; member-level NIC saving is contingent. Per F-4 §11.C.X proposed lock + sibling A11 M2-A-B2 `hmrcs-new-guidelines-for-llps-raise-concerns` for full depth.
- **Primary (the SDLT partnership relief — the most operative remaining benefit):** FA 2003 Sch 15 — partnership-SDLT regime. **Operative for LLP property incorporation:** Sch 15 para 10 sum-of-lower-proportions (SLP) mechanic can dramatically reduce SDLT where partnership / LLP transfers property to a connected LtdCo with members owning shares in same proportions. **Critical gate:** Sch 15 para 1 business test — the LLP must carry on a BUSINESS, not merely hold property (passive co-ownership at LLP level fails para 1; SLP relief unavailable). Cross-reference existing `partnership-sdlt-relief-schedule-15-fa-2003-incorporation-sum-lower-proportions` for depth + A7 M2-A-B2 `does-your-business-qualify-as-a-partnership` for the para 1 business gate analysis. **Practical positioning:** LLP-to-LtdCo incorporation with SLP relief is the most operationally-significant TAX benefit of using an LLP for property — but ONLY where para 1 business gate met + Sch 15 mechanics apply favourably (member share configurations matter).
- **Primary (the IHT BPR question — the most over-claimed benefit):** IHTA 1984 s.103 (relevant business property) + s.105 (eligible categories). **Critical:** BPR applies to interests in a BUSINESS, not interests in an investment vehicle. The Pawson investment-line test (Pawson v HMRC [2013] UKUT 050) excludes "wholly or mainly" investment businesses from BPR. **Practical consequence for typical property LLP:** a property-letting LLP is investment, NOT trading; BPR generally UNAVAILABLE — the LLP form does NOT create BPR where the underlying business is investment. Cross-reference existing `bpr-pure-btl-pawson-test-why-buy-to-let-fails-investment-line`. The honest position: LLP-form does NOT unlock BPR for landlord-investment businesses.
- **Primary (members' liability framework — the genuine non-tax benefit):** LLPA 2000 s.1(4)-(5) — members' liability limited to amounts agreed in LLP agreement / contributed to LLP. **Operative non-tax benefit:** asset protection — members' personal assets protected from LLP creditors (subject to standard exceptions: personal guarantees on borrowing; insolvency-related wrongful-trading-equivalent provisions; fraudulent conduct). This is the genuine LIMITED-LIABILITY benefit of LLP form vs general partnership. **Not a tax benefit** but commonly conflated with the tax discussion.
- **Primary (Section 24 + LLP + transparent regime interaction — the operational consequence):** because LLP is transparent under s.863, the s.272A residential-finance-cost restriction applies at member level on residential property finance costs. **Practical numerical consequence for typical HRT landlord:** member with £30k LLP-share of residential-property profit + £15k LLP-share of mortgage interest — taxed on full £30k at HRT (£12k income tax) with 20% credit on £15k interest (£3k credit) — same outcome as direct ownership. **The LLP form does NOT escape Section 24 for individual members on residential property** — this is the single most-misunderstood point in popular LLP commentary for landlords.
- **Primary (the flexible profit-allocation benefit — genuine but bounded):** ITTOIA 2005 Part 9 + s.863 — LLP profit allocation per LLP agreement, not statutory default. **Operative genuine benefit:** members can vary profit-share allocations year-to-year via LLP agreement (operationally easier than altering company share structures); members of different income-tax-brackets can have profit allocations weighted toward lower-bracket members (subject to GAAR + Ramsay; subject to mixed-membership rules where individual + corporate members co-exist; subject to settlements legislation ITTOIA 2005 ss.624-628 where allocations to spouses/civil partners or minor children fail bona fide commercial test). **Bounded benefit:** family LLPs can allocate flexibly within bona-fide commercial bounds; allocations that don't reflect actual capital + skill + time commitments are vulnerable to settlements challenges.
- **Primary (capital gains at member level — the operative CGT framework):** TCGA 1992 s.59A applies partnership treatment; SP D12 (HMRC Statement of Practice on Partnership CGT) operationalised for LLPs. **Operative consequences:**
  - Each member treated as holding fractional interest in each LLP chargeable asset; CGT triggered on actual disposals at member level.
  - Member's CGT annual exempt amount (£3k FY26) available against member's fractional share gains — **operative benefit vs LtdCo** (LtdCo has no AEA; chargeable gains added to CT-rated profits).
  - BADR (TCGA 1992 s.169H+ Business Asset Disposal Relief) available where LLP is TRADING and other conditions met; UNAVAILABLE for investment-property LLP per s.169L material disposal of business assets gateway (which requires trading status). Cross-reference existing BADR coverage.
  - Holdover relief (TCGA 1992 s.165) available for transfers of business interests in trading LLPs; ENTREPRENEUR'S RELIEF (s.165A trading-company definition) gateway likewise excludes pure investment LLPs.
- **House position reference:** §21 (Ltd Co + FIC family — comparison anchor for LLP vs LtdCo tax comparison); §22 (IHT — Pawson investment line per §22.1, BPR generally unavailable for pure investment LLP); §11 + §11.A (Companies House + ECCTA); F-2 (M2-A-B2) §11.C partnership architecture lock proposed; F-4 (M2-A-B2) §11.C.X salaried-member sub-section proposed; F-5 (M2-A-B2) §11.C.Y mixed-membership sub-section proposed; F-6 (this batch sibling `llp-accounts`) §11.C.Z LLP accounts sub-section proposed. **No NEW HP lock requested by this pick** — the tax-side architecture is fully covered by the F-2 / F-4 / F-5 lock requests; this page is the consumer of those locks rather than raising new lock territory.
