# Consequences for Register of Overseas Entities Non-Compliance: The Complete UK Penalty + HMLR Disposition-Block + Criminal Offence Stack for Overseas Property-Holding Companies

## Statutory anchor
- Primary: Economic Crime (Transparency and Enforcement) Act 2022 c. 10 ("ECTEA 2022"). Verified verbatim at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2022/10/contents on 2026-05-27 (sub-agent re-verifies at write time per §16.35). Operative anchors:
  - **ECTEA 2022 s.4** — "Application for registration" (the upstream obligation; the registration is itself the trigger for everything below).
  - **ECTEA 2022 s.7** — "Updating duty" (annual update obligation; 14-day window after end of update period — the trigger whose breach activates the consequence stack).
  - **ECTEA 2022 s.8** — "**Failure to comply with updating duty**" (criminal offence: entity + every officer of the entity who is in default; on conviction in England and Wales the maximum is a fine plus, for continued contravention, a daily default fine not exceeding the greater of £2,500 and one half of level 4 on the standard scale; in Scotland and Northern Ireland the maximum is a fine not exceeding level 5 on the standard scale plus a daily default fine not exceeding one half of level 5). **Per §11.A correction 2026-05-25: s.8 is the failure-to-update OFFENCE provision, not "removal from register" — removal is s.9.**
  - **ECTEA 2022 s.9** — "Removal from register" (separate Registrar's power to remove non-compliant entities from the register on application; distinct from the s.8 criminal route).
  - **ECTEA 2022 s.15A / s.15B / s.32A** — false-statement offences introduced via post-enactment amendments per the contents page (sub-agent verifies precise section attributions at write time per §16.35).
  - **ECTEA 2022 s.34** — "**Power to require overseas entity to register if it owns certain land**" (Secretary of State's compulsory-registration-notice power for unregistered overseas entities that own UK property; offence of failure to comply with a s.34 notice). **Per §11.A correction 2026-05-25: s.34 is NOT the HMLR disposition-block — that lives at LRA 2002 Sch 4A. s.34 is the upstream compulsory-registration power.**
  - **ECTEA 2022 s.36** — "**Meaning of 'daily default fine'**" — definitional section that applies CA 2006 s.1125 daily-default-fine concept to ECTEA Part 1.
- Primary (HMLR consequence — the operational showstopper): **Land Registration Act 2002 c. 9 Schedule 4A** (inserted by ECTEA 2022 Sch 3). Verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2002/9/schedule/4A on 2026-05-27. HMLR refuses to register dispositions (sales, leases over 7 years, legal mortgages, charges) by overseas entities that are not on the register OR that are not up to date with their updating duty. The disposition-block is the operational consequence the market actually feels.
- Supporting (civil penalty quantum): operative Penalties Regulations issued under ECTEA 2022 (Statutory Instrument — sub-agent verifies SI number + current quantum at write time per §16.27 rate-by-reference; **legacy guidance cited a fixed £2,500 + £500/day continuing-default civil penalty which may have moved since** — verify against the operative SI before stating the quantum). The civil penalty is SEPARATE from the s.8 criminal offence; both can apply to the same default.
- Supporting (companion register reforms via ECCTA 2023 Part 3): Economic Crime and Corporate Transparency Act 2023 c. 56. ECCTA 2023 Part 3 amends ECTEA 2022 in places (verification regime tightening, information-disclosure expansion, public-register data fields). **Sub-agent surfaced this overlay in B8 + B10 umbrella seeds; B13 confirms that the consequence-stack on this page is anchored in ECTEA 2022 (the original statute), with ECCTA 2023 amendments noted as upstream tightening of the regime that B13 is the consequences-side counterpart of.**
- Supporting (false statements + officer liability): CA 2006 false-statement offences framework (where ECTEA s.15A / 15B / 32A cross-references); MLR 2017 anti-money-laundering supervision regime (where verification agent failures create separate AML-side liabilities).
- House position reference: **§11 main text** (RoE in force since 1 August 2022; £2,500 + £500/day fixed civil penalty per legacy framing — verify against operative SI per §11.A). **§11.A Wave 9 mini-lock** (verified ECTEA 2022 section attributions hardened 2026-05-25: s.4 / s.7 / s.8 / s.9 / s.34 / s.36; LRA 2002 Sch 4A as the disposition-block anchor; false-statement offences at ss.15A / 15B / 32A; £2,500 + £500/day rate-by-reference to the operative Penalties Regulations SI). **§11.A do-not-write list:** "RoE only applies to new acquisitions" (false — applies to all post-1-Aug-2022 holdings); "ECTEA s.8 is removal from register" (false — s.8 is the criminal offence; removal is s.9); "ECTEA s.34 is the HMLR block" (false — s.34 is the compulsory-registration-notice power; HMLR block lives at LRA 2002 Sch 4A); "RoE annual update is due on calendar-year anniversary" (false — anchored to s.7 update period). ## Key questions this page must answer

1. **What is the headline consequence stack for RoE non-compliance — in one paragraph?** (Five parallel consequence regimes operate simultaneously on the same default: (i) civil financial penalty under operative Penalties Regulations issued under ECTEA 2022 (legacy quantum £2,500 + £500/day continuing default — verify against current SI at write per §16.27); (ii) criminal offence under ECTEA 2022 s.8 against the entity AND every officer in default (fine + daily default fine ≥ greater of £2,500 or half level 4 on the standard scale for E&W); (iii) HMLR disposition-block under LRA 2002 Sch 4A — sales, leases over 7 years, legal mortgages refused at title search; (iv) Companies House compulsory-registration-notice power under ECTEA 2022 s.34 for previously unregistered overseas entities owning UK property; (v) reputational + counterparty-due-diligence flag on the public register (lender, buyer's solicitor, JV partner, professional adviser visibility). The HMLR disposition-block is the operationally most material — the civil penalty is recurring annoyance, but the HMLR block stops transactions.)
2. **What is the HMLR disposition-block — what does it actually block?** (Under LRA 2002 Sch 4A (inserted by ECTEA 2022 Sch 3): HM Land Registry refuses to register dispositions by overseas entities that are NOT on the Register of Overseas Entities OR that are NOT up to date with their updating duty under ECTEA s.7. "Dispositions" caught: (a) sale of the freehold or registered leasehold; (b) grant of a lease for a term exceeding 7 years; (c) grant of a legal mortgage / charge over the registered estate. Operational consequence: a buyer's conveyancer's title search returns the non-compliant flag; the conveyancer either withdraws or refuses to exchange; refinancing stops at the lender's drawdown check; long business lease grants stop at HMLR submission. The block is procedural, not punitive: compliance restoration unblocks all subsequent dispositions, but the restoration lag is the operational problem.)
3. **What is the civil financial penalty — how much, how levied?** (Civil financial penalty regime under operative Penalties Regulations issued under ECTEA — sub-agent verifies SI number + current quantum at write per §16.27 rate-by-reference. Legacy framing: £2,500 fixed + £500/day continuing default — verify against operative SI before stating. Levied by Companies House by penalty notice. Right of appeal to the FTT. Quantum can stack against the same default — civil penalty + criminal conviction + restoration costs are all separate cost lines. Civil penalty is the recurring annoyance cost; HMLR disposition-block is the operationally severe cost.)
4. **What is the criminal offence under ECTEA s.8 — who is exposed?** (ECTEA 2022 s.8 creates a criminal offence committed by the ENTITY AND by every officer of the entity who is in default. "Officer" includes directors, general partners, secretaries, members of the management body — depending on the entity's home jurisdiction (a BVI company's officers will include its directors; a Cayman exempted company's officers include directors; a Jersey company's officers include directors; an English limited partnership's officers include the general partner). Maximum on conviction in E&W: a fine + daily default fine ≥ greater of £2,500 or half level 4 on the standard scale (rate-by-reference — verify at write); in Scotland and NI: fine ≤ level 5 + daily default fine ≤ half level 5. Daily default fine accrues per day of continued non-compliance — the cost compounds for entities that drift in non-compliance for months. Officer-level criminal liability is the material exposure for the directors / GPs personally — distinct from the entity-level civil penalty. Defence of "took all reasonable steps" available per general criminal-defence framework — but evidential burden is on the officer.)
5. **Who counts as an officer for the criminal offence — does it reach the ultimate beneficial owner?** (Officer for ECTEA s.8 purposes follows the entity's constitutional documents and home-jurisdiction company law — directors, general partners, secretaries, members of the management body. The ultimate beneficial owner (UBO) is NOT automatically an officer — the UBO sits behind the entity and is captured separately on the public register as the registrable beneficial owner. UBO criminal liability arises only where the UBO is also a director / officer of the entity OR where the UBO has played an active role in causing the non-compliance (consent / connivance / neglect per general directors' duties framework, plus the false-statement offences at ss.15A / 15B / 32A where the UBO has caused false information to be filed). The standard structure — UHNW-individual UBO behind a BVI corporate director behind a Jersey trust — does not automatically expose the UBO to ECTEA s.8 criminal liability. But the false-statement framework + civil-side direct liability under MLR 2017 + the ID-verification regime under ECCTA 2023 increasingly do.)
6. **What is the compulsory-registration-notice power under ECTEA s.34?** (For UNREGISTERED overseas entities that own UK property — i.e. entities that have either failed to register since 1 August 2022 OR that have acquired UK property since registration was required without registering. Secretary of State may serve a notice on the entity requiring it to register within a specified period. Failure to comply with the notice is itself an offence. The s.34 power is upstream of the s.7 / s.8 updating-duty consequence stack — it operates against entities not yet on the register at all. For entities ALREADY registered, s.34 is irrelevant and the operative consequence territory is s.7 + s.8 + LRA Sch 4A. Verify s.34 mechanic at write time against the operative section text per §16.35.)
7. **What are the false-statement offences under ss.15A / 15B / 32A?** (Three distinct false-statement offences introduced via post-enactment amendments to ECTEA: knowingly or recklessly providing false / misleading information at registration (s.15A territory); knowingly or recklessly providing false / misleading information at update (s.15B territory); related false-statement-on-removal-application offences (s.32A territory). Sub-agent verifies precise section attributions + offence elements + maximum sentence at write time per §16.35. Distinct from s.8 (which is the failure-to-update OFFENCE — non-action rather than false statement). Both can apply to the same filing — a late update statement containing false information attracts both s.8 (lateness) and s.15B (false content).)
8. **How does the consequence stack interact with the ECCTA 2023 amendments?** (ECCTA 2023 Part 3 amends ECTEA 2022 in places — tightening the verification regime, expanding information-disclosure on the public register, adding registrable-beneficial-owner data fields. The substantive consequence stack — s.7 update / s.8 offence / LRA Sch 4A disposition-block — was already in ECTEA 2022 and is NOT created by ECCTA 2023. What ECCTA 2023 did was raise the bar on what counts as a compliant filing (deeper verification + more disclosed information) — which by extension widens the surface area of potential non-compliance. Entities that were compliant under the original 2022 architecture should run a refresh check against the ECCTA-2023-tightened standard. Cross-link to B8 / B9 / B10 umbrella pages for the full reform scope.)
9. **What is the operational restoration sequence — how do I get back into compliance?** (Five steps: (i) instruct a UK-regulated verification agent (firm AML-supervised under MLR 2017 AND registered as a verification agent under SI 2022/725) to perform a fresh verification of the entity's registrable beneficial owners; (ii) prepare the overdue update statement reflecting the current state of beneficial ownership; (iii) file the update statement at Companies House; (iv) settle any civil penalty notice that has issued (separately appealable to the FTT); (v) wait for Companies House to update the entity's status on the public register and for HMLR to receive the updated status. Operational lag: typically 2-4 weeks for the full restoration cycle — which is often too long for a transaction with a hard exchange date. The criminal s.8 exposure does NOT extinguish on restoration — past offences remain prosecutable; CPS / Companies House Enforcement discretion applies.)
10. **What is the practical timeline if my entity is mid-transaction when I discover non-compliance?** (Operational reality: buyer's conveyancer's title search returns the non-compliant flag; conveyancer either withdraws or imposes conditions the seller cannot satisfy without restoring compliance. Restoration takes 2-4 weeks; transaction window often shorter. The transaction either (a) extends with both sides' agreement and restoration completes (rare — buyer often loses confidence or finds alternative property); OR (b) collapses (more common — buyer walks; seller loses the deal); OR (c) for ongoing rental income — the lease grant for the new tenant cannot be registered at HMLR if over 7 years, so the seller falls back on shorter leases (under-7-year leases do not require HMLR registration and so are NOT blocked by Sch 4A — a partial workaround for portfolio operators whose tenancies are typically short).)
11. **What is the lender + counterparty due-diligence consequence?** (For lenders: a non-compliant flag on the public register is a covenant breach under most standard lending documentation (information undertaking + general-compliance covenant). Lender either issues a remediation notice OR (where the loan is in default trigger territory) accelerates. Refinancing alternative lenders will refuse to draw down until compliance is restored. For JV partners + co-investors: information-undertaking covenants are typically tighter than at the lender level (specific RoE-compliance-warranty + indemnity clauses are now standard in post-2022 JV documentation); breach can trigger drag-along / forced-exit / buyout rights. For professional advisers: AML-supervised firms (UK accountants + solicitors) cannot continue to act for an entity in breach of its statutory disclosure obligations without raising MLR-2017-side concerns; the non-compliant flag is a Suspicious Activity Report (SAR) trigger if persistent. The market-side consequence stack is increasingly the operational driver of compliance rather than the statutory penalty.)
12. **Does the entity get removed from the register if it stays non-compliant?** (Separate route — ECTEA 2022 s.9 "Removal from register" — Registrar's power to remove a non-compliant entity from the register on application. **NOT the same as s.8 (which is the criminal offence) or LRA Sch 4A (which is the HMLR disposition-block).** Operational reality: removal under s.9 is rare; the standard enforcement pattern is civil-penalty-first + HMLR-block-by-operation-of-law + criminal-prosecution-for-persistent-failure. Where removal does occur, the entity must re-register from scratch to deal in UK property — the cost and timeline implications are material. Verify s.9 mechanic at write time against the operative section text per §16.35.)
13. **What is the difference between RoE non-compliance and ECCTA-2023 ID-verification non-compliance — are they the same consequence regime?** (No — distinct register, distinct statute, distinct consequence stack. RoE non-compliance is the consequence stack on this page (ECTEA 2022 + LRA 2002 Sch 4A); it applies to overseas entities owning UK property. ECCTA-2023 ID-verification non-compliance is the parallel consequence stack on the companies-side register (companies-house director + PSC ID verification under ECCTA 2023 ss.40-45 / s.64 / s.65) — applies to ALL UK companies + LLPs (not only overseas-owned). The two regimes can affect the same person (a director of a UK company that is itself owned by an overseas entity — both registers + both verification cycles apply) but the consequence stacks differ: ECCTA ID-verification breach blocks the confirmation statement filing at the company-register side; RoE breach blocks the property disposition at the HMLR side. Cross-link to B2 + B6 + B7 + the existing operational ID-verification walkthrough.)
