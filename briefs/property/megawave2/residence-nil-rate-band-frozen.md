# Residence Nil-Rate Band Frozen: What the 2030 Freeze Means for Property Owners

## Statutory anchor

- Primary (the RNRB architecture — the structural starting point):
  - **Inheritance Tax Act 1984 ss.8D-8M** (the RNRB suite — inserted by Finance (No. 2) Act 2015 s.9 + Sch 1, in force from 6 April 2017 with phased introduction). Verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1984/51 on 2026-05-27.
  - **IHTA 1984 s.8D** — entitlement to RNRB: a person dying on or after 6 April 2017 who satisfies the qualifying-residential-interest condition + closely-inherited condition + has not had RNRB withdrawn by the s.8FA taper.
  - **IHTA 1984 s.8E** — amount of the RNRB: the "default allowance" for the relevant tax year (the headline figure — £100,000 in 2017/18 phased up to £175,000 by 2020/21).
  - **IHTA 1984 s.8FA** — taper-down for estates over £2m: the RNRB is reduced by £1 for every £2 by which the net value of the estate exceeds the **taper threshold** of £2,000,000. Estate net value of £2.35m+ extinguishes the £175,000 RNRB entirely.
  - **IHTA 1984 ss.8G-8H** — transferable RNRB ("brought-forward allowance"): unused RNRB on the death of a first spouse (since 6 April 2017) can be transferred to the survivor's estate, doubling the headline allowance to up to £350,000 where neither was tapered down.
  - **IHTA 1984 s.8J** — closely-inherited condition: RNRB applies only where the qualifying residential interest passes to a "lineal descendant" (children, grandchildren — including step, adopted, fostered), or to a spouse of a deceased lineal descendant (subject to remarriage rules).
  - **IHTA 1984 s.8K** — qualifying residential interest: a dwelling-house which has been the deceased's residence at some point during their ownership (does NOT need to be the residence at the date of death).
  - **IHTA 1984 s.8L** — downsizing addition: where the deceased downsized (or disposed entirely of) a qualifying residence on or after 8 July 2015, the RNRB can be preserved up to the value of the foregone qualifying interest (subject to conditions in s.8FB-8FE).
- Primary (the freeze — the three-layer announcement timeline):
  - **First freeze — Finance Act 2021 s.86** (Spring Budget March 2021 announcement): froze the RNRB at £175,000 (and the £2m taper threshold at £2,000,000) for tax years 2021/22 through 2025/26. Verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2021/26/section/86 — Stage 2 confirms precise section + sub-sections.
  - **First extension — Autumn Statement November 2022 / Finance Act 2023** (Spring Finance Bill 2023): extended the RNRB freeze and the £2m taper threshold freeze through 2027/28 (Stage 2 verifies the precise enacting Finance Act section + commencement provisions).
  - **Second extension — Autumn Budget October 2024 / Finance Act 2025**: extended the RNRB freeze and the £2m taper threshold freeze further to April 2030 (Stage 2 verifies precise Finance Act 2025 enacting section number against legislation.gov.uk; at write time this is the announced + enacted further freeze per Autumn Budget 2024 — RUN session re-verifies the exact statutory section before publication).
  - **Concurrent freeze on the NRB:** the £325,000 nil-rate band is ALSO frozen on the same timeline (FA 2021 s.86 + subsequent extensions); the £325,000 figure has been unchanged since 2009/10, and the freeze extension to April 2030 means a 21-year frozen-NRB run.
  - **HP §15.X / §22.X.1 lock — the double-freeze trap:** the combined effect of frozen NRB £325k + frozen RNRB £175k + frozen £2m taper threshold, against rising house and asset prices, draws progressively more estates into IHT and progressively more substantial estates into the £2m taper zone. The fiscal drag is the principal practical consequence of the freeze, not the headline cash-impact on any one estate.
- Primary (the fiscal-drag mechanism — the structural backbone of the freeze's impact):
  - **Land Registry House Price Index (HPI)** — the calibration reference for property-value erosion of the frozen RNRB. UK average house price rose from ~£253,000 (April 2021, when the freeze began) to a typical ~£290,000-£295,000 by 2026; cumulative property-price growth across the 2021-2030 freeze period is the principal axis of erosion.
  - **Cumulative inflation 2021-2030** (ONS CPI projection — Stage 2 verifies most recent OBR projection): cumulative inflation across the freeze period materially erodes the real-terms value of the frozen £175k allowance. By 2030, the £175k allowance in 2021 real-terms is materially diminished.
  - **Affected-estate count growth** — OBR + HMRC projections (per Autumn Budget 2024 Policy Costings document — Stage 2 fetches): the number of estates paying IHT was projected to roughly double across the freeze period from ~4% of UK deaths (pre-freeze baseline) toward ~8-10% by end-decade; the increase is principally fiscal-drag-driven.
- Primary (the property-value-increase axis as the acute landlord + homeowner consequence):
  - **§22.X.1 / HP §22.1 lock** — the freeze interacts asymmetrically with property-rich estates: those whose primary asset is the family home (and any BTL portfolio) face the freeze most acutely because property-price growth typically exceeds the broader CPI rate. A homeowner whose family home was £350,000 at the start of the freeze and rises to £500,000 by 2030 has not "gained £150k of wealth" in real terms (allowing for inflation); but for IHT purposes, the estate has crossed thresholds and may now face IHT exposure that did not exist pre-freeze.
  - **§22.X.1 / HP §22.1 lock — the £2m taper trap:** the £2m threshold's freeze means estates that creep over £2m via property-price growth (very common for southern UK homeowners + BTL landlords) lose RNRB at the £1-per-£2 taper rate. A £2.35m estate has £0 RNRB; a £2.2m estate has £75k RNRB (£175k − £100k taper). The taper-zone is where modest portfolio growth + frozen threshold combine to maximum IHT impact per pound of estate growth.
- Primary (the planning-response menu — the structural breadth of options):
  - **Lifetime gifting (s.3A + s.7-year clock):** PETs to individuals + bare trusts; CLT into discretionary trusts at 20% lifetime rate (with s.260 holdover for residential property). See C14 lifetime-vs-death pillar; see C19 trust orientation pillar.
  - **Spouse exemption + transferable allowances (s.18 + s.8G-8H):** spousal-route preservation of unused NRB + RNRB doubles the headline relief to ~£1m combined for surviving spouse — but only if the family home is closely-inherited at second death AND the second-death estate is under the £2m taper.
  - **IPDI in will (s.49A):** the immediate-post-death-interest fixed-route preserves RNRB while giving life-tenant occupation/income rights; the trustees can be empowered for downstream flexibility.
  - **Downsizing addition (s.8L + ss.8FB-8FE):** where the family home has been sold or downsized (typical in retirement), the addition can preserve up to the foregone qualifying interest value if conditions are met. **Critical: requires evidence + tracking + an explicit downsizing-addition claim by personal representatives.**
  - **Settled-property routes (C19 orientation pillar):** lifetime CLT into discretionary trust with s.260 holdover; trade-off is 20% entry charge over NRB + 10-yearly periodic charge + loss of CGT uplift on death + loss of RNRB on the gifted property — the C19 pillar covers the trade-off. NOT a default route; suited only to substantial-portfolio + multi-generational + advice-budgeted profiles.
  - **Charitable bequest 36% reduced rate (s.7(4) + Sch 1A IHTA 1984):** estates leaving 10%+ of the baseline amount to qualifying charity get a reduced 36% IHT rate on the chargeable estate (vs 40%). Useful at the margin for moderate estates.
  - **BPR + APR portfolio restructuring (C17 BPR planning pillar):** trading-business holdings outside the BTL portfolio can attract BPR (subject to £1m cap from 6 April 2026 per FA 2025/2026 — Stage 2 verifies + uses commencement-tense framing). NOT applicable to passive BTL income; only relevant where landlord has a genuine trading business (e.g., property development, FHL on a Pawson-line-passing basis) alongside the rental portfolio.
  - **DoV post-death (s.142):** beneficiaries can vary an inheritance within 2 years post-death to optimise RNRB usage at the second death — particularly useful where the first-death estate was structured pre-RNRB-introduction.
- House position references: §15.1 (IHT general architecture); §15.3 (lifetime transfers — CLT vs PET fork); §22.X (post-FA-2025 LTR architecture for non-resident overlay); §22.X.1 (RNRB freeze fiscal-drag lock); §22.1 (asset-value fiscal-drag); §22.2 (CGT uplift on death + interaction with frozen allowances); §22.5 (spouse exemption + transferable allowances + closely-inherited routing); §22.11 (GROB risk if planning involves gift); §22.13 (relevant property regime — the trust-route trade-off if pursued); §15.4 (BPR/APR — for the BPR planning-response lever); §15.6 (non-resident IHT — for overseas landlords reading this page); §22.17 (probate + IHT computation interaction).

The page is honest about the policy lever: the freeze is a stealth tax that increases IHT receipts year-on-year without a headline rate change. Pre-empts the most common amateur misconceptions:

- "The RNRB went up to £175k and stays there" — partial truth. £175k IS the current rate but it has been frozen at that level since 2020/21 and (with the announced 2030 extension) will be frozen for a 10-year period — a material real-terms erosion.
- "The freeze is temporary and they'll uprate it eventually" — speculative; the policy direction across THREE successive freezes (2021, 2022, 2024) has been to extend rather than uprate; planning should assume the announced freeze holds.
- "If my estate is under £325k I don't need to worry about the RNRB anyway" — partial truth. Estates under the NRB don't need RNRB to be IHT-free, but estates near the threshold (especially with property growth ahead) should factor in the combined frozen-NRB + frozen-RNRB headroom over the planning horizon.
- "The £2m taper only affects rich people" — partial truth depending on geography. £2m estates are increasingly common in London + the South East, especially where the family home is £1m+ and there is also pension wealth + investments + a BTL or two. The £2m threshold has been frozen since RNRB introduction in 2017; many homeowners do not realise they are within striking distance of the taper.
- "I can put the house in trust to avoid the freeze impact" — partial truth with serious caveats. Putting the family home in trust crystallises the costs (CLT entry charge over NRB; SDLT on assumed debt if mortgaged; CGT exposure under TCGA s.17 unless within s.260) AND loses the RNRB on that property (because RNRB requires the qualifying residential interest to be in the estate at death and closely-inherited; trust-held property is neither). The C19 orientation pillar covers this trade-off.
- "Couples get £1m of allowances anyway" — only with conditions. The £1m figure assumes BOTH NRBs (£325k × 2 = £650k) + BOTH RNRBs (£175k × 2 = £350k) + closely-inherited family home at second death + estate under £2m for the survivor. Failure of ANY condition reduces the £1m figure materially.
- "Gifting £3,000 a year using the annual exemption solves the problem" — false. The £3,000 annual exemption is structurally trivial against typical estate-growth rates; it is a sensible regular-gifting practice but not a planning answer to the freeze for substantial estates.
- "The 7-year clock means anything gifted in year 1 is safe" — false. The clock starts from the date of the gift; survival to year 7 makes the PET fully exempt; death within 7 years brings the PET back into the estate with taper relief on the IHT (not on the value) for years 4-7. PETs are a planning tool, not a guarantee.
- "RNRB only applies if I die owning the house" — false in two directions: (a) the downsizing addition (s.8L + ss.8FB-8FE) preserves RNRB where downsizing occurred; (b) RNRB still requires the qualifying residential interest to pass to a lineal descendant (closely-inherited test under s.8J).

**Core worked-example vignette — the Surrey-couple RNRB extinguishment under the freeze**

Persona: Holloway-family married couple, both 68, owning family home in Surrey at £950k current value; £1.1m combined pension drawdown + investments + cash; one BTL flat at £400k. Combined gross estate ~£2.45m at first death. First death assumed in 2026; all-to-survivor by Will + spouse exemption (s.18) — no IHT charged at first death; full TNRB + TRNRB carried forward.

**At second death (assume ~5 years later, 2031):**
- Property growth assumption: 2% pa modest growth → family home £1.05m; BTL £440k.
- Pension drawdown net of withdrawals: assume £900k after the survivor's 5 years of drawdown.
- Cash + investments: assume £200k.
- Survivor's estate at second death = £1.05m + £440k + £900k + £200k = £2.59m.

**RNRB position under the freeze:**
- Combined RNRB available pre-taper: £175k + £175k (transferred from first death) = £350k.
- Estate exceeds £2m taper threshold by £590k.
- Taper reduction: £590k ÷ 2 = £295k reduction. (Note: HMRC view per IHTM46035 is that taper applies once to the combined allowance; Stage 2 RUN-session re-verifies cumulative-taper interaction with TRNRB.)
- Residual RNRB: £350k - £295k = £55k. (If RNRB extinguish point is £2.35m for an individual + £2.7m where TRNRB available — Stage 2 verifies precise cumulation rule.)

**IHT computation at second death (£2.59m estate, residual RNRB £55k assumed):**
- Available allowances: NRB £325k + TNRB £325k + £55k residual RNRB = £705k.
- Chargeable estate: £2.59m - £705k = £1.885m.
- IHT at 40%: £754,000.

**Comparison to pre-freeze counterfactual:** if RNRB and NRB had uprated with CPI from 2009-2030 (rough ~50% cumulative CPI), RNRB might have reached ~£260k per individual and NRB ~£480k by 2030 → combined £325k+£325k+£480k+£480k+£260k+£260k = £2.13m allowance (before taper); estate £2.59m vs allowance £2.13m → chargeable £460k @ 40% = £184k IHT. **The freeze costs this couple approximately £570k of IHT exposure (£754k - £184k).** Most of that delta is the frozen NRB + RNRB + frozen £2m taper threshold.

- Charitable bequest of ≥10% of net chargeable estate triggers Sch 1A 36% reduced rate — saves £75k+ in this scenario.
- IPDI structuring at first death keeps assets available for survivor but reduces second-death-estate composition.

- Sketch outcome: planning levers can materially reduce the £754k IHT exposure but require advice + multi-step execution + realistic time horizon.

| £2.0m or below | £175,000 (full) | £500,000 | normal 40p IHT on marginal pound |

## FAQ draft (10-12 entries, Stage 2 populated; RUN sub-agent polishes prose)

1. **What is the residence nil-rate band (RNRB)?**
   The RNRB is an additional inheritance tax allowance, set at £175,000 per individual, introduced from 6 April 2017 by IHTA 1984 ss.8D-8M. It applies on top of the standard £325,000 nil-rate band, but only on death (not on lifetime gifts), and only where a qualifying residential interest passes to a lineal descendant — children, grandchildren, step / adopted / fostered children, or the surviving spouse of a deceased lineal descendant. Unused RNRB transfers to a surviving spouse under s.8G-s.8H.

2. **What does "frozen" mean for the RNRB?**
   The RNRB has been held at £175,000 in nominal terms since the 2020/21 tax year (FA 2021 s.86 first freeze). The Autumn Budget 2024 extended the freeze to 5 April 2030. "Frozen" means the cash figure stays the same while everything else changes around it — house prices rise, inflation erodes the real value, and more estates are drawn over the threshold. This is called "fiscal drag" and is the principal real-world consequence of the freeze.

3. **When was the freeze announced and how has it been extended?**
   The freeze has three layers. First freeze: Spring Budget March 2021 froze the RNRB (and the £2m taper threshold) until 2025/26 via Finance Act 2021 s.86. First extension: Autumn Statement November 2022 extended the freeze to 2027/28 via Finance Act 2023. Second extension: Autumn Budget October 2024 extended the freeze further to 5 April 2030 via Finance Act 2025 (RUN session re-verifies the precise FA 2025 enacting section). The cumulative effect is a ten-year freeze of the RNRB at £175k.

4. **Is the £2m taper threshold also frozen?**
   Yes. The £2m threshold at which RNRB starts to taper has been frozen on the same schedule as the RNRB headline rate, since 2017 introduction. The £2m has never been uprated. With property and asset prices rising, more estates are creeping into the taper zone — particularly in London, the South-East, and other high-value regions. The double-freeze (RNRB + taper threshold) compounds the fiscal-drag impact.

5. **Is the £325,000 NRB also frozen?**
   Yes — and for much longer. The NRB has been held at £325,000 since the 2009/10 tax year. The same Finance Act 2021, Finance Act 2023, and Finance Act 2025 provisions that froze the RNRB also froze the NRB. By April 2030 this will be 21 consecutive years of frozen NRB. Real-terms the NRB has eroded by approximately half over that period when adjusted for cumulative CPI.

6. **How does fiscal drag actually work?**
   The rate stays the same in cash terms while everything else moves around it. House prices rise, inflation erodes purchasing power, pension pots grow with markets — but the IHT allowance does not change. Estates that were comfortably below the threshold drift over it. The number of estates paying IHT has approximately doubled in projection across the freeze period — from around 4% of UK deaths (pre-freeze baseline) toward 8-10% by the end of the decade. The Office for Budget Responsibility tracks this in its Economic + Fiscal Outlook.

7. **What is the £2m taper trap and why is it acute?**
   IHTA 1984 s.8FA reduces the RNRB by £1 for every £2 by which the estate exceeds £2m. A £2.35m estate has £0 RNRB; a £2.7m estate (where the second spouse has transferred RNRB) loses the full combined £350k. In the £2m-to-£2.35m band, every £1 of estate growth costs the estate £0.50 of RNRB headroom — combined with the 40% IHT rate on the marginal pound, the effective marginal IHT impact is around 60%. Property growth, pension growth, and modest investment growth can push southern UK homeowners into this band without their realising.

8. **Does the £1 million combined allowance for couples still hold under the freeze?**
   The "couples get £1 million" headline depends on three conditions all being met: (i) the survivor's estate is below the £2m taper threshold (otherwise some RNRB is tapered away); (ii) the qualifying residential interest is closely-inherited by lineal descendants at second death (not, for example, passed to a sibling or trust); (iii) BOTH NRBs are unused at first death (typically by all-to-survivor spousal Will). Failure of any condition reduces the £1m figure materially. The freeze does not change these conditions, but rising estate values mean condition (i) — the £2m taper — fails for an increasing share of couples.

9. **What is the downsizing addition and when does it apply?**
   Per IHTA 1984 s.8L + ss.8FB-8FE, where a deceased had sold or downsized from a qualifying residence on or after 8 July 2015, the RNRB can be preserved up to the value of the foregone qualifying interest, subject to detailed conditions. The personal representatives must claim the downsizing addition explicitly — it is not automatic. Documentary evidence of the original residence, the disposal value, the disposal date, and the subsequent estate composition is required (typically via HMRC form IHT435 + supporting schedules). The addition is a structurally important lever for the typical retirement-downsize narrative.

10. **What is the IPDI route in a Will?**
    An immediate post-death interest (IPDI) under IHTA 1984 s.49A allows assets to be settled into a trust at death giving a named life tenant (typically the surviving spouse) an interest in possession. The life tenant is treated as beneficially entitled to the underlying assets for IHT (so the spouse exemption applies at first death and the assets are aggregated with the life tenant's estate at second death), but the trustees can be empowered with overriding powers for downstream flexibility. IPDI preserves the RNRB position on closely-inherited routing at second death and is structurally useful for blended families, vulnerable beneficiaries, and contingency planning.

11. **What planning responses are available under the freeze?**
    The principal planning-response menu at orientation depth: (i) lifetime gifting using the PET / 7-year clock — see the lifetime-vs-death pillar; (ii) spouse exemption + transferable allowances under s.18 + s.8G-s.8H — the baseline; (iii) IPDI in the Will under s.49A; (iv) downsizing addition under s.8L where the home has been sold or downsized; (v) settled-property routes via the trust orientation pillar — with the GROB and CGT-uplift trade-offs covered there; (vi) charitable bequest of ≥10% of net estate to trigger the Sch 1A 36% reduced rate; (vii) BPR / APR portfolio restructuring under the FA 2025/2026 £1m combined cap — subject to genuine trading-business test for BPR; (viii) post-death deeds of variation under s.142 + s.62(6). Most plans use two or three levers in combination, not a single silver bullet.

12. **Should I rush to gift property now because of the freeze?**
    No. Lifetime gifting decisions should not be driven by freeze-news alone. The CGT-vs-IHT trade-off (lifetime gifts crystallise CGT immediately under TCGA 1992 s.17 with no holdover for non-trading assets, while hold-to-death secures the CGT uplift under s.62) often weighs against lifetime gifting for appreciated property — particularly for older donors. The lifetime-vs-death decision needs the full CGT + IHT total-tax framework, not just the IHT side. The freeze is a planning input, not a planning trigger.

13. **Where does the family home fit in this?**
    The family home is typically the largest single asset in a UK estate and is the asset most directly affected by the RNRB framework. Its IHT treatment is decision-rich: hold to death (RNRB + spouse exemption + CGT s.62 uplift); lifetime gift (loses RNRB; GROB risk if the donor stays in occupation; CGT s.17 immediate crystallisation); IPDI in Will (preserves RNRB + flexibility); settled-property route (loses RNRB; GROB trap if settlor-as-beneficiary). The family-home decision pillar covers the four-route comparison; this freeze pillar is the news + fiscal-drag framing that motivates the decision.
