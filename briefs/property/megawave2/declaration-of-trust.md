# Declaration of Trust on Property: What It Is, When You Need One, and How It Works

## Statutory anchor

- Primary (writing requirement for trusts of land): **Law of Property Act 1925 s.53(1)(b)** — verified verbatim at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/Geo5/15-16/20/section/53 on 2026-05-26: "a declaration of trust respecting any land or any interest therein must be manifested and proved by some writing signed by some person who is able to declare such trust or by his will." This is a substantive property-law requirement, not a procedural HMRC preference: an oral declaration of trust over land is unenforceable; a deed not satisfying s.53(1)(b) is void as to the trust it purports to declare.
- Primary (when it becomes a deed proper): **Law of Property (Miscellaneous Provisions) Act 1989 s.1** — deed-execution formalities (writing + signature + witnessing + delivery). A written declaration that is also witnessed is admitted as a deed and carries the deed's evidential weight in litigation; the s.53(1)(b) writing-and-signature minimum is enforceable without witnessing but is harder to defend in an HMRC enquiry or in trust litigation.
- Primary (the spouse 50/50 default that declarations of trust + Form 17 displace): **ITA 2007 s.836** — joint income of spouses / civil partners living together treated as 50/50 unless displaced by Form 17 declaration referencing actual unequal beneficial ownership. Verified at HP §24.1 lock (correction of earlier ITTOIA s.282 drift — the operative statute is ITA 2007 s.836 alone).
- Primary (Form 17 — the tax disclosure that references the deed): **Form 17 + TSEM9851 / TSEM9842** — HMRC's "Declaration of beneficial interests in joint property and income". 60-day filing window from the date of last spouse's signature; both spouses must sign; declared split must match underlying beneficial ownership (which is the role of the s.53(1)(b) deed).
- Primary (the SDLT assumed-debt trap when the deed redistributes beneficial ownership): **FA 2003 Sch 4 para 8** for SDLT (England + NI); **LTTA 2017 Sch 4** for LTT (Wales); **LBTT(S)A 2013 Sch 2** for LBTT (Scotland). Assumed debt on the property at the date of the deed is chargeable consideration; if the receiving party takes on a share of a mortgage above the nil-rate band (or any amount where additional-dwellings surcharge applies and the £40k threshold is crossed), SDLT / LTT / LBTT is due.
- Primary (the CGT route for inter-spouse property declarations): **TCGA 1992 s.58** — no-gain-no-loss on inter-spouse transfers where spouses living together; **s.58(1A)-(1D)** extends to 3-tax-year post-separation window per **Finance (No. 2) Act 2023 c.30 s.41(2)(6)**. HP §24.4 lock. Cohabitee declarations of trust do NOT get s.58 — connected-person disposal at MV under s.17 / s.286.
- Primary (settlements legislation traps for parent → child declarations): **ITTOIA 2005 s.624** (settlor-attribution) + **s.629** (minor-child attribution with £100 de-minimis, 18th-birthday cliff). HP §22.9-§22.10 locks.
- Primary (TOLATA 1996 — the trust-of-land regime under which a property declaration sits): **Trusts of Land and Appointment of Trustees Act 1996 ss.1-19** — converts the older Settled Land Act / trust-for-sale regime to the modern trust of land. A declaration of trust on land creates a TOLATA 1996 trust of land; the deed should not need to recite this, but the legal effect flows from TOLATA 1996.
- Supporting:
  - **Trustee Act 2000** — trustee duties under a declaration of trust.
  - **MLR 2017 reg 45 / SI 2017/692** — TRS registration obligation (HP §22.16) — most declarations of trust over rental property are registrable as taxable relevant trusts (or non-taxable express trusts, if rental income is below the tax threshold).
  - **IHTA 1984 s.18** — spouse exemption (interaction where the declaration is a transfer of beneficial value between spouses — see HP §22.5 + §22.X.6 for the LTR overlay).
- House position references: §15.2 (GROB on family-home gifts — see when a parent-to-child declaration triggers GROB if parent continues occupying); §22.5 (spouse exemption + LTR test); §22.9-§22.10 (settlements legislation for parent → child); §22.16 (TRS registration); §24 (Form 17 + joint ownership + spouse mechanics — locked, the operational sibling of this page); §24.6 (unmarried co-owners — no Form 17 route, deed alone governs); §24.7 (adult-child + minor-child co-ownership traps).

**Worked example: Mawell family — parent-to-adult-child declaration with GROB risk**

Persona: Mawell-family. Father (age 65) funds 60% of the £600k purchase of a property the adult daughter (age 32) will live in. Father wants to record the 60% beneficial interest via a declaration of trust.

Analysis:
- **Declaration of trust is required** under LPA 1925 s.53(1)(b) — must be in writing, signed.
- **Severance issue irrelevant** — first purchase, parties choose TIC from outset.
- **No SDLT assumed-debt issue** — father's purchase contribution is gift, not assumed-debt.
- **GROB risk**: if father visits and uses the property regularly without paying market rent, FA 1986 s.102 GROB engages — father's 60% beneficial interest may stay in his estate for IHT despite the legal-title structure.
- **Settlements legislation**: daughter is adult (32), so s.629 doesn't apply. s.624 doesn't apply because father has not retained an interest in the property (he holds 60% beneficially in his own right, not as settlor for daughter).
- **TRS registration**: required within 90 days as an express trust over rental property (or non-rental if used personally); penalty for non-registration up to £5k per HP §22.16.

## FAQ draft (10-12 entries, Stage 2 populated; RUN sub-agent polishes prose)

1. **What is a declaration of trust on property as a legal instrument?**
   A declaration of trust on property is a written document under Law of Property Act 1925 s.53(1)(b) that records the beneficial ownership of land or an interest in land. The legal owner (whose name is on the title at HM Land Registry) declares that they hold the property on trust for one or more beneficial owners in specified shares. The statute requires writing signed by someone able to declare the trust — an oral declaration over land is unenforceable.

2. **What is the difference between a declaration of trust, a deed of trust, and a trust of land?**
   A declaration of trust is the written instrument that records the trust (the minimum requirement under LPA 1925 s.53(1)(b)). A deed of trust is a declaration of trust additionally executed under Law of Property (Miscellaneous Provisions) Act 1989 s.1 deed formalities (writing, signature, witnessing, delivery) — same content, stronger evidential weight. A trust of land is the legal construct that arises when property is held on trust — typically created by a declaration of trust, or by operation of statute under Trusts of Land and Appointment of Trustees Act 1996.

3. **When do I actually need a declaration of trust?**
   Seven common fact-patterns trigger one: (1) spouses holding rental property with unequal beneficial shares wanting to file Form 17 for income-tax purposes; (2) unmarried co-owners with unequal contributions; (3) parent funding an adult-child purchase but wanting their beneficial interest recorded; (4) sibling co-ownership of inherited property with unequal contributions to ongoing costs; (5) JV partners co-owning a development property with non-50/50 profit-share; (6) cohabitees buying together with unequal deposits or mortgage exposure; (7) severance of joint tenancy followed by declaration of a new unequal TIC split.

4. **Can spouses jointly owning property as joint tenants file Form 17?**
   No. Joint tenancy is the "undivided 100% each" construct — there are no separate shares to declare an income split against. Form 17 only works when beneficial ownership is held as tenants in common with distinct shares. Couples wanting to use Form 17 to split rental income must (i) sever the joint tenancy under LPA 1925 s.36(2), (ii) execute a declaration of trust recording the new TIC shares, then (iii) file Form 17 within 60 days of both signatures. TSEM9850 confirms this sequence.

5. **What's the SDLT trap that catches most amateur Form-17 implementations?**
   When one spouse transfers a beneficial share to the other, the receiving spouse takes on a proportional share of any mortgage debt. The assumed debt counts as chargeable consideration under FA 2003 Sch 4 para 8. If the transferred share of mortgage debt exceeds the SDLT nil-rate band (£250k for residential from April 2025), SDLT becomes payable. Worse, the additional-dwellings surcharge applies where the receiving spouse already owns another property; the surcharge engages on any consideration above £40,000. The Wave 5 operational page walks a worked example.

6. **Does an inter-spouse declaration trigger CGT?**
   No, normally. TCGA 1992 s.58 gives no-gain-no-loss treatment on inter-spouse disposals where the spouses are living together. Following Finance (No. 2) Act 2023 c.30 s.41, the no-gain-no-loss treatment extends to disposals up to 3 tax years after separation. Cohabitee declarations do NOT get s.58 — they are connected-persons disposals at market value under TCGA 1992 s.17 / s.286, which can crystallise material CGT.

7. **When does a parent-to-minor-child declaration engage settlements legislation?**
   ITTOIA 2005 s.629 attributes income from settled property back to the parent-settlor where the child is under 18 and unmarried. A declaration of trust by parent over property held for the child engages s.629 — income attributed to the parent, until the child reaches 18. There is a £100 de minimis under s.629(3) but it's narrow. Parents using property declarations to "income-shift" to minor children should be advised that the strategy fails for income-tax purposes during the minor years.

8. **When does a parent-to-adult-child declaration risk GROB?**
   Where the parent retains any benefit from the property after declaring beneficial ownership to the child, FA 1986 s.102 Gifts With Reservation of Benefit treats the parent as still owning the property for IHT purposes despite the legal/beneficial transfer. Typical triggers: parent continues to occupy without paying market rent; parent retains control through being trustee; parent receives any informal "benefit" (free holidays, use of the property). The cure is a clean separation: parent moves out OR pays market rent fully documented.

9. **What writing does the declaration actually need?**
   Minimum: the property identified (address + title number), the parties identified, the beneficial shares specified, signed by the legal owner(s). Strongly recommended: dated; recital of contribution history; tie-breaker rule for shares not summing to 100%; witness signatures (which convert the declaration into a deed under LP(MP)A 1989 s.1, increasing evidential weight). For high-value or contentious cases, execution before a solicitor is best practice.

10. **Must a declaration of trust be registered with the Trust Registration Service?**
    Almost always yes for any deed creating a trust over property generating rental income or other taxable receipts (MLR 2017 reg 45 + TRSM10000+). The deadline is 90 days from trust creation. Non-taxable express trusts over residential property used personally are also generally registrable. HP §22.16 confirms penalty for non-registration is a case-by-case discretionary fine up to £5,000 (the graduated tariff misconception is wrong).

11. **What about the Stack v Dowden / Jones v Kernott line — can't I just rely on intention?**
    The Stack v Dowden / Jones v Kernott line on common-intention constructive trusts works where there is NO written declaration; the courts infer the beneficial split from contributions, conduct, and intention. But the inference process is expensive, slow, and uncertain — many co-ownership disputes end in litigation precisely because there is no written declaration. A clean s.53(1)(b) written deed avoids the litigation route entirely. The Stack/Kernott line is the safety net, not the planning tool.

12. **How does this page relate to your existing declaration-of-trust deep page?**
    Our existing page `declaration-of-trust-property-beneficial-ownership-mechanics-evidence-form-17` is the operational deep on the spouse/Form-17 fact-pattern: SDLT assumed-debt trap walked with a worked example, s.58 no-gain-no-loss route, deed execution discipline, HMRC enquiry pattern. It assumes the reader has decided to do this. This page (C4) sits one click earlier in the funnel and answers "what is a declaration of trust and which of the seven fact-patterns do I have". Readers who establish the spouse/Form-17 fact-pattern then go to the deep page; readers with one of the other six fact-patterns are routed accordingly.
