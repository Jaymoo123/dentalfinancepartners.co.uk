# The HMRC FIC Campaign and FIC Unit: What HMRC's Family Investment Company Review Found, and What FIC Scrutiny Looks Like Today

## Statutory anchor
- Primary (no dedicated FIC-Campaign statute — the FIC Campaign is an OPERATIONAL HMRC RESOURCING DECISION, not a statutory regime): no Schedule of an Act of Parliament establishes the FIC Unit or the FIC Campaign; the unit was created administratively within HMRC under the Commissioners' management power per the Commissioners for Revenue and Customs Act 2005. Sessions writing on this page must NOT cite a statutory FIC-Campaign anchor — the regime is administrative not statutory.
- Primary (the OPERATIVE legal architecture HMRC uses to scrutinise FICs — these are the enquiry-vectors that continue post-2021): **ITTOIA 2005 s.624** (settlements legislation — attributing income from settled property back to settlor where settlor retains an interest; primary HMRC attack on FIC share-class arrangements where founder retains control + economic interest) + **ITTOIA 2005 s.626** (spouse-exception — *Jones v Garnett* [2007] UKHL 35 Arctic Systems carve-out, narrow but operationally important for FIC spouse-shareholding architecture) + **ITTOIA 2005 s.629** + **s.631** (minor-child attribution — bites minor-child FIC shareholdings until age 18). Verified at https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2005/5/contents on 2026-05-27.
- Primary (substance-over-form challenge architecture): general anti-avoidance principles per *WT Ramsay Ltd v IRC* [1982] AC 300 + *Furniss v Dawson* [1984] AC 474 + *MacNiven v Westmoreland* [2001] UKHL 6 + *Tower MCashback v HMRC* [2011] UKSC 19 + *UBS AG v HMRC* [2016] UKSC 13 — the working judicial framework HMRC applies to attack pre-ordained tax-avoidance arrangements; FIC incorporation + share-class design + dividend-declaration patterns may attract Ramsay scrutiny where the substance does not match the form. Statutory General Anti-Abuse Rule (GAAR) at FA 2013 Part 5 (ss.206-215 + Sch 43) — formal GAAR procedure where Ramsay-judicial alone is insufficient.
- Primary (GROB challenge on founder-occupied FIC property): **FA 1986 s.102** + **s.102A** + **s.102B** (gifts with reservation of benefit — attributing the FIC-held property back to the founder's estate where the founder retains use without paying full market consideration). HMRC FIC-scrutiny pattern: founder transfers occupied property to FIC + retains rent-free occupation = GROB s.102B(3) bite + property remains in founder's estate at death for IHT. §22.6 + §22.11 locks.

- Supporting (FIC-corporate-side mechanics the campaign scrutinises): CTA 2010 ss.18N-Q (close investment-holding company framework — CIHC status verification + qualifying-purpose carve-out is one HMRC-scrutiny vector); CTA 2010 s.455 (overdrawn DLA — heightened HMRC scrutiny on founder's-loan account); CTA 2009 s.54 (rent-to-own-property-company hostility); ITEPA 2003 ss.173-191 (beneficial loan BIK). §21.5 + §21.1 + §21.4 + §21.7 locks.

## Key questions this page must answer

1. **What was the HMRC FIC Campaign / FIC Unit in one paragraph?** (A dedicated team within HMRC's Wealthy and Mid-Sized Business Compliance Directorate established in April 2019 to conduct a 12-month informal review of Family Investment Company structures. The unit was responding to political concern about perceived tax avoidance by wealthy families using FICs to extract investment income at lower corporation-tax rates than would apply to direct holding. The unit gathered data on FIC populations + structures + governance + dividend patterns + share-class arrangements through routine HMRC information powers + informal information requests + targeted enquiries. The unit was administrative not statutory — no Schedule of an Act of Parliament establishes the FIC Unit. The unit operated for approximately 24 months across 2019-2021 before concluding its review and being absorbed into the mainstream Wealthy Team.)
2. **What did the FIC Unit actually find?** (The unit's findings were published indirectly — there is NO PUBLISHED FIC UNIT FINAL REPORT. The principal source is Treasury Minutes published in response to Public Accounts Committee evidence in 2021, plus HMRC's broader Tax Information and Impact Notes published in subsequent years. Headline finding: FICs are **NOT being used as significant tax-avoidance vehicles in the population reviewed**. Founders generally have legitimate wealth-transfer + governance + family-business objectives; the corporate-tax + dividend-tax + IHT mechanics generally fall within accepted statutory framework. The unit DID identify drift patterns — substance-over-form risks on dividend declarations, settlements re-characterisation risks on minor-child shareholdings, GROB risks on founder-occupied properties, Ramsay-attack risks on aggressive incorporation arrangements — but these were found to be addressable via existing statutory tools rather than requiring new anti-FIC legislation.)
3. **Does the FIC Unit still exist?** (No — not as a dedicated team. The FIC Unit was disbanded in 2021 and its caseload absorbed into HMRC's mainstream Wealthy and Mid-Sized Business Compliance Directorate (commonly called the "Wealthy Team"). The Wealthy Team has continued to pursue FIC enquiries using the same four vectors the FIC Unit identified — but FIC scrutiny is now ONE of many topics on Wealthy Team caseworkers' agenda, not a dedicated programme. Sessions writing about HMRC's current FIC scrutiny must NOT refer to "the FIC Unit" in present tense — refer instead to "HMRC's Wealthy Team" or "HMRC's continuing scrutiny of FIC structures".)
4. **Where does HMRC FIC scrutiny live now?** (Within HMRC's Wealthy and Mid-Sized Business Compliance Directorate — known operationally as the Wealthy Team. The Wealthy Team is the unit within HMRC's Customer Compliance Group that handles compliance work for individuals + families + family-business structures with net wealth typically above £10m (criteria evolve — verify against current HMRC published guidance at write per §16.27). The Wealthy Team operates customer-relationship-managed (CRM) cases for the highest-wealth families + non-CRM cases for the wider Wealthy population. FIC structures held by CRM-cohort families receive proactive Wealthy Team engagement; FIC structures held by sub-CRM families are within Wealthy Team caseload but engaged reactively via discovery + enquiry mechanisms per §27.)
5. **What are the four enquiry vectors HMRC scrutinises in FIC structures today?** (Four operationally live vectors: (i) **settlements re-characterisation under ITTOIA 2005 s.624** — attributing income from settled property back to settlor where settlor retains an interest; HMRC challenges minor-child FIC shareholdings (s.629 minor-child attribution per §22.10) + spouse-shareholdings where Arctic Systems carve-out at s.626 is contested + multi-class share arrangements where the founder's economic interest is not clearly extinguished; (ii) **substance-over-form challenges on dividend extraction** — sham challenges per *Furniss v Dawson* / *Ramsay* line + GAAR procedure where appropriate; founder-controlled FICs that declare dividends mirroring the founder's personal income needs attract scrutiny if governance is not real (board meetings + minute books + arms-length dividend declaration discipline); (iii) **gifts with reservation of benefit on founder-occupied property** under FA 1986 s.102 + s.102B — attributing the FIC-held property back to the founder's estate where the founder occupies without paying full market rent; (iv) **Ramsay + GAAR challenges on incorporation arrangements** — incorporation timing immediately before a major disposal, share-class design tailored to extinguish a specific tax liability, pre-ordained arrangements without commercial substance.)
6. **Settlements re-characterisation — ITTOIA s.624 challenges?** (ITTOIA 2005 s.624 attributes income from "settled property" back to the settlor where the settlor (founder) retains an interest. For FIC structures: minor-child shareholdings (where dividends are attributed back to founder until child reaches 18 — s.629 + §22.10 lock); spouse shareholdings (where the Arctic Systems carve-out at s.626 is tested + *Jones v Garnett* [2007] UKHL 35 is cited — but only if dividend-paying preference share is a "right to income in a settlement"); multi-class share arrangements where founder retains voting control + economic-growth shares vest in next generation but the founder draws dividends — the settlements line can attack the dividend stream if the arrangement substantively retains founder economic interest. Operational defence: (a) avoid minor-child shareholdings until age 18; (b) document spouse-shareholding architecture against Arctic Systems criteria + ensure full participation rights for spouse; (c) design share classes with clear economic separation; (d) document settlements analysis at incorporation + on each share-class change.)
7. **Substance-over-form challenges on dividend extraction — the *Ramsay* / *Furniss v Dawson* line?** (HMRC challenges FIC dividend declarations that lack governance substance — declarations mirroring founder's personal income needs without arms-length discipline, dividend-control share classes triggered to match founder's expenditure, founder-only board decisions with no real consideration. The challenge framework: (i) *WT Ramsay v IRC* [1982] AC 300 + *Furniss v Dawson* [1984] AC 474 — pre-ordained-arrangement principle; (ii) *MacNiven v Westmoreland* [2001] UKHL 6 — commercial-vs-juristic concept distinction; (iii) statutory GAAR at FA 2013 Part 5 — formal anti-abuse procedure where Ramsay-judicial alone is insufficient. Operational defence: (a) genuine board meetings with full minute books + dated dividend declarations + reasoned consideration; (b) dividend declarations referenced to formal company-law procedures (final dividend by shareholder resolution OR interim dividend by board resolution); (c) financial-statement basis (distributable profits per CA 2006 s.829-853 + accounts evidence); (d) NO mirror-of-founder-personal-expenditure pattern.)
8. **GROB challenges on founder-occupied FIC property — FA 1986 s.102 / s.102B?** (Specific FIC-relevant GROB pattern: founder transfers occupied family home or holiday property to FIC + retains rent-free or below-market-rent occupation = FA 1986 s.102B(3) bite = property attributed back to founder's estate for IHT. HMRC actively scrutinises FIC structures where the underlying property includes founder-residence or founder-recreational use. Operational defence: (a) founder pays FULL MARKET RENT for any occupation; (b) market rent reviewed and documented annually with independent surveyor evidence; (c) ALL rent collected and treated as rental income of the FIC (with full tax treatment + no return of rent to founder via dividend-substitution); (d) where rent payment is not commercially feasible, the founder VACATES the property or the structure is restructured. §22.6 + §22.11 locks. Cross-link to existing `fic-estate-planning-landlord-portfolio-value-freezing-iht-mechanics` for the value-freeze mechanic + GROB-avoidance discipline.)
9. **Ramsay + GAAR challenges on incorporation arrangements?** (HMRC challenges FIC incorporation patterns that look pre-ordained to extinguish a specific tax liability: (a) incorporation timing immediately before a major disposal (s.162 incorporation relief sought but actual business test per *Ramsay v HMRC* [2013] UKUT 226 (TCC) failed); (b) share-class design tailored to a specific known event (e.g. share-class created weeks before a major dividend declaration with no other commercial purpose); (c) connected-party transfers at non-arms-length values (TCGA s.17 + s.18 connected-party rules + transfer-pricing under TIOPA 2010 Pt 4 + s.166); (d) accelerated incorporation under combined-route arrangements designed to extract a CGT-deferred + IHT-discounted + corporation-tax-rate-arbitrage outcome with no genuine commercial substance. The statutory GAAR procedure at FA 2013 Part 5 + Sch 43 may apply where Ramsay-judicial alone is insufficient. Operational defence: (a) document commercial purpose of incorporation independent of any specific tax outcome; (b) operate the FIC for a meaningful period before any major related transaction; (c) keep all incorporation steps + share-class designs + dividend patterns within accepted advisory frameworks; (d) seek formal advice from chartered tax adviser + obtain non-statutory clearance where genuinely uncertain.)
10. **How do I make my FIC enquiry-resilient — the operational defence checklist?** (Six-element checklist: (i) **real governance** — board meetings with full minute books, dated dividend declarations, reasoned consideration, financial-statement basis; (ii) **real share-class economics** — economic separation between classes documented + voting + dividend + capital-rights matched to family architecture not to specific tax outcomes; (iii) **arms-length transfers** — FIC funding at market value, founder loans at commercial interest where applicable, no connected-party non-arms-length values; (iv) **founder use of FIC property at full market rent** — annual rent review, surveyor evidence, all rent collected + treated as FIC income, NO GROB pattern; (v) **documentation discipline** — settlements analysis at incorporation, on each share-class change, on each material transaction; (vi) **professional advice + non-statutory clearance** for any aggressive arrangement; (vii) **continuous monitoring** — annual review of settlements / substance / GROB / Ramsay exposure as family + asset mix evolves over the planning horizon. Forward-link to `fic-property-corporate-governance-board-meetings-resolutions-discipline` for the governance deep-dive.)
11. **What do I do if HMRC opens an FIC enquiry — the first 30 days?** (Five-step engagement: (i) **acknowledge the enquiry notice** within 7 days + engage specialist tax counsel + accountant if not already engaged + secure all FIC governance + accounting + share-register + transfer documentation; (ii) **understand the enquiry scope** — is it routine (CT600 + dividend extraction); behavioural (settlements + substance-over-form + GROB + Ramsay); or fraud-track (CoP9 / CDF + criminal-prosecution exposure)? §27.5 lock; (iii) **respond on a controlled timeline** — HMRC information notices must be responded to within stated periods (typically 30-60 days, extendable); engage cooperatively where matters are clean; push back on disproportionate requests; (iv) **track behaviour-category exposure** per Sch 24 FA 2007 § 27.2 lock — careless / deliberate-not-concealed / deliberate-and-concealed; mitigation via unprompted-disclosure floor where applicable; (v) **prepare for closure-notice + FTT appeal route** if matters cannot be resolved by agreement — TMA 1970 s.28A + 30-day appeal window per §27.4 lock. Cross-link to §27 enquiry mechanics cluster.)
12. **Is the FIC Campaign coming back — should I expect new HMRC scrutiny?** (Speculative — sessions writing on this question must NOT assert a future return as fact.
