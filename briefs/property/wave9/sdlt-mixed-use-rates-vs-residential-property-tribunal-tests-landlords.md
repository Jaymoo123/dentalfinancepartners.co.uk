### Framing differentiator (Stage 2, 2026-05-25)

The page is NOT a general mixed-use SDLT explainer (existing `sdlt-mixed-use-property-classification` covers the classification basics); it IS a case-law walk-through for property investors buying houses with **grounds, paddocks, barns, outbuildings, equestrian use, agricultural overlay, commercial-on-the-ground-floor, partial-let-commercial elements** — the specific factual matrices the trilogy adjudicated. The page covers four things competitor coverage routinely flattens: (a) the **"garden or grounds" test under FA 2003 s.116(1)(b)** as interpreted by Hyman — the Upper Tribunal and Court of Appeal upheld that "grounds" is broader than "garden" and includes land used for purposes ancillary to the dwelling; the test is not "is the land actively used for residential purposes" but "does the land form part of the grounds of the dwelling at the effective date" (the s.116(1)(b) curtilage analysis); (b) the **Suterwalla refinement** (per HP §1.J) — the UT held the "use" test looks at use at the **effective date** of the transaction, not historic or intended use; a paddock genuinely used by a third party under a separate grazing licence at the effective date is NOT garden or grounds of the dwelling — but the analysis is fact-specific and the third-party use must be substantive and contemporaneous; (c) the **operational decision framework** for landlord investors — single-dwelling residential (residential rates, no MDR post-1-June-2024 per A1 + HP §1.H); 6+ dwellings (s.116(7) automatic non-residential — per HP §1.H surviving alternative); dwelling + working farm or business premises in one transaction (mixed-use, non-residential rates); dwelling + paddock with separate grazing rights (Suterwalla pattern, fact-specific); dwelling + amenity garden / grounds (residential per Hyman, no fragmentation); uninhabitable property (Bewley narrow exception — per HP §1.C Wave 7 lock, NOT to be conflated with Hyman/Suterwalla); (d) the **HMRC enquiry-attack pattern** — most buyer mixed-use claims fail; HMRC challenges on Hyman authority; the burden of proof is on the buyer to evidence non-residential character at the effective date; aggressive claims-firm posture overstates eligibility post-Hyman + post-Hortons Hall narrowing. ## Draft FAQ entries (Stage 2 populated 2026-05-25; session may rewrite or expand)

Target 10-12 FAQs. Draft set below; session adapts wording, ensures no duplication of body H2/H3 phrasing, and expands or contracts as needed for FAQPage schema cleanliness.

1. **What does the Hyman case actually say about "grounds" for SDLT?**
   Hyman v HMRC ([2019] UKFTT 469 (TC), upheld at [2021] UKUT 68 (TCC) and [2022] EWCA Civ 185) holds that "grounds" under FA 2003 s.116(1)(b) is broader than "garden" and includes land used for purposes ancillary to the dwelling. The test is not "is the land actively used for residential purposes" but "does the land form part of the grounds of the dwelling at the effective date". The Court of Appeal upheld the Upper Tribunal; the position is binding authority. Hyman tightened the case-law against taxpayer attempts to fragment large rural holdings into residential-house + non-residential-grounds.

2. **What did Suterwalla actually decide?**
   Suterwalla v HMRC [2024] UKUT 188 (TCC) is the Upper Tribunal refinement on the s.116(1)(b) test. The UT held that the "use" test for "garden or grounds" looks at use at the **effective date** of the transaction (not historic or intended use). A paddock genuinely let to a neighbouring farmer under a separate grazing licence at the effective date is NOT garden or grounds of the dwelling because it is under contemporaneous third-party commercial use. The analysis is fact-specific; the third-party use must be substantive, contemporaneous, and documented.

3. **What is Hortons Hall and why does it matter?**
   Hortons Hall (Horton Hall Estates Ltd v HMRC) is a First-tier Tribunal line of decisions (multiple cases; the controlling citation is verified per HP §1.J at write time) on the residential / non-residential line for substantial estates. The cases are typically cited as TAXPAYER LOSSES: extensive grounds claimed as mixed-use have been held to remain grounds-classified under Hyman authority. They function as the cautionary corollary to Suterwalla, reminding buyers that the third-party-genuine-use threshold is high and most fact patterns do not meet it.

4. **What is the difference between Bewley and Hyman?**
   Bewley v HMRC [2019] UKFTT 65 (TC) is a separate doctrine. Bewley is the s.116(1)(a) "suitable for use as a single dwelling" test (i.e., whether the building IS a dwelling at all). Hyman / Suterwalla / Hortons Hall is the s.116(1)(b) "garden or grounds" test (i.e., whether the LAND adjacent to the dwelling forms part of its grounds). Bewley deals with substantially uninhabitable / contaminated / requiring-complete-reconstruction properties. Hyman deals with paddocks, fields, barns, ancillary land adjacent to a habitable dwelling. Bewley has narrowed substantially post-Hyman + Mudan + MHB + Brown (per HP §1.C Wave 7 lock); A3 cross-links our Bewley page for the separate doctrine.

5. **What is the rate difference between residential and mixed-use SDLT?**
   Top residential rate is 12% (above £1.5m) plus the 5% additional-dwellings surcharge (per FA 2025 s.51 — see HP §1.I) = effective 17% top rate on the highest band for surcharge-applicable transactions. Top non-residential rate is 5% (above £250k per FA 2003 s.55 Table B). On a £2m transaction the SDLT difference can be £100k-£250k+ depending on band interaction. The economic stake explains why HMRC enquires mixed-use claims aggressively.

6. **What evidence do I need to claim mixed-use treatment?**
   At the effective date, you should be able to evidence: (a) third-party use of the non-residential element (signed licence agreement; payment record; third-party VAT registration or business rates evidence); (b) the third-party use is substantive and not aesthetic (genuine commercial activity, not a pretextual licence to a connected party); (c) contemporaneous documentation (photographs at effective date, surveyor / land-agent reports, conveyancing-pack evidence of the use). The burden of proof is on the buyer; HMRC enquiries will press on each evidence element.

7. **Does grazing rights granted to a neighbour automatically qualify under Suterwalla?**
   No. Suterwalla was fact-specific. The UT found the grazing licence in that case was genuine, third-party, contemporaneous with the effective date, and substantive. A bare grazing agreement granted shortly before completion, or one to a connected party, or one without active grazing activity, would not meet the same threshold. The decision turns on whether the third-party use is real and commercial at the effective date.

8. **What is HMRC's enquiry pattern on mixed-use claims?**
   HMRC actively enquires mixed-use claims, particularly those involving rural / equestrian / amenity-land scenarios where the rate-difference is material. The enquiry typically asks for: (a) evidence at the effective date of the non-residential character of the relevant land; (b) third-party use documentation; (c) explanation of any structural / use changes since acquisition that might suggest retrospective re-characterisation. Post-Hyman + Hortons Hall, the failure rate of buyer mixed-use claims is high; aggressive claims-firm marketing pages overstate eligibility.

9. **Can I claim mixed-use for a house with a large garden?**
   Almost certainly no. The Hyman ratio holds that "grounds" includes amenity garden / ancillary land. A large garden, however well-kept or however isolated, that is used or available for residential amenity is grounds of the dwelling and is residential. Mixed-use requires a non-residential element (commercial use, genuine third-party occupation, working agricultural use) at the effective date.

10. **What about a house with a paddock used by the seller's horses?**
    Fact-specific. If the paddock is genuinely used by a third party (e.g., the seller's neighbouring farming business under a separate licence; an equestrian business with documented commercial activity), Suterwalla supports a mixed-use position. If the paddock is used by the seller's personal horses (private leisure use) or is bare amenity land, the position is more likely residential under Hyman.

11. **Does the additional-dwellings 5% surcharge apply to mixed-use transactions?**
    No. The additional-dwellings surcharge (Sch 4ZA) does not apply to mixed-use transactions; the rate-application rule is at Sch 4ZA para 18 (verify exact paragraph reference at write time per §16.35). A successful mixed-use claim therefore both reduces the headline rate (non-residential top 5% vs residential top 12%) and avoids the additional 5% surcharge. The combined economic stake is what drives the post-Hyman enquiry pattern.

12. **I bought a property with mixed elements and HMRC is enquiring. What should I do?**
    Engage specialist representation early. The enquiry will press on evidence at the effective date — gather the conveyancing pack, third-party licence agreements, payment records, contemporaneous photographs, and any surveyor / land-agent reports. Be realistic about the post-Hyman + Hortons Hall narrowing; the case-law is against many fact patterns that claims firms market as eligible. If your fact pattern is genuinely Suterwalla-shaped (substantive third-party use at effective date), build the evidence pack rigorously. If not, weigh the cost of disputed proceedings against settlement at residential rates.

### Worked example 1: Suterwalla-shaped win, substantive third-party grazing licence

A buyer ("Acquirer J") purchases a country property for £1.8m: a 5-bedroom dwelling, formal garden, and an 8-acre paddock. The paddock is the subject of a 12-month grazing licence (signed 18 months before completion) granted to a neighbouring farming partnership for £4,500 annual licence fee, with documented monthly grazing activity (12 cattle present at the effective date). The licence is at arm's length; payments are evidenced; the cattle are on the paddock at completion.

- **Test:** at the effective date, is the paddock under genuine third-party commercial use such that it does not form part of the grounds of the dwelling? Per Suterwalla, the analysis looks at use at effective date.
- **Result:** strong mixed-use position. The licence is genuine, the third-party use is substantive (active commercial grazing), and the activity is contemporaneous with the effective date. Mixed-use rates apply: non-residential SDLT rates under FA 2003 s.55 Table B on the full £1.8m, NOT residential rates plus 5% additional-dwellings surcharge.
- **Calculation (verify current rates at write time per §16.35):** non-residential SDLT on £1.8m: 0% to £150k = £0; 2% £150k-£250k = £2,000; 5% above £250k on £1,550,000 = £77,500. Total roughly £79,500. Compare residential + 5% surcharge: residential SDLT on £1.8m roughly £141,750 base + £90,000 surcharge = £231,750 (verify against gov.uk SDLT calculator). Saving from successful mixed-use claim: approximately £152,000.
- **Operational point:** evidence pack is the load-bearing element. Acquirer J's conveyancing pack should include the licence agreement, payment evidence, photographs of the cattle on the paddock at completion week, and the licensee's business registration. HMRC enquiry pressure remains likely but the fact pattern matches Suterwalla.

### Worked example 2: Hyman-shaped loss, large amenity garden claimed as grounds

A buyer ("Acquirer K") purchases a £1.4m country house with 4 acres of garden and woodland. The grounds are well-maintained for residential amenity (lawns, paths, ornamental planting, a small stream). No third-party use; no commercial activity; the woodland is private. Acquirer K's conveyancer suggests a mixed-use claim on the basis that the woodland is "non-residential".

- **Test:** does the woodland form part of the grounds of the dwelling at the effective date per Hyman?
- **Result:** mixed-use position fails. Per Hyman (binding at [2022] EWCA Civ 185), "grounds" includes amenity land and woodland that forms part of the dwelling's curtilage. The lack of third-party commercial use is fatal; amenity / aesthetic use IS grounds use. HMRC would resist the claim; the buyer would face residential rates + 5% surcharge on enquiry.
- **Calculation:** residential SDLT on £1.4m + 5% surcharge: roughly £79,750 base + £70,000 surcharge = £149,750 SDLT. (Verify current rates at write time per §16.35.) A failed mixed-use claim would attract penalty exposure under Sch 24 FA 2007 if HMRC considers the claim careless or deliberate.
- **Operational point:** the temptation to claim mixed-use on amenity-only grounds should be resisted. Hyman / Hortons Hall close the door on that fact pattern. PTP's voice discipline (per HP §1.J) is the post-Hyman realism; do not lead buyers into untenable claims.

### Worked example 3: partial-commercial overlay, ground-floor shop with flat above

A buyer ("Acquirer L") purchases a property for £950k comprising a ground-floor retail unit (currently let to a coffee shop under a commercial lease with 7 years unexpired) and a 2-bedroom flat above (vacant possession at completion; intended for owner-occupation). The retail unit is genuinely commercial, occupied by the third-party lessee at the effective date, with full commercial-lease evidence and business-rates payment by the lessee.

- **Test:** the transaction comprises a residential element (the flat) AND a non-residential element (the retail unit) at the effective date. FA 2003 s.55(1)(b) applies: the transaction "consists of OR includes" non-residential property; mixed-use rates apply on the whole consideration.
- **Result:** clear mixed-use. Non-residential rates apply on the full £950k.
- **Calculation:** non-residential SDLT: 0% to £150k = £0; 2% £150k-£250k = £2,000; 5% above £250k on £700,000 = £35,000. Total £37,000. (Verify at write time.) Compare residential + 5% surcharge: residential SDLT on £950k roughly £42,750 base + £47,500 surcharge = £90,250. Saving: approximately £53,000.
- **Operational point:** ground-floor-commercial + upper-flat is the textbook mixed-use case. Evidence is straightforward (commercial lease, business rates demand on the lessee). No Hyman / Suterwalla complications; the commercial element is real and physically separate.
