## Statutory anchors + house-position locks

- **§27.1 four-bracket time-limit framework (verbatim from HP):**
  - **TMA 1970 s.34** — ordinary 4-year limit (no behaviour element).
  - **TMA 1970 s.36(1)** — careless 6-year (Sch 24 careless behaviour).
  - **TMA 1970 s.36(1A)** — deliberate 20-year (Sch 24 deliberate or deliberate-concealed).
  - **TMA 1970 s.36A** — offshore innocent-error 12-year (FA 2019 reform; applies WITHOUT carelessness or deliberate behaviour where loss of tax attributable to offshore income / gains / assets).
- **§27.1 s.29 discovery threshold + unlock conditions:**
  - **s.29(1):** officer must have "discovered" tax that ought to have been assessed but has not, OR an assessment that is or has become insufficient, OR relief given that is or has become excessive. Low practical bar — "view that there is an insufficiency".
  - **s.29(4):** first unlock condition — situation under s.29(1) was brought about carelessly or deliberately by taxpayer or person acting on their behalf.
  - **s.29(5):** second unlock condition — competent-officer test (officer could not have been reasonably expected, on information then available, to be aware of the under-assessment before enquiry-window closing point).
- **§27.1 competent-officer test authorities:**
  - **Veltema v HMRC** (FTT/UT) — early framing of s.29(5) competent-officer test.
  - **HMRC v Sanderson** [2016] EWCA Civ 19 — Court of Appeal refinement.
  - **HMRC v Tooth [2021] UKSC 17** — current Supreme Court authority on the deliberate-behaviour test; significant case for this page because Tooth ALSO **abandoned staleness as a standalone doctrine** (paras 76-78 per Lord Briggs + Lord Sales — sessions verify at write time).
- **§27.9 do-not-write list (critical for this page):**
  - "HMRC has 6 years to assess all under-declared rental income" — false; ordinary is 4 years under s.34; 6 years applies only for careless under s.36(1); 20 for deliberate; 12 for offshore innocent-error under s.36A.
  - "The 12-year offshore limit applies only to careless cases" — false; s.36A bites on innocent-error offshore matters.
- **Verification mandate at write time per §16.35:** WebFetch (a) https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1970/9/section/29 for s.29 verbatim; (b) https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1970/9/section/34 for s.34; (c) https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1970/9/section/36 for s.36 including s.36(1) and s.36(1A); (d) https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1970/9/section/36A for s.36A (FA 2019 insertion); (e) HMRC v Tooth [2021] UKSC 17 (bailii or Supreme Court website) for the staleness-doctrine abandonment paragraphs.

## Staleness doctrine — case-law depth (Stage 2 develops)

**The doctrine in plain terms.** Pre-2021, taxpayers argued that even where HMRC technically held the s.29(5) competent-officer position, an assessment issued years after the officer "discovered" the under-assessment was **stale** and invalid — i.e. the discovery had to be promptly acted on or HMRC lost the s.29 power. The doctrine was a judicial gloss on s.29(1)'s "discovers" — read as having an implicit promptness requirement.

**The case-law lineage:**

1. **Charlton v HMRC [2013] STC 866** (UT) — early articulation of the staleness argument; UT accepted that a discovery could lose its character if HMRC failed to act on it within a reasonable period.
2. **Pattullo v HMRC [2016] STC 2043** (UT) — affirmed the staleness doctrine; held that a 5-year delay between the officer's discovery and the s.29 assessment was material.
3. **Beagles v HMRC [2018] STC 2103** (UT) — refined the doctrine; held that staleness was a matter of fact-and-degree depending on the officer's continuing investigative activity.
4. **HMRC v Tooth [2021] UKSC 17** — the **abandonment** of staleness as a standalone doctrine. The Supreme Court (Lord Briggs + Lord Sales) held that the statutory language of s.29(1) does NOT support a freestanding staleness doctrine. The "discovery" requirement is a threshold condition met when the officer comes to the relevant view; subsequent delay does not unmake the discovery. **The doctrine is dead at Supreme Court level.** Verbatim from Tooth paras 76-78 (sessions verify exact paragraph numbers at write time).

**What replaces staleness post-Tooth.** The post-Tooth landscape:

- The s.29 power continues to exist as long as the s.29(1) threshold is met and the s.29(4) or (5) unlock condition applies.
- **The "discovery" can be made by an officer earlier than the assessment** without losing its statutory force.
- **Taxpayers cannot rely on delay alone** to defeat a discovery assessment.
- **Practical implication:** legacy strategy of "argue staleness if HMRC took too long" is dead. The fall-back is now the s.29(5) competent-officer test — was the officer's information sufficient at the enquiry-window closure point to be aware of the under-assessment?

**Why this matters for landlords.** Landlord enquiry timelines frequently run 2-4 years from HMRC opening an investigation. Pre-Tooth, taxpayers had a real fall-back argument on staleness; post-Tooth they do not. The page is the operational reset for landlord clients told by older advisers that "HMRC took too long, the assessment is stale" — that argument no longer runs.

## Brought-forward losses pre-FA 2008 amendment trap (Stage 2 develops)

**The historic mechanic.** Pre-FA 2008 reform, brought-forward losses claimed on a self-assessment return were treated under the historical s.29 framework as **NOT being assessments** within the s.29(1) discovery scope. The argument ran that a loss-carry-forward claim was a relief computation, not a chargeable assessment, so HMRC could not use the s.29 discovery power to recompute the loss after the assessment-time-limit closed for the loss-origination year.

**FA 2008 amendment.** FA 2008 introduced amendments to TMA 1970 schedule 1A and the s.29 framework to **bring loss-carry-forward claims within the discovery assessment scope** for losses accumulated post-amendment. The amendment effectively closed the historical gap — losses claimed on a return are now within the s.29 power for the year in which the loss is utilised (or in some cases, for the year in which the loss arises if the discovery is made within the relevant time limit).

**The trap for landlords with old losses.** Three scenarios where the pre-/post-FA 2008 distinction matters:

1. **Pre-FA 2008 losses brought forward into current returns.** Where a landlord brought forward rental losses accumulated before FA 2008 amendment and is now claiming relief in a current return, the s.29 power applies to the **current-year** loss-utilisation but the s.29 power for the **origination year** may already be time-barred.
2. **Loss-arising-year time limit.** Sessions writing must walk the interplay of (a) the 4-year limit on the loss-origination year under s.34, (b) the carry-forward chain extending the practical reach of HMRC's enquiry into the loss claim, and (c) the s.36 extension where carelessness or deliberate behaviour caused the loss to be overstated.
3. **Property-rental-loss specific.** Per ITTOIA 2005 s.118 + s.120, property rental losses can be carried forward indefinitely against future rental income from the same business. HMRC's s.29 reach into a loss claimed 8 years ago turns on whether the loss-origination year is within the careless 6-year / deliberate 20-year window, NOT on the 4-year ordinary window applied to the loss-utilisation year.

**Practical writing rule for sessions:** the pre-FA 2008 mechanic is now narrow but still operationally relevant for landlords with substantial accumulated rental losses. Stage 2 needs to walk one worked example: landlord with £80k brought-forward rental losses originating 2005/06 to 2007/08 (pre-FA 2008); HMRC opens current-year enquiry in 2026/27; HMRC's s.29 reach into the loss-origination years depends on the behaviour analysis for those years AND the pre-/post-FA 2008 amendment status of the loss claim.

## Worked scenarios (Stage 2 develops 4-5 across time-limit brackets)

Stage 2 must walk through:

1. **Ordinary 4-year on-shore careless not in play** — landlord with 2 years of arithmetic error in rental computations (no carelessness, just maths). HMRC discovers in year 3, well within s.34 4-year limit. s.29(5) competent-officer test required for assessment outside enquiry window — analysis turns on what HMRC could reasonably have spotted.
2. **Careless 6-year — Sch 24 careless behaviour established** — landlord failed to keep records adequately; HMRC discovers under-declaration year 5 from end of relevant year of assessment. s.36(1) extends to 6 years; s.29(4) unlock satisfied by Sch 24 careless determination.
3. **Deliberate 20-year — post-Tooth deliberate test** — landlord knowingly omitted £40k cash rental from returns over 5 years. HMRC discovers via informant 8 years after. s.36(1A) gives 20-year limit; Tooth deliberate-behaviour test requires that the taxpayer knew the statement was false at the time made.
4. **Offshore 12-year innocent-error — s.36A FA 2019** — landlord with Spanish holiday-let income; took advice that wrongly told them it was exempt under UK-Spain DTA. No carelessness (took professional advice with full disclosure). HMRC discovers 9 years later. s.36A 12-year offshore limit applies INDEPENDENT of behaviour — assessment within scope despite no carelessness.
5. **Brought-forward loss trap** — the worked example developed above; pre-FA 2008 origination years vs post-FA 2008 utilisation years; the s.29 reach analysis.
