## Worked discovery scenarios (Stage 2 provides 5; session writes to prose)

**Scenario 1 — Ordinary 4-year on-shore, no behaviour, s.29(5) competent-officer test in play.** Landlord with two years of straight arithmetic error in rental schedules. No carelessness — the figures were transcribed wrongly from rent statements. HMRC opens discovery in year 3 after the year of assessment, within s.34 4-year window. s.29(1)(b) threshold satisfied — assessment is insufficient. s.29(4) unlock unavailable — no carelessness. s.29(5) unlock analysis turns on whether the s.9A enquiry window had closed AND whether HMRC could reasonably have spotted the error from the information available. If HMRC had the rent statements attached to the return (or could reasonably have asked for them), the discovery may fail the s.29(5) competent-officer test (Sanderson framing). Page walks the test in operational language.

**Scenario 2 — Careless 6-year — Sch 24 careless behaviour established.** Landlord did not keep adequate rental records; HMRC discovers under-declaration in year 5 from end of relevant year. s.36(1) extends the time limit to 6 years. s.29(4) unlock satisfied by Sch 24 careless determination. Sessions writing should note: careless-behaviour analysis under Sch 24 para 3(1) tracks "reasonable care" — failure to instruct an accountant in complex affairs is rarely reasonable care; reliance on a competent professional WITH full disclosure of facts IS reasonable care (Hanson + David Collis line of authority on agent-error attribution). Cross-link to W7 B9 Perrin/reasonable-excuse page.

**Scenario 3 — Deliberate 20-year — post-Tooth deliberate test.** Landlord knowingly omitted £40k cash rental from returns over 5 years. HMRC discovers via informant 8 years after the year of assessment. s.36(1A) gives 20-year time limit. Post-Tooth, the deliberate-behaviour test requires the taxpayer (or person acting for them) **knew the statement to be false at the time it was made** (Tooth UKSC paras to be verified at write time). Pre-Tooth HMRC practice sometimes characterised "blind eye" or "wilful blindness" as deliberate; Tooth narrowed the test materially. For this scenario, knowing omission of cash rental crosses the test; landlord exposure runs to 20 years tax + interest + Sch 24 deliberate-band penalties.

**Scenario 4 — Offshore 12-year innocent error under s.36A FA 2019.** Landlord with Spanish holiday-let rental income; took written advice (with full disclosure of facts) from a UK adviser who incorrectly told them the income was exempt under the UK-Spain DTA. No carelessness (Hanson reliance defence available). HMRC discovers 9 years later via CRS information exchange. s.36A 12-year offshore limit applies **independent of behaviour** — the s.36A power bites on offshore matters even without carelessness or deliberate behaviour. Sessions must NOT collapse s.36A into the careless 6-year (s.36(1)) or deliberate 20-year (s.36(1A)) limbs — these are independent extensions per HP §27.1 do-not-write list. The Spanish-holiday-let scenario shows the s.36A reach in landlord-relevant practice.

**Scenario 5 — Brought-forward losses pre-FA 2008 amendment trap.** Landlord with £80k accumulated rental losses originating in 2005/06 to 2007/08 (pre-FA 2008 amendments to the s.29 + Sch 1A framework). Losses carried forward against current rental income; in 2026/27, landlord utilises £15k of the accumulated loss. HMRC opens an enquiry in 2028/29 challenging the original loss-origination years' computations. The s.29 reach into the **loss-origination year** depends on (a) whether the loss claim was made on a return then within s.29 discovery scope (pre-FA 2008 the argument ran that loss-carry-forward claims were relief computations not assessments) and (b) whether the behaviour analysis for those years meets the s.36(1) careless or s.36(1A) deliberate unlock — even if pre-FA 2008 reach is now available, the time bar may have closed for the loss-origination year. The s.29 reach into the **loss-utilisation year** (2026/27) is within ordinary 4-year limits. Page walks the practical consequence: if the loss-origination year cannot now be opened, the loss-utilisation challenge falls away (HMRC cannot rewrite a closed-year loss it has no power to reopen). Sessions verify exact FA 2008 + FA 2009 paragraph references at write time per drift watchpoint (iv).

## Suggested FAQ entries (10-12; Stage 2 drafts; session refines)

1. **Q:** "How far back can HMRC go to assess undeclared rental income?"
**A:** "It depends on the behaviour and where the income is from. The ordinary limit is **4 years** from the end of the tax year (TMA 1970 s.34) where no carelessness or deliberate behaviour is in play. If HMRC can show **careless behaviour** (Sch 24 FA 2007 careless category), s.36(1) extends the limit to 6 years. If HMRC can show **deliberate behaviour** (whether concealed or not), s.36(1A) extends it to 20 years. For **offshore rental income** — for example a Spanish holiday let or a US rental — s.36A FA 2019 gives HMRC 12 years even without any carelessness, simply because the income is offshore."

2. **Q:** "What is a 'discovery assessment' and when can HMRC use one?"
**A:** "A discovery assessment is the statutory power under TMA 1970 s.29 that lets HMRC reopen a tax year after the ordinary enquiry window under s.9A has closed. HMRC must have **discovered** something — that tax that should have been assessed has not been, that an assessment is insufficient, or that relief has become excessive (s.29(1)). The discovery bar is low — HMRC need only have come to a view that there is an insufficiency. After enquiry-window closure, HMRC must also satisfy one of two **unlock conditions**: either (a) carelessness or deliberate behaviour brought about the under-assessment (s.29(4)), or (b) the officer could not reasonably have been aware of the under-assessment from the information available at the enquiry-window closing point (s.29(5))."

3. **Q:** "Is the staleness doctrine still a defence to a late discovery assessment?"
**A:** "No. The Supreme Court abandoned the staleness doctrine in **HMRC v Tooth [2021] UKSC 17**. Pre-Tooth, taxpayers argued that even where HMRC technically held the s.29(5) position, a long delay between the officer's discovery and the actual s.29 assessment made the discovery 'stale' and invalid. The Supreme Court held the statutory language of s.29(1) does not support a freestanding staleness doctrine — the discovery requirement is a threshold met when the officer comes to the relevant view, and subsequent delay does not unmake it. The fallback argument is now the **s.29(5) competent-officer test** — was the officer's information sufficient at enquiry-window closure to be aware of the under-assessment?"

4. **Q:** "What is the s.29(5) competent-officer test?"
**A:** "It is the second of two unlock conditions HMRC must satisfy where the s.29 assessment is made outside the ordinary enquiry window. The test asks whether the officer could reasonably have been expected, on the information available to them before the enquiry-window closing point, to be aware of the under-assessment. The working judicial gloss runs from **Langham v Veltema [2004] EWCA Civ 193** through to **HMRC v Sanderson [2016] EWCA Civ 19** — both are Court of Appeal authorities on the test. The information bar is reasonably high; HMRC are not assumed omniscient. Where the return and accompanying documents did not in fact disclose the matter giving rise to the under-assessment, s.29(5) is generally satisfied."

5. **Q:** "Does the 12-year offshore time limit only apply to careless cases?"
**A:** "No. TMA 1970 s.36A (inserted by FA 2019) is an **innocent-error** extension specifically for offshore matters — it bites even where there is no carelessness or deliberate behaviour. The careless 6-year extension (s.36(1)) and deliberate 20-year extension (s.36(1A)) are separate, behaviour-driven extensions. s.36A is an additional offshore-specific extension that applies independent of behaviour, where the lost tax is attributable to offshore income, gains, or assets. For a landlord with overseas rental income, this means HMRC can reach back 12 years even where the taxpayer took professional advice in good faith."

6. **Q:** "If HMRC opens a discovery on a year I claimed brought-forward rental losses for, can they challenge the loss-origination year too?"
**A:** "It depends on the year the loss arose and the FA 2008 amendments to TMA 1970 schedule 1A. Pre-FA 2008, the historic argument ran that brought-forward loss claims were not 'assessments' within the s.29 discovery scope, so the s.29 power did not reach the loss-origination year once its ordinary time limit closed. FA 2008 amendments brought loss-carry-forward claims within the discovery framework for post-amendment loss years. For losses arising in older tax years, the pre-/post-FA 2008 analysis still matters operationally. If the loss-origination year is no longer within s.29 reach (because the time bar for that year has closed and no behaviour-driven extension applies), HMRC cannot rewrite the closed-year loss it has no power to reopen — even if they are validly enquiring into the loss-utilisation year. Sessions should pull the loss-by-loss origination dates and run the time-bar analysis year by year."

7. **Q:** "What is the time limit for HMRC to make a discovery assessment for capital gains tax on the sale of a buy-to-let?"
**A:** "The same four-bracket framework applies. Ordinary limit is 4 years from the end of the relevant tax year of the disposal (s.34). If carelessness is established (Sch 24 FA 2007), the limit extends to 6 years (s.36(1)). If deliberate behaviour is established, 20 years (s.36(1A)). For overseas property — say a holiday home in Portugal — s.36A FA 2019 gives a 12-year reach even without carelessness. Where 60-day CGT reporting under Sch 2 FA 2019 has been missed in addition to the SA return inaccuracy, both Sch 41 (failure to notify the 60-day return) and Sch 24 (inaccuracy on the SA return) penalty exposures can apply on the same disposal."

8. **Q:** "If I made a careful mistake on my return, can HMRC still make a discovery assessment after 4 years?"
**A:** "Not under the careless 6-year or deliberate 20-year extensions — those require HMRC to establish the behaviour. But the ordinary 4-year limit under s.34 still applies, and within that 4-year window HMRC can make a discovery assessment if the s.29 threshold and unlock conditions are met. After the 4 years close, only the behaviour-driven extensions or the offshore s.36A 12-year extension reopen the window. A genuine careful mistake — where reasonable care was taken — closes the s.36(1) and s.36(1A) routes."

9. **Q:** "Can HMRC make a discovery assessment if my professional adviser made an error?"
**A:** "Yes, in principle — s.29(4) explicitly catches situations brought about carelessly or deliberately by 'the taxpayer or a person acting on his behalf'. Agent error counts. But where the taxpayer gave the agent full and accurate disclosure of the facts, and the agent's error was the result of the agent's own carelessness rather than the taxpayer's, the analysis becomes more nuanced — the Hanson and David Collis line of authority on agent-error attribution is the working framework. For penalties, reasonable-excuse defences under Sch 24 para 18 and Sch 41 para 20 may also be available. Sessions facing this scenario should run the agent-attribution analysis carefully and document the disclosure trail to the agent."

10. **Q:** "What documents should I keep to defend an out-of-time discovery assessment?"
**A:** "TMA 1970 s.12B requires records to be kept for **5 years after 31 January following the tax year** for income tax (so a 2026/27 record must be kept until 31 January 2033). For corporation tax, the floor is 6 years from the end of the accounting period under CA 2006 s.388. As a practical matter, where a discovery assessment is made outside the ordinary 4-year window, the taxpayer's record-keeping discipline is often the load-bearing factor in challenging the s.29(5) competent-officer position — if the records show what was on the return and what information was reasonably available to HMRC at enquiry-window closure, the s.29(5) analysis becomes tractable. The firm's standing recommendation is to keep records for **7 years** as a one-year buffer above the s.12B floor."

11. **Q:** "What is the appeal route against a discovery assessment?"
**A:** "TMA 1970 s.31A gives a **30-day window** to appeal a discovery assessment (or any amendment in a closure notice). Late appeals can be accepted on reasonable-excuse grounds under the **Martland v HMRC [2018] UKUT 178** three-stage framework. Appeals go to the First-tier Tribunal (Tax Chamber) under the Tribunal Procedure (First-tier Tribunal) (Tax Chamber) Rules 2009 (SI 2009/273). HMRC's Alternative Dispute Resolution (ADR) process is available alongside the FTT route; engagement with ADR does not pause the appeal time limits. Where the discovery assessment is part of a wider enquiry, the s.28A closure-notice route may also be operationally relevant."

12. **Q:** "What if HMRC takes too long to make the discovery assessment after starting their enquiry?"
**A:** "Pre-Tooth, the staleness doctrine gave taxpayers a delay-based fall-back argument. Post-Tooth, delay alone does not defeat a discovery — the discovery is made when the officer comes to the view, and subsequent administrative delay does not unmake it. However, the s.28A(4) **closure-notice direction** is the working procedural tool for taxpayers stuck in long-running open enquiries: the taxpayer can apply to the First-tier Tribunal for a direction requiring HMRC to issue a closure notice within a specified period, and the tribunal must grant the direction unless HMRC have reasonable grounds for not issuing it (s.28A(6)). This is the procedural lever to break HMRC delay; the substantive delay argument under staleness is dead."
