## Worked penalty calculations (Stage 2 provides 6; session writes to prose)

**Scenario 1 — Careless unprompted, full quality, on-shore UK rental.** Landlord with two years of careless under-declaration of £15k each year (failed to keep adequate receipts; under-claimed nothing; over-stated nothing else). Total tax loss £6k per year × 2 = £12k. Behaviour: careless. Unprompted disclosure via LPC route (see C1). Full telling + helping + giving quality. Sch 24 para 10 careless-unprompted floor = **0%** (verbatim verified — no 12-month qualifier per F-5). Effective penalty: **£0**. Landlord pays £12k tax + statutory interest only. **F-5 callout (scenario): some advisers will tell the landlord the 0% floor only applies within 12 months of liability arising. That is wrong for Sch 24 — see scenario 2 callout.**

**Scenario 2 — Careless unprompted, partial quality (telling only), 4 years late — F-5 NAMED CALLOUT.** Same facts as scenario 1 but the landlord disclosed only the bare facts (telling) without quantification (no helping) and without document access (no giving). 4 years between the failure and the disclosure. **Critical:** the 12-month time bar that some advisers cite does NOT exist in Sch 24 — that is the Schedule 41 para 13 case A vs case B framework for failure-to-notify, not the Schedule 24 framework for inaccuracy. The Sch 24 careless-unprompted 0% floor remains available regardless of how long ago the inaccuracy occurred. What changes is the **quality reduction** — with only telling and no helping/giving, HMRC will reduce the penalty by less than the full reduction window. Reduction window for careless unprompted = 30% (max) minus 0% (floor) = 30 percentage points. With telling alone (and HMRC operationally weighting helping highest per the qualitative practice at CH82420 + CH82460), the achieved reduction is materially less than the full 30 percentage points — anchor figure of around 30% × 30% ≈ 9 percentage points reduction is industry shorthand, giving an effective penalty of around 21%. On £12k tax loss, the effective penalty is around £2.5k. (Sessions: see drift watchpoint (vi) on the 30/40/30 split — the precise weighting is industry shorthand not verbatim HMRC published guidance; frame qualitatively where possible.)

**Scenario 3 — Careless prompted (HMRC nudge letter triggered disclosure), full quality.** Same £12k tax loss as scenario 1. HMRC sent the landlord a nudge letter ("we have received information suggesting you may have rental income that has not been declared"); the landlord then engaged adviser and disclosed. Disclosure is **prompted** (the trigger was HMRC's letter, not the landlord's own initiative). Careless-prompted floor = **15%**. With full telling/helping/giving quality, achieved reduction = full reduction window from 30% to 15%. Effective penalty: 15% × £12k = **£1,800**. Compare to scenario 1 (£0) — the 'unprompted vs prompted' lever is worth £1,800 on this fact pattern alone. Hence the operational discipline of self-disclosure before HMRC's nudge letter arrives.

**Scenario 4 — Deliberate not concealed, unprompted, full quality.** Landlord knowingly omitted £25k cash rental per year over 2 years (knew about it; did not declare it; did not actively conceal — no false invoices, no offshore routing, just omission). Total tax loss £20k. Behaviour: deliberate-not-concealed per Sch 24 para 3 (post-Tooth test — knew the statement was false when made). Disclosure: unprompted (landlord came forward before HMRC contact). Para 10 floor = **20%**. With full quality reduction, achieved reduction = full window from 70% to 20%. Effective penalty: 20% × £20k = **£4,000**. (Note: deliberate behaviour also unlocks the s.36(1A) 20-year discovery time-limit per C3 — HMRC's reach into earlier years is materially broader for deliberate cases.)

**Scenario 5 — Deliberate and concealed, prompted, full quality.** Landlord ran £40k per year cash rental through a relative's bank account for 5 years and never declared it. HMRC discovered via informant (HMRC-prompted). Total tax loss £80k. Behaviour: deliberate-and-concealed per Sch 24 para 3 (knew the statement was false AND took active concealment steps — using third-party bank account is a textbook concealment indicator). Para 10 floor = **50%**. With full quality reduction (full cooperation, document access, quantification help) the effective penalty is 50% × £80k = **£40,000**. CoP9 / CDF route should be considered for criminal-immunity protection — see C1 page on the route-selection decision tree. (Note: deliberate-and-concealed also crosses Tooth's narrowed deliberate threshold and unlocks s.36(1A) 20-year window.)

**Scenario 6 — Category 2 offshore careless unprompted, full quality.** Landlord with Spanish holiday-let rental income; careless under-declaration; £30k tax loss over 4 years. Spain treated as Cat 1 (full information exchange under CRS) — but assume for illustration the income is from a Category 2 territory (partial information exchange). Para 4A + para 21A categorisation logic + para 4 standard maxima combine to give the Category 2 uplift × 1.5 on the standard maximum (45% max for careless). The **floor** also uplifts in parallel — the unprompted floor for Category 2 careless is 0% × 1.5 = 0% (verify at write time per drift watchpoint (v); Stage 2 working assumption: parallel uplift of both max and floor). Effective penalty with full quality: 0%. The Category 2 uplift bites where reduction is less than full — partial quality could yield up to the 45% max. (Sessions: the Treasury Order at para 21A designates which territories sit in Cat 1/2/3; verify current list at write time as classifications are dynamic.)

## Suggested FAQ entries (10-12; Stage 2 drafts; session refines)

1. **Q:** "What is the maximum HMRC inaccuracy penalty for a careless mistake on my landlord tax return?"
**A:** "Under Schedule 24 FA 2007 para 4, the standard maximum penalty for careless behaviour is **30%** of the potential lost revenue. Where the inaccuracy relates to an offshore matter in a Category 2 territory (partial information exchange) the maximum uplifts to 45%, and to 60% for a Category 3 territory (no information exchange). With full quality of disclosure under para 9 — telling HMRC about it, helping them quantify it, and giving access to records — the penalty can be reduced to 0% (unprompted) or 15% (prompted) under para 10."

2. **Q:** "Is it true that the 0% unprompted-disclosure floor only applies within 12 months?"
**A:** "**For Schedule 24 inaccuracy penalties: no.** Schedule 24 para 10 imposes no time-based qualifier on the careless-unprompted 0% floor. We verified this against legislation.gov.uk in May 2026 — the table at para 10 shows 0% as the minimum for the 30% standard percentage with no time condition attached. The 12-month qualifier some advisers cite is in **Schedule 41 FA 2008 para 13** (failure-to-notify) — there, the 'case A' minimum of 0% for non-deliberate failures applies if HMRC become aware of the failure within 12 months of the tax first becoming unpaid; otherwise the 'case B' minimum is 10%. These are different penalty regimes for different failures — Sch 24 for inaccuracy in a return already filed; Sch 41 for failure to notify chargeability in the first place. Some landlord cases will have exposure under both regimes on the same facts."

3. **Q:** "Can HMRC suspend a careless penalty? For how long?"
**A:** "Yes — Schedule 24 para 14 allows HMRC to suspend all or part of a careless inaccuracy penalty for a period not exceeding **2 years**. Suspension is **only available for careless** penalties; deliberate penalties cannot be suspended (para 14(3) restricts suspension to careless inaccuracies under paragraph 1). HMRC sets suspension conditions designed to help the taxpayer avoid further careless inaccuracies (typical conditions: file all SA returns on time during the suspension period; maintain digital records compliant with MTD ITSA; engage an accountant for the next X tax years). If the conditions are met across the suspension period, the penalty is cancelled. If breached, the penalty becomes payable. HMRC's refusal to suspend is appealable to the First-tier Tribunal."

4. **Q:** "What does 'telling, helping and giving' mean in HMRC penalty mitigation?"
**A:** "Under Schedule 24 para 9, a person 'discloses' an inaccuracy by (a) **telling** HMRC about it (clear admission of scope, periods, amounts); (b) **giving HMRC reasonable help** in quantifying the inaccuracy (calculations, schedules, supporting documents); and (c) **allowing HMRC access** to records to check the figures (rental schedules, bank statements, property contracts). All three limbs need to be addressed for the **maximum** reduction in the penalty — full marks on all three drops the penalty to the statutory floor under para 10; zero marks leaves the penalty at the statutory maximum. HMRC operationally weights the three limbs (with helping — full quantification — typically carrying the largest weight in practice). The 30%/40%/30% percentage shorthand sometimes cited is industry convention rather than verbatim HMRC published guidance; in practice the operational assessment is qualitative."

5. **Q:** "What is the difference between careless, deliberate, and deliberate-and-concealed under Sch 24?"
**A:** "**Careless** (para 3(1)(a)) means failure to take reasonable care — the test is fact-specific and tracks reliance on professional advisers (Hanson agent-error line). **Deliberate but not concealed** (para 3(1)(b)) means deliberate inaccuracy without active concealment steps — the test post-**HMRC v Tooth [2021] UKSC 17** is whether the taxpayer knew the statement was false at the time it was made. **Deliberate and concealed** (para 3(1)(c)) adds active concealment — false invoices, third-party bank account routing, document destruction. Maximum penalties step up across the bands (30% / 70% / 100% for Category 1 territories) and the offshore uplifts apply on top. Post-Tooth, the deliberate test is materially narrower than older HMRC practice — 'blind eye' or 'wilful blindness' framings often do not cross the test."

6. **Q:** "What is the post-Tooth deliberate-behaviour test and why does it matter for landlord penalty cases?"
**A:** "**HMRC v Tooth [2021] UKSC 17** is the current Supreme Court authority on the deliberate-behaviour test for Sch 24 + s.118(7) TMA 1970 purposes. The Supreme Court held that 'deliberate' requires the taxpayer (or person acting on their behalf) to have **known the statement to be false at the time it was made**. This is materially narrower than older HMRC practice, which sometimes treated 'blind eye' or 'wilful blindness' as deliberate. For landlord penalty cases the consequence is significant — many cases HMRC characterises as deliberate (with 70% or 100% maximums and the 20-year discovery time limit under s.36(1A)) may on Tooth analysis fall back into the careless band (30% maximum, 6-year time limit). Sessions and advisers on a deliberate-band proposal should run the Tooth test rigorously before conceding the band."

7. **Q:** "What counts as reasonable excuse against a Sch 24 penalty?"
**A:** "Reasonable excuse is a statutory defence under Sch 24 para 18 (and the parallel Sch 41 para 20 for failure-to-notify). The controlling authority is **Perrin v HMRC [2018] UKUT 156** — a four-stage test: (i) was there an excuse? (ii) was it reasonable in the circumstances? (iii) did it cause the failure? (iv) was the failure remedied without unreasonable delay once the excuse ended? Common landlord scenarios qualifying include serious illness during the return period, bereavement, HMRC system outage, and agent error with reasonable supervision (Hanson line). Common scenarios NOT qualifying include 'I didn't know I had to declare rental income' (Perrin test typically fails at limb 2) and 'my accountant didn't tell me' (depends on supervision under Hanson)."

8. **Q:** "Does the offshore Category 2 / Category 3 uplift apply to both the maximum and the floor?"
**A:** "Sch 24 para 4 sets the Category 1 maxima (30% / 70% / 100% for careless / deliberate-not-concealed / deliberate-and-concealed). The offshore uplift mechanic via para 4A reads through to para 21A for territory categorisation, with Category 2 territories (partial information exchange) attracting an effective ×1.5 multiplier and Category 3 territories (no information exchange) ×2 — taking the deliberate-and-concealed maximum to 200% in Category 3. The mitigation floors under para 10 uplift in parallel with the maxima on the working assumption of the legislation (sessions verify the precise floor-multiplier mechanic at write time per the live legislation.gov.uk text). The Treasury Order under para 21A designates which territories sit in each Category; the list is dynamic and should be verified at write time. Sch 21 FA 2015 adds a SEPARATE asset-move penalty on top of the Sch 24 offshore uplift where assets are moved from Cat 2/3 to defeat HMRC."

9. **Q:** "How do I appeal a Sch 24 penalty assessment?"
**A:** "TMA 1970 s.31A gives a **30-day window** from the date of the penalty notice. Late appeals can be accepted on reasonable-excuse grounds under the **Martland v HMRC [2018] UKUT 178** three-stage framework. Appeals go to the First-tier Tribunal (Tax Chamber) under the Tribunal Procedure Rules 2009 (SI 2009/273). HMRC's Alternative Dispute Resolution (ADR) process is available alongside the FTT route; engagement with ADR does not pause the 30-day appeal limit. For HMRC refusals to suspend a careless penalty under para 14, the same appeal route applies (with the Anderson-line authorities as the working judicial framework for suspension discretion)."

10. **Q:** "If my accountant made the mistake, can HMRC still impose a Sch 24 penalty on me?"
**A:** "Sch 24 explicitly catches situations brought about by 'the person or another person acting on the person's behalf' (Sch 24 para 18 + agent-attribution case law). Agent error counts. But where the landlord gave the agent full and accurate disclosure of the facts and the agent's error was the agent's own, the analysis becomes nuanced — the **Hanson v HMRC [2012] UKFTT 314 (TC)** and **David Collis v HMRC [2011] UKFTT 588 (TC)** line of authority on agent attribution is the working framework. Reasonable-excuse defences under para 18 may also be available (Perrin four-stage test). Sessions facing this scenario should document the disclosure trail to the agent and run the agent-attribution analysis carefully."

11. **Q:** "Can the same facts give rise to penalties under both Sch 24 and Sch 41?"
**A:** "**Yes.** Sch 24 (inaccuracy in a return already filed) and Sch 41 (failure to notify chargeability) are different penalty regimes for different failures. If a landlord never registered for self-assessment for rental income (failure to notify under TMA 1970 s.7) AND filed inaccurate returns for non-rental income, both Sch 41 and Sch 24 can apply on different limbs of the disclosure. The regimes **do not double-count on the same tax loss** — each penalty applies to its respective limb, not to overlapping amounts. The Let Property Campaign (see our LPC page) is the operational mitigation route that combines disclosure across both limbs."

12. **Q:** "How does the Sch 21 FA 2015 asset-move penalty interact with the Sch 24 offshore uplift?"
**A:** "Sch 21 FA 2015 is a **separate stacking penalty** that applies where assets are moved from a Category 2 or Category 3 territory to defeat HMRC. It sits on top of (and is in addition to) the Sch 24 offshore uplift under para 4A. So for a Category 3 deliberate-and-concealed inaccuracy with assets moved to defeat HMRC, the Sch 24 max is 200% AND the Sch 21 asset-move penalty applies in addition. Sessions writing on offshore deliberate scenarios must distinguish the two mechanisms — they are not the same and they stack."
