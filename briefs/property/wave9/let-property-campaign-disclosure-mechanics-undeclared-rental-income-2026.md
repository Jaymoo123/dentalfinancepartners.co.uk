## Worked penalty scenarios under LPC (Stage 2 provides 4; session writes to prose)

**Scenario 1 — Pure failure-to-notify, non-deliberate, unprompted, within 12 months (Sch 41 case A).** Landlord realised after 8 months they should have notified HMRC of rental income from a single property let mid-tax-year. £4k tax loss for the partial year. Self-disclosure via LPC before HMRC contact (unprompted). Sch 41 para 13 case A 0% floor available because HMRC become aware of the failure within 12 months of the tax first becoming unpaid. With full quality of disclosure (telling + helping + giving): **0% penalty**. Landlord pays £4k tax + statutory interest. The 12-month case A condition matters precisely here — same disclosure 18 months later would push the floor to case B 10% under Sch 41 para 13.

**Scenario 2 — Pure failure-to-notify, non-deliberate, unprompted, 4 years late (Sch 41 case B).** Same landlord facts but disclosure 4 years after the failure rather than 8 months. Outside the 12-month case A window. Sch 41 para 13 case B floor: 10%. With full quality: 10% × accumulated tax loss. On £20k tax loss accumulated over 4 years: **£2k penalty**. **F-5 CONTRAST CALLOUT:** if the same landlord HAD filed returns for those years but UNDER-declared the rental income (rather than not notifying at all), the failure would be a Sch 24 inaccuracy not a Sch 41 failure-to-notify — and the Sch 24 careless-unprompted 0% floor under para 10 would apply WITHOUT any 12-month time bar. The 12-month qualifier exists ONLY in Sch 41 case A vs case B; Sch 24 has no equivalent qualifier. This is the F-5 trap — see C2 for the full mitigation-mechanics treatment.

**Scenario 3 — Mixed exposure: some years never-notified, others filed-but-wrong, prompted by HMRC nudge letter.** Established landlord with 5 properties; never registered for self-assessment until 2024 (so 2018/19 through 2023/24 are never-notified — Sch 41 territory); from 2024/25 filed SA returns but under-declared the rental income by ~£15k per year (so 2024/25 onward is Sch 24 inaccuracy territory). HMRC nudge letter received March 2026 referring to property registry data. Disclosure via LPC after nudge letter = **prompted** on both limbs. Sch 41 prompted floor for non-deliberate (assuming careless behaviour): 10% on never-notified years. Sch 24 careless-prompted floor: 15% on filed-but-wrong years. Two-limb penalty calculation across the disclosure — sessions walk both with worked numbers. On total tax loss of £80k across both limbs: indicative penalty around £10k (Sch 41 limb) + £4k (Sch 24 limb) = **£14k total**. Compared to unprompted self-disclosure before the nudge letter: Sch 41 limb may have hit case B 10% anyway (outside 12 months), but Sch 24 careless-unprompted floor is 0% — so unprompted disclosure would have saved the £4k Sch 24 limb entirely. **The lesson on prompted vs unprompted: arriving 2 weeks before the nudge letter is worth £4k+ in this fact pattern.**

**Scenario 4 — Deliberate-but-not-concealed, unprompted via LPC.** Landlord knowingly omitted £10k cash rental per year over 5 years from filed returns. Total tax loss £20k. Behaviour: deliberate-not-concealed (knew the statement was false at time of filing per Tooth test; no active concealment via false invoices or routing — just omission). Self-disclosed via LPC. Sch 24 para 10 deliberate-not-concealed unprompted floor: **20%**. With full quality: 20% × £20k = **£4,000 penalty**. (Note: LPC may not be the appropriate route if HMRC has criminal-prosecution interest — see scenario 5 below on route selection; for deliberate-band cases with criminal exposure, switch to CoP9 / CDF for immunity protection.)

(Additional optional scenario — Category 2 offshore via WDF + FtC overlay for Spanish holiday-let income with 30 September 2018 deadline missed; covers the WDF differentiation from LPC.)

## Route-selection decision tree (Stage 2 develops; session writes to prose or table)

| Income type | Behaviour profile | HMRC status | **Route** |
|---|---|---|---|
| UK residential rental | Any (careless or deliberate, no criminal interest) | No HMRC contact OR civil enquiry only | **LPC** |
| UK commercial / mixed-use rental | Any | No HMRC contact | **DDS (Digital Disclosure Service)** |
| Offshore rental (UK tax on foreign property OR non-UK resident with UK rental) | Any | Any | **WDF + FtC overlay** (post-30-Sep-2018 deadline missed = 200%/100% FA 2017 Sch 18 framework) |
| Any (residential or offshore) | Deliberate-and-concealed AND criminal-prosecution interest from HMRC | HMRC fraud-investigation track open OR taxpayer wants criminal-immunity protection | **CoP9 / CDF** (HMRC-initiated OR taxpayer-requested via CDF request) — specialist territory; firm refers out |

**Edge cases:**

- **Spousal mis-attribution.** Income wholly attributed to spouse A but actually belongs to spouse B (Form 17 / default 50/50 — see W5 Form 17 page). If both have filed returns, two interlocking LPC disclosures — one per spouse — with one Sch 24 amendment and one Sch 41 failure-to-notify if applicable. Frame as "two parallel disclosures, single tax loss" — the tax loss does not double-count across spouses; the penalty exposure does (each spouse independently faces their own penalty band).
- **LtdCo landlord.** LPC is for individual residential landlords. LtdCo property income flows through corporation tax — voluntary disclosure for LtdCo undeclared rental uses the general voluntary-disclosure route via DDS or direct contact with HMRC. LPC is NOT the right route for a LtdCo landlord.
- **Trust landlord.** Trust income goes through TRS / trust SA returns. Voluntary disclosure uses the trust-specific route; LPC is NOT the right route for a trust.
- **Mixed UK + offshore exposure.** Use both LPC (for the UK residential rental limb) and WDF (for the offshore limb) — coordinated disclosure on the same factual matrix. Specialist firm coordination recommended.

## Suggested FAQ entries (10-12; Stage 2 drafts; session refines)

1. **Q:** "Who can use the Let Property Campaign?"
**A:** "The LPC is open to **UK-resident and non-UK-resident individuals** with undeclared **residential** UK rental income. This includes landlords who never registered for self-assessment, landlords who filed returns but under-declared rental income, and landlords with multiple properties or HMOs. The LPC is **NOT** available for: corporate landlords (LtdCo property SPVs — use the general voluntary-disclosure route); trust landlords (use the trust-specific route); commercial or mixed-use rental income (use the Digital Disclosure Service DDS); offshore rental income (use the Worldwide Disclosure Facility WDF, with the Failure-to-Correct FA 2017 Sch 18 framework overlaid where the 30 September 2018 deadline was missed)."

2. **Q:** "What is the 90-day deadline in the LPC?"
**A:** "Once you notify HMRC of your intent to disclose via the LPC landlord disclosure form, HMRC will acknowledge the notification within a short window. From the date of HMRC's acknowledgment you have **90 days** to (a) calculate the full tax + interest + penalty liability for all years being disclosed, (b) submit the disclosure form with the full computation, and (c) pay the full liability. The 90-day clock is firm; sessions should not approach the LPC route unless they can mobilise the disclosure work to that timetable. Extensions are sometimes granted for genuinely complex cases — engage HMRC early if extension may be needed."

3. **Q:** "Does notifying HMRC under LPC trigger an immediate penalty?"
**A:** "No. Notification is a **no-penalty-consequence** first step. The penalty assessment crystallises at the disclosure + payment stage, after you have submitted the full computation and HMRC have agreed (or amended) the figures. The notification step is the operational trigger for entering the LPC route; it does NOT itself expose you to a penalty. This is the architectural reason LPC encourages early notification — landlords can engage the route without immediate financial consequence."

4. **Q:** "What penalty band will I actually pay under LPC?"
**A:** "It depends on (a) the behaviour HMRC ascribes to the failure (careless / deliberate-not-concealed / deliberate-and-concealed); (b) whether the disclosure is unprompted (you came forward before HMRC contact) or prompted (you disclosed after a nudge letter, opened enquiry, or third-party information notice); (c) the quality of your disclosure (telling, helping, giving — see our Sch 24 mitigation page for the detailed framework). Sch 41 (failure-to-notify) applies to never-notified years; the case A unprompted floor is 0% within 12 months of the tax first becoming unpaid, otherwise case B is 10%. Sch 24 (inaccuracy) applies to filed-but-wrong years; the careless-unprompted floor is 0% with **no time-based qualifier**. Most LPC disclosures by residential landlords without HMRC criminal interest land in the careless band; with full unprompted disclosure within the Sch 41 12-month window, penalty can be 0%."

5. **Q:** "What is the difference between LPC, WDF, DDS, and CoP9?"
**A:** "These are four distinct disclosure routes that engage different penalty regimes. **LPC** is taxpayer-initiated, for UK residential rental income (individuals only), operating within Sch 41 and Sch 24 frameworks with unprompted-disclosure mitigation. **WDF** is taxpayer-initiated, for any offshore tax matters, with the FA 2017 Sch 18 Failure-to-Correct framework overlaid (200% standalone penalty for post-30-September-2018 disclosures). **DDS** is the general taxpayer-initiated disclosure route via gov.uk for tax matters not covered by LPC or WDF — commercial property income, non-rental income, LtdCo matters. **CoP9 / CDF** is HMRC's civil-fraud-investigation track, offering criminal-prosecution immunity for matters disclosed via the Contractual Disclosure Facility — HMRC-initiated (HMRC sends a CoP9 letter offering CDF) OR taxpayer-requested (taxpayer asks HMRC to put them under CoP9). LPC is NOT a substitute for CoP9 where criminal exposure exists; LPC offers no criminal-prosecution immunity."

6. **Q:** "Does LPC give me immunity from criminal prosecution?"
**A:** "**No.** LPC is a civil-penalty mitigation route; it does NOT offer criminal-prosecution immunity. The only HMRC route that offers criminal-immunity protection is the **Contractual Disclosure Facility (CDF) under Code of Practice 9 (CoP9)**. Where the undeclared rental income is sufficient in scale or involves active concealment that could attract HMRC's Fraud Investigation Service interest, LPC is the wrong route — switch to CoP9 / CDF for immunity protection. This is specialist territory; the firm refers CoP9 / CDF cases to specialist tax-investigations counsel."

7. **Q:** "What does 'unprompted' vs 'prompted' mean for my LPC penalty?"
**A:** "**Unprompted** means you came forward to HMRC about the under-declared rental income on your own initiative, before HMRC made any contact suggesting they were aware (or about to become aware) of the issue. **Prompted** means HMRC contacted you first — a nudge letter referencing property registry data, an information notice to your letting agent, an opened enquiry, an LPC-trigger email referencing data exchange — and you then disclosed in response. The mitigation floor for unprompted disclosure is materially lower than for prompted: under Sch 41 (un-notified years), unprompted non-deliberate within 12 months can be 0% vs 10% prompted; under Sch 24 (inaccurate-return years), unprompted careless can be 0% vs 15% prompted. On a six-figure tax loss the prompted/unprompted differential can run to tens of thousands. The operational discipline: if you suspect a nudge letter is on its way, self-disclose first."

8. **Q:** "What if I get HMRC's nudge letter before I disclose under LPC?"
**A:** "The disclosure is then **prompted**, not unprompted, and the higher mitigation floors apply. But LPC is still available as a route — the prompted floors (Sch 41 case A 10% / case B 10% non-deliberate; Sch 24 careless 15%) are still materially better than not disclosing at all and waiting for HMRC to assess. Engage adviser and respond to the nudge letter within HMRC's specified timetable — usually 30 days for a substantive response — and lodge the LPC notification in parallel. Our nudge-letter response playbook page covers the operational mechanics in detail."

9. **Q:** "Can I be charged penalties on BOTH the years I never notified AND the years I filed but under-declared?"
**A:** "Yes. The Sch 41 failure-to-notify regime applies to years you never notified HMRC of chargeability (under TMA 1970 s.7's 6-month notification rule); the Sch 24 inaccuracy regime applies to years you DID file a return but under-declared the rental income. On a mixed-exposure LPC disclosure (some years never-notified, others filed-but-wrong), both regimes apply on their respective limbs. They do NOT double-count on the same tax loss — each penalty applies to its own limb only. Sessions should be alert: if your prior adviser characterised everything as 'Sch 24' or everything as 'Sch 41', they may have got the split wrong and miscalculated the penalty exposure."

10. **Q:** "How does the 'quality of disclosure' affect my LPC penalty?"
**A:** "Under both Sch 41 and Sch 24, the penalty is reduced from the statutory maximum toward the statutory floor by reference to three quality limbs: **telling** HMRC about the inaccuracy (clear admission of scope, periods, amounts); **helping** HMRC quantify the inaccuracy (calculations, schedules, supporting documents); and **giving** HMRC access to records to check the figures (rental schedules, bank statements, property contracts). Full marks on all three drops the penalty to the floor; partial marks land somewhere between maximum and floor. HMRC weights the three limbs qualitatively in determining the reduction — helping (full quantification) typically carries the largest weight in practice. See our Sch 24 mitigation page for the detailed framework."

11. **Q:** "What records do I need to disclose under LPC?"
**A:** "Sessions assemble: (i) the rental income computations for each year being disclosed (gross rents, allowable expenses, finance costs subject to s.24 restriction, capital allowances if applicable); (ii) supporting documents — letting agent statements, bank statements showing rental deposits, tenancy agreements, mortgage statements, repair invoices, property contracts; (iii) prior tax returns for years already filed (to show what was originally declared); (iv) any HMRC correspondence on the matter. Statutory record-retention under TMA 1970 s.12B is 5 years after 31 January following the tax year for income tax — but in practice LPC disclosures often reach back further than the retention floor, so document reconstruction may be needed. Our record-retention page (W7 B10) covers the operational discipline."

12. **Q:** "If I am offshore (overseas property OR non-UK resident with UK rental), should I use LPC or WDF?"
**A:** "Use **WDF** (Worldwide Disclosure Facility) for offshore property income — that is the offshore-specific route. The Failure-to-Correct (FtC) framework under FA 2017 Sch 18 overlays WDF for any offshore tax matters where disclosure was due before 30 September 2018 and not made — post-deadline disclosure attracts a minimum 200% penalty (reducible to 100% on full unprompted disclosure). Non-UK resident landlords with UK rental income face a more nuanced choice: the rental income itself is UK-source, so LPC may apply for the rental limb, but if there is any offshore aspect (the landlord's other affairs; offshore bank accounts receiving rent), WDF + FtC may also engage. Mixed UK + offshore exposure requires coordinated WDF + LPC disclosure on the same factual matrix; specialist firm coordination recommended."
