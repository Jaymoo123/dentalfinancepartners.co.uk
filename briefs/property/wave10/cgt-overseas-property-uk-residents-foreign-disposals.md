# Working title: CGT on Overseas Property for UK Residents: FX Base Cost, SA108 and Treaty Relief

## Statutory anchor

**Primary:** TCGA 1992 s.1(1) — UK residents are subject to CGT on worldwide gains including gains on overseas property.

**Supporting:**
- TCGA 1992 s.38 / s.41 — allowable expenditure rules apply equally to overseas property (acquisition cost, enhancement expenditure, incidental costs of disposal).
- **FX conversion authority: Bentley v Pike [1981] 53 TC 590 and Capcount Trading v Evans [1992] 65 TC 545, per HMRC CG78310.** Each amount (acquisition cost, enhancement expenditure, disposal proceeds) converts to sterling at the spot rate on the date it was incurred or received. Never compute the gain in foreign currency and then convert the net gain at one rate. TCGA 1992 s.252 is NOT the authority here (s.252 concerns foreign-currency bank-account debts); do NOT cite it.
- TIOPA 2010 s.18 / s.112 — double taxation relief; credit for foreign tax paid on the same gain (credit method, NOT exemption). Credit is capped at the UK CGT attributable to the same gain.
- TCGA 1992 s.222-s.226 (PRR) — available on overseas property if it was the taxpayer's only or main residence at the relevant time; the 9-month final period and the nomination rules apply equally (no physical UK presence requirement for PPR; the property simply needs to have been the main residence).
- Self Assessment: SA108 (Capital Gains Summary) and foreign supplementary pages — both required for an overseas property disposal. NOT the 60-day UK land return.

**House position reference:** §10 (DTA general framing, foreign tax credit must be claimed). §16 (DTA article map; Art 13 capital gains; §16.3 specifically). §5 (CGT rates 18%/24%; 60-day reporting for UK land only — does NOT apply to overseas property). §39 (LOCKED: FX conversion per Bentley v Pike / Capcount Trading / CG78310; 60-day return is UK-land-only; overseas disposals report on SA108 + foreign pages at Self Assessment; TIOPA 2010 credit capped at UK CGT on same gain; s.252 is the WRONG citation and must NOT appear).

## Worked example: full FX conversion with FTCR

### Facts

Marcus is a UK tax resident. He bought a Spanish apartment in June 2015 for EUR 200,000. He sold it in September 2026 for EUR 320,000. He pays estate agent fees in Spain of EUR 9,600.

**Exchange rates:**
- June 2015 (acquisition date): EUR/GBP = 1.40 (i.e. £1 = EUR 1.40, so EUR 200,000 = £142,857)
- September 2026 (disposal date): EUR/GBP = 1.18 (i.e. £1 = EUR 1.18, so EUR 1 = £0.847)

**Spanish capital gains tax paid:** EUR 16,000 (equivalent to £13,559 at disposal-date rate).

### CGT computation

**Step 1: Convert each amount at its own transaction-date spot rate (Bentley v Pike / CG78310).**

| Amount | EUR | Rate | GBP |
|---|---|---|---|
| Acquisition cost (June 2015) | 200,000 | 1.40 | £142,857 |
| Disposal proceeds (Sep 2026) | 320,000 | 1.18 | £271,186 |
| Incidental costs of disposal (agent fees, Sep 2026) | 9,600 | 1.18 | £8,136 |

**Step 2: Compute the sterling gain.**

| | |
|---|---|
| Disposal proceeds | £271,186 |
| Less: incidental costs of disposal | (£8,136) |
| Net proceeds | £263,050 |
| Less: acquisition cost (sterling at acquisition date) | (£142,857) |
| **Gross gain** | **£120,193** |

Note: the sterling gain (£120,193) differs from the EUR gain converted at disposal rate (EUR 110,400 x 1/1.18 = £93,559). The difference (£26,634) reflects the FX component — sterling weakened against EUR between 2015 and 2026, which inflates the sterling gain. This is a genuine taxable component; there is no FX exemption.

**Step 3: Apply reliefs.**

- PPR: Marcus never occupied as main residence — no PPR available.
- AEA: £3,000 (2026/27).
- Chargeable gain: £120,193 - £3,000 = £117,193.
- Marcus is a higher-rate taxpayer: UK CGT = £117,193 x 24% = £28,126.

**Step 4: Foreign Tax Credit Relief (FTCR) — TIOPA 2010.**

- Spanish tax paid: EUR 16,000 = £13,559 (at disposal-date rate).
- UK CGT on the same gain: £28,126.
- FTCR cap: the lower of (a) foreign tax paid on the overseas gain (£13,559) and (b) UK CGT on the same gain (£28,126).
- FTCR = £13,559 (foreign tax is lower, so full credit given).
- **Net UK CGT payable: £28,126 - £13,559 = £14,567.**

**Step 5: Reporting.**

- NOT a 60-day return (UK land only — §39 / §5).
- Report on SA108 (Capital Gains Summary) + foreign supplementary pages in the annual Self Assessment return.
- Deadline: 31 January following the end of the tax year of disposal (31 January 2028 for a September 2026 disposal in 2026/27).

### Key insight from example

The FX component means the sterling gain (£120,193) is significantly higher than the EUR gain expressed in sterling at one rate. A UK resident who sold an overseas property when the pound was weak at acquisition and strong at disposal would see a reduced sterling gain (or even a loss) on the FX component, even if the local-currency price rose. The conversion MUST use two spot rates, not one.

## FAQ expansion (10 full draft answers)

### Q1: Do UK residents pay CGT on overseas property?

Yes. UK tax residents are subject to CGT on worldwide gains, including gains on property located outside the UK. This is established by TCGA 1992 s.1(1). It does not matter whether the overseas country also taxes the gain — the UK charges CGT on the same gain, subject to a credit for any foreign tax already paid (see Q3 below).

The only exceptions are limited: certain DTAs restrict UK taxing rights on capital gains (rare for property; most DTA Art 13 provisions give both states taxing rights on real property gains), and temporary non-residents returning to the UK may have specific rules under TCGA 1992 s.10A.

### Q2: How do I convert foreign currency amounts to sterling for CGT?

Each amount in the CGT computation converts to sterling at the exchange rate on the date that amount was incurred or received. This is established by Bentley v Pike [1981] 53 TC 590 and Capcount Trading v Evans [1992] 65 TC 545, confirmed by HMRC at CG78310.

In practice:
- The acquisition cost converts at the rate on the date you completed the purchase.
- Enhancement expenditure converts at the rate on the date it was incurred.
- Disposal proceeds convert at the rate on the date of completion of the sale.
- Incidental costs of disposal (legal fees, estate agent fees) convert at the rate on the date they were paid.

You must NOT compute the gain in foreign currency and then convert the net gain at a single rate. This approach is wrong in law and will produce a different (usually incorrect) result.

HMRC accepts the Bank of England's published spot rates or any reasonable contemporaneous published rate. Keep records of the source you use.

### Q3: Can I get credit for overseas CGT already paid?

Yes, subject to a cap. Under TIOPA 2010 and the double taxation agreement (DTA) between the UK and the relevant country, you can claim Foreign Tax Credit Relief (FTCR) for the foreign tax paid on the same gain. The credit reduces your UK CGT bill.

The cap is the lower of:
(a) the foreign tax actually paid on the overseas gain, and
(b) the UK CGT attributable to that same gain.

The credit is a pound-for-pound offset against UK CGT. It is NOT a deduction from the gain — it reduces the tax, not the taxable amount.

Crucially, FTCR does not eliminate the UK CGT liability entirely if the foreign tax was lower than the UK charge. Equally, if the foreign tax was higher than the UK CGT on the same gain, the excess foreign tax is not repayable and (usually) cannot be carried forward. The credit is capped at the UK liability on that gain.

### Q4: Do I need to file a 60-day return for overseas property?

No. The 60-day UK property return (introduced by Finance Act 2019 Sch 2) applies ONLY to disposals of UK land. Overseas property disposals do not trigger the 60-day reporting requirement, even if you are a UK resident and even if UK CGT is due.

Instead, you report the overseas gain on your annual Self Assessment return — form SA108 (Capital Gains Summary) plus the foreign supplementary pages. The deadline is 31 January following the end of the tax year in which you sold the property.

This is a common misconception: many UK residents who sell overseas property assume the 60-day clock applies. It does not. Late filing of the SA return attracts standard SA penalties, not the 60-day property-specific penalty regime.

### Q5: Which Self Assessment pages do I need for an overseas property disposal?

Two sets of pages are required:

**SA108 (Capital Gains Summary):** This is where you report the disposal, calculate the gain (in sterling), and claim reliefs (AEA, PPR, FTCR).

**Foreign supplementary pages (SA106):** These pages are used to report the foreign income and foreign tax. You record the gross overseas gain, the foreign tax paid, and claim the FTCR. HMRC cross-references SA108 and SA106 to verify the credit is not claimed twice.

Your tax adviser will typically prepare both forms together. If you file online via HMRC's Self Assessment portal, both sections are included in the same filing.

### Q6: What if the overseas country has already taxed the full gain at a higher rate than the UK?

If the overseas country's CGT rate on the gain is higher than the UK CGT rate, the FTCR credit is capped at the UK CGT on the same gain. The excess foreign tax (the amount by which the overseas charge exceeds the UK charge) cannot be credited, deducted from UK income, or carried forward.

In this scenario, the effective outcome is that the overseas tax absorbs all the UK liability, and your net UK CGT payable is nil. You do not get a refund of the excess.

This can feel unfair where the overseas rate is significantly higher than the UK rate (e.g. some US state and federal CGT rates combined exceed 24%). Tax treaty negotiations rarely resolve this for property gains. Planning the jurisdiction of disposal, timing, and residency status before the disposal is the practical mitigation route.

### Q7: Can I claim PPR on my overseas home?

Yes, if the overseas property was your only or main residence for the relevant period. TCGA 1992 s.222 does not restrict PPR to UK properties. An overseas main residence qualifies on the same terms as a UK one: actual occupation as your main home, nomination where required (where you own more than one residence), and the standard 9-month final-period rule.

The overseas property cannot simultaneously be your UK main residence and an overseas second home — PPR attaches to the property that was in fact your main residence. Nomination elections are made to HMRC.

Note: the overseas country may also tax the gain even if PPR eliminates the UK CGT. PPR only protects the UK liability. The FTCR credit cannot offset UK CGT that has been reduced to nil by PPR (there is no UK tax against which to credit it).

### Q8: What exchange rate should I use — acquisition date or disposal date?

Both. Each amount uses the rate on its own date:
- Acquisition cost: rate on the date you bought the property.
- Disposal proceeds: rate on the date you sold it.
- Enhancement costs: rate on the date each cost was incurred.

Never use a single rate for the entire computation. Using the disposal date rate for everything (a common error) is wrong in law and typically understates the gain where sterling weakened after you bought the property.

### Q9: Is there a non-resident CGT equivalent for overseas disposals?

No. The non-resident CGT regime (NRCGT) applies to non-UK residents selling UK property. It is the reverse of this situation. There is no NRCGT-equivalent system for UK residents selling overseas property — the standard UK CGT rules apply in full (worldwide gains, SA108 reporting, FTCR for foreign tax).

See the non-resident CGT page for the reverse flow: a non-UK resident selling UK property (different reporting requirements, different rules entirely).

**How this page differs from the non-resident CGT page:**
- THIS page: UK resident, overseas property — report on SA108, FTCR available, no 60-day return, PPR available if it was main residence.
- THAT page (non-resident CGT): non-UK resident, UK property — 60-day return required even if no tax due, different rates, different reliefs framework.

### Q10: How does FTCR differ from a DTA exemption?

Most DTAs use the credit method, not the exemption method, for property capital gains. Under the CREDIT method, the UK retains the right to tax the gain but gives a credit for the foreign tax paid (capped at the UK liability). Under the EXEMPTION method, the UK would simply not tax the gain at all (rare for property).

Almost all UK DTAs follow the OECD model at Art 13, which allows the country where the property is situated to tax the gain AND allows the UK to tax the same gain as a worldwide-income state, with the credit mechanism resolving the double charge. Do NOT write that a DTA "exempts" the overseas gain from UK CGT — this is almost never the case for property.
