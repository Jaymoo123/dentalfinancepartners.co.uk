## FAQ expansion (8-12 entries with draft answers)

**Q1: How many properties do I need before a limited company structure makes sense?**
There is no universal threshold. The key variables are: your marginal income tax rate (the S24 drag only bites higher and additional rate payers materially), the size of your interest bill, whether you can transfer properties without triggering large SDLT and CGT charges, and whether your lender offers competitive mortgage rates to companies. A landlord on the additional rate with a high LTV portfolio paying £30,000 or more in annual mortgage interest will often find the corporate route worthwhile on income-tax grounds alone. A basic-rate landlord with low leverage and plans to sell in the near term will often not. Run the numbers for your specific situation.

**Q2: Can I transfer my existing properties into a company without paying SDLT?**
No automatic exemption exists for transfers to a connected company. SDLT (or LBTT/LTT in Scotland/Wales) is charged on the open market value of the properties transferred, including the 5% additional-dwellings surcharge where applicable. The transfer also triggers CGT on any gain above your base cost. FA 2003 Sch 15 provides SDLT partnership relief in limited circumstances (partnership incorporating into a company), but the conditions are strict. In most cases the SDLT and CGT cost of incorporation is the deciding factor for existing landlords, not the ongoing tax savings.

**Q3: What is S24 and how much does it cost me?**
Section 24 of the Finance (No.2) Act 2015 removed the right for individual residential landlords to deduct mortgage interest from rental profits. Instead you receive a basic-rate tax credit (20%) on your finance costs. If you pay higher-rate tax (40%), you bear the extra 20% on every pound of mortgage interest out of your own pocket. From 2027/28, the credit rises to 22% (FA 2026 Sch 1), so the gap for higher-rate payers narrows from 20 percentage points to 18. S24 does not apply to companies.

**Q4: What is ATED and does it affect buy-to-let companies?**
The Annual Tax on Enveloped Dwellings (ATED) applies to UK residential property worth more than £500,000 held by a non-natural person (typically a company). The annual charge is set by reference to the property value band and is revalued every five years. For property held for a qualifying business purpose (genuine letting to unconnected tenants at market rent) a letting relief is available that eliminates the ATED charge. However, the annual return must still be filed by 30 April each year even where the relief is claimed. Failure to file carries automatic penalties.

**Q5: How does loss relief work if I have a large personal portfolio with some properties running at a loss?**
All UK residential letting income and losses are pooled as a single property business under ITTOIA 2005 Pt 3. Losses in one property automatically offset profits from others in the same tax year. If the overall property business runs at a net loss, that loss cannot be set sideways against other income (e.g. employment income); it carries forward indefinitely to offset future property profits. There is no time limit on the carry-forward.

**Q6: What are the IHT options for passing a property portfolio to the next generation?**
The four main routes are: (1) Outright gift (PET -- falls out of your estate after 7 years but crystallises CGT at the time of the gift); (2) FIC growth shares (PET on the share gift; founder retains control; value-freeze keeps preference value in estate; s.165 holdover not available for investment-FIC shares); (3) Discretionary trust (chargeable lifetime transfer; holdover relief under s.260 TCGA may be available; subject to 6% ten-year charges); (4) SIPP (pension pots pass outside your estate until April 2027 when IHTA 1984 as amended by FA 2024 brings them within charge). There is no one-size answer; the right route depends on the age of the donor, the size of the estate, and the liquidity of the assets.

**Q7: Can I use income splitting to reduce tax on my portfolio?**
Personal ownership: you can own property jointly with a spouse and split income in any proportion using a Form 17 declaration and a Deed of Trust (where the underlying beneficial interests are genuinely split). Without a Form 17 a 50:50 split is assumed by HMRC (ITTOIA 2005 s.836). The settlements legislation (ITTOIA 2005 s.624) can override income splitting where a gift arrangement is used -- the "bounteous gift" anti-avoidance applies. Inside a company: alphabet shares allow differential dividends to shareholder-directors, subject to the same settlements legislation limits for spouses and the child-attribution rule for minor children.

**Q8: What is the six-dwellings SDLT rule and does it help my portfolio acquisitions?**
FA 2003 s.116(7) allows a purchaser buying six or more dwellings in a single transaction to elect to pay SDLT at non-residential rates rather than residential rates (which include the 5% additional-dwellings surcharge). Non-residential rates are lower for larger transactions. Multiple Dwellings Relief (MDR) was abolished from 1 June 2024 (Spring Budget 2024), so the six-dwellings rule is now the main relief for bulk acquisitions. The rule applies only where the six-or-more dwellings are purchased in a single contract; it does not aggregate across separate transactions.

**Q9: What happens to my extraction strategy when the 2027/28 property income surcharge takes effect?**
From 6 April 2027, personal letting income is taxed at 22% (basic rate), 42% (higher rate), and 47% (additional rate) under FA 2026 ss.6-7. Director-shareholders of property SPVs who also retain personal letting income will face higher marginal rates on any dividends or salary that push them into the higher or additional bands -- affecting the optimal extraction mix. The pension lever becomes relatively more attractive as a way to defer personal-side tax; the DLA repayment route (tax-free) becomes more valuable. Extraction modelling should be run separately for 2026/27 and 2027/28.

**Q10: Should I form a group of SPVs or keep everything in one company?**
A single SPV is simpler and has lower ongoing compliance costs. A group of SPVs (each holding a subset of properties) provides liability compartmentalisation and can unlock group CT loss relief (where a 75% group relationship exists under CTA 2010 s.99+). The downside: each company has its own CT return, Companies House filing, accountancy bill, and dormancy/striking-off risk if unused. Most portfolio landlords with under 20 properties keep one or two SPVs; group structures are usually reserved for large-scale portfolios where liability protection or sectoral separation justifies the cost.

**Q11: What is the difference between a property SPV and a FIC for a portfolio landlord?**
A property SPV is typically a straightforward limited company holding BTL properties, extracting profits via salary and dividends to the director-shareholders. A FIC is a more sophisticated vehicle engineered around share-class design to route income and capital to different family members at different tax rates. FICs typically use preference shares (controlled by the founder) and growth shares (held by the next generation) to freeze the founder's estate value. The key distinctions: FICs are designed for intergenerational wealth transfer; SPVs are designed for operational efficiency. A landlord's first company is almost always an SPV; a FIC layer is added later when the estate-planning imperative grows.

These tools are starting points; they do not replace advice tailored to your specific portfolio, mortgage arrangements, and personal income.
