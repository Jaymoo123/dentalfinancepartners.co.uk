**Note on LSA/LSDBA interaction with in-specie contributions:**

### Worked example

**Full SIPP commercial property acquisition scenario**

Marcus is a self-employed accountant (age 52). He currently rents his office for £18,000/year. He wants to buy the freehold commercial office building for £400,000. He establishes a SIPP.

**Step 1 — Annual allowance contribution to fund the purchase.**
Marcus has never contributed to a pension (unused annual allowance carry-forward available: up to 3 prior years × £60,000 = £180,000 headroom, plus current year £60,000). He contributes £200,000 to the SIPP. Basic-rate tax relief adds 20% (gross contribution = £250,000 net of basic rate, but as a higher-rate taxpayer Marcus claims full 40% relief via SA). Net cost after all tax relief on a £200,000 contribution: he receives relief worth £80,000 (40% of £200,000), making net cost of contribution £120,000 to achieve £200,000 in the SIPP.

**Step 2 — SIPP borrowing.**
SIPP fund value after contribution: £200,000. Borrowing cap: 50% of net fund assets = £100,000. Total available for purchase: £200,000 + £100,000 = £300,000. Further fund growth required for £400,000 purchase, or Marcus makes further contributions over subsequent years.

Alternatively, Marcus has existing pension savings of £200,000 in another arrangement. On transfer to the SIPP: total SIPP assets £400,000. Borrowing still capped at 50% of net assets = £200,000 (if no existing borrowing). Total buying power: £600,000. Sufficient for the £400,000 purchase with headroom.

**Step 3 — Purchase mechanics.**
SIPP trustees purchase the commercial office freehold for £400,000. SDLT is payable on the commercial purchase (standard non-residential rates: 0% on first £150,000, 2% on £150,001-£250,000, 5% on £250,001+; for £400,000 commercial: £150,000 × 0% + £100,000 × 2% + £150,000 × 5% = £0 + £2,000 + £7,500 = £9,500 SDLT).

**Step 4 — Rent flow.**
Marcus's practice pays arm's-length market rent to the SIPP: £18,000/year. The rent is:
- Tax-deductible for Marcus's business (reduces taxable profit by £18,000/year — saving £7,200 at 40% marginal income tax rate).
- Tax-free inside the SIPP (registered pension scheme; no income tax on rental income within the scheme — s.24 finance-cost restriction has no application inside a pension wrapper).

Net annual benefit: £7,200 tax saving on rent deduction, plus the compounding of £18,000/year rental income in a tax-free wrapper.

**Step 5 — CGT on disposal.**
When the SIPP eventually sells the commercial property, any gain is exempt from CGT under TCGA 1992 s.271(1). If the property doubles in value to £800,000, a £400,000 gain is fully sheltered inside the SIPP — no CGT.

**Step 6 — What if the property were residential?**
If Marcus tried to buy a residential buy-to-let flat through the SIPP instead, FA 2004 Sch 29A para 6 classifies it as "taxable property." Acquisition triggers: (a) s.208 unauthorised payment charge of 40% on Marcus personally on the value of the asset acquired; (b) s.209 scheme sanction charge of 15% on the scheme administrator. Combined effective charge: 55% of £400,000 = £220,000 tax (before any surcharge) on top of the £400,000 purchase price. This is commercially unviable. Never put residential property in a SIPP.

**Step 7 — Lump-sum allowance (benefit crystallisation).**
When Marcus retires and crystallises benefits, he can take a pension commencement lump sum (PCLS) up to his remaining Lump Sum Allowance (LSA = £268,275 per FA 2004 s.637P). The rest of the SIPP fund remains in drawdown. The SIPP's commercial property is part of the fund value when calculating the LSA usage. The LSDBA (£1,073,100 per s.637R) is relevant if Marcus dies before crystallising benefits — it caps the tax-free element of death benefits.

### FAQ draft answers

**Q1: Can a SIPP buy any type of property?**
No. A SIPP (and SSAS) can only hold "permitted investments" and is specifically prohibited from holding "taxable property." Under FA 2004 Schedule 29A, taxable property includes residential property (homes, flats, holiday lets) and tangible moveable property. Commercial property (offices, warehouses, retail, factories, agricultural land) is specifically excluded from the taxable property definition under Sch 29A para 7 and is therefore a permitted SIPP investment. Mixed-use properties need careful analysis — if part is residential, the residential element may trigger the taxable property rules.

**Q2: What happens if a SIPP accidentally acquires residential property?**
The tax consequences are severe. Under FA 2004 ss.208-209, acquisition of taxable property by an investment-regulated scheme triggers: (a) an unauthorised payment charge of 40% (income tax) on the scheme member, and (b) a scheme sanction charge of 15% on the scheme administrator. The combined effective rate is up to 55-70% of the value of the residential property. These charges arise from the acquisition date and cannot be reversed by disposing of the property. Professional advice before any property acquisition by a SIPP is essential.

**Q3: How much can a SIPP borrow to fund a commercial property purchase?**
A registered pension scheme (including a SIPP) can borrow up to 50% of the net value of the scheme's assets immediately before the borrowing (FA 2004 s.182; formula: (APB + PB) / VA ≤ 0.5 for money-purchase arrangements, per PTM124000). So if the SIPP has £400,000 in assets, it can borrow up to £200,000, giving total buying power of £600,000. Exceeding the 50% cap triggers a scheme sanction charge (PTM135000). The borrowing must be on commercial terms; HMRC scrutinises connected-party loans.

**Q4: Can I transfer a commercial property I already own into my SIPP?**
Yes — this is called an in-specie contribution. The property is valued at market value, and the contribution counts against your annual allowance (£60,000 for 2026/27). Any unused annual allowance from the prior 3 tax years can be carried forward (potentially allowing contributions of up to £240,000 in a single year, subject to available unused allowance and earnings cap). You may have a CGT disposal on the transfer (at market value) but the SIPP itself is exempt from CGT on disposals (TCGA 1992 s.271). Income tax relief is available on the contribution at your marginal rate, up to your earnings. Note: the Lump Sum Allowance (£268,275) and LSDBA (£1,073,100) govern benefit crystallisation and death benefits, not the contribution itself — do not confuse them with the annual allowance.

**Q5: Can my business rent the property back from my SIPP?**
Yes, and this is one of the main tax advantages. Your business pays arm's-length market rent to the SIPP. The rent is fully tax-deductible for your business (reducing your income tax or corporation tax bill). Inside the SIPP, the rental income accumulates free of income tax. HMRC requires that the rent is genuinely at market rate — below-market rent is an indirect benefit to the member and can trigger unauthorised payment charges. Get an independent rental valuation.

**Q6: What is the difference between a SIPP and a SSAS for commercial property?**
Both are investment-regulated pension schemes under FA 2004 Sch 29A and can hold commercial property. The key practical differences: a SIPP is typically an individual arrangement run by a professional SIPP provider; a SSAS (small self-administered scheme) is a company-sponsored occupational scheme, usually for directors of a private company. A SSAS can also lend to the sponsoring employer (the "SSAS employer loan" under FA 2004 Sch 30: up to 50% of scheme assets, 5-year repayment, 1st-charge security, commercial interest rate). Both are subject to the same 50% borrowing cap and the same taxable property rules.

**Q7: Is there VAT to consider when a SIPP buys commercial property?**
Yes. If the seller of the commercial property has opted to tax (OTT) the property under VATA 1994 Sch 10, they must charge VAT at 20% on the sale price. The SIPP as buyer can register for VAT and reclaim input VAT if it intends to make taxable supplies (e.g., renting the property to a VAT-registered tenant who also opts to tax). The interaction between the SIPP's VAT status and the tenant's VAT registration needs to be planned carefully. Cross-reference the estate's OTT guide for detailed mechanics.

**Q8: What is the IHT position for commercial property held in a SIPP?**
Pension assets (including commercial property inside a SIPP) are currently outside the member's estate for IHT — they belong to the pension trust and are not "owned" by the member in the IHT sense. This makes a SIPP an effective IHT planning vehicle: on death before crystallisation, the pension fund (including the commercial property) can pass to nominated beneficiaries outside the estate. However, from 6 April 2027, unspent pension funds are scheduled to be brought within the scope of IHT (per §15.5 HP cross-reference). Writer: at write time, confirm whether the 2027 pension/IHT change has been legislated or is still pending; note the current law (2026-07-08) is that pension funds remain outside IHT, but this is due to change.

**Q9: Can a SIPP or SSAS hold commercial property jointly with the member personally?**
Yes. "Fractional interests" in commercial property (where the SIPP owns part and the member or another party owns the rest) are permitted. Each party's share must be clearly documented and the SIPP's interest must be managed for the benefit of the scheme beneficiaries. Rental income and costs are apportioned by ownership share. This is useful where the full property value exceeds the SIPP's buying power even with maximum borrowing.

**Q10: What happens to the commercial property when I retire?**
The commercial property remains inside the SIPP; you do not have to sell it to take pension benefits. You can take income from the rental income stream (going into drawdown) or take a pension commencement lump sum (PCLS) up to your Lump Sum Allowance (£268,275 per FA 2004 s.637P), with the rest remaining in drawdown. Eventually the trustee/provider may need to sell the property to fund pension income if rental income is insufficient. On sale, the gain is CGT-exempt inside the scheme (TCGA 1992 s.271(1)).

**Q11: Is the annual allowance or the Lump Sum Allowance the relevant limit for contributions?**
The annual allowance (£60,000 for 2026/27) controls how much you can contribute tax-effectively in a given year. The Lump Sum Allowance (LSA, £268,275) controls how much tax-free cash you can take at retirement. The Lump Sum and Death Benefit Allowance (LSDBA, £1,073,100) controls death benefits. These are different limits operating at different stages. When contributing property in-specie, the annual allowance is the relevant cap. When crystallising pension benefits, the LSA and LSDBA become relevant.

**Q12: What is the position on ATED for commercial property held in a SIPP?**
ATED (Annual Tax on Enveloped Dwellings) applies to residential dwellings worth over £500,000 held by non-natural persons. Commercial property is not a "dwelling" for ATED purposes, so ATED does not apply to commercial property inside a SIPP. This is distinct from the taxable property rules: ATED and the Sch 29A taxable property rules both separately prohibit SIPP/SSAS residential property holding (from different angles), but neither creates any liability for commercial property.
