### Statute verification results (Stage 2 WebFetch)

**TCGA 1992 s.162 (verified 2026-07-08):**
- Statutory conditions: (1) non-company transferor transfers to a company a business as a going concern; (2) together with whole assets of the business (or all assets other than cash); (3) wholly or partly in exchange for shares issued by the company; (4) claim made within deadline.
- The statute does NOT explicitly require the business to be a "trading" business. It uses "business as a going concern" without formal definition.
- **Ramsay position [2013] UKUT 226:** HMRC's position (and the tribunal's analysis in Ramsay) is that a property letting business can qualify for s.162 IF the level of activity rises to the level of a "business" rather than passive investment. The 20-hours-a-week benchmark from Ramsay is not a statutory threshold but a factual indicator used by the tribunal. HMRC's current operational guidance (CG65700+) reflects that letting can qualify but is fact-sensitive. Frame carefully: "contested territory; specialist advice essential before relying on s.162."
- **HP §7 confirmed:** s.24 reducer at 22% from 2027/28 per FA 2026 Sch 1. No drift.
- **HP §21.9 confirmed:** dividend rates from 6 April 2026: ordinary 10.75%, upper 35.75%, additional 39.35% (FA 2026 s.4 amended ordinary and upper rates; 39.35% pre-existing).

**CT rates (verified at write time instruction):** Main rate 25% (profits above £250k); small profits rate 19% (profits up to £50k); marginal rate 26.5% (£50k-£250k). Verify at gov.uk at write time per §16.27. Note CIHC (CTA 2010 s.18N): close company letting commercially to unconnected persons qualifies for small profits rate; letting to connected persons does not (HP §21.9).

**FA 2003 s.53 (connected party SDLT on incorporation) — rate-by-reference:** chargeable consideration for a transfer to a connected company is the higher of actual consideration and market value (s.53(1)). Standard SDLT rates apply on market value. For a £400k property with a £300k mortgage transferred to a connected LtdCo: SDLT on £400k market value (including 5% additional dwellings surcharge if applicable). Verify current SDLT bands from HP §1 at write time.

#### SDLT cost of incorporation worked example

Fact: personal name portfolio, single property, market value £400,000, outstanding mortgage £300,000. Transfer to connected SPV limited company.

SDLT on market value (connected party, s.53): £400,000.
SDLT calculation (England, 2026/27 standard + 5% surcharge):
- £0-£125,000: 5% surcharge = £6,250
- £125,001-£250,000: 2% + 5% = 7% = £8,750
- £250,001-£400,000: 5% + 5% = 10% = £15,000
- Total SDLT: £30,000

CGT on disposal at market value: deemed disposal at £400k; gain = £400k - original purchase cost. At 24% residential CGT rate (verify at write time per HP §5), gain of e.g. £100k = £24,000 CGT. Annual exempt amount (£3,000) reduces this.

**Total restructuring cost on one £400k property: potentially £54,000+ (£30k SDLT + £24k CGT, illustrative).** This is the "get it right before you buy" argument. Pre-purchase, this cost is zero.

### FAQ expansion (8-12 draft Q&As)

**Q1. Should I buy my first rental property in my personal name or through a limited company?**
A: It depends on your tax position and how many properties you plan to hold. If you pay higher-rate income tax, the s.24 finance cost restriction means you pay income tax on rental income before deducting mortgage interest, which creates a significant wedge. A limited company pays corporation tax on actual profit (after interest deduction), which at 19% for small profits is often lower. However, a company adds compliance costs (annual accounts, CT600, Companies House filings), and limited company buy-to-let mortgages typically carry higher interest rates than personal mortgages. For a single property with modest borrowing, the compliance friction may outweigh the tax saving. For a growing portfolio with heavy borrowing, the company route often wins from year 3-5 onwards. The critical point: get this decision right before you buy. Transferring later costs SDLT and potentially CGT.

**Q2. What is the s.24 problem and why does it matter for personal ownership?**
A: Section 24 ITTOIA 2005 / ITA 2007 restricts the tax deduction for mortgage interest and other finance costs for individual (personal ownership) landlords. Instead of deducting finance costs from rental income to arrive at taxable profit, you pay income tax on the full rental income and then receive a basic-rate (20% in 2026/27; 22% from 2027/28) credit against your tax bill. For a basic-rate taxpayer, the credit roughly matches the tax on the finance cost, so there is little impact. For a higher-rate taxpayer (40% in 2026/27; 42% from 2027/28), the credit is worth 20%-22% but the tax charge is 40%-42%, leaving a "wedge" of 20-22 percentage points on finance costs that becomes irrecoverable. Limited companies are not subject to s.24 (companies deduct interest as a business expense in the normal way).

**Q3. Does forming a partnership with my spouse avoid the s.24 problem?**
A: Partially, through income splitting, not through avoiding s.24 itself. A partnership between spouses does not disapply s.24: each partner pays tax on their share of the rental income with the same s.24 restriction applying individually. However, if one spouse is a basic-rate taxpayer, splitting income 50/50 (or at a ratio reflecting their actual entitlement) means the basic-rate portion avoids the higher-rate wedge. The benefit is income splitting, not a structural s.24 bypass. HMRC requires that income sharing genuinely reflects the partners' economic entitlement (see PIM1030). Artificially shifting all income to the lower-rate spouse to exploit the basic-rate credit without genuine partnership substance will attract scrutiny.

**Q4. What are the ongoing costs of running a property through a limited company?**
A: Annual accountancy fees for company accounts and CT600: typically £800-£2,000/year depending on complexity. Companies House annual confirmation statement: £13/year. CT compliance, VAT (if applicable), potential director's salary PAYE setup. Plus, to extract profits from the company, you pay a further layer of tax: dividend tax at 10.75%/35.75%/39.35% (2026/27, FA 2026 rates) on dividends above the £500 dividend allowance. The company structure is efficient for accumulating and reinvesting profits within the company but adds friction and cost if you want to extract cash regularly.

**Q5. What is the dividend allowance and dividend tax rates for 2026/27?**
A: From 6 April 2026, the dividend tax rates are: ordinary rate 10.75% (basic rate band), upper rate 35.75% (higher rate band), additional rate 39.35% (additional rate band). The dividend allowance is £500 per person (verify at write time). Dividends above the allowance are taxed at these rates. For a company landlord extracting profits via dividends, the combined CT (19%) and dividend tax (10.75% at ordinary rate) creates an effective combined rate below the personal 40% higher rate, which is the core case for the company structure for higher-rate landlords.

**Q6. Is a general partnership or LLP better than a company for property?**
A: A general partnership (or LLP) is transparent for income tax: each partner pays income tax on their share of partnership profits at their marginal rate, with s.24 applying to each partner individually. Unlike a company, there is no corporation tax layer and no dividend extraction needed. The benefit is income splitting between partners at different marginal rates. The drawback is that s.24 applies at the individual level (so a higher-rate partner still faces the s.24 wedge on their share of finance costs). An LLP adds limited liability protection vs a general partnership but is taxed identically for property income. For most property investors, a partnership's main advantage is income splitting simplicity; the company structure's main advantage is the CT rate and s.24 bypass.

**Q7. Can I transfer my existing personally-owned property into a company tax-efficiently?**
A: Rarely. Transferring property to a connected company triggers SDLT at market value (s.53 FA 2003) and a CGT disposal. On a £400k property with a £100k gain, this could cost £30k in SDLT and £24k in CGT (illustrative; see worked example above). TCGA 1992 s.162 (incorporation relief) can defer the CGT if the transfer qualifies as a "business as a going concern" receiving shares in exchange. However, HMRC contests whether a residential letting business qualifies as a "business" under s.162 (the Ramsay [2013] UKUT 226 case found that a high level of personal activity, running to around 20 hours a week, was needed for the letting to cross from passive investment to business). If s.162 is uncertain, CGT is due on transfer. The SDLT cost cannot be deferred. For most landlords, the restructuring cost makes post-purchase incorporation uneconomic compared to getting the structure right before buying.

**Q8. What is the 2027/28 rate change and does it affect the company vs personal decision?**
A: From 6 April 2027, property income in England, Wales and NI is taxed at 22% (basic), 42% (higher), 47% (additional) per FA 2026 (HP §7, enacted 18 March 2026). The s.24 reducer also rises to 22% in 2027/28. Key consequence: for basic-rate landlords, the reducer (22%) matches the property income rate (22%), so no new wedge opens. For higher-rate landlords, the wedge remains at 20pp (42-22) in 2027/28 vs 20pp (40-20) in 2026/27. The argument for incorporating does not weaken in 2027/28; the company rate (19% or 25% CT depending on profits) remains well below the 42% personal higher rate on rental income.

**Q9. What is the SDLT cost of incorporating if I own the property jointly with my spouse?**
A: A transfer from joint personal ownership to a connected LtdCo still triggers SDLT at market value under s.53 FA 2003 (each co-owner is connected to the company as a shareholder). SDLT is charged on the full market value. The Sch 15 SLP partnership route can sometimes achieve zero SDLT (if there is a genuine pre-existing partnership: partnership returns, separate accounts, joint borrowing), but HMRC scrutinises these closely. See the partnership SDLT relief guide for full mechanics; this page covers the pre-purchase decision, not the post-purchase restructuring route.

**Q10. Should a basic-rate taxpayer with one property use a company?**
A: Probably not. For a basic-rate landlord, the s.24 wedge is minimal (reducer roughly matches the rate). The compliance friction of a company (accountancy, Companies House, CT600) costs £800-£2,000/year or more. This overhead typically outweighs the modest CT saving for a single property with low profit. The company route becomes compelling when the landlord expects to grow to 3+ properties, is a higher-rate taxpayer, or wants to retain profits in the company for reinvestment. For a single property at basic rate, personal ownership is simpler and usually cheaper overall until a portfolio scale justifies the company structure.

**Q11. How does the partnership structure interact with s.24 if one partner is a higher-rate taxpayer?**
A: Each partner is assessed individually on their share of the partnership's property income, with s.24 applying to their individual share of the finance costs. If the higher-rate partner holds 50% of the income, they face the 40%/42% rate with a 20%/22% reducer on their 50% of finance costs. The lower-rate partner's 50% share is taxed at 20%/22% with the same reducer, effectively neutralising the wedge for that portion. Splitting income toward the lower-rate partner (within the bounds of genuine profit-sharing entitlement per PIM1030) reduces the household s.24 wedge without the complexity of a company.

**Q12. Does the 5% SDLT additional dwellings surcharge apply on incorporation?**
A: Yes, if the company does not already own the property. On a transfer to a connected LtdCo at market value (s.53 FA 2003), the company is purchasing a property. If the company's SDLT return is for an "additional dwelling" (which it will be if the company holds other properties, or if the purchase is of a residential property by a company), the 5% surcharge applies on top of standard SDLT bands. For a sole property being the first property transferred into a newly incorporated SPV, the surcharge position depends on whether the company already holds residential property or is a new entity with no prior property. Check the additional dwellings surcharge rules in the SDLT guide before proceeding. This is another reason to get the structure right before the first purchase.
