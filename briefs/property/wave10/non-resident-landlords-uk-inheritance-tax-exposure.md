### Worked example

**Scenario: long-term UK resident landlord departing for Dubai**

Giles owns two UK buy-to-let flats directly (total market value £900,000; outstanding mortgage £200,000 net of FA 2013 anti-avoidance — mortgage is recourse and not connected-party, so full deduction is available under IHTA 1984 s.162). He was UK-resident for 18 of the last 20 tax years before departing to Dubai in April 2025.

**Step 1 — Is Giles LTR?** Yes. He has 18 prior UK-resident tax years (within last 20). He is LTR from 6 April 2025.

**Step 2 — How long is the tail period?** Per IHTA 1984 s.6A table (HP §22.X.2): 18 prior UK-resident years = 8 consecutive non-UK tax years required to lose LTR status.

**Step 3 — IHT exposure while LTR.** Giles remains within UK IHT on worldwide assets for 8 consecutive non-UK-resident years after departure (i.e., until at minimum tax year 2033/34 if he remains abroad continuously).

**Step 4 — Chargeable estate on UK property alone.** UK flats £900,000 minus mortgage £200,000 = net £700,000. NRB £325,000. Chargeable excess: £375,000. IHT at 40% = £150,000.

**Step 5 — RNRB availability.** RNRB (£175,000 per person, transferable) applies only where the property is a "qualifying residential interest" passing to a direct descendant. Investment BTL property does NOT qualify for RNRB (it must be (or have been) the deceased's own residence). RNRB is therefore not available on investment flats.

**Step 6 — Planning options within current architecture.** (a) Spouse transfer: if Giles's spouse is also non-LTR, s.18 full exemption applies on the transfer; the combined estate's NRB + transferable NRB (£650,000) would reduce net liability. (b) Gradual gifting: outright gift starts a 7-year PET clock; CGT on the gift value applies at 24% (standard residential rate). (c) Life assurance in trust: covers the IHT liability without touching the asset. (d) s.267ZC election: not available unless Giles's spouse is or was LTR (spousal gateway only).

**Contrast — "pure" non-resident who was never UK-resident:**
Fatima purchased a UK investment flat for £500,000, was never UK-resident. Net value (no mortgage) = £500,000. She is non-LTR (never satisfied 10-of-20). Her non-UK assets are entirely outside UK IHT (s.6 excluded property). UK flat: £500,000 minus NRB £325,000 = £175,000 × 40% = £70,000 IHT on death. Planning is simpler: no tail-period, no LTR status to shed. Core risk is the UK situs asset itself; options are gifting (PET with CGT trigger) or trust if non-LTR settlor (Sch A1 does not apply to non-enveloped direct personal ownership — it applies to offshore structures holding UK residential property).

**Enveloped property (Sch A1 scenario):**
Fatima instead holds the UK flat through a BVI company (offshore close company). Under IHTA 1984 Sch A1, her interest in the BVI company is non-excluded to the extent its value is directly attributable to UK residential property. 5%+ interest threshold — she holds 100%. Result: the BVI company interest is treated as UK situs for IHT. The envelope achieves nothing for IHT and adds ATED risk for residential property over £500,000. Sch A1 applies equally to offshore partnerships.

### FAQ draft answers

**Q1: Do non-resident landlords pay UK inheritance tax?**
Yes, in two respects. First, any UK property held directly is always within UK IHT regardless of the owner's residence or LTR status (IHTA 1984 s.6: only non-UK situs property of non-LTR individuals is excluded). Second, a landlord who has been UK-resident for at least 10 of the previous 20 tax years is a "long-term UK resident" (LTR) under IHTA 1984 s.6A (inserted by FA 2025 s.44) and is therefore within UK IHT on worldwide assets, not just UK property. The old "non-domicile" exemption for overseas assets was abolished from 6 April 2025.

**Q2: What changed from 6 April 2025 for non-resident landlords and IHT?**
Before 6 April 2025, the key concept was "domicile": individuals who were not UK-domiciled were outside UK IHT on overseas assets. IHTA 1984 s.267 (deemed domicile) extended IHT to long-term UK residents even if technically non-UK-domiciled. From 6 April 2025, s.267 was abolished (FA 2025 s.44 + Sch 13). The operative test is now the LTR test in the new IHTA 1984 s.6A: 10 of the previous 20 UK-resident tax years. The outcome is similar in most cases, but the transition and tail-period mechanics are different and landlords with 14+ prior UK-resident years now face a longer tail period to lose worldwide-asset IHT exposure.

**Q3: If I've lived abroad for 3 years, am I outside UK IHT on overseas assets?**
Not necessarily. The tail period (how long you must remain non-UK-resident before losing LTR status) depends on how many years you were UK-resident before leaving. Under IHTA 1984 s.6A, the tail period scales from 3 years (for those with 13 or fewer prior UK-resident years) up to 10 years (for those UK-resident for all 20 of the preceding 20 years). A landlord who was UK-resident for 18 of the last 20 years needs 8 consecutive non-UK-resident years to lose LTR status. The previous advice "stay abroad 3 years to break deemed domicile" applies only to those with 13 or fewer prior UK-resident years under the new architecture.

**Q4: Does holding my UK property through an offshore company protect it from UK IHT?**
No. IHTA 1984 Schedule A1 (in force since 6 April 2017, inserted by Finance (No.2) Act 2017) applies a look-through to offshore structures. Your interest in an offshore close company or partnership is non-excluded to the extent its value is attributable to UK residential property. The 5%+ interest threshold means any meaningful shareholding or partnership stake triggers the rule. The offshore envelope adds ATED risk and costs but provides no IHT shelter on UK residential property.

**Q5: Can I deduct my mortgage from the IHT value of UK property?**
Debts secured on UK property are generally deductible under IHTA 1984 s.162. However, FA 2013 introduced anti-avoidance rules that restrict deduction where the debt is non-recourse, connected-party, or was artificially arranged to reduce the IHT value without an equivalent economic burden (the "abusive" debt test). Arm's-length, recourse commercial mortgages typically remain fully deductible. If the mortgage was taken out specifically to buy overseas assets while leaving UK property encumbered, HMRC may challenge the deductibility under the anti-avoidance provisions.

**Q6: My spouse is also non-resident. Can we use the spousal exemption to pass UK property tax-free?**
Yes, but the exemption scope depends on each spouse's LTR status. If both are non-LTR, the full unlimited s.18(1) exemption applies because neither is LTR and the transfer between them is fully exempt. If one is LTR and the receiving spouse is non-LTR, the exemption is limited to £325,000 (the NRB upper limit, referenced by IHTA 1984 s.18(2A)) unless the non-LTR spouse elects into LTR status under s.267ZC — but that election is only available where the non-LTR spouse has or had a spouse who was LTR within the 7-year window, and it brings worldwide assets into IHT scope. Professional advice on the election cost/benefit is essential.

**Q7: Does the residential nil-rate band (RNRB) apply to UK buy-to-let property?**
No. The RNRB (£175,000 per person) applies only where the property is (or has been) the deceased's own home and passes to a direct descendant. Investment buy-to-let property that the owner never lived in does not qualify. There is no RNRB planning route for pure investment portfolios.

**Q8: I have never been UK-resident. What is my IHT position?**
You are non-LTR (you have not satisfied the 10-of-20-year test). Your non-UK assets are excluded property under IHTA 1984 s.6 and outside UK IHT. Your UK property (and any other UK situs assets) is fully within UK IHT regardless of your non-resident status — UK situs is always chargeable. IHT will apply on your UK estate above the NRB (£325,000) at 40%. Planning should focus on structuring your UK property holding to minimise the taxable UK estate (gifting, trust structures, life assurance in trust).

**Q9: Can I put UK property into a trust to reduce IHT?**
For a non-LTR individual, settling UK property into a trust does not remove it from UK IHT because UK situs property is always within UK IHT. The trust itself would be subject to the IHT relevant property regime (10-year anniversary charges at up to 6% of value above NRB; exit charges on distributions). For a trust settled by a non-LTR settlor, the trust's non-UK assets would be excluded property (under IHTA 1984 s.48ZA, inserted by FA 2025 s.45), but UK property would remain within the regime. The practical utility of a trust for UK investment property from an IHT standpoint is limited — it may be useful for succession and control purposes, but it does not shelter UK situs property from IHT.

**Q10: What is the tax position if I gift my UK property to my children before death?**
An outright gift of UK property is a potentially exempt transfer (PET) for IHT purposes — if you survive 7 years from the date of the gift, the value falls outside your estate. However, the gift is also a disposal for CGT: you will pay CGT on the gain (current value minus original purchase price) at 24% for residential property (standard higher rate). If you gift to a child at below market value, the disposal is still treated as at market value for CGT purposes (TCGA 1992 s.17). The combined IHT-PET advantage and CGT cost must be modelled with a tax adviser. For long-term landlords with large unrealised gains, the CGT cost of gifting can exceed the IHT saving, especially as CGT rates have risen.

**Q11: What happens to my UK property if I die while still LTR but living abroad?**
Your worldwide estate (including any non-UK assets) is within UK IHT because you are LTR. Your UK property is also within UK IHT. Your executors must report the worldwide estate on IHT400. Any double-tax treaty between the UK and your country of residence may provide relief for tax paid there on the same assets, but the UK-IHT liability on UK property is not eliminated by a DTA — it is reduced only by credit relief.

**Q12: Will the IHT rules change again for non-residents?**
The FA 2025 LTR architecture was significant and was introduced alongside the wider non-dom reforms. The government has stated no further reforms are planned in the immediate term, but tax law changes regularly. The key current position (as of 2026-07-08) is: s.267 deemed domicile abolished; LTR test in s.6A operative; Sch A1 enveloped property look-through continues unchanged. Writer: do not speculate on future changes; direct readers to take professional advice annually given the rapidly evolving landscape.
