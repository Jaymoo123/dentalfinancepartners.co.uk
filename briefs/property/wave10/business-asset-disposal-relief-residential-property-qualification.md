### Worked example

**Scenario A: buy-to-let company disposal (fails BADR)**

Rachel incorporated her buy-to-let portfolio (6 properties, total value £1.4m) into PropCo Ltd in 2021. In 2026 she sells all shares in PropCo for £1.6m (gain £200,000 above indexed base).

PropCo's activities: collecting rent, managing leases, occasional maintenance. No development, no substantial services to tenants. PropCo is a property investment company, not a trading company under TCGA 1992 s.169S.

BADR not available. CGT on £200,000 gain at 24% (upper rate residential; but shares in an investment company attract the non-residential CGT rate which is also 24% post-FA 2025) = £48,000 tax. If BADR applied hypothetically: 18% × £200,000 = £36,000 — saving £12,000.

**Scenario B: genuine property trading company disposal (qualifies for BADR)**

Simon runs PropTrade Ltd: buys residential properties, renovates and sells them (10-12 transactions per year). He holds shares for 3 years (2023-2026) with a 30%+ stake. PropTrade's income is almost entirely from property sales (trading profit). PropTrade is a trading company per s.169S — it does not "to a substantial extent" carry on non-trading activities (CG64090 indicators: income is trading receipts; no significant passive-investment assets; expenses and management time are for the trade).

Simon sells his 30% stake in 2026/27 for a gain of £250,000. BADR applies (trading company, 2-year ownership, 5%+ stake per s.169I). Tax at 18% = £45,000. Without BADR: 24% = £60,000. Saving: £15,000. Lifetime limit check: Simon has used £0 of his £1m limit previously; this disposal uses £250,000, leaving £750,000 headroom.

**Scenario C: former FHL — post-abolition serviced accommodation edge case**

Carla ran a furnished holiday let (FHL) in the Cotswolds. Before 6 April 2025 (FHL abolition), she could have claimed BADR on disposal because FHL was deemed a qualifying trade under ITTOIA 2005 s.264. After 6 April 2025, that statutory deeming is gone (FA 2025 Sch 5). Carla now offers short-let serviced accommodation with daily cleaning, continental breakfast, and concierge services.

Does Carla's post-abolition operation qualify as a trade? This is fact-specific. Under HP §5.A, there is NO HMRC bright-line. The test is whether the services Carla provides are so extensive that the activity constitutes a genuine trade (badges of trade; BIM20050+ indicators) rather than passive property investment plus minor services. HMRC has not published post-abolition guidance specifically addressing this. The IHT BPR cases (Pawson [2013] UKUT 050 (TCC), Ross) are analogous at best; they apply a different statutory test (IHTA 1984 s.105(3) "mainly investments") and are not binding authority on BADR's s.169S trade test, which is stricter.

Writer: always present this as "fact-specific professional advice required — no safe harbour exists post-abolition." Never write "serviced accommodation qualifies automatically" or "serviced accommodation is now a trade."

### FAQ draft answers

**Q1: Can I claim BADR when I sell a buy-to-let property?**
No, not on a direct disposal of a buy-to-let investment property. BADR (under TCGA 1992 s.169H and s.169I) requires a "material disposal of business assets" — and "business" means a trade, profession, or vocation, not a property investment. Collecting rent from tenants is investment activity, not trading. The lower BADR rate (18% from 6 April 2026) does not apply to standard BTL sales; you pay the normal residential CGT rates (18% / 24%).

**Q2: What is the current BADR rate and lifetime limit?**
From 6 April 2026, the BADR rate is 18% (FA 2025 s.8 amending TCGA 1992 s.169N). The lifetime limit remains £1 million of qualifying gains across all disposals in your lifetime (s.169N(4) — unchanged). Note that before 6 April 2026 the rate was 14% (from 6 April 2025), and before that 10%. Gains above £1 million pay the standard CGT rate even if the disposal otherwise qualifies.

**Q3: Did the FHL abolition affect BADR?**
Yes. Before 6 April 2025, furnished holiday lets were treated as trading businesses under ITTOIA 2005 s.264, and a disposal of an FHL could qualify for BADR. The FHL regime was abolished from 6 April 2025 (Finance Act 2025 Schedule 5). There is now no statutory deeming that treats holiday lets or short-lets as trades for BADR. This means many former FHL owners who planned to sell and claim BADR at 10% have found the landscape has changed significantly: the rate is now 18% and the trade test must be met on first principles.

**Q4: Can a serviced accommodation business qualify for BADR post-FHL abolition?**
Possibly, but it is fact-specific. There is no HMRC bright-line or post-abolition published guidance on the point. The test under TCGA 1992 s.169S is whether the activity constitutes a genuine trade — which depends on the level and nature of services provided (cooking, cleaning, concierge, events management), the frequency and scale of operations, and whether the activity looks more like a hotel/guest house (trade) or a furnished rental (investment). HMRC's badges of trade (BIM20050+) are the relevant indicators. The IHT BPR cases (Pawson, Ross) are sometimes cited in this context but apply a different statutory test (IHTA 1984 s.105(3) "mainly investments") and are not binding authority on the BADR trading test — they are analogy only. If you believe your operation qualifies, specialist CGT advice before disposal is essential.

**Q5: Can I incorporate my property portfolio and then sell the company shares to claim BADR?**
No. If the portfolio is buy-to-let investment property, incorporating it creates an investment company, not a trading company. BADR on share disposals requires the shares to be in a "trading company" under TCGA 1992 s.169S — one that does not carry on "to a substantial extent" non-trading activities. A portfolio of investment properties is a non-trading activity; the company fails the trading test. Incorporation plus share sale does not convert investment gains into BADR-qualifying gains. (Additionally, incorporating the portfolio triggers SDLT, CGT — via TCGA 1992 s.162 incorporation relief, if available — and other costs.)

**Q6: What does "mainly trading" mean in practice?**
HMRC's CG64090 guidance sets out four holistic indicators: (1) income from non-trading activities relative to total income; (2) non-trading assets as a proportion of total assets; (3) time and expenses devoted to non-trading activities; (4) the company's history and pattern of activities. HMRC's soft threshold is that where the non-trading element is below 20% across these measures, it is unlikely to challenge trading status. However, there is no single percentage test, and the assessment is contextual. A mixed-use property company with substantial commercial lettings alongside some residential investment may need a case-by-case analysis.

**Q7: What if my property company does some development as well as some investment letting?**
The trading/investment mix must be assessed holistically. If development (buying to develop and sell) generates the majority of the company's income and assets, and letting is a minor ancillary activity, the company may still pass the trading test. If the reverse is true (lettings dominate, development is occasional), it probably fails. The key is that the non-trading element does not exceed, to a "substantial extent," the trading activities. This is a judgment call requiring professional advice and a review of the actual income, asset, and time profiles.

**Q8: Does the £1 million lifetime limit apply per disposal or in total?**
In total, across all qualifying BADR disposals in a lifetime. You track cumulative qualifying gains against the £1 million limit using TCGA 1992 s.169N. Once you have used £1 million of BADR-qualifying gains, any further qualifying disposals pay the standard CGT rate, not the BADR rate. If you made qualifying disposals before 6 April 2025 (at 10%) those used up lifetime limit too. Check your cumulative usage before planning a new disposal.

**Q9: What is the link between this page and the HMRC nudge-letter guide?**
The HMRC nudge-letter guide (at /blog/capital-gains-tax/hmrcs-nudge-letters-on-business-asset-disposal-relief on this site) covers what to do if HMRC has written to query a BADR claim you have already made. This page covers the upstream question: does your disposal qualify in the first place? If you are uncertain whether your disposal qualifies, read this page first and take advice before making the claim. If you have made a claim and received an HMRC letter, see the nudge-letter guide.

**Q10: Can I claim BADR on shares in a trading company that happens to own some investment property?**
Possibly, if the investment property is not "substantial" relative to the trading activities. A manufacturing company or retail business that owns its premises (used in the trade) is still a trading company. A mixed company where commercial property investment is the secondary activity alongside a genuine trade may still pass the test depending on the income, asset, and time indicators at CG64090. A company where the primary activity is property investment with a small trading element almost certainly fails. The assessment is asset-specific and requires professional judgment.

**Q11: What happened to the 10% BADR rate?**
The 10% BADR rate applied to qualifying disposals before 6 April 2025. From 6 April 2025, the rate rose to 14% (FA 2025 s.8). From 6 April 2026, it rose again to 18%. The historical 10% rate no longer applies to any disposal made on or after 6 April 2025. If you were planning a qualifying disposal, the rate increase since 2025 has significantly reduced the BADR advantage over the standard 24% residential CGT rate (now only a 6 percentage-point saving at the 18% rate, down from an 18 percentage-point saving at 10%).

**Q12: Should I rush a qualifying disposal to capture a lower rate?**
Timing a disposal to capture a lower rate is a legitimate planning consideration but must be weighed against commercial factors (market conditions, readiness to sell, tax on proceeds). The rate progression (14% to 6 April 2026, then 18%) is now fixed by FA 2025 s.8, so there is no current legislative reason to expect a further rate change in the near term. If you are considering a qualifying disposal, take advice on the rate applicable to your specific timeline.
