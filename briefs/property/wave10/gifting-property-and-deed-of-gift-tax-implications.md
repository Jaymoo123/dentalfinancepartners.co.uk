# Working title: Gifting Property and Deed of Gift: CGT, IHT, SDLT and the Tax Traps

## Statutory anchor

**Primary:**
- TCGA 1992 s.17 — deemed disposal at market value on gift to a connected person (children, siblings, parents, spouses excluded from CGT no-gain-no-loss treatment unless spousal transfer under s.58); CGT crystallises at market value regardless of actual consideration.
- IHTA 1984 s.3A — a gift to an individual is a Potentially Exempt Transfer (PET); no IHT at the time of gift; fully exempt if donor survives 7 years (§15.2 house position).

**Supporting:**
- TCGA 1992 s.58 — spousal / civil partner transfers: no-gain-no-loss; donee takes donor's historic base cost. Applies only to married couples / civil partners living together in the same tax year.
- TCGA 1992 s.286 — connected persons definition (gifts to children, siblings, parents are connected-person transactions, triggering s.17 market value rule).
- TCGA 1992 s.165 — hold-over relief on gifts of BUSINESS assets. Does NOT apply to investment residential BTL property (§22.6 house position explicitly: "no holdover for non-business BTL"). Applies to gifts into trust under s.260 if the trust is a CLT (not settlor-interested).
- IHTA 1984 s.102 FA 1986 (Gift with Reservation of Benefit / GROB) — if the donor retains any benefit in the gifted property (e.g. living there rent-free), the gift does NOT reduce the estate; no 7-year clock starts (§15.3 house position).
- IHTA 1984 s.7(4) — taper relief where donor dies between 3 and 7 years after the PET (§15.2: taper reduces TAX not value of the gift; 20%/40%/60%/80% reductions in the IHT charge at years 3/4/5/6 respectively).
- FA 2003 s.43 + Sch 4 para 8 — SDLT on chargeable consideration. Where a mortgaged property is gifted and the donee assumes the outstanding mortgage, the mortgage balance IS chargeable consideration for SDLT at standard residential rates. Para 8(1)(b): the assumed debt is treated as chargeable consideration. Para 8(2): chargeable consideration is capped at the property's market value (negative equity cap). A pure gift with NO outstanding mortgage = zero consideration = no SDLT. Verified at legislation.gov.uk 2026-07-08.
- FA 2003 s.53 — CHARGING rule imposing a market-value floor for transfers between connected companies; s.53 disapplies the Sch 3 para 1 zero-consideration exemption. Do NOT describe s.53 as a relief. The connected-company relief route is FA 2003 Sch 7 group relief (with 3-year de-grouping clawback).
- ITTOIA 2005 s.620 / s.624 — settlement provisions; outright gift of rental property to a minor child causes income to be attributed back to the parent-donor (§22.9 house position).

**House position reference:** §15.2 (PETs + 7-year clock, taper relief). §15.3 (GROB s.102 FA 1986, POAT, routes around GROB). §22 (s.17 deemed MV disposal; s.165 no holdover for investment BTL; s.286 connected persons). §22.6 (comparison: direct property gift vs FIC share gift). §22.9 (settlements ITTOIA s.624 for gifts to minor children). §1 (SDLT rates). §1.P (LOCKED: FA 2003 Sch 4 para 8 — assumed debt = chargeable consideration; para 8(2) MV cap; s.53 is a CHARGING rule not a relief; Sch 7 group relief is the connected-company route).

## Worked examples

### Example 1: CGT on gift of BTL flat to adult child

**Facts:**
- David owns a BTL flat (no PPR). Original cost: £180,000. Current market value: £340,000. Outstanding mortgage: nil.
- David gifts the flat to his son Jake (adult, unconnected by CGT definition — wait: children ARE connected under TCGA 1992 s.286, so s.17 applies).

**CGT computation (David, higher-rate taxpayer):**

| | |
|---|---|
| Deemed disposal proceeds (MV, s.17) | £340,000 |
| Less: original cost | (£180,000) |
| Less: incidental costs of acquisition (estimate) | (£2,500) |
| **Gross gain** | **£157,500** |
| Less: AEA (2026/27) | (£3,000) |
| **Taxable gain** | **£154,500** |
| CGT at 24% (higher rate, residential) | **£37,080** |

- Hold-over relief (s.165): NOT available — BTL flat is an investment asset, not a business asset. §22.6 confirmed.
- 60-day return: required (UK land, tax due).
- David pays CGT on a gift for which he received no cash. He must fund this from other resources.
- Jake's CGT base cost for future disposals: £340,000 (MV at date of gift).

### Example 2: SDLT on mortgaged gift

**Facts:**
- Same flat; David now has an outstanding mortgage of £90,000.
- David gifts to Jake; Jake assumes the mortgage (takes over the mortgage liability).

**SDLT computation:**
- Chargeable consideration = assumed mortgage balance = £90,000 (FA 2003 Sch 4 para 8(1)(b)).
- MV cap check: MV is £340,000; £90,000 is below MV — para 8(2) cap does not bite.
- Jake does NOT also own another property, so no 5% additional dwellings surcharge.
- SDLT bands on £90,000: 0% up to £125,000. SDLT = nil (the £90,000 falls within the nil-rate band).

If the mortgage had been £200,000:
- SDLT on £200,000: 0% on £125,000 + 2% on £75,000 = £1,500.
- If Jake already owns a property: 5% surcharge applies. SDLT = 5% on £125,000 + 7% on £75,000 = £6,250 + £5,250 = £11,750 (illustrative; writer to use current SDLT table from §1).

**Pure gift with no mortgage:** Zero SDLT. Zero consideration = no chargeable consideration.

### Example 3: IHT PET and taper relief

**Facts:**
- David gifts the flat (MV £340,000) on 1 March 2027. He has no other chargeable lifetime transfers. His NRB is £325,000.
- David dies on 1 September 2032 (5 years 6 months after the gift, within years 5-6 of the 7-year period).

**IHT position:**

The PET becomes chargeable on David's death (he did not survive 7 years). Value of gift: £340,000.

| | |
|---|---|
| PET value | £340,000 |
| Less: NRB (£325,000 — assumed unused) | (£325,000) |
| **Chargeable amount** | **£15,000** |
| IHT at 40% (gross) | £6,000 |
| Taper relief (years 5-6): 60% reduction in the TAX | (£3,600) |
| **IHT payable on the PET** | **£2,400** |

**Key points:**
- Taper reduces the TAX charge (£6,000), not the value of the gift (£340,000). The full £340,000 still uses up NRB.
- If David had surviving spouse, RNRB (£175,000 from §9 / §15.1) may shelter further, but that relief applies to the estate passing to direct descendants on death, not to lifetime gifts.
- If David had survived to 1 March 2034 (7 full years), the PET would have been fully exempt.

### Taper table (for writer to present as a box or table):

| Years between gift and death | % reduction in the IHT TAX charge |
|---|---|
| 0-3 years | No taper — full IHT charge |
| 3-4 years | 20% reduction |
| 4-5 years | 40% reduction |
| 5-6 years | 60% reduction |
| 6-7 years | 80% reduction |
| 7+ years | 100% — PET fully exempt |

Do NOT write "taper reduces the value of the gift" — taper reduces the IHT charge on the gift (§15.2 do-not-write).

## FAQ expansion (12 full draft answers)

### Q1: What is the CGT position when I gift a property?

When you give a property to a family member (or any connected person under TCGA 1992 s.286), you are treated for CGT purposes as if you sold it at its current market value on the date of the gift. This is the connected-person market-value rule under TCGA 1992 s.17. CGT is charged on the difference between the market value at the date of gift and your original acquisition cost (adjusted for allowable expenditure).

You pay CGT on the gain even though you received no money. The CGT must be funded from your own resources. The rate is 18% (if the gain, combined with your other income, falls within the basic-rate band) or 24% (if it falls in the higher-rate band). You must file a 60-day return and pay CGT within 60 days of completion.

For the full CGT mechanics, including how the computation works in detail, see the dedicated page: `cgt-gifting-property-family-members-uk`.

### Q2: Does gifting to a spouse trigger CGT?

No — a gift between spouses or civil partners who are living together in the same tax year is a no-gain-no-loss transfer under TCGA 1992 s.58. No CGT arises at the point of transfer. The receiving spouse takes the donor's historic base cost (the original acquisition cost, NOT the market value at the date of gift).

This means the gain is deferred, not eliminated. When the receiving spouse eventually sells the property, the gain will be computed from the original base cost. The advantage is that the receiving spouse may have their own AEA, basic-rate band, and potentially PPR relief available at that point.

The s.58 no-gain-no-loss rule does NOT apply if the spouses are separated and living apart at the time of transfer, or if the transfer is in a different tax year from the year of separation.

### Q3: What is a deed of gift and do I need one for property?

A deed of gift is a legal document that records the unconditional transfer of an asset (here, a property) from one person (the donor) to another (the donee) without monetary consideration. For land, a deed is required by law to effect the transfer (Law of Property Act 1925 s.52); a verbal gift of a house or flat is not legally effective.

From a tax perspective, the deed of gift matters because:
- It establishes the DATE of the gift, which starts the IHT 7-year clock.
- It provides evidence that the transfer was unconditional (relevant for the GROB analysis — see Q5 below).
- It supports the CGT computation by confirming the date of deemed disposal.

The deed does NOT itself reduce your tax liability. It is a legal instrument, not a tax relief. Its value is entirely evidential — it proves the gift happened on a specific date on specific terms.

You also need to register the change of ownership at HM Land Registry. The deed of gift is typically accompanied by a Land Registry TR1 form.

### Q4: How does SDLT apply when I gift a mortgaged property?

If you gift a property that has an outstanding mortgage and the donee takes over (assumes) that mortgage, the assumed mortgage balance is chargeable consideration for SDLT under FA 2003 Sch 4 para 8(1)(b). SDLT is then charged on the amount assumed at the standard residential rates (including the 5% additional dwellings surcharge where the donee already owns another property).

The chargeable consideration is capped at the market value of the property (para 8(2)) — relevant only in negative equity situations.

If you gift the property with NO mortgage, there is zero chargeable consideration and no SDLT arises.

Example: a gift of a flat worth £300,000 with £150,000 mortgage assumed. SDLT is charged on £150,000 at standard residential rates (0% on first £125,000, 2% on £25,000 = £500; plus 5% surcharge if applicable).

### Q5: What are the IHT implications of gifting property?

A gift of property to an individual (your child, sibling, friend) is a Potentially Exempt Transfer (PET) under IHTA 1984 s.3A. No IHT is payable at the time of the gift. The PET becomes fully exempt if you survive for 7 years from the date of gift. If you die within 7 years, the PET becomes chargeable.

The value that enters the IHT computation is the value at the date of gift (not the value at death). The chargeable amount is set against your available nil-rate band (£325,000, frozen to 2031). Any excess is charged at 40%, subject to taper relief if you survive at least 3 years (see Q6 below).

The IHT clock starts on the date the deed of gift is executed (or the date of the actual transfer if different). Keep the deed of gift as evidence of this date.

### Q6: When does the 7-year PET taper apply?

Taper relief under IHTA 1984 s.7(4) reduces the IHT TAX CHARGE (not the value of the gift) where you die between 3 and 7 years after making the gift. The reductions are:

| Years survived | Reduction in tax charge |
|---|---|
| Less than 3 years | None |
| 3-4 years | 20% |
| 4-5 years | 40% |
| 5-6 years | 60% |
| 6-7 years | 80% |
| 7+ years | Fully exempt |

A critical point: taper reduces the IHT charge (the 40% that applies to the chargeable excess above the NRB), NOT the value counted against your NRB. If the whole gift falls within the NRB, there is no IHT charge to taper anyway — the benefit of the taper only shows where the gift exceeds the NRB.

### Q7: Can I gift property to my children to avoid CGT?

No. Gifting to your children is a deemed disposal at market value (s.17) and triggers CGT in the same way as a sale. There is no gifting exemption from CGT for transfers to children (unlike the spousal exemption). Any gain between your acquisition cost and the market value at the date of gift is chargeable.

The CGT bill must be paid from your own resources — your children do not owe the CGT; you do, as the donor.

From an IHT perspective, the gift starts the 7-year clock, which is beneficial for the long-term estate planning goal. But the CGT cost is the price of that IHT planning. Many parents find the upfront CGT (at up to 24%) outweighs the IHT saving — particularly where the estate would not exceed the available nil-rate band anyway.

### Q8: What is the CGT treatment of a gift to a trust?

A gift into a trust is treated as a disposal at market value under TCGA 1992 s.17 (if the trust is a connected person, which discretionary and most interest-in-possession trusts are). Hold-over relief under s.260 may be available where the transfer into trust is a chargeable lifetime transfer (CLT) for IHT purposes — a transfer into a discretionary trust (not a bare trust, not a settlor-interested trust) can qualify. Where s.260 applies, the CGT gain is deferred into the trust's base cost; no CGT is paid at the point of gift into trust.

For investment BTL property, s.165 hold-over (business asset relief) does NOT apply (§22.6). The only hold-over route for BTL-into-trust is s.260, which requires the transfer to be a CLT.

Trust IHT rules are more complex than PET rules (discretionary trusts pay an entry charge at 20% on value above the NRB, plus 10-year and exit charges). Trusts are outside the scope of this page; a specialist should be consulted.

### Q9: Does gifting property affect my annual exempt amount?

Yes. The gift is a disposal for CGT purposes, so the gain enters your CGT computation for the tax year of the gift. Your annual exempt amount (AEA, £3,000 for 2024/25 onwards) is deducted from the chargeable gain. If the gain on the gift exceeds the AEA, the balance is taxable.

If you have other chargeable gains in the same tax year (for example, from selling shares or other property), the AEA is shared across all gains in that year. The AEA is not multiplied by the number of disposals; it is a single annual allowance.

### Q10: Can I use hold-over relief on a gift of property?

Only in limited circumstances. TCGA 1992 s.165 hold-over relief is available on gifts of BUSINESS assets. A buy-to-let flat or house let on an assured shorthold tenancy is an INVESTMENT asset, not a business asset, and s.165 does NOT apply. §22.6 house position locks this explicitly.

Hold-over under s.260 is available where the gift is into trust and constitutes a CLT (see Q8). This is the only common hold-over route for residential investment property.

Hold-over is NOT available on gifts to individuals (children, siblings, parents) of non-business property. The CGT charge cannot be deferred; it crystallises at the date of gift.

### Q11: What records do I need to support a deed of gift?

Keep the following:
- The executed deed of gift (stamped or certified copy).
- The Land Registry TR1 form (transfer of whole title) and the updated title register showing the donee as proprietor.
- An independent RICS valuation of the property at the date of gift (needed to support the s.17 market value figure for CGT and the PET value for IHT).
- Evidence of any mortgage assumed (lender confirmation of the balance at the date of gift — supports the Sch 4 para 8 SDLT computation).
- Correspondence confirming the gift was unconditional (supports the GROB analysis — evidence that the donor does not retain any benefit).

HMRC may enquire into gifts years after the fact (IHT enquiries can arise on death, which may be decades after the gift). Contemporaneous records are essential.

### Q12: How does gifting interact with the main residence nil-rate band?

The main residence nil-rate band (RNRB, £175,000, frozen to 2031 — §9 / §15.1) is available where a residence passes to direct descendants on the death of the owner. If you gift your main residence during your lifetime, you are giving away the asset that would otherwise have qualified for RNRB. The RNRB is an on-death relief for assets passing through the estate — it does not apply to lifetime gifts.

As a result, gifting your main home may deprive your estate of the RNRB benefit (up to £175,000 per person, or £350,000 for a couple). This is a frequently overlooked cost of lifetime gifting that can significantly reduce the overall IHT saving.

An additional trap: if you continue to live in your main home after gifting it (for example, because you are gifting to your children who move in alongside you), GROB may apply (see Q5, and the GROB section of this page). If GROB bites, not only does the gift NOT remove the property from your estate, but the RNRB is ALSO potentially affected because the GROB property is treated as remaining in the estate on different terms.

## Anti-templating cross-check

This brief is structurally distinct from the inherited and overseas briefs in three ways. First, this is the only brief where the donor (not the recipient) pays the CGT — and pays it even though they received no money. In the inherited brief, CGT falls on whoever SELLS (PR or beneficiary); in the overseas brief, CGT falls on the selling owner. In the gifting brief, CGT falls on the DONOR at the point of gifting, regardless of whether the donee ever sells. Second, this is the only brief with a multi-tax stack (CGT + IHT + SDLT + income attribution) — the other two are CGT-only with minor IHT cross-references. Third, the GROB trap (living in the gifted property after giving it away destroys the entire IHT plan) has no equivalent in the other briefs. A writer templating from the inherited or overseas brief would produce a CGT-only guide and miss the IHT and SDLT content entirely — which is the primary failure mode to guard against.
