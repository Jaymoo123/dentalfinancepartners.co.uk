## Stage 2 extensions (S2-INCOME-VAT, 2026-07-08)

### Statute verification results (Stage 2 WebFetch)

**ITTOIA 2005 Part 6A (verified 2026-07-08 via legislation.gov.uk):**

- **s.783BD:** "An individual's property allowance for a tax year is £1,000." Treasury may increase by regulations. The £1,000 figure is statutory; has not been amended by a subsequent Finance Act as of 2026-07-08 (verify at write time).
- **s.783BE:** Full relief applies where: (a) individual has relevant property income; (b) it does not exceed the property allowance; (c) no election to disapply under s.783BJ.
- **s.783BF:** When full relief applies, relievable receipts and associated expenses are excluded from the profit calculation entirely.
- **s.783BK:** Partial relief election: individual may elect partial relief for a tax year if relevant property income exceeds the property allowance. Election deadline: within one year of the Self Assessment filing deadline for the relevant year.
- **s.783BG / s.783BH:** Partial relief: deductible amount equals the property allowance (£1,000), not actual expenses. Actual expenses are excluded when partial relief applies.
- **s.783BN:** Exclusion where payments made by employer (or spouse/civil partner of employer) of the individual.
- **s.783BO:** Exclusion where payments made by a firm (partnership) of which the individual is a partner or connected with a partner.
- **s.783BP:** Exclusion where payments made by a close company of which the individual is a participator or associate.
- **s.783BM (VERIFIED):** This section covers exclusion where expenses are deducted against rent-a-room receipts. It is a rent-a-room interaction exclusion, NOT an employer/partnership/close company exclusion. The seed's reference to "s.783BM" under "blocked cases" for employer/own-company was WRONG. HP §41 correction is CONFIRMED: exclusions for employer/partnership/close company are ss.783BN/BO/BP.

**s.783BM additional note:** s.783BM operates as a gating mechanism blocking Part 6A Chapter 2 relief where the individual qualifies for rent-a-room relief AND meets either Condition A or Condition B relating to their rent-a-room elections. This is distinct from the employer/partnership/company exclusions. The page should cover both: (a) the three entity-payer exclusions (BN/BO/BP) and (b) the rent-a-room exclusion (BM) as separate points.

### Worked example data

#### Three-scenario decision table

| Scenario | Gross rental income | Actual deductible expenses (excl. finance costs) | Finance costs | Best route | Effective taxable profit |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| A: Income below allowance | £800 | £200 | £0 | Full relief (s.783BE): no SA liability on this property income | £0 (income excluded entirely) |
| B: Income above allowance, expenses below allowance | £1,400 | £600 | £0 | Partial relief (s.783BK): deduct £1,000 instead of £600 | £400 (vs £800 under actual expenses) |
| C: Income above allowance, expenses above allowance | £1,400 | £1,200 | £400 | Actual expenses: deduct £1,200 + claim s.24 credit on £400 finance costs | £200 profit + s.24 credit (vs £400 under partial relief) |
| D: Higher-rate landlord, large mortgage | £6,000 | £500 | £4,000 | Actual expenses: deduct £500 + claim s.24 20% credit on £4,000 | £5,500 profit, tax = £2,200 (40%) - £800 s.24 credit = £1,400 net tax. Partial relief would give £5,000 profit, tax = £2,000 - £800 credit = £1,200 net tax. Actual expenses better here. |

**Writer note on Scenario D:** the s.24 basic-rate credit applies regardless of whether the landlord uses actual expenses or partial relief. Under partial relief, the landlord cannot deduct actual expenses but can STILL claim the s.24 credit on the finance costs not deducted. Verify this point against HP §7 and ITTOIA 2005 s.274A/ss.274AA at write time; if the s.24 credit interaction with partial relief is uncertain, flag it in the body as a point requiring specialist advice.

**Break-even formula (use in body):**
Partial relief beats actual expenses when: £1,000 > sum of actual deductible expenses (excluding finance costs, since s.24 credit applies in both routes). For landlords with finance costs, the comparison is: is £1,000 deduction worth more than the actual non-finance expenses you can deduct? If actual non-finance expenses are under £1,000, partial relief wins. If over £1,000, actual expenses win.

#### Joint ownership worked example

A married couple own a rental property 50/50. Gross rent: £1,600/year. Each owns a 50% share (£800 gross income each). Each £800 is below the £1,000 property allowance (s.783BD). Each qualifies for full relief (s.783BE). No SA filing required on this property income for either owner. Combined rental income: £1,600, combined tax liability: £0.

Compare: if one spouse owned the whole property, gross income would be £1,600. Full relief only applies if income is at or below £1,000, so this owner would need to elect partial relief (deduct £1,000, taxable profit £600) or use actual expenses. Joint ownership doubles the per-person allowance in practice.

**Writer instruction:** include a clear statement that each co-owner gets their own £1,000 per person (not per property), per HP §41. Unmarried couples also each get their own £1,000 if they own jointly.

### FAQ expansion (8-12 draft Q&As)

**Q1. What is the property income allowance and who can claim it?**
A: The property income allowance is a £1,000 per-person, per-year exemption from income tax on property income (ITTOIA 2005 s.783BD, Part 6A). Any individual with rental income of £1,000 or less in a tax year pays no income tax on it and may not need to file a Self Assessment return for that income. Those earning more than £1,000 can elect partial relief, deducting £1,000 from their gross income instead of their actual expenses. It was introduced by Finance Act 2017 and applies from 2017/18 onwards.

**Q2. I earn £950 from a small rental. Do I still need to file a Self Assessment return?**
A: Probably not, for this income specifically. Under full relief (s.783BE), if your relevant property income does not exceed £1,000, the receipts and expenses are excluded from your property business calculation. HMRC guidance indicates that property income within the full relief limit generally does not trigger a SA filing obligation for that source. However, if you have other reasons to file SA (employment income over the higher rate threshold, capital gains, other self-employment income), you still file and simply show the property income as covered by the allowance. Check HMRC's SA1 trigger rules at write time.

**Q3. My rental income is £1,500 and my actual expenses are £400. Which route is better: partial relief or actual expenses?**
A: Partial relief is better here. Under partial relief (s.783BK), you deduct £1,000 from gross income, leaving a taxable profit of £500. Under actual expenses, you deduct £400, leaving a taxable profit of £1,100. Partial relief saves you tax on £600 of income.

**Q4. My rental income is £3,000, my actual expenses (excluding mortgage interest) are £2,200, and I have £800 of mortgage interest. Which route should I use?**
A: Actual expenses is better. Under actual expenses, you deduct £2,200 non-finance costs, leaving a profit of £800, then claim the s.24 basic-rate credit on £800 of finance costs. Under partial relief, you deduct £1,000, leaving a profit of £2,000, and can still claim the s.24 credit on £800 of finance costs. The actual expenses route gives a £1,200 lower profit figure. The s.24 credit is claimable under both routes (it is not blocked by the partial relief election), so the comparison comes down solely to whether actual non-finance expenses exceed £1,000 (yes, at £2,200, so actual expenses win).

**Q5. Can I switch between partial relief one year and actual expenses the next?**
A: Yes. The partial relief election (s.783BK) is made on a year-by-year basis and must be made within one year of the SA filing deadline for the relevant year. There is no multi-year commitment. Landlords with fluctuating income or expenses can choose the better route each year. The election is made on the SA tax return.

**Q6. My landlord income comes from a property I own jointly with my partner (not married). Do we each get a £1,000 allowance?**
A: Yes. The allowance is per person, not per property (HP §41). Each joint owner gets their own £1,000 against their share of the rental income. An unmarried couple each get their own £1,000, just as a married couple would. If you each receive £800 gross rental income from a 50/50 split of a property generating £1,600 rent, you each qualify for full relief and pay no tax.

**Q7. I let out a room in my home (rent-a-room) and also own a separate BTL property. Can I claim both rent-a-room relief and the property allowance?**
A: Not on the same income. Rent-a-room relief and the property income allowance are separate regimes. You can claim rent-a-room on your lodger income (up to the rent-a-room limit) AND the property allowance on your BTL property income separately, as they are different income sources. However, s.783BM prevents Part 6A relief from applying to income that is already within the rent-a-room relief regime. In practice: claim rent-a-room on the lodger income; claim the property allowance (or actual expenses) on the BTL income. Do not try to apply the property allowance to income already covered by rent-a-room.

**Q8. My employer pays rent to me for using my home office. Can I use the property allowance?**
A: No. Section 783BN excludes the allowance where the property income is paid by an employer (or their spouse/civil partner) to the employee. If your employer pays you rent for use of your home, that income is outside the property allowance. This is an anti-avoidance measure: the allowance is not intended to shelter what is effectively employment income in the form of rent.

**Q9. I own shares in a limited company that pays me rent for using a room in my home. Can I claim the property allowance?**
A: No. Section 783BP excludes the allowance where income is paid by a close company in which you are a participator or associate. A family property company where you own the shares and receive rent from it falls squarely within s.783BP. The allowance is unavailable; tax the rent as property income in the normal way (actual expenses route).

**Q10. Does the property allowance apply to garage or parking space rental income?**
A: Yes, provided the income is "relevant property income" (income from the property business). Rental income from garages, parking spaces, and storage units owned by the individual as part of their property business qualifies. Ground rent income also qualifies where it is taxed as property income. Furnished holiday letting income post-abolition of the FHL regime (from April 2025) is now treated as property business income and should qualify, but verify at write time whether HMRC has issued explicit guidance on this post-FHL-abolition question.

**Q11. What happens if I do not elect partial relief and my income is over £1,000?**
A: If your income exceeds £1,000 and you do not elect partial relief, full relief does not apply (income is above the threshold) and you are taxed on actual profit (income minus actual allowable expenses). Partial relief is optional and beneficial only if actual non-finance expenses are under £1,000. If you forget to elect and your return is filed on the actual expenses basis, HMRC will accept that; there is no penalty for not electing partial relief.

**Q12. Is there a minimum income level below which even the £1,000 allowance is not worth claiming?**
A: For income at or below £1,000, the full relief applies automatically (you do not need to claim it; it operates by excluding the income from your property business calculation). For income below your personal allowance and below £1,000, you would pay no tax anyway, so the allowance's value is administrative (simplified reporting). For income above £1,000 but below the personal allowance, the partial relief reduces your taxable profit figure, which matters for SA reporting and for avoiding interaction with personal allowance taper in higher income years. The allowance has real cash value primarily for higher-rate landlords whose rental income pushes them over a band boundary.
