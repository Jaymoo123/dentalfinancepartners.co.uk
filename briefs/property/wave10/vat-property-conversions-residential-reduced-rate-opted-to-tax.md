### Statute verification results (Stage 2 WebFetch)

**Sch 7A Group 6 "Residential conversions" (verified 2026-07-08):**
- Item 1: supply of qualifying services in course of a qualifying conversion.
- Item 2: supply of building materials incorporated by the person supplying the Item 1 services.
- Qualifying conversion types: (a) changed-number-of-dwellings conversion; (b) house-in-multiple-occupation conversion; (c) special residential conversion (non-residential to solely-residential-purpose).
- **Group 6 does NOT contain the 2+ years empty/unoccupied condition.** That condition is in Group 7 Note 3 only.

**Sch 7A Group 7 "Residential renovations and alterations" (verified 2026-07-08):**
- Items 1-2 mirror Group 6 structure.
- **Note 3 (Group 7 only):** premises must not have been lived in during the period of 2 years ending with the commencement of the relevant works. (Alternative: single dwelling acquired after 2-year vacancy with renovation within 1 year of purchase.)
- **HP §29.13 CONFIRMED CORRECT:** 2-year-empty condition is Group 7, not Group 6.

**Sch 8 Group 5 Item 1(b) (verified 2026-07-08):**
- "converting a non-residential building or a non-residential part of a building into a building designed as a dwelling or number of dwellings or a building intended for use solely for a relevant residential purpose"
- **Note (7)(b):** a building is "non-residential" for Item 1(b) if it either (i) was not designed or adapted for use as a dwelling or for a relevant residential purpose, or (ii) was constructed more than 10 years before the grant AND has not been used as a dwelling or for a relevant residential purpose in the preceding 10 years. (Verify precise language at write time per §16.36.)
- **Note (9):** converting a non-residential part of a building falls outside Items 1(b) and 3 unless the conversion creates additional dwellings.

### Worked example data

#### Example A: 5%/0%/20% conversion cost table

Scenario: Developer converts a former office building (single unit) into 4 residential flats. Total conversion build cost (labour + materials supplied by contractor): £400,000 exclusive of VAT.

| Supply | VAT treatment | VAT charged | Developer can recover? | Net cost to developer |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Conversion services (Sch 7A Grp 6, qualifying services) | 5% reduced rate | £20,000 | Yes, if VAT-registered and OTT active or input recovery available | £400,000 + irrecoverable VAT |
| Architect / professional fees | 20% standard rate | £10,000 (on £50k fees) | Partial: only if making taxable supplies | Depends on partial exemption |
| First grant of major interest (sale of the 4 flats) | 0% zero rate (Sch 8 Grp 5 Item 1(b)) | Nil | N/A | Developer recovers input VAT attributable to zero-rated supply |
| Retention of flats for residential letting | Exempt | N/A | No input tax recovery on letting costs | CGS clawback risk applies |

**Key planning point:** if the developer sells all 4 flats (zero-rated first grant), they can recover all input VAT including the 20% professional fees as attributable to zero-rated outputs. If they retain any flat for exempt residential letting, that flat's share of input VAT is blocked under partial exemption and CGS applies.

#### Example B: CGS clawback scenario

Developer incurs £300,000 VAT on conversion costs (£250k threshold breached: SI 1995/2518 reg 112 applies). At completion, developer lets 2 of the 4 flats exempt (residential letting) rather than selling. CGS adjustment period: 10 years.

Year 0 (completion): 50% of use is taxable (2 flats zero-rated sale), 50% exempt (2 flats let). Input VAT recovered: £300,000 x 50% = £150,000. Residual input VAT blocked: £150,000.

CGS annual interval: at each of intervals 2-10, if the use percentage changes, a clawback or repayment adjustment applies. Example: if in Year 3 the developer sells both retained flats (now zero-rated), taxable use rises to 100%, and a positive adjustment of £150,000 x (1/10) x (100% - 50%) = £7,500 is claimable per interval. Conversely, if the developer lets all 4 flats by Year 3, taxable use falls to 0% and a further clawback of £15,000/year is due to HMRC.

**Writer instruction:** present simplified version of this in a callout box; full CGS mechanics are on the CGS page (cross-link). Focus on the risk trigger: retaining converted flats for exempt letting after recovering input VAT on conversion costs.

#### Example C: OTT trap scenario

Fact: developer acquired a commercial office block 5 years ago and opted to tax at acquisition (OTT registered, VAT 1614A filed). Developer now converts the block to 12 residential flats.

Issue: the existing OTT on the commercial building does not automatically disappear when conversion works begin. The OTT continues unless:
- **Para 5 automatic disapplication** (Sch 10 para 5): applies when the building becomes a dwelling or is used for a relevant residential purpose. This kicks in at the point of completion/occupation as residential, not at the start of works.
- **VAT 1614D (para 6 recipient certification)**: buyer of a completed flat can issue a certificate to the developer disapplying the OTT on their purchase. This is buyer-side certainty for the zero-rate to apply.

If the developer sells the flats before issuing 1614D or before para 5 is satisfied, the sale could be subject to 20% VAT (standard-rated under the OTT) rather than 0% (zero-rated under Sch 8 Grp 5). Buyers will refuse to pay 20% on what should be a zero-rated residential purchase. This is the "OTT trap".

**Resolution:** developer should (a) confirm para 5 disapplication at completion of the residential conversion, and (b) obtain 1614D certifications from purchasers at exchange. Cross-link to OTT mechanics page for disapplication procedure detail.

### FAQ expansion (8-12 draft Q&As)

**Q1. Does the 5% reduced rate apply to both labour and materials on a residential conversion?**
A: Yes, under Sch 7A Group 6 Item 2, building materials supplied and incorporated by the contractor providing the conversion services also attract the 5% rate. Materials purchased separately by the developer and supplied to the contractor do not qualify (they are not "supplied in the course of" the qualifying conversion by the contractor). Architect and structural engineer fees are standard-rated (20%) as professional services, not qualifying conversion services.

**Q2. My office block has been used for business for 15 years. Does the 10-year rule in Note (7)(b) of Sch 8 Grp 5 mean my sale of the converted flats qualifies for zero-rating?**
A: It can. Note (7)(b) provides that a building counts as "non-residential" (and therefore eligible for the zero-rated first grant under Item 1(b)) if it was constructed more than 10 years before the grant of the major interest AND has not been used as a dwelling or for a relevant residential purpose in the 10 years before the grant. An office block with 15 years of commercial use and no residential occupation in the preceding 10 years satisfies this. Verify the building's use history carefully; mixed-use periods can complicate the analysis.

**Q3. I am converting a house into 3 self-contained flats. Does this qualify for the 5% reduced rate?**
A: Yes, this is a "changed-number-of-dwellings conversion" under Sch 7A Group 6. Converting a single dwelling into multiple separate dwellings (or vice versa) falls within this sub-category. The conversion services and incorporated materials supplied by your building contractor qualify for the 5% rate. Note: this is Group 6, not Group 7. The 2-year-empty condition does not apply here.

**Q4. What is the difference between Group 6 and Group 7 for VAT purposes?**
A: Group 6 (residential conversions) covers: changing the number of dwellings in a residential building, converting a non-residential building into dwellings, and HMO conversions. The key trigger is a change in the character or number of dwellings, or a switch from non-residential to residential use. Group 7 (residential renovations and alterations) covers renovating or altering existing residential premises that have been empty for at least 2 years. The 2-year-empty condition belongs to Group 7 only. Both attract the 5% reduced rate, but the qualifying conditions differ.

**Q5. My developer client has an OTT on a commercial property they are converting to flats. When should they worry?**
A: They should worry now. The OTT on the commercial building is a live VAT election. It means supplies in connection with that building can be subject to 20% VAT. On sale of the completed flats, the developer wants the zero-rate (Sch 8 Grp 5 Item 1(b)) to apply. For that to work, either the Sch 10 para 5 automatic disapplication must operate (the building has become a dwelling at completion) or buyers must issue VAT 1614D certificates disapplying the OTT before exchange. If the developer sells before the building is fully converted to residential, the OTT may still apply and the sale could be 20% VAT. Alert clients early; this is the most common costly VAT error on residential conversion projects.

**Q6. Can a non-VAT-registered self-converter claim VAT back on a non-residential conversion?**
A: Yes, via the DIY Builders Scheme under VATA 1994 s.35. This is a one-time VAT refund for individuals (not businesses) who convert a non-residential building into a dwelling for their own use. The claimant does not need to be VAT registered. The claim window (post-May 2024 rule change) requires submitting evidence within 3 months of the building being ready for occupation. Evidence includes VAT invoices, planning permission, and building regulations completion certificate. HMRC form VAT431C is used for conversion claims.

**Q7. Our developer will let the converted flats rather than sell them. Does the 5% rate on conversion services still apply?**
A: Yes, the 5% reduced rate on Group 6 conversion services is not conditional on selling the completed dwellings. The rate applies to the construction services themselves. However, once the developer decides to let rather than sell, they are making exempt supplies (residential letting), which means they cannot recover most of the input VAT on the conversion costs. The 5% rate reduces the VAT exposure, but the lack of recovery on the letting side is the bigger problem. If the CGS threshold (£250k of VAT on land/buildings) is breached, a 10-year CGS adjustment applies. Seek specialist advice before locking in a let strategy on a significant conversion project.

**Q8. Does the zero-rate on first grant require the developer to have been the original converter, or can a subsequent buyer also benefit?**
A: The zero-rate under Sch 8 Group 5 Item 1(b) is available to the person "converting" the building. It applies only to the first grant of a major interest (freehold sale or lease of 21+ years) by the converter. A second-hand sale of the converted flat (by someone who bought it from the converter) does not attract the zero-rate. That subsequent sale is exempt (residential property: Sch 9 Group 1).

**Q9. How does the 5% rate interact with my VAT registration threshold? I am a small developer doing one conversion project.**
A: VAT registration threshold is rate-by-reference (verify current figure at write time). If your taxable supplies (including any zero-rated and reduced-rated supplies) exceed the threshold in any rolling 12-month period, you must register. On a conversion project, the taxable turnover question turns on whether you are selling the completed dwellings (zero-rated supplies, count toward the threshold) or only incurring costs. A developer who completes and sells a single converted flat may cross the threshold on that one transaction. Register promptly; late registration triggers penalties on all taxable supplies from the date you were required to register.

**Q10. Can I reclaim VAT on professional fees (architects, surveyors) on a residential conversion?**
A: Yes, if you are VAT registered and making taxable supplies (zero-rated first grant on sale). Professional fees are standard-rated (20%), but they are attributable to your zero-rated sales, so the input VAT on them is recoverable in full. If you are making a mix of taxable and exempt supplies (some flats sold, some let), partial exemption applies and the professional fee input VAT must be apportioned. If you are a non-registered self-converter using the DIY scheme (s.35), professional fees are excluded from the refund (only VAT on materials and incorporated supplies qualifies).

**Q11. What happens to the CGS if I sell the converted flats after holding them for letting?**
A: The CGS adjustment period is 10 years from completion. At each annual interval, HMRC looks at the proportion of taxable vs exempt use. If you initially let the flats (exempt) and then sell them (zero-rated), each sale in the 10-year period generates a positive CGS adjustment: you can reclaim a proportion of the input VAT originally blocked. The calculation uses the standard CGS interval formula. Keep records of all CGS-eligible assets and their use changes throughout the 10-year period.

**Q12. Is the 5% reduced rate available on converting a pub into residential flats?**
A: Yes. A pub is a non-residential building. Converting it into dwellings is a "special residential conversion" under Sch 7A Group 6, or a "changed-number-of-dwellings conversion" if it already had some residential character. The 5% rate applies to qualifying conversion services. The pub's prior use (commercial, non-residential) makes it eligible for the Sch 8 Group 5 zero-rate on first grant of the completed flats, provided Note (7)(b)'s conditions are met (10+ year construction age and no residential use in preceding 10 years).
