### Profit band 1: £30,000 company profit (single director, no Employment Allowance)

**Strategy A: Salary £5,000 + remaining as dividend**

- Company profit: £30,000
- Salary: £5,000 (at NI secondary threshold; no employer NIC; no income tax below PA)
- CT-deductible salary: £5,000; remaining profit: £25,000
- CT on £25,000 at 19%: £4,750
- After-CT retained profit: £20,250
- Dividend declared: £20,250
- Personal income: £5,000 salary + £20,250 dividend = £25,250
- Income tax on dividend: £500 @ 0% (allowance); £12,070 (remaining basic band after salary) @ 10.75% = £1,298; total dividend tax: £1,298
- Total tax bill (CT + personal): £4,750 + £1,298 = £6,048
- Net cash to director: £25,250 - £1,298 = £23,952; effective total rate (CT + personal) on £30k gross: ~20.2%

**Strategy B: Salary £12,570 + remaining as dividend**

- Company profit: £30,000
- Salary: £12,570 (at PA; no income tax; employer NIC = 15% x (£12,570 - £5,000) = £1,136)
- CT-deductible cost: £12,570 + £1,136 = £13,706; remaining profit: £16,294
- CT on £16,294 at 19%: £3,096
- After-CT retained profit: £13,198
- Dividend declared: £13,198
- Personal income: £12,570 salary + £13,198 dividend = £25,768
- Income tax: salary = 0 (within PA); dividend: £500 @ 0%; £12,698 @ 10.75% = £1,365; total dividend tax: £1,365
- Total tax (CT + employer NIC + personal): £3,096 + £1,136 + £1,365 = £5,597
- Net cash to director: £25,768 - £1,365 = £24,403; effective total rate: ~18.7%

Writer note: Strategy B marginally beats A at £30k profit for a single director without EA because the higher salary saves more CT than the employer NIC it costs. Present both; note the Employment Allowance point (if EA available, employer NIC on £12,570 salary is zero, making Strategy B more attractive). Always caveat: figures are illustrative; verify thresholds at write time.

## FAQ expansion (8-12 entries with draft answers)

**Q1: What is the most tax-efficient salary for a buy-to-let company director in 2026/27?**
For a single-director company that cannot claim Employment Allowance, the most common choices are £5,000 (the NI secondary threshold -- no employer NIC, small CT deduction) or £12,570 (the personal allowance -- larger CT deduction but employer NIC of 15% on the £7,570 above the secondary threshold, currently about £1,136). At typical profit levels the employer-NIC cost of the higher salary is outweighed by the CT saving, making £12,570 the marginally better choice in most scenarios. Where the company also has other employees and qualifies for the Employment Allowance (£10,500 for 2026/27), taking a salary to the personal allowance is almost always better because the EA offsets the employer NIC entirely.

**Q2: How much dividend tax will I pay in 2026/27?**
Dividend tax rates from 6 April 2026 are 10.75% (basic rate), 35.75% (higher rate), and 39.35% (additional rate) under FA 2026 s.4. You receive a £500 annual dividend allowance at 0%. Dividends use up your income tax bands after your other income is counted first. So if you take a £5,000 salary, your remaining basic-rate band is about £45,270 (from £5,001 to £50,270); dividends up to that amount are taxed at 10.75%.

**Q3: Can I defer tax by leaving profits in the company?**
Yes. Profits retained in the company are subject to CT (19% at small profits, up to 25% at main rate) but are not personally taxable until distributed. This deferral is most valuable when you expect to be in a lower personal tax band in future years (e.g. on retirement, before drawing the pension). The risk is that extraction becomes more expensive if personal tax rates rise. For property companies, FA 2026 ss.6-7 will push personal property income rates to 22/42/47% from 2027/28, which could affect how dividend income stacks alongside any retained personal letting income.

**Q4: What is a director's loan and how does it help me extract profit tax-efficiently?**
A director's loan account (DLA) tracks money the company owes to the director. If you incorporated by transferring properties or cash into the company, the company owes you that amount as a DLA credit. Repayments of a DLA credit are not income and are not taxable -- they are simply returning your own money. This makes the DLA the first and most tax-efficient extraction route, ahead of salary or dividends. The DLA credit runs dry once the full balance has been repaid; after that, all extraction is via salary, dividends, or pension.

**Q5: What happens if I owe money to the company (overdrawn DLA)?**
If the company lends money to you (as a director-shareholder), this is an overdrawn DLA. Under CTA 2010 s.455, if the loan is not repaid within nine months of the company's year-end, the company pays a tax charge equal to the dividend upper rate for the tax year in which the loan was made -- 35.75% from 6 April 2026 (ITA 2007 s.8(2) as substituted by FA 2026 s.4). The charge is repayable to HMRC when you repay the loan to the company. This is not a permanent tax -- it is effectively a cash-flow cost of leaving money owed to the company unpaid.

**Q6: Are employer pension contributions a good extraction method?**
Often the best extraction route after DLA repayment, particularly at higher profit levels. Employer pension contributions: (1) are deductible for CT (reducing the company's CT bill); (2) do not trigger employer or employee NIC; (3) do not create personal income tax in the year of contribution; and (4) grow tax-free inside the pension. The annual allowance (currently £60,000 for 2026/27, verify at gov.uk) caps the total contributions across all sources in a year. For a director drawing a modest salary plus small dividends, there is typically plenty of annual-allowance headroom for the company to contribute.

**Q7: Can I pay dividends to my spouse to reduce the tax bill?**
If your spouse holds shares in the company as genuine co-shareholders (an outright gift of shares with no strings attached), they can receive dividends on those shares. The settlements legislation (ITTOIA 2005 s.624) applies where you have retained a benefit from the arrangement, but the *Jones v Garnett (Arctic Systems)* [2007] UKHL 35 carve-out under s.626 protects an outright gift of ordinary shares to a spouse. If your spouse is on a lower tax rate, their dividend income is taxed at a lower rate (10.75% basic vs your 35.75% higher rate). The gift of shares must be genuine -- not subject to any side arrangement requiring the spouse to return the dividends or shares.

**Q8: What is the difference between salary and dividends in terms of national insurance?**
Salary above the NI secondary threshold (£5,000) triggers employer NIC at 15% and employee NIC at 8% up to the upper earnings limit. Dividends are not subject to NIC at all. However, salary is deductible for CT while dividends are paid from post-tax profits. The interplay between these three rates (CT saving from salary deduction, employer NIC cost, personal NIC saved on dividends) is why the optimal salary is not zero and not the full personal allowance -- it depends on the specific rates and whether Employment Allowance applies.

**Q9: How will the 2027/28 property income surcharge affect my extraction planning?**
From 6 April 2027, personal rental income is taxed at 22% (basic), 42% (higher), and 47% (additional) under FA 2026 ss.6-7. Director-shareholders who also have personal letting income will find their income-tax bands consumed faster, pushing dividend income into the higher-rate band earlier. This increases the value of: (a) the pension lever (defer income, avoid higher-rate dividend tax); (b) the DLA repayment route (tax-free); (c) retaining profit in the company rather than distributing immediately. Run extraction modelling separately for 2026/27 and 2027/28.

**Q10: What is the "60% effective rate trap" and does it affect property company directors?**
If your total income (salary + dividends + personal rental income) is between £100,000 and £125,140, every additional £1 of income costs you 60p in effective tax: 40% income tax plus the phasing-out of your personal allowance (£1 of allowance lost for every £2 of income above £100k, costing an extra 20%). Property company directors who have personal rental income stacking on top of salary and dividends are at risk of falling into this trap. Employer pension contributions are the primary tool for reducing income below £100k and escaping the trap.

**Q11: Should I extract profit now or build up retained profits in the company?**
Retained profits in the company are only taxed at CT rates (19-25%), not at personal rates (20-47%). Keeping money inside the company and extracting over many years -- especially if you plan to retire or reduce your income -- can be tax-efficient. The risk: if tax rates rise or the company is wound up, the retained profits become distributable in a lump sum that could push you into higher bands. The company wind-up capital treatment (via a members' voluntary liquidation) can convert retained profits into a capital receipt taxed at 18/24% CGT rather than 39.35% dividend tax, but only where specific conditions are met and HMRC anti-avoidance provisions (ITTOIA 2005 s.396B -- transactions in securities) do not apply.

**Q12: Where can I model my extraction strategy?**
Use the Property Tax Partners Incorporation Cost Calculator at /calculators/incorporation-cost-calculator to compare the overall cost of moving existing properties into a company. For ongoing extraction planning, speak to a property tax specialist who can model salary, dividend, DLA, and pension scenarios for your specific profit level, other income, and plans. Generic calculators cannot account for the interaction between personal rental income, company extraction, and the 2027/28 rate changes.
