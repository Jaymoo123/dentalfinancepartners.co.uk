<p>Search results treat Etsy costs as three separate subjects. Fee calculators tell you what Etsy takes. VAT articles tell you Etsy charges VAT on something. Tax articles tell you HMRC might be interested. Almost nothing joins them, yet for a UK seller they are one chain: the fee stack determines your payout, VAT interacts with both the fees and the sales, and income tax is charged on a figure that appears nowhere on your bank statement.</p>

<p>This guide walks that chain end to end. It is not a fee calculator, and it deliberately quotes no fee percentages: rates change and vary by account, and the only schedule that matters is the one on your own Etsy statement. For the interactive version of the maths, use the <a href="/calculators/seller-take-home-calculator">seller take-home calculator</a> with your own numbers.</p>

<h2>The short answer</h2>

<p>Etsy deducts several fees before you are paid, and those fees can carry VAT of their own. What lands in your Etsy payout is already net of fees and is not your taxable profit or your VAT turnover. You are taxed on your gross sales less allowable costs, and VAT registration is tested on gross sales, not the payout.</p>

<h2>What Etsy actually deducts</h2>

<p>Etsy's deductions fall into a handful of named fee types, taken from the sale before your payout is released. The rates attached to each type change over time and can differ by seller, currency and listing, so take the figures from your own payment account statement rather than any third-party table. The types to look for are:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Listing fee.</strong> A small fixed charge per item listed, and again on renewal.</li>
  <li><strong>Transaction fee.</strong> A percentage of the sale amount, charged on the item price and usually on postage too.</li>
  <li><strong>Payment processing fee.</strong> A percentage of the order total plus a fixed amount per order, charged where Etsy Payments handles the money.</li>
  <li><strong>Offsite Ads fee.</strong> A percentage charged when a sale is attributed to Etsy's advertising on external platforms. Depending on your sales level, participation may not be optional.</li>
  <li><strong>Optional extras.</strong> Etsy Ads budgets, subscription plans and currency conversion charges where they apply to your account.</li>
</ul>

<p>Every one of these is a business cost. They are deductible when you work out your taxable profit, but they do not reduce your turnover: the sale is still worth what the buyer paid.</p>

<h2>VAT on your Etsy fees: the reverse charge</h2>

<p>Where your marketplace fees are billed from a supplier established outside the UK, the place of supply is the UK and you, the business customer, self-account for the VAT under the reverse charge in <a href="https://www.gov.uk/guidance/vat-place-of-supply-of-services-notice-741a">Notice 741A</a>. If you are VAT registered, you charge yourself UK VAT on the fee and usually recover the same amount as input tax on the same return, so the cash effect is often nil.</p>

<p>The trap is for sellers who are not yet registered. The value of reverse-charge services you receive counts toward the <a href="https://www.gov.uk/vat-registration">£90,000 registration threshold</a>. A seller sitting below the threshold on sales alone can be pushed over it by the running total of overseas platform and advertising fees. If your fees are billed with UK VAT already applied, the reverse charge does not arise on those invoices; check your Etsy billing documents to see which treatment applies to you. The mechanics are covered in depth in our guide to <a href="/vat/vat-on-marketplace-fees">VAT on marketplace fees</a>.</p>

<h2>VAT on your Etsy sales: who accounts for it</h2>

<p>Who pays the VAT on the sale itself turns on one fact: where your business is established. A UK-established seller accounts for their own VAT on Etsy sales once registered, the same as on any other channel. Etsy does not take that liability off you. The <a href="https://www.gov.uk/guidance/vat-and-overseas-goods-sold-to-customers-in-the-uk-using-online-marketplaces">deemed-supplier rules</a>, under which the marketplace collects and accounts for UK VAT on the sale, apply where the seller is not established in the UK, and overseas-established sellers have no registration threshold at all.</p>

<p>The two regimes must not be blurred. Reading a forum thread written by an overseas seller and concluding "Etsy handles my VAT" is how UK-established sellers end up unregistered past £90,000. <h2>Your income tax on Etsy profit</h2>

<p>Income tax starts from gross sales, deducts allowable costs, and taxes the profit. Etsy's fees, your materials, postage and packaging are all deductible costs, which is exactly why the payout figure is useless for tax: it has already had some costs removed and not others. Three separate numbers govern the small-seller position, and they must never be merged:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>The £1,000 trading allowance.</strong> If your gross trading income is £1,000 or less in the tax year, <a href="https://www.gov.uk/guidance/tax-free-allowances-on-property-and-trading-income">the trading allowance</a> means a return may not be needed. Above £1,000, you can deduct either the allowance or your actual expenses, not both. A maker with real material costs and Etsy fees is usually better off deducting actual expenses.</li>
  <li><strong>The £90,000 VAT threshold.</strong> A completely different test, on gross sales, described above. Nothing about the trading allowance touches it.</li>
  <li><strong>The platform-reporting exclusion.</strong> Etsy reports seller income to HMRC under the digital-platform reporting rules, with a narrow exclusion for the smallest sellers. That exclusion is about whether Etsy files a report on you, not about whether tax is due. Tax follows your trading status, not the report.</li>
</ul>

<p>One more point that catches side-hustle sellers: if you have a day job, your personal allowance is normally already used against your salary through PAYE. Etsy profit stacks on top, so it is typically taxed from the first pound at your marginal rate rather than enjoying a fresh tax-free band. To check whether your own position triggers a return, run the <a href="/calculators/side-hustle-tax-checker">side hustle tax checker</a>.</p>

<h2>A worked example: one sale, three layers</h2>

<p>Take a UK-established, VAT-registered Etsy seller whose own statement shows total Etsy deductions running at 12 percent of the gross order value. That 12 percent is this example seller's blended figure from their own statement, not a quoted Etsy rate; yours will differ.</p>

<ol>
  <li><strong>The sale.</strong> A buyer pays £50 including postage. Because the seller is UK-established and VAT registered, the £50 is a gross, VAT-inclusive sale on which the seller accounts for output VAT. The full £50, not the payout, is what feeds the turnover figure.</li>
  <li><strong>The fees.</strong> Etsy deducts £6 across its fee types, and the payout is £44. Where those fees are billed from abroad, the seller reverse-charges UK VAT on the £6: output VAT declared and, for this fully taxable business, the same amount recovered as input tax on the same return.</li>
  <li><strong>The profit.</strong> Suppose materials and postage cost £14. Taxable profit on the sale is the net sale value less the £6 fees and £14 costs, not the £44 payout. The payout number never appears in either the VAT return or the profit computation as a starting point.</li>
</ol>
