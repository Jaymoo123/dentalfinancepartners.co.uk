<p>A wave of headlines in 2024 warned UK sellers to expect a tax crackdown. Platforms including eBay, Vinted, Etsy and Amazon began sharing seller data with HMRC. For many occasional sellers, the natural assumption was that a new tax-free limit had been created or changed. It had not. The figure that actually matters, the <strong>£1,000 trading allowance</strong>, has existed for years and was not touched. This page corrects that misconception, explains when the allowance applies and when it does not, and addresses the more important underlying question: whether what you are doing is trading in the first place.</p>

<h2>The short answer: no new limit was created</h2>

<p>The 2024 platform-reporting changes required digital marketplaces to collect and report seller data to HMRC under rules derived from the OECD DAC7 framework. This is a <strong>reporting change</strong>, not a tax change. It altered what information eBay and Vinted send to HMRC; it did not create a new tax-free limit, lower an existing one, or introduce a new tax on selling. For the full detail on what the platform-reporting rules actually require, see our guide on <a href="/blog/platform-reporting-and-hmrc-letters/platform-reporting-rules">platform reporting and HMRC letters</a>.</p>

<p>The figure that actually governs whether occasional sellers have a Self Assessment obligation on trading income is the <strong>£1,000 trading allowance</strong>, which has been in place since 2017.</p>

<h2>What the trading allowance is</h2>

<p>Under <a href="https://www.gov.uk/guidance/tax-free-allowances-on-property-and-trading-income">HMRC's trading income allowance guidance</a>, if your annual gross trading income is <strong>£1,000 or less</strong>, you may not need to file a Self Assessment return for that income. The word "may" matters: if you have other income that triggers a Self Assessment obligation (employment income over certain thresholds, savings interest, dividends), you will still need to file, even if your trading income is below £1,000.</p>

<p>Gross income means your total receipts before any deductions. For a marketplace seller, that is your total sales proceeds, not what the platform pays out after fees. The VAT-threshold equivalent applies here too: it is what the customer paid, not what you received.</p>

<h2>Above £1,000: allowance or actual expenses, not both</h2>

<p>If your gross trading income exceeds £1,000 in a tax year, you have a choice about how to calculate your taxable profit. You cannot use both routes.</p>

<table>
  <thead>
    <tr>
      <th>Route</th>
      <th>How it works</th>
      <th>Best when</th>
    </tr>
  </thead>
  <tbody>
    <tr>
      <td><strong>Trading allowance deduction</strong></td>
      <td>Deduct £1,000 from gross income. No itemised expenses needed.</td>
      <td>Your actual costs are low, you have no meaningful cost of goods sold, and your gross income is only modestly above £1,000.</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td><strong>Actual expenses</strong></td>
      <td>Deduct real costs: cost of goods sold, platform fees, postage, packaging, subscriptions.</td>
      <td>You buy stock to resell. Real costs will usually exceed £1,000 and the actual-expenses route reduces your taxable profit further.</td>
    </tr>
  </tbody>
</table>

<h3>Worked example: reseller on eBay</h3>

<p>A reseller buys job lots and sells them on eBay. In the 2025/26 tax year, gross sales receipts are £6,200. Cost of goods sold plus eBay fees plus postage totals £4,100.</p>

<ul>
  <li>Using the trading allowance: taxable profit = £6,200 minus £1,000 = <strong>£5,200</strong></li>
  <li>Using actual expenses: taxable profit = £6,200 minus £4,100 = <strong>£2,100</strong></li>
</ul>

<p>Actual expenses produce a taxable profit roughly £3,100 lower. The allowance route would cost this seller significantly more tax. For goods sellers with real cost of goods sold, <strong>using the trading allowance instead of actual costs is usually the worse choice</strong>, and is a common mistake among sellers new to Self Assessment.</p>

<p>Source: <a href="https://www.gov.uk/guidance/tax-free-allowances-on-property-and-trading-income">gov.uk, tax-free allowances on property and trading income</a>.</p>

<h2>The bigger question: is what you do actually trading?</h2>

<p>The trading allowance and the actual-expenses choice only matter if you are trading. Many sellers who received a letter from HMRC (prompted by the platform-reporting data) are not trading at all. Selling personal possessions you no longer want, whether on Vinted, eBay or Depop, is <strong>generally not trading</strong> and creates no Self Assessment obligation regardless of how much you receive.</p>

<p>The boundary is not defined by a sales count, a pound threshold, or the number of items listed. It is defined by HMRC's <strong>badges of trade</strong>, a set of qualitative indicators developed through case law and codified in <a href="https://www.gov.uk/hmrc-internal-manuals/business-income-manual/bim20205">HMRC's Business Income Manual from BIM20205 onward</a>.</p>

<h2>The badges of trade applied to resellers and declutterers</h2>

<p>The badges are indicators, not a checklist where ticking a set number produces a definitive answer. HMRC and courts look at the overall picture. The table below shows how the key badges typically apply to two contrasting seller types.</p>

<table>
  <thead>
    <tr>
      <th>Badge</th>
      <th>Occasional declutterer</th>
      <th>Buy-to-resell trader</th>
    </tr>
  </thead>
  <tbody>
    <tr>
      <td><strong>Profit motive</strong></td>
      <td>Goal is to clear items no longer wanted. Profit, if any, is incidental.</td>
      <td>Goods are acquired specifically because they can be sold at a higher price. Profit is the purpose.</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td><strong>Frequency of transactions</strong></td>
      <td>Sporadic. Items are listed as the need to clear space arises.</td>
      <td>Regular, repeated purchasing and selling cycles. Restocking is planned.</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td><strong>Nature of the asset</strong></td>
      <td>Items owned and used personally before sale: clothing, electronics, books.</td>
      <td>Stock acquired specifically for resale, never used personally. May be modified or repackaged.</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td><strong>How goods were acquired</strong></td>
      <td>Received as gifts, bought for personal use, or inherited.</td>
      <td>Purchased wholesale, from auctions, or at below-market prices with a view to resale.</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td><strong>Modification or improvement</strong></td>
      <td>No modification. Items sold as found.</td>
      <td>Cleaning, repairing, rephotographing or bundling goods to increase saleability.</td>
    </tr>
  </tbody>
</table>

<p>These are the kinds of questions HMRC will examine if the platform-reporting data prompts an enquiry. The presence of several trading badges, particularly a clear profit motive combined with regular purchasing for resale, is strong evidence of a trade. A single sale of a used item at a profit is rarely enough on its own.</p>

<p>For the full HMRC analysis of the badges and the case law behind them, see <a href="https://www.gov.uk/hmrc-internal-manuals/business-income-manual/bim20205">BIM20205 onward in the Business Income Manual</a>.</p>

<h2>If you are trading: Self Assessment and what comes next</h2>

<p>If the badges point toward a trade and your taxable profit (after the allowance or actual expenses) exceeds your personal allowance combined with any other income, you will have tax to pay and a Self Assessment filing obligation.</p>

<p>The registration deadline is firm. Under <a href="https://www.gov.uk/register-for-self-assessment">gov.uk/register-for-self-assessment</a>, a new sole-trader seller who becomes chargeable to tax must tell HMRC by <strong>5 October following the end of the relevant tax year</strong>. So for income in the year to 5 April 2026, the registration deadline is 5 October 2026. Missing this deadline can result in penalties.</p>

<p>Once registered, your first Self Assessment return will cover the full tax year. If your trading income is growing and you expect to pass the MTD for Income Tax threshold (£50,000 combined self-employment and property income from April 2026, falling in later years), see our guide on <a href="/blog/making-tax-digital-and-self-assessment/mtd-itsa-online-sellers">MTD for Income Tax for online sellers</a> for what that means in practice.</p>

<p>If you sell on multiple platforms, or if your turnover is approaching the VAT registration threshold (measured on gross marketplace receipts, not net payout), a <a href="/for/marketplace-sellers">marketplace-seller accountant</a> can assess your position across all channels. If HMRC has already written to you, see our guide on <a href="/services/hmrc-letter-online-sales">responding to an HMRC letter about online sales</a>.</p>

<h2>Common failure modes</h2>

<p>These are the mistakes that generate unexpected tax bills or penalties for online sellers:</p>

<ol>
  <li><strong>Using the trading allowance when actual costs are higher.</strong> If you buy stock to resell and your costs of goods sold alone exceed £1,000, claiming the allowance rather than actual expenses overstates your taxable profit.</li>
  <li><strong>Treating the platform-reporting exclusion as a tax-free amount.</strong> The rules that determine whether a platform must report your data to HMRC are not a tax-free limit. They are reporting rules. Tax on trading income applies based on your actual profit and the applicable allowances, not on whether a platform has reported you.</li>
  <li><strong>Assuming no return is needed because sales are under £1,000.</strong> The trading allowance covers you only if the income is trading income and only if it is below £1,000 gross. Other income sources can still require a return even if trading income is low.</li>
  <li><strong>Missing the 5 October registration deadline.</strong> If you become chargeable to tax, registration is required by 5 October after the tax year ends. Late registration attracts penalties separate from any tax owed.</li>
  <li><strong>Treating declutter proceeds and trading income as the same thing.</strong> Selling personal possessions is generally not trading. Applying the trading-allowance rules to non-trading receipts is unnecessary and can cause confusion when filling in a return.</li>
</ol>
