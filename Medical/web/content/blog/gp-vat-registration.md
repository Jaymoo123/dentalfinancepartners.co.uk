<p>For most GP practices the honest answer is reassuring: <strong>genuine medical care is exempt from VAT, and NHS GMS or PMS income is outside the scope of VAT altogether</strong>, so neither counts towards the registration threshold. A practice only has to think about registering when its <strong>taxable (non-exempt) turnover</strong>, things like purely cosmetic work, medico-legal reports, or some administrative and occupational-health services, climbs past <strong>£90,000</strong> in a rolling 12-month period. Many wholly NHS or wholly clinical practices never get close.</p>

<p>The complications come from mixed income. A practice that blends NHS funding, private clinical care, cosmetic treatments, room hire and occupational-health contracts has to sort each income stream into the right VAT box, and only one of those boxes (taxable supplies) moves you towards registration. This guide explains where the lines fall, when registration is genuinely triggered, and how partial exemption affects what you can recover. It is general information, not advice for your specific practice.</p>

<h2>Is There VAT on Private Medical Care?</h2>

<p>Generally, no. This is the single most misunderstood point in GP VAT. The supply of <strong>medical care by a registered medical practitioner is exempt from VAT under VATA 1994 Schedule 9 Group 7 Item 1</strong> (Item 2 extends the same treatment to other registered health professionals and to people working under their supervision). The exemption does not turn on whether the patient is NHS or private. It turns on the <strong>principal purpose</strong> of the service being the <strong>protection, maintenance or restoration of the individual's health</strong>, within the doctor's registered field.</p>

<p>So a private consultation, a diagnosis, a course of treatment or a follow-up, all of it is normally <strong>exempt</strong>, exactly like the equivalent NHS care. It is simply wrong to assume that "private means standard-rated". Private clinical care is exempt by default; the standard-rated cases are the exceptions set out below.</p>

<h2>NHS Income Is Outside the Scope of VAT</h2>

<p>NHS GMS and PMS funding (the Global Sum, QOF, enhanced services and the like) sits in a separate category again: it is <strong>outside the scope of VAT</strong>. That matters for two reasons. First, you never charge VAT on it. Second, and crucially, <strong>both outside-the-scope NHS income and exempt private medical care are ignored when you test your turnover against the £90,000 registration threshold.</strong> Only taxable (non-exempt) supplies count.</p>

<p>This is why a busy NHS practice can turn over several hundred thousand pounds a year and have no obligation, and often no ability, to register for VAT. The headline turnover is irrelevant to the VAT question; only the taxable slice is.</p>

<h2>VAT Registration Thresholds for GP Practices</h2>

<p>You must register for VAT once your <strong>taxable (non-exempt) turnover exceeds £90,000</strong> (the threshold from 1 April 2024, unchanged for 2026/27) in any rolling 12-month period. There is also a forward-looking test: if you expect your taxable turnover to exceed £90,000 in the <strong>next 30 days alone</strong>, you must register straight away. The <strong>deregistration threshold is £88,000</strong>, the level at which a registered practice whose taxable turnover falls back can apply to come off the register.</p>

<p>For a GP practice, taxable turnover (the figure measured against the threshold) typically includes:</p>
<ul>
<li>Purely cosmetic or aesthetic procedures with no therapeutic purpose</li>
<li>Medico-legal and expert-witness reports written for a third party's decision</li>
<li>Some occupational-health services and certification or administrative work with no care element</li>
<li>Genuinely non-medical services such as certain training courses or commercial room hire (subject to the land rules below)</li>
</ul>

<p>It does <strong>not</strong> include NHS GMS or PMS income (outside the scope) or exempt private medical care. Mixing those up is the most common reason a practice either registers when it did not need to, or fails to spot when it genuinely must.</p>

<h2>Which GP Income Is VATable? A Quick Map</h2>

<p>Sorting income correctly is the whole game. As a working map:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>NHS GMS / PMS funding</strong>, outside the scope. Not VATable, does not count towards the threshold.</li>
<li><strong>Private clinical care</strong> (consultations, diagnosis, treatment), exempt where the principal purpose is the patient's health. Does not count towards the threshold.</li>
<li><strong>Cosmetic / aesthetic work with no therapeutic purpose</strong>, standard-rated at 20%. Counts towards the threshold. The same procedure done to restore health can be exempt, so purpose, not the procedure name, decides.</li>
<li><strong>Medico-legal and expert-witness reports</strong>, standard-rated, because the principal purpose is to let a third party (court, insurer, employer) make a decision, not to treat the patient.</li>
<li><strong>Occupational health</strong>, mixed. The clinical care element can be exempt, but pre-employment screening, fitness certificates and reports for the employer's decision are typically standard-rated.</li>
<li><strong>Room hire</strong>, usually exempt as a licence to occupy land, unless the practice has opted to tax the property, in which case it becomes standard-rated.</li>
<li><strong>Subscriptions / membership / training income</strong> with no care element, often standard-rated; judged on what is actually supplied.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Cosmetic Work: Purpose Decides</h3>

<p>HMRC tests the <strong>principal purpose</strong> of each supply. A treatment carried out purely to change appearance, with no therapeutic indication, is standard-rated. The identical treatment carried out to protect, maintain or restore health (reconstruction after illness, injury or surgery, for example) can fall back into exemption. A clinic running both has to split its income and watch the taxable (cosmetic) element against the £90,000 threshold in its own right.</p>

<h2>A Worked Example: Mixed NHS and Private Income</h2>

<p>Consider an anonymised GP partnership. In a 12-month period it records roughly £620,000 of NHS GMS funding, £140,000 of private clinical care (consultations and minor procedures with a clear therapeutic purpose), and two smaller streams: about £55,000 of occupational-health work for local employers and around £20,000 of purely cosmetic treatments.</p>

<p>Despite turning over more than £800,000, the partnership tests only its <strong>taxable</strong> supplies against the threshold. The £620,000 NHS income is outside the scope and the £140,000 of clinical care is exempt, so both are excluded. Of the occupational-health income, suppose £40,000 is reports and certification for employers (standard-rated) and £15,000 is genuine clinical care (exempt). The taxable total is therefore roughly £40,000 of occupational-health work plus £20,000 of cosmetic work, about <strong>£60,000</strong>. That is below £90,000, so registration is not yet required.</p>

<p>If the cosmetic clinic grows, or the practice wins a larger occupational-health contract, that taxable figure can cross £90,000 quickly. The lesson is to monitor the taxable slice on a rolling monthly basis, not the headline turnover, so registration is spotted in time rather than discovered late.</p>

<h2>Managing VAT with Mixed Supplies (Partial Exemption)</h2>

<p>A practice that does register, because its taxable supplies pass £90,000, will almost always have both exempt and taxable income. That makes it <strong>partially exempt</strong>, and partial exemption governs how much input VAT (the VAT on your costs) you can actually recover.</p>

<p>In outline, VAT on costs used directly for taxable supplies is recoverable, VAT on costs used directly for exempt supplies is not, and VAT on shared overheads (premises, utilities, general admin) is apportioned. The VAT attributable to exempt supplies is recoverable only if it passes the <strong>de minimis test</strong>: on average <strong>no more than £625 per month (£7,500 per year) AND no more than 50% of your total input VAT</strong>. Cross either limit and the exempt-related input VAT becomes non-recoverable. Because most GP practices are dominated by exempt and outside-the-scope income, the recoverable element is often small, which is exactly why registering rarely pays for a mainly clinical practice.</p>

<h2>How to Register for VAT</h2>

<p>Registration is completed online through HMRC's VAT registration service. You will typically need:</p>
<ul>
<li>Business details and structure (sole trader, partnership or company)</li>
<li>Taxable turnover for the past 12 months</li>
<li>Expected taxable turnover for the next 12 months</li>
<li>Details of which supplies are taxable, exempt and outside the scope</li>
<li>Business bank account information</li>
</ul>

<p>You must register within <strong>30 days of the end of the month in which your taxable turnover crossed £90,000</strong> (or immediately, on the forward-looking 30-day test). Late registration can lead to penalties plus the VAT that should have been accounted for from the correct date, so timing matters.</p>

<h3>Choosing a VAT Scheme</h3>

<p>A registered practice can consider:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Standard VAT accounting:</strong> full input and output VAT, with returns under Making Tax Digital for VAT.</li>
<li><strong>Cash accounting:</strong> account for VAT when payment is received or made, which can help cash flow.</li>
</ul>

<p>The flat rate scheme is generally a poor fit for a practice with large exempt and outside-the-scope income, because the flat percentage is applied to relevant turnover and you give up normal input VAT recovery, so it is rarely the right answer here. Scheme choice depends on the actual mix of taxable supplies and costs, and is worth modelling before you commit.</p>

<h2>Common VAT Mistakes GP Practices Make</h2>
<ul>
<li>Treating headline turnover as the threshold test, rather than the taxable (non-exempt) slice.</li>
<li>Assuming all private work is standard-rated; genuine private clinical care is exempt.</li>
<li>Assuming all medical income is exempt and overlooking the cosmetic and medico-legal carve-outs.</li>
<li>Forgetting that occupational-health income is split between exempt care and standard-rated reporting.</li>
<li>Missing the 30-day registration deadline once the taxable element crosses £90,000.</li>
<li>Overestimating recoverable input VAT in a partially exempt, mainly clinical practice.</li>
</ul>

<h2>When to Seek Professional Advice</h2>

<p>GP VAT decisions hinge on correctly characterising each income stream and then applying partial exemption, an area where general accountants frequently misclassify NHS, exempt and taxable supplies. Consider specialist input if:</p>
<ul>
<li>Your practice mixes NHS, private clinical, cosmetic and occupational-health income</li>
<li>You are running, or planning, a cosmetic or aesthetic clinic alongside the practice</li>
<li>Your taxable (non-exempt) turnover is approaching £90,000</li>
