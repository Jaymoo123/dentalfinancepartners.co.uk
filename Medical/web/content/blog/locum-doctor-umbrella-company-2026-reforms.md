<p>The <strong>locum doctor umbrella company 2026 reforms</strong> are real, but they are widely misreported. The headline change that took effect from <strong>6 April 2026</strong> is not a licensing scheme and it does not abolish IR35. It is a <strong>joint and several liability (JSL)</strong> measure that moves the risk of unpaid PAYE and National Insurance up the labour supply chain, onto recruitment agencies and, in some cases, the end client.</p>

<p>If you are a locum doctor paid through an umbrella company, this page explains what actually changed, what has not (yet) changed, and how the three common ways to be paid (umbrella, your own personal service company, or agency PAYE) compare. This is general information for UK doctors, not personal advice.</p>

<h2>What the 2026 Umbrella Reform Actually Does</h2>

<p>From <strong>6 April 2026</strong>, where a worker is supplied through an umbrella company, HMRC can recover any PAYE income tax and National Insurance that the umbrella fails to pay from another party in the chain. The legislation makes the umbrella and the relevant party <strong>jointly and severally liable</strong>, meaning HMRC can collect the full amount from whichever party it chooses.</p>

<p>For a typical locum placement, the relevant party is the <strong>recruitment agency</strong> that contracts directly with the NHS trust or private clinic. Where a trust engages an umbrella directly with no agency in between, the <strong>end client</strong> can be the liable party instead.</p>

<p>Two points matter for understanding the scale of the change. First, there is no general reasonable-care defence: an agency that did thorough compliance checks can still be pursued if the umbrella defaults. Second, this is a <strong>tax measure about who pays PAYE, not a regulation or licensing measure</strong>. It does not require umbrellas to hold a government licence.</p>

<h2>What Has Not Changed (and the Regulation Still to Come)</h2>

<p>It is just as important to be clear about what the 2026 reform does <strong>not</strong> do, because the umbrella market is full of overstated claims.</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>It is not a licensing scheme.</strong> The wider statutory definition and regulation of umbrella companies, the labour-supply-chain enforcement direction, is a <strong>separate workstream</strong> that has been signalled but is not part of the April 2026 PAYE change. We deliberately avoid quoting a single fixed start date for that wider regulation, because it is not yet in force. Treat any specific date you see for full umbrella licensing as provisional until it is actually enacted.</li>
<li><strong>It does not abolish IR35.</strong> The off-payroll rules are unchanged. Anyone telling you IR35 has been repealed for locum doctors is wrong.</li>
<li><strong>It does not change how PAYE is calculated.</strong> Your tax and National Insurance on umbrella pay are worked out the same way; what changes is who carries the risk if the umbrella does not hand that money to HMRC.</li>
</ul>

<p>The practical effect for you is indirect. Because agencies and trusts now carry real tax exposure, they have a strong incentive to use only compliant umbrellas and to drop providers that look risky. That should squeeze out the worst non-compliant schemes, which is good for locums who were unknowingly exposed to disguised-remuneration arrangements.</p>

<h2>How You Get Paid: Umbrella vs PSC vs Agency PAYE</h2>

<p>The umbrella reform is easier to understand once you see where the umbrella sits among your options. There are three common routes for a locum doctor.</p>

<h3>1. Umbrella Company (PAYE Employee of the Umbrella)</h3>

<p>You become an <strong>employee of the umbrella company</strong>. The umbrella receives an assignment rate from the agency, deducts its margin and employment costs, then pays you through PAYE with income tax and National Insurance deducted at source. You get statutory employment rights (holiday pay, statutory sick and family pay, auto-enrolment pension). Because you are an employee on PAYE, <strong>IR35 does not bite</strong> on this arrangement in the usual way. The 2026 JSL reform applies specifically to this route.</p>

<h3>2. Your Own Personal Service Company (PSC)</h3>

<p>You work through your own limited company. Here the <strong>off-payroll / IR35 rules</strong> decide whether the engagement is inside or outside IR35. For NHS trust work (a public-sector hirer), the <strong>trust or the fee-paying agency determines your status</strong> and operates PAYE on inside-IR35 engagements; the determination is not yours to make. Most NHS locum sessions are likely to be caught by IR35 because of the level of control and integration. A PSC only earns its overhead where you have genuinely <strong>outside-IR35 private work</strong> (for example self-pay clinics or some private-sector engagements with a small client). See our guide on the <a href="/blog/locum-doctor-limited-company-pros-and-cons">locum doctor limited company route</a> and the detail in <a href="/blog/locum-doctor-ir35-what-you-need-to-know">locum doctor IR35</a>.</p>

<h3>3. Agency PAYE (Employee of the Agency)</h3>

<p>The agency puts you on its own payroll as an employee, deducts PAYE at source and provides employment rights directly, with no umbrella margin in the chain. For inside-IR35 NHS work this is often the cleanest option, because there is no third party taking a margin and no umbrella to default on its PAYE.</p>

<p>One thing every locum should weigh, whichever route you take: <strong>only income that is genuinely NHS-pensionable counts towards your NHS pension</strong>. Income routed through a personal service company and taken as dividends is <strong>not NHS-pensionable</strong>, so any tax saving has to be set against lost pension accrual. Freelance GP locums pension their eligible income through the locum forms; see <a href="/blog/nhs-pension-for-locums-form-a-form-b">NHS pension for locums (Form A and Form B)</a>.</p>

<h2>Expenses and Tax Reliefs Under Umbrella Pay</h2>

<p>As an umbrella employee, your scope to claim work expenses is limited compared with a sole trader, and the umbrella reform reinforces tight expense discipline across the sector. The reliefs that genuinely apply to medical professionals still stand, including your <strong>GMC annual retention fee</strong>, medical indemnity for private and non-clinical work, and relevant Royal College or specialty subscriptions on HMRC's approved List 3.</p>

<p>If you also do self-employed locum or private work alongside umbrella pay, that side is where most of your expense claims and mileage relief belong. For <strong>business mileage between work sites</strong> in 2026/27, the HMRC approved rate is <strong>55p per mile for the first 10,000 business miles and 25p per mile thereafter</strong> (the first-10,000 rate rose from 45p on 6 April 2026; home to your first site is non-deductible commuting). Our <a href="/blog/locum-doctor-expenses-what-you-can-claim">locum doctor expenses guide</a> sets out what you can and cannot claim, and the <a href="/blog/locum-doctor-tax-complete-guide">complete locum doctor tax guide</a> covers self-assessment and National Insurance for self-employed sessions.</p>

<p>On National Insurance for any self-employed locum income, note the current position: <strong>Class 4 is 6% on profits between £12,570 and £50,270, then 2% above that (2025/26)</strong>, and <strong>Class 2 is no longer a required payment from 6 April 2024</strong> (you keep your state-pension entitlement where profits are at or above the small-profits threshold, without a weekly charge). These do not apply to your umbrella pay, which is taxed as employment income.</p>

<h2>Making Tax Digital: Does It Affect Umbrella Locums?</h2>

<p>Making Tax Digital for Income Tax (MTD for ITSA) starts to apply from <strong>6 April 2026 for those with qualifying income over £50,000</strong> (then £30,000 from 6 April 2027 and £20,000 from 6 April 2028). Qualifying income is your gross self-employment and property income, not employment income. So <strong>pure umbrella (PAYE) pay does not count</strong> towards the MTD threshold.</p>

<p>Where it does matter is if you combine umbrella work with self-employed locum sessions or private work: that self-employed income can take you over £50,000 and bring you into MTD from April 2026. Limited companies are outside MTD for ITSA entirely, so a PSC locum is not in scope for that regime.</p>

<h2>What Locum Doctors Should Do Now</h2>

<h3>Check Your Umbrella Is Compliant</h3>

<p>Read your <strong>key information document</strong> and payslips. Your gross-to-net should be transparent: assignment rate, employer National Insurance, apprenticeship levy and the umbrella margin clearly shown, then PAYE on what is left. Be wary of any arrangement promising unusually high take-home, loans, advances, or pay split into a small taxable element plus a large non-taxable one. Those are the disguised-remuneration schemes the reform is designed to flush out, and you, not just the umbrella, can end up facing an HMRC bill.</p>

<h3>Decide Whether Umbrella, PSC or Agency PAYE Fits Your Work</h3>

<p>If almost all your work is inside-IR35 NHS sessions, umbrella or agency PAYE is usually simpler than a PSC. If you have a meaningful slice of genuinely outside-IR35 private work, a personal service company may earn its keep, but always model the <strong>NHS pension accrual you give up</strong> on company income alongside any tax saving.</p>

<h3>Keep Clean Records</h3>

<p>Whether you are PAYE through an umbrella or running self-employed sessions, keep your contracts, payslips, expense receipts and evidence of professional status. Good records protect you if HMRC ever looks up the chain at an umbrella you used.</p>
