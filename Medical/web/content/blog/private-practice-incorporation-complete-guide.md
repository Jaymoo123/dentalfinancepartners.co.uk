<p>For a growing number of doctors and consultants, the question is not <em>how</em> to incorporate a private practice but <em>whether</em> to. This guide is the decision, worked through on 2026/27 numbers: sole trader versus limited company, what actually drives the saving, the NHS pension and IR35 traps that can reverse it, and three worked examples at £40,000, £80,000 and £150,000 of private profit so you can see roughly where you sit.</p>

<p>It is general information, not personal advice. The right answer depends on your income mix, how much is NHS-pensionable, your annual-allowance position and your plans, so model your own figures before you act. If you have already decided and want the mechanics, our <a href="/blog/medical-practice-incorporation-step-by-step">step-by-step incorporation guide</a> covers forming the company, transferring the business and the registrations. This guide is about the choice itself.</p>

<h2>First principle: incorporation is a private-work decision only</h2>

<p>Before any arithmetic, one rule governs everything below. A <strong>limited company cannot hold an NHS GMS or PMS contract</strong>, and income derived from NHS work is <strong>not NHS-pensionable</strong> when routed through a company. So you cannot "incorporate your NHS practice". What you can incorporate is your <strong>private and non-NHS work</strong>: private consultations and self-pay clinics, insurance medicals, medico-legal and expert-witness reporting, occupational health, cosmetic and aesthetic work, education and media, and freelance locum work that genuinely sits outside IR35.</p>

<p>Your NHS role is unaffected. A hospital consultant stays an NHS employee; a GP stays a partner or salaried GP. Only that NHS post (and a GP partnership's NHS profit share) is pensionable. Anyone advising you to "put your medical income through a company" has not understood the contract rules. Our overview of <a href="/blog/private-practice-tax-nhs-and-private-income">private practice tax with NHS and private income</a> shows how the two sides sit side by side.</p>

<h2>Sole trader versus limited company: how each is taxed in 2026/27</h2>

<p>The choice is between being taxed personally on your private profit as it arises, or taxing it inside a company and then again, more lightly, when you take it out. Here is what each route costs.</p>

<h3>As a sole trader (or partner) on private work</h3>

<p>Private profit is taxed as your income in the year it arises. On top of your NHS income it is likely to fall in the higher or additional band:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Income tax:</strong> 20% basic, <strong>40%</strong> higher (£50,270 to £125,140), <strong>45%</strong> additional (over £125,140), with the personal allowance tapering away between £100,000 and £125,140 (an effective 60% band in that slice).</li>
<li><strong>Class 4 National Insurance:</strong> <strong>6%</strong> on profit between £12,570 and £50,270, then <strong>2%</strong> above £50,270. Class 2 is no longer a required flat payment from 6 April 2024.</li>
<li><strong>Admin:</strong> a self-assessment return and simple records. From 6 April 2026, Making Tax Digital for Income Tax applies to sole-trader and landlord income where qualifying income exceeds £50,000, adding quarterly updates.</li>
</ul>

<p>The virtue of sole-trader status is simplicity and that the profit is yours the moment it is earned. The cost is that every pound of private profit stacked on top of NHS income is taxed at your marginal rate straight away, with no timing lever.</p>

<h3>Through a limited company on private work</h3>

<p>The company is taxed first, then you are taxed on extraction:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Corporation tax:</strong> <strong>19%</strong> on profits up to £50,000, <strong>25%</strong> over £250,000, and marginal relief between (standard fraction 3/200, FY2025 and FY2026), an effective marginal rate of about <strong>26.5%</strong> in the £50,000 to £250,000 band. See <a href="/blog/gp-corporation-tax">corporation tax for doctors</a> for the detail.</li>
<li><strong>Extraction as dividends:</strong> from <strong>6 April 2026</strong> (Finance Act 2026 s.4), dividends above the £500 allowance are taxed at <strong>10.75%</strong> ordinary, <strong>35.75%</strong> upper and <strong>39.35%</strong> additional rate, with no National Insurance. For 2025/26 the rates were 8.75%, 33.75% and 39.35%.</li>
<li><strong>Salary:</strong> a small salary is deductible for the company but the company pays employer (secondary Class 1) NIC at <strong>15%</strong> on pay above the <strong>£5,000</strong> secondary threshold (from 6 April 2025). The £10,500 Employment Allowance is <strong>not available to a company whose only employee is a single director</strong>, so solo medical companies often set salary at or near the secondary threshold.</li>
<li><strong>Admin:</strong> annual accounts and a confirmation statement at Companies House, a corporation tax return, PAYE payroll, dividend paperwork and corporate indemnity. Budget a realistic recurring cost for this, because it has to be cleared before incorporation saves you anything.</li>
</ul>

<p>The key insight is that the company rate only helps on profit you leave inside the company. The moment you draw it as a dividend you add the dividend rate on top of the corporation tax already paid, so it is the <strong>combined rate</strong> that decides whether you are better off, not the 19% headline. For a fuller benefits-and-drawbacks treatment aimed at GPs, see <a href="/blog/gp-limited-company-tax-benefits-drawbacks">GP limited company tax benefits and drawbacks</a>.</p>

<h2>Worked examples: £40k, £80k and £150k of private profit</h2>

<p>These illustrate the shape of the decision for a doctor who already uses their personal allowance and basic-rate band on NHS income, so private profit sits in the higher or additional band. They are simplified (round figures, a single director on a token salary at the secondary threshold, ignoring the £500 dividend allowance and small reliefs) to show the direction of travel, not to be a personal calculation. Your own numbers will differ.</p>

<h3>Example 1: £40,000 private profit (draw it all)</h3>

<p><strong>Sole trader:</strong> 40% income tax plus 2% Class 4 on £40,000 is roughly £16,800, leaving about £23,200. If any of it crosses £125,140 the picture worsens, but assume it stays in the 40% band here.</p>

<p><strong>Company, fully extracted:</strong> corporation tax at 19% on £40,000 is £7,600, leaving £32,400 of post-tax profit. Extract it all as dividends at the upper rate of 35.75% and that costs about £11,583, so you keep roughly £20,817, before the extra running cost of the company. That is <em>worse</em> than sole trader here, and once £1,500 to £2,500 of annual compliance cost is added the gap widens.</p>

<p><strong>Verdict:</strong> at £40,000 drawn in full, incorporation does not pay. The dividend-rate rise and the double layer of tax on extraction cancel the corporation-tax advantage. Sole-trader simplicity wins unless you can retain most of the profit or have a specific structural reason (see below).</p>

<h3>Example 2: £80,000 private profit</h3>

<p><strong>Sole trader:</strong> 40% plus 2% on £80,000 (assuming it stays under £125,140) is about £33,600, leaving roughly £46,400.</p>

<p><strong>Company, fully extracted:</strong> corporation tax on £80,000 spans the small-profits and marginal bands; at an effective rate of around 20% to 21% here call it about £16,500, leaving roughly £63,500 to extract. Dividends at 35.75% on that cost about £22,700, leaving roughly £40,800 in hand, again slightly <em>behind</em> sole trader once you drain the company.</p>

<p><strong>Company, retaining £30,000:</strong> now the arithmetic turns. Draw £50,000 of dividends and leave £30,000 (post-corporation-tax) in the company for a future lower-income year, and you defer the dividend tax on the retained slice entirely. The retained profit can fund <strong>employer pension contributions</strong> (deductible for corporation tax, no NIC, no dividend tax) or be drawn later when you cut sessions or take partial retirement. That timing lever, not the headline rate, is where the company earns its keep at this level.</p>

<p><strong>Verdict:</strong> at £80,000 the case is genuine but conditional. If you must draw every pound, stay a sole trader. If you can retain, pension or time extraction, incorporation starts to work.</p>

<h3>Example 3: £150,000 private profit</h3>

<p><strong>Sole trader:</strong> a large slice now falls in the 45% additional-rate band (and the £100,000 to £125,140 personal-allowance taper stings on the way through). Income tax and Class 4 on £150,000 of higher-and-additional-rate profit is broadly £60,000 to £64,000, leaving roughly £86,000 to £90,000, and MTD quarterly reporting applies.</p>

<p><strong>Company:</strong> corporation tax on £150,000 (mostly in the 26.5% marginal band) is around £34,000 to £36,000, leaving roughly £114,000 to £116,000 inside the company. Drawn in full at 35.75%/39.35% the extraction tax is heavy and the total-tax gap to sole trader narrows, but the value at this level comes from <em>not</em> drawing it all: substantial <strong>employer pension contributions</strong> (up to the £60,000 annual allowance plus any carry-forward), retaining profit across years, and paying a genuinely employed spouse. A doctor at £150,000 of private profit who can pension £40,000 to £60,000 through the company and defer the rest can materially beat the sole-trader outcome, while a doctor who needs all £150,000 in cash this year gains far less.</p>

<p><strong>Verdict:</strong> at £150,000 incorporation is usually worth serious modelling, but the win is structural (pension, retention, family shareholding, future sale), not a simple rate arbitrage.</p>

<p>The pattern across all three: <strong>drawing everything out narrows or reverses the saving; retaining, pensioning or timing extraction is what makes a company pay.</strong></p>

<h2>The NHS pension interaction (the factor that decides most cases)</h2>

<p>Every pound routed through a company as a dividend earns <strong>zero NHS pension accrual</strong>. The NHS Pension Scheme is defined benefit (everyone now accruing in the 2015 CARE section at 1/54th of pensionable earnings each year), and that accrual is valuable. So the honest comparison is never tax-versus-tax alone; it is tax saving versus pension accrual given up.</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Hospital consultants:</strong> only the NHS post is pensionable in any case. Private work was never pensionable, so incorporating it loses <em>no</em> pension. For consultants with meaningful private income, this removes the biggest objection and is why incorporation more often stacks up for them than for GPs.</li>
<li><strong>GPs:</strong> solo and locum income can be pensioned through the practitioner route (the Type 1 Annual Certificate for partners, Type 2 self-assessment for salaried GPs, Locum forms A and B for freelancers). Routing that income through a company removes the accrual, which frequently outweighs the tax saving. GP <em>partnership</em> profit cannot be incorporated at all.</li>
<li><strong>Annual-allowance taper:</strong> a high earner breaching the <strong>£60,000 annual allowance</strong> (2025/26), or caught by the taper where threshold income exceeds £200,000 and adjusted income exceeds £260,000 (down to a £10,000 floor), may actively <em>want</em> private income held outside pensionable pay to protect the allowance. For them the loss of accrual is a feature, not a cost. See our <a href="/blog/nhs-pension-annual-allowance-complete-guide">annual allowance guide</a>.</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Never look at the tax saving in isolation; always pair it with the pension-accrual position.</strong> This single factor flips more incorporation decisions than any rate change.</p>

<h2>IR35 and the managed-service-company rules for consultants</h2>

<p>Incorporating does not switch off the off-payroll rules. If you provide services through a personal service company, <strong>IR35</strong> asks whether, but for the company, you would be an employee of the engager. For NHS Trust and other public-sector work the hirer decides status; for medium and large private hirers the hirer issues a Status Determination Statement; only small private clients leave the company to self-assess. Where an engagement is <strong>inside IR35</strong>, the fee-payer deducts PAYE and NIC before paying the company, so the salary-and-dividend efficiency is gone for that income. Our note on <a href="/blog/locum-doctor-ir35-what-you-need-to-know">IR35 for doctors</a> explains who decides, and <a href="/blog/locum-doctor-limited-company-pros-and-cons">locum limited company pros and cons</a> covers sessional work in depth.</p>

<p>Watch two related traps. The <strong>managed service company</strong> rules bite where a third-party provider (not a genuine accountant) controls a doctor's company and takes a cut of the income; a caught MSC has its income taxed as employment income, so avoid packaged "we run your company for you" schemes and use a normal accountant. Genuinely mixed private work (self-pay clinics, medico-legal, occupational health) with multiple payers and real business risk is the comfortable side of both rules; a single ongoing engagement that looks like disguised employment is the dangerous side.</p>

<h2>Spouse shareholding and family planning</h2>

<p>A company allows income to be shared with a spouse or family member, but it must have genuine commercial substance. Ordinary shares carrying full rights (votes, capital and dividends) transferred between spouses are generally an outright gift outside the settlements anti-avoidance legislation, so dividends on them are taxed on the recipient. But <strong>alienated or restricted shares</strong>, dividend-only or non-voting classes, or arrangements that shift income with no real economic gift, can be caught and taxed back on you. A spouse who does real work for the company can additionally take a <strong>market-rate salary</strong> as a deductible cost. Structure this deliberately and take advice before issuing shares, because a defensible arrangement and an aggressive one look similar on the surface and are taxed very differently. Our note on a <a href="/blog/family-investment-company-doctors-consultants">family investment company for doctors</a> covers longer-term wealth planning.</p>

<h2>Extraction strategy: getting money out efficiently</h2>

<p>Once incorporated, how you take money out matters as much as the decision to incorporate. A typical efficient mix for a medical company:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>A small salary</strong> at or near the £5,000 secondary threshold (or higher where a genuinely employed spouse unlocks the Employment Allowance), preserving state-pension qualifying years while limiting employer NIC.</li>
<li><strong>Dividends</strong> to use the basic-rate band and the £500 allowance, declared only from distributable profits, each with a board minute and voucher.</li>
<li><strong>Employer pension contributions</strong> as the standout route: deductible for corporation tax on a paid basis (FA 2004 s.196), no NIC, no dividend tax, subject to the £60,000 annual allowance and three-year carry-forward. This is often the single most efficient way to take value out of a medical company, though it builds a personal pension pot, not NHS accrual.</li>
<li><strong>Retained profit</strong> left in the company for a lower-income future year, avoiding a spike in a high-income year and the personal-allowance taper above £100,000.</li>
<li><strong>A future capital exit:</strong> on a genuine sale or winding-up of a private practice, <strong>Business Asset Disposal Relief</strong> can apply at <strong>18% from 6 April 2026</strong> (14% from 6 April 2025 to 5 April 2026, 10% before), within the £1m lifetime limit and two-year qualifying period, and on incorporation of a private practice with chargeable private goodwill, <strong>s.162 incorporation relief</strong> (TCGA 1992 s.162, amended by FA 2026 s.39 to a claimed relief from 6 April 2026) can defer the gain. Note that a GP cannot sell NHS goodwill at all (prohibited since 1 April 2004, SI 2019/251). See <a href="/blog/incorporation-relief-private-medical-practice-s162">incorporation relief (s.162)</a> and <a href="/blog/selling-private-medical-practice-cgt-badr">selling a private medical practice</a>.</li>
</ul>

<p>Beware the <strong>director's-loan (s.455) trap</strong>: a medical company is a close company, so drawing more than salary and dividends can support leaves an overdrawn loan, charged at <strong>33.75%</strong> (loans made in 2025/26) or <strong>35.75%</strong> (from 6 April 2026) on the balance outstanding nine months and one day after the year-end. It is repaid under s.458 once cleared, but the relief is deferred, so plan drawings so the loan does not run overdrawn. Our note on the <a href="/blog/consultant-directors-loan-account-s455-medical-company">consultant director's loan account</a> covers this, and <a href="/blog/surplus-cash-medical-limited-company-options">surplus cash options</a> covers what to do with profit you retain.</p>

<h2>When NOT to incorporate</h2>

<p>Incorporation is oversold. It is usually the wrong call where:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Your income is overwhelmingly NHS.</strong> The contract cannot be incorporated and the pension accrual on that income is too valuable to give up.</li>
<li><strong>Private profit is modest (roughly under £80,000) and you draw it all.</strong> The saving does not clear the running cost, as Example 1 shows.</li>
<li><strong>You need every pound of profit as cash each year.</strong> Without the ability to retain or pension, the company adds a second tax layer for little benefit.</li>
<li><strong>Your private work is inside IR35.</strong> The fee-payer deducts PAYE and NIC anyway, so the company gives you compliance cost with none of the extraction efficiency.</li>
<li><strong>You would be tempted into an MSC scheme.</strong> A packaged provider taking a cut and controlling the company risks the managed-service-company rules and can destroy the saving.</li>
<li><strong>Simpler options are unexhausted:</strong> optimising expenses and drawings as a sole trader, using pension carry-forward, or in-scheme options such as Added Pension may solve the problem without a company at all.</li>
</ul>

<h2>A hybrid worth considering</h2>

<p>For many doctors the best answer is not all-or-nothing. Keep NHS work (and, for GPs, the partnership share) where it belongs and pensioned, and incorporate <em>only</em> the private income streams that sit outside the pension anyway. That hybrid captures the corporation-tax and pension-contribution advantages on private work without giving up NHS accrual, and it is often the structure that models best for a consultant or GP with a substantial private practice alongside NHS commitments.</p>

<h2>Getting the decision right for you</h2>

<p>The worked examples show the shape, but the decision is personal: your income mix, how much is NHS-pensionable, your annual-allowance and taper position, whether private work is inside or outside IR35, your family circumstances and your retirement timeline. The wrong call can cost more in lost pension accrual than it ever saves in tax, and for NHS work it is not even possible.</p>
