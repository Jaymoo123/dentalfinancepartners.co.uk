<p>Finding the right <strong>GP accountant services</strong> changes how you manage your medical practice finances. Whether you are a GP partner with a profit share to allocate, a salaried GP with locum income on the side, or a consultant building private work, a specialist medical accountant understands the structures and rules that general practitioners and hospital doctors actually face.</p>

<p>Unlike a high-street accountant, a medical specialist knows GMS and PMS contracts, the Global Sum and Carr-Hill weighting, QOF, the NHS Pension Scheme and the GP pension certificates. This guide is the hub: it walks through the full service range and links to the detailed guide for each area, so you can see what a complete medical accounting service looks like before you go deeper.</p>

<h2>What GP Accountant Services Include</h2>

<p>Professional <strong>GP accountant services</strong> go well beyond bookkeeping. A complete service for a UK practice or doctor usually spans:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Partnership accounts and the SA800</strong>, with profit allocation and capital account management for GP partnerships.</li>
<li><strong>Personal self-assessment</strong>, covering partnership profit shares, salaried income, locum work and any private or media income.</li>
<li><strong>NHS pension certification and planning</strong>, including the Type 1, Type 2 and locum forms and annual allowance work.</li>
<li><strong>Payroll and PAYE</strong> for the practice team and salaried GPs.</li>
<li><strong>VAT</strong> where private, cosmetic or medico-legal work brings it into scope.</li>
<li><strong>Tax planning and incorporation advice</strong> for private work, weighed against the NHS pension consequences.</li>
<li><strong>Bookkeeping, software and Making Tax Digital</strong> support to keep records compliant and quarterly updates on track.</li>
</ul>

<p>The sections below introduce each area. For the underlying detail, follow the linked guide rather than expecting every number here, this page is the map, not the territory.</p>

<h2>Partnership Accounts and Profit Sharing</h2>

<p>GP partnerships have their own accounting rhythm. The partnership files an <strong>SA800</strong> and each partner's profit share flows to the partnership pages of their personal return, taxed as trading income. A partner is taxed on their <strong>profit share, not their drawings</strong>, so getting the allocation right matters for every partner's tax position and NHS pension.</p>

<p>Profit shares can reflect seniority, sessions, list responsibilities and non-clinical roles, and the accounts also handle capital accounts on partner entry and exit, premises and equipment. We cover the mechanics in the <a href="/blog/gp-partnership-tax-complete-guide">GP partnership tax guide</a> and the allocation choices in <a href="/blog/gp-partnership-profit-sharing-tax-planning">GP partnership profit sharing and tax planning</a>. Where the practice income itself needs explaining, see <a href="/blog/how-gms-funding-works-global-sum-carr-hill-explained">how GMS funding works (Global Sum and Carr-Hill)</a>.</p>

<h2>NHS Pension: Certificates and Annual Allowance</h2>

<p>The NHS pension is the single most medical-specific part of the work, and it has two halves: getting the right <strong>certificate</strong> filed, and managing the <strong>annual allowance</strong>.</p>

<p>Certificates follow your role. A GP partner (Type 1) completes the <strong>Annual Certificate of Pensionable Profits</strong>; a salaried GP (Type 2) completes the <strong>Type 2 self-assessment</strong>; a freelance locum pensions income via <strong>Locum forms A and B</strong> through PCSE. We explain the locum route in <a href="/blog/nhs-pension-for-locums-form-a-form-b">NHS pension for locums (Form A and Form B)</a>.</p>

<p>On the tax side, the standard pension <strong>annual allowance is £60,000</strong>. It tapers where <strong>threshold income exceeds £200,000 and adjusted income exceeds £260,000</strong>, down to a floor of <strong>£10,000</strong>, and for a defined-benefit scheme the measure is the <strong>pension input amount</strong> (capitalised growth), not contributions paid. Note this is the level since April 2023, when the allowance rose from its former £40,000 and the floor rose from £4,000. The lifetime allowance was <strong>abolished from 6 April 2024</strong> and replaced by the Lump Sum Allowance (£268,275) and the Lump Sum and Death Benefit Allowance (£1,073,100). Where a charge arises, <strong>Scheme Pays</strong> can settle it. The full detail sits in the <a href="/blog/nhs-pension-annual-allowance-complete-guide">NHS pension annual allowance guide</a> and the <a href="/blog/nhs-pension-scheme-pays-doctors-deadlines">Scheme Pays deadlines guide</a>.</p>

<h2>Personal Tax and Self-Assessment</h2>

<p>Most doctors have more than one income source, which is where careful self-assessment earns its keep:</p>

<ul>
<li>Partnership profit shares from NHS and private work.</li>
<li>Locum income from several engagements.</li>
<li>Teaching, training and appraisal fees.</li>
<li>Medical writing, media and consultancy work.</li>
<li>Investment income and let property.</li>
</ul>

<p>A specialist accountant claims the right allowable expenses (the <strong>GMC retention fee</strong>, indemnity for private and non-clinical work, relevant Royal College and BMA subscriptions, and business mileage at the HMRC rate of <strong>55p per mile for the first 10,000 business miles in 2026/27</strong>, 25p thereafter), applies <strong>Class 4 National Insurance at 6% then 2%</strong> (Class 2 is no longer a required payment), and handles HMRC correspondence. The full deductions checklist is in our <a href="/blog/gp-tax-deductions-complete-list-2026">GP tax deductions list for 2026</a>, with the process covered in the <a href="/blog/gp-tax-return">GP tax return guide</a> and broader strategy in <a href="/blog/gp-tax-advice">GP tax advice</a>.</p>

<h2>Payroll, PAYE and the Practice Team</h2>

<p>A GP practice is an employer. Salaried GPs, nurses, reception and admin staff all run through <strong>PAYE</strong>, with employer (secondary Class 1) National Insurance at 15% above the £5,000 secondary threshold and auto-enrolment pension duties. A specialist payroll service also handles the NHS pension employer side and starters and leavers cleanly. See <a href="/blog/gp-payroll-services">GP payroll services</a> for how this works in practice.</p>

<h2>VAT for Medical Practices</h2>

<p>Genuine <strong>medical care by a registered practitioner is exempt from VAT</strong> where the principal purpose is the protection, maintenance or restoration of health, and NHS GMS and PMS income is outside the scope of VAT. The watch-items are purely cosmetic or aesthetic work, medico-legal and expert-witness reports, and some occupational-health and administrative services, which can be <strong>standard-rated</strong>.</p>

<p>The VAT registration threshold is <strong>£90,000</strong> of taxable (non-exempt) turnover (from 1 April 2024), with deregistration at £88,000, and only non-exempt turnover counts. A practice with both exempt and taxable supplies runs partial exemption. The detail, including when a doctor crosses the line, is in <a href="/blog/gp-vat-registration">GP VAT registration</a>.</p>

<h2>Locum Doctors and IR35</h2>

<p>Locum work has its own service strand. A freelance locum is usually a <strong>sole trader</strong>, but some operate through a <strong>personal service company</strong>, which brings the <strong>off-payroll (IR35) rules</strong> into play. For NHS Trust work the status determination sits with the Trust or fee-payer, not the locum; for medium and large private hirers it sits with the hirer; only small private clients leave the decision with the company. The off-payroll rules have not been abolished. We cover the engagement-by-engagement reality in the <a href="/blog/locum-doctor-tax-complete-guide">locum doctor tax guide</a> and <a href="/blog/locum-doctor-ir35-what-you-need-to-know">locum doctor IR35</a>.</p>

<h2>Incorporation and Private Practice</h2>

<p>Incorporation is the area where specialist judgement matters most, because the headline tax comparison is only half the story. A <strong>limited company cannot hold an NHS GMS or PMS contract</strong>, and company income and dividends are <strong>not NHS-pensionable</strong>, so incorporation is a private-work decision and any tax saving has to be set against the loss of NHS pension accrual.</p>

<p>The tax machinery still moves with the year: corporation tax runs from 19% on profits up to £50,000 to 25% above £250,000 with marginal relief between, and <strong>dividend rates rose to 10.75% ordinary and 35.75% upper from 6 April 2026</strong> (additional rate unchanged at 39.35%, allowance £500), which narrows the saving further. We model both sides in the <a href="/blog/medical-practice-incorporation-step-by-step">medical practice incorporation guide</a>, with the company tax detail in <a href="/blog/gp-corporation-tax">GP corporation tax</a> and the mixed-income picture in <a href="/blog/private-practice-tax-nhs-and-private-income">private practice tax (NHS and private income)</a>.</p>

<h2>Practice Sale, Goodwill and CGT</h2>

<p>A point that surprises doctors moving from other sectors: <strong>NHS GP goodwill cannot be sold</strong>, and has not been saleable since 1 April 2004 (the current instrument is SI 2019/251). A GP transaction is about tangible assets, premises and capital accounts, not NHS goodwill. The only goodwill a GP can sell is <strong>private</strong> goodwill.</p>

<p>Business Asset Disposal Relief therefore applies only to a private-practice disposal or a share sale of an incorporated private practice. The BADR rate is <strong>18% for a disposal on or after 6 April 2026</strong> (it was 14% from 6 April 2025 to 5 April 2026, and 10% before that), within the £1m lifetime limit. The rules are set out in <a href="/blog/can-gp-practice-goodwill-be-sold-nhs-rules">can GP practice goodwill be sold</a> and <a href="/blog/selling-private-medical-practice-cgt-badr">selling a private medical practice (CGT and BADR)</a>.</p>

<h2>Bookkeeping, Software and Making Tax Digital</h2>

<p>Good records underpin every other service. Cloud bookkeeping gives a real-time view of the practice or your personal position and feeds into <strong>Making Tax Digital for Income Tax</strong>, which began for sole traders and landlords with qualifying income over <strong>£50,000 from 6 April 2026</strong> (then £30,000 from April 2027 and £20,000 from April 2028). Limited companies are out, and general partnerships are deferred, but most full-time locums and unincorporated private GPs are in scope. See the <a href="/blog/gp-bookkeeping-guide-uk">GP bookkeeping guide</a> for setting this up.</p>

<h2>Choosing the Right Medical Accountant</h2>

<p>Not every accountant understands medical practice. When comparing <strong>GP accountant services</strong>, look for:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Medical specialisation</strong>, a genuine focus on doctors and practices rather than a general client base.</li>
<li><strong>NHS pension fluency</strong>, current on the annual allowance, the taper, Scheme Pays and the Type 1, Type 2 and locum certificates.</li>
<li><strong>Contract knowledge</strong>, GMS, PMS, Global Sum, Carr-Hill, QOF and PCN income, not UDAs or dental concepts.</li>
<li><strong>Regulatory awareness</strong>, the GMC, the NHS England Performers List and CQC, and which fees are deductible.</li>
<li><strong>Modern systems</strong>, cloud software and a portal so records and updates stay current through the year.</li>
<li><strong>Proactive advice</strong>, strategic planning around the annual allowance and incorporation, not just year-end compliance.</li>
</ul>

<h2>When to Engage Professional Help</h2>

<p>Some situations make specialist <strong>GP accountant services</strong> worthwhile rather than optional:</p>

<ul>
<li>Joining or leaving a GP partnership, or a buy-in or buy-out of capital.</li>
<li>Starting private practice alongside NHS work.</li>
<li>Considering incorporation of private work or restructuring.</li>
<li>Facing NHS pension annual allowance charges or a Scheme Pays decision.</li>
<li>An HMRC enquiry or a compliance question.</li>
<li>Planning retirement, partial retirement or succession.</li>
</ul>
