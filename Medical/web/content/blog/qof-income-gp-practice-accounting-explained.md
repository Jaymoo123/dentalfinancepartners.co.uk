<p>The <strong>Quality and Outcomes Framework (QOF)</strong> is the quality-and-incentive layer of GP funding, and it confuses partners more than almost any other income line. The reason is timing: the cash arrives in two parts and across two financial years. Monthly <strong>aspiration payments</strong> come through the year, and then an <strong>achievement reconciliation</strong> arrives after year-end. So the QOF money in the bank in any given year is never the same as the QOF the practice actually earned for that year.</p>

<p>This guide explains the points-based mechanics first, then does the part many summaries skip: how QOF income should be <strong>recognised in the practice accounts</strong> (accruing the expected achievement, not just the cash received) and how it is <strong>taxed</strong> as ordinary practice trading profit. The aim is a partner who can read the QOF line in their accounts and understands why the cash and the profit do not match. QOF sits alongside the core funding covered in <a href="/blog/how-gms-funding-works-global-sum-carr-hill-explained">how GMS funding works</a>; if you have arrived from that guide, this is the detailed treatment of the QOF line.</p>

<h2>What QOF is (and where it sits in GP funding)</h2>

<p>QOF is the <strong>voluntary</strong> annual quality-and-incentive scheme that rewards practices for measured quality of care. A practice chooses whether to take part each year, and most do because it is a meaningful income stream. It sits <strong>alongside</strong> the core Global Sum (the main per-weighted-patient payment explained in <a href="/blog/how-gms-funding-works-global-sum-carr-hill-explained">how GMS funding works</a>), not inside it.</p>

<p>The scheme is <strong>points-based</strong>. The practice scores points against a set of clinical and other indicators, and those points convert to money. Crucially for the rest of this article, QOF is <strong>income to the practice</strong> that ultimately forms part of the partners' profit. It is not separate or special money; it is practice trading income, taxed as part of partnership profit, which we cover in our <a href="/blog/gp-partnership-tax-complete-guide">GP partnership tax complete guide</a>. QOF is also distinct from <a href="/blog/enhanced-services-gp-practice-income-tax">enhanced services</a>, which are a different income stream covered separately.</p>

<p>One framing helps before we go into the mechanics. QOF is best thought of as the <strong>variable, quality-linked</strong> layer of practice income, in contrast to the largely automatic core Global Sum. The Global Sum arrives because the practice has a registered, weighted list; QOF arrives because the practice has demonstrably done certain things well. That distinction matters for managing the practice, because QOF income is something a practice can lose, gain or improve through how it organises its clinical work and its data, whereas the core funding moves mostly with the list and the annual uplift. Good <a href="/blog/gp-practice-management">GP practice management</a> treats QOF as an income stream to be actively managed rather than a windfall to be banked.</p>

<h2>How QOF points work</h2>

<h3>Indicators and the points total</h3>

<p>The indicators themselves fall into broad domains. Most points sit in the <strong>clinical domain</strong>, rewarding the management of long-term conditions such as diabetes, hypertension, asthma and chronic obstructive pulmonary disease, typically through measures of registration, monitoring and control. Alongside these have sat indicators relating to public-health priorities and, in some years, quality-improvement modules where the practice works on a defined area of care. The exact mix and emphasis change as the scheme is reviewed, so the safe statement is that QOF rewards measured quality across clinical and related domains, with the detail of the current indicator set sitting in the live QOF guidance rather than in any fixed summary.</p>

<p>QOF is structured as a set of <strong>indicators</strong>, each worth a number of points, summing to an annual maximum. The indicator set and the maximum number of points are <strong>set nationally and reviewed each year</strong>: indicators are added, retired or amended as clinical priorities change, and the total available points moves with them. Recent years have used a points total in the region of several hundred points, but treat that as a hedged, date-tagged illustration rather than a fixed figure, because the total is reviewed annually. The current indicator set and points maximum should always be checked in the current QOF guidance.</p>

<h3>The value of a point</h3>

<p>Each point has a <strong>national base value</strong> that is uplifted annually as part of the contract settlement, and that base value is then adjusted at practice level (covered below). The point value was uprated for 2025/26, but you should not lock any specific pounds-per-point figure as permanent. It moves each year, so the safe approach is to confirm the current point value in the Statement of Financial Entitlements or the current QOF guidance.</p>

<h3>The two practice adjustments</h3>

<p>The national point value is adjusted at practice level by two factors, so that the same point is worth more to a larger or higher-need practice:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>List size</strong> relative to the national average list. A larger practice serves more patients per indicator, so its points are scaled up accordingly.</li>
<li><strong>Disease prevalence</strong>, the adjusted prevalence factor. A practice with a higher prevalence of a given condition is doing more work on the indicators relating to it, so the value is adjusted to reflect that.</li>
</ul>

<p>The result is that two practices scoring the same number of points can earn different amounts of QOF income, because their list size and prevalence differ. The precise multipliers are technical and set within the scheme, so we describe the mechanism qualitatively here rather than inventing numbers.</p>

<p>The prevalence adjustment in particular catches people out, because it means QOF income is not a pure measure of effort. A practice can work very hard on a clinical area where its registered prevalence is comparatively low and earn less from those points than a practice with a higher prevalence doing the same clinical work. That is the scheme working as intended, since it is trying to direct reward towards where the disease burden actually sits, but it means a partner cannot judge QOF performance purely by the cash. Two practices with identical clinical achievement can show materially different QOF income, and neither is doing anything wrong. The lesson for reading the accounts is to look at points achieved and the practice's own trend year on year, not just the pounds.</p>

<h2>Aspiration payments and achievement payments (the two-part cash flow)</h2>

<p>This is the heart of why QOF confuses people. The money does not arrive in a single lump after the practice has earned it. It arrives in two distinct forms.</p>

<h3>Aspiration payments</h3>

<p>An <strong>aspiration payment</strong> is a part-payment made <strong>through the year, in advance</strong> of the practice's confirmed achievement. It is broadly based on a percentage of the prior year's achievement, or an agreed aspiration, and is paid monthly via the commissioner and the PCSE route. In effect, the practice receives a steady flow of QOF cash during the year on account of what it is expected to achieve, before that achievement has been measured.</p>

<h3>Achievement payments</h3>

<p>After the year-end, the practice's <strong>actual achievement</strong> is measured against the indicators. The practice is then paid the <strong>balance</strong>: the total achievement value for the year, minus the aspiration already paid during it. Where achievement comes in <strong>below</strong> the aspiration already paid, there can be a clawback or adjustment, because the practice will have been paid more on account than it ultimately earned.</p>

<h3>Why the cash crosses two years</h3>

<p>Put the two together and the timing problem becomes clear. In any single financial year, the practice receives <strong>aspiration for the current year</strong> plus the <strong>achievement balance for the prior year</strong>. Both land in the same year. That is exactly why the cash received in a year is <strong>not</strong> the same as the QOF the practice actually earned for that year. This is the key accounting hook, and it drives everything in the next section.</p>

<p>It is worth walking through a simple sequence to make the overlap vivid. In Year 1, the practice receives aspiration payments for Year 1 through the year. After Year 1 closes, its Year 1 achievement is measured and the Year 1 balance is paid <em>during Year 2</em>. Meanwhile, Year 2 has its own aspiration arriving through Year 2. So the cash banked in Year 2 is the Year 1 achievement balance plus the Year 2 aspiration, a mixture of two different years' entitlements. Anyone who looks only at the bank in Year 2 sees a figure that belongs partly to Year 1 and partly to Year 2, and that figure will rarely if ever equal the QOF the practice actually earned for Year 2. The same overlap repeats every year, so the mismatch is permanent, not a one-off.</p>

<p>The size of the overlap depends on how aspiration is set. Where aspiration is pitched as a high percentage of expected achievement, the year-end balance is small and the cash sits close to the earned figure. Where aspiration is pitched conservatively, the year-end balance is larger and the two diverge more. Either way the accounts have to bridge the gap, which is the job of the accrual.</p>

<h2>How QOF income is recognised in the practice accounts</h2>

<h3>Accrual, not just cash</h3>

<p>The correct treatment is to recognise the <strong>QOF the practice has earned for the accounting period</strong>, that is its expected achievement, rather than simply the cash that happened to arrive. In practice that means carrying a <strong>debtor or accrual</strong> for the achievement balance not yet received, and offsetting the aspiration already received against it. This accruals approach is the reason <strong>QOF in the accounts differs from QOF in the bank</strong>. A practice that records only the cash will misstate its profit, sometimes materially, in any year where achievement and aspiration diverge.</p>

<h3>Estimating year-end achievement</h3>

<p>Because the accounts need the earned figure before actual achievement is confirmed, the practice (with its accountant) <strong>estimates</strong> the likely year-end achievement to accrue the right amount. That estimate draws on performance to date, prior-year achievement and clinical judgement about likely scores. If the estimate turns out to be too high or too low, it is simply <strong>corrected the following year</strong> when actual achievement is known, so the cumulative position self-corrects over time.</p>

<p>A sensible estimate is built rather than guessed. The practice looks at how many points it has effectively secured against indicators with a strong performance position, applies a realistic view to indicators still in progress, and reflects any known issues (a clinical area that has slipped, a data-recording problem) that would pull the year-end score down. Where the practice has a stable track record, the prior year is a reasonable anchor adjusted for any changes in the indicator set. Where the practice has had an unusual year, the estimate leans more on current performance than on history. The point is a defensible figure, neither optimistic nor pessimistic, because a consistently biased estimate either flatters or depresses profit and feeds straight into the partners' tax and drawings.</p>

<p>It is also worth accruing prudently where there is genuine uncertainty about whether an achievement balance will be paid in full, for example where there is a known data or verification issue. The aim of the accrual is to recognise income the practice can reasonably expect to receive for work it has done, not to book optimistic figures that may later be clawed back. A measured, evidence-based estimate is what makes the QOF line in the accounts both accurate and durable.</p>

<h3>Why it matters for partners</h3>

<p>Drawings and profit-share decisions should track <strong>earned profit, not the cash timing</strong>. If partners take drawings against a year that happens to have heavy QOF cash inflows from two years' entitlements, they can over-draw relative to the profit actually earned. The accrual also matters for <strong>fairness on partner changes</strong>: a partner joining or leaving mid-year should have their share based on the QOF accrued to the relevant date, not the lumpy cash. The broader accounts and bookkeeping that house this treatment are covered in our <a href="/blog/gp-accounting-guide">GP accounting guide</a> and <a href="/blog/gp-bookkeeping-guide-uk">GP bookkeeping guide</a>; this section is the QOF-specific overlay on them. The lumpy cash flow itself is worth planning for, which we discuss in <a href="/blog/gp-financial-planning">GP financial planning</a>.</p>

<p>There is a subtle trap in the first year a practice gets its QOF accounting right after years of cash-basis treatment. Switching to accruals can make one year's profit look distorted, because the practice picks up both the cash it would have recognised and the new accrual it should always have carried. This is a transition effect, not a real change in the practice's earning power, and it should be explained clearly to the partners so they do not read a one-off accounting catch-up as a genuine jump or fall in income. After the transition year, the accrual simply rolls forward and the profit settles to reflect what was actually earned each year.</p>

<h2>How QOF income is taxed</h2>

<p>QOF is <strong>ordinary practice trading income</strong>. There is nothing exotic about its tax treatment. It is part of the partnership's taxable trading profit, allocated to partners under the profit-sharing agreement and taxed on each partner's <strong>profit share</strong>, with <strong>Class 4 National Insurance</strong>, through the partnership return (SA800) and the partnership pages (SA104) of each partner's personal return. The mechanics of that flow are set out in full in our <a href="/blog/gp-partnership-tax-complete-guide">GP partnership tax complete guide</a>.</p>

<p>The point to hold onto is that there is <strong>no special QOF tax regime</strong>. QOF is taxed <strong>when earned</strong>, on the accruals basis in the accounts, not when the cash arrives. So the same accrual that gives the right profit figure also gives the right taxable figure. Any National Insurance rate or banding reference should be taken from the current year's figures, which our partnership tax guide keeps current rather than us restating them here.</p>

<p>This is also why the accrual is not merely a presentational nicety: it is the figure that drives each partner's tax. If a practice mis-states QOF by recognising cash rather than earned income, it mis-states the partnership's taxable profit, and therefore every partner's tax bill, in the same breath. In a year where two years' worth of QOF cash arrives, the cash-basis error would overstate profit and over-tax the partners; in a lean cash year it would understate profit and risk an under-provision that surfaces later. Getting the accrual right is the thing that keeps the tax right, which is one more reason it is worth doing properly rather than defaulting to whatever the bank statement shows.</p>

<p>On pensions, one short point. Because QOF feeds NHS-derived profit, it forms part of <strong>pensionable profit</strong> for a Type 1 partner, certified through the Annual Certificate of Pensionable Profits. Income routed through a limited company is the opposite: a company cannot hold a GMS or PMS contract and company income is <strong>not</strong> NHS-pensionable. So the company route does not build NHS pension on this income. For the detail, see our guides to <a href="/blog/gp-pension-contributions-tax-relief">GP pension contributions and tax relief</a> and <a href="/blog/gp-corporation-tax">GP corporation tax</a>.</p>

<h2>Common QOF accounting and tax mistakes</h2>

<p>A handful of QOF errors recur across practices, and all of them flow from the two-year cash timing:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Treating QOF as cash-in.</strong> Recording only the cash received and missing the accrual, which misstates profit in any year where aspiration and achievement diverge.</li>
<li><strong>Forgetting the prior-year achievement balance.</strong> Overlooking that the prior year's achievement payment lands in the current year, which inflates the apparent current-year income.</li>
<li><strong>Double-counting aspiration and achievement.</strong> Recognising both the aspiration and the full achievement for the same year without offsetting, which overstates income.</li>
<li><strong>Not reconciling.</strong> Failing to check the QOF actually paid against what was expected, so errors and clawbacks go unnoticed until they compound.</li>
</ul>

<p>None of these is hard to avoid. They are avoided by accruing QOF consistently and reconciling it at the year-end, which is exactly the discipline that keeps each partner's profit share and tax position accurate.</p>

<p>A further mistake worth naming is treating a clawback as a surprise rather than a built-in feature. Because aspiration is paid in advance, a year of lower-than-expected achievement will mechanically produce an adjustment, and a practice that has spent the full aspiration as if it were earned income can find itself short. The accrual protects against this by recognising only what the practice expects to keep, so the accounts and the partners' drawings already reflect the likely position rather than the gross cash. Where a practice keeps its QOF performance data and its accruals aligned through the year, the year-end reconciliation becomes a confirmation rather than a shock.</p>
