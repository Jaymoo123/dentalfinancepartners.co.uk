<p>GP accounting involves managing the financial affairs of medical partnerships, from profit allocation to tax compliance. Whether you're a GP partner, salaried GP considering partnership, or looking to understand your practice finances better, getting your accounting right is crucial for financial success and regulatory compliance.</p>

<p>This guide covers everything you need to know about GP accounting, from partnership structures to tax obligations and NHS pension implications.</p>

<h2>Understanding GP Partnership Accounting</h2>

<p>Most GP practices operate as partnerships, which creates specific accounting requirements. Unlike limited companies, partnerships are transparent for tax purposes — meaning profits flow through to individual partners who pay personal tax on their share.</p>

<p>Partnership accounting involves several key elements:</p>

<ul>
<li>Recording all practice income (GMS payments, QOF bonuses, private work)</li>
<li>Tracking all allowable expenses (staff costs, premises, equipment)</li>
<li>Calculating profit shares for each partner</li>
<li>Preparing annual accounts and tax returns</li>
<li>Managing cash flow and working capital</li>
</ul>

<p>The partnership must file annual accounts with HMRC, and each partner receives tax computations showing their individual share of profits.</p>

<h2>GP Partnership Profit Allocation</h2>

<p>Profit sharing arrangements vary between practices, but common methods include:</p>

<h3>Equal Share Arrangements</h3>
<p>All partners receive equal profit shares regardless of different roles or seniority. Simple to administer but may not reflect varying contributions.</p>

<h3>Performance-Based Allocation</h3>
<p>Profits allocated based on factors like patient numbers, sessions worked, or specific responsibilities (senior partner, training, clinical leads). More complex but often fairer.</p>

<h3>Hybrid Approaches</h3>
<p>Combination of base equal share plus performance elements. For example, 70% equal sharing, 30% based on sessions or patient lists.</p>

<p>A typical GP partner earning £120,000 from a practice with strong QOF performance might see profits allocated as: £80,000 base share, £25,000 performance bonus, £15,000 from enhanced services.</p>

<h2>Tax Implications for GP Partners</h2>

<p>GP accounting directly impacts your tax position. Partnership profits are subject to:</p>

<ul>
<li>Income tax at 20%, 40%, or 45% depending on total income</li>
<li>Class 2 National Insurance (£3.45 per week for 2025/26)</li>
<li>Class 4 National Insurance (9% on profits between £12,570 and £50,270, then 2%)</li>
</ul>

<p>The basis period reform from April 2024 means profits are now taxed on a current year basis, ending the previous overlap relief system. This particularly affects new partners and those leaving partnerships.</p>

<h3>Allowable GP Expenses</h3>
<p>GP accounting must correctly identify allowable expenses to minimise tax liability:</p>

<ul>
<li>GMC registration and professional indemnity (MDU/MPS fees)</li>
<li>Continuing professional development and training</li>
<li>BMA membership and professional subscriptions</li>
<li>Medical equipment and consultation room costs</li>
<li>Motor expenses for home visits</li>
<li>Locum insurance and professional costs</li>
</ul>

<h2>NHS Pension and GP Accounting</h2>

<p>GP accounting must consider <a href="/nhs-pension">NHS pension</a> implications, particularly the annual allowance. For 2025/26, the standard annual allowance is £60,000, but high-earning GPs face tapered allowances.</p>

<p>The tapered annual allowance applies when:</p>
<ul>
<li>Threshold income exceeds £200,000</li>
<li>Adjusted income exceeds £260,000</li>
</ul>

<p>A GP partner with £180,000 practice profits plus £30,000 other income would face threshold income of £210,000, potentially triggering taper. The minimum tapered allowance is £10,000.</p>

<p>GP accounting should track pensionable income and growth to manage annual allowance exposure.</p>

<h2>Cash Flow Management in GP Practices</h2>

<p>Effective GP accounting includes robust cash flow management. NHS payments arrive monthly but expenses are ongoing, creating potential cash flow gaps.</p>

<p>Key cash flow considerations:</p>

<ul>
<li>Managing quarterly tax payments on account</li>
<li>Planning for equipment purchases and practice improvements</li>
<li>Budgeting for locum cover during holidays and training</li>
<li>Setting aside funds for partnership buyouts or retirements</li>
</ul>

<p>Many practices maintain working capital equivalent to 2-3 months' expenses to smooth cash flow variations.</p>

<h2>Record Keeping and Compliance</h2>

<p>GP accounting requires meticulous record keeping to satisfy HMRC requirements and support business decisions. Essential records include:</p>

<ul>
<li>All income records (NHS payments, private work, other sources)</li>
<li>Expense receipts and invoices with clear business purposes</li>
<li>Mileage logs for home visits and medical travel</li>
<li>Bank statements and reconciliations</li>
<li>Partnership agreements and profit sharing arrangements</li>
</ul>

<p>Digital record keeping is increasingly common, with cloud-based systems allowing real-time access to financial data.</p>

<ul>
