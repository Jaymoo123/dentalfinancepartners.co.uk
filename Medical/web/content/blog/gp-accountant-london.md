<p>Choosing the right <strong>GP accountant in London</strong> matters because general-practice finances are unlike almost any other small business. Your income comes from an NHS contract that no generalist accountant ever sees, your pension is a defined-benefit scheme with its own certification machinery, and your tax position spans a partnership, your personal return and, increasingly, private and medico-legal work. This page sets out what a specialist <strong>GP accountant in London</strong> actually does, the 2026/27 figures that drive the numbers, and how the London context (higher earnings, valuable premises, mixed NHS and private income) changes the planning. It is general information for GPs, partners, salaried doctors and locums, not personal advice.</p>

<h2>Why London GPs need a specialist medical accountant</h2>

<p>The tax rules are UK-wide, so a London GP is not taxed under a different code. What is different is the <em>profile</em>. London GPs are more likely to cross the pension annual allowance taper, more likely to hold high-value owned surgery premises, and more likely to add private, occupational-health or medico-legal income on top of NHS work. Each of those raises questions a generalist accountant rarely meets.</p>

<p>A specialist <strong>GP accountant in London</strong> understands how general-practice money actually flows. NHS core funding arrives as the <strong>Global Sum</strong>, a per-patient payment weighted by the <strong>Carr-Hill formula</strong>, plus <strong>QOF</strong> (the Quality and Outcomes Framework), enhanced services and <strong>Primary Care Network (PCN) / Network Contract DES</strong> funding including the Additional Roles Reimbursement Scheme. GPs do not bill UDAs or dental bands, and there is no single national per-patient value because the figures are weighted and uplifted each year. An accountant who knows that vocabulary is starting from the right place. For the funding mechanics, see our explainer on <a href="/blog/how-gms-funding-works-global-sum-carr-hill-explained">how GMS funding works</a> and on <a href="/blog/qof-income-gp-practice-accounting-explained">QOF income in practice accounts</a>.</p>

<h2>Getting your role right: partner, salaried GP, locum or consultant</h2>

<p>Tax status follows the substance of the working arrangement, not the label, and the four common roles sit very differently:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>GP partner.</strong> Self-employed. The partnership files an SA800 and each partner's profit share flows to the partnership pages of their personal return, taxed as trading income with Class 4 NIC. Partners are taxed on their <strong>profit share, not their drawings</strong>, so reconciling drawings against actual profit at year-end is central work.</li>
<li><strong>Salaried GP.</strong> An employee of the practice, taxed under PAYE with Class 1 NIC, and an active NHS pension member.</li>
<li><strong>Locum GP.</strong> Usually a sole trader (self-assessment), or less commonly through a personal service company. A sole-trader locum has no intermediary so IR35 does not apply; a company changes that picture.</li>
<li><strong>Hospital consultant.</strong> An NHS employee (PAYE) who may also do private work as a sole trader, partnership or company; only the NHS employment is pensionable.</li>
</ul>

<p>Getting this mapping right at the outset prevents the most common and expensive errors. If you are weighing a move into partnership, our guides on <a href="/blog/gp-partner-vs-salaried-gp-tax-comparison">GP partner versus salaried GP tax</a> and <a href="/blog/becoming-gp-partner-financial-implications">becoming a GP partner</a> go deeper than we can here.</p>

<h2>Partnership and practice accounting</h2>

<p>Your <strong>GP accountant in London</strong> should handle the full cycle: bookkeeping, annual accounts, the SA800 partnership return, and the profit allocation between partners under the partnership agreement (prior shares, seniority, sessions and any property element). Many London practices have complex arrangements, multiple sites, federated or PCN working, or dispensing income, and these need an accountant who has seen them before. For the underlying mechanics, see our <a href="/blog/gp-partnership-tax-complete-guide">GP partnership tax guide</a> and <a href="/blog/gp-partnership-profit-sharing-tax-planning">profit-sharing and tax planning</a> explainer.</p>

<h3>Premises: a bigger feature for London GPs</h3>

<p>Surgery premises are a far larger issue for GPs than for most healthcare businesses, and London property values make the stakes higher. Premises are often held in a <strong>separate property partnership or LLP</strong> outside the medical partnership. Income support comes via <strong>notional rent</strong> (for owner-occupiers, on a current-market-rent basis assessed by the District Valuer), legacy <strong>cost rent</strong> (closed to new schemes), or improvement grants, under the NHS (General Medical Services) Premises Costs Directions 2024. These amounts are property-specific, so no accountant should quote a figure without your valuation. A specialist will also flag the <strong>last man standing</strong> risk, where a single remaining partner is left holding the whole premises lease and liability. See our guides on <a href="/blog/gp-surgery-notional-rent-vs-cost-rent-explained">notional rent versus cost rent</a>, <a href="/blog/gp-surgery-premises-own-vs-rent-tax-guide">owning versus renting premises</a> and the <a href="/blog/gp-partnership-last-man-standing-premises-risk">last man standing premises risk</a>.</p>

<h2>NHS pension: where London GPs gain or lose the most</h2>

<p>For higher-earning London GPs, the NHS pension is usually where a specialist accountant earns their keep. All active members now accrue in the <strong>2015 CARE section</strong> (1/54th of each year's pensionable earnings), with the older 1995 and 2008 final-salary sections held as legacy service. The annual allowance change in April 2023 (the limit rose from £40,000 to £60,000) gave higher earners more room, but London partners and consultants can still breach it.</p>

<p>The key figures for <strong>2025/26</strong>: the annual allowance is <strong>£60,000</strong>, measured for a defined-benefit scheme by the <strong>pension input amount</strong> (capitalised growth), not the contributions paid. It tapers where <strong>threshold income exceeds £200,000 and adjusted income exceeds £260,000</strong>, falling by £1 for every £2 of adjusted income above £260,000, down to a floor of <strong>£10,000</strong>. The money purchase annual allowance is <strong>£10,000</strong>. The lifetime allowance was <strong>abolished from 6 April 2024</strong> and replaced by the Lump Sum Allowance (£268,275) and the Lump Sum and Death Benefit Allowance (£1,073,100). A London GP earning into six figures with strong pension growth needs the input amount monitored each year, because an allowance breach is taxed at marginal rate. Where a charge arises, <strong>Scheme Pays</strong> can settle it from the pension itself.</p>

Your accountant should also handle the certification side: the <strong>Type 1 Annual Certificate of Pensionable Profits</strong> for partners, the <strong>Type 2 self-assessment</strong> for salaried GPs, and <a href="/blog/nhs-pension-for-locums-form-a-form-b">Locum forms A and B</a> for freelance locums.</p>

<h2>Tax returns, expenses and Making Tax Digital</h2>

<p>Beyond the accounts, a good <strong>GP accountant in London</strong> manages each individual's self-assessment and claims the right expenses. Allowable costs include medical indemnity (note that NHS GP clinical negligence is state-indemnified under CNSGP from 1 April 2019, so paid indemnity is now mainly for private and non-clinical work), the <strong>GMC retention fee</strong>, BMA and relevant Royal College subscriptions on HMRC's List 3, CPD, equipment via capital allowances, and business mileage between sites at <strong>55p per mile for the first 10,000 business miles in 2026/27</strong> (the rate rose from 45p on 6 April 2026) and 25p thereafter. Home-to-first-site travel is non-deductible commuting.</p>

<p>On National Insurance, <strong>Class 4 NIC is 6%</strong> on profits between £12,570 and £50,270 and 2% above (2025/26), and <strong>Class 2 is no longer a required payment</strong> from 6 April 2024 for profits at or above the small profits threshold. For expense detail, see our <a href="/blog/gp-tax-deductions-complete-list-2026">complete GP tax deductions list for 2026</a> and <a href="/blog/gp-home-office-expenses-tax-relief">home-office relief</a> guide.</p>

<p><strong>Making Tax Digital for Income Tax</strong> arrives for sole-trader and locum GPs with qualifying income over <strong>£50,000 from 6 April 2026</strong> (then £30,000 from 2027 and £20,000 from 2028). GP partnerships are <strong>deferred with no confirmed date</strong>, and limited companies are outside MTD for ITSA entirely. Most full-time locums and unincorporated private GPs are in scope from April 2026, so a London accountant should be getting you onto compatible software now.</p>

<h2>Private work, VAT and incorporation</h2>

<p>London concentrates private, occupational-health and medico-legal opportunities, and each has a tax twist. On <strong>VAT</strong>, genuine medical care by a registered practitioner is exempt, and NHS contract income is outside the scope, so neither counts toward the threshold. But <strong>cosmetic-only procedures, medico-legal and expert-witness reports, and some occupational-health services</strong> can be standard-rated, and once that taxable (non-exempt) turnover exceeds <strong>£90,000</strong> in a rolling 12 months (the threshold since 1 April 2024) registration is required. See our <a href="/blog/gp-vat-registration">GP VAT registration</a> guide and the wider <a href="/blog/private-practice-tax-nhs-and-private-income">private practice tax</a> explainer.</p>

<p>On <strong>incorporation</strong>, the single most important point is that it is a <strong>private-work decision only</strong>. A limited company cannot hold an NHS GMS or PMS contract, and company income (and the dividends taken from it) is <strong>not NHS-pensionable</strong>. So any tax saving from a company has to be set against lost NHS pension accrual. The 2026/27 dividend rates (ordinary <strong>10.75%</strong>, upper <strong>35.75%</strong>, additional <strong>39.35%</strong> unchanged, allowance <strong>£500</strong>, from 6 April 2026) make the headline saving smaller than it once was. For the trade-off, see our guides on the <a href="/blog/gp-limited-company-tax-benefits-drawbacks">GP limited company decision</a> and <a href="/blog/incorporation-relief-private-medical-practice-s162">incorporation relief on a private practice</a>.</p>

<h2>Selling, retiring or restructuring</h2>

<p>A point London GPs often misunderstand: you <strong>cannot sell the goodwill of an NHS practice</strong>. The prohibition has applied since 1 April 2004 (now under SI 2019/251), and it also blocks selling company shares whose value includes that goodwill. A partner entry or exit is therefore about tangible assets, owned premises and the partnership capital account, plus any genuinely private goodwill. Business Asset Disposal Relief can apply only to a private-practice or private-company disposal (never to NHS goodwill), at <strong>18% for a disposal on or after 6 April 2026</strong> (it was 14% from 6 April 2025 to 5 April 2026, and 10% before that), with a £1m lifetime limit. Our guides on <a href="/blog/can-gp-practice-goodwill-be-sold-nhs-rules">whether GP goodwill can be sold</a> and <a href="/blog/selling-private-medical-practice-cgt-badr">selling a private medical practice</a> cover the detail.</p>

</ul>
