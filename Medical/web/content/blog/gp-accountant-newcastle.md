<p>Finding the right <strong>GP accountant in Newcastle</strong> matters because medical practice finance is genuinely different from ordinary business accounting. Tyneside doctors deal with the NHS Pension Scheme, GP partnership profit shares, surgery premises income and the tapered annual allowance, none of which a generalist firm sees day to day. This page sets out what a specialist medical accountant does for Newcastle GPs and how to choose one, with the key 2026/27 figures you should expect them to know.</p>

<p>It is general information for doctors across Newcastle, Gateshead and the wider North East, not personal advice. Our firm works only with medical professionals, so the framing below reflects how GP and consultant finances actually work.</p>

<h2>Why GP accounting in Newcastle is a specialist job</h2>

<p>A GP partner is self-employed. The partnership files an <strong>SA800</strong> return, each partner's profit share flows to the partnership pages of their personal <strong>SA104</strong>, and crucially a partner is taxed on their <strong>profit share, not their drawings</strong>. A salaried GP is an employee taxed under PAYE. A freelance locum is usually a sole trader filing an SA103. Each route has its own NHS pension treatment and its own tax return, and getting them confused is exactly the kind of error a generalist makes.</p>

<p>On top of that, GP practice income arrives through NHS-specific channels: the <strong>Global Sum</strong> (a per-patient payment weighted by the <strong>Carr-Hill formula</strong>), the <strong>Quality and Outcomes Framework (QOF)</strong>, enhanced services and <strong>Primary Care Network (PCN) and Network Contract DES</strong> funding, including the Additional Roles Reimbursement Scheme. There is no single national per-patient value; the figures are weighted and uplifted annually. A specialist already knows how these flow into partnership profit and out to each partner.</p>

<h2>Services a Newcastle medical accountant provides</h2>

<h3>GP partnership accounts and tax returns</h3>

<p>Newcastle GP partnerships need accurate annual accounts, an <strong>SA800</strong> partnership return, and a clean allocation of each partner's profit share according to the partnership agreement. A specialist handles profit-sharing ratios, prior shares, partner drawings reconciled against true profit, and the partnership capital accounts that drive any partner buy-in or buy-out. For the wider picture, see our <a href="/blog/gp-partnership-tax-complete-guide">GP partnership tax guide</a> and our note on <a href="/blog/gp-partnership-profit-sharing-tax-planning">profit-sharing tax planning</a>.</p>

<h3>NHS pension certification and annual allowance</h3>

<p>This is where a medical accountant earns their place. NHS pension certification is form-specific: a partner (a Type 1 medical practitioner) completes the <strong>Annual Certificate of Pensionable Profits</strong>, a salaried GP (Type 2) completes the <strong>Type 2 self-assessment</strong>, and a freelance locum pensions income through <strong>Locum forms A and B</strong> via PCSE. Get the certificate wrong and the contributions, and ultimately the pension, are wrong.</p>

<p>The bigger planning point for higher-earning Newcastle partners is the <strong>annual allowance</strong>. For 2025/26 it is <strong>£60,000</strong>, tapering where <strong>threshold income exceeds £200,000 and adjusted income exceeds £260,000</strong>, down to a floor of <strong>£10,000</strong>. For the NHS defined-benefit scheme the measure is the <strong>pension input amount</strong> (the capitalised growth in your benefits), not the contributions you pay, which surprises a lot of doctors. Unused allowance can be carried forward from the previous three tax years. Where a charge still arises, <strong>Scheme Pays</strong> can settle it. Our <a href="/blog/nhs-pension-annual-allowance-complete-guide">NHS pension annual allowance guide</a>, the <a href="/blog/nhs-pension-tapered-annual-allowance-calculator">tapered annual allowance calculator</a> and our <a href="/blog/nhs-pension-scheme-pays-doctors-deadlines">Scheme Pays deadlines</a> article go into the mechanics.</p>

<h3>Locum and salaried GP tax</h3>

<p>Newcastle has a large locum and salaried-GP workforce. A freelance locum files self-assessment as a sole trader, claims medical-specific expenses and must keep the NHS pension running through the correct forms. We cover the detail in our guides to <a href="/blog/locum-doctor-tax-complete-guide">locum doctor tax</a>, <a href="/blog/locum-doctor-expenses-what-you-can-claim">locum expenses</a> and <a href="/blog/nhs-pension-for-locums-form-a-form-b">the NHS pension for locums</a>. A salaried GP weighing up partnership can compare the two routes in our <a href="/blog/gp-partner-vs-salaried-gp-tax-comparison">partner versus salaried GP comparison</a>.</p>

<h3>Self-assessment, expenses and NIC</h3>

<p>Allowable expenses for a self-employed Newcastle GP include medical indemnity (where it covers private or non-clinical work, since NHS GP clinical negligence is state-indemnified by CNSGP from 1 April 2019), the <strong>GMC retention fee</strong>, relevant Royal College and BMA subscriptions on HMRC's List 3, CPD, equipment (usually via capital allowances), home-office apportionment and business motoring. The <strong>HMRC approved mileage rate is 55p per mile for the first 10,000 business miles in 2026/27</strong> (it rose from 45p on 6 April 2026) and <strong>25p</strong> thereafter; travel from home to your first site is non-deductible commuting.</p>

<p>On National Insurance, <strong>Class 4 is 6% on profits between £12,570 and £50,270 and 2% above</strong> (2025/26), and <strong>Class 2 is no longer a required payment from 6 April 2024</strong>, so a self-employed GP at or above the small-profits threshold keeps state-pension entitlement without a weekly charge. See our <a href="/blog/gp-tax-return">GP tax return guide</a> and <a href="/blog/gp-tax-deductions-complete-list-2026">2026 deductions list</a> for the full picture.</p>

<h2>Newcastle GP accounting issues that need specialist input</h2>

<h3>Partner entry, exit and succession</h3>

<p>Newcastle practices regularly handle partner retirements, new joiners and mergers. A point worth stating plainly: <strong>NHS GP goodwill cannot be sold</strong>, and has not been since 1 April 2004 (the prohibition now sits in SI 2019/251). So a partner's buy-in or buy-out is about a share of the partnership's <strong>tangible assets, any owned premises and the capital accounts</strong>, not a goodwill payment. The only goodwill a GP can sell is genuine <strong>private</strong> goodwill from non-NHS work. Our article on <a href="/blog/can-gp-practice-goodwill-be-sold-nhs-rules">whether GP practice goodwill can be sold</a> explains the rule, and <a href="/blog/becoming-gp-partner-financial-implications">becoming a GP partner</a> covers the buy-in.</p>

<h3>Surgery premises</h3>

<p>Premises are a bigger feature for GPs than for most professions. They are often held in a separate property partnership or LLP, with income support through <strong>notional rent</strong> (a current-market-rent basis assessed by the District Valuer), legacy <strong>cost rent</strong> or improvement grants. Notional-rent amounts are property-specific, so there is no headline figure to quote. The <strong>last man standing</strong> risk, where a single remaining partner is left holding the whole premises and lease liability, is a real planning point for any shrinking Newcastle partnership. See our guides to <a href="/blog/gp-surgery-notional-rent-vs-cost-rent-explained">notional rent versus cost rent</a>, <a href="/blog/gp-surgery-premises-own-vs-rent-tax-guide">owning versus renting surgery premises</a> and the <a href="/blog/gp-partnership-last-man-standing-premises-risk">last man standing premises risk</a>.</p>

<h3>Mixed NHS and private income</h3>

<p>Many Newcastle GPs supplement NHS work with private income such as occupational health, insurance medicals, medico-legal reports or cosmetic procedures. Allocating expenses between NHS and private work, and getting the VAT treatment right, needs care. Genuine medical care by a registered practitioner is VAT-exempt, but purely cosmetic procedures, medico-legal and expert-witness reports and some occupational-health work are standard-rated and count towards the <strong>£90,000 VAT registration threshold</strong> (deregistration £88,000, in force from 1 April 2024). Only non-exempt turnover counts. Our <a href="/blog/private-practice-tax-nhs-and-private-income">private practice tax guide</a> and <a href="/blog/gp-vat-registration">GP VAT registration</a> article cover this.</p>

<h3>Should you incorporate?</h3>

<p>Incorporation is a <strong>private-work decision only</strong>. A limited company cannot hold an NHS GMS or PMS contract, and company income and dividends are <strong>not NHS-pensionable</strong>, so taking private income as dividends loses NHS accrual entirely. The corporation tax rates are 19% on profits up to £50,000 and 25% above £250,000 with marginal relief between. Dividend tax rises from 6 April 2026: the <strong>ordinary rate to 10.75%</strong> and the <strong>upper rate to 35.75%</strong> (the additional rate stays at 39.35%), with a £500 allowance, which narrows the case further. Any tax saving must always be set against the pension-accrual loss. Read our <a href="/blog/gp-limited-company-tax-benefits-drawbacks">GP limited company guide</a> and <a href="/blog/medical-practice-incorporation-step-by-step">incorporation step by step</a> before deciding.</p>

<h2>Making Tax Digital for Newcastle GPs</h2>

<p>Making Tax Digital for Income Tax begins on <strong>6 April 2026</strong> for sole traders and landlords with qualifying income over <strong>£50,000</strong>, then <strong>£30,000 from April 2027</strong> and <strong>£20,000 from April 2028</strong>. Most full-time locums and unincorporated private GPs in Newcastle will be in scope from April 2026. Limited companies are out, and general partnerships are deferred with no confirmed date, so a GP partnership is not yet mandated at partnership level (though a partner's own private-work income can still bring them in personally). A specialist can get your digital records and software ready in good time.</p>
