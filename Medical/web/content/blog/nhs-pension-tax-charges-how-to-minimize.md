<p>For high-earning doctors, the most expensive line on a tax return often has nothing to do with the work itself. It is the <strong>annual allowance charge</strong> on growth in the NHS Pension Scheme, a tax on a pension you cannot spend yet, frequently running into thousands of pounds. The good news is that, handled in the right order, a large part of it is avoidable or deferrable. This guide sets out the strategy: how to use carry forward, when Scheme Pays earns its place, how to manage the taper, and the traps that quietly cost consultants and GP partners money.</p>

<p>This is a strategy page. If you want the underlying rules and figures, see our <a href="/blog/nhs-pension-annual-allowance-complete-guide">NHS pension annual allowance complete guide</a>; to estimate where the taper puts your own allowance, use the <a href="/blog/nhs-pension-tapered-annual-allowance-calculator">tapered annual allowance calculator</a>. Here we focus on what to actually do about a charge.</p>

<h2>First, work out whether you have a charge at all</h2>

<p>The charge is measured against your <strong>pension input amount</strong>, which is the capitalised growth in your defined-benefit NHS pension over the year, not the contributions taken from your payslip. This catches doctors out: a pay rise, a clinical excellence or merit award, a return to full-time work, or a year of strong CPI-linked revaluation can all push growth well above the cash that left your pay packet.</p>

<p>That growth is then compared with your available allowance:</p>

<ul>
<li>the <strong>standard annual allowance is £60,000</strong> (2025/26);</li>
<li>plus any <strong>unused allowance carried forward</strong> from the previous three tax years;</li>
<li>reduced by the <strong>taper</strong> if you are a high earner (see below).</li>
</ul>

<p>Only the excess above your available allowance is charged, and it is charged at your <strong>marginal income tax rate</strong> (so for an additional-rate taxpayer, up to 45% of the excess). Your NHS pension <a href="/blog/nhs-pension-annual-allowance-complete-guide">pension savings statement</a> from NHSBSA shows the input amount, but it does not factor in your carry forward or your personal taper, so the headline figure on it is rarely the charge you actually owe.</p>

<h2>Step one: use carry forward before anything else</h2>

<p>Carry forward is the single most effective and most overlooked tool. It lets you add <strong>unused annual allowance from the previous three tax years</strong> to the current year, so a one-off spike in growth can often be soaked up entirely with no charge due.</p>

<p>The mechanics matter:</p>

<ul>
<li>You must use the <strong>current year's allowance in full first</strong>, then draw on the three prior years, <strong>earliest year first</strong>.</li>
<li>You must have been a <strong>member of a registered pension scheme</strong> in each year you want to carry forward from (NHS membership counts, even in a year you used none of the allowance).</li>
<li>The tapered allowance reduces the amount available to carry forward from a tapered year, so a string of high-income years leaves less in the bank.</li>
</ul>

<p>As a general illustration, a consultant whose input amount exceeds the current allowance by £30,000, but who has £35,000 of unused allowance banked across the prior three years, would carry forward £30,000 and pay no charge at all. The point is to check carry forward before assuming a charge is payable, and to protect it by not wasting allowance in low-growth years (for example by leaving the scheme unnecessarily).</p>

<h2>Step two: understand and manage the taper</h2>

<p>The <strong>tapered annual allowance</strong> is what turns a manageable position into a large charge for many consultants and GP partners. It bites where, for 2025/26, your <strong>threshold income exceeds £200,000 AND your adjusted income exceeds £260,000</strong>. Above that, your allowance falls by <strong>£1 for every £2 of adjusted income over £260,000</strong>, down to a floor of <strong>£10,000</strong> (reached once adjusted income hits £360,000).</p>

<p>The lever here is the two income measures, because they are defined differently:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Threshold income</strong> broadly excludes pension input but includes most other taxable income. Keeping threshold income at or below £200,000 takes you out of the taper entirely, however high your adjusted income is.</li>
<li><strong>Adjusted income</strong> adds your pension input amount back in, which is why high pension growth can itself push you deeper into the taper.</li>
</ul>

<p>Practical management is about controlling the income that feeds those measures: the timing of private earnings, gift aid and personal pension relief (which reduce threshold income), and decisions about how much non-NHS income to take in your own name versus through a separate structure. A worked, calculator-led approach is far safer than a rule of thumb here, so map your own figures with the <a href="/blog/nhs-pension-tapered-annual-allowance-calculator">tapered annual allowance calculator</a> before acting. Note that since 6 April 2023 the floor has been £10,000 and the entry threshold £260,000 adjusted income; the older £40,000 allowance, £240,000 threshold and £4,000 floor no longer apply.</p>

<h2>Step three: decide whether to use Scheme Pays</h2>

<p>Where a charge remains after carry forward, <strong>Scheme Pays</strong> lets the NHS scheme settle it directly with HMRC, so you do not have to find the cash from current income. It is a deferral, not a discount: the scheme recovers the amount through a <strong>permanent actuarial reduction</strong> in your future pension, set by NHSBSA and GAD factors.</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Mandatory Scheme Pays</strong> is available where the charge <strong>exceeds £2,000</strong> AND your <strong>NHS pension input alone exceeds the standard £60,000</strong> allowance.</li>
<li>A charge driven only by the taper (so your input is below £60,000 but your tapered allowance is lower still) falls outside the mandatory route and is <strong>voluntary Scheme Pays</strong> only, at the scheme's discretion.</li>
<li>The election <strong>deadline is 31 July in the year after the following tax year</strong>, so a 2025/26 charge must be elected by <strong>31 July 2027</strong>. Missing it can leave you funding the charge personally.</li>
</ul>

<p>The decision is a cost-of-capital question: is the actuarial reduction over your remaining lifetime worth more or less than paying the charge now from after-tax income or investments. Because the deadlines and the mandatory-versus-voluntary distinction are easy to get wrong, the detail sits in our dedicated guide to <a href="/blog/nhs-pension-scheme-pays-doctors-deadlines">Scheme Pays deadlines for doctors</a>.</p>

<h2>Other levers, used with care</h2>

<h3>Manage pensionable pay and growth, do not just react to it</h3>

<p>Because the charge follows pension growth, smoothing that growth helps. The timing of a merit award, a partnership profit uplift, or a move from less than full time back to full time all feed the input amount. Spreading a step change across tax years, or pairing it with a year of available carry forward, can keep you under the allowance. This is planning, not avoidance, and it has to be weighed against the value of the extra pension being given up.</p>

<h3>Partial retirement instead of opting out</h3>

<p>If your accrual is consistently generating charges, a blanket opt-out is usually the wrong tool. <strong>Partial (flexible) retirement</strong>, available across all sections since 1 October 2023, lets you draw 20% to 100% of your accrued benefits while continuing to work and re-accrue in the 2015 scheme, provided your pensionable pay or commitment falls by at least 10% for the first 12 months. It is generally a better-understood way to reduce future growth than leaving the scheme outright. See our <a href="/blog/nhs-pension-partial-retirement-doctors-guide">partial retirement guide for doctors</a> before deciding.</p>

<h3>Why opting out is rarely a tax win</h3>

<p>Leaving the NHS Pension Scheme to dodge a charge forfeits guaranteed CARE accrual at 1/54th of pensionable earnings each year, automatic death-in-service cover and ill-health protection. The annual allowance charge is almost always far smaller than the lifetime value of the benefits surrendered. Treat opting out as a last resort, modelled in full, not a quick fix.</p>

<h3>The incorporation trap on private work</h3>

<p>Consultants and GPs with private income sometimes look at a company to keep that income out of pensionable pay and ease the taper. It can do that, but with a hard limit: <strong>company income and dividends are not NHS-pensionable</strong>, and a company <strong>cannot hold an NHS GMS or PMS contract</strong>. So you gain taper headroom on one slice of income while losing pension accrual on it entirely. The dividend rates also rose from 6 April 2026 (ordinary 8.75% to 10.75%, upper 33.75% to 35.75%, additional 39.35% unchanged, allowance £500), which narrows the residual saving. Any incorporation case has to be modelled with the pension-accrual loss alongside the tax saving, never the saving alone. We cover this in <a href="/blog/gp-limited-company-tax-benefits-drawbacks">the limited-company decision for doctors</a> and in <a href="/blog/gp-pension-contributions-tax-relief">GP pension contributions and tax relief</a>.</p>

<h2>The lifetime allowance is gone, but lump-sum caps remain</h2>

<p>The old <strong>lifetime allowance was abolished from 6 April 2024</strong>, so there is no longer a single ceiling on the value of your pension. It is replaced by the <strong>Lump Sum Allowance of £268,275</strong> (the cap on tax-free pension lump sums) and the <strong>Lump Sum and Death Benefit Allowance of £1,073,100</strong>. For most doctors this removes one historic worry, but it does not change the annual allowance position, which is where the live charges now sit. Be wary of older guidance that still treats £1,073,100 as a lifetime allowance; that framing is out of date.</p>

<h2>A note for GP partners and the McCloud remedy</h2>

<p>GP partners have an extra wrinkle: pensionable pay derives from net NHS profit and is certified through the annual Type 1 Certificate of Pensionable Profits, so a profit uplift can drive a higher input amount a year or more after the event. Build that lag into your planning. Separately, if you had NHS service in the remedy period (1 April 2015 to 31 March 2022), the <strong>McCloud remedy</strong> may have changed historic input amounts and produced revised pension savings statements, which can re-open earlier annual allowance positions. Our <a href="/blog/mccloud-remedy-nhs-pension-doctors-explained">McCloud remedy explainer</a> sets out how that reconciliation works.</p>

<h2>Common and costly mistakes</h2>

<ul>
<li>Assuming the figure on the pension savings statement is the charge, without applying carry forward or your personal taper.</li>
<li>Failing to elect Scheme Pays by the 31 July deadline, then funding the charge personally.</li>
<li>Opting out of the scheme to "save tax", forfeiting accrual worth far more than the charge.</li>
<li>Treating Scheme Pays as free money rather than a permanent benefit reduction.</li>
<li>Incorporating private work for a tax saving without pricing in the lost NHS accrual.</li>
<li>Making decisions on one year's numbers instead of a multi-year carry-forward view.</li>
</ul>

<h2>How a specialist medical accountant helps</h2>

<p>The value is in sequencing: confirming the true input amount, applying carry forward correctly across three years, modelling the taper against both income measures, and only then deciding between paying personally and electing Scheme Pays, all set against your retirement timing. Because the NHS rules interact with the wider tax position (private income, incorporation, the taper), getting the order right is where charges are genuinely reduced rather than just shuffled.</p>
