Sheffield's GPs face challenges a general accountant rarely sees, from GP partnership profit-sharing to NHS pension annual allowance charges that need specialist handling.</p>

<h2>Why Sheffield GPs Need a Specialist Medical Accountant</h2>

<p>GP partnerships are taxed quite differently from ordinary businesses. Core NHS funding arrives as the <strong>Global Sum</strong> (weighted by the Carr-Hill formula), topped up by <strong>QOF</strong>, enhanced services and <strong>PCN / Network Contract DES</strong> income, and a partner is taxed on their share of practice profit rather than on drawings. The transition to the tax-year basis from April 2024 added a further layer of timing complexity for partnerships still working through transition profits. A Sheffield <strong>GP accountant</strong> who works regularly with GMS and PMS practices understands how these strands feed your taxable profit. You can read how the funding works in our guide to <a href="/blog/how-gms-funding-works-global-sum-carr-hill-explained">how GMS funding works (Global Sum and Carr-Hill)</a>.</p>

<p>Specialist input is not only about compliance. A GP partner with a higher profit share can easily miss opportunities to manage pension growth, claim every legitimate medical expense, or plan the timing of income across tax years. Those are exactly the areas where a medical-sector accountant earns their keep.</p>

<h2>Core Services from a Sheffield GP Accountant</h2>

<h3>GP Partnership Accounts and Tax Returns</h3>

<p>Sheffield GP partnerships need accounts that accurately reflect the profit-sharing arrangement in the partnership agreement and the way NHS income is recognised. The partnership files an <strong>SA800</strong>, and each partner's share flows to the partnership pages of their own Self Assessment, taxed as trading income with <strong>Class 4 National Insurance at 6%</strong> on profits between £12,570 and £50,270, then 2% above (2025/26). Getting the profit allocation or the expenses split wrong can mean overpaid tax or an avoidable HMRC enquiry. For the wider picture, see our <a href="/blog/gp-partnership-tax-complete-guide">GP partnership tax guide</a> and <a href="/blog/gp-partnership-profit-sharing-tax-planning">GP partnership profit-sharing and tax planning</a>.</p>

<p>Salaried GPs are employees taxed under PAYE, but many also pick up locum sessions or private work on the side, which brings a Self Assessment return into play. Our comparison of the <a href="/blog/gp-partner-vs-salaried-gp-tax-comparison">tax position of a GP partner versus a salaried GP</a> sets out the differences.</p>

<h3>NHS Pension Annual Allowance Management</h3>

<p>Higher-earning Sheffield GPs are the group most likely to meet pension annual allowance problems. The <strong>annual allowance is £60,000 for 2025/26</strong>. It <strong>tapers</strong> where threshold income exceeds £200,000 and adjusted income exceeds £260,000, falling by £1 for every £2 of adjusted income above £260,000, down to a <strong>£10,000</strong> floor. (The £40,000 allowance and £4,000 floor that applied before April 2023 no longer apply.)</p>

<p>The crucial point for a defined-benefit scheme like the NHS Pension Scheme is that the allowance is measured against your <strong>pension input amount</strong> (the capitalised growth in your benefits over the year), not the contributions you pay. A partner can therefore breach the allowance in a year of strong revaluation without contributing a penny more. Where a charge arises, <strong>Scheme Pays</strong> can settle it from the scheme in exchange for a permanent benefit reduction. We cover the detail in our <a href="/blog/nhs-pension-annual-allowance-complete-guide">NHS pension annual allowance guide</a>, the <a href="/blog/nhs-pension-tapered-annual-allowance-calculator">tapered annual allowance explainer</a>, and the <a href="/blog/nhs-pension-scheme-pays-doctors-deadlines">Scheme Pays deadlines guide</a>.</p>

<h3>Locum Doctor Tax and IR35 Status</h3>

<p>Sheffield's teaching hospitals and surgeries create plenty of locum work, and how a locum is structured drives the tax. A <strong>sole-trader locum</strong> has no intermediary, so IR35 does not apply and status is judged on the usual employed-versus-self-employed factors. A locum working through a <strong>personal service company</strong> is within the off-payroll rules: for NHS Trust work the Trust or fee-payer issues the status determination, not the locum, and the same applies to medium or large private hospitals. IR35 has not been abolished, despite what you may read.</p>

<p>We help Sheffield locums choose and run the right structure, keep status documented, and pension their NHS locum income correctly through <a href="/blog/nhs-pension-for-locums-form-a-form-b">Locum forms A and B</a>. See also our guides to <a href="/blog/locum-doctor-ir35-what-you-need-to-know">locum doctor IR35</a> and the <a href="/blog/locum-doctor-limited-company-pros-and-cons">pros and cons of a locum limited company</a>.</p>

<h2>Sheffield-Specific Medical Accounting Considerations</h2>

<h3>Teaching Hospital and Mixed NHS Income</h3>

<p>Sheffield's strong links with Sheffield Teaching Hospitals NHS Foundation Trust mean many local GPs and consultants pick up training payments, clinical supervision and research income alongside their core role. These sources have their own tax treatment and timing, and mixing them up creates avoidable complications. A specialist keeps each stream in the right box.</p>

<h3>Private Practice Alongside NHS Work</h3>

<p>Many Sheffield medical professionals combine NHS work with private practice, whether through Spire Sheffield, the private wing of a local hospital, or independent clinics across the city. <strong>NHS GMS and PMS income is outside the scope of VAT</strong>, and genuine private medical care by a registered practitioner is <strong>VAT-exempt</strong> under the medical-care exemption where the principal purpose is protecting, maintaining or restoring health. The watch-items are purely cosmetic work, medico-legal and expert-witness reports, and some occupational-health services, which can be standard-rated. The <strong>VAT registration threshold is £90,000</strong> of taxable (non-exempt) turnover (deregistration £88,000) from 1 April 2024, so most clinicians never reach it on medical care alone. Our guides to <a href="/blog/private-practice-tax-nhs-and-private-income">tax on combined NHS and private income</a> and <a href="/blog/gp-vat-registration">GP VAT registration</a> go further.</p>

<p>Splitting expenses between NHS and private work needs care. Professional indemnity, the <strong>GMC retention fee</strong>, Royal College and BMA subscriptions on HMRC's approved list, and equipment costs all need a defensible allocation. Note too that NHS GP clinical negligence is covered at no subscription by <strong>CNSGP</strong> from 1 April 2019, so a GP's own paid indemnity is now mainly for private and non-clinical work. For mileage between sites the approved rate is <strong>55p per mile for the first 10,000 business miles in 2026/27</strong> (it rose from 45p on 6 April 2026), then 25p, with home-to-first-site travel treated as non-deductible commuting. See our <a href="/blog/gp-tax-deductions-complete-list-2026">complete list of GP tax deductions</a>.</p>

<h2>Should a Sheffield GP Incorporate?</h2>

<p>Incorporation is a <strong>private-work decision only</strong>. A limited company <strong>cannot hold an NHS GMS or PMS contract</strong>, and any income routed through a company is <strong>not NHS-pensionable</strong>, so private or locum income taken as dividends loses NHS pension accrual entirely. The pure tax saving from incorporating private work is modest at typical profit levels, and the dividend-rate rise from 6 April 2026 (ordinary <strong>10.75%</strong>, upper <strong>35.75%</strong>, additional <strong>39.35%</strong> unchanged, allowance £500, up from 8.75% / 33.75% in 2025/26) narrows it further. Any incorporation comparison must therefore be modelled against the pension-accrual loss, never the tax saving alone. Our guides to the <a href="/blog/gp-limited-company-tax-benefits-drawbacks">benefits and drawbacks of a GP limited company</a> and <a href="/blog/incorporation-relief-private-medical-practice-s162">incorporation relief for a private medical practice</a> set out the trade-offs.</p>

<h2>Practice Sale, Goodwill and Premises</h2>

<p>One rule catches GPs out more than any other. <strong>NHS GP goodwill cannot be sold</strong>, and has not been able to be since 1 April 2004 (now under SI 2019/251). So a GP transaction is about tangible assets, owned premises and partnership <strong>capital accounts</strong>, plus any genuinely private goodwill, not NHS goodwill. <strong>Business Asset Disposal Relief</strong> applies only to a private-practice or private-company disposal, at <strong>18% for disposals on or after 6 April 2026</strong> (it was 14% from 6 April 2025 to 5 April 2026, and 10% before that), with a £1m lifetime limit.</p>

<p>Premises are a bigger feature for GPs than for most professions and are often held in a separate property partnership, with income support through notional rent or legacy cost rent. The "last man standing" risk, where one remaining partner is left holding the whole premises liability, is a real planning point in Sheffield as elsewhere. See <a href="/blog/can-gp-practice-goodwill-be-sold-nhs-rules">whether GP practice goodwill can be sold</a>, our guide to <a href="/blog/gp-surgery-notional-rent-vs-cost-rent-explained">notional rent versus cost rent</a>, and the <a href="/blog/gp-partnership-last-man-standing-premises-risk">last man standing premises risk</a>.</p>

<h2>Making Tax Digital for Sheffield GPs</h2>

<p><strong>Making Tax Digital for Income Tax</strong> mandates digital records and quarterly updates by qualifying income, phased in at <strong>£50,000 from 6 April 2026</strong>, then £30,000 from April 2027 and £20,000 from April 2028. Limited companies are out, and general partnerships are deferred with no confirmed date, so a GP partnership is not yet mandated at partnership level. But a partner or salaried GP with sole-trader private or locum income above the threshold is brought in on their personal return, and most full-time locums and unincorporated private GPs are in scope from April 2026. Getting compatible software and clean records in place early avoids a scramble.</p>
