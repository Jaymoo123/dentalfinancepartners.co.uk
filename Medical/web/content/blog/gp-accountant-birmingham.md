<p>Finding the right <strong>GP accountant in Birmingham</strong> makes a real difference to your financial position as a doctor. Whether you are a GP partner running a practice through a GMS or PMS contract, a salaried GP balancing NHS and private income, a freelance locum, or a consultant with private work alongside your NHS post, you need an accountant who works with medical clients every day, not one who sees a handful a year.</p>

<p>The genuinely tricky areas of GP finance, the NHS Pension Scheme and its annual allowance, partnership profit-share accounting, locum pension certification, premises and the rule that NHS goodwill cannot be sold, are exactly where general high-street accountants tend to struggle. This page sets out the services Birmingham doctors most often need, the 2026/27 figures that matter, and how a specialist works with you. It is general information, not personal advice.</p>

<h2>Who we work with in Birmingham and the Midlands</h2>

<p>Doctors do not all sit in the same tax position, and the right advice depends on your role:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>GP partners</strong> are self-employed. The partnership files an SA800 and each partner's share flows to the partnership pages of their personal return, taxed as trading income with Class 4 National Insurance. Partners are taxed on their <strong>profit share, not their drawings</strong>, which is one of the most common points of confusion we straighten out.</li>
<li><strong>Salaried GPs</strong> are employees, taxed under PAYE with Class 1 National Insurance and active NHS pension membership. Private sessions on top can change the picture.</li>
<li><strong>Freelance locums</strong> are usually sole traders (or, less often, work through a personal service company). See our <a href="/blog/locum-doctor-tax-complete-guide">locum doctor tax guide</a> and, for the IR35 questions, our <a href="/blog/locum-doctor-ir35-what-you-need-to-know">locum IR35 explainer</a>.</li>
<li><strong>Hospital consultants</strong> are NHS employees who may also do private work. Only the NHS employment is pensionable, so private income taken through a company is never NHS-pensionable.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Services Birmingham medical accountants provide</h2>

<h3>GP practice accounts and partnership tax</h3>
<p>Partnership accounts, profit allocation between partners, drawings against anticipated profit trued up at year-end, and capital-account tracking for partner buy-ins and buy-outs. Core practice income comes via the <strong>Global Sum</strong> (weighted by the Carr-Hill formula), <strong>QOF</strong>, <strong>enhanced services</strong> and <strong>PCN / Network Contract DES</strong> funding (including ARRS), with no single national per-patient value. For how the funding stack works, see our <a href="/blog/how-gms-funding-works-global-sum-carr-hill-explained">GMS funding explainer</a> and <a href="/blog/gp-partnership-profit-sharing-tax-planning">partnership profit-sharing guide</a>.</p>

<h3>NHS pension and the annual allowance</h3>
<p>Since <strong>1 April 2022</strong> every active member accrues in the <strong>2015 CARE section</strong> (1/54th of each year's pensionable earnings, revalued at CPI plus 1.5%); the 1995 and 2008 sections remain only as legacy service. The historic change that raised the annual allowance from £40,000 to £60,000 took effect in April 2023, and for <strong>2025/26 the annual allowance is £60,000</strong>. It tapers by £1 for every £2 of adjusted income above <strong>£260,000</strong> (where threshold income also exceeds <strong>£200,000</strong>), down to a <strong>£10,000</strong> floor. For defined-benefit NHS membership the measure is pension growth (the pension input amount), not contributions paid, which is why higher-earning partners and consultants can be caught without realising it. The <strong>lifetime allowance was abolished from 6 April 2024</strong> and replaced by the Lump Sum Allowance (£268,275) and the Lump Sum and Death Benefit Allowance (£1,073,100). Where a charge arises, <strong>Scheme Pays</strong> can settle it. Detail in our <a href="/blog/nhs-pension-annual-allowance-complete-guide">NHS pension annual allowance guide</a>, <a href="/blog/nhs-pension-tapered-annual-allowance-calculator">tapered allowance guide</a> and <a href="/blog/nhs-pension-scheme-pays-doctors-deadlines">Scheme Pays deadlines</a>.</p>

<h3>Personal tax across NHS and private income</h3>
<p>Self-assessment for mixed income streams, allowable expenses and payments on account. For self-employed work, <strong>Class 4 National Insurance is 6%</strong> on profits between £12,570 and £50,270 and 2% above (the old 9% main rate ended on 6 April 2024), and <strong>Class 2 is no longer a required payment</strong> for profits at or above the small-profits threshold. Allowable costs include medical indemnity, the GMC retention fee, relevant Royal College and BMA subscriptions, CPD, and business mileage at <strong>55p per mile for the first 10,000 business miles in 2026/27</strong> (this rate rose from 45p on 6 April 2026) and 25p thereafter. Our <a href="/blog/gp-tax-deductions-complete-list-2026">2026 GP tax deductions list</a> covers what you can claim.</p>

<h3>Limited company and incorporation advice for private work</h3>
<p>Incorporation is a <strong>private-work decision only</strong>: a company cannot hold an NHS GMS or PMS contract, and company income or dividends are <strong>not NHS-pensionable</strong>. So any corporation-tax or dividend saving on private or non-NHS work must be set against the loss of NHS pension accrual. The maths has tightened: from <strong>6 April 2026</strong> dividend tax is <strong>10.75%</strong> ordinary and <strong>35.75%</strong> upper (the additional rate stays 39.35%), with a £500 allowance, up from 8.75% and 33.75% in 2025/26. We model the tax saving and the pension loss together. See our <a href="/blog/gp-limited-company-tax-benefits-drawbacks">GP limited company guide</a> and, where it applies, <a href="/blog/incorporation-relief-private-medical-practice-s162">section 162 incorporation relief</a>.</p>

<h2>Making Tax Digital for Income Tax</h2>

<p>Making Tax Digital for Income Tax (MTD for ITSA) now starts at a <strong>£50,000 qualifying-income threshold from 6 April 2026</strong> (falling to £30,000 from April 2027 and £20,000 from April 2028), tested on the relevant prior year's return. <strong>Limited companies are out</strong> (so a locum trading through a company is not in MTD for ITSA), and <strong>general partnerships are deferred</strong> with no confirmed date, so a GP partnership is not mandated at partnership level yet. Most full-time locums and unincorporated private GPs exceed £50,000 and are in scope from April 2026, so digital record-keeping and quarterly updates need setting up now rather than later.</p>
