Whether you are a GP partner managing a profit share, a salaried GP watching your NHS pension annual allowance, a consultant balancing NHS sessions with private work, or a locum doctor working across Bristol's practices and hospitals, specialist medical accounting makes a real difference to what you keep. A dedicated <strong>GP accountant in Bristol</strong> understands these income streams and the medicine-specific rules that sit behind them, rather than treating a GP like any other self-employed professional.</p>

<h2>Why Bristol Doctors Need a Specialist Medical Accountant</h2>

<p>Doctors carry income that standard accountants rarely meet. GP partners receive a profit share from GMS or PMS contract funding (the Global Sum weighted by the Carr-Hill formula, plus QOF, enhanced services and Network Contract DES funding), not a salary, and they are taxed on that profit share rather than on their drawings. Salaried GPs are employees taxed under PAYE. Consultants combine NHS employment with private practice. Locum doctors must get their employment status right.</p>

<p>A specialist <strong>GP accountant in Bristol</strong> handles all of this in one place: GMS and PMS contract changes feeding into partnership accounts, the NHS pension annual allowance, and the structuring of private income. We do not use dental concepts here. There are no UDAs or treatment bands in general practice, and, critically, NHS GP goodwill cannot be sold, so a Bristol practice transaction is about tangible assets, premises and capital accounts, never a goodwill sale.</p>

<h2>NHS Pension Annual Allowance for Bristol GPs and Consultants</h2>

<p>The NHS pension annual allowance is the single issue that catches most successful Bristol doctors. For 2025/26 the standard annual allowance is <strong>£60,000</strong>. It tapers where your <strong>threshold income exceeds £200,000 and your adjusted income exceeds £260,000</strong>, reducing by £1 for every £2 of adjusted income above £260,000, down to a floor of <strong>£10,000</strong>. The money purchase annual allowance, relevant where you flexibly access a defined-contribution AVC, is also <strong>£10,000</strong>.</p>

<p>For a defined-benefit scheme like the NHS scheme, the measure is your <strong>pension input amount</strong>, the capitalised growth in your benefits over the year, not the contributions deducted from your pay. That distinction surprises many doctors, because a good pay rise or a step up to partner can generate input well above what landed in your bank account. Unused allowance can be carried forward from the previous three tax years, current year first.</p>

<p>The standard allowance rose from £40,000 to £60,000 in April 2023, and the old lifetime allowance was abolished from 6 April 2024, replaced by the Lump Sum Allowance of <strong>£268,275</strong> and the Lump Sum and Death Benefit Allowance of <strong>£1,073,100</strong>. If you are a Bristol consultant at Southmead taking extra private sessions, or a partner whose profit share has climbed, a specialist review of your pension input against these limits is where the planning lives. For the full mechanics see our <a href="/blog/nhs-pension-annual-allowance-complete-guide">NHS pension annual allowance guide</a> and, where a charge cannot be avoided, our explainer on <a href="/blog/nhs-pension-scheme-pays-doctors-deadlines">Scheme Pays and its deadlines</a>.</p>

<p>Where a charge does arise, <strong>Mandatory Scheme Pays</strong> lets the NHS scheme settle it for a permanent reduction in benefits, available where the charge exceeds £2,000 and your NHS pension input alone exceeds the £60,000 standard allowance. The election deadline is 31 July in the year after the charge crystallised, so a 2025/26 charge must be elected by 31 July 2027.</p>

<h2>GP Partnership Accounts and Profit Sharing in Bristol</h2>

<p>Bristol GP partnerships need accurate profit allocation and clean partnership accounts. The partnership files an SA800, and each partner's share flows to the partnership pages of their own Self Assessment return, taxed as trading income with Class 4 National Insurance. Remember that partners are taxed on their profit share, not their drawings, so year-end truing up of drawings against actual profit matters.</p>

<p>Partners with different income levels face different annual allowance positions and different marginal tax rates, so allocation timing and individual planning go hand in hand. A specialist also handles the NHS pension certification correctly: a GP partner completes the Type 1 <strong>Annual Certificate of Pensionable Profits</strong> each year via PCSE, while a salaried GP completes the Type 2 self-assessment. For the bigger picture on partner taxation, see our <a href="/blog/gp-partnership-tax-complete-guide">GP partnership tax guide</a> and, if you are weighing the move, <a href="/blog/gp-partner-vs-salaried-gp-tax-comparison">GP partner versus salaried GP</a>.</p>

<h2>GP Surgery Premises and Practice Transactions in Bristol</h2>

<p>Premises are a bigger feature for GPs than for most professions, and Bristol's mix of converted period buildings and purpose-built surgeries makes this live. Owned premises are often held in a separate property partnership or LLP outside the medical partnership, with income support coming through <strong>notional rent</strong> (an owner-occupier basis assessed by the District Valuer at current market rent) or the legacy <strong>cost rent</strong> scheme. These amounts are property-specific and District-Valuer-assessed, so there is no standard figure to quote.</p>

<p>Two planning points recur. First, the <strong>"last man standing" risk</strong>, where a single remaining partner is left holding the whole premises liability and lease, needs managing before it becomes acute. Second, because <strong>NHS GP goodwill cannot be sold</strong> (prohibited since 1 April 2004, currently under SI 2019/251), a Bristol partner's entry or exit is priced on a share of tangible assets and the partnership capital accounts, plus any genuinely private goodwill, not on an NHS goodwill multiple. We cover this in detail in <a href="/blog/can-gp-practice-goodwill-be-sold-nhs-rules">whether GP practice goodwill can be sold</a> and <a href="/blog/gp-surgery-notional-rent-vs-cost-rent-explained">notional rent versus cost rent</a>.</p>

<h2>Locum Doctor Tax and IR35 in Bristol</h2>

<p>Bristol's hospitals and practices use locum doctors heavily, which raises specific status and IR35 questions. A sole-trader locum filing the SA103 has no intermediary, so IR35 does not apply and status is judged on the employed-versus-self-employed factors instead. A locum working through a <strong>personal service company</strong> is within the off-payroll framework, and who decides status depends on the hirer:</p>

<ul>
<li>For <strong>NHS Trust</strong> and other public-sector work, the Trust (or the agency as fee-payer in a chain) determines status and operates PAYE and NIC on inside-IR35 engagements. The Status Determination sits with the Trust, not with you, though you can challenge it through the client-led disagreement process.</li>
<li>For <strong>medium or large private hospitals and clinics</strong>, the hirer determines status and issues a Status Determination Statement with reasons.</li>
<li>For <strong>small private clients</strong>, the old rule survives and the personal service company itself decides its IR35 status.</li>
</ul>

<p>IR35 has not been abolished and the off-payroll rules have not been repealed, so getting determinations right across multiple Bristol hirers (you can hold a mix of inside and outside engagements) is part of the job. For more, see <a href="/blog/locum-doctor-ir35-what-you-need-to-know">locum doctor IR35</a> and the broader <a href="/blog/locum-doctor-tax-complete-guide">locum doctor tax guide</a>.</p>

<h2>Private Practice and Incorporation for Bristol Doctors</h2>

<p>Many Bristol consultants and GPs combine NHS work with private practice, whether through Spire Bristol Hospital, Nuffield Health Bristol or independent consulting rooms. Mixed income needs careful structuring, and incorporation is sometimes raised as a saving. The key point a specialist will make plainly: <strong>incorporation is a private-work decision only</strong>. A limited company cannot hold an NHS GMS or PMS contract, and income routed through a company is <strong>not NHS-pensionable</strong>, so private or locum income taken as dividends loses NHS pension accrual entirely. For a consultant, only the NHS employment is pensionable; private work never is.</p>

<p>On the tax side, for 2026/27 the dividend rates rose from 6 April 2026 to <strong>10.75% ordinary, 35.75% upper and 39.35% additional</strong> (the additional rate is unchanged), with the dividend allowance at <strong>£500</strong>. Corporation tax is 19% on profits up to £50,000, 25% above £250,000, with marginal relief between. At typical private-income levels the pure tax saving from incorporating is modest, and the 2026/27 dividend-rate rise narrows it further, so any saving must always be weighed against the NHS pension accrual you would give up. We set out both sides in <a href="/blog/medical-practice-incorporation-step-by-step">medical practice incorporation step by step</a> and <a href="/blog/gp-limited-company-tax-benefits-drawbacks">GP limited company benefits and drawbacks</a>.</p>

<h2>Expenses, VAT, NIC and MTD for Bristol Doctors</h2>

<p>Day-to-day compliance still has medicine-specific edges that a general accountant can miss:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Expenses and mileage:</strong> medical indemnity (MDU, MPS or MDDUS), the GMC retention fee, relevant Royal College and specialty fees, BMA subscription on HMRC's List 3, CPD and accountancy fees are deductible. HMRC business mileage rose to <strong>55p per mile for the first 10,000 business miles in 2026/27 (up from 45p on 6 April 2026), then 25p</strong>. Note CNSGP has provided state indemnity for NHS GP clinical negligence in England since 1 April 2019, so a GP's own indemnity is now mainly for private, non-clinical and regulatory cover. See our <a href="/blog/gp-tax-deductions-complete-list-2026">complete GP tax deductions list for 2026</a>.</li>
<li><strong>National Insurance:</strong> for the self-employed, Class 4 is <strong>6% on profits between £12,570 and £50,270, then 2%</strong>, and Class 2 has not been a required payment since 6 April 2024 (your record is protected automatically where profits reach the Small Profits Threshold), so there is no weekly Class 2 charge to budget for.</li>
<li><strong>VAT:</strong> genuine medical care by a registered practitioner is VAT-exempt, and NHS contract income is outside the scope of VAT, so neither counts towards registration. The registration threshold is <strong>£90,000</strong> of taxable (non-exempt) turnover (deregistration £88,000), and it is the taxable slice (cosmetic-only work, medico-legal and expert-witness reports) that can push a Bristol doctor over. See our <a href="/blog/gp-vat-registration">GP VAT registration guide</a>.</li>
<li><strong>Making Tax Digital:</strong> MTD for Income Tax started on 6 April 2026 for sole traders and landlords with qualifying income over <strong>£50,000</strong>, so most full-time Bristol locums and unincorporated private GPs are in scope now. Limited companies are out and general partnerships are deferred.</li>
</ul>
