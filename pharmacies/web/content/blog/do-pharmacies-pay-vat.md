<h2 id="do-pharmacies-pay-vat-short-answer">Do pharmacies pay VAT? The short answer</h2>

<p>Yes, almost every community pharmacy is VAT-registered. But the more useful answer is that a pharmacy does not have a single VAT status the way a pure retailer does. Its supplies are mixed: <a href="https://www.gov.uk/guidance/health-professionals-pharmaceutical-products-and-vat-notice-70157">NHS-dispensed prescription drugs are zero-rated, while most over-the-counter retail sales are standard-rated at 20%</a>. That mix is what defines the pharmacy's VAT profile and why it behaves differently from almost any other business on the high street.</p>

<p>The practical result is that a pharmacy is a <strong>VAT-mixed business</strong>: part of its income carries 20% VAT, part carries 0%, and the firm usually reclaims more input VAT than a pure retailer would expect. Getting the split right, and choosing the correct retail scheme to reflect it, is one of the most pharmacy-specific accounting challenges there is.</p>

<h2 id="why-pharmacy-is-vat-mixed">Why a pharmacy is a VAT-mixed business</h2>

<p>Most businesses have a straightforward VAT position: they charge VAT on everything they sell (standard-rated) or they do not charge it at all. A pharmacy sits in neither camp cleanly. It operates two distinct supply types at the same till:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Dispensing against NHS prescriptions</strong> (the dispensary side): zero-rated under <a href="https://www.gov.uk/guidance/health-professionals-pharmaceutical-products-and-vat-notice-70157">VAT Notice 701/57</a>.</li>
  <li><strong>Over-the-counter medicines, toiletries, cosmetics, and general retail</strong> (the shop side): standard-rated at 20%.</li>
</ul>

<p>These two revenue streams sit within the same four walls, share the same overheads, and are submitted on the same VAT return. The challenge for a pharmacy's accounts is separating them accurately so that VAT is correctly accounted for on each side of the business.</p>

<h2 id="what-is-zero-rated-dispensing">What is zero-rated: dispensing against an NHS prescription</h2>

<p>Under <a href="https://www.gov.uk/guidance/health-professionals-pharmaceutical-products-and-vat-notice-70157">HMRC's VAT Notice 701/57</a>, the dispensing of medicines by a registered pharmacist against a valid prescription is zero-rated for VAT. This applies to the NHS dispensing side of the business: the supply to the patient carries no VAT charge.</p>

<p>Zero-rated means the supply is taxable at 0%, not that it falls outside VAT altogether. That distinction matters enormously for what happens with input VAT on costs, which is covered below.</p>

<p>Note that pharmacist private services, including some private consultations and clinic services, can be either exempt or standard-rated rather than zero-rated, depending on the nature of the supply. That is the territory of the <a href="/blog/vat-and-retail-schemes/vat-on-private-services-pharmacy-first">VAT on private services and Pharmacy First income</a> post. The focus here is the NHS dispensing and OTC retail split.</p>

<h2 id="what-is-standard-rated-otc">What is standard-rated: over-the-counter medicines and general retail</h2>

<p>Most products sold from the pharmacy's retail shelves are standard-rated at 20%. This includes:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Over-the-counter medicines (painkillers, antihistamines, cold remedies) sold without a prescription</li>
  <li>Toiletries, cosmetics, and personal-care products</li>
  <li>Supplements, vitamins, and health foods in most cases</li>
  <li>General retail lines: stationery, photographic supplies, seasonal gifts</li>
</ul>

<p>The standard rating applies to these lines regardless of whether the buyer is a patient of the pharmacy. The classification follows the VAT treatment of the product, not the identity of the buyer.</p>

<p>The result is that a pharmacy with a busy retail section charges and collects 20% output VAT on a substantial part of its turnover, while its dispensing income carries none. Both streams appear on the same VAT return, and a retail scheme is almost always needed to split the takings correctly.</p>

<h2 id="zero-rated-not-exempt">Are pharmacies VAT exempt? No, and why that word matters</h2>

<p>The phrase "pharmacies are VAT exempt" is the most common and most expensive misconception in this area. It is wrong, and the difference between zero-rated and exempt has real cash consequences.</p>

<p>If NHS dispensing were exempt, a pharmacy would be blocked from reclaiming the input VAT on the costs attributable to it: fit-out, dispensary equipment, stock, overheads. Because dispensing is zero-rated instead, those reclaims remain available. That is a structurally better position, and it applies to almost every community pharmacy in England.</p>

<p>A pharmacy is a taxable business making a mix of taxable supplies (zero-rated and standard-rated). It is not VAT-free, non-taxable, or exempt.</p>

<h2 id="registration-threshold-red-herring">The registration threshold is a red herring for pharmacies</h2>

<p>The <a href="https://www.gov.uk/vat-registration">VAT registration threshold is £90,000</a> of taxable turnover in any rolling 12-month period. For most small businesses, that threshold drives the registration decision. For a pharmacy, it is largely irrelevant.</p>

<p>A community pharmacy with an active NHS contract will almost always exceed £90,000 in taxable turnover (zero-rated dispensing income counts as taxable turnover for registration purposes) before it has traded for any meaningful period. Registration is typically compulsory from the outset or very close to it.</p>

<p>More important is what happens when a pharmacy that is below the threshold considers voluntary registration. Because dispensing income is zero-rated, voluntary registration is normally advantageous: the pharmacy gains the right to reclaim input VAT on its purchases and overheads without being required to charge output VAT on its zero-rated dispensing income. That is an asymmetry that works in the pharmacy's favour.</p>

<h2 id="why-voluntary-registration-usually-helps">Why voluntary registration usually helps a pharmacy</h2>

<p>For most businesses, the trade-off on voluntary VAT registration is straightforward: you gain input VAT reclaims but take on the administrative burden and the obligation to charge output VAT on your sales, which can affect pricing. For a pharmacy, the calculation looks different.</p>

<p>Because NHS dispensing is zero-rated, the pharmacy:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Pays no output VAT on a large part of its income (the dispensing side)</li>
  <li>Still reclaims input VAT on costs attributable to that zero-rated dispensing</li>
  <li>Collects and accounts for 20% output VAT on OTC retail (as it would have to once above the threshold anyway)</li>
</ul>

<p>The net effect is that a pharmacy in VAT recovery usually recovers more from HMRC per period than a comparable purely standard-rated retailer, because its input VAT base is not matched against zero-rated dispensing output VAT (there is none to offset). The exact recovery depends on how costs are split between the zero-rated and standard-rated activities, but the structural direction holds for most community pharmacies.</p>

<p>Voluntary registration also avoids the awkward position of crossing the threshold mid-year and having to account retrospectively for VAT on OTC sales already made. Registering from the outset is nearly always cleaner for a pharmacy planning its accounts.</p>

<h2 id="getting-the-mix-right">Getting the mix right: retail schemes and the VAT service</h2>

<p>Identifying that a pharmacy is VAT-mixed is the start, not the finish. The practical work is splitting the zero-rated and standard-rated takings accurately on every VAT return. A pharmacy typically cannot itemise every individual sale at point of transaction, so <a href="https://www.gov.uk/hmrc-internal-manuals/vat-retail-schemes">HMRC's retail schemes</a> provide approved methods for apportioning total takings between the two rates.</p>

<p>Choosing the wrong retail scheme, or applying the right one incorrectly, systematically overpays VAT. Where private pharmacist services are also in scope, a partial-exemption calculation may be needed on top. And the FP34 submission cycle means pharmacy income and VAT periods do not always align in the way a standard-rated retailer's would.</p>
