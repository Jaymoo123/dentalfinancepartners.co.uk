<section class="bluf-box" aria-label="Quick answer">
<h2>What is the Drug Tariff? The short answer</h2>
<p>The <strong>Drug Tariff</strong> is the monthly schedule published by <a href="https://www.nhsbsa.nhs.uk/pharmacies-gp-practices-and-appliance-contractors/drug-tariff" rel="noopener noreferrer">NHS Business Services Authority (NHSBSA)</a> that sets what NHS England reimburses community pharmacies for dispensing prescriptions, plus the fees, allowances and rules that apply to those payments. It sits within the <a href="https://www.england.nhs.uk/community-pharmacy-contractual-framework/" rel="noopener noreferrer">Community Pharmacy Contractual Framework (CPCF)</a>, which is the overarching contract between NHS England and pharmacy contractors. Pharmacy income is contract-driven, not till-driven: it flows from the tariff and the CPCF, not from shop turnover.</p>
<p>This guide covers the mechanism. For live reimbursement prices, always use the <a href="https://www.nhsbsa.nhs.uk/pharmacies-gp-practices-and-appliance-contractors/drug-tariff" rel="noopener noreferrer">NHSBSA Drug Tariff portal</a> directly. The "latest changes" section below carries the most recent update and its as-at date.</p>
</section>

<h2>Reimbursement vs remuneration: the two halves of pharmacy income</h2>
<p>One of the most important distinctions in understanding how the Drug Tariff works is the split between <strong>reimbursement</strong> and <strong>remuneration</strong>. Both live within the Drug Tariff framework and the CPCF, but they cover entirely different things.</p>

<p>Both are published as part of the Drug Tariff document, but they flow from different economic decisions. Reimbursement tries to track the cost of the drug. Remuneration recognises the labour, dispensing risk, and service value the pharmacy provides. Understanding which half is moving when you see income change is the first step in margin analysis.</p>

<section>
<h2>How a Drug Tariff change reaches your margin</h2>
<p>When NHSBSA revises a reimbursement price in the monthly tariff, the effect on a pharmacy's dispensing margin is direct and immediate from the following month's payment. Understanding the mechanism matters more than any specific figure, because the direction of the change (up or down) and which category it falls in determines how your income is affected.</p>

<p>The core dynamic, sourced to <a href="https://www.nhsbsa.nhs.uk/pharmacies-gp-practices-and-appliance-contractors/drug-tariff" rel="noopener noreferrer">HP 8 (NHSBSA Drug Tariff)</a>, is this: <strong>gross margin on dispensing is set centrally and retrospectively adjusted</strong>. You buy the drug from a wholesaler at a market price. You dispense it. NHSBSA then reimburses you at the Drug Tariff price for that month. The spread between those two figures is your gross margin on that item, and it can change each month without any action on your part.</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Tariff price rises above what you paid:</strong> margin on that item improves.</li>
  <li><strong>Tariff price falls below what you paid:</strong> margin on that item is compressed or turns negative.</li>
  <li><strong>Concession granted:</strong> a temporary uplift to the tariff price for a short-supply drug, partially protecting margin during a supply disruption. The concession expires; you must check each month whether it is still active.</li>
</ul>

<p>This mechanism is why pharmacies cannot manage dispensing profitability with ordinary retail margin logic. The "selling price" (reimbursement) is fixed by NHSBSA, not by the pharmacy. The "cost price" (wholesaler price) is determined by supply and demand. Margin is the residual, and it moves monthly. For a detailed look at how Category M clawbacks work within this mechanism, see the related post on <a href="/blog/nhs-contract-and-income/category-m-clawbacks-explained">Category M clawbacks explained</a>.</p>
</section>

<section>
<h2>Concessions, broken bulk, and the volatile edges of the tariff</h2>
<p>Two provisions at the edges of the Drug Tariff generate regular financial questions from dispensing pharmacies.</p>

<h3>Concessions</h3>
<p>When a medicine is in short supply and pharmacies cannot source it at anywhere near the normal tariff reimbursement price, NHSBSA can grant a <strong>concession price</strong>: a higher reimbursement price for that drug for that month only. Concessions are published monthly alongside the main tariff update. They are not automatic; a pharmacy needs to be aware of which drugs have a concession for the month and, in some cases, must meet certain conditions. They expire at month-end unless renewed. NHSBSA is the only source; check the <a href="https://www.nhsbsa.nhs.uk/pharmacies-gp-practices-and-appliance-contractors/drug-tariff" rel="noopener noreferrer">current edition</a> at the start of each month.</p>

<h3>Broken bulk</h3>
<p>Where a drug is only available in pack sizes larger than the prescribed quantity, a pharmacy may have to open a full pack to dispense part of it, leaving a partial pack that cannot easily be used. The Drug Tariff includes provisions to allow reimbursement of the full pack in qualifying circumstances, recognising the economic cost of broken packs. The conditions are set out in the tariff rules and are subject to change; the applicable edition at the time of dispensing governs.</p>

<p>Both provisions illustrate the broader point: the Drug Tariff is a live, operational document that requires active monthly monitoring, not a static reference. Financial planning that assumes tariff prices and concession states are stable will underestimate income volatility.</p>

<h2>How to check the current Drug Tariff</h2>
<p>The authoritative and only binding source is the <a href="https://www.nhsbsa.nhs.uk/pharmacies-gp-practices-and-appliance-contractors/drug-tariff" rel="noopener noreferrer">NHSBSA Drug Tariff portal</a>. It provides:</p>
<ul>
  <li>The current monthly edition as a downloadable document</li>
  <li>A searchable online price look-up tool for individual drug lines</li>
  <li>The current month's concession list</li>
  <li>Archived previous editions for retrospective checking</li>
</ul>
<p>Third-party reposts and PMR system price feeds may lag the official edition or carry errors. For any financial decision, dispensing claim, or margin calculation, use the NHSBSA portal as the primary source and treat other feeds as convenience tools only.</p>

<section>
<h2>Understanding the tariff is half of managing margin</h2>
<p>Reading the Drug Tariff tells you what NHSBSA will reimburse you. It does not tell you how that compares to what you are actually spending to dispense, how your margin stacks up against comparable pharmacies, or where the largest variances in your model sit.</p>
<p>That second half, measuring your actual margin against the tariff, requires benchmarking: comparing your dispensing costs, purchase prices and reimbursement receipts against sector norms. Pharmacies that understand their tariff position but have not benchmarked their buying costs regularly find margin leakage that the tariff alone cannot explain. If understanding where your dispensing margin sits relative to the sector would help, the <a href="/services/pharmacy-benchmarking-margin">pharmacy benchmarking and margin review service</a> covers that analysis.</p>
