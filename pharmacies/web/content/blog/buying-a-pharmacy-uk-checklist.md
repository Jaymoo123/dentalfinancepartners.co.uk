<p>When a listing appears for a community pharmacy, the instinct is to look at the shop, the location and the headline turnover. Those are the wrong starting points. The asset you are acquiring is the NHS pharmaceutical contract that entitles the pharmacy to dispense NHS prescriptions and receive reimbursement under the Drug Tariff. Without that contract, the premises are a retail unit. This checklist works through the regulated, financial and structural steps a buyer needs to navigate, in the order they matter.</p>

<p><em>This guide covers England. Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland operate under different contractual frameworks; NHS contract content here reflects the England Community Pharmacy Contractual Framework (CPCF).</em></p>

<h2 id="the-nhs-contract-is-the-asset">The short answer: the NHS contract is the asset and market entry is regulated</h2>

<p>A community pharmacy's income is reimbursement plus remuneration under the <a href="https://www.england.nhs.uk/community-pharmacy-contractual-framework/">Community Pharmacy Contractual Framework</a>, not shop takings. The value follows the contract. Under the <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2013/349/contents">NHS (Pharmaceutical and Local Pharmaceutical Services) Regulations 2013</a>, opening, relocating or buying a pharmacy requires satisfying a regulated market-entry process. A change of ownership does not automatically preserve or transfer the contract: the incoming owner must comply with the Regulations and meet registration requirements before dispensing can continue under the new ownership.</p>

<p>This single fact separates pharmacy acquisition from buying most other businesses, and it is the point most general business brokers understate.</p>

<h2 id="what-you-are-actually-buying">What you are actually buying: the contract, item volume and goodwill</h2>

<p>The three components that drive the value of a community pharmacy are:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>The NHS contract.</strong> The right to dispense NHS prescriptions and receive NHSBSA reimbursement, governed by the <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2013/349/contents">2013 Regulations</a>. Without it, the business is a retail shop with a dispensary fit-out.</li>
  <li><strong>Item volume.</strong> The number of prescription items dispensed per month is the primary driver of dispensing income and, in turn, of goodwill value. A buyer paying a premium is paying for a contracted, recurring income stream measured in items per month.</li>
  <li><strong>Goodwill.</strong> In a pharmacy, goodwill sits on top of the tangible assets and is almost entirely attributable to the contract and item volume. <a href="/blog/selling-a-pharmacy/pharmacy-goodwill-what-its-worth">What pharmacy goodwill is worth</a> is explored in detail in a sibling post; this checklist covers the tax treatment at acquisition and its due-diligence implications.</li>
</ul>

<p>Shop fittings, dispensary robots, refrigeration and signage are tangible fixed assets. They are a small fraction of the total consideration in most pharmacy transactions. Overweighting them in valuation is a first-time-buyer error.</p>

<h2 id="regulated-market-entry">Regulated market entry: the NHS (Pharmaceutical and LPS) Regulations 2013</h2>

<p>The <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2013/349/contents">NHS (Pharmaceutical and Local Pharmaceutical Services) Regulations 2013</a> set the framework for market entry in England. The key principle is that the pharmaceutical needs of a neighbourhood are assessed by NHS England and pharmacy provision is controlled accordingly. This means:</p>

<ul>
  <li>A new pharmacy cannot simply open anywhere; it must either fit a market-entry category (for example, a 100-hours pharmacy or a distance-selling pharmacy) or be an existing contracted pharmacy changing hands.</li>
  <li>A change of ownership of an existing pharmacy requires the incoming owner to notify the NHS and satisfy the Regulations' conditions. The outgoing owner's contract does not automatically vest in the buyer on completion.</li>
  <li>Relocating a pharmacy after purchase also engages a separate process under the Regulations and is not guaranteed to be approved.</li>
</ul>

<p>Legal and regulatory advice on the change-of-ownership notification process sits with a specialist healthcare solicitor. From the accounting side, the key implication is that the transaction timetable must allow for the notification and approval process, and no income from the NHS should be assumed until the ownership change is formally registered. The FP34 payment cycle (monthly submission, payment roughly two months later under the <a href="https://www.nhsbsa.nhs.uk/pharmacies-gp-practices-and-appliance-contractors/submitting-prescriptions">NHSBSA payment process</a>) means a delayed approval has a working-capital cost that must be modelled.</p>

<h2 id="gphc-registration">GPhC registration and the superintendent pharmacist requirement</h2>

<p>Every pharmacy premises must be registered with the <a href="https://www.pharmacyregulation.org/pharmacies">General Pharmaceutical Council (GPhC)</a>. When ownership changes, the registration must reflect the new owner. For companies (as opposed to individual pharmacist-owners), there is an additional requirement: the company must appoint a GPhC-registered superintendent pharmacist.</p>

<p>The superintendent pharmacist takes named responsibility for the pharmacy's pharmaceutical services. This is an ownership and registration mechanics requirement; this post does not cover the professional or clinical dimensions of the role, which sit with the GPhC and the Royal Pharmaceutical Society.</p>

<p>Practical implications for a buyer:</p>

<ul>
  <li>If buying through a company, the superintendent pharmacist must be identified and confirmed before or at completion, not after.</li>
  <li>If the selling pharmacist-owner was also the superintendent and they are leaving the business, a gap in superintendent cover is a regulatory problem, not just an operational one.</li>
  <li>GPhC registration of the premises under the new ownership is part of the completion checklist, not a post-completion tidying exercise.</li>
</ul>

<h2 id="goodwill">Goodwill in the price and why it dominates</h2>

<p>Goodwill in a community pharmacy acquisition is not the same as goodwill in a café or a solicitors' practice. It is driven almost entirely by the NHS contract and monthly item volume. A buyer paying a significant sum for a pharmacy is paying for a contracted, NHSBSA-reimbursed dispensing income stream that has barriers to entry written into statute.</p>

<p>Two tax points are load-bearing:</p>

<ol>
  <li><strong>Corporation tax relief on goodwill is restricted on a company purchase.</strong> Under <a href="https://www.gov.uk/guidance/corporation-tax-relief-on-goodwill-and-relevant-assets">HMRC's goodwill and relevant assets rules</a>, relief is only available in limited cases at fixed rates when goodwill is acquired as part of a business purchased through a company. A buyer expecting to amortise goodwill against corporation tax profits in the normal way may be disappointed. Structuring decisions (asset vs share deal; individual vs company buyer) affect whether any relief is available at all.</li>
  <li><strong>No specific multiple or benchmark is stated here.</strong> Pharmacy goodwill does trade on recognisable methods (adjusted EBITDA multiples and pence-per-item benchmarks), but the actual figure in any transaction depends on the specific pharmacy's item volume, contract type, location and trading history. Valuation stays at method level until a cited, sourced figure is in your hands from a specialist pharmacy broker.</li>
</ol>

<h2 id="due-diligence">Due diligence: the items a pharmacy buyer cannot skip</h2>

<p>General business due diligence (audited accounts, lease, staff, litigation) applies, but a pharmacy has several layers that a generalist accountant will not know to pull.</p>

<h3 id="dd-fp34">FP34 and NHSBSA payment history</h3>

<p>Prescriptions are submitted to the NHSBSA monthly on the FP34 schedule and payment arrives roughly two months later, with an advance on account (see the <a href="https://www.nhsbsa.nhs.uk/pharmacies-gp-practices-and-appliance-contractors/submitting-prescriptions">NHSBSA submission and payment process</a>). Due diligence must include:</p>

<ul>
  <li>At least 24 months of FP34 submissions and corresponding NHSBSA payment remittances, to verify item volumes and reconcile against the accounts.</li>
  <li>Analysis of item volume trends: is the contract growing, flat or declining, and why?</li>
  <li>The advance-on-account balance at the point of sale, which is a working-capital item on completion.</li>
</ul>

<h3 id="dd-drug-tariff">Drug Tariff and Category M margin story</h3>

<p>Gross margin on NHS dispensing is not stable. The <a href="https://www.nhsbsa.nhs.uk/pharmacies-gp-practices-and-appliance-contractors/drug-tariff">Drug Tariff</a> sets reimbursement prices and Category M clawbacks adjust the margin retrospectively to reflect actual purchase prices in the market. Due diligence should establish:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Whether the reported gross margin reflects actual post-clawback income or a pre-clawback figure that flatters the profit picture.</li>
  <li>Whether any Category M clawback liabilities were outstanding at the accounts date.</li>
  <li>The pharmacy's Drug Tariff category and whether any imminent Drug Tariff changes affect the forward margin profile.</li>
</ul>

<h3 id="dd-vat">VAT mix</h3>

<p>A community pharmacy is a <a href="https://www.gov.uk/guidance/health-professionals-pharmaceutical-products-and-vat-notice-70157">VAT-mixed business</a>: NHS-dispensed prescription drugs are zero-rated, most OTC retail is standard-rated, and some private services may be exempt or standard-rated depending on the supply. This means:</p>

<ul>
  <li>The pharmacy almost certainly reclaims more input VAT than a pure retailer, but only if the retail scheme and partial-exemption position are correctly set up.</li>
  <li>Due diligence should confirm the VAT retail scheme in use, verify that it is appropriate for the actual sales mix, and check for any VAT assessments or disputes in the last four years.</li>
  <li>The VAT position is a day-one accounting task for the incoming owner; inheriting a mis-applied retail scheme creates a retrospective liability risk.</li>
</ul>

<h3 id="dd-lease-tupe">Lease, premises and TUPE</h3>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Lease:</strong> Confirm the lease term, rent review mechanism, break clauses and, critically, whether the landlord's consent is required for an assignment or share sale. A lease that cannot be assigned kills an asset deal.</li>
  <li><strong>TUPE:</strong> Staff employed in the pharmacy transfer to the buyer under the Transfer of Undertakings (Protection of Employment) Regulations 2006. Review all employment contracts, salary levels, holiday accrual and any outstanding grievances or tribunal claims before agreeing consideration.</li>
  <li><strong>Pharmacy First and service income:</strong> Confirm which NHS advanced and enhanced services the pharmacy provides (Pharmacy First and similar) and whether those service agreements transfer or must be re-applied for under new ownership. Service income is a growing revenue line and a gap in provision affects day-one income.</li>
</ul>

<h2 id="funding-and-affordability">Funding and affordability: what finance-ready means</h2>

<p>Lenders who specialise in pharmacy acquisition finance (typically healthcare-specialist banks and brokers) assess the deal differently from a standard commercial property lender. They look at the contract, the item volume trend and the net income after NHSBSA reimbursement, not just the balance sheet.</p>

<p>Finance-ready accounts for a pharmacy buyer typically require:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Two to three years of the target pharmacy's accounts, preferably audited or reviewed by a pharmacy-specialist accountant who can present them in a format lenders recognise.</li>
  <li>A working-capital model that accounts for the FP34 payment lag (cash in roughly two months after dispensing) and any Category M clawback timing.</li>
  <li>A clear picture of the VAT position and any outstanding liabilities that affect the net asset value.</li>
</ul>
