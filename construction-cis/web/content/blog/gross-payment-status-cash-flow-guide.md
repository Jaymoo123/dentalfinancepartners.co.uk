<h2>Gross payment status is a cash-flow instrument, not a tax break</h2>

<p>Most guides treat gross payment status (GPS) as a registration tier to qualify for. True, but it undersells what GPS does. GPS is a <strong>cash-flow instrument</strong>. It converts the 20% the Construction Industry Scheme would otherwise deduct from your payments into a <strong>0% rate</strong>, so the money stays in your business across the year instead of being withheld by HMRC and reclaimed later. For a construction business of any size that is one of the largest working-capital levers available, and the April 2026 rules have made keeping it materially harder.</p>

<p>This pillar quantifies what GPS is worth at real turnover levels, models what losing it costs in withheld cash and director exposure, and maps the April 2026 maintenance framework in full. If you want the qualifying side, the three tests, the application and how to get over the threshold, our companion guide on <a href="/blog/cis-compliance/cis-gross-payment-status-guide">how to qualify, apply and keep gross payment status</a> covers that ground. This post is about the money and the maintenance.</p>

<h2>The mechanism: 0% versus 20% versus 30%</h2>

<p>Under CIS a contractor must deduct money from a subcontractor's payment and hand it to HMRC as an advance against the subcontractor's eventual tax and National Insurance. For 2026/27 there are three rates, set by the subcontractor's registration status, and the difference between them is the whole cash-flow story.</p>

<table>
<tr><th>Status</th><th>CIS deduction rate</th><th>What happens to the cash</th></tr>
<tr><td>Gross payment status (GPS)</td><td>0%</td><td>Paid in full; nothing withheld; tax settled at year end</td></tr>
<tr><td>Registered subcontractor</td><td>20%</td><td>20% of the labour element withheld every payment, reclaimed later</td></tr>
<tr><td>Unregistered subcontractor</td><td>30%</td><td>30% of the labour element withheld every payment, reclaimed later</td></tr>
</table>

<p>One rule governs all three rates and is constantly misread, so state it plainly. <strong>CIS deductions apply to the labour element only.</strong> The cost of materials a subcontractor buys for the job is excluded. On a £1,000 invoice split £600 labour and £400 materials, the 20% (or 30%) bites on the £600 labour, not the full £1,000. GPS removes even the labour deduction, because the rate is 0% across the board. Throughout this guide, where we talk about "net CIS turnover" we mean that labour and construction-services element, excluding VAT and materials, which is the same base the deduction is calculated on.</p>

<p>The tax itself is identical under all three positions; what changes is timing. With the 20% rate HMRC collects on account every month, before any expenses or personal allowance are taken into account, so most registered subcontractors overpay and are owed a refund at the year end. With GPS, nothing is withheld and the holder budgets for and pays the whole liability after the year end through Self Assessment or Corporation Tax. GPS therefore changes <strong>when</strong> and <strong>how</strong> the tax is collected, never <strong>whether</strong> it is due. The benefit is the use of the money in the meantime.</p>

<p>Because the deduction is a flat percentage of the labour base, the value of GPS scales directly with turnover. The figures are the annual cash withheld at source, which is exactly the cash GPS keeps in the business.</p>

<p>Read the table as a cash-flow exposure, not a tax bill. The "withheld" columns are money that leaves your account at source and does not come back until you reclaim it, even though your actual liability is almost always lower once expenses and allowances are applied. The GPS saving columns are the same figures, because GPS keeps every pound of that withholding in the business. The direction is unambiguous: the more you turn over, the more GPS is worth. At £1m of net CIS turnover, the gap between holding GPS and dropping to the unregistered 30% rate is £300,000 of cash a year tied up with HMRC.</p>

<h2>How CIS withholding squeezes a construction business</h2>

<p>These numbers matter so much in construction because of the shape of the cash cycle. A subcontractor on the 20% rate does not choose when the deduction happens; it comes off every payment, every month, before the cash arrives. But the costs that money was meant to cover do not wait.</p>

<p>Materials are the obvious pressure point. A subcontractor often buys materials up front, before the job is invoiced and long before payment clears, yet the materials cost is excluded from the CIS deduction base while the labour income that would fund it has 20% stripped out at source. Payroll is the second: wages and your own subcontractors must be paid on a fixed cycle regardless of when CIS deductions come back. And the pipeline stretches the gap further, because winning the next stage of work means committing cash before the last stage has settled.</p>

<p>The result is a structural working-capital squeeze. The withheld 20% is, in effect, an interest-free loan to HMRC that you fund from your own balance sheet until you reclaim it, through Self Assessment after the year end for a sole trader, or in real time through the Employer Payment Summary for a limited company. Either way the cash has left first. GPS removes that loan entirely, so the money you need to buy materials and run payroll is in your account when you need it, not in an HMRC reclaim queue.</p>

<h2>Worked example: a bricklaying firm at £180,000 turnover</h2>

<p>Take a bricklaying firm with <strong>£180,000 of net CIS income</strong> a year, currently registered and on the 20% rate. The arithmetic is direct: 20% of £180,000 is <strong>£36,000 withheld at source over the year</strong>. With GPS, that figure is <strong>£0</strong>.</p>

<p>Spread across the year, the firm loses the use of £36,000 ÷ 12 = <strong>£3,000 a month</strong> in cash that would otherwise be available the moment each payment lands. With GPS, that £3,000 a month stays in the business. Put concretely, the firm can fund <strong>£3,000 a month of extra materials, around £36,000 across a 12-month year, without reaching for an overdraft or invoice finance</strong>. Over a year that is the materials budget for a meaningful run of jobs, financed entirely out of cash that GPS leaves in place.</p>

<p>The eventual tax position is unchanged. On the 20% rate the firm would have reclaimed most of that £36,000 after the year end, because the deduction is taken before expenses and allowances. With GPS it pays the correct, lower liability directly. The difference is purely timing, and for a firm buying bricks today to invoice next month, timing is the whole game. To see how a deduction splits across a real invoice, our <a href="/blog/cis-compliance/cis-subcontractor-verification">guide to verifying subcontractors and applying the right rate</a> walks through the labour-versus-materials split.</p>

<h2>The three qualifying tests, in brief</h2>

<p>GPS is held by passing three tests, all of which must be met and which HMRC assesses together. We summarise them here because maintenance is built on the same tests; the full detail and the application steps are in the <a href="/blog/cis-compliance/cis-gross-payment-status-guide">qualifying guide</a>.</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Business test.</strong> You carry out construction work, or supply labour for it, in the UK, and run the business through a bank account.</li>
<li><strong>Turnover test.</strong> Your net CIS turnover over the last 12 months reaches the threshold for your entity type: £30,000 for a sole trader, £30,000 per partner or £100,000 total for a partnership, £30,000 per director or £100,000 total for a limited company, and £30,000 per controller for a closely controlled company of five or fewer controllers. Net excludes VAT and materials.</li>
<li><strong>Compliance test.</strong> Every tax obligation has been met on time for the past 12 months, with no late Self Assessment returns, no overdue tax and no PAYE defaults.</li>
</ul>

<p>The compliance test is the one that matters most for maintenance, because it is the test HMRC re-runs at every annual review. Qualifying once does not bank it; you have to keep passing it.</p>

<h2>What changed in April 2026: before and after</h2>

<p>Until April 2026, holding GPS was essentially a matter of staying current with your own filings and payments. Finance Act 2026 changed that, in force from <strong>6 April 2026</strong>, by adding a fraud-focused layer that reaches outward into your supply chain and downward to individual directors. The table sets the old and new positions side by side. These provisions are enacted law: Finance Act 2026 received Royal Assent on 18 March 2026, and the statutory anti-fraud changes (sections 62A and 62B and the section 66 amendments) took effect on 6 April 2026 directly under FA 2026 section 222. SI 2026/289 separately commences the regulation-level changes, the reinstated nil return and the Regulation 23A public sector exemption.</p>

<table>
<tr><th>Aspect</th><th>Pre April 2026</th><th>From April 2026</th></tr>
<tr><td>Revocation trigger</td><td>Generally a clear compliance failure (late returns, overdue tax)</td><td>Also where a contractor knew or should have known about fraud in the supply chain</td></tr>
<tr><td>Notice required</td><td>Removal typically followed notice and a clear failure</td><td>Immediate revocation, with no advance notice, on the fraud ground</td></tr>
<tr><td>Reapplication ban</td><td>1 year after removal</td><td>5 years where GPS is removed on fraud grounds</td></tr>
<tr><td>Knowledge-based penalty</td><td>No standalone CIS penalty for paying or reporting with knowledge of deliberate failure</td><td>s.62A: 20% of the payment, on the payer. s.62B: 100% of the sum a false return treats as paid, on the return-maker. Officer-liability rules can reach directors personally</td></tr>
<tr><td>Nil return obligation</td><td>Not required (removed in 2015)</td><td>Reinstated; nil CIS returns must be filed in months with no subcontractor payments</td></tr>
</table>

<p>The single most important shift is the "should have known" standard. A <strong>failure to carry out due diligence is itself sufficient</strong> for HMRC to revoke GPS: HMRC need not prove you knew about fraud or intended anything. If you paid a subcontractor you could and should have checked, and did not, you can be inside the test. That is what turns GPS maintenance from a filing exercise into an active control.</p>

<h2>The April 2026 maintenance framework</h2>

<p>Keeping GPS from 6 April 2026 means running three workstreams together. None of them is optional, and the second and third are new.</p>

<h3>1. The annual compliance review</h3>

<p>HMRC reviews every GPS holder at least once a year against the compliance test. Everything must be current: Self Assessment, Corporation Tax, PAYE and CIS filings all on time, and no overdue tax. From April 2026 a clean CIS record also means filing <strong>nil CIS300 returns</strong> in any month where you paid no subcontractors, because the nil-return obligation has been reinstated after being removed in 2015. A holder who slips into late returns, an overdue bill, a PAYE default or a missed nil return can have GPS withdrawn at the review and dropped back to the 20% rate, with the full cash-flow cost in the tables above. The discipline is a clean compliance calendar, run year-round rather than reconstructed at review time. Our <a href="/blog/cis-compliance/cis-supply-chain-compliance-due-diligence">guide to CIS supply-chain compliance and due diligence</a> sets out how to keep that calendar alongside the checks below.</p>

<h3>2. Ongoing supply-chain due diligence</h3>

<p>This is the workstream most existing guidance misses entirely. To stay clear of the "should have known" standard, you must run, and be able to evidence, due diligence <strong>before paying any subcontractor</strong>. Three checks are central:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Re-verify the subcontractor's CIS status with HMRC.</strong> This sets the correct deduction rate and confirms the subcontractor is genuine. Re-verification, not a one-off check at the start of the relationship, is the point: status can change.</li>
<li><strong>Run a Companies House legitimacy check.</strong> Confirm the business exists, is active, and matches the entity you contracted with. A dissolved or mismatched company is a red flag the rules expect you to catch.</li>
<li><strong>Carry out bank account name verification.</strong> Confirm the payee account belongs to the entity you contracted with, so payment is not being redirected to a substituted or fraudulent party.</li>
</ul>

<p>The standard is met by the <em>absence</em> of reasonable checks, so the practical defence is a documented trail. A saved verification reference, a Companies House check on file and a confirmed bank match for every subcontractor is your evidence that you did not have reason to know about a problem. Verification always set the deduction rate; from 6 April 2026 it doubles as the control that protects your GPS.</p>

<h3>3. The knowledge-based penalties under sections 62A and 62B</h3>

<p>The third strand is not a task but a risk you have to manage. Under new <strong>FA 2004 section 62A</strong>, a person who makes a payment under a construction contract knowing, or having reason to know, that a connected party deliberately failed to comply with CIS is liable to a penalty of <strong>20% of the payment</strong>. <strong>Section 62B</strong> covers the parallel case of a return made with that knowledge, and the amount there is heavier: it is <strong>an amount equal to the sum the return treats as deducted and paid</strong>, that is <strong>100% of the sum falsely returned, with no percentage reduction</strong>. The penalty falls on the <strong>payer or return-maker</strong>, which is usually the company; there is no special director rate and no 30% figure in these sections. What removes the corporate firewall is the separate, long-standing <strong>officer-liability rules</strong>: where a company's deliberate behaviour produces the penalty, HMRC can recover part or all of it from the responsible officers personally. For a limited-company subcontractor that elevates supply-chain hygiene from a company compliance matter to a personal financial exposure for whoever signs off payments, which is why the due-diligence workstream cannot be delegated and forgotten: the person carrying the ultimate risk is the director.</p>

<h2>Worked example: the full cost of losing GPS on fraud grounds</h2>

<p>Put the maintenance framework against a worst case to see why it is worth the effort. Take a limited company with <strong>£500,000 of net CIS turnover</strong> a year that loses GPS on fraud grounds in April 2026, after HMRC concludes it should have known about a fraudulent subcontractor in its chain.</p>

<p><strong>The cash-flow hit.</strong> Once GPS is gone, 20% is deducted at source again: 20% of £500,000 is <strong>£100,000 a year withheld</strong>. Under the new 5-year reapplication ban, the company cannot get GPS back for five years. Over that period it cycles roughly £100,000 a year, around <strong>£500,000 of cash over five years</strong>, through HMRC before reclaiming it. It eventually recovers the overpaid element, but the working-capital drain runs for half a decade, which for a business of this size can be the difference between trading through and going under.</p>

<p><strong>The penalty hit.</strong> Suppose the company made £200,000 of payments under construction contracts knowing a connected party had deliberately failed to comply. Under section 62A the penalty is 20% of those payments, so <strong>£40,000, charged on the company as the payer</strong>. Because the failure was deliberate, HMRC can then recover that penalty from the responsible officers personally under the officer-liability rules, so the £40,000 can follow the director rather than staying with the company. There is no separate 30% director charge; the exposure is the 20% penalty, plus the prospect of it being transferred to whoever was responsible.</p>

<p>So the cost of a single avoidable lapse is a <strong>£500,000 multi-year cash drain on the business plus a £40,000 penalty that can land on the director personally</strong>, against a maintenance routine of three documented checks before each payment and a clean filing calendar. The asymmetry is the entire argument for taking GPS maintenance seriously.</p>

<h2>How to protect GPS in practice</h2>

<p>Turning the framework into routine is mostly about making the right checks standing processes rather than ad hoc reactions. Three habits cover it.</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>A standing verification process.</strong> Build the three due-diligence checks into your pay run so no subcontractor is paid without a CIS re-verification, a Companies House check and a bank name match on file. Make it a gate, not a courtesy, so a missing check stops the payment rather than being noticed afterwards.</li>
<li><strong>Disciplined record keeping.</strong> Keep the verification references, Companies House results and bank confirmations together, dated, for every subcontractor and every payment cycle. The records are the evidence that satisfies the "should have known" standard, so the filing system is the control.</li>
<li><strong>A regular eligibility health check.</strong> Do not wait for HMRC's annual review to find out you have drifted. Re-run the turnover and compliance tests yourself each quarter so any slippage in net CIS turnover or any late filing is caught while it can still be fixed.</li>
</ul>
