<h2>What the domestic reverse charge means for a main contractor</h2>

<p>The VAT domestic reverse charge (DRC) for construction services has been in force since 1 March 2021. Its effect on main contractors is the opposite of its effect on subcontractors: instead of subcontractors invoicing you with VAT and you paying them that VAT (then reclaiming it from HMRC on your VAT return), the DRC moves the accounting obligation to you. The subcontractor invoices you without VAT, and you account for the VAT to HMRC directly in your own return.</p>

<p>For most fully VAT-recovering businesses, the net cash effect is zero. You record the VAT as both output tax (box 1) and input tax (box 4) on the same return. The two entries cancel. However, the entries must still be made correctly, and getting them wrong, or missing a DRC supply entirely, can lead to VAT errors that HMRC will pick up on review.</p>

<p>This guide is written for the contractor audience: the party receiving the subcontractor's invoice. If you are a subcontractor trying to understand what to put on your invoice, read our complementary guide to <a href="/blog/vat-and-mtd/vat-reverse-charge-for-cis-subcontractors">VAT reverse charge for CIS subcontractors</a>. For the general overview of how the DRC works across the supply chain, see the <a href="/blog/vat-and-mtd/vat-reverse-charge-construction">VAT domestic reverse charge construction guide</a>.</p>

<h2>The five conditions: all must be met</h2>

<p>The DRC applies to a supply only when <strong>all five of the following conditions are satisfied</strong>. As the contractor, you are the customer in this analysis. If any condition is missing, your subcontractor should be charging VAT normally and you should be paying it.</p>

<table>
<thead>
<tr><th>Condition</th><th>What it means for you as contractor</th></tr>
</thead>
<tbody>
<tr><td>1. Specified CIS service</td><td>The work must be a construction service caught by CIS: building, alteration, repair, extension, demolition, installation of heating, plumbing, electrical or ventilation systems, internal cleaning during construction, painting and decorating, and most civil engineering works. Design-only, surveying, and off-site manufactured components are excluded.</td></tr>
<tr><td>2. Both parties VAT-registered</td><td>You and the subcontractor must both be registered for VAT. If either party is unregistered, normal VAT rules apply.</td></tr>
<tr><td>3. Both parties CIS-registered</td><td>You and the subcontractor must both be registered under the Construction Industry Scheme. Verify your subcontractor's CIS status through the HMRC verification service before their first payment: this also satisfies one of the three due-diligence steps required from 6 April 2026 under Finance Act 2026.</td></tr>
<tr><td>4. You are not the end user</td><td>The DRC applies only if you will supply the construction services on (for example, you are the main contractor packaging subcontract work into a larger contract). If you are consuming the building work yourself, you are the end user and the DRC does not apply. See the end-user section below.</td></tr>
<tr><td>5. Standard-rated or reduced-rated supply</td><td>The work must attract standard VAT (20%) or reduced VAT (5%). Zero-rated supplies, most importantly new-build residential construction, are outside the DRC entirely.</td></tr>
</tbody>
</table>

<p>Keep written evidence that conditions 3 and 4 were satisfied at the time of each supply. If HMRC enquires, your file note or the subcontractor's written confirmation is far more defensible than a verbal understanding.</p>

<h2>What you must do when the DRC applies</h2>

<p>When you receive a valid reverse-charge invoice from a subcontractor, you take the following steps.</p>

<ol>
<li><strong>Confirm the invoice is correct.</strong> A proper DRC invoice shows the net value of the work, no VAT charged to you, the VAT rate that would apply, the amount of VAT you are responsible for, and the reverse-charge legend (typically: "Reverse charge: customer to account for VAT to HMRC at the applicable rate on the VAT-exclusive price shown").</li>
<li><strong>Calculate the VAT you owe.</strong> Apply the stated VAT rate to the net invoice value. At 20%, a £5,000 net invoice carries £1,000 of VAT that you must account for.</li>
<li><strong>Make the VAT return entries.</strong> Record the £1,000 in box 1 (output tax due) and in box 4 (input tax recoverable, assuming full recovery). Record the net invoice value (£5,000) in box 7 (value of purchases). You do not adjust box 6 (value of sales) for this transaction.</li>
<li><strong>Pay the subcontractor the net amount, less any CIS deduction.</strong> The subcontractor is not owed the VAT element because you are accounting for it yourself. Apply the CIS deduction to the labour portion of the net invoice. See the worked example below.</li>
</ol>

<h2>The end-user exception: when DRC does not apply to you</h2>

<p>The most widely misunderstood aspect of the DRC is the end-user exception. Many contractors assume that because they are the "contractor" in CIS terms, the DRC automatically applies to supplies made to them. That is not correct. What matters is whether you will consume the building work yourself, not your CIS registration status.</p>

<p>You are the end user (and the DRC does not apply) if you are:</p>

<ul>
<li>A property owner commissioning work on your own building (whether owner-occupied, let, or used in your own business).</li>
<li>A tenant fitting out or refurbishing your own leasehold premises.</li>
<li>A property developer building for your own portfolio, to let or to sell as finished units. You are incorporating the construction services into a finished product you own. You do not sell construction services onward.</li>
<li>A company constructing a new facility for its own business use.</li>
</ul>

<p>In each case, your subcontractor should charge you VAT in the normal way. The DRC applies only where the customer is making an onward supply of construction services. A main contractor who has taken on a construction contract and engages subcontractors to fulfil it is not an end user: they are selling construction services onward to the principal. That is the scenario where the DRC applies between the main contractor and the sub.</p>

<p>Do not conflate the CIS distinction between contractor and subcontractor with the end-user test. They measure different things. A large property developer can be simultaneously a CIS-registered deemed contractor (for CIS purposes, because they spend over £3 million a year on construction work) and an end user (for DRC purposes, because they do not sell construction services onward). In that scenario, their subcontractors must charge VAT normally, even though the developer is in the CIS system.</p>

<h2>How to handle an incorrect invoice from your subcontractor</h2>

<p>Two types of error occur regularly in practice.</p>

<p><strong>Subcontractor charges VAT when DRC should apply.</strong> The subcontractor has added 20% VAT to an invoice that should have been reverse-charge. You should not pay the VAT element and should not reclaim it as input tax (HMRC may disallow it). Contact the subcontractor and ask them to issue a corrected invoice with a reverse-charge legend. If VAT has already been paid by mistake, a credit note and revised invoice is the correct route. Keep records of the correction process.</p>

<p><strong>Subcontractor issues no VAT when they should have charged it (DRC does not actually apply).</strong> This typically happens when the customer is the end user but the subcontractor applies the DRC anyway. In this case, the subcontractor should issue a corrected invoice charging VAT, and you should pay it. End-user status is worth clarifying in writing at the outset of a relationship to avoid this situation.</p>

<h2>Coding DRC in accounting software</h2>

<p>Most accounting software used by CIS contractors handles the DRC through a specific tax code or purchase type. The general approach is the same across platforms: you select the DRC tax code when posting the purchase, and the software automatically generates the offsetting entries in boxes 1, 4 and 7 of the VAT return.</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Xero:</strong> Use the "Reverse Charge Expenses" tax rate when coding DRC purchases. Xero posts the VAT to both output and input tax automatically.</li>
<li><strong>QuickBooks:</strong> Select "Domestic Reverse Charge" as the VAT code on the bill. QuickBooks generates the box 1 and box 4 entries without a separate manual step.</li>
<li><strong>Sage:</strong> Use the T21 tax code for purchases subject to the DRC. Sage 50 and Sage Accounting both apply the reverse entries automatically when T21 is selected.</li>
<li><strong>FreeAgent:</strong> Use the "Reverse charge" VAT rate when entering purchase bills. FreeAgent populates the relevant return boxes.</li>
</ul>

<p>Always check that your software version is up to date and that the tax code is correctly set up in your chart of accounts before relying on the automation. Miscoded DRC transactions are a frequent cause of VAT return errors.</p>

<h2>The 5% de minimis rule</h2>

<p>Where a contract mixes DRC-applicable CIS services with other supplies, the 5% de minimis concession can determine whether the DRC applies at all. HMRC is explicit: <strong>the 5% must not be calculated on an invoice-by-invoice basis.</strong> It is assessed against overall contract values. In addition, both the supplier and the customer must agree in writing, from the start of the contract, that the concession should apply. Without that upfront mutual written agreement, the concession cannot be relied upon regardless of what the invoice-level proportions look like.</p>

<p>Where both conditions are met (the DRC element is 5% or less of the overall contract value, and both parties have agreed this in writing at contract inception), normal VAT rules apply to the whole contract and the DRC is ignored for that contract.</p>

<p>Illustration: a subcontractor is engaged under a contract worth £200,000 to supply and install pre-fabricated structural steelwork. At contract inception both parties agree in writing that the de minimis concession applies. Over the life of the contract the on-site fixing labour (a specified CIS service) amounts to £8,000, which is 4% of the £200,000 contract value. Since 4% is below 5% and the written agreement is in place, normal VAT applies across the board. No DRC entries are required on any invoice under that contract.</p>

<p>If the fixing element were expected to amount to £11,000 (5.5% of contract value), the threshold would be exceeded. The concession would not apply, and the DRC would apply to the CIS portion of the contract, regardless of how individual invoices happen to fall.</p>

<p>Two important practical points follow from the per-contract basis. First, the assessment must be made at contract inception, not retrospectively once invoices have been issued. Second, where a contract scope changes materially, the parties should review whether the concession still applies to the revised contract values.</p>

<h2>April 2026 due diligence and the DRC connection</h2>

<p>Finance Act 2026, which received Royal Assent on 18 March 2026, introduced tightened GPS anti-fraud rules in force from 6 April 2026. One of the three due-diligence steps required before every subcontractor payment is verifying the subcontractor's CIS status through HMRC's verification service. This step also satisfies condition 3 of the DRC five-condition test (both parties CIS-registered). The two compliance regimes now overlap: your monthly subcontractor verification routine directly reduces the risk of DRC errors as well as protecting your Gross Payment Status.</p>

<h2>Practical checklist before processing a DRC invoice</h2>

<ul>
<li>Confirm the work is a specified CIS service (not design-only or off-site manufacture).</li>
<li>Confirm you are VAT-registered.</li>
<li>Confirm the subcontractor is VAT-registered (check their VAT number on the invoice).</li>
<li>Confirm the subcontractor is CIS-registered (verify with HMRC; record the verification reference and date).</li>
<li>Confirm you are not the end user. If there is doubt, document your position in writing.</li>
<li>Confirm the supply is standard-rated or reduced-rated (not zero-rated new-build).</li>
<li>For mixed-supply contracts: check whether you and the subcontractor have agreed in writing at contract inception that the 5% de minimis concession applies, and whether the DRC element of the overall contract value is 5% or less. If both conditions are met, normal VAT applies to the whole contract. Do not assess the 5% on individual invoices.</li>
<li>Code the purchase correctly in your accounting software using the DRC tax code.</li>
<li>Make the CIS deduction on the labour element and report the payment on your next CIS300 return.</li>
</ul>
