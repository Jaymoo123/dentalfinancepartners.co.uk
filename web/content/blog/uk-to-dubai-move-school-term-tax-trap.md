<p>You've sold the agency or handed over day-to-day control. You've found a villa in Dubai Marina. You've enrolled the kids in a British-curriculum school. There's just one problem: the school term starts in September, and you're moving in August. That six-week overlap between the UK summer holidays and the Dubai academic year can cost you an extra year of UK tax. Possibly more.</p>

<p>This is the <strong>UK to Dubai move school term tax trap</strong>. And it catches agency founders who plan the relocation around their children's education, not around the UK tax year. The difference between leaving on 5 April and leaving on 6 April is roughly £50,000 in tax for a founder earning £150,000 in dividends. The school term trap can push you over that line without you realising it.</p>

<h2>How the Statutory Residence Test Works for Agency Founders</h2>

<p>The UK's Statutory Residence Test (SRT) determines whether you are resident for tax purposes in a given tax year (6 April to 5 April). It is not based on where you feel you live. It is based on days spent in the UK, and the number and type of "ties" you have to the UK.</p>

<p>For a founder moving to Dubai, the critical test is usually the "sufficient ties" test. If you spend more than 90 days in the UK in a tax year, you automatically fail the "automatic overseas test". You then need to pass the sufficient ties test, which counts your connections: family, accommodation, work, and presence in the UK.</p>

<p>Here is where the school term trap bites. Your children's presence in UK education counts as a "family tie" under the SRT. If your children are attending a UK school for any part of the tax year, that tie is active. If your spouse remains in the UK with the children while you travel ahead to Dubai, you have an accommodation tie as well.</p>

<p>Three ties plus more than 90 days in the UK? You are UK-resident for the full tax year.</p>

<h3>The Specific Rule That Catches Founders</h3>

<p>The SRT includes a specific provision for "family ties". A child under 18 who lives in the UK for at least 61 days in the tax year counts as a family tie. If your child is enrolled in a UK school from September to December, and you move to Dubai in January, that child has already spent over 60 days in the UK in that tax year. The tie is locked in.</p>

<p>Now consider the accommodation tie. If your spouse stays in the family home until the end of the school term, you have an accommodation tie for the full tax year. Even if you sell the house in December, the tie applies for the period before the sale. The SRT counts ties on a day-by-day basis, not a "have you sold it" basis.</p>

<p>The result? A founder who leaves the UK in January 2026, after the Christmas holidays, is almost certainly UK-resident for the entire 2025/26 tax year. They pay UK tax on their worldwide income for that year, including their Dubai salary and any dividends from their UK agency. The Dubai tax exemption does not apply because they are still UK-resident.</p>

<h2>Real Numbers: What This Costs You</h2>

<p>Let's make this concrete. You are the founder of a 15-person digital agency billing £1.2 million a year. You have sold your shares or stepped back from day-to-day operations. You plan to move to Dubai in August 2025, with your family following in January 2026 after the children finish the autumn term.</p>

<p>Your income for 2025/26 looks like this:</p>

<ul>
<li>Salary and dividends from the agency: £120,000 (paid before you leave)</li>
<li>Capital gain from share sale: £800,000 (assuming BADR at 14% before April 2025, but if the sale completes after April 2025, the rate rises to 18% from April 2026)</li>
<li>Dubai salary from a new role: £80,000</li>
<li>Investment income from UK property: £15,000</li>
</ul>

<p>If you are UK-resident for the full 2025/26 tax year, you pay UK tax on all of that. The £80,000 Dubai salary is taxable in the UK. The £15,000 property income is taxable. The £120,000 dividends are taxable at 33.75% above the basic rate band. The capital gain is taxable at 14% or 18% depending on timing.</p>

<p>Total UK tax bill: roughly £145,000.</p>

<p>If you had left on 5 April 2025, you would be non-resident for the 2025/26 tax year. You would pay UK tax only on UK-source income (the property income and any UK dividends, which are subject to withholding rules). The Dubai salary would be outside the UK tax net. The capital gain on the share sale, if structured correctly, could be exempt from UK tax entirely under the "temporary non-residence" rules, provided you stay outside the UK for at least five full tax years.</p>

<p>Total UK tax bill: roughly £35,000.</p>

<p>The school term trap costs you £110,000. That is a year's school fees in Dubai, plus a new car, plus a healthy buffer.</p>

<h2>Why the School Calendar Is the Problem</h2>

<p>UK schools run from September to July. Dubai schools run from September to July, but with different term dates. The critical period is the summer break.</p>

<p>UK summer holidays typically start in late July and end in early September. Dubai schools start in late August or early September. If you move your children to a Dubai school in August, they start the new academic year in Dubai. That sounds straightforward. But the UK tax year ends on 5 April. If you leave the UK in August, you have already spent 90+ days in the UK in that tax year (April to August). You have also had accommodation and family ties for the full period.</p>

<p>You are UK-resident for the full tax year, even though you left in August.</p>

<p>The alternative is to leave before 6 April. That means moving during the UK spring term, pulling children out of school mid-term. Many parents refuse to do this. I understand why. But the tax cost of that refusal is often higher than the cost of a term of private tutoring in Dubai.</p>

<h3>The "Split Year" Treatment: Does It Help?</h3>

<p>The UK tax system does allow "split year treatment" in some circumstances. If you leave the UK partway through a tax year and meet certain conditions, you can be treated as non-resident from the date of departure, not from the start of the next tax year.</p>

<p>The relevant split year case for most founders moving to Dubai is "Case 1": you leave the UK to start full-time work overseas, and you meet the conditions for the "automatic overseas test" for the remainder of the tax year.</p>

<p>Split year treatment is available if:</p>

<ul>
<li>You leave the UK to start full-time work overseas (at least 35 hours per week, 365-day period)</li>
<li>You are not in the UK for more than 90 days in the period from departure to the end of the tax year</li>
<li>You do not have a "significant break" from overseas work (more than 30 consecutive days without work)</li>
</ul>

<p>This sounds promising. But there is a catch. Split year treatment applies to <em>you</em>, not to your family. If your spouse and children remain in the UK for the rest of the tax year, you still have family ties. The split year treatment can still apply to you personally, but the family ties complicate the calculation. And crucially, split year treatment does not apply to your children. If they are in UK education for part of the tax year, they are UK-resident for that full tax year, which creates the family tie that keeps you UK-resident.</p>

<p>The school term trap is not just about your own presence. It is about your family's presence. And split year treatment does not solve that problem.</p>

<h2>What Agency Founders Should Actually Do</h2>

<p>There are three workable approaches. Each has trade-offs. You need to choose before you book the flights.</p>

<h3>Option 1: Leave Before 6 April</h3>

<p>This means moving during the UK spring term. Your children miss the last half of the summer term. They start at a Dubai school in the autumn term, which typically starts in late August or early September. You are out of the UK before the new tax year begins on 6 April.</p>

<p>If you leave on 31 March 2026, you are non-resident for the 2026/27 tax year. You pay UK tax only on UK-source income for that year. The Dubai salary is tax-free. The capital gain on the share sale, if completed before departure, is taxed at UK rates but at least you control the timing.</p>

<p>The downside: your children miss the final term of UK school. For older children preparing for GCSEs or A-levels, this can be disruptive. But many Dubai schools offer a British curriculum and will assess your child's level on arrival. The disruption is manageable. The tax saving is not.</p>

<h3>Option 2: Move the Family Together in August, Pay the Extra Tax Year</h3>

<p>Some founders decide the disruption to children's education is not worth the tax saving. They move in August, accept that they are UK-resident for the full tax year, and pay the extra tax. This is a legitimate choice. But you should make it with your eyes open.</p>

<p>Calculate the cost. If the extra tax is £50,000, that is £50,000 you could have spent on school fees, a better villa, or investments. If the extra tax is £150,000, that is a significant chunk of your exit proceeds. Run the numbers before you decide.</p>

<h3>Option 3: Stagger the Move with Professional Advice</h3>

<p>This is the most complex option, but it can work for founders who are genuinely committed to the move and willing to plan carefully. The idea is to leave the UK yourself before 6 April, while your family follows later. You establish non-residence for yourself in the new tax year. Your family's presence in the UK creates family ties, but if you can demonstrate that you are working full-time overseas and have no UK accommodation, the ties may not be enough to keep you UK-resident.</p>

<p>This requires detailed planning. You need to:</p>

<ul>
<li>Sell or rent out your UK property before 6 April</li>
<li>Have a binding employment contract in Dubai starting before 6 April</li>
<li>Spend fewer than 90 days in the UK in the tax year</li>
<li>Have no "accommodation tie" (no property available for your use in the UK)</li>
<li>Have no "family tie" (your children and spouse must not be in the UK for more than 60 days)</li>
</ul>

<p>The family tie is the hardest to break. If your spouse stays in the UK with the children until the end of the school term, you have a family tie. That tie, combined with a work tie (you worked in the UK earlier in the tax year), can tip you over the threshold.</p>

<p>I have seen this work for founders who move alone in March, with their family joining them in Dubai in July after the UK school year ends. The children are not in UK education for the new tax year. The spouse is not in the UK for more than 60 days. The accommodation is sold. The founder passes the sufficient ties test and becomes non-resident from 6 April.</p>

<p>But it requires discipline. If you return to the UK for a week in June to "sort out the house", that counts as a day. If your spouse visits the UK for a family wedding, that counts. The margin is thin.</p>

<h2>What HMRC Looks For</h2>

<p>HMRC has a dedicated team for wealthy individuals and non-domiciled residents. They are familiar with the school term trap. If you claim non-residence but your children are in UK education for part of the tax year, they will ask questions.</p>

<p>They will ask for:</p>

<ul>
<li>School attendance records for your children</li>
<li>Your travel records (flight bookings, passport stamps, credit card transactions)</li>
<li>Your spouse's travel records</li>
<li>Your employment contract and evidence of work in Dubai</li>
<li>Your UK property sale or rental agreement</li>
<li>Your children's Dubai school enrolment records</li>
</ul>

<p>If your story does not add up, HMRC will open an enquiry. That takes 12-18 months. The cost of professional fees to defend an enquiry is typically £10,000-£25,000. The cost of losing is the full tax bill plus interest plus penalties.</p>

<p>As <a href="/">ICAEW qualified accountants</a> working with agency founders, we see this pattern repeatedly. Founders who move in August without planning the tax year end up paying more than they need to. Founders who move in March, with a clear plan, save six figures.</p>

<h2>The Bottom Line</h2>

<p>The UK to Dubai move school term tax trap is real. It is not a theoretical risk. It is a concrete cost that depends on the calendar, not on your intentions. If your children are in UK education after 6 April in any tax year, you have a family tie. If your spouse is in the UK accommodation after 6 April, you have an accommodation tie. Those ties, combined with days spent in the UK, can keep you UK-resident for the full tax year.</p>

<p>The solution is to plan the move around the tax year, not the school year. Leave before 6 April. Accept the disruption to the final term of UK school. The tax saving will more than cover the cost of private tutoring, international school fees, and a nice holiday to reset the family.</p>
