<p>If you run a marketing or digital agency from Dubai, you've probably heard the headline: UAE corporate tax is 9% on profits above AED 375,000. Simple enough, right?</p>

<p>Not quite.</p>

<p>There is a rule buried in the Corporate Tax Law that catches agency founders out regularly. It is the <strong>connected person</strong> rule. And it can mean your owner-manager salary is taxed twice — once in your hands as income, and again in your company's hands as a disallowed expense.</p>

<p>Let me explain how this works, why it matters for agency founders, and what to do about it.</p>

<h2>What Is the UAE Corporate Tax 9% Connected Person Rule?</h2>

<p>The UAE introduced a 9% corporate tax rate from financial years starting on or after 1 June 2023. The rate applies to taxable profits exceeding AED 375,000. Below that, the rate is 0%.</p>

<p>But the law also includes transfer pricing rules. These rules require that transactions between "connected persons" are priced at arm's length. A connected person includes your spouse, your children, your parent companies, your sister companies — and crucially, <strong>you</strong> as an owner-manager.</p>

<p>Here is the specific risk. If you are a director and employee of your agency, any salary, bonus, or benefit you take is a transaction with a connected person. The tax authority can challenge whether that amount is at arm's length. If they decide it isn't, they can disallow the expense in your company's corporation tax computation. Your company then pays 9% (or 0% if below the threshold) on that disallowed amount. Meanwhile, you have already paid personal tax on the salary in your home country (or in the UAE if you are a resident individual with other income).</p>

<p>Double taxation in practice.</p>

<h2>How This Applies to Agency Founders</h2>

<p>Most agency founders in the UAE structure their affairs like this:</p>

<ul>
<li>They own 100% of a Dubai-based agency company.</li>
<li>They pay themselves a monthly salary, often set at a round number like AED 15,000 or AED 25,000.</li>
<li>They take the rest as dividends or director's fees.</li>
<li>They assume the salary is a deductible expense for corporate tax purposes.</li>
</ul>

<p>That assumption is what the connected person rule targets.</p>

<p>If your salary is below what an unrelated third party would pay someone doing your role, the tax authority can say: "That salary is not at arm's length. We are adding it back to your company's taxable profits."</p>

<p>And if your salary is above market rate — say you pay yourself AED 80,000 per month when a comparable role would pay AED 40,000 — the excess can also be challenged.</p>

<p>The result is the same either way. Your company loses a deduction. You still pay personal tax on the full amount. The effective tax rate on that portion of your income jumps.</p>

<h3>Real Example: A 12-Person Digital Agency in Dubai</h3>

<p>Let me give you a worked example.</p>

<p>Sarah runs a digital agency in Dubai Media City. She has 12 staff and turns over AED 4.2 million per year. She pays herself a salary of AED 20,000 per month (AED 240,000 per year). Her company's total expenses are AED 3.6 million, leaving a profit of AED 600,000.</p>

<p>Under the standard reading, her company pays 9% on AED 225,000 (AED 600,000 minus AED 375,000 threshold) = AED 20,250 corporate tax.</p>

<p>But the tax authority reviews her salary and decides that an unrelated general manager doing her role would be paid AED 45,000 per month. They determine her salary is not at arm's length. They add back AED 300,000 (AED 540,000 arm's length salary minus AED 240,000 actual salary) to her company's taxable profits.</p>

<p>Now the company's taxable profit is AED 900,000. Tax payable is 9% on AED 525,000 = AED 47,250. That is an extra AED 27,000 in tax. And Sarah has already paid personal tax on her AED 240,000 salary in the UK (if she is UK resident) or in the UAE (if she has other UAE income).</p>

<p>She has been double-taxed on AED 240,000 of income. The company lost a deduction. She gained nothing.</p>

<h2>What Counts as Arm's Length for an Owner-Manager?</h2>

<p>This is where it gets subjective. There is no fixed rate card for director salaries in the UAE. The tax authority will look at:</p>

<ul>
<li>Your role and responsibilities.</li>
<li>The size and revenue of your agency.</li>
<li>Industry benchmarks for similar roles.</li>
<li>Whether you have other income streams (dividends, director's fees, rental income).</li>
</ul>

<p>If you are the sole director and your agency turns over AED 1 million, a salary of AED 10,000 per month might be defensible. If you turn over AED 10 million and pay yourself AED 10,000, the tax authority will ask why.</p>

<p>The safest approach is to document your rationale. Keep a record of market rate data for your role. Use job boards, salary surveys, or a benchmarking report from a recruitment agency. If you ever get challenged, you have evidence to support your position.</p>

<h2>Does the Connected Person Rule Apply to Dividends?</h2>

<p>Yes and no. Dividends are distributions of profit, not expenses. They are not deductible for corporate tax purposes anyway. So there is no expense to disallow.</p>

<p>But the connected person rule can still apply if you pay dividends to a connected person (yourself, your spouse, your holding company) in a way that is not at arm's length. For example, if you pay a dividend to a connected person that is not based on their shareholding, that could be challenged.</p>

<p>For most agency founders who own 100% of the shares, dividends are straightforward. You take them in proportion to your shareholding. No issue.</p>

<p>The risk is specifically around salary, bonuses, director's fees, and benefits in kind. These are expenses. They are the ones the tax authority can disallow.</p>

<h2>How to Structure Your Owner-Manager Salary Correctly</h2>

<p>There is no one-size-fits-all answer. But here is a framework that works for most agency founders.</p>

<h3>Step 1: Determine a Defensible Salary</h3>
<p>Benchmark your role. If you are the CEO of a 15-person agency, look at what a CEO of a similar-sized agency in Dubai would earn. Use data from Michael Page, Hays, or Robert Half. Keep a copy of the benchmark in your company records.</p>

<h3>Step 2: Set Your Salary at That Level</h3>
<p>Do not set it artificially low to avoid personal tax. Do not set it artificially high to reduce corporate tax. Set it at a defensible market rate. If you want to take more money out of the company, use dividends.</p>

<h3>Step 3: Document Your Decision</h3>
<p>Write a brief memo explaining why you chose that salary. Reference the benchmark data. File it with your company's tax records. If HMRC or the UAE Federal Tax Authority ever ask, you have a paper trail.</p>

<h3>Step 4: Review Annually</h3>
<p>Your role changes as your agency grows. Review your salary each year against current market data. Adjust if needed. Document the change.</p>

<h2>What About UK Agency Founders Moving to Dubai?</h2>

<p>If you are a UK agency founder considering a move to Dubai, the connected person rule interacts with UK tax rules in a few ways.</p>

<p>First, if you remain UK tax resident, your worldwide income is subject to UK tax. Your UAE salary is taxed in the UK, subject to double tax relief. The UAE corporate tax your company pays does not give you UK personal tax relief. So if your company's salary deduction is disallowed in the UAE, you pay extra UAE corporate tax and still pay UK personal tax on the full salary.</p>

<p>Second, if you become UAE tax resident, you are generally not subject to UK tax on your UAE employment income (provided you meet the statutory residence test). But the connected person rule still applies to your UAE company. You need to get the salary right at the UAE level regardless of your personal tax status.</p>

<p>Third, if you hold shares in a UK agency and a UAE agency, the connected person rules can apply across both entities. The UAE tax authority can look at transactions between your UAE company and your UK company if they are connected. That includes management fees, service charges, and cost allocations.</p>

<h2>Common Mistakes Agency Founders Make</h2>

<p>I see the same errors repeatedly. Here are the ones to avoid.</p>

<p><strong>Mistake 1: Setting salary at zero.</strong> Some founders pay themselves nothing, relying entirely on dividends. The tax authority can impute a market salary and disallow a notional expense. You end up with a tax bill on income you never received.</p>

<p><strong>Mistake 2: Setting salary at a round number with no rationale.</strong> AED 15,000, AED 20,000, AED 30,000. If you cannot justify it, the tax authority will not accept it.</p>

<p><strong>Mistake 3: Ignoring the rule entirely.</strong> "It's only 9% on profits above AED 375k, so it doesn't matter." It matters because the disallowance can push you above the threshold, or increase your effective rate significantly.</p>

<p><strong>Mistake 4: Mixing personal and business expenses.</strong> If you pay personal expenses through the company and treat them as salary or benefits, the connected person rule applies. The tax authority can disallow those expenses entirely.</p>

<h2>What to Do Now</h2>

<p>If you run an agency in the UAE, or are planning to set one up, the connected person rule is not something to ignore. It is a real risk that can cost you thousands in extra tax.</p>

<p>Here is what I recommend:</p>

<ul>
<li>Review your current salary structure. Is it defensible? If not, change it.</li>
<li>Document your rationale. Keep a benchmark report in your files.</li>
<li>Speak to a qualified accountant who understands UAE corporate tax and the connected person rules. Do not rely on a generalist.</li>
</ul>

<p>If you are a UK agency founder moving to Dubai, get advice on both sides. The interaction between UK and UAE tax rules is complex. One mistake can cost you dearly.</p>
