<h2>Most Agency Founders Get Pricing Wrong Because They Skip This Step</h2>

<p>You cannot set pricing until you know your break-even number. It is that direct.</p>

<p>Yet most agency founders I meet when they first come to us at <a href="/">Agency Founder Finance</a> have never calculated theirs. They pick a day rate because it sounds right. They match a competitor. They multiply what they used to earn as a freelancer by 1.5 and call it a day.</p>

<p>That approach works until it doesn't. And when it stops working, the agency is usually burning cash, the director's loan account is overdrawn, and the founder is working 60-hour weeks for less than they could earn employed.</p>

<p>This is not a theoretical post. This is the exact calculation we run through with every new client. If you take 30 minutes to work through this, you will know your break-even number. And you will never set a price blind again.</p>

<h2>What Is Break-Even Monthly Revenue?</h2>

<p>Break-even monthly revenue is the minimum income your agency must generate each month to cover all costs. Not to make a profit. To survive.</p>

<p>If you bill less than this number, you are losing money. Every month. Even if your bank balance looks fine because you have not paid the VAT bill yet.</p>

<p>There are two types of cost you need to capture:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Fixed costs</strong> — rent, software subscriptions, salaries, insurance, accountancy fees. These stay roughly the same regardless of how much work you do.</li>
<li><strong>Variable costs</strong> — freelancers, contractors, platform spend, materials. These move with your revenue.</li>
</ul>

<p>Your break-even number sits where total revenue covers total fixed costs plus total variable costs. Nothing left over. Zero profit. Zero loss.</p>

<p>From that number, you work backwards to set pricing that builds in real margin. Let me show you how.</p>

<h2>Step 1: List Every Fixed Cost Your Agency Has</h2>

<p>Start with a clean list. Do not guess. Pull the actual numbers from your bank statements or accounting software. If you use Xero or QuickBooks, run a report for the last three months and take the average.</p>

<p>Here is a typical fixed cost list for a 12-person digital agency billing around £800,000 per year, based in Manchester Northern Quarter:</p>

<ul>
<li>Office rent and business rates: £3,200 per month</li>
<li>Salaries (permanent staff, including employer NI and pension): £38,000 per month</li>
<li>Software subscriptions (Asana, Slack, Adobe, Google Workspace, agency tools): £1,400 per month</li>
<li>Accountancy and bookkeeping: £650 per month</li>
<li>Insurance (PI, employer's liability, cyber): £320 per month</li>
<li>Hosting and infrastructure: £480 per month</li>
<li>Marketing and business development: £1,200 per month</li>
<li>Phone and broadband: £280 per month</li>
<li>Professional subscriptions and training: £200 per month</li>
<li>Miscellaneous (bank fees, stationery, travel): £400 per month</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Total fixed costs: £46,130 per month</strong></p>

<p>Your numbers will differ. That is fine. The structure is what matters. Capture everything. If you pay for something annually (like PI insurance), divide by 12 and add it in.</p>

<h2>Step 2: Calculate Your Variable Cost Percentage</h2>

<p>Variable costs are trickier because they scale. The standard approach is to express them as a percentage of revenue.</p>

<p>For most agencies, the biggest variable cost is freelancers and contractors. If you bill a project for £10,000 and pay a freelancer £4,000 to deliver it, your variable cost on that project is 40%.</p>

<p>Other variable costs might include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Paid media spend (for performance agencies)</li>
<li>Print or production costs (for creative agencies)</li>
<li>Subcontracted development work (for web agencies)</li>
<li>Commission payments</li>
</ul>

<p>Look at your last six months of work. What percentage of revenue went to variable costs? If you do not have that data, estimate it conservatively. Most project-based agencies sit between 25% and 40%.</p>

<p>For our example agency, let us assume variable costs run at 30% of revenue.</p>

<h2>Step 3: Calculate Your Break-Even Monthly Revenue</h2>

<p>Here is the formula:</p>

<p><strong>Break-even revenue = Fixed costs ÷ (1 - Variable cost percentage)</strong></p>

<p>Plug in the numbers from our example:</p>

<p>Break-even revenue = £46,130 ÷ (1 - 0.30)</p>
<p>Break-even revenue = £46,130 ÷ 0.70</p>
<p>Break-even revenue = <strong>£65,900 per month</strong></p>

<p>This agency needs to bill £65,900 every single month just to cover its costs. Every pound above that is profit (before tax). Every pound below it is a loss.</p>

<p>If this agency bills £800,000 per year, that is £66,667 per month on average. They are just scraping by. One bad month, one client churn, and they are in the red.</p>

<p>This is why knowing your break-even number is essential agency finance basics. Without it, you cannot tell whether your pricing is adequate or whether you are running a busy unprofitable business.</p>

<h2>Step 4: Set Your Target Monthly Revenue (Including Profit)</h2>

<p>Breaking even is not the goal. You want profit. Profit pays your dividend, funds your growth, covers unexpected costs, and builds the value of your agency for an eventual exit.</p>

<p>Decide what profit margin you want. For a healthy agency, I recommend targeting at least 20% net profit margin on top of your break-even number.</p>

<p>Target revenue = Break-even revenue ÷ (1 - Target profit margin)</p>

<p>Using our example:</p>
<p>Target revenue = £65,900 ÷ (1 - 0.20)</p>
<p>Target revenue = £65,900 ÷ 0.80</p>
<p>Target revenue = <strong>£82,375 per month</strong></p>

<p>That is £988,500 per year. A significant jump from £800,000. This agency either needs to grow revenue by nearly £190,000 or cut costs. Or raise prices.</p>

<h2>Step 5: Work Backwards to Set Your Day Rate or Project Pricing</h2>

<p>Now you know what you need to earn each month. The next step is setting rates that deliver that number.</p>

<p>Start with your team's available billable hours. If you have 12 people, not all of them are billable. Account managers, the founder, and admin staff typically are not. Assume 8 billable team members in our example.</p>

<p>Each billable person has roughly 160 available hours per month (40 hours x 4 weeks). But they will not bill all of those hours. They have internal meetings, training, holiday, sickness. A realistic utilisation rate for a healthy agency is 70-80%.</p>

<p>At 75% utilisation:</p>
<p>8 people x 160 hours x 75% = <strong>960 billable hours per month</strong></p>

<p>Now divide your target monthly revenue by billable hours:</p>
<p>£82,375 ÷ 960 hours = <strong>£85.80 per hour blended rate</strong></p>

<p>That is the average rate you need across your team. Junior staff might bill at £60. Senior staff at £120. The blend must average £85.80.</p>

<p>If your current average is £70, you are under-pricing by nearly 20%. That is the gap you need to close.</p>

<h2>Retainer Pricing vs Project Pricing: The Break-Even Impact</h2>

<p>Retainers are easier to model for break-even because the revenue is predictable. If you sign a £8,000 per month retainer, you know exactly what it contributes.</p>

<p>Project billing creates spikes. You might bill £40,000 in January and £20,000 in February. That makes cash flow management harder. Your break-even number does not change, but your ability to hit it every month depends on your pipeline.</p>

<p>If your agency relies heavily on project work, build a 3-month rolling break-even forecast. Look at your pipeline and ask: do I have enough confirmed work to cover costs for the next 90 days? If not, you need to either win more work or reduce fixed costs.</p>

<p>For agencies with a mix of retainers and projects, I recommend aiming for retainers to cover at least 60% of your break-even number. That gives you a stable base. Projects then become your profit engine.</p>

<h2>What Happens When You Ignore Your Break-Even Number</h2>

<p>I see the same pattern repeatedly. An agency founder calls us because they are confused. Revenue is growing. The team is busy. But there is never enough cash to pay the VAT bill or take a dividend.</p>

<p>We run the break-even calculation. Almost always, the agency is pricing below break-even on certain clients or services. They are subsidising low-margin work with their own time and not realising it.</p>

<p>The fix is not complicated. It is uncomfortable. You have to raise prices, fire low-value clients, or cut costs. But you cannot make that decision until you have the number in front of you.</p>

<p>If you are an agency founder reading this and you have never calculated your break-even, do it this week. Pull the numbers. Run the formula. Then look at your current pricing and see where you stand.</p>

<h2>Using Break-Even to Inform Your Agency Structure</h2>

<p>Your break-even number also influences decisions about agency structure. If your fixed costs are too high, you are carrying overhead that makes you fragile. Every downturn becomes existential.</p>

<p>Many agency founders we work with consider a <a href="/blog/incorporation-and-structure">holding company structure</a> when they have multiple revenue streams or plan to exit. That structure can help separate cost centres and protect profitable parts of the business from the rest.</p>

<p>But structure is secondary to the numbers. First, get your break-even right. Then think about how to organise around it.</p>

<h2>Common Mistakes in Break-Even Calculations</h2>

<p><strong>Forgetting your own salary.</strong> Many founders do not pay themselves a market rate. If you are taking £24,000 as salary and dividends but could earn £80,000 employed, your break-even is artificially low. Include a realistic salary for yourself in fixed costs.</p>

<p><strong>Ignoring employer NI and pension.</strong> A £40,000 salary costs you closer to £46,000 once you add employer NI at 13.8% and minimum pension contributions at 3%. Use the real cost, not the salary figure.</p>

<p><strong>Using annual averages for monthly costs.</strong> Some costs hit quarterly or annually. Spread them across 12 months. Otherwise your break-even number bounces around and you make bad decisions.</p>

<p><strong>Confusing cash flow with profit.</strong> You might have £80,000 in the bank and think you are profitable. But if £20,000 of that is VAT you have collected and not yet paid, your break-even is different. Separate VAT, corporation tax, and PAYE from your operational numbers.</p>

<h2>When to Recalculate Your Break-Even</h2>

<p>Your break-even number is not static. Recalculate it whenever:</p>
<ul>
<li>You hire or fire someone</li>
<li>You move offices or change your overhead significantly</li>
<li>Your mix of retainer vs project work shifts</li>
<li>You add or remove a service line</li>
<li>Your variable cost percentage changes (e.g. you start using more freelancers)</li>
</ul>

<p>I recommend a formal review every quarter. Put it in your calendar. It takes 30 minutes and it is the single most useful financial exercise you can do for your agency.</p>
