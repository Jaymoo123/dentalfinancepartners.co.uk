<p>You have a 12-person digital agency billing £800k per year. You are moving to Dubai. You want to take your client contracts with you. But you cannot just email them a new invoice from your Dubai entity and hope for the best. Most contracts have clauses that explicitly prevent this. Breach them, and you are looking at repudiation, damages claims, or losing the client entirely.</p>

<p>Transferring client contracts to a Dubai entity is a legal process, not an administrative one. It requires either novation, assignment, or a renegotiation of the existing terms. The right route depends on what your contracts say about change of control, jurisdiction, and governing law.</p>

<p>This guide covers the three main methods for transferring contracts, what to look for in your existing agreements, and how to approach clients without spooking them.</p>

<h2>Why You Cannot Just Switch the Invoicing Entity</h2>

<p>Your UK agency is a separate legal person from your new Dubai entity. If you start performing work through the Dubai company but the contract names the UK company, you are in breach. The client can refuse to pay, terminate the agreement, or claim damages.</p>

<p>The problem is worse if your contract has a jurisdiction clause specifying English courts and English law. Moving performance to a Dubai entity creates a practical and legal mess. The client agreed to deal with a UK-registered company subject to UK law. Change that unilaterally, and you have broken the contract.</p>

<p>Most agency founders assume their client relationships are personal enough that the legal entity does not matter. It does. Until you have a dispute, an audit, or a client who decides to enforce their rights.</p>

<h2>What Your Contracts Actually Say</h2>

<p>Before you do anything, pull every client contract you have. Read the following clauses specifically:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Change of control clause.</strong> This gives the client the right to terminate if you sell the company, change ownership, or transfer the contract to another entity. Some are triggered by a change in "effective control" of the agency, which a Dubai relocation can trigger even if you keep the same shareholding structure.</li>
<li><strong>Assignment clause.</strong> This says whether you can transfer the contract to a third party. Many contracts say "no assignment without the client's prior written consent." Some say "no assignment at all."</li>
<li><strong>Jurisdiction and governing law clause.</strong> This specifies which country's courts handle disputes. If it says England and Wales, your Dubai entity cannot rely on it.</li>
<li><strong>Non-compete and non-solicit clauses.</strong> These can prevent you from approaching the client after termination of the UK contract, which complicates a clean handover.</li>
</ul>

<p>If your contract is silent on assignment and change of control, the default position under English law is that you can assign the benefit of the contract (the right to be paid) but not the burden (the obligation to perform the work). That is a problem, because agency work is mostly about performance.</p>

<h2>Method One: Novation</h2>

<p>Novation is the cleanest way to <strong>transfer client contracts to a Dubai entity</strong>. It replaces the UK company with the Dubai company in the contract. All rights and obligations transfer. The client agrees to deal with the new entity from a specified date.</p>

<p>Novation requires a three-way agreement between you (the UK company), the client, and your Dubai entity. It is a formal legal document. It typically includes:</p>

<ul>
<li>A release of the UK company from all future obligations</li>
<li>An assumption of all future obligations by the Dubai entity</li>
<li>The client's consent to the change</li>
<li>A confirmation that existing liabilities remain with the UK company unless agreed otherwise</li>
</ul>

<p>Novation is best for long-term retainer contracts where the relationship is ongoing. It gives the client certainty and protects you from future claims.</p>

<p>The downside: it requires the client to actively sign something. Some clients will refuse. Some will use it as an opportunity to renegotiate pricing or scope. You need to plan for that.</p>

<h2>Method Two: Assignment</h2>

<p>Assignment transfers the benefit of the contract (the right to receive payment) but not the burden (the obligation to do the work). That makes it unsuitable for most agency contracts, where you are obligated to deliver services.</p>

<p>If your contract allows assignment, you can assign the right to invoice and collect payment to your Dubai entity. But you remain liable for performing the work under the UK company. That defeats the purpose of moving operations to Dubai.</p>

<p>In practice, assignment is only useful if you are keeping the UK company alive to perform the work and routing payments through the Dubai entity. That is a more complex structure and creates transfer pricing issues. It is rarely the right answer for a full relocation.</p>

<h2>Method Three: Termination and New Contract</h2>

<p>If your contract has a notice period, you can terminate the UK agreement and offer the client a new contract with your Dubai entity. This is the simplest method legally, but it carries commercial risk.</p>

<p>The client is not obliged to sign the new contract. They can walk away. If you have a strong relationship, they probably will not. But if a competitor is circling, or if the client has procurement rules that require competitive tendering, you could lose the business.</p>

<p>This method also triggers any non-solicit or non-compete clauses in the original contract. Check those carefully before you terminate.</p>

<p>For short-term or project-based contracts, termination and re-signing is often the most practical route. For long-term retainers, novation is safer.</p>

<h2>How to Approach Clients About the Transfer</h2>

<p>Clients do not care about your tax planning. They care about service continuity, data protection, and legal risk. Frame the conversation around those priorities.</p>

<p>Here is a typical approach:</p>

<ol>
<li>Tell them you are relocating to Dubai for personal or operational reasons.</li>
<li>Explain that your Dubai entity will take over the contract to ensure continuity.</li>
<li>Reassure them that the same team, same processes, and same service levels apply.</li>
<li>Confirm that data will be handled in compliance with UK GDPR (which applies extraterritorially).</li>
<li>Ask for their consent to novate the contract or sign a new one.</li>
</ol>

<p>Do not mention tax savings, lower corporate tax rates, or the UAE's 0% personal income tax. That information is irrelevant to the client and creates unnecessary concern.</p>

<p>Give clients a timeline. Most will agree if you handle it professionally. The ones that do not are usually clients you would have lost anyway.</p>

<h2>What Happens to Existing Liabilities</h2>

<p>When you <strong>transfer client contracts to a Dubai entity</strong>, existing liabilities do not automatically move. If you delivered work under the UK contract but invoiced after the transfer, the UK company may still be liable for defects or breaches that occurred before the novation.</p>

<p>Your novation agreement should specify which liabilities stay with the UK company and which pass to the Dubai entity. Typically, pre-transfer liabilities stay with the UK company. Post-transfer liabilities go to the Dubai entity.</p>

<p>If you have any ongoing disputes, warranty claims, or service credits owed, resolve them before the transfer. Otherwise, the client may refuse to sign the novation until the issue is settled.</p>

<h2>Data Protection and GDPR Implications</h2>

<p>If you are moving client data to a Dubai entity, you are transferring personal data outside the UK and EEA. The UAE does not have an adequacy decision from the UK (or the EU). That means you need alternative transfer mechanisms.</p>

<p>The most common solution is the UK International Data Transfer Agreement (IDTA) or standard contractual clauses (SCCs). You will need to sign these with each client before transferring data to your Dubai entity.</p>

<p>If your contracts have data processing clauses, those will need updating too. The client's data processor changes from your UK company to your Dubai entity. That is a material change under UK GDPR.</p>

<p>Get legal advice on this. The ICO can fine you up to £17.5 million or 4% of global turnover for unlawful data transfers. It is not a risk worth taking.</p>

<h2>VAT and Tax Implications of the Transfer</h2>

<p>Transferring contracts to a Dubai entity changes your VAT position. If your Dubai entity has no UK establishment, it cannot register for UK VAT. That means you cannot charge UK VAT on invoices to UK clients.</p>

<p>Instead, the client may need to account for VAT under the reverse charge mechanism if they are a UK business. If the client is a consumer or a non-business, you may need to register for VAT in the UK as a non-resident trader.</p>

<p>On the corporation tax side, the Dubai entity will be subject to UAE corporate tax (9% on profits above AED 375,000 from June 2023). But if the Dubai entity has a UK permanent establishment (a fixed place of business, a UK office, or a UK-based team), those profits are taxable in the UK too.</p>

<h2>What to Do If a Client Refuses</h2>

<p>Some clients will refuse to novate or sign a new contract. Their reasons vary: procurement policy, legal risk aversion, or simply not wanting to deal with a foreign entity.</p>

<p>If a client refuses, you have three options:</p>

<ol>
<li>Keep the UK company alive to service that client. You can run it as a dormant or low-activity company while your Dubai entity handles new business.</li>
<li>Subcontract the work from your Dubai entity to your UK company. The UK company performs the work, the Dubai entity invoices the client. This creates transfer pricing obligations and VAT complications, but it is workable.</li>
<li>Walk away from the client. If the client represents a small portion of your revenue, it may be simpler to terminate and focus on your Dubai operation.</li>
</ol>

<p>Option one is the most common for agency founders with one or two reluctant clients. It keeps the relationship intact and avoids confrontation.</p>

<h2>Legal Advice You Need Before Moving</h2>

<p>Transferring client contracts to a Dubai entity is a legal process, not an accounting one. You need a solicitor who specialises in commercial contracts and international corporate law. Do not rely on templates from the internet.</p>

<p>Specifically, you need advice on:</p>

<ul>
<li>Whether your existing contracts allow novation or assignment</li>
<li>How to draft a novation agreement that protects you from future claims</li>
<li>Data transfer mechanisms under UK GDPR</li>
<li>Whether your Dubai entity creates a UK permanent establishment</li>
<li>How to handle client objections without breaching confidentiality or non-solicit clauses</li>
</ul>

<p>Budget £2,000 to £5,000 for legal fees, depending on how many contracts you have and how complex they are. It is cheaper than defending a breach of contract claim.</p>

<h2>Timeline for a Clean Transfer</h2>

<p>A clean transfer takes 8 to 12 weeks from start to finish. Here is a realistic timeline:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Week 1-2:</strong> Review all client contracts. Identify which have change of control, assignment, and jurisdiction clauses.</li>
<li><strong>Week 3-4:</strong> Get legal advice on the transfer method for each contract.</li>
<li><strong>Week 5-6:</strong> Approach clients. Explain the move. Ask for consent.</li>
<li><strong>Week 7-8:</strong> Draft and sign novation agreements or new contracts.</li>
<li><strong>Week 9-10:</strong> Update data processing agreements and sign IDTA/SCCs.</li>
<li><strong>Week 11-12:</strong> Migrate operations. Close UK company or leave it dormant.</li>
</ul>

<p>If clients drag their feet, the timeline extends. Start early. Do not wait until your UK company is wound up.</p>

<h2>Final Checks Before You Go</h2>

<p>Before you move, make sure you have:</p>

<ul>
<li>Signed novation agreements or new contracts for every client you want to keep</li>
<li>Updated data processing agreements and signed IDTA/SCCs</li>
<li>Closed or transferred your UK company's VAT registration</li>
<li>Filed final corporation tax returns and paid any outstanding liabilities</li>
<li>Transferred intellectual property (brand, website, software) to the Dubai entity</li>
<li>Updated your professional indemnity insurance to cover the Dubai entity</li>
</ul>

<p>If your UK company has a directors' loan account balance, clear it before you leave. HMRC can pursue you personally for unpaid tax even if you are in Dubai.</p>
