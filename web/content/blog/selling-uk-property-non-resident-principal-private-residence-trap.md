<p>You moved to Dubai two years ago. Your UK home, the flat in Shoreditch you bought in 2015, has been rented out since you left. Now you are ready to sell it and use the proceeds to fund your agency's next growth phase. You assume there will be no UK tax to pay because it was your home. That assumption could cost you tens of thousands of pounds.</p>

<p>The rule is called principal private residence relief, or PPR relief. It exempts you from capital gains tax (CGT) on the sale of your main home. But once you become non-resident and rent the property out, HMRC treats those let years differently. The relief does not simply vanish. It gets restricted. And the restriction is often larger than agency founders expect.</p>

<p>Here is how the trap works, how to calculate what you actually owe, and what you can do about it before you exchange contracts.</p>

<h2>What Principal Private Residence Relief Actually Covers</h2>

<p>PPR relief is straightforward when you live in a property and sell it while still living there. The entire gain is exempt from CGT. No tax to pay, no return to file (for UK residents).</p>

<p>But the rules change when you sell UK property as non resident. PPR relief still applies, but only for periods when the property was actually your main home. The key word is "periods."</p>

<p>HMRC looks at your entire ownership period and divides it into three categories:</p>
<ul>
<li>Actual occupation: the years you lived in the property as your main home</li>
<li>Deemed occupation: certain periods HMRC treats as if you lived there, even if you did not</li>
<li>Let periods: years the property was rented out to tenants</li>
</ul>
<p>PPR relief covers the first two categories. It does not cover the third. And that is where the trap springs.</p>

<h2>The Let Period Trap in Detail</h2>

<p>Say you bought a flat in Manchester's Northern Quarter in 2016 for £200,000. You lived there until 2022. Then you moved to Dubai and rented it out. You are now selling in 2025 for £350,000.</p>

<p>Total gain: £150,000. Ownership period: 9 years. Let period: 3 years (2022 to 2025).</p>

<p>On the face of it, you might think 6 years of occupation means 6/9 of the gain is exempt. That is partially correct. But the 3 let years are not exempt. So 3/9 of the gain, or £50,000, is potentially chargeable.</p>

<p>But it gets worse. There is also a final period exemption. For UK residents, the last 9 months of ownership are always treated as deemed occupation, regardless of where you live. For non-residents, that final period is reduced to 0 months. You lose it entirely.</p>

<p>In the example above, that final period exemption would have covered the last 9 months of the let period. Without it, an additional chunk of gain becomes taxable.</p>

<h3>The Lettings Relief That Used to Help</h3>

<p>Until April 2020, there was a separate relief called lettings relief. It could exempt up to £40,000 of the gain attributable to let periods. That relief was abolished for most people. If you rented out your home after 5 April 2020, you cannot use it.</p>

<p>If you rented out before that date and are selling now, you may have some transitional protection. But for most agency founders who moved to the UAE in the last five years, lettings relief is gone.</p>

<h2>How the Tax Bill Builds Up</h2>

<p>Using the same example: £150,000 gain, 3 let years out of 9. The gain attributable to let periods is roughly £50,000. You also lose the final 9 months exemption, adding roughly another £12,500 to the chargeable gain.</p>

<p>Total chargeable gain: £62,500.</p>

<p>As a non-resident, you pay CGT at 18% (basic rate) or 24% (higher rate) on residential property. If you are a higher rate taxpayer in the UK (which most agency founders with significant rental income or dividend income will be), you pay 24%.</p>

<p>£62,500 at 24% = £15,000 tax due.</p>

<p>That is £15,000 you probably did not budget for. And it is due within 60 days of completion, not at the end of the tax year.</p>

<h2>Who Is Most at Risk</h2>

<p>This trap catches three types of agency founder most often:</p>
<ul>
<li>Founders who moved to the UAE and kept their UK home as a rental for 2-5 years before selling</li>
<li>Founders who bought a new UK home, moved out of the old one, and rented the old one before selling</li>
<li>Founders who lived in a property, moved abroad for work, and let the property through a managing agent without realising the tax implications</li>
</ul>
<p>If you fall into any of these categories, you need to calculate your PPR relief restriction before you agree a sale price.</p>

<h2>What You Can Do About It</h2>

<h3>Option 1: Sell Before You Leave</h3>
<p>This is the cleanest solution. If you sell your UK home while you still live in it, PPR relief covers the entire gain. No CGT. No return. No trap. If you are planning a move to the UAE, consider selling first and renting in Dubai for a few months while you find your permanent home.</p>

<h3>Option 2: Move Back In Before Selling</h3>
<p>If you are already abroad, this is harder. But if you can return to the UK and live in the property for a period before selling, you regain PPR relief for that period. The let period gain remains taxable, but the final period exemption reapplies. You need to actually live there, not just visit. HMRC will check utility bills, council tax records, and bank transactions.</p>

<h3>Option 3: Accept the Tax and Plan for It</h3>
<p>Sometimes the numbers still work. If your gain is small relative to the property value, or if you have other capital losses to offset, the CGT bill might be manageable. The key is knowing the number before you exchange contracts, not after.</p>

<h2>Filing Requirements for Non-Residents</h2>

<p>When you sell UK property as non resident, you must report the sale to HMRC within 60 days. This is a separate filing from your annual self-assessment tax return. You do it through the Capital Gains Tax on UK Property account on HMRC's website.</p>

<p>You also need to include the sale on your annual SA100 return, even if you are non-resident. Missing the 60-day deadline triggers automatic penalties: £100 initially, then more if you are more than 6 months late.</p>

<p>If you are a UAE resident for tax purposes (which most agency founders are, given the 0% personal tax rate), you still file a UK return. Non-residence does not mean no filing obligation. It means you only report UK-source gains and income.</p>

<h2>How to Calculate Your PPR Relief Restriction</h2>

<p>You can do this yourself using HMRC's calculator, but it is easy to get wrong. The key inputs are:</p>
<ul>
<li>Date of purchase and purchase price</li>
<li>Date you moved out (actual occupation ends)</li>
<li>Dates of any let periods</li>
<li>Date you became non-resident for UK tax purposes</li>
<li>Sale date and sale price</li>
<li>Costs of acquisition and disposal (legal fees, estate agent, stamp duty)</li>
<li>Capital improvements (not repairs)</li>
</ul>
<p>The calculation splits the gain by months, applies PPR relief to qualifying months, and then applies the final period exemption (if you qualify). It is a formula, not a judgement call. But getting the months right matters. A single month error can change the tax bill by hundreds of pounds.</p>

<h2>What If You Let the Property to a Family Member?</h2>

<p>Some founders let their UK home to a parent or sibling at a reduced rent, thinking this avoids the let period trap. It does not. HMRC treats any letting at less than market rate as a non-commercial arrangement. The property is still considered let, and PPR relief is still restricted on those periods.</p>

<p>The only exception is if the family member lives rent-free and you have not claimed any rental income. In that case, HMRC may treat the period as deemed occupation, but only if you can show the property was genuinely unavailable for commercial letting. This is a grey area. Do not rely on it without professional advice.</p>

<h2>Interaction with the 90-Day Rule</h2>

<p>If you are a UAE resident but spend more than 90 days in the UK in a tax year, you risk becoming UK resident again under the Statutory Residence Test. If that happens, your PPR relief calculation changes. You may regain the final period exemption, but you also become liable to UK tax on your worldwide income and gains.</p>

<p>For agency founders running a business from Dubai while visiting the UK frequently, this is a real risk. Keep a diary of days spent in the UK. If you are close to 90 days, consider reducing your visits or restructuring your travel.</p>

<h2>Should You Use a Holding Company for UK Property?</h2>

<p>Some founders ask whether holding UK property through a limited company avoids the PPR trap. It does not. Companies do not qualify for PPR relief at all. If you own a UK property through a company, you pay corporation tax on the gain at 25% (assuming profits above £250k), and then additional tax when you extract the proceeds as dividends.</p>

<p>For a property that was your home, individual ownership is almost always better for tax purposes. The PPR relief trap exists, but the tax rate on the chargeable gain (18-24%) is still lower than the effective rate through a company (25% corporation tax plus dividend tax).</p>

<p>If you are considering a holding company structure for multiple properties or your agency, speak to an accountant who understands both UK property tax and agency structures. As ICAEW qualified accountants, we see founders make this mistake regularly.</p>

<h2>Practical Steps Before You Sell</h2>
<ol>
<li>Get a valuation of the property at the date you moved out. This helps split the gain between your occupation period and the let period.</li>
<li>Gather all records of capital improvements: new kitchen, new boiler, extension, loft conversion. These reduce the gain.</li>
<li>Check your UK day count for the last 6 tax years. If you have exceeded 90 days in any year, your residence status may be in question.</li>
<li>Calculate the estimated CGT bill using HMRC's online calculator or ask your accountant to run the numbers.</li>
<li>Set aside the expected tax in a separate account. The 60-day payment window is tight.</li>
</ol>
<p>If your contractor mix or personal circumstances have changed in the last 12 months, ask your accountant before year-end. The PPR trap is one of those problems that is easy to fix before you exchange contracts and impossible to fix after.</p>

<h2>Final Warning</h2>
<p>The principal private residence trap is not a niche issue. It affects every agency founder who moves abroad, keeps their UK home, rents it out, and sells later. HMRC collects millions in unexpected CGT this way every year. Do not be one of the founders who discovers the bill after completion.</p>
<p>If you are planning to sell UK property as non resident, get a professional calculation done before you list the property. The cost of advice is a fraction of the tax you might overpay or the penalties you might incur.</p>
