<p>Most agency founders I meet have no idea what they should be paying their accountant. They got a quote when they incorporated, accepted it, and have been paying the same monthly fee for three years. Meanwhile their agency has gone from a solo operation to a 15-person shop billing £1.2m. And the fee? Still the same flat rate they agreed when they were turning over £80k.</p>

<p>That is a mistake. Your <strong>agency accountant cost</strong> should reflect what you actually need, not what a standard pricing grid says. And here is the thing: most accountants will negotiate if you ask the right way. They just will not volunteer the discount.</p>

<p>This guide covers exactly how to approach that conversation. What drives the fee. What you can cut. What you should never cut. And the specific numbers you need to have ready before you pick up the phone.</p>

<h2>What Actually Drives Your Agency Accountant Cost</h2>

<p>Before you negotiate, you need to understand what you are paying for. Most agency accountants charge based on a combination of five factors:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Revenue turnover</strong> — higher turnover means more transactions, more VAT work, more complexity in the P&L</li>
<li><strong>Headcount and payroll</strong> — every employee means RTI submissions, pension auto-enrolment, P11Ds for benefits, and employer NI calculations</li>
<li><strong>Contractor volume</strong> — IR35 determinations, SDS paperwork, and contractor payment runs add time</li>
<li><strong>Transaction volume</strong> — a retainer-heavy agency with 20 monthly invoices is cheaper to manage than a project agency doing 200 invoices a month with variable billing</li>
<li><strong>Service scope</strong> — management accounts, cash flow forecasting, quarterly reviews, and exit planning all cost extra</li>
</ul>

<p>If your agency is a straightforward limited company with three employees, a handful of retainers, and no contractors, your <strong>agency accountant cost</strong> should be at the lower end. If you are running a 30-person digital agency with 15 contractors, multiple entities, and a complex group structure, you will pay more. That is fair.</p>

<p>What is not fair is paying the higher rate when your situation is simple.</p>

<h2>Typical Agency Accountant Cost Ranges for 2025/26</h2>

<p>These are real numbers from the market, not theoretical ranges. They vary by location (London firms charge more than Manchester or Bristol firms) and by specialism. An ICAEW qualified firm that works exclusively with agencies will charge differently from a general practice that handles corner shops and agencies alike.</p>

<p><strong>Solo agency founder (turnover £50k-£150k)</strong><br>
Monthly fee: £150-£300 per month<br>
Includes: year-end accounts, corporation tax return, personal tax return, basic VAT, payroll for one director</p>

<p><strong>Small agency (2-5 people, turnover £150k-£500k)</strong><br>
Monthly fee: £350-£600 per month<br>
Includes: everything above plus payroll for staff, basic management accounts, VAT, contractor IR35 support</p>

<p><strong>Growing agency (6-15 people, turnover £500k-£1.5m)</strong><br>
Monthly fee: £600-£1,200 per month<br>
Includes: full management accounts, quarterly reviews, cash flow forecasting, multi-entity if needed, more complex IR35 work</p>

<p><strong>Established agency (15+ people, turnover £1.5m+)</strong><br>
Monthly fee: £1,200-£2,500+ per month<br>
Includes: group structures, consolidated accounts, exit planning, BADR structuring, full financial director support</p>

<p>These are starting points. Your actual fee depends on the specific factors we covered above.</p>

<h2>How to Negotiate: The Five-Step Process</h2>

<p>Here is the practical process. Do this before your next renewal or before you switch firms.</p>

<h3>Step 1: Audit What You Actually Use</h3>

<p>Pull your last 12 months of invoices from your accountant. Next to each line item, mark whether you actually used that service. Did you have quarterly review calls, or did they get cancelled? Did you receive management accounts, or did they go straight to your spam folder?</p>

<p>If you are paying for services you do not use, that is the first thing to cut. Many accountants bundle services into a fixed monthly fee. If you never take the quarterly call, ask to drop to a lower tier.</p>

<h3>Step 2: Know Your Numbers</h3>

<p>Before you call, have these ready:</p>

<ul>
<li>Your current annual turnover</li>
<li>Your headcount (employees and contractors separately)</li>
<li>Your average monthly transaction count (invoices in and out)</li>
<li>Whether you use accounting software they support (Xero, QuickBooks, FreeAgent)</li>
<li>Whether you do your own bookkeeping or they do it</li>
</ul>

<p>Accountants price based on risk and time. If you can demonstrate that your agency is low-risk (clean bookkeeping, no HMRC queries, no late filings) and low-time (you do the bookkeeping, you use automated software), you have leverage.</p>

<h3>Step 3: Benchmark Against Similar Agencies</h3>

<p>Ask three other agency founders what they pay. Not your competitors — founders in different niches. A PR agency founder with 8 people in Manchester. A web design agency founder with 5 people in Bristol. A recruitment agency founder with 12 people in the Northern Quarter.</p>

<p>If you are paying £900 a month and similar-sized agencies are paying £550, you have a data point. Use it. Say "I have spoken to three other agency founders at similar scale and they are paying between £500 and £650. Can you explain why my fee is higher?"</p>

<p>This works. Accountants know the market. If they cannot justify the difference, they will often adjust.</p>

<h3>Step 4: Negotiate the Scope, Not Just the Price</h3>

<p>Do not just ask for a discount. That feels confrontational and rarely works. Instead, ask to restructure the scope. Say something like:</p>

<p>"I am looking at my fee for next year. I want to stay with you, but I need to bring the cost down. Can we look at what services I actually need versus what I am paying for?"</p>

<p>This opens a collaborative conversation. You might agree to:</p>

<ul>
<li>Drop quarterly management accounts to half-yearly (saves £100-£200 per month)</li>
<li>Handle your own VAT returns (saves £50-£100 per month if you use Xero or QuickBooks)</li>
<li>Move from monthly payroll to quarterly payroll for directors only (saves £30-£50 per month)</li>
<li>Reduce contractor IR35 support if your contractor mix has changed</li>
</ul>

<p>If your agency has grown, you might actually need more services, not fewer. That is fine. But you should be paying for what you use, not a bundle that includes things you do not need.</p>

<h3>Step 5: Ask About Multi-Year or Referral Discounts</h3>

<p>Some accountants offer a discount if you commit to a two-year engagement. Others offer a referral credit if you introduce another agency founder. These are not standard, but they exist. Ask.</p>

<p>If you are a clean, low-maintenance client who pays on time and provides organised records, you are valuable. Good accountants know this. They would rather keep you at a slightly lower fee than lose you to a competitor.</p>

<h2>What You Should Never Cut From Your Agency Accountant Cost</h2>

<p>Negotiating is smart. Cutting the wrong things is not. Here is what you should keep, no matter what:</p>

<p><strong>Corporation tax compliance.</strong> Filing your CT600 late or incorrectly triggers penalties that dwarf any saving. This is non-negotiable.</p>

<p><strong>VAT compliance.</strong> If you are VAT registered, your returns must be accurate and on time. HMRC's Making Tax Digital (MTD) system flags errors automatically now. Do not risk it.</p>

<p><strong>IR35 support if you use contractors.</strong> If you have contractors and your agency is medium or large, you are responsible for issuing Status Determination Statements. Get this wrong and you face the tax bill plus penalties. Our <a href="/blog/contractors-and-ir35">IR35 guidance for agencies</a> covers this in detail.</p>

<p><strong>Year-end accounts and filing.</strong> Your statutory accounts must be filed at Companies House and HMRC. This is the core service. Do not try to do this yourself unless you have accounting experience.</p>

<p><strong>Exit planning if you are within 3-5 years of selling.</strong> If you plan to sell your agency, the structure decisions you make now determine whether you pay 14% CGT under BADR or 24% standard CGT. A good accountant saves you tens of thousands. Do not cut this.</p>

<h2>When to Switch Firms Instead of Negotiating</h2>

<p>Sometimes negotiation does not work. If your accountant refuses to adjust, or if their service quality is poor, it may be time to move. Signs it is time to switch:</p>

<ul>
<li>They take more than two weeks to respond to emails</li>
<li>They do not proactively flag tax planning opportunities</li>
<li>They have no experience with agency-specific issues like IR35, utilisation rates, or retainer accounting</li>
<li>Their fee has increased without explanation</li>
<li>You have outgrown their expertise</li>
</ul>

<p>If you switch, look for an ICAEW qualified firm that works exclusively with agencies. General practice accountants are fine for a corner shop. They are not fine for a growing agency with contractors, multiple revenue streams, and an exit plan. Our <a href="/services">agency accounting services</a> page explains what specialist support looks like.</p>

<h2>The One Thing That Changes Everything: Your Bookkeeping Quality</h2>

<p>Here is a truth most accountants will not tell you directly: the single biggest factor in your <strong>agency accountant cost</strong> is the state of your bookkeeping.</p>

<p>If you send your accountant a messy shoebox of receipts, bank statements with uncategorised transactions, and a spreadsheet that does not reconcile, they will charge you more. They have to. It takes hours to clean up.</p>

<p>If you use Xero or QuickBooks, reconcile your bank feed weekly, categorise transactions correctly, and upload receipts using Dext, your accountant's time drops by 50-70%. That saving should flow back to you.</p>

<p>Ask your accountant: "If I improve my bookkeeping to X standard, can you reduce my fee by Y?" Most will say yes. They would rather spend their time on tax planning than data entry.</p>

<h2>Real Example: How a 12-Person Digital Agency Cut Their Fee by 30%</h2>

<p>A digital agency in Soho, turning over £850k with 12 staff and 8 contractors, was paying £1,100 per month. They had been with their accountant for three years without a review.</p>

<p>They audited their usage and found they were paying for quarterly management accounts they never read, monthly payroll processing for directors who were on fixed dividends anyway, and IR35 support for contractors who were all inside IR35 on a blanket determination (which was wrong, but that is a separate issue).</p>

<p>They called their accountant and proposed:</p>

<ul>
<li>Drop to half-yearly management accounts (saved £150/month)</li>
<li>Move payroll to quarterly (saved £50/month)</li>
<li>Handle their own VAT returns using Xero (saved £80/month)</li>
<li>Move to a fixed-fee retainer with no automatic annual increase (saved £50/month)</li>
</ul>

<p>Total saving: £330 per month, or nearly £4,000 per year. The accountant agreed because the client was organised, paid on time, and had been with them for three years.</p>

<p>That is the model. Audit, propose, negotiate.</p>

<p>Do the audit. Know your numbers. Benchmark against peers. Negotiate the scope, not just the price. And never cut the services that protect your business from HMRC penalties or missed tax planning opportunities.</p>
