<p>If you are a UK agency founder moving to Dubai, the first question is not about tax rates. It is about whether you can keep trading with your existing UK clients without a local distributor. That is a daily practical concern, not a theoretical one.</p>

<p>The choice between a free zone company and a mainland company in the UAE determines exactly that. Get it wrong and you could lose clients, create contractual headaches, or face unexpected tax bills in the UK. Get it right and you operate as normal, just from a different time zone.</p>

<p>This guide explains the difference in plain English. No fluff. Just what you need to know to make the right call for your agency.</p>

<h2>What Changes When You Move to Dubai?</h2>
<p>When you relocate to Dubai as a UK agency founder, your business structure changes at a fundamental level. Your UK limited company may stay open, or you may close it and trade through a new UAE entity. Either way, your client contracts need to be honoured.</p>

<p>The UAE offers two primary company structures for foreign founders: free zone and mainland. Each has different rules about who you can trade with, how you invoice, and whether you need a local partner.</p>

<p>For agency founders, the critical distinction is this: can your UAE company invoice a UK client directly, or do you need a local distributor or agent in the UAE to do so?</p>

<h2>Free Zone Company: The Basics</h2>
<p>A free zone company is set up in one of Dubai's designated economic zones. Examples include Dubai Multi Commodities Centre (DMCC), Dubai Silicon Oasis, and Jebel Ali Free Zone. Each free zone has its own regulator and rules.</p>

<p>Free zone companies are designed for businesses that trade internationally. They allow 100% foreign ownership, zero corporate tax (currently, though UAE corporate tax at 9% applies from June 2023 for profits above AED 375,000), and no requirement for a local UAE partner.</p>

<p>But here is the catch for agency founders: most free zones restrict you to trading <strong>outside</strong> the UAE. You cannot typically trade directly with UAE-based clients from a free zone company. That is not your problem if your clients are in the UK, Europe, or the US. It becomes a problem if you want to pick up local UAE clients too.</p>

<h3>Can a Free Zone Company Invoice UK Clients Directly?</h3>
<p>Yes. A free zone company can invoice UK clients directly. There is no requirement for a local distributor or agent in the UAE. You issue an invoice from your free zone company to the UK client, they pay you, and you account for the income in your free zone company's books.</p>

<p>This is the standard model for UK agency founders moving to Dubai. You keep your existing client relationships, your contracts are assigned to the new entity (with the client's consent), and you carry on working. The only difference is your company's registered address changes to a Dubai free zone.</p>

<h3>What About VAT and UK Tax?</h3>
<p>Your free zone company is not UK-resident for tax purposes. It is a UAE tax resident. That means you do not pay UK corporation tax on the profits of the free zone company. You pay UAE corporate tax (currently 9% above AED 375,000 profit).</p>

<p>UK VAT is a separate matter. If your UK clients are VAT-registered businesses, they will likely want a valid VAT invoice. Your free zone company can issue invoices without UK VAT, provided the supply is outside the scope of UK VAT. But you must check the specific VAT treatment of your services. For most agency services (marketing, web design, PR), the place of supply is where the customer belongs. If the customer is UK-based and VAT-registered, the supply is outside the scope of UK VAT but the customer may need to account for reverse charge VAT. This is a technical area. Get advice from a firm like ours that understands both UK and UAE VAT.</p>

<h2>Mainland Company: The Basics</h2>
<p>A mainland company is a UAE company registered with the Department of Economic Development (DED) in Dubai. It allows you to trade anywhere in the UAE, including directly with local clients, and also internationally.</p>

<p>Historically, mainland companies required a UAE national as a local partner holding 51% of the shares. That changed in 2021. Now, mainland companies in most sectors allow 100% foreign ownership. But there are still nuances, particularly around professional services licences.</p>

<p>For agency founders, a mainland company gives you more flexibility. You can trade with UK clients directly, and you can also trade with UAE-based clients. That matters if you plan to build a local client base alongside your UK work.</p>

<h3>Can a Mainland Company Invoice UK Clients Directly?</h3>
<p>Yes, absolutely. A mainland company can invoice UK clients directly. There is no requirement for a local distributor. You issue the invoice from your mainland company, the UK client pays you, and you account for the income in your mainland company's books.</p>

<p>The difference from a free zone is that a mainland company has no restriction on trading within the UAE. So if you decide to pitch to a Dubai-based company six months after moving, you can do so without setting up a second entity.</p>

<h3>What About the Local Partner Requirement?</h3>
<p>For most agency activities (management consultancy, marketing, PR, web design), you can now own 100% of a mainland company. The old 51% local sponsor requirement has been removed for over 1,000 commercial activities. But you may still need a local service agent for certain administrative tasks, and some activities still require a local partner. Check your specific licence category before proceeding.</p>

<h2>Which Structure Is Right for You?</h2>
<p>The answer depends on your specific circumstances. Let me give you three scenarios.</p>

<h3>Scenario 1: You Have Only UK Clients and No Plans for UAE Clients</h3>
<p>You run a 12-person digital agency billing £800k per year. All your clients are in London, Manchester, and Bristol. You are moving to Dubai for lifestyle reasons but will continue working with the same clients remotely.</p>
<p><strong>Verdict: Free zone is the right choice.</strong> You get 100% ownership, lower setup and renewal costs, and no restriction on trading with UK clients. The fact you cannot trade with UAE clients does not matter because you have no intention of doing so.</p>

<h3>Scenario 2: You Have UK Clients and Want to Build a UAE Client Base</h3>
<p>You run a creative agency with a retainer book of UK clients. You are moving to Dubai and want to start pitching to local UAE companies within the first year.</p>
<p><strong>Verdict: Mainland is the better choice.</strong> You can keep your UK contracts and also trade directly with UAE clients. A free zone would require a second entity to service UAE clients, which adds cost and complexity.</p>

<h3>Scenario 3: You Are a Sole Trader Web Designer Turning Over £65k</h3>
<p>You have three UK retainer clients and some project work. You are moving to Dubai and will continue working from a co-working space. You have no plans to hire locally.</p>
<p><strong>Verdict: Free zone is the right choice.</strong> The lower setup costs and flexi-desk options suit a smaller operation. You can always upgrade to a mainland company later if your circumstances change.</p>

<h2>What Happens to Your Existing UK Contracts?</h2>
<p>This is where many agency founders trip up. You cannot simply start invoicing from your new UAE company without telling your UK clients. Your existing contracts are with your UK limited company. To move them to your UAE entity, you need to:</p>

<ol>
<li>Review each contract for assignment clauses. Some contracts prohibit assignment without the client's written consent.</li>
<li>Notify your clients in advance. A professional email explaining the change and reassuring them that service levels will not drop is essential.</li>
<li>Get written consent from each client where required. Do this before you issue the first invoice from your UAE company.</li>
<li>Close the UK company properly. If you are winding up your UK company, follow the correct process (Members' Voluntary Liquidation or striking off) to avoid future liabilities.</li>
<li>Check your UK personal tax position. If you remain UK resident for part of the tax year, your worldwide income may still be subject to UK tax. The statutory residence test determines this.</li>
</ol>

<p>As ICAEW qualified accountants working with agency founders, we see clients who skip step 2 and end up with angry clients or contractual disputes. Do not be that person.</p>

<h2>What About UK Tax on the UAE Company's Profits?</h2>
<p>This is a common concern. The short answer is: if you are genuinely non-UK resident and your UAE company is managed and controlled in the UAE, the profits are not subject to UK corporation tax. The UAE company pays UAE corporate tax on its profits.</p>

<p>But HMRC looks closely at "management and control" tests. If you are physically in Dubai but making all key decisions from a laptop in a London coffee shop, HMRC may argue the company is UK-resident. The practical test is where board meetings are held, where strategic decisions are made, and where the company's bank accounts are operated.</p>

<p>For most agency founders genuinely relocating to Dubai, this is straightforward. Keep your business operations in the UAE, hold board meetings there, and maintain a physical presence. Do not try to be a "Dubai tax resident" while living in Surrey.</p>

<h2>Bank Account Opening: A Practical Headache</h2>
<p>Both free zone and mainland companies face similar challenges when opening a UAE bank account. Banks want to see a physical presence, a clear business plan, and evidence of your client contracts. They also want to understand the source of funds.</p>

<p>For agency founders with UK clients, the process is usually smoother than for businesses with unknown international clients. Your UK client contracts serve as evidence of legitimate business activity. Keep them ready in digital format.</p>

<p>Expect the process to take 4-8 weeks. Some free zones offer banking support as part of their setup package. Use it.</p>

<h2>Visa and Residency: Both Structures Work</h2>
<p>Both free zone and mainland companies can sponsor your UAE residency visa. The process is similar. You will need a tenancy contract (or Ejari), your company licence, and a medical test. The visa is typically valid for 2-3 years.</p>

<p>For agency founders, the key point is that your visa is tied to your company. If you close the company, you lose the visa. Plan accordingly.</p>

<h2>Making the Decision</h2>
<p>Here is my recommendation for most UK agency founders moving to Dubai:</p>

<ul>
<li>If you have only UK clients and no plans for UAE clients: <strong>Free zone</strong>. Lower cost, simpler setup, and you keep your existing contracts.</li>
<li>If you have UK clients and want UAE clients: <strong>Mainland</strong>. More flexibility, and you avoid the cost of a second entity later.</li>
<li>If you are unsure: <strong>Start with a free zone</strong>. You can always convert to a mainland company later (though it is not a simple process). The free zone gives you time to understand the market.</li>
</ul>

<p>Whichever structure you choose, the answer to the core question is the same: you can keep your existing UK client contracts. Neither structure requires a local distributor. The difference is about flexibility and cost, not capability.</p>
