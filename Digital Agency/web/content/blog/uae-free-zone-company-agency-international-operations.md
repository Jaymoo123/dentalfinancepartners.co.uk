<p>If you run a UK agency and your client base is shifting international, or you are spending significant time in the Middle East, a UAE free zone company might be on your radar. The pitch is attractive: zero corporation tax, zero personal income tax, and a base in Dubai or Abu Dhabi that signals credibility to clients in the region.</p>

<p>But the gap between "it sounds good" and "it works for my agency" is wider than most founders expect. I have sat with agency owners who moved too fast, triggered UK tax charges they did not see coming, or set up structures that HMRC later challenged. The setup itself is straightforward. The UK tax and substance requirements are where it gets complicated.</p>

<p>This guide covers what a UAE free zone company actually involves, how it interacts with your UK tax position, and what you need to have in place before you register. I am writing this as an ICAEW qualified accountant who works with agency founders. None of this is personal advice. Your situation will differ. But these are the questions you need answers to before you spend a penny on setup fees.</p>

<h2>What Is a UAE Free Zone Company?</h2>

<p>A UAE free zone is a designated economic area where businesses can operate with 100% foreign ownership, zero corporate tax (for now), and simplified visa processes. There are over 40 free zones in the UAE. The most relevant for agency founders are Dubai Multi Commodities Centre (DMCC), Dubai Internet City, Abu Dhabi Global Market (ADGM), and Sharjah Research Technology and Innovation Park.</p>

<p>Each free zone issues a licence that allows you to conduct specific activities. For agency founders, the relevant licence categories are typically "consulting", "media", "technology", or "general trading" depending on what your agency actually does. A digital agency billing for strategy and creative work would typically use a consulting or media licence. A web development shop might use a technology licence.</p>

<p>The key features of a UAE free zone company are straightforward:</p>

<ul>
<li>100% foreign ownership, no local sponsor required</li>
<li>Zero corporate tax on qualifying income (though this is changing from 2023 onwards, which I will cover below)</li>
<li>Zero personal income tax on salary and dividends taken from the company</li>
<li>Ability to sponsor your own residency visa and family visas</li>
<li>No currency controls, you can move money in and out freely</li>
<li>Physical or flexi-desk office space as part of the licence package</li>
</ul>

<p>That sounds like a clean deal. And for some agency founders, it genuinely works. But the tax treatment in the UK and the substance requirements in the UAE are where most people get unstuck.</p>

<h2>Why Would a UK Agency Founder Want a UAE Free Zone Company?</h2>

<p>The most common reasons I see from agency founders are these:</p>

<p><strong>International client base.</strong> If 60% or more of your agency's revenue comes from clients outside the UK, the argument for basing yourself somewhere tax-neutral gets stronger. You are not using UK infrastructure to generate that income. Why pay UK corporation tax on it?</p>

<p><strong>Time spent in the UAE.</strong> Some founders already spend three to six months per year in Dubai for lifestyle, family, or health reasons. If you are there that much anyway, structuring your agency around a UAE entity makes practical sense.</p>

<p><strong>Tax efficiency on dividends.</strong> UK dividend tax rates are 8.75% at basic rate, 33.75% at higher rate, and 39.35% at additional rate. If you are taking six-figure dividends from your UK agency, the tax bill is material. A UAE structure removes that layer entirely, provided you are genuinely non-UK resident and operating from the UAE.</p>

<p><strong>Exit planning.</strong> Selling a UK company triggers capital gains tax at 14% under BADR (up to £1m) or 20% above that. Selling a UAE free zone company can be structured differently, and some founders use a UAE holding company as part of a wider exit strategy.</p>

<p>These are legitimate reasons. But they all depend on one thing: that you actually move your tax residence and your agency's management to the UAE. You cannot keep living in Shoreditch, commuting to a WeWork in Soho, and claim your company is run from a flexi-desk in Dubai Internet City. HMRC will see through that.</p>

<h2>UK Tax Implications: The Part Most Founders Miss</h2>

<p>Setting up a UAE free zone company does not automatically mean you stop paying UK tax. The UK taxes on two bases: residence and source. If you are UK resident, you pay UK tax on your worldwide income. If your company is UK tax resident, it pays UK corporation tax on its worldwide profits.</p>

<h3>Your Personal Tax Residence</h3>

<p>To stop being UK resident, you need to leave the UK and meet the Statutory Residence Test (SRT). The SRT is a set of rules that count your days in the UK, your ties to the UK (family, accommodation, work, and so on), and whether you are working abroad full-time.</p>

<p>For agency founders, the key thresholds are these:</p>

<ul>
<li>If you spend 183 days or more in the UK in a tax year, you are UK resident. Full stop.</li>
<li>If you spend fewer than 16 days in the UK (and were not UK resident in any of the previous three years), you are non-resident.</li>
<li>If you spend between 16 and 182 days, the tie-breaker tests apply. The most relevant for agency founders is the "full-time work abroad" test. You need to work at least 35 hours per week overseas, with fewer than 31 days of UK work in the tax year, and fewer than 91 days spent in the UK overall.</li>
</ul>

<p>If you do not meet these tests, you remain UK resident. That means your UAE free zone company's dividends and salary are taxable in the UK at your marginal rates. The zero-tax benefit of the UAE disappears.</p>

<h3>Your Company's Tax Residence</h3>

<p>Even if you become non-UK resident personally, your UAE free zone company might still be UK tax resident. The UK determines corporate residence by where the company's central management and control actually sits. That is a facts-and-circumstances test.</p>

<p>If you are the sole director and you make all strategic decisions from your laptop in a coffee shop in Manchester, the company is UK resident. HMRC will look at where board meetings happen, where bank mandates are signed, where client contracts are negotiated, and where the key decisions are made. If the answer is "the UK", the company pays UK corporation tax at 19-25% on its worldwide profits.</p>

<p>To avoid this, you need genuine substance in the UAE. That means a physical office (not just a virtual desk), local bank accounts, local directors (or at least local meetings), and a clear paper trail showing that management happens in the UAE.</p>

<h2>Substance Requirements: What You Actually Need in Place</h2>

<p>The UAE itself has tightened its substance requirements in recent years. The Economic Substance Regulations (ESR) apply to free zone companies that carry on "relevant activities". For agency founders, the relevant activity is typically "headquarters" or "consulting".</p>

<p>Under ESR, your UAE free zone company must:</p>

<ul>
<li>Be directed and managed in the UAE (board meetings held there, minutes kept locally)</li>
<li>Have adequate physical presence (office space, equipment, staff)</li>
<li>Incur adequate operating expenditure in the UAE</li>
<li>Conduct its core income-generating activities in the UAE</li>
</ul>

<p>If you fail to meet these requirements, you face penalties. First offence: AED 50,000 (about £10,500). Repeat offences: AED 400,000 (about £85,000) and possible licence suspension.</p>

<p>For a one-person agency founder, meeting substance requirements is difficult. You need a physical office. You need to be physically present for a meaningful amount of time. You need to show that the company's business is genuinely run from the UAE, not just invoiced from there.</p>

<p>This is where the "flexi-desk" trap catches people. Many free zones offer a basic package that includes a virtual desk and a PO box. That does not meet ESR requirements. If HMRC or the UAE authorities investigate, you have no substance to point to.</p>

<h2>Corporate Tax in the UAE: The 2023 Change</h2>

<p>From June 2023, the UAE introduced a federal corporate tax of 9% on taxable profits exceeding AED 375,000 (about £80,000). Profits below that threshold are taxed at 0%. Free zone companies that meet certain conditions can still benefit from a 0% rate on qualifying income under the "Qualifying Free Zone Person" regime.</p>

<p>To qualify for the 0% rate, your free zone company must:</p>

<ul>
<li>Have adequate substance in the UAE</li>
<li>Derive qualifying income (broadly, income from other free zone entities or foreign sources)</li>
<li>Not have a permanent establishment in mainland UAE</li>
<li>Comply with transfer pricing rules</li>
</ul>

<p>For most agency founders, the 0% rate will still apply if your clients are outside the UAE and you meet the substance test. But this is not automatic. You need to file a corporate tax return and demonstrate compliance. The days of "zero tax, no paperwork" are over.</p>

<h2>Step-by-Step: Setting Up a UAE Free Zone Company for Your Agency</h2>

<p>If you have read the above and still want to proceed, here is the practical process. I am assuming you are setting up a single-owner agency structure.</p>

<h3>Step 1: Choose the Right Free Zone</h3>

<p>DMCC is the most popular for agency founders. It is well-regarded, has a strong reputation, and its licence covers consulting, media, and technology activities. ADGM is more suited to financial services or holding companies. Dubai Internet City is good for tech-focused agencies. Sharjah RTP is cheaper but less established.</p>

<p>Speak to a UAE-based corporate service provider who works with UK founders. They will know which free zone matches your activity and budget.</p>

<h3>Step 2: Decide on Licence Type</h3>

<p>For a marketing or creative agency, a "consulting" or "media" licence is standard. For a web development or SEO agency, "technology" may be more appropriate. The licence defines what you can invoice for. If your agency does strategy, creative, and development, you may need a licence that covers all three, or you may need to set up two entities.</p>

<h3>Step 3: Register the Company</h3>

<p>You submit your passport copy, business plan, and licence application to the free zone authority. Approval typically takes 5-10 working days. You pay the licence fee, rent for your physical office (or flexi-desk), and visa processing fees. Total first-year costs for a basic setup are usually between £3,000 and £6,000, depending on the free zone and office size.</p>

<h3>Step 4: Open a UAE Bank Account</h3>

<p>This is often the hardest part. UAE banks have tightened compliance significantly. You will need your company incorporation documents, proof of your business activity, invoices or contracts with clients, and a personal visit to the bank. Some free zones offer banking concierge services. Expect the process to take 2-6 weeks.</p>

<p>You also need to consider how money moves between your UAE company and your UK personal account. If you are non-UK resident, you can transfer dividends and salary freely. If you remain UK resident, those transfers are taxable.</p>

<h3>Step 5: Apply for Your Residency Visa</h3>

<p>Your free zone company sponsors your residency visa. You need to complete a medical test, obtain an Emirates ID, and get your passport stamped. The visa is typically valid for 2-3 years and is renewable. You must be physically in the UAE for the medical and biometrics.</p>

<h3>Step 6: Structure Your UK Exit</h3>

<p>If you are moving your tax residence, you need to plan the transition. You should:</p>

<ul>
<li>Notify HMRC of your departure using form P85</li>
<li>Settle any outstanding UK tax liabilities</li>
<li>Consider whether your UK agency company should be closed, sold, or left dormant</li>
<li>Review your UK property and investment holdings (non-residents pay different tax rates on UK rental income and capital gains)</li>
</ul>

<p>If you keep your UK agency trading, you need to manage the transfer of clients and contracts to the UAE entity. This is not a simple invoice-switch. HMRC will look at whether the economic activity has genuinely moved.</p>

<h2>Common Mistakes I See Agency Founders Make</h2>

<p><strong>Setting up the UAE company first, asking questions later.</strong> I have had founders call me after they have paid the setup fee and visa costs, only to discover they cannot meet the substance requirements or that their UK tax position is worse. Do the planning first.</p>

<p><strong>Assuming a flexi-desk is enough.</strong> It is not. Not for ESR, not for HMRC, not for the UAE corporate tax regime. You need a physical office you actually use.</p>

<p><strong>Keeping UK residency while running a UAE company.</strong> If you spend more than 90 days in the UK, work from UK coffee shops, and have your family in London, you are UK resident. Your UAE company is a tax problem, not a solution.</p>

<p><strong>Ignoring transfer pricing.</strong> If your UAE company charges your UK company for services, the price must be at arm's length. HMRC can challenge inflated management fees or royalty payments. Keep proper transfer pricing documentation.</p>

<p><strong>Thinking the UAE is a tax-free haven with no compliance.</strong> It is not. You need to file corporate tax returns, maintain proper accounting records, and comply with ESR. The compliance burden is lower than the UK, but it is real.</p>

<h2>When a UAE Free Zone Company Makes Sense</h2>

<p>Here is where I see it work well:</p>

<ul>
<li>You genuinely relocate to the UAE for 183+ days per year</li>
<li>Your agency's clients are predominantly outside the UK</li>
<li>You have a physical office and local bank account in the UAE</li>
<li>You are prepared to file tax returns in both jurisdictions (UAE and UK) for at least the first year</li>
<li>You have a clear plan for your UK company (close, sell, or leave dormant)</li>
</ul>

<p>If you tick all five boxes, a UAE free zone company can be a legitimate and tax-efficient structure for your agency's international operations. If you tick three or fewer, it is probably not right for you.</p>
