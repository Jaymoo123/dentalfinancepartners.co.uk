<p>Making Tax Digital for Income Tax (MTD for ITSA) is coming. If you are an agency founder drawing dividends from your own company, the way you record those dividends is about to change. It is not just about declaring the total on your tax return any more. HMRC will expect to see digital records of each dividend payment, held in compatible software, and submitted quarterly.</p>

<p>This article covers exactly what you need to track for <strong>digital records dividends MTD agency</strong> compliance, how to store those records, and what happens if you do not get your record-keeping in order before the deadlines.</p>

<h2>What Is MTD for ITSA and Why Does It Affect Dividends?</h2>

<p>MTD for ITSA (Making Tax Digital for Income Tax Self Assessment) is HMRC's programme to digitise the tax system. From April 2026, sole traders and landlords with qualifying income over £50,000 must keep digital records and submit quarterly updates. From April 2027, the threshold drops to £30,000.</p>

<p>If you are an agency director drawing dividends, those dividends count as income for MTD purposes. You cannot just note them on a spreadsheet and submit a figure at year-end. You need a digital record of each dividend, held in MTD-compatible software, and included in your quarterly updates.</p>

<p>This is a significant shift. Many agency founders treat dividends as a simple director's decision: vote a dividend, transfer the money, move on. Under MTD, that approach will not satisfy the record-keeping requirements.</p>

<h2>What Counts as a Dividend for MTD Purposes?</h2>

<p>Dividends paid by a UK company to its shareholders count as income for the individual recipient. For MTD for ITSA, you must record:</p>

<ul>
<li>Each dividend payment you receive from your agency (or any other company)</li>
<li>The date of the dividend payment</li>
<li>The gross amount of the dividend (before any tax taken off, though UK dividends typically come without tax deducted)</li>
<li>The type of dividend (interim or final)</li>
<li>The company paying the dividend</li>
</ul>

<p>This applies whether you are taking a regular monthly dividend, a quarterly top-up, or a single year-end distribution. Every payment must be recorded digitally.</p>

<h3>What About Dividends from an Investment Portfolio?</h3>

<p>If you hold shares in other companies outside your agency, those dividends must also be recorded. The same rules apply. HMRC does not distinguish between dividends from your trading company and dividends from a passive investment portfolio. Both are income for MTD purposes.</p>

<h2>What Software Can You Use for Digital Records of Dividends?</h2>

<p>Not all accounting software is MTD-compatible. You need software that HMRC has approved for MTD for ITSA submissions. The major players are already on board:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Xero</strong>, fully MTD-compatible, handles dividend recording through its transactions feed</li>
<li><strong>QuickBooks</strong>, MTD-ready, with dividend categorisation built in</li>
<li><strong>FreeAgent</strong>, popular with agency founders, MTD-compatible, includes dividend tracking</li>
<li><strong>Sage</strong>, MTD-ready, though the interface varies by product</li>
</ul>

<p>If you are using a spreadsheet to record dividends and manually entering figures into your tax return, that will not work under MTD. Spreadsheets are not MTD-compatible unless they are linked to bridging software that submits the data to HMRC. Most agency founders will find it simpler to move to proper accounting software.</p>

<p>As ICAEW qualified accountants, we typically recommend Xero or FreeAgent for agency clients. Both handle dividend recording cleanly and integrate with your bank feed, so the transaction arrives in the software automatically. You then categorise it as a dividend, add the relevant details, and the record is stored digitally.</p>

<h2>What Information Must Your Digital Record Contain for Each Dividend?</h2>

<p>For each dividend payment you receive, your digital record must include:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Date of payment</strong>, the date the money lands in your personal account</li>
<li><strong>Gross amount</strong>, the full dividend before any notional tax credit (which no longer exists for most dividends, but the gross figure is still what you report)</li>
<li><strong>Company name</strong>, the legal entity paying the dividend</li>
<li><strong>Dividend type</strong>, interim or final</li>
<li><strong>Dividend voucher reference</strong>, if your company issues dividend vouchers, the voucher number or a reference to it</li>
</ul>

<p>You also need to record the tax deducted at source, if any. For most UK dividends, there is no tax deducted. But if you receive dividends from overseas companies, there may be withholding tax. That must be recorded too.</p>

<h3>Do You Need to Record the Dividend Voucher Itself?</h3>

<p>The digital record is the data, not the scanned document. You do not need to upload a PDF of the dividend voucher to your software. But you should keep the voucher as a supporting document. If HMRC queries a dividend entry, you need to be able to produce the voucher showing the board resolution, the date, and the amount.</p>

<p>Keep dividend vouchers in a digital folder, named clearly. "Dividend_Voucher_2025_06_30.pdf" is better than "scan0001.pdf". Your accountant will thank you.</p>

<h2>How Do Quarterly Updates Work for Dividends?</h2>

<p>Under MTD for ITSA, you submit quarterly updates to HMRC showing your income and expenses for that quarter. Dividends are part of your income. So each quarter, the dividends you received in that period must be included in the update.</p>

<p>This does not mean you pay tax quarterly. The quarterly updates are cumulative data submissions. Your final tax liability is calculated at year-end when you submit your end-of-period statement and final declaration. But HMRC gets to see your income (including dividends) every three months.</p>

<p>For agency founders who take irregular dividends, this means you need to be disciplined about recording each payment as it happens. If you take a large dividend in January and do not record it until April, your Q1 update will be wrong. HMRC may ask questions.</p>

<h2>What Happens If You Do Not Keep Digital Records?</h2>

<p>HMRC has not yet published the full penalty regime for MTD for ITSA record-keeping failures. But based on the VAT MTD regime, you can expect:</p>

<ul>
<li>Point-based penalties for late submissions</li>
<li>Financial penalties for failure to keep adequate digital records</li>
<li>Possible compliance checks if your records are consistently poor</li>
</ul>

<p>There is no grace period. From your first mandated quarter, you must have digital records in place. If you are above the £50,000 threshold, that means April 2026. If you are between £30,000 and £50,000, April 2027.</p>

<p>If you are reading this and thinking "I will just estimate the dividend figure for the quarterly update and sort it out at year-end", do not. HMRC's systems will flag discrepancies between your quarterly updates and your year-end figures. The whole point of MTD is to reduce the tax gap caused by poor record-keeping and late filing.</p>

<h2>Practical Steps for Agency Founders Right Now</h2>

<p>You do not need to wait until 2026 to get ready. Here is what you can do today:</p>

<h3>1. Check Your Software</h3>
<p>If you are not already using MTD-compatible accounting software, move now. Xero, QuickBooks, FreeAgent, and Sage are all solid options. Your accountant can help you choose the right one for your agency. Most offer free trials, so you can test the dividend recording workflow before committing.</p>

<h3>2. Set Up Dividend Recording in Your Software</h3>
<p>In Xero, for example, you can create a "Director's Dividend" account in your chart of accounts. When a dividend payment hits your personal bank account (which you can link to the software via bank feed), you categorise it to that account. Add the date, the company name, and any notes. That is your digital record.</p>

<h3>3. Create a Dividend Voucher Template</h3>
<p>If you do not already have a standard dividend voucher template, create one. It should include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Company name and registered number</li>
<li>Date of the board resolution</li>
<li>Amount per share and total dividend</li>
<li>Your name and shareholding</li>
<li>Signature (digital is fine)</li>
</ul>
<p>Keep a PDF copy of every completed voucher in a dedicated folder. This is your audit trail.</p>

<h3>4. Review Your Dividend Frequency</h3>
<p>If you currently take one large dividend at year-end, consider splitting it into quarterly payments. This makes your quarterly MTD updates more accurate and reduces the risk of a large discrepancy between quarters. It also smooths your personal cash flow.</p>

<h3>5. Talk to Your Accountant</h3>
<p>If your accountant has not raised MTD for ITSA with you yet, bring it up. Ask them specifically about <strong>digital records dividends MTD agency</strong> compliance. They should be able to show you exactly how your software will handle dividend recording and quarterly submissions.</p>

<h2>What About Dividends from Your Agency to a Holding Company?</h2>

<p>If you operate through a group structure with a holding company and a trading subsidiary, dividends paid between companies are not income for the individual. They are inter-company transactions. MTD for ITSA applies to individuals, not companies. Corporation Tax MTD is a separate programme with a later timeline.</p>

<p>But if you then draw dividends from the holding company to yourself personally, those personal dividends must be recorded digitally under MTD for ITSA. The corporate structure does not exempt you from individual record-keeping.</p>

<h2>Common Mistakes Agency Founders Make with Dividend Records</h2>

<p>We see the same errors repeatedly:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Recording the net amount instead of the gross.</strong> For UK dividends, the gross and net are usually the same (no tax deducted). But if you receive a dividend from an overseas company with withholding tax, record the gross amount and the tax separately.</li>
<li><strong>Missing the date.</strong> HMRC wants the payment date, not the date you recorded it. If you pay yourself a dividend on 31 March but do not record it until 10 April, use 31 March as the date.</li>
<li><strong>Combining multiple dividends into one entry.</strong> Each dividend payment needs its own record. If you take two dividends in the same quarter, record them separately.</li>
<li><strong>Not keeping dividend vouchers.</strong> The digital record in your software is the primary record. But the voucher is your evidence. If HMRC challenges a dividend entry, the voucher is your defence.</li>
</ul>

<h2>What If You Are Already Using MTD-Compatible Software?</h2>

<p>You are ahead of the curve. But check that your dividend recording process is clean. Many agency founders use their software for business transactions but record dividends manually or in a separate spreadsheet. That will not work under MTD.</p>

<p>Every dividend must be in the software, categorised correctly, and included in your quarterly updates. If your current process involves exporting data from your software and adjusting it in Excel, you need to fix that. The digital record must be the source of truth, not a spreadsheet that sits alongside it.</p>

<h2>The Bottom Line on Digital Records for Dividends Under MTD</h2>

<p>MTD for ITSA is not optional. If your qualifying income (including dividends) is above £50,000, you must comply from April 2026. If it is above £30,000, April 2027. The requirement to keep digital records of dividends is part of that compliance.</p>

<p>The good news is that the software handles most of the heavy lifting. If you set up your dividend recording correctly now, the quarterly updates will be straightforward. The hard part is changing the habit of treating dividends as a casual year-end adjustment.</p>

<p>Start today. Check your software, set up your dividend accounts, create your voucher template, and talk to your accountant. The agency founders who get their digital record-keeping right early will find MTD a minor administrative task rather than a major headache.</p>
