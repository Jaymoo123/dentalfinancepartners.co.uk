<p>If you are a self-employed agency founder moving to Dubai, health insurance is not optional. It is a legal requirement for your residence visa. You cannot complete the visa process without proof of valid cover.</p>

<p>This is the first thing most founders get wrong. They assume they can sort it out after they arrive, or that the basic cover provided by the visa processing centre is enough. Neither is true.</p>

<p>This article covers exactly what you need to know about <strong>health insurance Dubai self employed</strong> founders must arrange: the two main types of plan, what they cost, how age affects your premium, and what to look for in a policy that suits an agency owner who might be flying between Dubai and London regularly.</p>

<h2>Why Health Insurance Is Mandatory for Your Dubai Visa</h2>

<p>The Dubai Health Authority (DHA) requires every resident to have health insurance. This is not a recommendation. It is a condition of your residence visa application. Without a valid insurance certificate, your visa will not be issued.</p>

<p>If you are sponsoring your own visa through a freelance permit or a company setup in a free zone, you are responsible for arranging your own cover. Your sponsor (the free zone authority or your own company) does not provide it for you.</p>

<p>The same applies to any dependents you bring with you. Your spouse and children must also have insurance. You will need to show proof of cover for them at the visa application stage.</p>

<h2>The Two Main Types of Health Insurance Plan</h2>

<p>There are two broad categories of health insurance in Dubai. The one you choose depends on your budget, your health needs, and whether you travel regularly.</p>

<h3>Essential Benefits Plan (EBP)</h3>

<p>The Essential Benefits Plan is the minimum required by law. It is a basic plan designed to cover essential healthcare needs. It costs less than a comprehensive plan, but it has limits.</p>

<p>Key features of the EBP:</p>
<ul>
<li>Annual premium: approximately AED 700 to AED 1,500 (roughly £150 to £330) depending on age</li>
<li>Coverage limit: up to AED 150,000 per year</li>
<li>Network: limited to specific hospitals and clinics in Dubai</li>
<li>Outpatient cover: limited to a set number of GP visits per year</li>
<li>Maternity cover: limited, with a waiting period</li>
<li>No cover for pre-existing conditions for the first six months</li>
</ul>
<p>If you are young, healthy, and rarely visit a doctor, the EBP might be enough. But for an agency founder who travels, works long hours, and values convenience, it often falls short.</p>

<h3>Comprehensive Health Insurance</h3>

<p>A comprehensive plan offers significantly more cover. It includes a wider network of hospitals and clinics, higher coverage limits, and often includes international cover or outpatient treatment without strict limits.</p>

<p>Key features of a comprehensive plan:</p>
<ul>
<li>Annual premium: approximately AED 5,000 to AED 25,000 (£1,100 to £5,500) depending on age, cover level, and provider</li>
<li>Coverage limit: typically AED 1 million to AED 5 million per year</li>
<li>Network: includes major private hospitals like Mediclinic, Saudi German, and Emirates Hospital</li>
<li>Outpatient cover: usually unlimited or high cap</li>
<li>Maternity cover: better, but still with waiting periods</li>
<li>Pre-existing conditions: may be covered after a medical assessment</li>
<li>International cover: many plans include cover for treatment outside the UAE, useful if you travel back to the UK regularly</li>
</ul>
<p>For most agency founders I speak to, a comprehensive plan is the right choice. The extra cost is not huge relative to your income, and the peace of mind is worth it.</p>

<h2>How Age Affects Your Premium</h2>

<p>Age is the single biggest factor in your premium. Insurers price risk by age band. The older you are, the more you pay.</p>

<p>Here is a rough guide to annual premiums for a comprehensive plan in 2025/26:</p>
<ul>
<li>Age 25-30: AED 4,000 to AED 6,000 (£880 to £1,320)</li>
<li>Age 31-40: AED 6,000 to AED 10,000 (£1,320 to £2,200)</li>
<li>Age 41-50: AED 10,000 to AED 18,000 (£2,200 to £3,960)</li>
<li>Age 51-60: AED 18,000 to AED 30,000 (£3,960 to £6,600)</li>
<li>Age 60+: premiums rise significantly, and some insurers may decline cover</li>
</ul>
<p>These are indicative figures. Actual premiums vary by provider, your health history, and the specific plan features you choose.</p>

<p>If you are over 60, you need to plan ahead. Some free zones and visa types have age limits. You may need to arrange cover before you apply for the visa.</p>

<h2>What to Look for in a Policy as an Agency Founder</h2>

<p>Not all comprehensive plans are the same. Here are the specific things to check before you buy.</p>

<h3>Network Coverage</h3>
<p>Does the plan include the hospitals and clinics you want to use? If you live in Dubai Marina, you want a plan that covers the nearby clinics. If you prefer Mediclinic or Saudi German, check they are in-network. Out-of-network treatment can mean higher co-pays or no cover at all.</p>

<h3>Outpatient Cover</h3>
<p>Some plans cap GP visits at 10 or 15 per year. If you have young children or a chronic condition, that cap will bite. Look for plans with unlimited or high-cap outpatient cover.</p>

<h3>Maternity Cover</h3>
<p>If you plan to have children in Dubai, check the maternity cover carefully. Most plans have a 12-month waiting period. That means you need to hold the policy for a full year before you can claim for maternity care. Some plans exclude maternity altogether. Do not assume it is included.</p>

<h3>International Cover</h3>
<p>If you travel back to the UK or elsewhere for work, look for a plan that includes international cover. Some plans cover you worldwide except the USA. Others cover only the UAE. If you need treatment in London, you want a plan that covers it.</p>

<h3>Pre-existing Conditions</h3>
<p>Most basic plans exclude pre-existing conditions for the first six months. Comprehensive plans may cover them after a medical assessment. If you have a condition like asthma, diabetes, or high blood pressure, declare it upfront. Hiding it can invalidate your cover later.</p>

<h3>Co-pays and Deductibles</h3>
<p>Some plans have a co-pay (you pay 20% of each claim up to a limit) or a deductible (you pay the first AED 500 or AED 1,000 of each year's claims). Lower premiums often mean higher co-pays. Work out the total cost, not just the monthly premium.</p>

<h2>How to Buy Health Insurance in Dubai</h2>

<p>You have three main routes to buy health insurance as a self-employed founder.</p>

<h3>Through Your Free Zone or Visa Processing Agent</h3>
<p>Many free zones and visa processing centres offer a basic insurance package as part of the visa application. This is usually the EBP. It meets the minimum legal requirement, but it is often not good cover. Check the details before accepting it. You can always upgrade later.</p>

<h3>Direct from an Insurer</h3>
<p>Major insurers in Dubai include AXA, Cigna, Bupa, Oman Insurance, and Nextcare. You can buy a policy directly from their websites. The advantage is you choose the plan. The disadvantage is you need to compare options yourself.</p>

<h3>Through a Broker</h3>
<p>This is often the best route for agency founders. A good broker will compare plans from multiple insurers, explain the differences, and help you choose the right one. They also handle claims and renewals. The cost is usually the same as buying direct because brokers earn commission from the insurer. Use a broker regulated by the DHA or the UAE Insurance Authority.</p>

<h2>What Happens If You Do Not Have Insurance</h2>

<p>The consequences are straightforward. Without valid health insurance, your residence visa will not be issued or renewed. If you are already resident and let your insurance lapse, you face fines.</p>

<p>The fine for not holding valid health insurance in Dubai is currently AED 500 per month for each uninsured individual. That applies to you and any dependents. It adds up quickly.</p>

<p>More practically, if you need medical treatment without insurance, you pay the full cost. A simple hospital visit can cost AED 1,000 to AED 2,000. A surgical procedure can run into tens of thousands. Insurance is not just a legal requirement. It is financial protection.</p>

<h2>Tax Implications for UK Agency Founders</h2>

<p>If you are a UK agency founder moving to Dubai, you need to think about how health insurance fits into your tax structure.</p>

<p>If you operate through a UK company and are moving to Dubai, your company can pay for your health insurance as a business expense. The premium is deductible against corporation tax, provided it is for a genuine business purpose (keeping you healthy enough to work).</p>

<p>If you are setting up a UAE company and moving your operations there, the insurance is a personal expense. Your UAE company can pay it on your behalf, but it will be treated as a benefit in kind. The tax treatment in the UAE is simpler because there is no personal income tax. But you need to keep proper records.</p>

<h2>Practical Steps to Get Covered</h2>

<p>Here is the sequence I recommend to agency founders moving to Dubai.</p>

<ol>
<li><strong>Before you apply for the visa:</strong> Research plans and get quotes. Use a broker if you want a comparison.</li>
<li><strong>At visa application:</strong> You will need to show proof of insurance. If you have not bought a policy yet, the visa centre will offer you the EBP. Take it if you need to, but plan to upgrade later.</li>
<li><strong>Within the first month:</strong> Buy a comprehensive plan that suits your needs. Cancel the EBP once the new policy is active. You will get a pro-rata refund on the EBP.</li>
<li><strong>Each year:</strong> Review your plan before renewal. Your health needs change. Your age changes. The market changes. Do not auto-renew without checking.</li>
</ol>

<h2>Common Mistakes Agency Founders Make</h2>

<p>I see the same mistakes repeatedly. Here are the ones to avoid.</p>

<p><strong>Buying the cheapest plan.</strong> The EBP is cheap for a reason. If you need treatment, you will hit the coverage limit quickly. A single hospital stay can use up the entire AED 150,000 limit. Then you are paying the rest yourself.</p>

<p><strong>Not checking the network.</strong> You buy a plan that covers a hospital on the other side of Dubai. Then you realise the clinic near your apartment is not in-network. You end up paying co-pays or travelling across the city. Check the network before you buy.</p>

<p><strong>Ignoring maternity waiting periods.</strong> If you plan to have a child within the next 12 months, you need a plan with no waiting period or a shorter one. Most plans have a 12-month wait. Plan ahead.</p>

<p><strong>Not declaring pre-existing conditions.</strong> Insurers check medical records when you claim. If you hid a condition, they can deny the claim and cancel the policy. Declare everything upfront.</p>

<p><strong>Assuming your UK insurance covers you.</strong> Most UK private health insurance policies do not cover treatment in the UAE. Some international policies do, but check the small print. You need a UAE-compliant policy for your visa.</p>

<h2>Final Thoughts</h2>

<p>Health insurance in Dubai is straightforward once you understand the basics. You need a DHA-compliant plan to get your visa. You have a choice between the basic EBP and a comprehensive plan. For most agency founders, the comprehensive plan is the better value, even though it costs more upfront.</p>

<p>Age is the main cost driver. Buy early, while you are younger and healthier. The premium will be lower, and you will avoid the age-related surcharges that hit after 50.</p>
