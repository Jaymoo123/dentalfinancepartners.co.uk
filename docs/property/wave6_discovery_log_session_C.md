## D-11 OTHER — HMRC CA11520 + Hora Tevfik FTT 2019 confirm HMO shared kitchen/bathroom/lounge are within the single dwelling-house for s.35
**Surfaced at:** 2026-05-23 (C7, step 7 §16.35 per-write verification)
**Detail:** HMRC Capital Allowances Manual CA11520 is the authoritative HMRC position that an HMO with shared internal amenities is usually a single dwelling-house under CAA 2001 s.35. The manual instructs officers to escalate any contrary claim ("notify the Capital Allowances single point of contact for your area"). The First-tier Tribunal in Hora Tevfik v HMRC [2019] TC07129 confirmed this position; the case is the working FTT authority for the s.35 boundary in multi-let residential property. Tevfik accepted in principle that TRUE common parts (entrance lobbies, communal corridors outside dwellings, lifts, fire alarm + electrical systems serving the building) can fall outside s.35, but the working scope is purpose-built blocks of self-contained flats, not HMOs with shared internal kitchen/bathroom/lounge. Pages on the property site (including the legacy `hmo-capital-allowances-multi-tenant-landlords-claim`) that frame shared HMO amenities as claimable contradict both authorities.
**Recommendation:** Add a §25.11 sub-position to house_positions.md codifying the HMO-vs-flats-block boundary explicitly with the CA11520 + Tevfik citations. Audit the rest of the C-bucket briefs for similar HMO communal-amenity framings. Schedule the legacy HMO-CA page for either repoint-to-C7 or in-place rewrite.

## D-13 OTHER — Quantity surveyor's apportionment report as the defensible evidence pack for common-parts claims
**Surfaced at:** 2026-05-23 (C7, step 6 planning the documentation pack)
**Detail:** The Tevfik decision turned on the failure to apportion. A floor plan marked with the dwelling-house and common-parts boundary, plus a contemporaneous capital allowances valuation report from a quantity surveyor with capital-allowances expertise, is the working defensive evidence base. The QS report identifies each item, its installation location, its category (main pool / special rate pool / AIA-eligible / revenue), and ties it to invoices. Without this pack, the claim is defenseless on enquiry; with it, the s.35 + common-parts argument is asserted on documentation HMRC cannot easily contradict. The break-even for commissioning a QS report is around £20-30k of common-parts spend at CT rates.
**Recommendation:** Future-wave page: "When to commission a capital allowances QS report for a property acquisition or refurbishment" — covers the cost-benefit, the QS scope, the contemporaneous-evidence requirement, and the four-year CT amendment window for retrospective claims. Could pair with a calculator: "Is a QS capital allowances report worth commissioning for your spend level?" (inputs: total spend, % allocated to common parts vs in-dwelling, CT rate, QS fee estimate).
