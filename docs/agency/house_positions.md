## 1. Company structure and incorporation posture for agencies (locked, 2026-07-05)

**Position.** A UK agency can trade as a **sole trader**, a **general partnership** (Partnership Act 1890, tax-transparent, joint and several liability), an **LLP** (Limited Liability Partnerships Act 2000, members taxed as partners, limited liability), or a **limited company** (Companies Act 2006, pays corporation tax, owners extract via salary and dividends, limited liability). The overwhelming majority of digital/creative/marketing agencies incorporate as a **limited company** once profits are meaningful, because agencies retain earnings for hiring and reinvestment, want limited liability against client-work and employment exposure, and plan for a share sale on exit (§5). **Incorporation** of an existing sole-trade/partnership agency transfers the trade and goodwill to a new company for shares, with **TCGA 1992 s.162 incorporation relief** deferring the CGT on chargeable assets (notably goodwill) where the **whole business is transferred as a going concern (all assets other than cash) wholly or partly for shares**; the deferred gain rolls into the base cost of the shares and crystallises on a later share disposal (cash consideration restricts relief pro rata). **Goodwill amortisation relief** for the company is restricted: generally unavailable for acquisitions 8 July 2015 to 31 March 2019, and from **1 April 2019** given at a **fixed 6.5% per year** but ONLY where the goodwill is acquired with qualifying intellectual property and capped at 6x the qualifying-IP spend (CTA 2009 Part 8 Ch 15A / s.879A+). Agency goodwill is largely people-and-relationship goodwill, so the qualifying-IP condition frequently fails; do not promise 6.5% relief as automatic.

- **Statutory hooks:** Partnership Act 1890; Limited Liability Partnerships Act 2000; Companies Act 2006; **TCGA 1992 s.162** (incorporation relief) https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12/section/162 ; s.165 (gift holdover); **CTA 2009 Part 8 Ch 15A / s.879A+** (goodwill 6.5% restricted relief, FA 2019); VATA 1994 / VAT Notice 700/9 (transfer of a going concern); CAA 2001 s.266/s.267 (succession election).
- **HMRC manual anchors:** CG65700+ (s.162 incorporation relief); CIRD44050+ (goodwill/relevant-assets relief); VTOGC.
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** present the structure choice as a trade-off, not a ranking; note most agencies incorporate. State s.162 **defers** (does not cancel) the gain into the shares. Use 6.5% goodwill relief only for post-1-April-2019 acquisitions and surface the qualifying-IP / 6x-cap condition (a blanket "always 6.5%" is wrong, and agency goodwill often fails the IP condition). Do NOT present incorporation as automatically tax-saving (the 2026/27 dividend rise narrows it, §3). Cross-ref §2, §3, §5.

## 2. Director pay: salary, dividends, employer NIC and the Employment Allowance caveat (locked, 2026-07-05)

**Position.** A director-shareholder of an agency company extracts profit through a mix of **salary** (corporation-tax-deductible for the company, taxable as employment income, subject to NIC), **dividends** (from post-tax profit, no NIC, taxed at dividend rates, §3) and **employer pension contributions** (deductible on a paid basis, no NIC; annual allowance £60,000, tapered above adjusted income £260,000 to a £10,000 floor, FA 2004 s.196). The usual owner-manager pattern is a **modest salary plus dividends**.

**Employer (secondary Class 1) NIC.** The company pays employer NIC at **15%** on salary above the **secondary threshold of £5,000 a year** from **6 April 2025** (the Autumn 2024 Budget cut the threshold to £5,000 and raised the rate to 15%). **This supersedes the old 13.8% rate and £9,100 threshold, which applied to 5 April 2025 and must NOT be used for 2025/26 onward except as a clearly date-tagged historic figure.** The **Lower Earnings Limit is £6,500/year** (2025/26), preserving the director's state-pension entitlement even where little NIC is due.

**Employment Allowance caveat (this is the load-bearing caveat for agencies).** The **Employment Allowance is £10,500**, but it is **not available to a company whose only employee is a single director**. Many small agencies are exactly that (a solo founder-director with only freelancers, no employees on PAYE), so they **cannot** claim the EA. That is why a one-director agency company often sets salary at the **secondary threshold (£5,000)** to avoid employer NIC entirely, while an agency with at least one genuinely employed non-director (for example a genuinely employed spouse on a market-rate wage, or the first PAYE hire) can claim the £10,500 allowance and lift the director's salary to the personal allowance (£12,570) more efficiently. The site's own `employer-ni.ts` correctly enforces the 2+-employee condition. Always state the EA assumption; never assert a single "optimal salary" without it.

- **Statutory hooks:** **SSCBA 1992** secondary Class 1 NIC (15% above £5,000 from 6 Apr 2025); **National Insurance Contributions Act 2014** (Employment Allowance £10,500, single-director restriction); ITEPA 2003 (employment income); **FA 2004 s.196** (employer pension deduction); ITA 2007 s.8 (dividend rates, §3).
- **Authority anchors:** gov.uk/guidance/rates-and-thresholds-for-employers-2025-to-2026 (15% / £5,000, EA £10,500, LEL £6,500).
- **HMRC manual anchors:** NIM06000+ (secondary NIC); NIM24175+ (Employment Allowance); BIM37700+ (spouse wages, wholly and exclusively, commensurate with work done).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** always use employer NIC **15% / £5,000 (from 6 Apr 2025)**, never 13.8% / £9,100 for current advice. State the **single-director EA exclusion** explicitly whenever "optimal salary" comes up (it is the most common agency trap: a solo founder-director cannot claim the EA). Spouse/first-hire salary must be **genuine and market-rate**. Model both the secondary-threshold and personal-allowance salaries, stating the EA assumption. Cross-ref §3, §2.A, §7.

### 2.A Director's loan accounts and the s.455 charge (locked, 2026-07-05)

**Position.** An agency company is normally a **close company**. An **overdrawn director's loan account** (drawings ahead of declared salary or dividend, common in feast-and-famine agency cash flow) triggers a **CTA 2010 s.455 charge**: the company pays an amount equal to the **dividend upper rate** on the loan outstanding **9 months and 1 day** after the period end, that is **33.75% for loans made in 2025/26** and **35.75% for loans made on or after 6 April 2026** (tracks ITA 2007 s.8 as amended by FA 2026 s.4). The charge is **temporary**: **s.458 relief** repays it once the loan is repaid, released or written off, but the relief is **deferred to 9 months and 1 day after the end of the accounting period in which repayment occurs** (4-year claim window). Anti-avoidance blocks repay-and-redraw: the **30-day rule (s.464ZA)** and the **arrangements rule (s.464C/464D)** for balances of £15,000 or more. A loan **over £10,000** at any point in the year is also a **beneficial-loan benefit in kind** (ITEPA 2003 ss.173 to 191) unless interest is paid at the **official rate**, reportable on P11D with **Class 1A NIC at 15%**.

- **Statutory hooks:** **CTA 2010 s.455** https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2010/4/section/455 ; s.458; s.464ZA; s.464C/s.464D; ITEPA 2003 ss.173 to 191.
- **HMRC manual anchors:** CTM61500+ / CTM61630; EIM26100+.
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** state the s.455 rate **with its date band** (33.75% pre-6-Apr-2026, 35.75% on/after), the 9-months-and-1-day timing, and that s.458 relief is **deferred**, not immediate. Do NOT describe s.455 as a permanent tax. Cross-ref §2, §3.

## 4. R&D relief: merged scheme, ERIS, and the agency-eligibility honesty boundary (locked, 2026-07-05)

**Position.** For accounting periods **beginning on or after 1 April 2024** a single **merged R&D scheme** applies (SME and RDEC regimes abolished for these periods): a **taxable expenditure credit of 20%** of qualifying R&D spend, giving a **net benefit of roughly 15%** after corporation tax. Loss-making, R&D-intensive SMEs can instead claim under **Enhanced R&D Intensive Support (ERIS)**: the intensity threshold is **qualifying R&D spend of at least 30% of total expenditure** (lowered from 40%), giving an **86% enhanced deduction** and a **14.5% payable credit** on the surrendered loss. From 1 April 2024 the merged scheme also **restricts overseas subcontractor/EPW costs** (only UK or qualifying-overseas-territory work qualifies) and tightens the "who did the R&D" subcontracting rules.

**The agency-eligibility honesty boundary (this is the load-bearing agency position, and the site must be scrupulously honest here).** **Many, probably most, agency projects do NOT qualify.** Qualifying R&D must seek an **advance in science or technology** and resolve **genuine scientific or technological uncertainty** through systematic investigation. That excludes the bulk of agency work: standard web development on existing frameworks (WordPress, Shopify, Webflow, React/Python glue), routine or creative design, implementing off-the-shelf software for clients, marketing campaigns without technical innovation, and project/account management. What CAN qualify is a genuine technical advance: building a novel algorithm, a proprietary ML/AI tool where existing tools are inadequate, or a new technical process with real uncertainty. HMRC is **actively auditing R&D claims**, with penalties up to 100% of the relief for incorrect claims, so pages must steer founders away from over-claiming, not toward it. For claims, the mechanism requires the **additional information form** and (for larger/first-time claims) a claim-notification, plus a technical narrative and project-level cost/time records.

- **Statutory hooks:** Finance Act 2024 (merged scheme + ERIS, periods beginning on/after 1 Apr 2024); CTA 2009 Part 13 (R&D) as amended; gov.uk "Research and Development (R&D) tax relief: the merged scheme and enhanced R&D intensive support".
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** use the merged scheme **20% credit / ~15% net**; use ERIS **30% intensity threshold, 86% enhanced deduction, 14.5% payable credit** (the estate-verified figures, NOT the site's `rd-tax-credit.ts` 27%/40% constants, which are stale, §9). Lead with the **eligibility boundary honesty**: state plainly that routine agency work does not qualify, name the excluded activities, and warn about HMRC compliance risk. Do NOT imply R&D relief is an easy win for a typical agency. Flag the overseas-subcontractor restriction. Cross-ref §9 (the compute-lib and content defects to fix in the wave).

## 5. Exit and sale: BADR, CGT, asset-vs-share, and earn-outs (locked, 2026-07-05)

**Position.** On an agency exit the core fork is **share sale vs asset (trade-and-assets) sale**. On a **share sale** the founder disposes of shares (one CGT disposal, BADR potentially available, buyer inherits the company and its history); on an **asset sale** the company sells its assets (corporation tax on gains inside the company), then the owner faces a second tax layer on extraction or on a later Members' Voluntary Liquidation (capital distribution potentially within BADR, subject to the phoenix TAAR). Buyers usually prefer assets; sellers usually prefer shares (single CGT layer, BADR). Model both.

**CGT and BADR.** Individual CGT is **18% / 24%** (from 30 Oct 2024), AEA **£3,000**. **BADR** gives a reduced rate on qualifying business disposals up to a **£1,000,000 lifetime limit per individual**, at **10%** to 5 Apr 2025, **14%** for disposals 6 Apr 2025 to 5 Apr 2026, and **18%** from 6 Apr 2026. Conditions held throughout the **2-year period to disposal**: for a share sale, a **personal company** holding (at least **5% of ordinary share capital and 5% of voting rights**, plus a 5% economic entitlement) with the seller an **officer or employee** throughout, and the company trading. The **6 Apr 2026 step from 14% to 18% is a live planning lever** for late-2025/26 completions (an unconditional exchange on or before 5 April 2026 fixes the 14% rate even if completion follows; a conditional contract does not, TCGA 1992 s.28).

**Earn-outs (very common in agency deals).** Where part of the price is **unascertainable** at disposal (a genuine earn-out contingent on future performance), the seller is treated as receiving a **separate chargeable asset**, the right to the future payment (the *Marren v Ingles* principle); CGT is charged at disposal on the valued right, then again on the second disposal when it is satisfied. Two consequences: **BADR generally does not reach the second disposal** (so the earn-out element is usually taxed at the standard CGT rate, not the BADR rate), and a later shortfall can be **carried back** (TCGA 1992 s.279A-s.279D). Where consideration is **ascertainable but deferred** (fixed instalments), the full amount is taxed at disposal, but **s.280 instalment relief** can spread the tax where consideration is payable over more than 18 months. **Substance test on earn-outs:** if the earn-out is conditional on the seller's continued personal service/employment, HMRC can treat it as **employment income (income tax up to 45% plus NIC)** rather than capital; structure and document it as consideration for shares. EMI options held by employees can qualify for BADR on exit if the holding conditions are met.

- **Statutory hooks:** **TCGA 1992** ss.169H-169S (BADR), s.28 (time of disposal), s.22 (capital sums), s.279A-s.279D (earn-out shortfall carry-back), s.280 (instalments); ***Marren v Ingles* [1980] 1 WLR 983 (HL)**; CTA 2010 s.1030A + TAAR (MVL capital distribution / phoenix).
- **Authority anchors:** gov.uk/business-asset-disposal-relief; gov.uk/capital-gains-tax/rates; HS275; HMRC CG64174.
- **HMRC manual anchors:** CG63950+ (BADR); CG14850+ / CG14990 (Marren v Ingles unascertainable consideration); CG14910+ (s.280 instalments); CTM36300+ (phoenix TAAR).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** state the BADR rate **with its exact date band** every time (14% now, 18% from 6 Apr 2026). Separate **asset sale** from **share sale** and note the double tax layer on an asset sale. On earn-outs, note BADR usually does not reach the second disposal, and flag the **income-vs-capital substance test** (do not let an earn-out be linked to the seller's ongoing employment). Do NOT state BADR is still 10%, and never write "10% to 18% on 6 April 2025" (that is the live content defect, §9). Cross-ref §1 (s.162 to access BADR on a later share sale), §8 (non-resident sale timing).

## 6. VAT: threshold, flat rate and the limited-cost-trader trap (locked, 2026-07-05)

**Position.** Agency services (creative, digital, marketing, media, consultancy) are **standard-rated at 20%**. A business must register when **taxable turnover exceeds £90,000** in any rolling 12 months (or is expected to in the next 30 days; raised from £85,000 on 1 April 2024); the **deregistration threshold is £88,000**. **Making Tax Digital for VAT** applies to all VAT-registered businesses since April 2022.

**Flat Rate Scheme and the limited-cost-trader trap (the load-bearing VAT position for agencies).** The FRS lets a business pay a single flat percentage of VAT-inclusive turnover instead of tracking input VAT; join if expected taxable turnover is **£150,000 or less (ex VAT)**, with a **1% discount in the first year** of registration. The marketing/advertising sector rate is nominally 11%-12.5%, BUT most agencies are **limited cost traders (limited cost businesses)**: if goods (not services) cost **less than 2% of VAT-inclusive turnover, or less than £1,000 a year**, the flat rate is the punitive **16.5%**. Agencies are overwhelmingly labour-and-software businesses that buy almost no goods, so they are nearly always limited cost traders, which makes the FRS **usually a bad deal** (16.5% of gross typically exceeds what they would pay on the standard scheme after reclaiming input VAT). The site's `vat-scheme.ts` compute lib correctly applies the 2%/£1,000 goods test and the 16.5% rate. Note also **place-of-supply / reverse charge** matters for agencies with overseas (including UAE) clients: B2B services to a business customer outside the UK are generally outside the scope of UK VAT (customer accounts under reverse charge), which affects the registration-threshold calculation (OPEN-ITEM: place-of-supply detail is fact-specific, state the principle and advise a check).

- **Statutory hooks:** **VATA 1994** (charge; Sch 1 registration £90,000 / deregistration £88,000); VAT Regulations 1995 (SI 1995/2518) Part VIIA (flat rate); place of supply of services (VATA 1994 s.7A and Sch 4A).
- **Authority anchors:** gov.uk/vat-registration/when-to-register (£90,000); gov.uk/vat-flat-rate-scheme/how-much-you-pay (16.5% limited cost, 1% first-year discount).
- **HMRC manual anchors:** VATREG; FRS7000+ (flat rate, limited cost trader); VATPOSS (place of supply).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** use **£90,000** registration / **£88,000** deregistration, never the old £85,000. Always flag the **16.5% limited-cost-trader rate** and the 2%/£1,000 goods test as the reason **most agencies should not use the FRS**. State MTD for VAT applies since April 2022. For overseas-client questions state the reverse-charge principle and advise a place-of-supply check (do not assert a fixed outcome). Cross-ref §8.

## 7. IR35 and off-payroll: agencies engaging contractors (locked, 2026-07-05)

**Position.** Two IR35 angles matter for an agency. **(a) The agency as an engager.** When an agency engages a contractor who works through their own personal service company (PSC), and the agency is a **medium or large client** (broadly, meeting two of: turnover over £15m, balance sheet over £7.5m, more than 50 employees, per the Companies Act 2006 size test as uplifted in 2025), the **off-payroll rules in Chapter 10, Part 2 ITEPA 2003** put the **status determination on the client/fee-payer, not the PSC**, from 6 April 2021: the client must issue a **Status Determination Statement (SDS)** with reasons, taking reasonable care, and the fee-payer deducts PAYE/NIC if inside. Where the agency is a **small client**, the **original Chapter 8 IR35 rules** apply and the **contractor's PSC determines its own status**. An agency in a labour-supply chain can also be the **fee-payer** (with the deduction and liability obligations) even where another party is the client, so chain position matters. **(b) The founder's own status.** A founder contracting their own services out through a PSC is themselves within IR35 on those engagements. Status rests on case-law factors (control, personal service/substitution, mutuality of obligation, financial risk, integration), assessed via HMRC's **CEST** tool and the Employment Status Manual; the contract label does not govern.

- **Statutory hooks:** **ITEPA 2003 Chapter 10, Part 2** (off-payroll, client-led, from 6 Apr 2021) and **Chapter 8, Part 2** (original IR35, small clients) https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2003/1 ; small-company size test in the Companies Act 2006 (uplifted 2025).
- **HMRC manual anchors:** ESM10000+ (off-payroll); ESM0500+ (status factors); ESM11000+ (CEST).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** for **medium/large agencies engaging contractors**, the **SDS sits with the agency-as-client** (with a client-led disagreement process); for **small agencies**, the contractor's PSC decides. Flag the agency's possible **fee-payer** role in a chain. State status is substance over the contract label. Do NOT write "the contractor always decides their own status" (only true for small clients / pre-April-2021). OPEN-ITEM: confirm the exact 2025-uplifted Companies Act small-company thresholds at source before asserting the numbers on a page (state the principle, not relied-on exact figures). Cross-ref §2 (extraction blocked when a founder's own engagement is inside).

### 8.A Temporary non-residence (UK primary, LOCKED)

**Position.** A founder who leaves the UK and disposes of assets while non-resident can be dragged back into UK CGT by the **temporary non-residence rule**: it bites where the individual had **sole UK residence in 4 or more of the 7 tax years before departure** AND the **period of non-residence is 5 years or less (5 complete tax years is the safe-harbour test)**; a disposal made during the temporary non-residence period is charged in the **year of return** as if made while UK resident. The rule catches the common agency pattern of selling up, moving to Dubai for 3-4 years, then returning for family reasons. It applies to shares and business assets (UK residential property is always UK-taxable regardless). **BADR is not available on a disposal made while non-resident** (BADR requires UK residence at disposal). The **UK-UAE double tax treaty does not override** the domestic temporary non-residence rule. The site's existing content states this correctly (5 complete tax years, s.10A TCGA 1992, the 4-of-7 condition), so the position is sound; the only content fix needed is the BADR-rate defect embedded in these pages (§9).

- **Statutory hooks:** **TCGA 1992 s.10A / s.10AA** (temporary non-residence, gains); **FA 2013 Sch 45 Part 4** (temporary non-residence, income; SRT day-count tests); the UK-UAE double taxation convention.
- **HMRC manual anchors:** RDRM12600+ (temporary non-residence); RFIG (Statutory Residence Test).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** state there is **no automatic UK exit charge** on unlisted agency shares, but the **temporary non-residence rule** taxes a non-resident-period disposal on return unless the founder is non-resident for **5 complete tax years** (with the 4-of-7 prior-residence condition). State **BADR is unavailable while non-resident** and the **UK-UAE treaty does not override** the rule. Do NOT promise a tax-free exit on a short (sub-5-year) Dubai stint. Cross-ref §5, §8.B, §8.C.

### 8.B UK permanent establishment risk for a UAE company (UK primary principle LOCKED; treaty-article detail OPEN)

**Position.** A founder who incorporates the agency in the UAE (for the 0% / 9% regime) but continues to work from or serve the UK risks HMRC arguing the **UAE company has a UK permanent establishment** (a fixed place of business, or a dependent agent habitually concluding contracts in the UK) under the UK-UAE treaty. If HMRC succeeds, the UAE company pays **UK corporation tax (19% / 25%)** on the profits attributable to the UK activity, losing the 0% UAE rate. Agencies are especially exposed because the value is the founder's own time: if the founder is in the UK winning and delivering the work, HMRC can argue the real business is in the UK. A co-working desk used regularly, or a home room used for client calls, can be enough (substance over label).

- **Statutory hooks:** CTA 2009 Part 24 / s.5 (UK CT on a non-UK company trading through a UK PE); the **UK-UAE double taxation convention, Article 5** (permanent establishment).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** state the **PE risk principle** and the UK CT consequence firmly (it is UK-side law). OPEN-ITEM: the site content cites a specific treaty limb (Article 5(3)(k) service-PE, and a construction-project 12-month limb) as the trigger; **do not lock the exact treaty-article sub-paragraph or day threshold** without verifying the current UK-UAE convention text, treat those as content-derived and advise specialist confirmation.
