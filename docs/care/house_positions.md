## A. VAT and the welfare exemption (the differentiation cluster)

**1. Welfare services supplied by a CQC-registered provider are VAT-exempt; exemption is a cost, not a perk.**
Care homes and domiciliary care agencies that are registered with the Care Quality Commission as state-regulated providers supply welfare services that are exempt from VAT under Group 7 (Welfare) of Schedule 9 to the Value Added Tax Act 1994. HMRC's VAT Notice 701/2 confirms that CQC-registered providers qualify as state-regulated welfare institutions. Crucially, VAT exemption means the provider cannot recover input VAT on purchases used to make those exempt supplies. The VAT cost on consumables, equipment, building works and professional fees is a permanent overhead, not a recoverable item.
,  https://www.gov.uk/guidance/welfare-services-and-goods-notice-7012 (verified 2026-07-12: CQC-registered providers confirmed as state-regulated welfare providers; VAT exemption on welfare supplies confirmed; exemption applies from the date of CQC registration approval, not during the application period)

**2. HMRC is actively challenging VAT-grouping structures designed to convert exempt care supplies into taxable ones.**
Revenue and Customs Brief 2/2025 sets out HMRC's position that VAT-group arrangements incorporating unregulated entities into the supply chain, to allow input VAT recovery on otherwise exempt welfare supplies, constitute tax avoidance. HMRC will refuse new VAT group registrations using these structures and is exercising its power to remove parties from existing VAT groups where these arrangements are present. Any care-group VAT planning must begin from this brief; pre-2025 scheme marketing cannot be relied on.
,  https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/revenue-and-customs-brief-2-2025-the-use-of-vat-grouping-within-the-care-industry/use-of-vat-grouping-within-the-care-industry (verified 2026-07-12: HMRC challenge confirmed; "refuse VAT group registrations designed to facilitate these structures" confirmed; removal powers confirmed)

**3. VAT registration is compulsory only once taxable turnover (not total turnover) exceeds £90,000.**
Exempt care fees do not count toward the VAT registration threshold. A provider whose entire income is from state-regulated welfare services may have total income of several million pounds but zero taxable turnover and therefore no registration obligation. Mixed providers (for example those running a day centre that sells goods or non-exempt courses) must monitor their taxable supplies separately.
,  https://www.gov.uk/vat-registration/when-to-register (verified 2026-07-12: rolling 12-month taxable turnover test confirmed; £90,000 threshold confirmed)

**4. Mixed taxable/exempt providers must run partial exemption calculations; de minimis limits decide whether any input VAT is recoverable.**
Where a provider makes both exempt and taxable supplies, input VAT must be attributed and the partial exemption standard method (or an approved special method) applied. The de minimis test allows full recovery if exempt input tax does not exceed £625 per month on average and is less than half of total input tax in the period. Providers with minimal taxable activity will rarely meet the de minimis threshold and will lose most input VAT.
,  https://www.gov.uk/guidance/partial-exemption-vat-notice-706 (verified 2026-07-12: de minimis limits confirmed at £625/month average; two-part test confirmed)

## B. Workforce pay: sleep-ins, travel time and NMW (the domiciliary moat)

**5. For sleep-in shifts, only time actually awake for the purposes of working counts for National Minimum Wage.**
The Supreme Court settled this in Royal Mencap Society v Tomlinson-Blake [2021] UKSC 8. Workers on sleep-in shifts who are permitted to sleep are not entitled to NMW for the sleeping period; they are entitled only for time they are awake and working. The position changes if sleeping facilities are not provided or if the worker is required to remain active throughout (for example where only brief naps are permitted). HMRC's NMW manual at NMWM08180 reflects the salaried-hours treatment: hours during which sleeping facilities are provided and the worker is not awake for the purposes of working are excluded from the minimum wage calculation.
,  https://www.gov.uk/guidance/calculating-the-minimum-wage/working-hours-for-which-the-minimum-wage-must-be-paid (verified 2026-07-12: Mencap Supreme Court judgment confirmed; "awake for the purposes of working" test confirmed; sleeping facilities condition confirmed)
,  https://www.gov.uk/hmrc-internal-manuals/national-minimum-wage-manual/nmwm08180 (verified 2026-07-12: salaried hours exclusion for sleeping time confirmed; "not awake for the purposes of working" confirmed)

**6. Travel time between care calls is working time for NMW; it must be paid at or above the applicable rate.**
Domiciliary care workers travelling between a client's home and the next client's home are working during that journey for NMW purposes. This is the single most common source of NMW underpayment in the sector. Rota systems and invoicing models that pay only for face-to-face contact time and nothing for inter-call travel create unlawful pay shortfalls. Commuting from home to the first call or from the last call home is excluded.
,  https://www.gov.uk/guidance/calculating-the-minimum-wage/working-hours-for-which-the-minimum-wage-must-be-paid (verified 2026-07-12: work-related travel time confirmed as working time; commuting exclusion confirmed)

**7. NLW and NMW from 1 April 2026: £12.71 (21 and over), £10.85 (18 to 20), £8.00 (under-18 and apprentice).**
The National Living Wage applies to workers aged 21 and over. The apprentice rate applies to apprentices under 19 and to those aged 19 or over who are in the first year of their apprenticeship; once they complete their first year, apprentices aged 19 and over move to their age-appropriate rate. For a domiciliary agency, the NLW of £12.71 is the floor for the dominant cost line before employer NIC and holiday pay are added.
,  https://www.gov.uk/national-minimum-wage-rates (verified 2026-07-12: £12.71 for 21+, £10.85 for 18 to 20, £8.00 for under-18 and apprentice confirmed for April 2026)

**8. Mileage reimbursement: AMAP rises to 55p per mile for the first 10,000 miles from 6 April 2026.**
Domiciliary care operators reimbursing workers for inter-call travel at the AMAP rate must use 55p per mile (up from 45p) for the first 10,000 business miles from 6 April 2026. Reimbursement above AMAP is taxable; reimbursement below AMAP allows the worker to claim a mileage deduction. For high-mileage care staff this is a material employer cost and should be reflected in fee modelling.
,  https://www.gov.uk/guidance/rates-and-thresholds-for-employers-2026-to-2027 (verified 2026-07-12: 55p per mile confirmed for cars/vans for 2026-27)

**9. Carers engaged as self-employed are almost always employees or workers in substance.**
Employment-status misclassification is the care sector's standing PAYE and NMW audit risk. Workers on rotas who cannot send substitutes, are told when and where to work, and are subject to the provider's direction will be employees for PAYE purposes regardless of the label on their contract. HMRC audit activity in care is focused precisely on this pattern. Back-dated PAYE, NIC and NMW liability can reach years into the past.
,  https://www.gov.uk/employment-status/employee (verified 2026-07-12: employment tests confirmed; zero-hours and rota arrangements do not create self-employment automatically)

**10. Holiday pay for irregular-hours workers accrues based on hours already worked; the 12.07% shorthand reflects the statutory 5.6-week entitlement expressed as a proportion of the working year.**
Workers without regular hours (bank, zero-hours, casual) accrue holiday at a rate based on hours worked each pay period. The accrual method is the correct approach for these workers. Errors in holiday pay calculation for domiciliary care staff (particularly failing to include average overtime and travel payments in the holiday pay reference period) are a common Employment Tribunal category.
,  https://www.gov.uk/holiday-entitlement-rights (verified 2026-07-12: irregular-hours accrual confirmed; accrual based on hours already worked confirmed; 12.07% holiday entitlement rate stated: "Their entitlement will be 12.07% of the hours they worked in a pay period")

## C. Employer costs and payroll

**11. Employer NIC is 15% above the secondary threshold of £5,000 per year (£96 per week, £417 per month) from April 2025.**
The secondary threshold has been £5,000 per year since April 2025; the same figure applies in 2026-27. For a care provider with a large workforce of part-time workers, each worker exceeding the weekly threshold of £96 generates employer NIC at 15% on earnings above that level. Modelling per-head NIC cost is more accurate than applying a blanket percentage of total payroll.
,  https://www.gov.uk/guidance/rates-and-thresholds-for-employers-2025-to-2026 (verified 2026-07-12: 15% rate confirmed; £5,000 annual secondary threshold confirmed; £96 weekly / £417 monthly confirmed)

**12. Employment Allowance of £10,500 per year offsets employer NIC for eligible businesses.**
Most care operators are eligible for the Employment Allowance, which reduces the employer NIC bill by up to £10,500 per tax year. For a small domiciliary agency or single-home operator whose total employer NIC liability is below £10,500, the allowance eliminates the bill entirely. Larger groups may not fully benefit if NIC liability spans multiple associated entities.
,  https://www.gov.uk/claim-employment-allowance (verified 2026-07-12: £10,500 confirmed)
,  https://www.gov.uk/guidance/rates-and-thresholds-for-employers-2026-to-2027 (verified 2026-07-12: £10,500 confirmed for 2026-27)

## D. Sponsored workforce (post-2025 compliance cluster)

**13. The Health and Care Worker visa defines which overseas workers can be sponsored for care roles; salary floors and eligibility rules move regularly and pages must carry a date.**
The Health and Care Worker visa route covers qualified doctors, nurses, health professionals and adult social care professionals employed by an approved UK sponsor. Eligibility depends on the role, the sponsor's licence status and meeting a minimum salary requirement that varies by role. The visa is valid for up to five years; the immigration healthcare surcharge is waived for this route. Because salary thresholds change, any published figure must be dated to the review period in which it was confirmed.
,  https://www.gov.uk/health-care-worker-visa/eligibility (verified 2026-07-12: route confirmed for adult social care professionals; sponsor licence requirement confirmed; healthcare surcharge waiver confirmed; salary varies by role confirmed)

**14. Sponsoring overseas care workers requires a sponsor licence, with ongoing HR record-keeping duties and per-worker fees that providers routinely underestimate.**
Employers must hold a Home Office sponsor licence to assign a certificate of sponsorship. The licence carries real compliance obligations: records of sponsored workers must be maintained, absences reported and roles verified. The cost of a sponsor licence application plus the immigration skills charge (levied per sponsored worker per year) is a cost-per-head that must be built into care fee models and budgets.
,  https://www.gov.uk/uk-visa-sponsorship-employers (verified 2026-07-12: sponsor licence requirement confirmed; record-keeping duties confirmed; immigration skills charge confirmed)

## E. Buying, building and selling care businesses

**15. Annual Investment Allowance covers up to £1,000,000 of qualifying plant and machinery spend per year.**
Specialist care equipment, hoists, beds, electrical fit-out and similar items are qualifying plant and machinery. AIA at £1m has applied from 1 January 2019. For a new or refurbishing care home, AIA frequently absorbs the entire year-one plant spend at a 100% deduction.
,  https://www.gov.uk/capital-allowances/annual-investment-allowance (verified 2026-07-12: £1,000,000 AIA limit confirmed; from 1 January 2019 confirmed)

**16. Beyond the AIA: the main-rate writing-down allowance is 14% from April 2026 and a new 40% first-year allowance applies to qualifying main-pool additions.**
Finance Act 2026 (c. 11) s.28 substitutes 14% for 18% as the main-rate writing-down allowance in CAA 2001 s.56, effective from 1 April 2026 (corporation tax) and 6 April 2026 (income tax), with a blended rate for straddling accounting periods. Section 29 introduces a new 40% first-year allowance (s.45U CAA 2001) for new, unused main-pool plant and machinery. Capital allowance sequencing for care-home fit-out: claim AIA first, then FYA on residual main-pool additions, then WDA at 14% on the remaining pool.
,  https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2026/11/section/28 (verified 2026-07-12: 18% to 14% substitution in CAA 2001 s.56 confirmed; effective dates confirmed)
,  https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2026/11/section/29 (verified 2026-07-12: 40% first-year allowance introduced under s.45U confirmed; new, unused main-pool plant condition confirmed)

**17. Structures and Buildings Allowance: 3% straight-line on qualifying construction costs for new builds and extensions.**
Care homes built or extended on or after 29 October 2018 qualify for SBA at 3% per year (rising from 2% from 1 April/6 April 2020). The allowance runs for 33 and one-third years from first non-residential use. For a new 40-bed care home costing £4m in construction, SBA generates £120,000 of annual allowances (3% of £4m) until the pool is exhausted.
,  https://www.gov.uk/guidance/claiming-capital-allowances-for-structures-and-buildings (verified 2026-07-12: 3% from 1 April 2020 / 6 April 2020 confirmed; 33 and one-third year write-off confirmed)

**18. Corporation tax: 19% small profits rate up to £50,000; 25% main rate above £250,000; marginal relief between.**
These rates have applied from 1 April 2023. For propco/opco structures common in care (one company owns the property, another operates the care business), associated company rules reduce the thresholds proportionately; the group may reach the 25% rate sooner than a standalone operator would.
,  https://www.gov.uk/corporation-tax-rates (verified 2026-07-12: 19% small profits rate, 25% main rate, £50,000/£250,000 thresholds and marginal relief confirmed)

**19. Selling a care business: Business Asset Disposal Relief is available but the rate is 18% for disposals from 6 April 2026 (not 10%).**
BADR reduces the CGT rate on qualifying business disposals. The rate was 10% for disposals on or before 5 April 2025, 14% between 6 April 2025 and 5 April 2026, and is 18% for disposals from 6 April 2026 onwards. The standard CGT rate for higher-rate taxpayers on non-BADR gains is 24% from 6 April 2026, so the BADR saving is now 6 percentage points (24% less 18%). BADR conditions require 2 years of trading ownership before disposal; propco/opco structures can break eligibility.
,  https://www.gov.uk/business-asset-disposal-relief (verified 2026-07-12: 18% rate from 6 April 2026 confirmed; prior rates of 10% and 14% confirmed; conditions confirmed)
,  https://www.gov.uk/capital-gains-tax/rates (verified 2026-07-12: 24% standard higher-rate CGT from 6 April 2026 confirmed; 18% BADR rate from 6 April 2026 confirmed)

**20. Care homes pay business rates; Small Business Rate Relief applies below £15,000 rateable value (100% below £12,000).**
Most residential care homes are assessed at rateable values well above £15,000 and do not qualify for SBRR. Domiciliary agencies with small offices may be below the threshold. The business rates system is separate from CQC registration fees.
,  https://www.gov.uk/apply-for-business-rate-relief/small-business-rate-relief (verified 2026-07-12: 100% relief below £12,000 rateable value confirmed; taper to zero at £15,000 confirmed)

## F. CQC registration and the regulator's financial paperwork

**21. CQC registration is mandatory before providing regulated activities; the application includes a financial leg.**
Any person or organisation proposing to carry on a regulated activity (including personal care, accommodation for persons requiring nursing or personal care, and nursing care) must be registered with the Care Quality Commission before they begin. The application requires financial information about the proposed provider. Trading before registration is a criminal offence under the Health and Social Care Act 2008.
,  https://www.cqc.org.uk/guidance-providers/registration (verified 2026-07-12: mandatory pre-operation registration confirmed; regulated activities requirement confirmed; financial information requirement confirmed)

**22. New providers must submit a financial viability statement to CQC, normally prepared or signed by an accountant.**
CQC's registration process for new providers includes a requirement to submit a financial viability statement using CQC's own template. This is a productised accounting service: the provider needs an accountant to prepare or validate the statement. Competitors already offer this as a standalone engagement.
,  https://www.cqc.org.uk/guidance-regulation/providers/registration/supporting-documents-provider/document/financial-viability-template (verified 2026-07-12: CQC FVS template URL confirmed live; requirement for new provider registration confirmed)

**23. Large corporate care providers are subject to CQC market oversight; financial distress monitoring is a regulatory reality the content can cite for sector-risk narrative.**
The Care Quality Commission monitors the financial health of the largest care providers under the market oversight regime established by the Care Act 2014. Providers meeting the scale thresholds must notify CQC of material changes in their financial position. This framework exists because the collapse of a large provider can disrupt care for many residents simultaneously.
,  https://www.cqc.org.uk/guidance-providers/market-oversight-corporate-providers/market-oversight-adult-social-care (verified 2026-07-12: market oversight regime confirmed; financial distress monitoring confirmed; Care Act 2014 basis confirmed)

## G. Funding mix: who actually pays the fees

**24. NHS-funded nursing care is a flat weekly NHS payment to nursing homes: £267.68 standard rate, £368.24 higher rate, from 1 April 2026.**
FNC is an NHS contribution to the nursing care component of a nursing home placement where a resident does not qualify for full NHS continuing healthcare. The NHS pays the nursing home directly at the standard or higher rate regardless of the home's own fee level. From 1 April 2026, the standard weekly rate is £267.68 (up from £254.06) and the higher weekly rate is £368.24. Providers must account for FNC income separately from local authority and self-funder fee income.
,  https://www.gov.uk/government/news/better-community-care-thanks-to-nursing-funding-boost (verified 2026-07-12: £267.68 standard rate from 1 April 2026 confirmed; £368.24 higher rate confirmed; prior standard rate of £254.06 confirmed)

**25. NHS continuing healthcare changes the VAT and fee analysis for a placement entirely.**
Where a resident is assessed as qualifying for full NHS continuing healthcare, the NHS funds the full package of care (both nursing and personal care elements) directly. The provider's supply is to the NHS, not to the individual. This alters the VAT analysis and the fee negotiation framework. Pages should not conflate FNC (a nursing top-up) with full CHC funding.
,  https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/national-framework-for-nhs-continuing-healthcare-and-nhs-funded-nursing-care (verified 2026-07-12: CHC national framework publication confirmed; distinction between FNC and CHC confirmed)

**26. Local authority fee levels sit inside the Care Act statutory guidance and the market sustainability regime; the "fair cost of care" framework is the provider's negotiation tool.**
Local authorities have a statutory duty to pay providers a fee that reflects the actual cost of care. The Care Act 2014 statutory guidance and the accompanying market sustainability funding and guidance set out the framework within which LAs must operate. Providers challenging below-cost LA rates should reference this framework and the LA's own market sustainability assessment data. This is a material ongoing revenue risk for providers who are substantially LA-funded.
,  https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/care-act-statutory-guidance/care-and-support-statutory-guidance (verified 2026-07-12: Care Act statutory guidance confirmed; fair cost of care obligations confirmed)
,  https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/market-sustainability-and-improvement-fund-2024-to-2025 (verified 2026-07-12: market sustainability fund guidance confirmed)

## H. Admin regimes

**27. Making Tax Digital for Income Tax: £50,000 threshold from 6 April 2026; £30,000 from 6 April 2027; £20,000 from 6 April 2028.**
Sole-trader care operators and landlords with combined self-employment and property income above £50,000 must use MTD-compatible software and submit quarterly updates from 6 April 2026. The threshold drops to £30,000 from 6 April 2027 and to £20,000 from 6 April 2028. Owner-operated domiciliary agencies and single-home operators held personally are directly in scope at the first threshold.
,  https://www.gov.uk/guidance/check-if-youre-eligible-for-making-tax-digital-for-income-tax (verified 2026-07-12: £50,000 threshold from 6 April 2026 confirmed; £30,000 from 6 April 2027 confirmed; £20,000 from 6 April 2028 confirmed)

**28. Dividends from care companies: 10.75% (basic rate), 35.75% (higher rate), 39.35% (additional rate) from 6 April 2026.**
Owner-directors of care companies incorporated under the standard owner-managed model (salary to personal allowance, remainder as dividends) pay dividend tax at these rates on amounts above the £500 annual dividend allowance. The rates apply from 6 April 2026.
,  https://www.gov.uk/tax-on-dividends (verified 2026-07-12: 10.75% basic, 35.75% higher, 39.35% additional rates confirmed; £500 dividend allowance confirmed)

## I. Data asset sources

**29. Companies House SIC 87xxx codes cover residential care; SIC 88xxx covers non-residential social care without accommodation.**
SIC 87100 (residential nursing care activities), 87200 (residential care for mental health, substance abuse and learning disability), 87300 (residential care for the elderly and disabled) and 87900 (other residential care) cover care home operators. SIC 88100 (social work activities without accommodation for the elderly and disabled) and 88990 (other social work without accommodation) cover domiciliary and day-service operators. SIC codes are self-reported at incorporation and may not be updated; the index page must carry this caveat prominently.
,  https://resources.companieshouse.gov.uk/sic/ (verified 2026-07-12: SIC 87/88 structure confirmed)
,  https://download.companieshouse.gov.uk/en_output.html (verified 2026-07-12: bulk data download confirmed)

**30. Skills for Care state-of-the-sector report is the authoritative workforce data source for the care site data asset.**
Skills for Care publishes annual workforce intelligence covering registered providers, staff numbers, vacancy rates, turnover and pay across adult social care in England. This data underpins any data-asset coverage of the sector's workforce crisis narrative and supports the NLW/NIC cost modelling content.
,  https://www.skillsforcare.org.uk/Adult-Social-Care-Workforce-Data/Workforce-intelligence/publications/national-information/The-state-of-the-adult-social-care-sector-and-workforce-in-England.aspx (verified 2026-07-12: Skills for Care state-of-report landing page confirmed live; latest edition covers 2024 data for adult social care sector)

## Consistency rules for writers

- NMW rates (position 7) and mileage (position 8) are year-sensitive; re-verify at content-write time per the estate fresh-data rule.
- Sleep-in NMW (position 5): the Mencap ruling means providers do not pay NMW for sleeping periods; never frame sleep-in shifts as fully NMW-paid throughout.
- Travel-time NMW (position 6): inter-call travel must be paid; never reduce to "care workers are paid per visit".
- VAT exemption (position 1): exemption is a cost, not a benefit; never frame it as a tax advantage.
- RCB 2/2025 (position 2): pre-2025 VAT-grouping schemes are under active HMRC challenge; never present them as established planning.
- BADR rate (position 19): the rate is 18% for disposals from 6 April 2026, not 10%; the 10% rate ended April 2025.
- FNC rates (position 24): £267.68 standard from 1 April 2026; re-verify annually before any fee-model content is written.
