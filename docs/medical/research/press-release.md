# Press summary: annual allowance pension tax charges reached £350m in 2023/24 across UK pension schemes

*A press-ready data summary. Faceless by design: attribute to "Medical Accountants UK
analysis of HMRC and NHSBSA open data". No named spokesperson. Free to use with a
link to medicalaccounts.co.uk.*

Report page: https://www.medicalaccounts.co.uk/research/annual-allowance-pension-tax-index
Raw CSV: https://www.medicalaccounts.co.uk/research/annual-allowance-pension-tax-index/data
Methodology: https://www.medicalaccounts.co.uk/research/annual-allowance-pension-tax-index (methodology section)

## The headline

Across all UK registered pension schemes, annual allowance charges settled
through Scheme Pays reached **£350m in 2023/24 (provisional)**, up from
**£64m in 2016/17**, according to Medical Accountants UK analysis of HMRC
Private pension statistics.

The number of people reporting pension savings above the annual allowance through
Self Assessment peaked at **56,270 individuals in 2021/22** (all UK registered
schemes, revised figure), up from **18,720 in 2016/17**. The 2021/22 peak was
reached despite the standard allowance remaining at £40,000 throughout that
period.

An NHS-specific layer, drawn from NHSBSA Freedom of Information data for the
England and Wales scheme, shows that **46,135 officer members** (hospital doctors
and other non-GP staff) and **7,991 practitioner members** (GPs) had pension
growth above the standard allowance in 2021/22. The officer count reflects that
year's high CPI revaluation of the 2015 NHS pension scheme, which inflated
members' measured pension input independently of any change in pay.

---

## Key findings

- **£350m in Scheme Pays charges (2023/24, provisional), all UK registered
  pension schemes (HMRC).** The value of annual allowance charges settled through
  pension schemes' Accounting for Tax returns has risen more than five-fold since
  2016/17.
- **56,270 individuals reported pension savings above the annual allowance through
  Self Assessment in 2021/22 (all UK schemes, revised), the series peak (HMRC).**
  This is a count of individuals, not a tax charge value: the Self Assessment
  column records excess contributions above the allowance, not the charge itself.
- **46,135 NHS officer members exceeded the standard annual allowance in 2021/22
  (NHS Pension Scheme, England and Wales, NHSBSA FOI snapshot).** Alongside 7,991
  NHS practitioners (GPs). Together, 54,126 NHS members exceeded the standard
  allowance that year.
- **The standard allowance fell from £215,000 in 2006/07 to £40,000, then rose to
  £60,000 from 2023/24 (HMRC).** Falling allowances, not only growing pensions,
  drove the rise in the number of members pulled over the threshold.
- **The apparent fall in Self Assessment figures in 2022/23 is a reporting
  artefact.** NHS and other public-service members were directed to report 2022/23
  charges through HMRC's public service pension adjustment service (the McCloud
  remedy route) rather than Self Assessment, pulling a large block of charges out
  of the SA count. The underlying burden did not fall.
- **Annual data, around two years in arrears.** HMRC publishes each July; the
  2025 edition reaches 2023/24, still provisional. The next edition is due summer
  2026.

## Angles per desk

### GP and BMA trade press (Pulse, GPonline, BMA News)

The NHSBSA FOI data allows a practitioner-specific read. In 2021/22, 7,991 GP
members of the NHS Pension Scheme (England and Wales) had pension growth above
the standard annual allowance (NHSBSA FOI-02228, snapshot basis, standard
allowance only). A separate FOI release (FOI-02711) shows that in 2019/20,
GPs registered 8,239 Scheme Pays election forms and hospital doctors 9,745,
together accounting for the majority of roughly 19,900 forms that year (England
and Wales, submission-date basis). These counts sit inside the national
whole-market figure of £350m in Scheme Pays charges (2023/24, all UK schemes,
HMRC). The HMRC series has no NHS-specific money split; the FOI layer supplies
the NHS colour.

Suggested angle: "How many GPs exceeded the annual allowance" (with the
2021/22 FOI count and the 2016/17 step-up when the taper was introduced).

### Medico-finance (Medeconomics, medical money pages)

The combination of the HMRC money spine and the NHS corroboration layer gives
medico-finance desks a sourced, year-by-year picture of charge growth they can
illustrate. The key numbers: Scheme Pays values up from £64m (2016/17) to £350m
(2023/24, provisional), across all UK schemes. NHS officer members over the
standard allowance rose from 12,562 in 2015/16 to 46,135 in 2021/22 before
dropping sharply in 2022/23 (a reporting artefact, not a real fall).

Suggested angle: "The annual allowance charge in numbers" (the £ spine, the NHS
FOI counts, and the policy context of the taper introduction and the 2023/24
allowance rise to £60,000).

### Health-policy (HSJ)

The McCloud reporting artefact is directly relevant to health-policy readers.
The 2022/23 Self Assessment fall (56,270 to 34,190 individuals) is the result of
a deliberate policy routing change, not a genuine decline in the pension tax
burden on NHS staff. The 2021/22 NHS spike (46,135 officers over the standard
allowance) is explained by that year's high CPI revaluation of the 2015 scheme,
a mechanical effect of how defined-benefit pension growth is calculated. The
2023/24 allowance rise to £60,000 will eventually reduce the measured burden, but
the data lags by around two years, so the full effect is not yet visible.

Suggested angle: "What the numbers say about the annual allowance after McCloud"
(policy context, the McCloud routing effect on SA data, the 2023/24 allowance
reform, and what to watch for when the 2024/25 data publishes in summer 2026).

### Personal-finance nationals

The whole-market HMRC series is directly relevant to personal-finance coverage,
because annual allowance charges affect all UK pension savers, not only NHS
doctors. The Scheme Pays value series is the clearest single money figure: £64m
in 2016/17 rising to £350m in 2023/24 (provisional), a period during which the
standard allowance stayed at £40,000 while pensions grew. The 2023/24 allowance
rise to £60,000 is the first substantive relief in a decade.

Suggested angle: "Annual allowance charges in pension savings: the data behind
the £350m Scheme Pays bill" (the HMRC whole-market spine, the policy driver of
falling allowances, and the 2023/24 reform).

### Regional pairing ("doctors in [city]" hooks)

The HMRC series is a UK-wide whole-market figure, so it does not break down
by region. However, regional desks can pair the national trend with local NHS
staffing context using publicly available NHS workforce data. The angle works
as: "Doctors across [region] are among the 1.87 million active NHS Pension
Scheme members in England and Wales (at 31 March 2025) who face the annual
allowance. Nationally, 46,135 officer members exceeded the standard allowance
in 2021/22 alone (NHS Pension Scheme, England and Wales, NHSBSA FOI)."

The local specificity comes from the NHS workforce or the GP practice statistics;
the national charge data (both the HMRC money spine and the NHSBSA FOI counts)
provides the sourced backdrop.

**Flags on specific figures.**

- 56,270 SA individuals (2021/22): revised.
- 46,135 NHS officer members (2021/22): NHSBSA FOI snapshot, England and Wales,
  standard allowance only, pre-McCloud-rollback basis, as at 26 September 2024.
- 2022/23 NHS FOI figures (total 33 members shown as exceeding the allowance):
  radically undercounted at the snapshot date; not used in this release.

**The Self Assessment value column is not a tax charge.** The HMRC SA column
records the value of pension savings above the annual allowance (excess
contributions), not the value of the tax charge itself. HMRC does not publish an
SA annual allowance charge value. Only the Accounting for Tax (Scheme Pays)
column carries a genuine settled-charge figure.

**Scope.** HMRC figures: all UK registered pension schemes. NHSBSA figures: NHS
Pension Scheme, England and Wales. Scotland (SPPA) and Northern Ireland (HSC)
run separate schemes and are not included in the NHSBSA data.

**Update cadence.** Annual. HMRC publishes each July; next edition due summer
2026. No monthly or quarterly updates.
