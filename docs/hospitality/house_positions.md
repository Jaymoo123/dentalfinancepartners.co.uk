## A. Food and drink VAT (the traffic cluster)

**1. Hot takeaway food is standard-rated (20%); most cold takeaway food is zero-rated.**
Hot food is determined by five tests: whether it was intentionally heated for consumption, heated to order, kept hot after cooking (for example in a heated cabinet), supplied in heat-retentive packaging, or advertised and marketed as a hot supply. Food that meets any one of these tests is standard-rated. Freshly baked goods that are incidentally warm at point of sale but satisfy none of the five tests may remain zero-rated.
,  https://www.gov.uk/guidance/catering-takeaway-food-and-vat-notice-7091 (verified 2026-07-12: all five tests confirmed; "freshly baked" caveat confirmed)

**2. Anything consumed on the supplier's premises is catering and standard-rated.**
This applies regardless of whether the food is hot or cold, and includes food courts and areas with designated seating. A business selling cold sandwiches with a seating area must charge standard rate on eat-in sales.
,  https://www.gov.uk/guidance/catering-takeaway-food-and-vat-notice-7091 (verified 2026-07-12: "supplies consumed on the supplier's premises are always standard-rated")

**3. Zero-rating for food has four standing carve-outs: confectionery, crisps and savoury snacks, soft drinks, and alcohol.**
Confectionery includes chocolates, sweets and chocolate-covered biscuits. Savoury snacks covers potato crisps, puffed cereals and roasted or salted nuts when packaged and ready to eat. Soft drinks covers carbonated beverages, fruit cordials and squashes. All supplies of alcoholic drinks are standard-rated regardless of context. Ice cream is also standard-rated. Most other food for human consumption is zero-rated.
,  https://www.gov.uk/guidance/food-products-and-vat-notice-70114 (verified 2026-07-12: all four categories confirmed; ice cream confirmed standard-rated)

**4. VAT registration is compulsory once rolling 12-month taxable turnover exceeds £90,000.**
The trigger is a rolling 12-month test, not an annual accounting period. A cafe that crosses the threshold mid-year must register within 30 days. Hospitality businesses with mixed standard-rated and zero-rated supplies should monitor monthly, not at year-end only.
,  https://www.gov.uk/vat-registration/when-to-register (verified 2026-07-12: "your total taxable turnover for the last 12 months goes over £90,000")

**5. The VAT Flat Rate Scheme sector rates differ by hospitality trade.**
Catering (including restaurants, licensed and unlicensed, cafes, sandwich shops, takeaways and mobile stands): **12.5%**. Public houses (licensed clubs and pubs however managed): **6.5%**. Hotels and accommodation (bed and breakfast, hotels, motels, self-catering, youth hostels, camping and caravan sites): **10.5%**. A business applying the wrong sector rate is accounting incorrectly; the tradescode determines the rate, not the business's own description. The limited-cost-trader flat rate of 16.5% (which applies when goods purchased are less than 2% of FRS turnover or £1,000/year, whichever is higher) can override the sector rate for wet-led businesses with low goods spend.
,  https://www.gov.uk/hmrc-internal-manuals/vat-flat-rate-scheme/frs7300 (verified 2026-07-12: 12.5% catering, 6.5% pubs, 10.5% hotels confirmed) and https://www.gov.uk/vat-flat-rate-scheme (verified 2026-07-12: FRS eligibility and general mechanics confirmed)
Limited-cost-business rate confirmed: "You pay a higher rate of 16.5%" where goods cost less than 2% of turnover, or less than £1,000 a year if costs are more than 2% of turnover. https://www.gov.uk/vat-flat-rate-scheme/how-much-you-pay (checker-verified 2026-07-12)

**6. TOMS applies when a hotel or operator packages bought-in travel elements.**
If an operator bundles accommodation, transport or other travel services bought from third parties and sells them as a package, the Tour Operators' Margin Scheme applies: VAT is due only on the margin (the difference between the price charged to the customer and the cost of the bought-in elements). Input VAT on the bought-in elements cannot be recovered. TOMS applies even if travel is not the operator's main activity.
,  https://www.gov.uk/guidance/tour-operators-margin-scheme-for-vat-notice-7095 (verified 2026-07-12: margin-only VAT confirmed; no input VAT recovery on bought-in elements confirmed; wide application confirmed)

## B. Tips, tronc and payroll (the differentiation cluster)

**7. Tips paid through an independent tronc are free of employer and employee NIC; PAYE income tax still applies.**
Where gratuities are paid via an independently run tronc and the employer is not involved in the allocation either directly or indirectly, there is no NIC liability for either employer or employee. The troncmaster must genuinely control who receives what; any employer involvement in allocation destroys the NIC exemption. Income tax via PAYE is still due on tronc receipts. This position is set out in the National Insurance Manual at NIM02922 (and the wider NIM tronc chapter). Checker-confirmed 2026-07-12: NIM02922 is the correct anchor for the core exemption; NIM02935 covers the separate case of employer top-up payments, which are contractual earnings and NIC-liable (https://www.gov.uk/hmrc-internal-manuals/national-insurance-manual/nim02935).
,  https://www.gov.uk/hmrc-internal-manuals/national-insurance-manual/nim02922 (verified 2026-07-12: "If gratuities are paid via an independently run tronc and the employer is not involved in the allocation either directly or indirectly, there is no NICs liability") and https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/e24-tips-gratuities-service-charges-and-troncs (verified 2026-07-12: E24 guidance confirmed as covering NIC treatment and troncs; full HTML body not parsed this pass, detailed PAYE/independence text anchored via NIM02922)

**8. Since the Employment (Allocation of Tips) Act 2023, 100% of qualifying tips must reach workers.**
The Act (2023 c. 13), in force from 1 October 2024, requires employers to pass on all tips to workers without deductions and to do so fairly, in accordance with the statutory Code of Practice. Employers must have a written tips policy and keep records of tip allocation. Agency workers are covered. Workers can bring claims to an employment tribunal. The Act does not prescribe the distribution method but requires it to be fair and transparent.
,  https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2023/13/contents (verified 2026-07-12: Employment (Allocation of Tips) Act 2023 confirmed; sections on independent troncs, agency workers, written policy and enforcement confirmed) and https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/distributing-tips-fairly-statutory-code-of-practice (verified 2026-07-12: statutory Code of Practice confirmed, effective 1 October 2024; "all tips to be passed on to workers without deductions" confirmed) and https://www.gov.uk/tips-at-work (verified 2026-07-12: written policy and record-keeping obligations confirmed)

**9. Cash tips kept directly by staff are the worker's own tax responsibility; employer-distributed tips must go through payroll.**
Tips handed directly by a customer to a member of staff (and kept by that person, not pooled by the employer) are the employee's personal income and the employee is responsible for declaring them. Tips paid into a system the employer controls and then paid out are employment income and must go through payroll.
,  https://www.gov.uk/tips-at-work (verified 2026-07-12)

**10. Tips of any kind can never count toward the National Minimum Wage or National Living Wage.**
This is a long-standing rule confirmed by the GOV.UK tips guidance: "If you get tips at work they do not count towards the National Minimum Wage." This means a business that relies on tips to bring workers up to minimum wage rates is breaking the law.
,  https://www.gov.uk/tips-at-work (verified 2026-07-12)

**11. NLW and NMW from 1 April 2026: £12.71 (21+), £10.85 (18 to 20), £8.00 (under-18 and apprentice).**
The National Living Wage applies to workers aged 21 and over. The apprentice rate applies to apprentices under 19 and to those aged 19 or over who are in the first year of their apprenticeship; once they complete their first year, apprentices aged 19+ move to their age-appropriate rate.
,  https://www.gov.uk/national-minimum-wage-rates (verified 2026-07-12: all three rates confirmed for April 2026)

**12. Employer NIC is 15% above the secondary threshold of £5,000 per year (£96 per week, £417 per month) from April 2025.**
The secondary threshold has been £5,000 per year since April 2025 and the same figure applies in 2026-27. Employers begin paying NIC when an employee's earnings exceed this threshold.
,  https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/rates-and-allowances-national-insurance-contributions/rates-and-allowances-national-insurance-contributions (verified 2026-07-12: 15% rate confirmed for both 2025-26 and 2026-27; secondary threshold confirmed)

**13. Employment Allowance of £10,500 per year offsets employer NIC for eligible businesses.**
Most hospitality employers are eligible for the Employment Allowance, which reduces their employer NIC bill by up to £10,500 per tax year. This is material for small kitchens and front-of-house teams carrying part-time and zero-hours workers. Employers whose NIC liability is below £10,500 benefit in full.
,  https://www.gov.uk/claim-employment-allowance (verified 2026-07-12: £10,500 confirmed) and https://www.gov.uk/guidance/rates-and-thresholds-for-employers-2026-to-2027 (verified 2026-07-12: £10,500 confirmed in 2026-27 thresholds)

**14. Casual and zero-hours workers are almost always employees for PAYE purposes, not self-employed.**
The employment status tests on GOV.UK identify workers as likely employees when they are required to work regularly, are told how and when to work, cannot freely substitute someone else, and work under the employer's control and direction. Zero-hours arrangements alone do not make someone self-employed. Misclassification as self-employed is a common HMRC audit focus in the hospitality sector and carries back-dated NIC and income tax liability.
,  https://www.gov.uk/employment-status/employee (verified 2026-07-12: key employment tests confirmed; zero-hours not a disqualifier for employee status)

## C. Alcohol, licensing and duties (pubs and bars moat)

**15. Buying alcoholic stock from unapproved wholesalers breaches AWRS due-diligence requirements.**
Any business buying alcohol for resale from a UK wholesaler must verify that the wholesaler is approved by HMRC under the Alcohol Wholesaler Registration Scheme (AWRS) and must repeat this check regularly, keeping records as evidence of due diligence. The wholesaler's Unique Reference Number (URN) should be verified before purchase and periodically thereafter.
,  https://www.gov.uk/guidance/the-alcohol-wholesaler-registration-scheme-awrs (verified 2026-07-12: buyer due-diligence obligation confirmed; URN check and record-keeping confirmed; wholesalers trading without approval face penalties)

**16. Draught relief gives lower alcohol duty rates on qualifying draught products sold from containers of at least 20 litres.**
Draught products at 3.5% to below 8.5% ABV attract duty of £19.45 per litre of pure alcohol (beer, spirits, wine and other fermented products) or £8.95 per litre (still cider, sparkling cider 3.5% to 5.5% ABV). This is materially lower than the equivalent packaged rate of £22.58 per litre for beer at 3.5% to 8.4% ABV. GP% and pricing models for cask and keg products should use the draught rate, not the packaged rate. Products below 1.2% ABV attract zero duty.
,  https://www.gov.uk/guidance/alcohol-duty-rates (verified 2026-07-12: draught relief rate table confirmed; 3.5-8.5% ABV beer/wine/spirits £19.45, still cider £8.95, packaged beer £22.58 confirmed)

**17. A premises licence and a designated premises supervisor (DPS) holding a personal licence are both required to sell alcohol in England and Wales.**
"Businesses, organisations and individuals who want to sell or supply alcohol in England and Wales must have a licence or other authorisation from a licensing authority." Additionally, all such businesses "must have a designated premises supervisor" who holds a personal licence. Tax treatment of licensing costs is split, not uniformly deductible: HMRC's BIM61405 (licensed houses, legal etc expenses) confirms that "legal etc expenses incurred in connection with the first application for a licence are not an allowable deduction from trading profits" (Kneeshaw v Albertolli), while "legal expenses incurred in obtaining renewal of an existing licence are allowable as a deduction". Pages must distinguish first-application costs (capital, not deductible) from renewal costs (deductible).
https://www.gov.uk/hmrc-internal-manuals/business-income-manual/bim61405 (checker-verified 2026-07-12; corrects the producer draft, which asserted blanket deductibility)
,  https://www.gov.uk/guidance/alcohol-licensing (verified 2026-07-12: premises licence obligation confirmed; DPS/personal licence requirement confirmed)

**18. Gaming machines require Machine Games Duty registration before the machines are made available to play.**
Any operator whose machines offer cash prizes must register for Machine Games Duty before those machines go live. The duty falls on the operator holding the qualifying gambling or alcohol licence. Registration is not required for machines where prizes are less than the cost to play.
,  https://www.gov.uk/guidance/machine-games-duty (verified 2026-07-12: registration-before-play requirement confirmed; qualifying licence holders confirmed)

## D. Property costs and rates

**19. Retail, Hospitality and Leisure business rates relief has ended for new claims from 1 April 2026; from that date businesses use revised rate multipliers.**
The GOV.UK guidance states: "You cannot make a new claim for retail, hospitality and leisure relief. From 1 April 2026, you will need to use rate multipliers to calculate your business rates bill." Pages must not assert a current RHL relief percentage without checking the current-year position. The scheme operated at varying percentages and cash caps in prior years and was subject to annual change.
,  https://www.gov.uk/business-rates-relief/retail-discount (verified 2026-07-12: confirmed that new claims are no longer available from 1 April 2026; rate multipliers apply instead)
2026-27 multipliers confirmed (England): RHL properties pay 38.2p (rateable value below £51,000) or 43p (£51,000 to £499,999), set 5p below the national equivalents of 43.2p and 48p; properties at £500,000 or more pay 50.8p. https://www.gov.uk/calculate-your-business-rates and https://www.gov.uk/guidance/business-rates-multipliers-qualifying-retail-hospitality-or-leisure (checker-verified 2026-07-12). Transitional protection for existing relief was not separately confirmed; do not assert it.

**20. Small Business Rate Relief: 100% at rateable value up to £12,000; tapers to zero at £15,000.**
Properties with a rateable value of £12,000 or less receive 100% relief on business rates, provided it is the only property the business uses. The relief tapers between £12,001 and £15,000, with the page giving examples of 50% at £13,500 and 33% at £14,000. Many takeaways and small cafes qualify and do not claim.
,  https://www.gov.uk/apply-for-business-rate-relief/small-business-rate-relief (verified 2026-07-12: 100% at £12,000 confirmed; taper confirmed; £13,500/50% and £14,000/33% examples confirmed)

**21. B&B and guest-house owners letting rooms in their own home may use Rent-a-Room relief of up to £7,500 per year, but only where it is genuinely their residence.**
The Rent-a-Room Scheme allows up to £7,500 of letting income per year to be tax-free (£3,750 if shared with another person). It explicitly covers bed and breakfast and guest houses. The condition is that the owner must live in the property; it cannot be applied to a property used solely as a trading premises. Operating a full guest house as a trade (not letting part of a home) is a trade, and trading income rules apply rather than Rent-a-Room.
,  https://www.gov.uk/rent-room-in-your-home/the-rent-a-room-scheme (verified 2026-07-12: £7,500 confirmed; B&B and guest house explicitly confirmed; residence condition confirmed)

## E. Structure, tax and compliance

**22. The Annual Investment Allowance covers up to £1,000,000 of qualifying plant and machinery spend per year.**
Kitchen equipment, fit-out, refrigeration units and similar items are qualifying plant. AIA at £1m has applied from 1 January 2019. From the 2026-27 tax year, the main pool Writing Down Allowance is 14% (reduced by Finance Act 2026) and a 40% First Year Allowance is available for qualifying main pool additions (estate ground truth: property_capital_allowances_2026_ground_truth). Capital-allowance sequencing matters for large refits: claim AIA first, then FYA, then WDA.
,  https://www.gov.uk/capital-allowances/annual-investment-allowance (verified 2026-07-12: £1,000,000 AIA limit confirmed; "from 1 January 2019" confirmed)
FA 2026 confirmed: Finance Act 2026 (c. 11) s.28 substitutes 14% for 18% as the main rate of writing-down allowances (CAA 2001 s.56), effective for chargeable periods from 1 April 2026 (CT) / 6 April 2026 (IT), with a blended rate for straddling periods; s.29 introduces the new 40% first-year allowance. https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2026/11/section/28 and https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/new-first-year-allowance-and-main-rate-of-writing-down-allowances/capital-allowances-new-first-year-allowance-and-reducing-main-rate-writing-down-allowances (checker-verified 2026-07-12)

**23. Corporation tax: 19% small profits rate up to £50,000; 25% main rate above £250,000; marginal relief between.**
These rates have applied from 1 April 2023. The thresholds are reduced proportionately for short accounting periods and by the number of associated companies, so group-owned hotel operations may face the main rate sooner. Ring-fence profits from oil and gas are taxed differently and are not relevant to this site.
,  https://www.gov.uk/corporation-tax-rates (verified 2026-07-12: 19% small profits rate, 25% main rate, £50,000/£250,000 thresholds, marginal relief confirmed)

**24. MTD for Income Tax applies to sole-trader operators with self-employment and property income over £50,000 from 6 April 2026.**
From that date, affected individuals must use MTD-compatible software to keep digital records and send quarterly updates to HMRC. The £50,000 threshold is confirmed for April 2026. Sole-trader cafe, takeaway and pub operators above this threshold are in scope from day one.
,  https://www.gov.uk/guidance/sign-up-your-client-for-making-tax-digital-for-income-tax (verified 2026-07-12: "required to use Making Tax Digital for Income Tax from 6 April 2026 if their total annual income from self-employment and property is over £50,000" confirmed)
£30,000 threshold confirmed: GOV.UK states that if qualifying income was over "£30,000 for the 2025 to 2026 tax year, you will need to use it from 6 April 2027" (and over £20,000 for 2026-27, from 6 April 2028). https://www.gov.uk/guidance/check-if-youre-eligible-for-making-tax-digital-for-income-tax (checker-verified 2026-07-12)

**25. Cash basis is the default for unincorporated businesses from April 2024; accruals can be elected.**
From 6 April 2024, cash basis became the standard method for sole traders and partnerships without corporate partners. Traditional accruals accounting is still available by election. Stock-heavy hospitality businesses (those with significant food and drink inventory) typically benefit from accruals, because cash basis can distort profit recognition when stock is held at period end. This should be an active decision, not a default.
,  https://www.gov.uk/simpler-income-tax-cash-basis (verified 2026-07-12: cash basis as standard method confirmed; accruals available by election confirmed; reference to "before 6 April 2024" section confirming the change confirmed)

**26. Food businesses must register with the local authority at least 28 days before they begin trading.**
This applies in England, Wales and Northern Ireland. Registration is required before the first day of trading, not just before opening to the public. While the GOV.UK guidance page does not specify a fee, registration is widely understood to be free; the legal duty to register is clear ("You are required to register at least 28 days before trading").
,  https://www.gov.uk/food-business-registration (verified 2026-07-12: 28-day pre-trading registration requirement confirmed for England, Wales and Northern Ireland)
Both points confirmed: GOV.UK states "Registration of your food business is free and can't be refused" (https://www.gov.uk/guidance/starting-a-food-business, checker-verified 2026-07-12). Failure to register is an offence: regulation 19(1) of the Food Safety and Hygiene (England) Regulations 2013 (SI 2013/2996) makes contravention of the specified provisions an offence, and Schedule 2 specifies the Article 6(2) Regulation (EC) 852/2004 requirement to notify the competent authority of each food establishment. https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2013/2996/regulation/19 and https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2013/2996/schedule/2 (checker-verified 2026-07-12)

## F. Data and asset methodology positions

**27. Hospitality company counts derive from Companies House SIC 55/56 registrations; the self-reported SIC caveat must be stated openly.**
Section I of the Companies House SIC code list covers Accommodation (SIC 55) and Food and Drink Service Activities (SIC 56), including SIC 55100 (hotels and similar accommodation), 56101 (licensed restaurants), 56102 (unlicensed restaurants and cafes), 56103 (takeaways and mobile food stands), 56301 (licensed clubs) and 56302 (public houses and bars). SIC codes are self-reported at incorporation and may not be updated when a business changes activity; the index page must carry this caveat prominently.
,  https://resources.companieshouse.gov.uk/sic/ (verified 2026-07-12: SIC 55/56 structure and relevant codes confirmed) and https://download.companieshouse.gov.uk/en_output.html (verified 2026-07-12: bulk data download page confirmed, snapshot dated July 2026)

**28. Dissolution counts are cross-checked against Insolvency Service statistics before any "closures" headline ships.**
The Insolvency Service publishes monthly company and individual insolvency statistics. From April 2024 these are published as separate company and individual series (the unified monthly collection was retired). Any hospitality "closures" or "failures" figure in site content or the data index must be reconciled against the Insolvency Service data before publication.
,  https://www.gov.uk/government/collections/monthly-insolvency-statistics (verified 2026-07-12: confirmed as the archive collection; note that from April 2024 the data is split into separate company and individual series)

## Consistency rules for writers

- Figures in positions 11, 12, 19 and 22 are year-sensitive; re-verify at content-write time per the estate fresh-data rule.
- Never imply tips can count toward NMW/NLW (position 10); this is a recurring misconception in hospitality content.
- Tronc NIC exemption (position 7) requires genuine troncmaster independence; any employer allocation involvement destroys it. Do not frame it as automatic.
- The Tips Act 2023 (position 8) has been in force since 1 October 2024; do not frame it as forthcoming.
- VAT catering rules (positions 1 to 3) require careful hot/cold and eat-in/takeaway distinction; never reduce to "hot food is taxed".
- RHL relief has ended for new claims from April 2026 (position 19); do not assert a relief percentage without checking the current-year position.
- Cash basis default (position 25): default since April 2024 only; pre-April 2024 clients may still be on accruals by default.
- TOMS (position 6) applies to any operator packaging bought-in travel elements, not only dedicated tour operators.
