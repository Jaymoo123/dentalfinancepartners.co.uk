## 1. Associate self-employment status (locked, 2026-06-03)

**Position.** A UK dental associate is normally self-employed for tax purposes, but status is determined by the **substance of the working arrangement, not the contract label**. The BDA model associate agreement is drafted to support self-employed status and is good evidence, but it does not guarantee HMRC or a tribunal will accept the status. HMRC weighs the established status factors: **control, personal service/substitution, mutuality of obligation, financial risk, and integration**. None is decisive alone; the overall picture governs. Most associates pass because the dental model (clinical autonomy, own indemnity and loupes, fee-split payment) naturally supports self-employment, but a rostered, practice-supplied, no-autonomy arrangement carries genuine status risk.

- **Statutory / authority hooks:** there is no single statutory "self-employment" test; status rests on case-law factors derived from *Ready Mixed Concrete v Minister of Pensions* [1968] 2 QB 497 and the later body of employment-status case law. HMRC applies these via the **Check Employment Status for Tax (CEST)** tool and the Employment Status Manual.
- **HMRC manual anchors:** **ESM0500+** (employment status, the irreducible minimum and the factors); CEST guidance at ESM11000+.
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** lead with "substance over paperwork"; the BDA model contract is supportive evidence, not a shield. Walk the factors as they actually present in a dental setting. Do NOT state that the BDA agreement "guarantees" self-employed status. Do NOT cite a fixed statutory section as the status test (there isn't one). Cross-ref §1.A for limited-company locums.

### 1.A IR35 / off-payroll for limited-company locums on NHS engagements (locked, 2026-06-03)

**Position.** A dentist operating through a personal service company (PSC) on NHS engagements is inside the off-payroll regime. From **6 April 2021**, where the engaging client is **medium or large** (most NHS practices and dental groups are), the **client, not the PSC, determines IR35 status** and must issue a **Status Determination Statement (SDS)** with reasons, taking reasonable care. Inside-IR35: the fee-payer operates PAYE/NIC deductions before paying the PSC, so the company receives net and tax-efficient salary/dividend extraction of that income is not available. Outside-IR35: the fee-payer pays gross and the PSC extracts normally. A locum across multiple practices may hold a mix of inside and outside determinations.

- **Statutory hooks:** off-payroll working rules in **Chapter 10, Part 2, Income Tax (Earnings and Pensions) Act 2003 (ITEPA 2003)** (the "Chapter 10" client-led rules from 6 Apr 2021); the original intermediaries legislation (IR35) is **Chapter 8, Part 2 ITEPA 2003**. https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2003/1
- **HMRC manual anchors:** **ESM10000+** (off-payroll working / IR35).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** state clearly that the SDS sits with the practice/client, not the locum; the locum can challenge a determination within the 45-day client-led disagreement process. Do NOT write that "the dentist decides their own IR35 status" for medium/large NHS clients (that was the pre-April-2021 position). Cross-ref §5 on whether the limited-company route still earns its overhead.

### 1.B Foundation dentist (DFT/VT) salaried first year and the move to self-employment (locked, 2026-06-03, HP-lock gate Wave 3 cluster A)

**Position.** A newly qualified dentist intending NHS practice in England must normally complete **one year of dental foundation training (DFT, historically "vocational training"/VT)**. During DFT the foundation dentist is a **salaried employee** (PAYE, not self-employed): tax and Class 1 NIC are deducted at source, the trainer/practice runs the payroll, and the dentist is an **NHS Pension Scheme officer** for that year (the DFT salary is pensionable). The **national DFT salary is £42,408 from 1 April 2025** (set in the Primary Dental Services Statement of Financial Entitlements; paid the same across England, Wales and Northern Ireland; a 35-hour week). After DFT the dentist typically becomes a **self-employed associate** (status per §1) on a fee-split or per-UDA basis, which is the **point at which Self Assessment, payments on account (§8), allowable expenses (§8) and the practitioner pension route (§2.C) all begin**. The first self-employed year is when the §8 payments-on-account "double bill" first bites (see §8).

- **Authority anchors:** NHS Health Careers "Pay for dentists" (DFT salary £42,408 from 1 Apr 2025); NHSBSA FD training payments (KA-02197); NHS England / COPDEND DFT handbook; Primary Dental Services SFE Directions (DHSC, annual). DFT salary verified at primary/authoritative source on 2026-06-03.
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** treat the DFT year as **salaried/PAYE and pensionable (officer)**, NOT self-employed. Date-tag the £42,408 salary as "from 1 April 2025" (it is uplifted annually). Frame the *transition* to self-employment as the trigger for registering for Self Assessment, the payments-on-account shock and the switch from Class 1 (employee) to Class 4 NIC. Do NOT describe a foundation dentist as self-employed during DFT. Cross-ref §1, §2.C, §8.

### 1.C Employment status of dental hygienists, therapists and other DCPs (locked, 2026-07-09, HP-lock gate Wave 5)

**Position.** Dental care professionals (hygienists, therapists, dental nurses, orthodontic therapists, clinical dental technicians, dental technicians — the GDC-registered DCP titles) have **no BDA model agreement and no NHS performers list**, so the §1 associate presumption does NOT transfer to them. Their status sits on a genuine facts-based spectrum: a **dental nurse or receptionist working set hours under practice control is an employee (PAYE)**; a **hygienist or therapist working sessional day-rates across several practices, providing some of their own equipment/materials, carrying their own indemnity, able to send a suitably qualified substitute, and (since the GDC's 2013 Direct Access decision) able to see patients without a dentist's prescription for most hygiene/therapy work, can be genuinely self-employed**; and **mixed treatment (PAYE at one practice, self-employed at another) is common and legitimate** — status is decided engagement by engagement, not person by person. The determinative factors are the ordinary employment-status tests (personal service/substitution, control, mutuality of obligation, financial risk, integration), read through HMRC's ESM guidance and CEST; the label in the contract does not decide it. The engaging practice bears PAYE risk if it wrongly treats an employed DCP as self-employed (§13 payroll obligations then apply).

- **Statutory hooks:** ITEPA 2003 Part 2 (employment income; s.4/s.5 employment definition); SSCBA 1992 (Class 1 vs Class 2/4 boundary); ITTOIA 2005 Part 2 (trade profits where self-employed).
- **HMRC manual anchors:** ESM0500+ (status factors), ESM0530-ESM0545 (substitution, mutuality), CEST tool; GDC Direct Access guidance (2013) and GDC Scope of Practice (DCP titles).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** never state a blanket "hygienists are self-employed" or "DCPs are employees" position — always frame status as facts-based per engagement, list the factors, and distinguish the DCP position from the §1 associate position (no BDA model, no performers list, direct access). Address the DCP as the audience (their own tax registration, Class 2/4 NIC per §8/§8.A, expenses, mixed PAYE+SE self-assessment), not the employing principal (that side lives in §13 and the nurse-hiring page). Cross-ref §1, §8, §13.

## 2. NHS Pension Scheme (locked, 2026-06-03)

**Position.** The NHS Pension Scheme is a defined-benefit scheme in three sections. **1995 section** (final salary, 1/80th accrual + 3× lump sum, normal pension age 60, closed to new entrants from 1 Apr 2008). **2008 section** (final salary, 1/60th, no automatic lump sum, NPA 65). **2015 section** (Career Average Revalued Earnings / CARE, **1/54th** of each year's pensionable earnings, active revaluation **CPI + 1.5%**, NPA linked to State Pension Age). From **1 April 2022 all active members accrue in the 2015 section** regardless of prior protection.

- **Statutory hooks:** the schemes are made under the **Superannuation Act 1972** (1995/2008) and the **Public Service Pensions Act 2013** (2015 scheme); current rules in the NHS Pension Scheme Regulations (SI 2015/94 for the 2015 scheme). Scheme administration: NHS Business Services Authority (NHSBSA).
- **Authority anchors:** NHSBSA member guides; NHS Employers "comparing the sections".
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** anchor "everyone accrues in 2015 from 1 Apr 2022"; describe 1995/2008 only as legacy/historic service. Use 1/54th and CPI+1.5% exactly. Do NOT describe the 2015 scheme as final salary (it is CARE). Cross-ref §2.A (McCloud), §2.B (annual allowance), §2.C (incorporation trap).

### 2.A McCloud remedy (locked, 2026-06-03)

**Position.** The 2015 transitional protections were held age-discriminatory (*McCloud / Sargeant*, Court of Appeal 2018). The remedy: service in the **remedy period 1 April 2015 to 31 March 2022** for eligible members was **rolled back into the legacy section (1995 or 2008) from 1 October 2023**, with a **deferred choice at retirement** between legacy and 2015 terms for that period. Eligibility: a member who **joined on or before 31 March 2012 and was an active member on 1 April 2015**. Consequential annual-allowance and tax corrections have been processed by NHSBSA/HMRC.

- **Statutory hooks:** **Public Service Pensions and Judicial Offices Act 2022** (the McCloud remedy primary legislation); remedy regulations made thereunder. https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2022/7
- **Authority anchors:** NHSBSA "Public service pensions remedy (McCloud remedy)"; "Rollback of remedy period service".
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** state the remedy period dates exactly (1 Apr 2015 to 31 Mar 2022) and that the choice is made **at retirement**, not now. Frame McCloud as generally neutral-to-positive for the individual (they get the better of two options). Do NOT tell members to make the choice now or imply a deadline to elect before retirement.

### 2.B Annual allowance, tapering and Scheme Pays (locked, 2026-06-03)

**Position.** The pension **annual allowance is £60,000** (2025/26). It **tapers** where **threshold income exceeds £200,000 AND adjusted income exceeds £260,000**, reducing by £1 for every £2 of adjusted income above £260,000, down to a **minimum of £10,000**. For DB schemes the measure is the **pension input amount** (capitalised growth), not contributions paid. High-earning practitioners with significant historic service can breach and face an annual-allowance charge at their marginal rate. **Scheme Pays** lets the NHS scheme settle the charge in exchange for a reduction in eventual benefits. The **money purchase annual allowance is £10,000** (relevant where DC AVCs are taken flexibly). Unused annual allowance can be **carried forward from the previous three tax years** (current year first), which often absorbs a one-off pension-input spike before a charge bites.

- **Statutory hooks:** **Finance Act 2004** Part 4 (annual allowance, ss.227-228ZA; tapered annual allowance s.228ZA; Scheme Pays s.237B).
- **HMRC manual anchors:** **PTM050000+** (limits on tax-relieved contributions / annual allowance); **PTM056000+** (tapered annual allowance); **PTM057000+** (Scheme Pays).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** use £60,000 / £200,000 threshold income / £260,000 adjusted income / £10,000 floor exactly, each tagged 2025/26. Emphasise that pension growth (input amount), not contributions, is what is measured. Scheme Pays is usually the cash-flow-sensible route. Do NOT use the old £40,000 allowance or the pre-2023 £240,000 adjusted-income threshold.

### 2.C Pensionable pay (practitioner vs officer) and the incorporation trap (locked, 2026-06-03)

**Position.** For **practitioner** dentists (sole trader or partnership), pensionable earnings derive from **net NHS-derived income** (scheme-specific permitted-expenses rules, year-end certificate, tiered employee contribution rate). For an **incorporated** principal taking a PAYE salary, the dentist is treated as an **officer**: only the **PAYE salary is pensionable, and dividends are not pensionable**. This is the **incorporation pension trap**: a principal who switches, say, £150,000 of pensionable NHS-derived profit into £30,000 salary + £100,000 dividend cuts pensionable pay to £30,000, losing accrual that can total tens of thousands over a 10 to 15 year run to retirement. **AVCs/Added Pension:** Money Purchase AVCs (separate DC pot) and Added Pension (extra DB accrual in-scheme) both attract income-tax relief subject to the annual allowance.

- **Statutory hooks:** NHS Pension Scheme Regulations (practitioner vs officer definitions and pensionable pay rules); tax relief under Finance Act 2004 Part 4.
- **Authority anchors:** NHSBSA practitioner/officer guidance; Added Pension and MPAVC scheme material.
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** this is the single most important cross-link to the incorporation decision. Always pair the incorporation tax saving with the pension-accrual loss (model both, never the tax saving alone). State plainly that **dividends are not pensionable**. Cross-ref §5. Do NOT imply incorporation is pension-neutral. **Provider vs associate distinction (refined at HP-lock gate 2026-06-03, Wave 2 C2):** the flat "dividends are not pensionable" holds for an incorporated **associate / performer** (officer route) and for profit **above** the NHS pensionable ceiling. The better-evidenced exception (NHSBSA NPE Provider guidance + FAQ KA-02063 / KA-02080) is an incorporated **GDS/PDS contract-holding provider**: the provider can remain in the scheme and pension **salary and dividends actually drawn, up to the net pensionable earnings (NPE) ceiling**, with **retained profit and anything above the ceiling not pensionable**. **NPE ceiling confirmed at NHSBSA primary source on 2026-06-03 (Wave 3 cluster B): the maximum NPE/NPEE that can be declared for a single GDS/PDS contract is 43.9% of the Total Contract Value (TCV)** (the pensionable-earnings ceiling for ALL dentists on the contract combined); the provider allocates within that ceiling to active performers/associates, each performer's NPE deducting from the 43.9% pool, with any balance split between partners per the partnership agreement; a dentist cannot claim a colleague's pensionable income; reconciled via the Annual Reconciliation Report (ARR). So state the trap as the default for incorporated **associates**, and for incorporated **providers** frame it as "you can pension drawn income up to the NPE ceiling, not retained profit". The genuinely case-specific residue (still confirm with NHSBSA) is the NPE apportionment across multiple providers/associates and unusual share structures. Do NOT tell an incorporated associate they can pension dividends.

### 2.D Scheme Pays mechanics (locked, 2026-06-03, HP-lock gate)

**Position.** Where an annual allowance charge arises, **Scheme Pays** lets the NHS scheme settle it for a permanent reduction in benefits. **Mandatory Scheme Pays** is available where the charge **exceeds £2,000 AND** the pension input amount exceeds the **standard £60,000** annual allowance (a charge driven only by the taper below £60k is voluntary-only). The **election deadline is 31 July** in the year following the relevant tax year (FA 2004 s.237BA), with an extended limb where the scheme notifies a revised input amount on or after 2 May. No election once all benefits are taken. The reduction is applied via scheme-set factors.

- **Statutory hooks:** FA 2004 **s.237B** (Scheme Pays liability), **s.237BA** (deadline).
- **HMRC manual anchors:** **PTM056400+**.
- **Practical writing rule:** distinguish mandatory (charge > £2,000 AND input > £60k) from voluntary Scheme Pays; state the 31 July deadline. Cross-ref §2.B.

### 2.E Partial and early retirement (locked, 2026-06-03, HP-lock gate)

**Position.** **Partial (flexible) retirement** (all sections from **1 October 2023**; Northern Ireland 1 April 2024): a member can draw **20% to 100%** of accrued benefits in up to two events while continuing to work and **re-accrue in the 2015 scheme**, provided pensionable pay or commitment reduces by **at least 10% for the first 12 months**. **Early retirement** before normal pension age (minimum pension age **55**; NPAs 60 (1995) / 65 (2008) / State Pension Age (2015)) applies a **permanent actuarial reduction** (NHSBSA/GAD-set factors, do NOT lock a fixed percentage as GAD revises them); **ERRBO** (Early Retirement Reduction Buy Out, 2015 scheme) can buy out up to 3 years of reduction and is itself annual-allowance-exempt. Commutation of pension to lump sum is **12:1**.

- **Authority anchors:** NHSBSA partial-retirement and early-retirement factor guidance (factor tables are NHSBSA/GAD-set).
- **Practical writing rule:** state partial retirement from 1 Oct 2023, the 20-100% range and the 10% reduction condition. For early retirement lock the PRINCIPLE (permanent actuarial reduction) and point to the NHSBSA factor table, do NOT lock a fixed % reduction. Cross-ref §2.A, §2.B.

## 3. NHS dental contracts (locked, 2026-06-03)

**Position.** In England, Wales and Northern Ireland the dominant mechanism uses **Units of Dental Activity (UDAs)**; **Scotland** uses the item-of-service **Statement of Dental Remuneration (SDR)**, not UDAs. Contract types in England: **GDS** (General Dental Services, the standard open-ended contract, the large majority), **PDS** (Personal Dental Services, time-limited, often specialist/local, now rare), **ODS** (out-of-hours/operational). **There is no national UDA value**: each contract's per-UDA value was set at the 2006 baseline (historic test-year fees ÷ contracted UDAs) and uplifted annually; England values typically range roughly **£15 to £45+, clustering £25 to £35**. Treatment bands: **Band 1 = 1 UDA, Band 2 = 3 UDAs, Band 3 = 12 UDAs, urgent = 1.2 UDAs**. **Patient charges go towards (not on top of) the contract value.** England patient charges from 1 April 2026: **Band 1 £27.90, Band 2 £76.60, Band 3 £332.10, urgent £27.90** (verified NHS.uk 2026-06-03; the 1 Apr 2025 rates were £27.40 / £75.30 / £326.70 / £27.40). They uplift each April, so date-tag them.

- **Year-end reconciliation / clawback:** the contract is target-based with monthly payments; at year-end actual UDA delivery is reconciled against target. Under-delivery triggers **clawback** of the unearned portion (commonly engaged below ~96% of target); manage by **monthly UDA-vs-target tracking**.
- **Contract on sale:** asset sale transfers the contract by **novation with commissioner consent** (some commissioners use the sale as a trigger to renegotiate value, seen at 5 to 10% reductions); share sale keeps the contract within the company (no novation).
- **2025/26 reform direction:** incremental (banding flexibility, preventive funding, recall-interval alignment, limited carry-over). Realistic planning assumption: UDA mechanism continues through 2025/26 with phased incremental reform, not wholesale replacement. **Price a deal on the contract as it stands, not on assumed reform upside.**
- **Authority anchors:** NHS England commissioning guidance; the NHS (General Dental Services Contracts) Regulations 2005; DHSC patient-charge written statements.
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** always say "there is no national UDA value; use the per-UDA value of the specific contract". Tag patient charges as 2026/27 England (Band 1 £27.90 / Band 2 £76.60 / Band 3 £332.10 / urgent £27.90 from 1 Apr 2026; they uplift each April, so date them). Keep reform language hedged and non-committal. Do NOT quote a single national UDA rate. Do NOT present patient charges as income on top of the contract value. Cross-ref §4 (sale), §6 (VAT on NHS vs cosmetic).

> VERIFY (RESOLVED 2026-06-03, Wave 3 cluster B): the clawback engagement threshold is confirmed at NHS England primary source. Deliver **96% to 100%** of contracted activity (under-deliver by **4% or less**) and the shortfall is **carried forward** into the next contractual year, NOT clawed back; deliver **below 96%** and the commissioner **recovers the overpayment** for undelivered activity. So 96% is the genuine clawback line. See §3.A for the full reconciliation mechanics.

### 3.A UDA year-end reconciliation, carry-forward and clawback (locked, 2026-06-03, HP-lock gate Wave 3 cluster B)

**Position.** The GDS/PDS contract is **target-based with smooth monthly payments**; at year-end (and a mid-year review) actual UDA delivery is reconciled against the contracted target, and the position is settled as follows (England; verified at NHS England year-end-reconciliation guidance on 2026-06-03):

- **96% to 100% delivery (under-delivery of 4% or less):** the shortfall is **carried forward** into the next contractual year's requirements (the practice must make up the under-delivered UDAs the following year), and is **not** clawed back as cash. This is the "4% carry-forward" rule.
- **Below 96% delivery:** the commissioner **recovers the overpayment** for the activity not delivered (clawback), up to the full annual contract value. **96% is the genuine clawback line.**
- **Over-delivery:** the commissioner **may** allow a tolerance and pay for delivery up to **102%** (a 2% over-delivery tolerance), up to **104%** for activity under pre-approved oral health programmes, and in some cases up to **110%** by prior agreement where commissioner resources allow. Over-delivery payment is discretionary, not guaranteed, so do NOT bank it in a model.
- **UDA credits and band changes (from 1 April 2026 reform):** Band 1 standalone fluoride-varnish and Band 1 urgent UDA credits count towards contract delivery and so feature in the mid-year and year-end reconciliation. Keep reform detail hedged and date-tagged (the wider April-2026 quality-and-payment reforms are phased; price on the contract as it stands).

- **Authority anchors:** NHS England "Guidance on dental contract management arrangements for year-end reconciliation" (annual); NHSBSA "Mid-year review and year-end reconciliation"; NHS (GDS Contracts) Regulations 2005. Figures verified at NHS England primary guidance on 2026-06-03.
- **Accounting treatment note (for B2/B4):** monthly contract payments are recognised as income; a year-end **clawback** liability (below 96%) is an accrued liability/provision reducing income, while a **carry-forward** (96-100%) is a delivery obligation, not a cash clawback, but is a disclosure/going-concern point because the next year carries a heavier delivery target on the same money. Always separate the two: clawback hits cash, carry-forward hits next year's workload.
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** state the **96% clawback line** and the **4% carry-forward** rule precisely; frame over-delivery tolerance (102%/104%) as discretionary. Date-tag the April-2026 reform points and keep them hedged. Cross-ref §3, §2.C (NPE/pensionable pay on the same contract value), §6 (VAT on the private side of a mixed practice).

## 4. Goodwill valuation and practice-sale tax (locked, 2026-06-03)

**Position.** Dental goodwill is valued primarily on **normalised EBITDA × a market multiple**; goodwill is typically 60 to 80% of total practice price (tangibles the balance). Indicative 2025/26 multiples: NHS-heavy single-handed in lower-demand regions ~0.6 to 0.9×; mixed multi-surgery normal-demand ~0.9 to 1.2×; private-focused high-demand ~1.1 to 1.4× (corporate strategic premiums can exceed). Treat multiples as ranges, never a single number.

**Goodwill amortisation tax relief (buyer/company side):**
- Pre-8 July 2015: relief generally available on amortisation under the then-current intangibles regime.
- **8 July 2015 to 31 March 2019: no relief** on goodwill amortisation (relief withdrawn by F(No.2)A 2015).
- **On or after 1 April 2019: fixed-rate relief at 6.5% per year** (effectively a 15-year write-down), introduced by **Finance Act 2019** (CTA 2009 Part 8 **Chapter 15A**, s.879A onward). Conditions: the relevant asset (goodwill/customer-related intangibles) must be **acquired as part of a business acquisition** that **also includes qualifying IP assets**, and relief is **capped at 6× the qualifying-IP expenditure**. Relief is claimed in the CT computation (not automatic in substance for the cap test).

**Business Asset Disposal Relief (BADR), seller side:** reduced CGT rate on qualifying gains up to a **£1m lifetime limit per individual** (limit fixed since 11 Mar 2020, not indexed). Rate: **10%** to 5 Apr 2025; **14%** for disposals 6 Apr 2025 to 5 Apr 2026; **18%** from 6 Apr 2026. Conditions held throughout the **2-year period to disposal**: trading business/company; for a share sale, **5% of ordinary share capital and 5% of voting rights**, plus **officer or employee** status, plus 5% economic entitlement.

**s162 incorporation relief:** **TCGA 1992 s.162** defers CGT on transfer of the **whole business as a going concern** to a company **wholly or partly for shares** (cash consideration restricts relief pro rata); the deferred gain rolls into the share base cost and crystallises on later share disposal. Used pre-sale to convert an unincorporated practice so a later share sale can access BADR.

**24-month pre-sale planning** framing: BADR's 2-year qualifying period and EBITDA normalisation both need ~24 months' lead time.

- **Statutory hooks:** TCGA 1992 **s.169H to s.169S** (BADR) https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12 ; TCGA 1992 **s.162** (incorporation relief) https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12/section/162 ; CTA 2009 **s.879A+** / Part 8 Ch 15A (goodwill relief) https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2009/4/section/879A ; Finance Act 2019 Sch 9; Finance Act 2026 (rate change to BADR carried by the BADR schedule above).
- **HMRC manual anchors:** **CG63950+** (Business Asset Disposal Relief); **CG65700+** (s162 incorporation relief); **CIRD44050+** (intangibles: relief for goodwill/relevant assets, restrictions).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** state the BADR rate **with its exact date band** every time (the 6 Apr 2026 step to 18% is a live planning lever for late-2025/26 completions). Use 6.5% goodwill relief only for post-1-April-2019 acquisitions and surface the qualifying-IP/6×-cap condition (a generic "buy after April 2019 and you always get 6.5%" claim is wrong because of the IP condition). Do NOT state BADR is still 10%. Cross-ref §5 (structure), §7 (capital allowances on tangibles in the price split).

### 4.A Disposal timing and deferred or contingent consideration (locked, 2026-06-03, HP-lock gate)

**Position.** The **CGT date of disposal** is the date of the **contract**, not completion, where the contract is **unconditional** (TCGA 1992 s.28(1)). Where the contract is **conditional** (including conditional on a third-party consent such as NHS contract novation), disposal is the date the **condition is satisfied** (s.28(2)). This is the lever for the 6 Apr 2026 BADR step: an **unconditional exchange on or before 5 April 2026 fixes the 14% rate** even if completion follows later; a conditional contract does not.

**Deferred or contingent consideration (earn-outs).** Where part of the price is **unascertainable** at disposal (a genuine earn-out contingent on future performance), the seller is treated as receiving at disposal a **separate chargeable asset**, the right to the future payment (the *Marren v Ingles* principle; the right is a chose in action). CGT is charged at disposal on the **valued right**, then again on the **second disposal** when the right is satisfied (cash received against the s.22 capital sum). Two consequences to state: (1) **BADR generally does not reach the second disposal** (the right is not itself a qualifying business asset), so the earn-out element is usually taxed at the standard CGT rate, not the BADR rate; (2) if the eventual payment is **less** than the valued right, the loss can be **carried back** against the original gain (s.279A to s.279D). Where consideration is **ascertainable but deferred** (fixed instalments), the full amount is taxed at disposal, but **s.280 instalment relief** lets the tax be paid by instalments over up to **8 years** where consideration is payable over a period exceeding **18 months**.

- **Statutory hooks:** **TCGA 1992 s.28** (time of disposal: unconditional = contract date; conditional = condition satisfied); **s.22** (capital sums derived from assets, the second disposal); ***Marren v Ingles* [1980] 1 WLR 983 (HL)** (unascertainable consideration = separate chargeable chose in action); **s.279A to s.279D** (carry-back of a loss on a later earn-out shortfall); **s.280** (instalment relief, consideration payable over more than 18 months, tax by instalments up to 8 years).
- **HMRC manual anchors:** **CG14250+** (time of disposal, contracts); **CG14850+ / CG14990** (unascertainable deferred consideration, Marren v Ingles); **CG14910+** (s.280 instalments).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** state s.28 precisely (unconditional contract = exchange date governs; conditional = condition-satisfied date), NOT a blanket "completion governs". On earn-outs always note BADR usually does not reach the second disposal and that a later shortfall can be carried back. Cross-ref §4 (BADR rate bands), §5.

### 4.B IHT business property relief after FA 2026: the £2.5m allowance (locked, 2026-07-09, HP-lock gate Wave 5, verified legislation.gov.uk)

**Position.** From **6 April 2026**, 100% business property relief (and agricultural property relief) is capped by a new allowance: **IHTA 1984 s.124D (inserted by FA 2026 Sch 12)** gives each person a **£2.5 million allowance** for 100% relief on qualifying business/agricultural property; qualifying value **above the allowance gets 50% relief**. The allowance operates on a **rolling 7-year basis** (transfers within the 7 years ending with the relevant transfer use it up) and applies to transfers of value and Chapter 3 charges **on or after 6 April 2026** (FA 2026 Sch 12 para 17(1)); it is **indexed from 6 April 2031** (CPI, rounded to nearest £1,000). For a dental practice: an unincorporated practice interest or unquoted trading-company shares remain **relevant business property** (2-year ownership, not wholly/mainly investments), but a valuable practice plus surgery premises can now exceed £2.5m, making the 50% tier and lifetime-gift sequencing a live planning question. The **s.113A donee-must-hold condition** still applies to PETs: BPR on a failed PET requires the donee to have kept the qualifying property (or replacement) until the donor's death. On death, assets get the **CGT uplift (TCGA 1992 s.62)** — the core tension: **lifetime gift with s.165 holdover saves nothing on CGT base cost and starts the 7-year IHT clock vs. holding until death for the CGT uplift plus BPR (now capped)**.

- **Statutory hooks:** IHTA 1984 **s.124D** (£2.5m 100%-relief allowance, inserted by FA 2026 Sch 12; 50% above; rolling 7 years; indexation from 2031) https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2026/11/schedule/12 ; ss.104/105 (relevant business property, 100%/50% categories); s.106 (2-year ownership); s.112 (excepted assets); s.113A/s.113B (PET clawback, donee-must-hold, replacement property); s.3A (PETs); TCGA 1992 s.62 (death uplift, no CGT on death).
- **Verified:** £2.5m figure, 7-year rolling basis, 6 Apr 2026 commencement and 2031 indexation verified verbatim at legislation.gov.uk FA 2026 Sch 12 on 2026-07-09.
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** always date-split BPR at 6 April 2026 and never describe 100% BPR as unlimited for post-6-Apr-2026 transfers. State the allowance as £2.5m per person (NOT £1m — a £1m figure circulating from announcement-era coverage is WRONG against the enacted text). Present the gift-now-vs-hold-to-death trade-off (holdover base cost + 7-year clock vs. CGT uplift + capped BPR) as a decision with numbers, and flag s.113A on any PET of practice assets. Cross-ref §4 (goodwill/sale), §4.C (holdover), §12 (partnership shares).

### 4.C CGT gift holdover relief on practice assets: TCGA 1992 s.165 (locked, 2026-07-09, HP-lock gate Wave 5, verified legislation.gov.uk)

**Position.** A **gift (or undervalue transfer) of business assets** — practice goodwill, surgery premises used in the trade, unlisted shares in a personal trading company — can qualify for **holdover relief under TCGA 1992 s.165**: the transferor's gain is held over and the transferee takes the asset at **base cost reduced by the held-over gain** (no CGT now; the gain crystallises on the transferee's later disposal). Conditions: the asset must be **used for the purposes of a trade, profession or vocation** carried on by the transferor or their personal company, or be **shares in a trading company that are unlisted or in the transferor's personal company**. The claim is **joint** (transferor + transferee), except transfers to trustees where the transferor claims alone. **s.165(3)(ba) blocks holdover on a disposal of shares where the transferee is a company.** Holdover interacts with BADR as an election: **holding over forfeits the chance to bank BADR at today's rate** (18% from 6 Apr 2026, §4) — for a retiring principal gifting to a child who will sell soon, paying BADR-rate CGT now can beat holdover (which passes a low base cost on). Partial consideration (e.g. child pays part of value) restricts the relief; actual proceeds above allowable cost are taxed now.

- **Statutory hooks:** TCGA 1992 **s.165** (relief for gifts of business assets; joint claim; trustees exception) https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12/section/165 ; **s.165(3)(ba)** (no relief where share transferee is a company); Sch 7 (extension: personal-company and group conditions); s.169H+ (BADR interaction, per §4); s.17 (market-value rule for connected-person disposals); s.18/s.286 (connected persons).
- **Verified:** conditions, joint-claim wording and s.165(3)(ba) verified at legislation.gov.uk on 2026-07-09.
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** present holdover as a **deferral with a base-cost price**, not an exemption; always pair it against the BADR-now alternative with a worked comparison, and against death-uplift-plus-BPR (§4.B). Note the market-value rule applies to any connected-person transfer regardless of price actually paid. Claim mechanics: HS295 claim form, joint signatures. Cross-ref §4, §4.A, §4.B, §12.

## 5. Profit extraction: partnership vs LLP vs limited company (locked, 2026-06-03)

**Position.** Three structures. **Sole trader / general partnership:** personal income tax + Class 4 NIC on the profit share; simplest; full NHS-pensionable on NHS-derived profit. **LLP:** members taxed personally as in a partnership, with limited liability; often the right answer for 2 to 4 principals who value NHS pension accrual and want liability cover. **Limited company:** company pays corporation tax, principal extracts via salary + dividend; loses NHS pension accrual on the dividend portion (see §2.C).

**Corporation tax:** **19%** small-profits (profits ≤ £50,000), **25%** main (> £250,000), **marginal relief** tapering between £50,000 and £250,000 (FY2025 and FY2026).

**Dividend tax:**
- 2025/26: ordinary **8.75%**, upper **33.75%**, additional **39.35%**; **dividend allowance £500**.
- From **6 April 2026** (FA 2026 s.4): ordinary **10.75%**, upper **35.75%**, additional **39.35%** unchanged; allowance **£500**. Tag the year on every dividend figure.

**Personal income tax / NIC (2025/26):** personal allowance £12,570 (tapered above £100,000); basic 20% to £50,270; higher 40% to £125,140; additional 45% above. **Class 4 NIC 6%** (£12,570 to £50,270) then **2%**. Combined self-employed marginal rates: basic ~26%, higher ~42%, additional ~47%.

**Spouse employment:** allowable only where the **work is genuine and the salary is market-rate**; HMRC disallows illusory or above-market spouse pay. Document hours/duties, pay through PAYE.

**Employer cost of salary (limited company), 2025/26 to 2026/27 (locked at HP-lock gate 2026-06-03):** the company pays **employer (secondary Class 1) NIC at 15%** on salary above the **secondary threshold of £5,000** (cut to £5,000 and the rate raised to 15% from 6 April 2025), with the **LEL £6,708** preserving the director's state-pension and NHS-officer entitlement. The **Employment Allowance is £10,500** but is **not available to a company whose only employee is a single director**, which is why one-director dental companies often set salary at the secondary threshold rather than the full personal allowance (a genuinely employed spouse can flip that answer). **Employer pension contributions** are a tax-efficient extraction route: **deductible for corporation tax on a paid basis** (FA 2004 s.196, wholly and exclusively, BIM46035), with **no NIC**, subject to the §2.B annual allowance and carry-forward. Director's loan accounts: see §5.A.

**Headline point:** at 2025/26 rates the pure tax saving from incorporation is small at typical principal profits; the real drivers are NHS pension flexibility, retained earnings for reinvestment, spouse employment, and sale planning. The **2026/27 dividend rate rise narrows the incorporation advantage further**.

- **Statutory hooks:** CTA 2010 Part 3 (small-profits rate and marginal relief, ss.18 to 19); ITTOIA 2005 Part 4 Ch 3 (dividends); ITA 2007 s.8 (dividend rates, amended by FA 2026 s.4); SSCBA 1992 s.15 (Class 4 NIC).
- **HMRC manual anchors:** dividends BIM/CTM; spouse-wages deductibility **BIM37700+** (wages must be wholly and exclusively for the trade and commensurate with work done).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** never state a dividend rate without its tax-year tag; from 6 Apr 2026 use 10.75%/35.75%. Always pair the incorporation tax comparison with the NHS-pension-accrual loss (§2.C). Do NOT present incorporation as a clear tax win at typical dental profit levels in 2025/26. Cross-ref §2.C, §4.

### 5.A Director's loan accounts in a close company (locked, 2026-06-03, HP-lock gate)

**Position.** A dental company is a **close company**, so an **overdrawn director's loan account** (drawings taken ahead of declared salary, dividend or profit) triggers a **section 455 charge**: the company pays CT at the **dividend upper rate** on the loan outstanding **9 months and 1 day** after the period-end. That rate is **33.75% for loans made in 2025/26** and **35.75% for loans made on or after 6 April 2026** (it tracks the FA 2026 s.4 dividend upper rate). The charge is **temporary**: **s.458 relief** repays it once the loan is repaid, released or written off, but the relief is **deferred to 9 months and 1 day after the end of the accounting period in which repayment occurs**, with a **4-year** claim window (the "L2P" claim). Anti-avoidance blocks repay-and-redraw: the **30-day rule (s.464ZA)** where £5,000 or more is repaid and redrawn within 30 days, and the **arrangements rule (s.464C/464D)** where the balance is £15,000 or more and there is an intention to redraw. A loan **over £10,000** at any point in the year is also a **beneficial-loan benefit in kind** (ITEPA 2003 ss.173 to 191) unless interest is paid at the **official rate (3.75% from 6 April 2025)**, reportable on P11D with **Class 1A NIC at 15%**.

- **Statutory hooks:** **CTA 2010 s.455** (charge on loans to participators; rate = dividend upper rate); **s.458** (relief on repayment/release/write-off); **s.464ZA** (30-day rule), **s.464C / s.464D** (£15,000 arrangements rule); **ITEPA 2003 ss.173 to 191** (beneficial loans, £10,000 threshold, official rate). https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2010/4/section/455
- **HMRC manual anchors:** **CTM61500+ / CTM61630** (loans to participators, s.455/s.458, bed-and-breakfasting); **EIM26100+** (beneficial loans, official rate).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** state the s.455 rate **with its date band** (33.75% on pre-6-Apr-2026 loans, 35.75% on/after), the 9-months-and-1-day timing, and that s.458 relief is **deferred**, not immediate. Flag the £10,000 BIK threshold and the official rate. Do NOT describe s.455 as a permanent tax (it is repayable) or imply the relief is instant. Cross-ref §5.

### 5.B Business-loan and finance-cost deductibility: practice acquisition, refinancing and working capital (locked, 2026-06-03, HP-lock gate Wave 3 cluster C)

**Position.** Interest and finance costs on borrowing **for the purposes of the trade** are generally a **deductible business expense**, but the route differs by structure and the **capital-vs-revenue** distinction matters.

- **Unincorporated (sole trader / partnership):** interest on a loan taken **wholly and exclusively for the trade** (to buy the practice/goodwill, fund working capital, finance equipment, or refinance existing business borrowing) is **deductible against trading profit** (ITTOIA 2005, the trading-expense rules; BIM45650+ on interest). The **loan principal is NOT deductible** (it is capital); only the **interest and qualifying finance charges** are. Overdraft interest and arrangement/commitment fees on business facilities are similarly deductible where the facility is for the trade. (Note the cash-basis interest restriction for very small businesses using the cash basis; most dental practices use accruals.)
- **Incorporated (company):** finance costs are dealt with under the **loan-relationship rules (CTA 2009 Part 5)** and are normally deductible as a **non-trading or trading debit** on an accruals basis, including interest on acquisition borrowing and refinancing. The **Corporate Interest Restriction (TIOPA 2010 Part 10)** caps net interest deductions at broadly **30% of UK tax-EBITDA**, but **only where the group's net tax-interest expense exceeds the £2,000,000 annual de minimis** (per group). A single dental company is effectively **never** caught by the CIR, so for practical dental purposes company interest is fully deductible (state the £2m de minimis so the page is correct, but note it does not bite typical practices).
- **Capital vs revenue, and what is NOT deductible:** the **repayment of loan principal** is never deductible (capital). **Incidental costs of obtaining loan finance** (arrangement fees, broker fees, security/valuation costs for the loan) are generally **deductible** (for companies under CTA 2009 s.307/s.329 loan-relationship rules; for the unincorporated under the trading rules, BIM45800+). Interest on borrowing for a **non-trade purpose** (e.g. funds extracted for personal use) is not deductible.
- **Refinancing:** replacing one business loan with another for the same trade keeps the interest deductible; the purpose test follows the **use of the borrowed money**, not the identity of the lender. Early-repayment charges on refinancing business debt are generally deductible. Watch the personal-vs-trade line where refinancing releases cash to the owner.
- **Acquisition structure interaction:** on a **share purchase** of a dental company, interest on the borrowing sits in the acquiring person/company and its deductibility depends on structure (a personal loan to buy company shares is generally **not** trade-deductible for an individual; an asset/goodwill purchase by a trading sole trader/company gives trade-deductible interest). Cross-ref §4 (asset vs share sale) and §5 (extraction).

- **Statutory hooks:** **ITTOIA 2005** trading-expense / interest rules (unincorporated); **CTA 2009 Part 5** (loan relationships, company finance costs), s.307/s.329 (incidental costs of finance); **TIOPA 2010 Part 10** (Corporate Interest Restriction, £2m de minimis, 30% fixed-ratio).
- **HMRC manual anchors:** **BIM45650+** (interest and finance costs, unincorporated), **BIM45800+** (incidental costs of loan finance); **CFM30000+ / CFM95000+** (loan relationships, Corporate Interest Restriction).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** state that **interest is deductible, principal is not**; route it correctly (trading rules if unincorporated, loan-relationship rules if a company); flag the **£2m CIR de minimis** as the reason a normal dental company gets full relief; and watch the **trade-vs-personal purpose** test on refinancing that releases cash and on **share-purchase** borrowing. Do NOT say loan repayments are deductible. Do NOT assert the CIR restricts a typical single-practice company. Cross-ref §4, §5, §7 (equipment finance lease-vs-buy), §8 (business-loan interest already noted).

## 6. VAT in a dental practice (locked, 2026-06-03)

**Position.** The supply of **dental care (medical care) and dental prostheses by a person registered in the dentists' register or the dental care professionals register** is **exempt** from VAT under **VATA 1994 Schedule 9 Group 7**, whether NHS-funded or private. The exemption turns on the supply being **medical care** (principal purpose: protecting, maintaining or restoring health). **Purely cosmetic / aesthetic treatment with no therapeutic purpose is standard-rated** (facial aesthetics such as Botox/fillers for cosmetic purposes, and tooth whitening, are the classic borderline cases HMRC scrutinises). A practice with both exempt and taxable supplies operates **partial exemption** (apportion input VAT; a special method can be agreed where the standard method is unfair). **VAT registration threshold £90,000** of taxable (non-exempt) turnover (raised from £85,000 on 1 April 2024); exempt income does not count towards the threshold.

- **Statutory hooks:** **VATA 1994 Schedule 9 Group 7** (health and welfare, dental exemption) https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1994/23/schedule/9 ; registration in **VATA 1994 Schedule 1**; partial exemption in the VAT Regulations 1995 (SI 1995/2518) regs 99 to 111.
- **HMRC manual anchors:** **VATHLT2300+** (VAT: dental and dental care; medical-care/principal-purpose test); partial exemption **PE21500+**.
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** lead with "dental care is VAT-exempt under Sch 9 Group 7; cosmetic-only work can be standard-rated". Frame whitening and facial aesthetics as the watch-items requiring a principal-purpose assessment. Use the **£90,000** threshold and note it counts **non-exempt** turnover only. Do NOT say all dental income is exempt without the cosmetic carve-out. Do NOT use the old £85,000 threshold for current advice.

### 6.A Partial exemption (locked, 2026-06-03, HP-lock gate)

**Position.** A practice with both exempt (dental care) and taxable (cosmetic-only, some retail/lab) supplies is **partially exempt** and recovers only the input VAT attributable to taxable supplies. Inputs split three ways: **directly attributable to taxable** (recoverable), **directly attributable to exempt** (irrecoverable), and **residual/overhead** (recovered by the standard method: taxable turnover divided by total turnover, **rounded up to the next whole %**). Exempt input VAT is also fully recoverable if it passes the **de minimis** test: average **£625 per month (£7,500 per year) AND no more than 50% of total input VAT**. A **special method** (floor-area, staff-time, etc.) can be agreed with HMRC where the standard method is unfair. A **partial-exemption annual adjustment** trues up the four quarterly recoveries after the VAT year.

- **Statutory hooks:** VAT Regulations 1995 (SI 1995/2518) **regs 99 to 110** (attribution, standard method, de minimis, annual adjustment); VAT Notice 706.
- **HMRC manual anchors:** **PE21500+** (de minimis), **PE30000+** (standard method), **PE60000+** (annual adjustment).
- **Practical writing rule:** always state both de minimis limbs together (£625/month AND ≤50%) and the standard-method round-up. Cross-ref §6.B, §6.C.

### 6.B Capital Goods Scheme (locked, 2026-06-03, HP-lock gate)

**Position.** Where a partially exempt practice spends **£250,000 or more (VAT-exclusive) on land or a building** (or £50,000+ on a single computer, aircraft or ship), the **Capital Goods Scheme** spreads input-VAT recovery over a **10-year adjustment period** (5 years for computers). Each interval, recovery is recalculated against that year's taxable-use %, clawing back or refunding VAT as the exempt/taxable mix shifts. A **final adjustment** crystallises on sale of the asset or on deregistration.

- **Statutory hooks:** VAT Regulations 1995 **regs 112 to 116**; VAT Notice 706/2.
- **HMRC manual anchors:** **PE67100+**.
- **Practical writing rule:** use £250,000 (land/buildings, 10 intervals) and £50,000 (computers, 5 intervals). A surgery refurbishment or freehold purchase by a partly-exempt practice is the classic trigger. Cross-ref §6.A, §7 (same fit-out spend that drives capital allowances).

### 6.C VAT deregistration (locked, 2026-06-03, HP-lock gate)

**Position.** A practice may **deregister** when taxable turnover falls below the **deregistration threshold of £88,000** (note: registration threshold is £90,000, deregistration is £88,000) or when it stops making taxable supplies (e.g. drops cosmetic work to go fully NHS-exempt). On deregistration a **deemed supply** (VATA 1994 **Schedule 4 para 8**) brings VAT to account on the **replacement-cost value** of stock and assets on which input VAT was recovered, unless the total VAT due is **£1,000 or less** (de minimis). Notify HMRC within **30 days**. A **final Capital Goods Scheme adjustment** is also due.

- **Statutory hooks:** VATA 1994 Sch 1 (deregistration threshold £88,000); **Sch 4 para 8** (deemed supply on deregistration); VAT Notice 700/11.
- **HMRC manual anchors:** **VATDREG16050+**.
- **Practical writing rule:** distinguish the £90,000 registration threshold from the £88,000 deregistration threshold. Flag the deemed-supply charge on retained assets (the £1,000 de minimis often saves small practices). Cross-ref §6.B.

### 6 note — prostheses and dental technicians, Item 2A (locked, 2026-06-03)

The Sch 9 Group 7 exemption extends beyond a dentist's direct care: **Item 2A** exempts the supply of **dental prostheses by a registered dentist, dental care professional, or registered dental technician** (crowns, dentures, bridges for a named patient). Finished-prosthesis supplies down that chain to the named patient are exempt; standard-rated supplies arise on retail/general products outside it. Anchors: **VATHLT2450 / VATHLT2490**.

### 6.D Paying VAT late: penalties, interest and Time to Pay (locked, 2026-07-09, HP-lock gate Wave 5, verified gov.uk)

**Position.** For a VAT-registered practice that cannot pay a VAT bill on time, the regime (post-Jan-2023 penalty reform, rates as uprated with effect announced July 2025): **late-payment interest runs from day 1** until paid (rate per gov.uk at write time — do not hardcode); a **first penalty of 3% of the VAT outstanding at day 15** applies where payment is 16-30 days late, with **a further 3% of the amount still outstanding at day 30**; from **day 31 a second penalty accrues daily at 10% per year** on the outstanding balance. A **Time to Pay (TTP) arrangement** agreed with HMRC stops further late-payment penalties accruing from the date it is proposed (provided the taxpayer keeps to it) — interest still runs. This is the benchmark any "VAT loan" must beat: compare the lender's rate against HMRC interest + penalty exposure, and note TTP is the no-borrowing alternative. Interest on business borrowing to fund a tax bill of the practice is deductible per the ordinary finance-cost rules (§5.B) where the loan is for business purposes; penalties and HMRC interest on late VAT are NOT deductible.

- **Sources:** gov.uk "How late payment penalties work if you pay VAT late" (3% day-15 / +3% day-30 / 10% p.a. from day 31; TTP effect) verified 2026-07-09; FA 2021 Sch 26 (penalty framework); gov.uk late-payment interest rates page (rate floats with base rate — fetch at write time).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** always present TTP alongside commercial borrowing as the two routes for a VAT cash-flow gap, with the penalty/interest arithmetic. Never hardcode the HMRC interest rate; date-tag the 3%/3%/10% penalty figures. Remember most dental practices are NOT VAT-registered (§6) — scope any VAT-payment content to registered mixed/cosmetic practices, partial-exemption practices (§6.A) and CGS cases (§6.B). Cross-ref §5.B (deductibility), §6, §6.A, §6.B.

## 7. Capital allowances (locked, 2026-06-03)

**Position.** **Annual Investment Allowance (AIA) £1,000,000** per year (permanent), giving 100% first-year relief on qualifying plant and machinery, which covers most surgery equipment (chairs, X-ray, autoclaves, compressors, loupes). A **surgery fit-out** splits between **general plant and machinery** (main-rate pool, writing-down allowance **18%, reducing to 14% from 1 April 2026 / 6 April 2026 per FA 2026 s.28, see §7.A**) and **integral features / fixtures** (special-rate pool, **6%** WDA, unchanged) such as electrical, cold and hot water, heating, air and ventilation systems; AIA can be allocated to either, and is best directed at the special-rate (6%) items first. On a **practice purchase including fixtures**, a **CAA 2001 s.198 election** fixes the value attributed to fixtures between seller and buyer (joint election, **2-year time limit** to make it under s.201); missing it forfeits the buyer's fixtures allowances. **SBA** (Structures and Buildings Allowance) at **3% per year** may apply to qualifying construction/premises spend incurred on/after 29 October 2018 (excludes the land and the plant already in pools).

- **Statutory hooks:** **CAA 2001** s.38A and s.51A (AIA); s.56 (main-rate WDA: 18% reducing to 14% per FA 2026 s.28); s.104A to s.104D (special-rate pool, 6%, integral features); FA 2026 s.29 (40% FYA); **s.198** (fixtures apportionment election) https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2001/2/section/198 and s.201 (time limit); SBA in CAA 2001 Part 2A.
- **HMRC manual anchors:** **CA23080+** (AIA); **CA22300+** (integral features); **CA26800+** (fixtures elections, s.198/s.199); **CA90000+** (SBA).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** use AIA £1m; the main-rate WDA is **18% reducing to 14% from April 2026** (date-tag it, FA 2026 s.28, see §7.A), special rate 6%. On purchases, always flag the s.198 election and its 2-year deadline (a common, preventable loss). Do NOT claim AIA on cars. Do NOT state a flat 18% WDA for periods after April 2026. Do NOT describe SBA as covering plant (it covers the building structure only). Cross-ref §4 (price split between goodwill and tangibles).

### 7.A FA 2026 capital-allowance changes (locked, 2026-06-03, HP-lock gate)

**Position.** Two FA 2026 changes correct and extend §7. (1) The **main-rate writing-down allowance falls from 18% to 14%** for relief from **1 April 2026 (corporation tax) / 6 April 2026 (income tax)** (FA 2026 **s.28**); a chargeable or basis period straddling the date uses a **hybrid (time-apportioned) rate**. The **special-rate pool stays 6%**. (2) A new **40% first-year allowance** on new main-rate plant and machinery applies from **1 January 2026** (FA 2026 **s.29**), to **both companies and unincorporated businesses**, **new (not second-hand) only**, excluding cars. **Full expensing** (companies only: 100% FYA on new main-rate plant, 50% on new special-rate) remains permanent. **AIA £1m is unchanged** and is usually still the first port of call for unincorporated practices and for mixed or second-hand spend.

- **Statutory hooks:** FA 2026 **s.28** (WDA 18% to 14%), **s.29** (40% FYA); CAA 2001 s.39 / s.45S (full expensing).
- **Practical writing rule:** date-tag the main-rate WDA every time (18% to Mar/Apr 2026, then 14%). For a NEW main-rate asset, compare AIA (100%) vs the 40% FYA (leaves the balance in the pool). Full expensing is companies-only. Do NOT state a flat 18% WDA for periods after April 2026.

### 7.B Balancing adjustments on disposal (locked, 2026-06-03, HP-lock gate)

**Position.** When plant or fixtures on which allowances were claimed are sold (including in a practice sale), the **disposal value (capped at original cost)** comes out of the pool. If it exceeds the pool balance a **balancing charge** claws back excess allowances (taxable); on a cessation a **balancing allowance** may instead arise. This is the engine of the §7.C election: the seller's disposal value is the buyer's claimable base. AIA-relieved kit still carries a disposal value on sale (a balancing-charge surprise).

- **Statutory hooks:** CAA 2001 **ss.55 to 59** (pooling, WDA, balancing adjustments); s.61 (disposal value).
- **Practical writing rule:** flag the balancing charge on AIA-relieved kit sold with the practice. Cross-ref §4, §7.C.

### 7.C The s.198 election conditions (locked, 2026-06-03, HP-lock gate)

**Position.** A buyer's fixtures claim on a second-hand property is conditioned (post-April-2014) by the **fixed-value requirement** and the **pooling requirement** (CAA 2001 **s.187A**): the past owner must have pooled the fixtures, and a **s.198 joint election** must fix the transfer value (capped at the lower of the seller's original cost or the sale price) within **2 years** (s.201). Miss it and the buyer's fixtures allowances are lost permanently. The buyer wants a high election value (more future allowances), the seller a low one (smaller balancing charge): an equal-and-opposite negotiation to settle in the SPA.

- **Statutory hooks:** CAA 2001 **s.187A** (fixed-value + pooling), **s.198 / s.199** (election), **s.201** (2-year limit).
- **HMRC manual anchors:** **CA26800+**.
- **Practical writing rule:** state both s.187A conditions and the 2-year deadline; frame the election value as a negotiation. Cross-ref §4, §7.B.

### 7.D Lease vs buy: capital-allowance treatment (locked, 2026-06-03, HP-lock gate)

**Position.** How equipment is financed changes the tax route. **Outright purchase** and **hire purchase**: capital allowances on the cash price (HP brings the asset into the pool as if owned once in use, s.67; the interest element is separately deductible). **Finance lease** and **operating lease**: no capital allowances for the lessee, but the **rentals are revenue-deductible**. So AIA/FYA front-loads relief on a purchase or HP, while a lease spreads relief through the profit and loss account.

- **Statutory hooks:** CAA 2001 **s.67** (HP and similar); lease rentals deductible under the ITTOIA 2005 / CTA 2009 trading rules.
- **Practical writing rule:** map the four methods (buy, HP, finance lease, operating lease) to allowances vs deductible rentals. Do NOT claim capital allowances on leased (non-HP) equipment.

### 7.E AIA timing machinery (locked, 2026-06-03, HP-lock gate)

**Position.** AIA is **£1m per 12-month period**, **time-apportioned** for short or long periods, and **cannot be carried forward** (unused AIA is lost). The **incurred date** is normally the contract/obligation date (or the payment-due date if payment falls more than 4 months later); the asset must be **brought into use** to claim. A period **straddling** an AIA-change date is split. Year-end timing of a large equipment purchase, accelerating into a high-profit year or deferring to use a fresh period's AIA, is a live planning lever.

- **Statutory hooks:** CAA 2001 **s.51A** (AIA amount, apportionment), **s.5** (incurred date).
- **Practical writing rule:** AIA does not carry forward, so use-it-or-lose-it timing matters; apportion for short periods. Cross-ref §7.A (compare with the 40% FYA).

## 11. Tax residence: the Statutory Residence Test (locked, 2026-06-03, HP-lock gate Wave 3 cluster A)

**Position.** A dentist's UK income-tax exposure on overseas work turns on **tax residence**, determined by the **Statutory Residence Test (SRT)** in **Finance Act 2013 Schedule 45** (in force from 6 April 2013). The test runs in a fixed order for a tax year: (1) **automatic overseas tests** (if met, non-resident); then (2) **automatic UK tests** (if met, resident); then (3) the **sufficient ties test**. A "day" is generally a day on which the individual is in the UK at **midnight**.

**Automatic overseas tests (any one makes you non-resident):** present in the UK **fewer than 16 days** in the tax year (if UK resident in any of the prior 3 years); **fewer than 46 days** (if not UK resident in any of the prior 3 years); or **full-time work overseas** (broadly 35+ hours/week average) with **fewer than 91 days** in the UK and **no more than 30 UK workdays**.

**Automatic UK tests (any one makes you resident, unless an overseas test was met first):** **183 days or more** in the UK in the tax year; the **only home** (or all homes) being in the UK for a qualifying period; or **full-time work in the UK** over a 365-day period.

**Sufficient ties test (when neither set of automatic tests is decisive):** count UK ties and read across to the day count. The **five ties**: **family** (spouse/civil partner/minor child UK-resident), **accommodation** (available UK accommodation used in the year), **work** (40+ UK workdays of 3+ hours), **90-day** (90+ days in the UK in either of the prior 2 years), and the **country tie** (UK is the country of most midnights — **leavers only**). **Arrivers** (not UK resident in any of the prior 3 years): 46-90 days needs **all 4** ties, 91-120 days needs **3+**, over 120 days needs **2+**. **Leavers** (UK resident in 1+ of the prior 3 years): 16-45 days needs **4+**, 46-90 days needs **3+**, 91-120 days needs **2+**, over 120 days needs **1+** (leavers can have a fifth, the country tie). **Split-year treatment** can apply in the year of departure or arrival so the year is split into a UK and an overseas part (8 split-year cases). A dentist who remains **UK-resident** is taxable on **worldwide** income, with **double taxation relief** under the relevant treaty (foreign tax credit) to prevent the same income being taxed twice; a **non-resident** is generally taxable only on UK-source income.

- **Statutory hooks:** **Finance Act 2013 Schedule 45** (SRT, including the automatic tests, ties test and split-year cases). https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2013/29/schedule/45 ; double taxation relief under TIOPA 2010 Part 2 and the relevant double-tax treaty.
- **HMRC manual / guidance anchors:** **RDR3** (SRT guidance note); Residence, Domicile and Remittance / **Residence and FIG Regime Manual RFIG20000+** (ties test tables at RFIG20520). Day-count tables verified at primary/HMRC-manual source on 2026-06-03.
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** run the SRT in order (overseas tests, then UK tests, then ties). State the exact thresholds (16/46 days, 183 days, the ties tables) and tag the country tie as **leavers only**. Flag split-year treatment and double-tax relief but keep treaty specifics general (they vary by country). Note **domicile/remittance is a separate question** from residence and the FIG (foreign income and gains) regime replaced the non-dom remittance basis from 6 April 2025 (keep light; out of scope for most dentists). Do NOT assert a dentist is non-resident simply because they work abroad: a single tax year abroad rarely meets the full-time-work-overseas test, and the ties test commonly keeps a leaver UK-resident. Always say "this depends on the SRT and is fact-specific; take advice before assuming non-residence." Cross-ref §8, §11.A (temporary non-residence), §11.B (FIG), §11.C (treaty tie-breaker).

### 11.A Temporary non-residence anti-avoidance (locked, 2026-06-04, HP-lock gate Wave 4 cluster B)

**Position.** A dentist who leaves the UK, realises income or gains while abroad, and then **returns within a few years** can be caught by the **temporary non-residence** anti-avoidance rule, which charges certain amounts that arose during the absence in the **year of return** as if they had arisen then. The rule bites where **both** conditions are met: (1) the individual had **sole UK residence in 4 or more of the 7 tax years immediately before the year of departure** (a residence-history condition); and (2) the **temporary period of non-residence is 5 years or less** (counted on the SRT/split-year basis). If the absence is genuinely **longer than 5 years**, or the 4-of-7 history is not met, the rule does not apply. The amounts brought back into charge in the year of return include certain **capital gains** (TCGA 1992 s.10A / s.10AA), and certain **income** that can be sheltered by a short absence: notably **closely-controlled-company dividends/distributions, certain pension lump sums and flexible drawdown, chargeable-event (life-policy) gains, and remitted/relevant foreign income** caught under the income limbs (FA 2013 Sch 45 Part 4). Routine **earned income** for work physically done abroad during a genuine non-resident period is **not** caught by this rule (that is governed by residence/treaty, §11/§11.C); the rule targets investment-type and one-off receipts deliberately timed into a gap year of non-residence.

- **Statutory hooks:** **TCGA 1992 s.10A / s.10AA** (temporary non-residents, capital gains charged in the year of return; rewritten by FA 2013 Sch 45 para 119) https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12/section/10A ; **Finance Act 2013 Schedule 45 Part 4** (temporary non-residence: definitions of "temporarily non-resident", "temporary period of non-residence", "year of return"; the income limbs) https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2013/29/schedule/45 .
- **HMRC manual anchors:** Residence and FIG Regime Manual (temporary non-residence) RFIG; **CG26500+** (temporary non-residents, s.10A). Conditions verified at FA 2013 Sch 45 explanatory notes / legislation.gov.uk on 2026-06-04 (4-of-7 prior-year residence; 5-years-or-less; charge in the year of return).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** state both conditions precisely (4-of-7 prior-year sole UK residence AND a temporary period of 5 years or less) and that the charge lands in the **year of return**. Make clear the rule targets investment-type receipts (close-company dividends, pension lump sums, chargeable-event gains, certain gains) deliberately timed into a short absence, NOT ordinary salary for work done abroad. Do NOT tell a dentist a 2 to 3 year posting cleanses a UK gain or a pre-departure dividend; flag the 5-year line as the planning threshold. Cross-ref §11, §11.B, §11.C, §5.A (close-company distributions).

### 11.B The 4-year FIG (foreign income and gains) regime from 6 April 2025 (locked, 2026-06-04, HP-lock gate Wave 4 cluster B)

**Position.** From **6 April 2025** the **remittance basis and the concept of domicile were abolished** for income tax and CGT and replaced by the residence-based **4-year foreign income and gains (FIG) regime**. A **qualifying new resident** (someone who becomes UK resident after **at least 10 consecutive tax years of non-UK residence**) can **claim** relief on **qualifying foreign income and gains arising on or after 6 April 2025** for up to **4 consecutive tax years** starting with their first year of UK residence. This is mainly relevant to a dentist **returning to the UK after a long spell abroad** (10+ years non-resident) or arriving from overseas: in those first 4 years their overseas investment income and foreign gains can be exempt if claimed. The claim is made on the **Self Assessment return**, can be made source-by-source, and claiming **forfeits the personal allowance and the CGT annual exempt amount** for that year (so it is a calculation, not an automatic win). FIG relieves **foreign** income/gains only, not UK-source income, and does **not** turn off the SRT or the temporary-non-residence rule.

- **Statutory hooks:** Finance Act 2025 (non-dom reform / FIG regime, ITTOIA/TCGA amendments from 6 April 2025); ITA 2007 qualifying-new-resident conditions. gov.uk "Check if you can claim the 4-year foreign income and gains regime"; HS266 (FIG helpsheet).
- **HMRC manual anchors:** **Residence and FIG Regime Manual RFIG40000+** (FIG regime, qualifying new residents, qualifying foreign income/gains). Verified at gov.uk on 2026-06-04 (10-year non-residence eligibility; 4 consecutive years; claim required; PA + AEA forfeited on a FIG claim; remittance basis abolished 6 Apr 2025).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** position FIG as the **returner/arriver** relief (10+ years non-resident, first 4 years back), claimed on the SA return, and always flag that **claiming costs the personal allowance and the CGT annual exempt amount** so it must be modelled. Make clear it relieves **foreign** income/gains only and does not switch off the SRT or temporary-non-residence. Keep it light for the typical UK-career dentist (most will not qualify, needing the 10-year clean break). Do NOT describe the old remittance basis as still available; it ended 6 April 2025. Cross-ref §11, §11.A.

### 11.C Double-tax-treaty residence tie-breaker and credit relief (locked, 2026-06-04, HP-lock gate Wave 4 cluster B)

**Position.** A dentist can be **resident in two countries at once** under each country's domestic law (UK-resident under the SRT and resident in the host country under its rules). Where the UK has a **double taxation agreement (DTA)** with that country, the treaty **residence article (OECD Model Article 4)** applies a **tie-breaker cascade** to allocate treaty residence to **one** state, applied in order until one is decisive: (1) **permanent home** available; (2) **centre of vital interests** (closer personal and economic relations); (3) **habitual abode**; (4) **nationality**; (5) **mutual agreement** between the two competent authorities. Once a test settles it, later tests are not used. Treaty residence in the **other** state can then restrict the UK's taxing rights on particular income under the relevant article (employment, pensions, etc.). Separately, where income **is** taxed in both countries, **double taxation relief** under **TIOPA 2010 Part 2** gives the UK resident a **foreign tax credit** (capped at the UK tax on the same income) so the same income is not taxed twice; the net cost is broadly the **higher** of the two countries' rates, not the sum. Treaty specifics vary by country, so state the principle and say "check the specific treaty".

- **Statutory hooks:** the relevant **double taxation agreement** (OECD Model Article 4 residence tie-breaker, Article 15 employment income, etc.); **TIOPA 2010 Part 2** (double taxation relief, foreign tax credit). https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2010/8/part/2
- **HMRC manual anchors:** **INTM154000+** (treaty residence / dual residence tie-breaker, Article 4); **DT** double-taxation-relief manual (by territory); foreign tax credit relief. Tie-breaker cascade verified at HMRC INTM on 2026-06-04.
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** run the tie-breaker in order (permanent home, centre of vital interests, habitual abode, nationality, mutual agreement) and note it can make a UK-SRT-resident dentist treaty-resident elsewhere for the relevant income. Always separate the **tie-breaker** (which country gets to tax) from **credit relief** (avoiding double tax where both do). Keep treaty detail general and country-dependent; do NOT assert a specific allocation without the named treaty. Cross-ref §11, §11.A, §8.

## 12. Partnership changes: incoming and outgoing partners (locked, 2026-06-03, HP-lock gate Wave 4 cluster A)

**Position.** A dental partnership (and an LLP, which is taxed as a partnership) is **tax-transparent**: the firm does not pay income tax; each partner is taxed individually on their **share of the firm's profit per the profit-sharing arrangement for the period** (**ITTOIA 2005 s.850**; losses allocated the same way under s.850 and the partnership-loss rules). A salaried associate who **buys in to become an equity partner** moves from §1 self-employed-associate taxation to being taxed on a profit share; a partner who **retires/exits** ceases to be allocated profit from the exit date. The three transition mechanics every buy-in/buy-out page must run:

- **Goodwill on a change in partnership shares (CGT, seller/leaver and continuing partners).** Per **HMRC Statement of Practice D12** (CG27000+/CG27170), a change in a partner's **fractional share** of the partnership's chargeable assets (goodwill, freehold, fixtures) is a **part-disposal** by the partner whose share reduces and an acquisition by the partner whose share increases. Where an **incoming partner pays existing partners for goodwill** and the payment passes **outside the partnership accounts** (or where goodwill is revalued and credited to capital accounts), that payment is **consideration for a disposal**: the receiving partners have a CGT disposal of the slice of goodwill given up (potentially within **BADR**, see §4, if that partner meets the 2-year and economic-interest conditions), and the paying partner's cost is carried until their own later exit. A **no-payment reallocation at book value** (e.g. shifting profit shares without paying for goodwill, goodwill not recognised) is typically **no-gain/no-loss**. State the rule, then say the exact figures need the firm's balance sheet and SP D12 computation.
- **SDLT where the partnership holds land (freehold or a chargeable leasehold).** A transfer of an interest in a partnership is generally **outside SDLT for a pure trading partnership with no land**, but where the **partnership property includes a chargeable interest in land** the transfer of a partnership interest is treated as a **land transaction** under **FA 2003 Schedule 15 para 14**, with chargeable consideration computed by the **"sum of the lower proportions" (SLP)** method (**Sch 15 para 20**); the partner-connection map reduces the charge (a transfer purely among connected continuing partners can compute to a low or nil charge). Equally, **putting land into the partnership** (para 10) or **taking it out** (para 18) is within the special charging code. **Do NOT assert a fixed SDLT figure** on a buy-in/buy-out: the SLP turns on who is connected to whom and the firm's land value, so state the para 14/20 principle and say "compute the SLP and take advice".
- **Funding the buy-in / buy-out (finance-cost deductibility).** Interest on a loan an **incoming partner** takes to buy a partnership share, or that the **continuing partners** take to fund a retiring partner's exit, is deductible where the borrowing is **wholly and exclusively for the trade** (per §5.B; for an individual partner buying into a trading partnership, loan interest to buy into the partnership is relievable under the qualifying-loan-interest rules, ITA 2007 s.398, where the conditions are met). Route it correctly and flag the trade-vs-personal purpose test. Principal is never deductible (§5.B). Cross-ref §5.B, §4.

- **Statutory hooks:** **ITTOIA 2005 s.850** (allocation of firm's profits/losses) https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2005/5/section/850 ; **HMRC SP D12** (partnerships and CGT, part-disposal on a sharing-ratio change); **TCGA 1992** (part-disposal mechanics, BADR conditions per §4); **FA 2003 Schedule 15** paras 10/14/18/20 (SDLT on partnership land transactions and the SLP); **ITA 2007 s.398** (qualifying loan interest to buy into a partnership). 
- **HMRC manual anchors:** **CG27000+ / CG27170** (SP D12, partnership share changes); **SDLTM33000+ / SDLTM33730 / SDLTM33750** (Sch 15 partnerships, para 14 transfer of interest, para 20 SLP); **PM130000+** (partnership manual, profit allocation); **BIM72000+** (changes in partners).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** lead with "the partnership is tax-transparent; each partner is taxed on their profit share (s.850)". On a buy-in/buy-out always separate **three** things: the **income-tax** change (profit-share allocation from the date of change), the **CGT** on goodwill (SP D12 part-disposal; BADR per §4 only if that partner's conditions are met), and **SDLT** (only if the firm holds land; para 14/20 SLP, fact-specific, never a fixed figure). Pair the buy-in/buy-out funding with §5.B (interest deductible, principal not). Keep SDLT and BADR availability **hedged and fact-specific**. Do NOT state a flat SDLT charge or assume BADR automatically reaches a partner's goodwill slice. Cross-ref §4 (goodwill valuation, BADR, s.162), §5 (structure/extraction), §5.B (funding), §12.A (squat new-trade losses where a buy-in funds a start-up).

### 12.A Early-trade-losses relief: the squat-practice ramp (locked, 2026-06-03, HP-lock gate Wave 4 cluster A)

**Position.** A **squat practice** (a new practice built from scratch, no patient base on day one) typically runs at a **tax loss for the early ramp**: heavy capital allowances on the fit-out (AIA/FYA per §7/§7.A front-load relief), pre-revenue running costs, and a patient list that builds slowly. An **unincorporated** squat (sole trader or partnership) can use the loss in three ways: **early-trade-losses relief (ITA 2007 s.72)** carries a loss made in **any of the first 4 tax years** of the trade **back against the individual's general income of the 3 tax years before the loss year, earliest year first** (so a dentist who was an employed/associate earner before opening can reclaim tax paid in those earlier years, a real cash injection during the ramp); **sideways relief (s.64)** sets the loss against **general income of the loss year and/or the previous year**; and **carry-forward (s.83)** carries an unrelieved loss forward against **future profits of the same trade**. Sideways and early-trade relief are subject to the **commercial-basis** condition (the trade must be carried on commercially with a view to profit) and the general **loss-relief cap** (the greater of £50,000 or 25% of adjusted total income, on sideways/early-trade claims against other income). A **company** squat instead carries losses under the corporation-tax loss rules (carry-back 12 months, otherwise carry-forward), with no s.72 equivalent. Working-capital planning must fund the **cash burn** through the ramp regardless of the tax relief, because the relief lands after the loss year.

- **Statutory hooks:** **ITA 2007 s.72** (early-trade-losses relief, first 4 years, carry back 3 years earliest-first) https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2007/3/section/72 ; **s.64** (trade loss against general income, loss year and/or previous year); **s.83** (carry-forward against same-trade profits); **ss.24A/103C** (general loss-relief cap, greater of £50,000 or 25% of adjusted total income); company losses under **CTA 2010 Part 4**.
- **HMRC manual anchors:** **BIM85000+ / BIM85015 / BIM85060+** (trade loss reliefs, s.64/s.72/s.83); **HS227** (losses helpsheet).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** for a squat, map the three unincorporated reliefs (s.72 early-trade carry-back as the standout for a dentist with prior earnings; s.64 sideways; s.83 carry-forward), flag the **commercial-basis** condition and the **£50,000/25% cap**, and state plainly that a **company** squat has no s.72 equivalent (carry-back is 12 months). Separate the **tax relief** (lands later) from the **working-capital need** (must be funded through the ramp now). Cross-ref §7/§7.A (capital allowances driving the loss), §8 (self-assessment), §12 (partnership squat), §5.B (funding).

## 13. Employing staff: payroll, employer cost and auto-enrolment (locked, 2026-06-04, HP-lock gate Wave 4 cluster C)

**Position.** When a dental practice employs staff (a nurse, a practice manager, a receptionist, a hygienist on a contract of employment, a genuinely employed spouse), it takes on three connected obligations: **PAYE/RTI payroll**, the **employer's cost of the wage**, and **pension auto-enrolment**. The total cost of an employee is always **more than the headline salary**, and the page must build the true cost.

- **PAYE and Real Time Information (RTI).** The employer must operate **PAYE**, deducting income tax and employee (primary Class 1) NIC from wages, and report **on or before each payday** under RTI (Full Payment Submission), paying HMRC monthly (or quarterly for small employers). A practice taking on its first employee registers as an employer with HMRC. Late RTI/late payment carries penalties.
- **Employer (secondary Class 1) NIC.** The employer pays NIC on the employee's earnings above the **secondary threshold of £5,000 at 15% from 6 April 2025** (per §5; the threshold was cut to £5,000 and the rate raised to 15% on 6 Apr 2025). The **Employment Allowance is £10,500** and offsets employer NIC, but is **not available where the only employee is a single director** (§5); a practice with genuine non-director staff can claim it, materially reducing the employer NIC bill on the first ~£10,500 of secondary NIC. The **LEL (£6,708)** preserves the employee's state-pension/contributory entitlement even where little or no NIC is actually due.
- **Pension auto-enrolment (the workplace pension duty).** Every employer must **automatically enrol** eligible jobholders into a qualifying workplace pension and contribute. **Eligible jobholder** = aged 22 to State Pension Age and earning over the **earnings trigger of £10,000 (2025/26)**. Contributions are calculated on **qualifying earnings**, the band **£6,240 to £50,270 (2025/26)**. The **minimum total contribution is 8% of qualifying earnings** (since 6 April 2019), of which the **employer must pay at least 3%** and the employee pays the balance (**5%**, including basic-rate tax relief). Workers below the trigger or under 22 may have a **right to opt in** (with employer contribution) or to join (without), depending on their earnings band. Auto-enrolment has **re-enrolment every ~3 years** and a **declaration of compliance** duty. The **employer's 3% pension contribution is a deductible business expense** (trading deduction / employer pension contribution rules) and carries **no NIC**.
- **The real cost of an employee.** Headline salary **plus** employer NIC (15% above £5,000, net of any Employment Allowance) **plus** employer pension (3% of qualifying earnings minimum) **plus** the cost to run payroll **plus** other on-costs (holiday pay, statutory sick/maternity pay exposure, training, indemnity where relevant). Always model the loaded cost, not the salary. **Employer's liability insurance is compulsory** (Employers' Liability (Compulsory Insurance) Act 1969) and is an allowable expense.

- **Statutory hooks:** **PAYE/RTI** under the Income Tax (PAYE) Regulations 2003 (SI 2003/2682) and ITEPA 2003 Part 11; **secondary Class 1 NIC** under SSCBA 1992 (15% above the £5,000 secondary threshold from 6 Apr 2025, per §5); **Employment Allowance** (National Insurance Contributions Act 2014, £10,500, single-director restriction); **auto-enrolment** under the **Pensions Act 2008** Part 1 (employer duties, qualifying earnings, minimum contributions) and the automatic enrolment thresholds review (earnings trigger £10,000, qualifying earnings band £6,240 to £50,270, 2025/26); **employer pension contribution deduction** FA 2004 s.196 (per §5); **Employers' Liability (Compulsory Insurance) Act 1969**.
- **HMRC / regulator anchors:** gov.uk "Workplace pensions: what you, your employer and the government pay" (8% total / 3% employer / 5% employee on qualifying earnings); The Pensions Regulator employer duties + earnings thresholds; gov.uk PAYE/RTI for employers; HMRC EIM/NIM for secondary Class 1. Auto-enrolment thresholds + contribution split verified at gov.uk / DWP threshold review on 2026-06-04.
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** always build the **loaded cost** of an employee (salary + 15% employer NIC above £5,000 + 3% employer pension + payroll + on-costs), not the salary alone. Use the **2025/26 auto-enrolment figures** (trigger £10,000, band £6,240 to £50,270, 8% total / 3% employer minimum / 5% employee) and **date-tag** them (the thresholds are reviewed annually; they were held at 2024/25 levels for 2025/26). State that the **Employment Allowance (£10,500)** is available to a practice with genuine non-director staff but **not** to a single-director company (§5). Note PAYE/RTI is **on or before payday**, and employer's liability insurance is **compulsory**. Do NOT state employer NIC at the old 13.8%/£9,100 figures (now 15%/£5,000 from 6 Apr 2025, §5). Do NOT omit the pension and on-costs from the cost of a hire. Cross-ref §5 (employer NIC, Employment Allowance, employer pension deduction), §8 (the practice's own expense deduction for wages), §13.A (trivial benefits for the team).

### 13.A Trivial benefits and tax-efficient staff perks (locked, 2026-06-04, HP-lock gate Wave 4 cluster C)

**Position.** A practice can give staff certain **small perks free of tax and NIC** under the **trivial benefits exemption (ITEPA 2003 s.323A)**. A benefit is exempt where **all four** conditions are met: (1) it **cost £50 or less** to provide (per benefit, not per year; if the cost exceeds £50 the **whole** amount is taxable, not just the excess); (2) it is **not cash or a cash voucher** (a non-cash voucher such as a store gift card is fine); (3) it is **not a reward for services or performance** (so not a bonus by another name); and (4) it is **not provided under the employment contract or a salary-sacrifice arrangement**. Typical qualifying perks: a birthday gift, a team meal out, flowers, a small festive hamper, a bottle of wine. There is **no annual limit for ordinary employees** (each sub-£50 benefit can be repeated, though a clear pattern that looks like contractual reward risks the "not in recognition of services" condition). For a **director or other office-holder of a close company** (and members of their family/household), an **annual cap of £300** applies across all trivial benefits in the tax year (**s.323B**, the "available exempt amount"), still subject to the £50-per-benefit limit. Because a dental company is a **close company**, the practice owner-director is within the £300 annual cap. Other tax-efficient/exempt staff provisions to flag (each with its own conditions): an **annual staff event** exemption (e.g. a Christmas party) up to **£150 per head per year** (ITEPA s.264; all-employee, recurring/annual, £150 is an all-or-nothing limit per head); **mobile phone** (one per employee, s.319); **eye tests/glasses for VDU use**; and **pension contributions** (the genuinely tax-efficient large lever, §5/§2.B).

- **Statutory hooks:** **ITEPA 2003 s.323A** (trivial benefits exemption: £50, not cash/voucher, not for services, not contractual) https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2003/1/section/323A ; **s.323B** (annual exempt amount £300 for directors/office-holders of close companies); **s.264** (annual parties/functions, £150 per head); **s.319** (mobile phones). 
- **HMRC manual anchors:** **EIM21864+** (trivial benefits exemption, £50 cap, close-company director £300 cap); **EIM21690+** (annual parties, £150). £50/£300 caps + s.323A/s.323B verified at legislation.gov.uk / gov.uk on 2026-06-04.
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** state all four trivial-benefit conditions together and stress the £50 is a **cliff edge** (over £50 = the whole amount taxable). Flag the **£300 annual cap for the owner-director** because a dental company is a close company. Keep the **£150 staff-event** limit separate (it is per head per year and also all-or-nothing). Frame perks as a genuine but **modest** tool, and point to **employer pension contributions** as the real tax-efficient reward lever (§5, §2.B). Do NOT describe a cash bonus or a contractual perk as a trivial benefit. Do NOT say trivial benefits are unlimited for a director (the £300 close-company cap applies). Cross-ref §5, §13.

- **Residence/mobility (Wave 4 cluster B):** **TCGA 1992 s.10A/s.10AA** (temporary non-residents, gains charged in the year of return); **Finance Act 2013 Schedule 45 Part 4** (temporary non-residence: 4-of-7 prior-year residence, 5-years-or-less, income limbs); **Finance Act 2025** (FIG regime from 6 Apr 2025: qualifying new resident, 10+ years prior non-residence, 4 years' relief, PA/AEA forfeited on claim; remittance basis + domicile abolished); **TIOPA 2010 Part 2** (double taxation relief / foreign tax credit); OECD Model **Article 4** residence tie-breaker (permanent home, centre of vital interests, habitual abode, nationality, mutual agreement). Manuals: RFIG (temporary non-residence + FIG); CG26500+ (s.10A); INTM154000+ (treaty residence tie-breaker); DT manual (by territory).
- **Staff/payroll/perks (Wave 4 cluster C):** **Pensions Act 2008 Part 1** (auto-enrolment employer duties; 2025/26 trigger £10,000, qualifying earnings band £6,240 to £50,270, 8% total / 3% employer min / 5% employee); **Income Tax (PAYE) Regulations 2003** + ITEPA 2003 Part 11 (PAYE/RTI, on-or-before payday); **SSCBA 1992** secondary Class 1 NIC (15% above £5,000 from 6 Apr 2025, §5); **National Insurance Contributions Act 2014** (Employment Allowance £10,500, single-director restriction); **ITEPA 2003 s.323A** (trivial benefits £50, not cash/voucher/services/contract), **s.323B** (£300 close-company director annual cap), **s.264** (annual function £150/head), **s.319** (mobile phone); **FA 2004 s.196** (employer pension deduction); **Employers' Liability (Compulsory Insurance) Act 1969**. Manuals: EIM21864+ (trivial benefits), EIM21690+ (annual parties); The Pensions Regulator employer duties.
