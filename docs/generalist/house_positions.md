## 1. Business structures: sole trader, partnership, limited company (locked figures, DRAFT 2026-06-12)

**Position.** Three core structures for an owner-managed UK business. **Sole trader:** the individual and the business are the same legal person; profits taxed as income (income tax + Class 4 NIC, §2); unlimited personal liability; simplest to run; registers for Self Assessment. **Partnership (general):** two or more persons carrying on a business in common; **tax-transparent**, each partner taxed individually on their profit share per the profit-sharing arrangement (ITTOIA 2005 s.850, §6); unlimited liability (joint and several). **LLP:** members taxed as partners (transparent) but with limited liability; a separate legal person registered at Companies House. **Limited company:** a separate legal person; the company pays corporation tax (§3) on profit, and the owner extracts via salary and dividends (§4); limited liability; more administration (statutory accounts, confirmation statement, corporation tax return). No structure is universally best: the answer turns on profit level, liability exposure, reinvestment needs, and extraction plans.

- **Statutory hooks:** Partnership Act 1890 (general partnerships); Limited Liability Partnerships Act 2000 (LLPs); Companies Act 2006 (companies); ITTOIA 2005 Part 2 (trading income, sole traders/partners) and s.850 (partner profit allocation) https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2005/5/section/850 ; CTA 2009 (company profits charged to corporation tax).
- **HMRC manual anchors:** BIM (business income manual, trade computation); PM (partnership manual) PM130000+ (profit allocation).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** lead with "structure is a trade-off, not a ranking". Always present sole trader / partnership / company as alternatives with their own tax, liability and admin profiles. State the partnership is **tax-transparent** (partners taxed individually, the firm does not pay income tax). Do NOT claim a limited company is "always more tax-efficient" (at typical owner-manager profits the saving is modest and the 2026/27 dividend rise narrows it, §4). Cross-ref §1.A (incorporation), §3 (CT), §4 (extraction).

### 1.A Incorporation: moving from sole trader to limited company (DRAFT 2026-06-12)

**Position.** Incorporating transfers the trade and its assets to a new company in exchange for shares (and possibly other consideration). Two reliefs commonly apply. **TCGA 1992 s.162 incorporation relief** defers the CGT on chargeable business assets (notably goodwill) where the **whole business is transferred as a going concern, with all its assets (other than cash), wholly or partly for shares**: the deferred gain rolls into the base cost of the shares and crystallises on a later share disposal (cash consideration restricts the relief pro rata). Alternatively, **s.165 gift holdover relief** can defer a gain on a transfer of business assets. **Goodwill amortisation relief** for the company is restricted: relief on purchased goodwill is generally unavailable for acquisitions between 8 July 2015 and 31 March 2019, and from 1 April 2019 is given at a **fixed 6.5% per year** but only where the goodwill is acquired with qualifying intellectual property and capped at 6x the qualifying-IP spend (CTA 2009 Part 8 Ch 15A). Stamp duty, VAT (transfer of a going concern, TOGC), PAYE re-registration and capital-allowance continuity (assets transferred at tax written-down value via election, or at market value) all need handling. Incorporation is rarely a pure tax win at typical owner-manager profit; weigh administration, the dividend-rate rise (§4) and the loss of simplicity.

- **Statutory hooks:** **TCGA 1992 s.162** (incorporation relief) https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12/section/162 ; **s.165** (gift holdover relief); **CTA 2009 Part 8 Ch 15A / s.879A+** (corporate intangibles / goodwill, 6.5% restricted relief, FA 2019); VATA 1994 / VAT Notice 700/9 (TOGC); CAA 2001 s.266/s.267 (succession election, transfers at tax written-down value).
- **HMRC manual anchors:** **CG65700+** (s.162 incorporation relief); **CIRD44050+** (goodwill/relevant-assets relief, restrictions); VTOGC (transfer of a going concern).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** present incorporation as a calculation, not a default. State s.162 defers (does not cancel) the gain into the shares. On goodwill relief use 6.5% only for post-1-April-2019 acquisitions and surface the qualifying-IP / 6x-cap condition (a blanket "buy after April 2019 and you always get 6.5%" is wrong). Flag the capital-allowance election and the TOGC point. Do NOT present incorporation as automatically tax-saving. Cross-ref §3, §4, §5 (CGT/BADR on a later sale).

## 2. Sole-trader / partnership taxation: income tax, Class 4 NIC, self-assessment (locked figures, DRAFT 2026-06-12)

**Position.** A sole trader or partner pays **income tax** on trading profit at the individual's marginal rate plus **Class 4 NIC**. **Income tax (2025/26, rUK rates):** personal allowance **£12,570** (tapered away £1 for every £2 above £100,000, gone at £125,140); basic **20%** to £50,270; higher **40%** to £125,140; additional **45%** above. (Scotland sets its own non-savings/non-dividend bands and rates, so date-and-jurisdiction tag any rate for a Scottish trader.) **Class 4 NIC (2025/26): 6%** on profits £12,570 to £50,270, then **2%** above £50,270. **Class 2 NIC** liability was **removed from 6 April 2024**: a self-employed person with profits at or above the Small Profits Threshold is treated as having paid and keeps state-pension entitlement; those below can still pay voluntary Class 2. **Basis of assessment:** the **tax-year basis** applies from 2024/25 (basis-period reform, §2.A).

- **Statutory hooks:** ITTOIA 2005 Part 2 (trading income); ITA 2007 Part 2 (income tax rates and personal allowance); **SSCBA 1992 s.15** (Class 4 NIC) and s.11 (Class 2, reformed by the National Insurance Contributions Act 2024 from 6 Apr 2024).
- **HMRC manual anchors:** BIM (trade profit computation); NIM24000+ (Class 4); self-assessment SAM.
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** use Class 4 6%/2% and the 2025/26 bands, each date-and-jurisdiction tagged. State plainly that **Class 2 is no longer payable from 6 Apr 2024** for profits above the Small Profits Threshold (do NOT tell a sole trader to pay a weekly Class 2 charge). Flag Scotland's separate income-tax bands. Cross-ref §2.A (basis-period reform), §2.B (self-assessment deadlines), §8 (expenses).

### 2.A Basis-period reform: the tax-year basis (locked, DRAFT 2026-06-12)

**Position.** From **2024/25** unincorporated businesses are taxed on the **tax-year basis**: profits for the year to 5 April (or 31 March, treated as coterminous), regardless of the accounting date. **2023/24 was the transition year**, in which a business with a non-March/April year end was taxed on its normal 12 months **plus** the "transition part" up to 5 April 2024, with **overlap relief** (from earlier years) deducted. Any net **transition profit** is **spread over five tax years (2023/24 to 2027/28)** by default, with an option to accelerate. From 2024/25 onward, a business not using a 31 March / 5 April year end must apportion two sets of accounts (or use provisional figures) to land on the tax-year basis.

- **Statutory hooks:** Finance Act 2022 Sch 1 (basis-period reform); ITTOIA 2005 Part 2 Ch 15 as amended.
- **HMRC manual anchors:** BIM81200+ (basis periods, tax-year basis, transition and overlap relief).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** state the tax-year basis applies **from 2024/25**, 2023/24 was the transition year, and transition profit spreads **five years to 2027/28**. Recommend a 31 March / 5 April accounting date to avoid annual apportionment. Use overlap relief on the transition. Do NOT describe the old "current year basis" / prior-year rules as still operating. Cross-ref §2, §8.

### 2.B Self-assessment deadlines and payments on account (locked, DRAFT 2026-06-12)

**Position.** Self-assessment filing and payment deadlines: register by **5 October** after the end of the first tax year of trading; **paper return 31 October**; **online return and balancing payment 31 January** after the tax year. **Payments on account (POAs):** two interim payments toward the next year's liability, due **31 January** and **31 July**, **each 50% of the prior year's income-tax-plus-Class-4 liability**, required where the prior liability **exceeded £1,000 and less than 80% was collected at source**. The first self-employed year creates the well-known "double bill" (the balancing payment for year one plus the first POA, both on 31 January). Late filing: an automatic **£100** penalty, then daily and further penalties; late payment carries interest plus 5% surcharges at 30 days / 6 months / 12 months.

- **Statutory hooks:** **TMA 1970 s.59A** (payments on account), s.59B (balancing payment), s.7/s.8 (notice to file); FA 2009 Sch 55 (late-filing penalties) and Sch 56 (late-payment penalties).
- **HMRC manual anchors:** SAM (self-assessment manual); payments on account SAM1000+.
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** state the 31 Jan / 31 July POA dates and the 50%-of-prior-year mechanic, the £1,000 / 80%-at-source trigger, and the first-year "double bill". Use the £100 late-filing penalty. Cross-ref §2, §9 (MTD changes the in-year obligations from April 2026).

## 3. Corporation tax: rates and marginal relief (locked figures, DRAFT 2026-06-12)

**Position.** A company pays **corporation tax** on its taxable profit. **Small profits rate 19%** where augmented profits do not exceed the **lower limit £50,000**; **main rate 25%** where they exceed the **upper limit £250,000**; **marginal relief** tapers the effective rate between £50,000 and £250,000 (FY2025 and FY2026). The limits are **divided by the number of associated companies** and **time-apportioned** for short periods. Marginal relief uses the standard fraction (3/200). The effective marginal rate on profits in the £50,000 to £250,000 band is **26.5%** (the slice between the limits is taxed at the margin to blend 19% up to 25%). The corporation tax return (CT600) and payment are due **9 months and 1 day** after the period end (large companies pay by quarterly instalments).

- **Statutory hooks:** **CTA 2010 Part 3** (ss.18 to 19 small profits rate and marginal relief; associated-company and limit-apportionment rules); CTA 2009 (charge to CT on income and the loan-relationship/intangibles regimes).
- **Authority anchors:** https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/rates-and-allowances-corporation-tax/rates-and-allowances-corporation-tax (19%/25%, £50k/£250k, marginal relief, FY2025 and FY2026).
- **HMRC manual anchors:** CTM01000+ (charge and rates); marginal relief CTM03900+; associated companies CTM03940+.
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** use 19% / 25% with the £50,000 / £250,000 limits and note marginal relief between them (effective ~26.5% in the band). Always flag that the limits are **shared between associated companies** and time-apportioned (a common trap for owner-managers with two companies). State CT is due **9 months and 1 day** after the period end. Do NOT state a single flat CT rate. Cross-ref §4 (extraction), §7 (capital allowances reduce taxable profit).

## 4. Profit extraction: salary, dividends and the employer-NIC position (locked figures, DRAFT 2026-06-12)

**Position.** A director-shareholder of an owner-managed company extracts profit through a mix of **salary** (deductible for the company, taxable as employment income, subject to NIC), **dividends** (paid from post-tax profit, no NIC, taxed at dividend rates) and **employer pension contributions** (§10). The usual owner-manager pattern is a **modest salary plus dividends**, with the salary set either at the secondary threshold or at the personal allowance depending on whether the company can claim the Employment Allowance (see the employer-NIC block below).

**Corporation tax on the profit first:** §3 (19% / 25% / marginal relief).

**Dividend tax:**
- 2025/26: ordinary **8.75%**, upper **33.75%**, additional **39.35%**; **dividend allowance £500**.
- From **6 April 2026 (FA 2026 s.4):** ordinary **10.75%**, upper **35.75%**; additional **39.35%** unchanged; allowance **£500**. **Tag the tax year on every dividend figure.**

**Employer (secondary Class 1) NIC, the resolved position (verified gov.uk 2026-06-12):** the company pays employer NIC at **15%** on salary above the **secondary threshold of £5,000 a year (£96/week)**, with effect **from 6 April 2025** (the Autumn 2024 Budget cut the threshold to £5,000 and raised the rate to 15%). **This supersedes the old 13.8% rate and £9,100 threshold, which applied up to 5 April 2025 and must NOT be used for 2025/26 onward.** The **Lower Earnings Limit is £6,500/year (£125/week)** for 2025/26, preserving the director's state-pension entitlement even where little NIC is due. The **Employment Allowance is £10,500** but is **not available to a company whose only employee is a single director**: that is why a one-director company often sets salary at the secondary threshold (£5,000), while a company with at least one genuinely employed non-director (for example a genuinely employed spouse on a market-rate wage) can claim the £10,500 allowance and lift the director's salary to the personal allowance (£12,570) more efficiently.

> ESTATE-WIDE NOTE (prominent). The corpus warning that pages may state employer NIC at **13.8% / £9,100** is a stale-corpus artefact for any period from 6 April 2025. The verified law for 2025/26 (and continuing into 2026/27) is **15% above a £5,000 secondary threshold**. Every generalist salary/dividend/extraction page, every "optimal director's salary" page, and every "cost of employing staff" page (§9) must use 15% / £5,000 and must NOT use 13.8% / £9,100 except as a clearly date-tagged historic figure (to 5 April 2025). Treat any 13.8%/£9,100 token in a live page as a corrective-rewrite hit.

**Optimal-salary logic (2025/26):** with no Employment Allowance (single-director company), a salary at the **secondary threshold (£5,000)** avoids employer NIC entirely; some set it at the **personal allowance (£12,570)** accepting employer NIC of 15% on the £7,570 band (about £1,135) because the salary is corporation-tax-deductible and the director still has personal allowance to use. With the Employment Allowance available (a genuine second employee), a £12,570 salary is usually optimal because the EA absorbs the employer NIC. Always model both; do not assert a single "best salary" figure without stating the EA assumption.

**Employer pension contributions** are a tax-efficient extraction route: deductible for corporation tax on a **paid basis** (FA 2004 s.196, wholly and exclusively), with **no NIC**, subject to the §10 annual allowance and carry-forward.

- **Statutory hooks:** **CTA 2010 Part 3** (CT rates, §3); **ITA 2007 s.8** (dividend rates, amended by **FA 2026 s.4** https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2026/11/section/4 ); ITTOIA 2005 Part 4 Ch 3 (dividend income); **SSCBA 1992** secondary Class 1 NIC (15% above £5,000 from 6 Apr 2025); National Insurance Contributions Act 2014 (Employment Allowance £10,500, single-director restriction); ITEPA 2003 (employment income, salary); **FA 2004 s.196** (employer pension deduction).
- **Authority anchors:** https://www.gov.uk/guidance/rates-and-thresholds-for-employers-2025-to-2026 (employer NIC 15% / £5,000, EA £10,500, LEL £6,500); https://www.gov.uk/tax-on-dividends (dividend rates + £500 allowance).
- **HMRC manual anchors:** dividends CTM/SAIM; employer NIC NIM06000+; Employment Allowance NIM24175+; spouse wages **BIM37700+** (wholly and exclusively, commensurate with work done).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** never state a dividend rate without its tax-year tag (from 6 Apr 2026 use 10.75% / 35.75%). Always use employer NIC **15% / £5,000 secondary threshold (from 6 Apr 2025)** and **never 13.8% / £9,100** for current advice. State the **Employment Allowance £10,500 single-director exclusion** explicitly when discussing optimal salary. Spouse salary must be **genuine and market-rate** (HMRC disallows illusory/above-market pay). Do NOT present a single "optimal salary" without the EA assumption. Cross-ref §3, §4.A (director's loans), §9 (employing staff), §10 (pensions), §5 (CGT/BADR on exit).

### 4.A Director's loan accounts and the s.455 charge (locked, DRAFT 2026-06-12)

**Position.** An owner-managed company is normally a **close company**. An **overdrawn director's loan account** (drawings ahead of declared salary or dividend) triggers a **CTA 2010 s.455 charge**: the company pays an amount equal to a percentage of the loan outstanding **9 months and 1 day** after the period end, at a rate equal to the **dividend upper rate** for the year the loan is made, that is **33.75% for loans made in 2025/26** and **35.75% for loans made on or after 6 April 2026** (the rate tracks ITA 2007 s.8 as amended by FA 2026 s.4). The charge is **temporary**: **s.458 relief** repays it when the loan is repaid, released or written off, but the relief is **deferred to 9 months and 1 day after the end of the accounting period in which repayment occurs** (4-year claim window). Anti-avoidance blocks repay-and-redraw: the **30-day rule (s.464ZA)** and the **arrangements rule (s.464C/464D)** for balances of £15,000 or more. A loan **over £10,000** at any point in the year is also a **beneficial-loan benefit in kind** (ITEPA 2003 ss.173 to 191) unless interest is paid at the **official rate**, reportable on P11D with **Class 1A NIC at 15%** (the Class 1A rate aligns with the secondary Class 1 rate, so 15% from 6 Apr 2025).

- **Statutory hooks:** **CTA 2010 s.455** (charge on loans to participators, rate = dividend upper rate) https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2010/4/section/455 ; **s.458** (relief on repayment); **s.464ZA** (30-day rule), **s.464C / s.464D** (£15,000 arrangements rule); **ITEPA 2003 ss.173 to 191** (beneficial loans, £10,000 threshold, official rate).
- **HMRC manual anchors:** **CTM61500+ / CTM61630** (loans to participators, s.455/s.458, bed-and-breakfasting); **EIM26100+** (beneficial loans, official rate).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** state the s.455 rate **with its date band** (33.75% on pre-6-Apr-2026 loans, 35.75% on/after), the 9-months-and-1-day timing, and that s.458 relief is **deferred**, not immediate. Flag the £10,000 BIK threshold. Do NOT describe s.455 as a permanent tax (it is repayable) or imply the relief is instant. Cross-ref §4.

## 5. Capital gains tax and Business Asset Disposal Relief (locked figures, DRAFT 2026-06-12)

**Position.** **CGT for individuals** on chargeable assets: from **30 October 2024** the main rates are **18%** (gains within the basic-rate band) and **24%** (gains above it), applied to **all chargeable assets** including residential property (the previously higher 28% / 24% residential rates were aligned). **Annual exempt amount £3,000** (2025/26 and 2026/27). Gains are reported through Self Assessment; UK residential-property gains have a separate **60-day** reporting-and-payment obligation.

**Business Asset Disposal Relief (BADR):** a reduced CGT rate on qualifying business disposals up to a **£1,000,000 lifetime limit per individual** (fixed since 11 March 2020, not indexed). Rate: **10%** to 5 Apr 2025; **14%** for disposals 6 Apr 2025 to 5 Apr 2026; **18%** from 6 Apr 2026. Conditions held throughout the **2-year period to disposal**: a trading business (sole trader/partnership disposing of the business or a qualifying asset on cessation) or, for a **share sale**, a **personal company** holding (at least **5% of ordinary share capital and 5% of voting rights**, plus a 5% economic entitlement) with the seller an **officer or employee** throughout, and the company trading.

**Asset sale vs share sale (a core owner-manager fork).** On a **share sale** the seller disposes of shares (one CGT disposal, BADR potentially available, buyer takes the company with its history and latent liabilities). On an **asset (trade and assets) sale** the **company** sells its assets (corporation tax on any gains/balancing charges inside the company), then the owner extracts the net proceeds (a second tax layer on extraction, or on a later liquidation where a **Members' Voluntary Liquidation** can deliver a capital distribution within BADR, subject to the Targeted Anti-Avoidance Rule on phoenixism). Buyers usually prefer assets (clean, step-up, no inherited liabilities); sellers usually prefer shares (single CGT layer, BADR). The structure drives very different tax, so model both.

- **Statutory hooks:** **TCGA 1992** s.1H and s.4 (CGT rates 18%/24% from 30 Oct 2024), s.1K (annual exempt amount); **ss.169H to 169S** (BADR) https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12 ; CTA 2010 s.1030A and the TAAR (capital treatment on a distribution in a winding up) for MVLs.
- **Authority anchors:** https://www.gov.uk/business-asset-disposal-relief (10%/14%/18% schedule); https://www.gov.uk/capital-gains-tax/rates (18%/24%, AEA £3,000).
- **HMRC manual anchors:** **CG63950+** (BADR); CG10000+ (CGT general); CTM36300+ (distributions in a winding up / phoenix TAAR).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** state the BADR rate **with its exact date band** every time (the 6 Apr 2026 step to 18% is a live planning lever for late-2025/26 completions). Use CGT **18%/24%** and AEA **£3,000**, both date-tagged. Always separate **asset sale** from **share sale** and note the double tax layer on an asset sale. ## 6. Partnerships and partnership changes (locked, DRAFT 2026-06-12)

**Position.** A partnership (and an LLP taxed as a partnership) is **tax-transparent**: the firm files a partnership return but does not pay income tax; each partner is taxed individually on their **profit share per the profit-sharing arrangement for the period** (**ITTOIA 2005 s.850**), and losses are allocated the same way. On a **change of partners** (an incoming equity partner buying in, or a partner retiring), three things move at once and must be kept separate: (1) the **income-tax** profit-share allocation changes from the date of change; (2) any **goodwill** payment is a **CGT** matter (a change in a partner's fractional share of the firm's chargeable assets is a part-disposal under **HMRC Statement of Practice D12**; where an incomer pays existing partners for goodwill outside the accounts, that is consideration for a disposal, potentially within BADR for the receiving partner if their §5 conditions are met; a no-payment reallocation at book value is typically no-gain/no-loss); and (3) **SDLT** arises only where the **partnership holds a chargeable interest in land**, in which case a transfer of a partnership interest is a land transaction under **FA 2003 Sch 15 para 14** with chargeable consideration by the **"sum of the lower proportions"** (Sch 15 para 20), which is fact-specific and can compute to a low or nil charge among connected partners. **LLP salaried-member rules** (ITTOIA 2005 s.863A onward) can re-characterise a "fixed-share" member as an employee for tax if the three conditions (disguised salary, no significant influence, no significant capital contribution) are all met.

- **Statutory hooks:** **ITTOIA 2005 s.850** (profit allocation, transparency) https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2005/5/section/850 ; s.863A+ (salaried members of LLPs); **HMRC SP D12** (partnerships and CGT); **FA 2003 Sch 15** paras 10/14/18/20 (SDLT on partnership land, transfer of interest, SLP); ITA 2007 s.398 (qualifying loan interest to buy into a partnership).
- **HMRC manual anchors:** **PM130000+** (profit allocation), PM250000+ (salaried members); **CG27000+ / CG27170** (SP D12 share changes); **SDLTM33000+ / SDLTM33730 / SDLTM33750** (Sch 15, para 14/20); BIM72000+ (changes in partners).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** lead with "the partnership is tax-transparent; each partner is taxed on their profit share (s.850)". On a buy-in/buy-out separate the **three** strands (income-tax allocation, CGT on goodwill per SP D12, SDLT only if the firm holds land). Keep SDLT and BADR availability **hedged and fact-specific** (never a flat SDLT figure). Flag the **LLP salaried-member** trap for fixed-share members. Cross-ref §1 (structures), §5 (BADR), §8 (loss reliefs for a new venture).

## 7. VAT (locked figures, DRAFT 2026-06-12)

**Position.** A business must register for VAT when **taxable turnover exceeds £90,000** in any rolling 12 months, or is expected to exceed £90,000 in the next 30 days (raised from £85,000 on 1 April 2024). Voluntary registration below the threshold is possible (to reclaim input VAT). The **deregistration threshold is £88,000**. Standard rate **20%**; reduced **5%**; zero-rated and exempt categories apply per VATA 1994. **Making Tax Digital for VAT** has applied to **all VAT-registered businesses since April 2022** (digital records and MTD-compatible software for returns), regardless of turnover.

**Cash accounting scheme:** account for VAT on **payments received and made** rather than invoice dates (helps cash flow and gives automatic bad-debt relief); join if taxable turnover is **£1.35 million or less**, leave when it exceeds **£1.6 million**.

**Flat Rate Scheme (FRS):** pay a single flat percentage of **VAT-inclusive** turnover instead of tracking input VAT; join if expected taxable turnover is **£150,000 or less (ex VAT)**, with a **1% discount in the first year** of VAT registration. **Limited cost business (limited cost trader):** if goods cost **less than 2% of turnover, or less than £1,000 a year**, the flat rate is the high **16.5%**, which usually makes the FRS unattractive for labour-only/service businesses (most contractors are limited cost businesses).

- **Statutory hooks:** **VATA 1994** (charge, Sch 1 registration/deregistration thresholds); Value Added Tax Regulations 1995 (SI 1995/2518) (cash accounting Part VIII, flat rate Part VIIA); MTD for VAT under the VAT (Amendment) Regs.
- **Authority anchors:** https://www.gov.uk/vat-registration/when-to-register (£90,000); https://www.gov.uk/vat-flat-rate-scheme/eligibility (£150,000) and /how-much-you-pay (16.5% limited cost, 1% first-year discount).
- **HMRC manual anchors:** VATREG (registration); FRS7000+ (flat rate, limited cost trader); VCAS (cash accounting).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** use **£90,000** registration / **£88,000** deregistration and never the old £85,000 for current advice. State **MTD for VAT applies to all VAT-registered businesses since April 2022**. For the FRS always flag the **16.5% limited cost business** rate and its 2%/£1,000 goods test (the reason most service contractors should not use the FRS). Use cash-accounting £1.35m join / £1.6m leave. Cross-ref §9 (MTD ITSA is separate, income tax not VAT).

## 8. Capital allowances (locked figures, DRAFT 2026-06-12)

**Position.** Capital allowances give tax relief on qualifying capital spend (plant and machinery, integral features), replacing non-deductible depreciation. **Annual Investment Allowance (AIA) £1,000,000 per 12-month period** (permanent), 100% relief on most plant and machinery, available to companies and unincorporated businesses, time-apportioned for short periods and **not carried forward** (use-it-or-lose-it). **Main-rate writing-down allowance: 18%, reducing to 14% from 1 April 2026 (CT) / 6 April 2026 (IT)** per **FA 2026 s.28** (a straddling period uses a hybrid time-apportioned rate). **Special-rate pool: 6%** WDA (integral features: electrical, cold/hot water, heating, lighting, air and ventilation systems, plus long-life assets), **unchanged**. **New 40% first-year allowance** on **new and unused** main-rate plant and machinery (not second-hand, not cars) **from 1 January 2026** per **FA 2026 s.29**, available to companies and unincorporated businesses. **Full expensing (companies only): 100% FYA** on new main-rate plant and **50% FYA** on new special-rate plant, permanent. **Cars** are excluded from AIA and the FYAs; relief is by WDA at 18% / 6% by CO2 emissions (or 100% FYA for new zero-emission cars). **Structures and Buildings Allowance** at **3% per year** on qualifying construction (not land, not pooled plant).

- **Statutory hooks:** **CAA 2001** s.38A/s.51A (AIA), **s.56** (main-rate WDA, 18% to 14% per FA 2026 s.28 https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2026/11/section/28 ), ss.104A to 104D (special-rate 6%), **ss.45U/45V** (40% FYA per FA 2026 s.29 https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2026/11/section/29 ), s.39/s.45S (full expensing), Part 2A (SBA 3%); s.198 (fixtures election on a property purchase, 2-year limit s.201).
- **HMRC manual anchors:** CA23080+ (AIA); CA22300+ (integral features); CA23150+ (full expensing); CA26800+ (fixtures elections); CA90000+ (SBA).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** use AIA £1m; **date-tag the main-rate WDA every time** (18% to Mar/Apr 2026, then 14%; FA 2026 s.28); special rate 6%. For a **new** main-rate asset compare AIA (100%) with the 40% FYA (which leaves the balance in the pool) and note **full expensing is companies-only**. Do NOT claim AIA or the FYAs on cars. Do NOT state a flat 18% WDA for periods after April 2026. Cross-ref §3 (reduces CT profit), §2 (reduces trading profit), §5 (balancing charges on a sale).

## 9. Employing staff: PAYE/RTI, employer cost and auto-enrolment (locked figures, DRAFT 2026-06-12)

**Position.** Taking on an employee creates three connected obligations and a **loaded cost well above the headline salary**: **PAYE/RTI payroll**, the **employer cost of the wage**, and **pension auto-enrolment**.

- **PAYE and Real Time Information (RTI).** Operate PAYE (deduct income tax and employee Class 1 NIC), report **on or before each payday** via a Full Payment Submission, and pay HMRC monthly (quarterly for small employers). A first-time employer registers as an employer with HMRC. Late RTI filing carries monthly penalties scaled by headcount: **£100 (1 to 9 employees), £200 (10 to 49), £300 (50 to 249), £400 (250+)**.
- **Employer (secondary Class 1) NIC.** **15% above the secondary threshold of £5,000 a year, from 6 April 2025** (per §4; not the old 13.8% / £9,100). The **Employment Allowance £10,500** offsets employer NIC for a business with genuine non-director staff (not a single-director company). The **LEL £6,500/year** preserves the employee's contributory entitlement.
- **Pension auto-enrolment.** Automatically enrol eligible jobholders (aged 22 to State Pension Age, earning over the **earnings trigger £10,000**, 2025/26) into a qualifying scheme. Contributions are on **qualifying earnings** (the band **£6,240 to £50,270**, 2025/26): **minimum total 8%**, of which the **employer pays at least 3%** and the employee the balance (**5%**, including tax relief). Re-enrolment every ~3 years; a declaration of compliance is due. The employer contribution is **deductible** and carries **no NIC**.
- **The real cost of a hire.** Salary **plus** employer NIC (15% above £5,000, net of any Employment Allowance) **plus** employer pension (3% minimum of qualifying earnings) **plus** payroll running cost **plus** on-costs (holiday pay, statutory sick/maternity exposure). **Employer's liability insurance is compulsory.**

- **Statutory hooks:** Income Tax (PAYE) Regulations 2003 (SI 2003/2682) + ITEPA 2003 Part 11 (PAYE/RTI); FA 2009 Sch 55 (RTI late-filing penalties); SSCBA 1992 secondary Class 1 (15% above £5,000 from 6 Apr 2025); National Insurance Contributions Act 2014 (Employment Allowance £10,500, single-director restriction); **Pensions Act 2008 Part 1** (auto-enrolment); Employers' Liability (Compulsory Insurance) Act 1969.
- **Authority anchors:** https://www.gov.uk/guidance/rates-and-thresholds-for-employers-2025-to-2026 (15% / £5,000, EA £10,500, LEL £6,500); The Pensions Regulator employer-duties + thresholds.
- **HMRC manual anchors:** PAYE/RTI PAYE5000+; NIM06000+ / NIM24175+ (secondary NIC, Employment Allowance); auto-enrolment via The Pensions Regulator.
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** always build the **loaded cost** (salary + 15% employer NIC above £5,000 + 3% employer pension + payroll + on-costs), not the salary alone. Use the **2025/26** auto-enrolment figures (trigger £10,000, band £6,240 to £50,270, 8% / 3% / 5%) and date-tag them. Use the RTI penalty bands (£100/£200/£300/£400). **Do NOT state employer NIC at 13.8% / £9,100** (now 15% / £5,000). Cross-ref §4 (employer NIC, EA), §9.A (trivial benefits).

### 9.A Trivial benefits and tax-efficient staff perks (locked, DRAFT 2026-06-12)

**Position.** Small perks are exempt from tax and NIC under the **trivial benefits exemption (ITEPA 2003 s.323A)** where **all four** conditions are met: (1) cost **£50 or less** per benefit (a cliff edge: over £50 and the **whole** amount is taxable); (2) not cash or a cash voucher (a store gift card is fine); (3) not a reward for services or performance; (4) not contractual or under salary sacrifice. For a **director or office-holder of a close company** (most owner-managed companies), an **annual cap of £300** applies (s.323B), still subject to the £50-per-benefit limit. Separately, an **annual staff event** (for example a Christmas party) is exempt up to **£150 per head per year** (s.264, all-or-nothing per head), and one **mobile phone** per employee is exempt (s.319). The genuinely large tax-efficient lever remains **employer pension contributions** (§4, §10).

- **Statutory hooks:** **ITEPA 2003 s.323A** (trivial benefits) https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2003/1/section/323A ; **s.323B** (£300 close-company director cap); **s.264** (annual function £150/head); **s.319** (mobile phone).
- **HMRC manual anchors:** EIM21864+ (trivial benefits); EIM21690+ (annual parties).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** state all four trivial-benefit conditions and stress the £50 cliff edge. Flag the **£300 annual cap** for the owner-director (close company). Keep the **£150** staff-event limit separate. Do NOT call a cash bonus or contractual perk a trivial benefit, and do NOT say trivial benefits are unlimited for a director. Cross-ref §4, §9.

## 10. Pensions: annual allowance and tapering (locked figures, DRAFT 2026-06-12)

**Position.** The pension **annual allowance is £60,000** (2025/26) for total tax-relieved pension input (employer plus personal, gross). It **tapers** where **threshold income exceeds £200,000 AND adjusted income exceeds £260,000**, reducing by **£1 for every £2** of adjusted income above £260,000, down to a **minimum tapered allowance of £10,000**. The **money purchase annual allowance is £10,000** (triggered by flexibly accessing a DC pension). **Unused allowance carries forward three tax years** (current year used first). Tax relief on personal contributions is limited to the higher of **100% of relevant UK earnings** or **£3,600 gross** (the £2,880 net non-earner floor). For an owner-manager, **employer (company) pension contributions** are the efficient route: corporation-tax-deductible on a paid basis (FA 2004 s.196), no NIC, and not limited by the individual's relevant earnings (only by the annual allowance and the wholly-and-exclusively test).

- **Statutory hooks:** **Finance Act 2004 Part 4** (annual allowance ss.227 to 228ZA; tapered annual allowance s.228ZA; MPAA; carry-forward); s.196 (employer contribution CT deduction, paid basis).
- **Authority anchors:** https://www.gov.uk/tax-on-your-private-pension/annual-allowance (£60,000; taper at threshold income £200,000 / adjusted income £260,000).
- **HMRC manual anchors:** PTM050000+ (annual allowance); PTM056000+ (taper); PTM055000+ (MPAA).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** use £60,000 / £200,000 threshold income / £260,000 adjusted income / £10,000 floor, each tagged 2025/26. For owner-managers, route large contributions through the **company** (deductible, no NIC, not earnings-capped) and check the annual allowance and carry-forward. Do NOT use the old £40,000 allowance or the pre-2023 £240,000 adjusted-income threshold. Cross-ref §4, §9.

## 11. Property income (cross-niche, kept light) (locked figures, DRAFT 2026-06-12)

## 12. Mileage and travel (locked figures, DRAFT 2026-06-12)

**Position.** **Approved Mileage Allowance Payments (AMAP)** let a business pay (or an employee/sole trader claim) a tax-free per-mile rate for business use of a personal car or van: **55p for the first 10,000 business miles in the tax year, then 25p, from 2026/27 (6 April 2026)** (the first-10,000 rate rose from **45p to 55p**; 45p applied for 2011/12 to 2025/26). Motorcycles 24p; an extra 5p per passenger. **Ordinary commuting (home to a permanent workplace) is not business travel** and is not claimable. The **24-month rule** (ITEPA 2003 s.339): a workplace is **temporary** only while the employee expects to attend it for **24 months or less and for less than 40% of working time**; once attendance is expected to exceed 24 months (or 40% of time), it becomes a **permanent workplace** and travel to it becomes non-deductible commuting. Sole traders deduct genuine business mileage (the AMAP rates, or actual costs apportioned) under the wholly-and-exclusively rule.

- **Statutory hooks:** **ITEPA 2003 ss.229 to 236** (approved mileage allowance payments / mileage allowance relief); **s.338 / s.339** (travel for necessary attendance; permanent vs temporary workplace, 24-month rule); ITTOIA 2005 s.34 (sole-trader wholly-and-exclusively).
- **Authority anchors:** https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/rates-and-allowances-travel-mileage-and-fuel-allowances/travel-mileage-and-fuel-rates-and-allowances (55p/25p from 2026/27; 45p prior).
- **HMRC manual anchors:** EIM31200+ (AMAP); EIM32000+ (travel, temporary workplace, 24-month rule).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** use **55p/25p (from 6 April 2026)** and date-tag it; use **45p** only for 2025/26 and earlier (clearly tagged). State the **home-to-permanent-workplace commuting** rule and the **24-month / 40%** temporary-workplace test. Do NOT treat ordinary commuting as deductible. ## 13. IR35 and off-payroll working (locked, DRAFT 2026-06-12)

**Position.** A contractor working through a **personal service company (PSC)** is within the **intermediaries legislation (IR35)**. For engagements with **medium or large clients** (private or public sector), the **off-payroll working rules in Chapter 10, Part 2 ITEPA 2003** apply: from **6 April 2021** the **client (or the fee-payer in the chain), not the PSC, determines status** and must issue a **Status Determination Statement (SDS)** with reasons, taking reasonable care. Inside IR35: the fee-payer deducts PAYE and NIC before paying the PSC (so tax-efficient salary/dividend extraction of that income is not available); outside IR35: the PSC is paid gross and extracts normally (§4). For **small clients** (broadly meeting two of: turnover not over £15m, balance sheet not over £7.5m, not over 50 employees, the Companies Act 2006 small-company test, uplifted in 2025), the **original Chapter 8 IR35 rules** apply and the **PSC itself determines its status**. Status rests on case-law factors (control, personal service/substitution, mutuality of obligation, financial risk, integration), assessed via HMRC's **CEST** tool and the Employment Status Manual; the contract label does not govern.

- **Statutory hooks:** **ITEPA 2003 Chapter 10, Part 2** (off-payroll, client-led, from 6 Apr 2021) and **Chapter 8, Part 2** (original IR35, small clients) https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2003/1 ; small-company size test in the Companies Act 2006.
- **HMRC manual anchors:** **ESM10000+** (off-payroll working); ESM0500+ (employment status factors); ESM11000+ (CEST).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** for medium/large clients say the **SDS sits with the client**, not the contractor, with a client-led disagreement process; for **small clients** the **PSC decides its own status** (Chapter 8). State status is substance over the contract label and walk the factors. Do NOT write that "the contractor always decides their own IR35 status" (that is only the small-client/pre-April-2021 position). Cross-ref §4 (extraction blocked when inside), §12 (travel rules differ inside/outside).
