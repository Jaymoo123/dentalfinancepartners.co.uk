## 1. Business structures for law firms (locked, 2026-06-03)

**Position.** A law firm in England and Wales can be carried on as a **sole practitioner**, a **general (traditional) partnership** (Partnership Act 1890), a **limited liability partnership / LLP** (Limited Liability Partnerships Act 2000), or a **company / Alternative Business Structure (ABS)**. The structure drives both tax and regulatory authorisation. **Tax transparency:** a sole trader is taxed personally; a general partnership and an LLP are **tax-transparent** (the entity pays no income tax/corporation tax on trading profit; each partner/member is taxed personally on their profit share, see §2); a company pays **corporation tax** and the owners extract by salary/dividend (see §3). **Liability:** a general partnership has **unlimited joint and several liability**; an LLP and a company give limited liability (subject to a member's own negligence and to any personal guarantees). **Regulatory authorisation:** every SRA-regulated firm must be authorised by the SRA and must appoint a **COLP** (Compliance Officer for Legal Practice) and a **COFA** (Compliance Officer for Finance and Administration). A firm with **non-lawyer owners or managers** must be licensed as an **ABS / licensed body** under Part 5 of the **Legal Services Act 2007**; the SRA is a designated licensing authority. A wholly lawyer-owned company or LLP is authorised as a "recognised body".

- **Statutory / authority hooks:** Partnership Act 1890; Limited Liability Partnerships Act 2000 https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2000/12 ; Companies Act 2006; **Legal Services Act 2007 (c. 29) Part 5 (ss.71 to 111)** ABS / licensed bodies https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2007/29/contents ; SRA Authorisation of Firms Rules (COLP/COFA, recognised vs licensed bodies); ITTOIA 2005 s.863 (LLP transparency).
- **Authority anchors:** SRA "Authorisation of firms"; SRA "Becoming an ABS".
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** lead with "the structure choice is a tax decision AND a regulatory decision; both must be satisfied". State that general partnerships and LLPs are tax-transparent and the company route brings corporation tax + dividend extraction. Note an LLP/company needs SRA recognised-body or (if non-lawyer ownership) ABS licensing. Do NOT imply a firm can incorporate freely without SRA authorisation. Cross-ref §2 (member tax), §3 (extraction), §9 (sale).

## 2. LLP member taxation (locked, 2026-06-03)

**Position.** An LLP is **tax-transparent**: under **ITTOIA 2005 s.863** all the LLP's activities are treated as carried on in partnership by its members, so the LLP pays no corporation tax on trading profit and each member is taxed as a **self-employed partner** on their **allocated profit share** (ITTOIA 2005 s.850 governs allocation by the profit-sharing arrangement). The charge is on the **allocated profit share, not on drawings**: drawings are cash advances against the share, so a member who draws less than their allocation still pays tax on the full allocation (the retained balance is working capital). Members pay income tax and **Class 4 NIC** on the share (see §3) through self-assessment, with **payments on account** (31 January and 31 July). Each member reports their share from the partnership return (SA800) on the partnership pages of their SA100. **Capital buy-in / loan interest relief:** interest on a personal loan taken to fund a genuine capital contribution to (or to buy into) the LLP is deductible against the member's income under **ITA 2007 ss.398 to 412** (qualifying loan to invest in a partnership), while the member remains a partner and the capital stays in.

- **Statutory / authority hooks:** **ITTOIA 2005 s.863** (LLP transparency) https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2005/5/section/863 ; **ITTOIA 2005 s.850** (allocation of partnership profits/losses); ITA 2007 ss.398 to 412 (interest relief on a loan to invest in a partnership); TMA 1970 s.59A (payments on account).
- **HMRC manual anchors:** **PM130000+ / PM163000+** (partnership profit allocation, LLPs); **BIM82000+** (computation of partnership profits); SAIM10000+ (qualifying loan interest).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** lead with "you are taxed on your profit share, not your drawings" (the single most common partner cash-flow trap). State the LLP pays no corporation tax on trading profit. Flag qualifying-loan interest relief on capital buy-in as a live deduction. Do NOT describe an LLP member as an employee by default (the exception is §2.A). Do NOT say members are taxed on drawings. Cross-ref §2.A, §3, §4.

### 2.A Salaried member rules (FA 2014) (locked, 2026-06-03)

**Position.** The **salaried member rules** (Finance Act 2014, inserting **ITTOIA 2005 ss.863A to 863G**) can re-classify an LLP member as an **employee for tax** (PAYE + employer NIC on their reward) if **all three conditions are met** (fail any one and the member stays self-employed):
- **Condition A · disguised salary (s.863B):** met if it is reasonable to expect that **at least 80%** of the member's total reward for their services is "disguised salary" (fixed, or varying without reference to the LLP's overall profits/losses).
- **Condition B · significant influence (s.863C):** met if the member does **not** have significant influence over the affairs of the LLP.
- **Condition C · capital contribution (s.863D):** met if the member's capital contribution is **less than 25%** of their disguised salary.

The rules apply year by year and to all members, but in practice catch **fixed-share and salaried partners**, not full-equity partners (whose reward genuinely varies, failing Condition A). The usual planning lever is to fail one condition: make more than 20% of reward genuinely profit-dependent (fail A), grant real influence (fail B), or commit capital of at least 25% of disguised salary, genuinely at risk (fail C). s.863G is the anti-avoidance / arrangements provision.

- **Statutory / authority hooks:** **ITTOIA 2005 ss.863A to 863G** (FA 2014) · s.863A framework https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2005/5/section/863A ; s.863B (Condition A, 80%) https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2005/5/section/863B ; s.863C (Condition B); s.863D (Condition C, 25%) https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2005/5/section/863D ; s.863G (anti-avoidance).
- **HMRC manual anchors:** **PM250000+** (salaried members), PM256000+ (Conditions A/B/C), PM259000+ (anti-avoidance / TAAR).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** use the section range **ss.863A to 863G** and the exact thresholds: Condition A **80%** disguised salary, Condition C capital **less than 25%** of disguised salary, Condition B no significant influence. State "all three must be met; fail one and the member stays self-employed". Frame it as primarily a fixed-share/salaried-partner issue. Do NOT say the partner title decides status (substance governs). Cross-ref §2, §9 (a caught member may not have a BADR-qualifying interest).

### 2.B Mixed-membership partnership rules (locked, 2026-06-03, HP-lock gate)

**Position.** Where a law firm LLP or partnership admits a **non-individual member** (typically a **company**, the "corporate member") so that profit is allocated to it and retained at the **corporation-tax rate** (19% to 25%, §3) instead of being taxed on the individual partners at up to ~47% (income tax + Class 4 NIC), the **mixed-membership partnership rules** can **reallocate** the excess back to the individual members and tax it on them. The rules (ITTOIA 2005 **ss.850C to 850E**, inserted by Finance Act 2014 Schedule 17, effective **6 April 2014**) bite where a non-individual partner B has a profit share and either condition is met. **Condition X (deferred profit):** amounts representing an individual partner A's **deferred** remuneration, benefits or returns are included in B's profit share, so A's share and tax are lower than they would otherwise be. **Condition Y (power to enjoy):** B's profit share **exceeds the appropriate notional profit** (a genuine commercial return, being a notional return on B's contributed capital plus a notional arm's-length consideration for B's services) AND A has the **power to enjoy** B's profit share (connection under ITA 2007 s.993, or arrangements enabling A to benefit). Where either is met, **s.850C(4)** increases A's taxable profit share by so much of B's share as it is **reasonable to suppose** is attributable to A's deferred profit or power to enjoy, on a **just and reasonable** basis. **s.850D** extends the charge to an **individual who is not a partner** but whose services generate the diverted profit; **s.850E** is relieving (a later payment by B out of the reallocated excess is not taxed again on A, absent tax-avoidance arrangements). A **genuine** corporate member earning only its appropriate notional profit (a real return on capital it actually contributed plus an arm's-length charge for services it actually provides) is **not** caught: the rules target profit diversion, not commercial structuring.

- **Statutory / authority hooks:** **ITTOIA 2005 ss.850C, 850D, 850E** (mixed-membership partnerships, FA 2014 Sch 17, from 6 Apr 2014) · s.850C "Excess profit allocation to non-individual partners" (Condition X deferred profit, s.850C(8); Condition Y appropriate notional profit, s.850C(10) to (17), plus power to enjoy, s.850C(18) to (21); reallocation s.850C(4)) https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2005/5/section/850C ; base allocation rule ITTOIA 2005 s.850 / s.849; "connected" via ITA 2007 s.993.
- **HMRC manual anchors:** **PM210000+** (mixed-membership partnerships), **PM214000+** (excess profit allocation: the conditions and the reallocation mechanism).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** explain the corporate-member structure honestly. A company member CAN retain genuinely-its-own profit at the CT rate, but s.850C reallocates any **excess** above the appropriate notional profit back to the individual partners who have the power to enjoy it or who deferred profit into it. State that the structure only works for the company's **genuine commercial return** (return on real capital plus an arm's-length charge for real services), and that the mixed-membership rules (plus the general anti-abuse rule) make aggressive profit-parking ineffective. Do NOT present a corporate member as a clean way to halve a partner's tax. Conductor verified the s.850C operative wording at legislation.gov.uk on 2026-06-03. Cross-ref §2, §2.A, §3.

## 3. Partner / member income tax, NIC and profit extraction (locked, 2026-06-03)

**Position.** A self-employed partner/member pays **income tax** on their profit share at the 2025/26 rates (personal allowance £12,570, tapered above £100,000 and lost at £125,140; basic 20% to £50,270; higher 40% to £125,140; additional 45% above) plus **Class 4 NIC at 6%** on profits £12,570 to £50,270 and **2%** above (2025/26). **Class 2 NIC liability was removed from 6 April 2024**: a partner with profits at/above the Small Profits Threshold is treated as having paid and keeps state-pension entitlement (those below can pay voluntary Class 2). For an **incorporated** firm (company/ABS), the company pays **corporation tax** (19% small-profits on profits up to £50,000, 25% main above £250,000, marginal relief between, FY2025 and FY2026) and the owner extracts by **salary + dividend**. **Dividend tax:** 2025/26 ordinary **8.75%** / upper **33.75%** / additional **39.35%**, allowance **£500**; from **6 April 2026** (Finance Act 2026 c.11 s.4) ordinary **10.75%** / upper **35.75%**, additional **39.35%** unchanged, allowance £500. **Employer (secondary Class 1) NIC** is **15%** on salary above the **£5,000 secondary threshold** from 6 April 2025; the **Employment Allowance is £10,500** but is not available to a company whose only employee is a single director. **Director's loan / s.455** (close company): the charge tracks the dividend upper rate, **33.75%** on loans made before 6 Apr 2026 and **35.75%** on loans on/after, repayable under s.458. Tag every dividend and s.455 figure with its date.

- **Statutory / authority hooks:** SSCBA 1992 s.15 (Class 4 NIC); s.11 as amended by the National Insurance Contributions Act 2024 (Class 2 reform); ITA 2007 s.8 (dividend rates, amended by **FA 2026 s.4**); CTA 2010 Part 3 ss.18 to 19 (small-profits rate, marginal relief); CTA 2010 s.455/s.458 (loans to participators); secondary Class 1 NIC (employer 15% above £5,000); ITTOIA 2005 s.850 (allocation).
- **HMRC manual anchors:** dividends CTM/BIM; **NIM24000+** (Class 4); **CTM61500+** (s.455 loans to participators); spouse-wages deductibility **BIM37700+**.
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** never state a dividend or s.455 rate without its tax-year tag (from 6 Apr 2026 use 10.75% / 35.75%). Use Class 4 6%/2% and state Class 2 is no longer payable from 6 Apr 2024. For the partnership-vs-incorporation comparison, pair the corporation-tax/dividend modelling with the loss of tax transparency and the partner's own pension/NIC position; do NOT present incorporation as a flat tax win at typical partner profits (the 2026/27 dividend rise narrows it). Cross-ref §2, §2.A, §10 (pensions).

### 3.A Apprenticeship Levy and the tax-bill funding line (locked, 2026-06-03, HP-lock gate)

**Position.** A law firm with an **annual pay bill over £3,000,000** pays the **Apprenticeship Levy at 0.5%** of its pay bill (pay bill = earnings subject to employer Class 1 secondary NIC: salaries, bonuses, commissions), reduced by a **£15,000 annual allowance** (so the levy effectively bites only on pay bill above £3,000,000; connected employers share one £15,000 allowance). Levy funds sit in the firm's apprenticeship service account, can fund apprenticeship training including the **Level 7 solicitor apprenticeship** (which incorporates the SQE), can be **transferred to other employers up to 50% of the previous year's levy funds** (raised from 25% on 22 April 2024), and expire after 24 months. Non-levy-paying firms use government co-investment. The levy is a deductible business cost. Government funding for Level 7 apprenticeships was restricted from January 2026 (verify current eligibility before relying). **Tax-bill funding note:** interest on a loan taken to pay a partner's **personal income tax or Class 4 NIC is NOT an allowable deduction** (personal tax is an application of profit, not a trade purpose, HMRC BIM45690); this is distinct from the §2 qualifying-loan interest relief on partnership **capital** (ITA 2007 ss.398-412), which IS relievable, and from a genuine business working-capital facility, the interest on which is deductible.

- **Statutory / authority hooks:** Finance Act 2016 Part 6 (Apprenticeship Levy); gov.uk "Pay Apprenticeship Levy" + "Transferring apprenticeship funds" (0.5% / £15,000 / £3,000,000 conductor-verified at gov.uk 2026-06-03; 50% transfer per the transfer guidance); HMRC **BIM45690** (interest: business vs private purpose); ITA 2007 ss.398-412 (§2).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** levy is **0.5%** of pay bill over **£3,000,000**, **£15,000** allowance; transfer up to **50%** (from 22 Apr 2024); the Level 7 solicitor apprenticeship is fundable (verify the Jan-2026 funding eligibility). Interest on a loan to pay a partner's **personal tax is not deductible** (contrast capital buy-in interest, §2). Cross-ref §3, §10.

## 4. Basis period reform and WIP / lock-up (locked, 2026-06-03)

**Position.** **Basis period reform (Finance Act 2022)** moved unincorporated businesses (sole traders, partnerships, LLPs) from the old current-year basis on the accounting-period ending in the tax year to a **tax-year basis** (profits taxed for the actual tax year 6 April to 5 April) from **2024/25**, with **2023/24 the transition year**. Firms with a non-31-March/5-April year end faced a transition: the "transition profits" (the catch-up from the old basis date to 5 April 2024, net of unused **overlap relief**) are **spread over five tax years (2023/24 to 2027/28)** unless the firm elects to accelerate. Many established partners have little or no overlap relief, so the transition can bring a one-off extra tax charge; this is a planning and cash-flow point, not a permanent change. **WIP / lock-up:** law firms recognise **work in progress** as revenue under **FRS 102** (revenue recognised as the firm obtains the right to consideration for performance, on a reliable-measurement basis), so unbilled time sits on the balance sheet as an asset and is taxed as it is recognised, not when billed. The old UITF 40 position (mandatory recognition of service WIP at selling price) is now subsumed into FRS 102 revenue recognition. **Cash basis:** from **6 April 2024** the cash basis became the **default** for unincorporated businesses (the £150,000 entry / £300,000 exit turnover thresholds were abolished), but **LLPs and partnerships with a corporate partner are excluded**, so most law firms continue on the **accruals basis** (electing under ITTOIA 2005 s.25C where needed). Reducing lock-up (WIP days + debtor days) is the core working-capital lever for a law firm.

- **Statutory / authority hooks:** **Finance Act 2022 s.7 and Schedule 1** (basis period reform, transition profits, spreading); ITTOIA 2005 Part 2 (trade profits, overlap relief); ITTOIA 2005 **s.25 / s.25A to s.25C** (cash basis default and election to accruals); FRS 102 Section 23 (revenue).
- **HMRC manual anchors:** **BIM81200+ / BIM81300+** (basis periods, transitional rules); **BIM70000+** (cash basis); revenue recognition guidance.
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** state the tax-year basis from **2024/25** (transition year 2023/24) and the 5-year spreading of transition profits. State WIP is recognised under **FRS 102** and that **LLPs are excluded from the cash basis** (so accruals applies). Do NOT say basis period reform starts in 2026/27 (a common error: it is 2024/25). Do NOT tell an LLP it can use the cash basis. Cross-ref §3, §9 (WIP on sale).

> RESOLVED (Cluster C HP-lock gate, 2026-06-03, conductor verified at legislation.gov.uk): **Finance Act 2022 Schedule 1** re-fetched. Transition year is **2023-24** (para 65). Transition profits (the transition part to 5 April 2024, less overlap relief, para 70 Step 3) are spread at **20% of the transition profits in each of the four tax years beginning 2023-24** (2023/24, 2024/25, 2025/26, 2026/27), with the **balance treated as arising in the fifth tax year (2027/28)** (para 72(3) to (4) verbatim, conductor-confirmed at source). A trader may **elect to accelerate** an additional specified amount into an earlier year (para 73). On permanent cessation before the spread completes, the remaining balance is brought into charge in the cessation year (para 72(6)). Transition profits are taxed as a **standalone charge** (relieved in net income then a standalone tax added back, para 75 / HMRC BIM81290), so they do not of themselves enter net income for the £100,000 personal-allowance taper, HICBC or student loans, BUT the taper still applies where **total** income (standard plus transition) exceeds £100,000. Transition profits **are within Class 4 NIC**. Sessions may now state the 20%/5-year spread and the acceleration election as locked; still compute each partner's overlap relief from their own records.

### 4.A Contingent-fee revenue recognition (CFA / DBA) and the caps (locked, 2026-06-03, HP-lock gate)

**Position.** On **conditional-fee (CFA)** and **damages-based (DBA)** matters, revenue and WIP are recognised under **FRS 102 Section 23** only when the amount can be **reliably measured** AND it is **probable** the economic benefit will flow: on a no-win-no-fee matter that defers recognition until success/recovery is probable, so contingent WIP is carried conservatively and the profit (and the output-VAT tax point, §6.D) crystallise at the win, not as time is spent. **CFA caps:** the success fee is capped at **100% of base costs** (Conditional Fee Agreements Order 2013, SI 2013/689, art 3); in personal-injury proceedings at first instance the success fee taken from damages is capped at **25%** of specified damages (general damages for PSLA and past pecuniary loss, net of CRU sums) (art 5); statutory frame in CLSA 1990 s.58. **DBA caps** (Damages-Based Agreements Regulations 2013, SI 2013/609): a payment must not exceed, **including VAT**, **25%** in a personal-injury claim at first instance (reg 4), **50%** in other claims (reg 4), and **35%** in an employment matter (reg 7). The DBA cap is VAT-**inclusive** (VAT is carved out of the cap, not added on top). Post-**LASPO (1 April 2013)** a CFA success fee and ATE premium are generally **not recoverable** from the losing party.

- **Statutory / authority hooks:** FRS 102 Section 23 (revenue); **CLSA 1990 s.58** (CFAs); **Conditional Fee Agreements Order 2013 (SI 2013/689) arts 3, 5** (100% base-cost cap; 25% PI damages cap); **Damages-Based Agreements Regulations 2013 (SI 2013/609) regs 4, 7** (25% PI / 50% other / 35% employment, each including VAT); LASPO 2012.
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** contingent revenue is recognised at the **win** (probable + measurable), not as time is billed; the **DBA caps INCLUDE VAT** (do NOT add VAT on top of the cap); do NOT say a success fee or DBA is recoverable from the loser post-LASPO. Cross-ref §4, §6.D, §6.

## 5. SRA Accounts Rules 2019 and the client account (regulatory context, locked, 2026-06-03)

**Position (regulatory, not tax).** The **SRA Accounts Rules 2019** (in force **25 November 2019**) govern how an SRA-regulated firm in England and Wales handles **client money**. **Client money (Rule 2)** is money held or received relating to regulated services, on behalf of a third party, as trustee or office-holder, or in respect of fees and unpaid disbursements held before delivery of a bill. **Client account (Rule 3):** client money must be held in a separate client account at a bank or building society branch in England and Wales, named to include the firm name and the word "client", and kept separate from the firm's own money. **Banking-facility prohibition (Rule 3.3):** a firm must NOT use a client account to provide banking facilities to clients or third parties; every payment in, transfer or withdrawal must relate to the **delivery of regulated services**. **Interest (Rule 7):** the firm must account to the client/third party for a **fair sum of interest** on client money held (a fairness test by amount and duration; a contrary written agreement is permitted). **Reconciliation (Rule 8.3):** the client account must be reconciled (ledger totals to cash book to bank statement) **at least every five weeks**, signed off by the COFA or a manager. **Accountant's report (Rule 12):** a firm that has held client money in the period must obtain an **accountant's report** within six months of the period end; the firm is **exempt (Rule 12.2)** if all the client money held was held on an account whose statement balance did **not exceed an average of £10,000 and a maximum of £250,000** in the period. The **COFA** carries primary responsibility for compliance; records are kept for at least six years.

- **Statutory / authority hooks:** **SRA Accounts Rules 2019** (made under the Legal Services Act 2007 and the Solicitors Act 1974) · Rule 2 (client money), Rule 3 / 3.3 (client account, banking-facility prohibition), Rule 7 (interest), Rule 8.3 (five-weekly reconciliation), Rule 12 (accountant's report + the average £10,000 / maximum £250,000 exemption) https://www.sra.org.uk/solicitors/standards-regulations/accounts-rules/ ; SRA Authorisation of Firms Rules (COFA).
- **Authority anchors:** SRA "Accounts Rules" and SRA "Warning notices" on client money; SRA "Accountant's report".
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** frame this as **regulatory/compliance context, NOT tax**. Use the exemption test exactly: **average not exceeding £10,000 AND maximum not exceeding £250,000** (do NOT write "average £250" or any £250 figure: that is wrong). State the five-weekly reconciliation and the Rule 3.3 banking-facility prohibition. Keep client-money tax-neutral (client money is not the firm's income). Do NOT give regulatory-defence or SRA-enforcement advice. Cross-ref §6 (VAT on the firm's own fees, which IS taxable), §9 (transferring client balances on a sale needs client consent).

### 5.A Estate, PR, attorney and deputy money as client money (locked, 2026-06-03, HP-lock gate)

**Position.** Money a firm holds when instructed by **personal representatives** to administer an estate (PR funds, property-sale proceeds, money awaiting distribution) is **client money** under Rule 2.1 (money relating to regulated services / held on behalf of a third party). Money held by a firm acting **AS executor, trustee, attorney (donee of an LPA or EPA), or Court of Protection deputy** is client money under **Rule 2.1(c)** ("as a trustee or as the holder of a specified office or appointment, such as donee of a power of attorney, Court of Protection deputy"). Full client-account discipline applies (Rule 3.3 banking-facility prohibition, Rule 7 interest, Rule 8.3 five-weekly reconciliation, Rule 12 report); keep **one ledger per estate / trust / client**. Attorney and deputy money sits under a **second regime**: OPG (Office of the Public Guardian) supervision (general supervision in year one; a **security bond must be in place before a deputy can act**; an annual deputy report to the OPG).

- **Statutory / authority hooks:** **SRA Accounts Rules 2019 Rule 2.1(c)** (trustee / office-holder limb naming attorney + deputy); Mental Capacity Act 2005 + OPG supervision (deputies/attorneys); Trustee Act 1925 / 2000 (PRs and trustees). Records 6 years.
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** estate/PR money = client money; firm-as-fiduciary (executor/trustee/attorney/deputy) money = Rule 2.1(c) client money; full client-account discipline applies; note the OPG layer for attorney/deputy. Cross-ref §5, §5.B, §5.C, §6.

### 5.B Residual and unclaimed client balances (locked, 2026-06-03, HP-lock gate)

**Position.** A residual or unclaimed client-account balance of **£500 or less on any one client matter** may be withdrawn and paid to a **charity of the firm's choice WITHOUT prior SRA authorisation**, provided ALL conditions are met: reasonable steps taken to return the money to the rightful owner (judged by the balance's age, amount, available contact details and the cost of tracing); records of those steps retained **at least six years**; a **central register** (rightful-owner name, amount, recipient charity and its **charity number**, date of payment); the **charity receipts and confirmation of any indemnity** kept; and **no deduction** of tracing or communication costs from the balance. For amounts **OVER £500** the firm must obtain the **SRA's prior written authorisation** before withdrawing. This is a disposal of capital under **Rule 5.1(c)** (withdrawal in prescribed circumstances), distinct from the Rule 7 fair-interest duty.

- **Statutory / authority hooks:** **SRA Accounts Rules 2019 Rule 5.1(c)** (withdrawal on the SRA's authorisation or in prescribed circumstances); SRA guidance "Withdraw client money" / "Residual client account balances" (the £500 route + over-£500 authorisation). Conductor-verified verbatim at sra.org.uk 2026-06-03.
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** use **£500 per matter** (charity, no SRA authority, all conditions met) vs **over £500** (SRA prior written authority); state the conditions; do NOT deduct tracing costs from the balance; this is capital disposal, not interest (§5 Rule 7). Cross-ref §5, §5.F.

### 5.C Professional executor and trustee remuneration (locked, 2026-06-03, HP-lock gate)

**Position.** A firm named as **executor** or appointed as **trustee** may take fees only with authority: either a **professional charging clause** in the will or trust instrument (validated by **Trustee Act 2000 s.28**, "acting in a professional capacity" defined in s.28(5)), or, where the instrument is silent, the **statutory default in s.29** (a trust corporation, or a trustee acting in a professional capacity that is **not a sole trustee** and where **each other trustee has agreed in writing**, may receive reasonable remuneration; **no s.29 entitlement** where the instrument or an enactment already provides for remuneration, s.29(5)). Trustee Act 2000 applies to **PRs as to trustees**. Those fees are the firm's **trading income** (income tax + Class 4 NIC via the partners, or corporation tax for a company) and a **standard-rated VAT supply (20%)**; they are the firm's income, distinct from the estate/trust client money it controls (§5.A).

- **Statutory / authority hooks:** **Trustee Act 2000 s.28 / s.29** (professional / trust-corporation remuneration) https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2000/29/section/29 ; ITTOIA 2005 / CTA 2009 (trading income); VATA 1994 (standard-rated). Conductor-verified at legislation.gov.uk 2026-06-03 via the brief.
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** a firm-fiduciary needs a **charging clause (s.28)** or the **s.29 default** (not available to a sole trustee under s.29(2); needs each other trustee's written agreement); the fees are **taxable trading income + standard-rated VAT**, not client money. Cross-ref §5.A, §6, §9.

### 5.D Third-party managed accounts (TPMA) (locked, 2026-06-03, HP-lock gate)

**Position.** A firm may use a **third-party managed account (TPMA)** instead of operating its own client account. **SRA Accounts Rules Rule 11** permits a TPMA only if: (**Rule 11.1**) using the account does **NOT result in the firm receiving or holding client money**, and the firm takes reasonable steps before accepting instructions to ensure the client understands the contractual terms, how the TPMA fees are paid and who bears them, and the client's right to terminate the agreement and dispute payment requests; and (**Rule 11.2**) the firm obtains **regular statements** from the provider and checks they accurately reflect all transactions. Because TPMA money is **not "held or received"** by the firm it is **not client money** under Rule 2 (which can take the firm out of the Rule 12 report trigger, §5.G). The condition that the provider be an **FCA-authorised payment institution** operating an **escrow** service (money beneficially owned by the third party) sits in the **SRA Glossary definition** of "third party managed account", NOT in Rule 11 itself.

- **Statutory / authority hooks:** **SRA Accounts Rules 2019 Rule 11.1 / 11.2**; **SRA Glossary "third party managed account"** (FCA-authorised payment institution / escrow); SRA TPMA guidance. Conductor-verified at sra.org.uk 2026-06-03.
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** TPMA = **Rule 11** (NOT Rule 4.2); the money is **not client money**; the provider must be **FCA-authorised** (per the Glossary). Cross-ref §5, §5.G.

### 5.E Mixed-money receipts (locked, 2026-06-03, HP-lock gate)

**Position.** Where a single payment is a **mixed payment** (part client money, part the firm's own money, e.g. settlement of a bill plus money on account plus unpaid disbursements), **SRA Accounts Rules Rule 4.2** requires the firm to **allocate the funds PROMPTLY** to the correct client account or business account. The standard is **"promptly"**: there is **no fixed day-count** (do not import the 14-day VAT tax-point rule, which is a different concept, §6.D). Before transferring the firm's own costs out of a client account, **Rule 4.3** requires a **bill of costs (or other written notification of the costs incurred)** to be given first, and any such payment must be for the **specific sum** identified in the bill.

- **Statutory / authority hooks:** **SRA Accounts Rules 2019 Rule 4.2** (mixed payments, "promptly"), **Rule 4.3** (bill before transferring costs out). Conductor-verified at sra.org.uk 2026-06-03.
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** allocate mixed payments **"promptly"** (do NOT state a 14-day or any fixed deadline); a **bill must precede** moving the firm's costs out of client account (Rule 4.3). Cross-ref §5, §5.F, §6.D.

### 5.F Suspense, unidentified and dormant client balances (locked, 2026-06-03, HP-lock gate)

**Position.** Suspense ledgers should be used **minimally and briefly**; **unidentified receipts** must be investigated and allocated to the correct client ledger as soon as possible; **dormant matter balances** should be returned to the rightful owner. These surface at the **Rule 8.3 five-weekly reconciliation** and must be **cleaned up before the Rule 12 accountant's report** (a long-standing suspense balance is a classic report-qualification trigger). A genuinely unreturnable balance follows the **§5.B residual-balance route**.

- **Statutory / authority hooks:** **SRA Accounts Rules 2019 Rule 8.3** (reconciliation), **Rule 12** (report); SRA guidance on residual/suspense balances.
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** keep suspense minimal and short-lived; investigate unidentified receipts; clear before the report; route a genuinely unreturnable balance via **§5.B**. Cross-ref §5.B, §5.G.

### 5.G Running a firm without a client account: report trigger vs exemption (locked, 2026-06-03, HP-lock gate)

**Position.** The **Rule 12.1 accountant's-report TRIGGER** is "if you have, at any time during an accounting period, **held or received** client money": a firm that holds **NO client money** (paying third parties directly, and/or using a TPMA, §5.D) does not meet the trigger and needs **no report**. This is **distinct from the Rule 12.2 EXEMPTION**, which is for a firm that **did** hold client money but only small balances (**average not exceeding £10,000 AND a maximum not exceeding £250,000**). A no-client-account model removes client-money risk and the report cost but shifts disbursement funding and timing onto the firm (VAT / disbursement implications, §6).

- **Statutory / authority hooks:** **SRA Accounts Rules 2019 Rule 12.1** (trigger), **Rule 12.2** (exemption, average £10,000 / maximum £250,000); **Rule 11** (TPMA). Conductor-verified at sra.org.uk 2026-06-03.
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** distinguish the **Rule 12.1 trigger** (hold no client money → no report) from the **Rule 12.2 exemption** (held small balances); do NOT conflate them; do NOT use any "£250" figure for the exemption (it is average £10,000 / maximum £250,000). Cross-ref §5.D, §5.

## 6. VAT on legal services and disbursements (locked, 2026-06-03)

**Position.** Legal services supplied by a solicitor (advice, conveyancing, litigation, drafting) are **standard-rated for VAT at 20%**: there is no exemption for residential conveyancing. A firm must register once **taxable turnover exceeds £90,000** (registration threshold from 1 April 2024) and may deregister below the **£88,000** deregistration threshold. The signature issue is the **disbursement-vs-supply** test. A payment is a **disbursement (outside the scope of VAT, recharged with no VAT)** only if **all eight conditions** in VAT Notice 700 §25.1.1 are met: (1) the firm acted as the client's **agent** when paying the third party; (2) the **client received and used** the goods/services; (3) the **client was responsible** for paying the third party; (4) the client **authorised** the payment; (5) the client **knew** a third party would supply; (6) the outlay is **separately itemised**; (7) the firm recovers **only the exact amount paid**; (8) the goods/services are **clearly additional** to the firm's own supply. If any condition fails, the payment is part of the firm's **standard-rated** supply and VAT is added on the recharge. Genuine disbursements in practice: **court and tribunal fees, Land Registry registration fees, SDLT/LBTT/LTT paid for the client** (taxes/statutory fees the client is liable for, where the firm is a pure conduit). NOT disbursements (standard-rated recharges): **bank/CHAPS transfer fees** the firm's bank charges the firm, **photocopying, postage, courier**, and (post-Brabners, see §6.A) **search fees the firm uses in its own advice**.

- **Statutory / authority hooks:** **VATA 1994** (standard rate, supply of services); registration/deregistration in **VATA 1994 Schedule 1** (£90,000 / £88,000); the disbursement conditions in **VAT Notice 700 §25.1.1** / HMRC manual **VTAXPER39000** https://www.gov.uk/hmrc-internal-manuals/vat-taxable-person/vtaxper39000 .
- **HMRC manual anchors:** **VTAXPER37000 to VTAXPER40000** (agency and disbursements, the eight conditions); VAT Notice 700 §25.
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** lead with "legal services are standard-rated at 20%; a disbursement only escapes VAT if ALL EIGHT conditions are met". Use **£90,000 registration / £88,000 deregistration**. Court fees, Land Registry registration fees and SDLT/LBTT/LTT are the clean disbursements; CHAPS/bank fees and the firm's own overheads are standard-rated recharges. Do NOT label every third-party cost a disbursement. Do NOT use the old £85,000 registration threshold. Cross-ref §6.A (search fees / Brabners), §6.B (counsel), §7 (SDLT the firm advises on).

### 6.A Conveyancing VAT and property search fees (Brabners) (locked, 2026-06-03)

**Position.** Whether a **property search fee** is a disbursement turns on **how the firm uses the result**, not on whether it was obtained by post or online. In **Brabners LLP v HMRC [2017] UKFTT 0666 (TC)** the Tribunal held that **electronic property search fees**, where the firm used the search results "as part and parcel of its overall service" (interpreting them, reporting on them, advising), were **NOT disbursements**: the searches were a cost component of the firm's standard-rated supply, so VAT was due (the firm faced a £67,776 assessment). HMRC's settled position in **Revenue and Customs Brief 6 (2020)** withdrew the informal 1991 **postal-search concession** from **1 December 2020** and aligned postal and electronic searches on the same functional test: if the firm merely passes the search result to the client unused/uninterpreted (pure conduit, meeting the eight conditions) it can be a disbursement; if the firm uses or interprets it in its advice it is part of the firm's taxable supply and VAT is charged. **CHAPS/telegraphic-transfer fees** the firm's bank charges the firm are standard-rated on recharge (the bank's supply is to the firm, not the client).

- **Statutory / authority hooks:** ***Brabners LLP v HMRC* [2017] UKFTT 0666 (TC), TC06093** https://financeandtax.decisions.tribunals.gov.uk/judgmentfiles/j10061/TC06093.pdf ; **Revenue and Customs Brief 6 (2020)** https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/revenue-and-customs-brief-6-2020-vat-treatment-of-property-search-fees-charged-by-solicitors-and-conveyancers ; VAT Notice 700 §25.1.1 (the eight conditions, §6).
- **Authority anchors:** Law Society "VAT treatment of disbursements and expenses"; CLC guidance on VAT on disbursements.
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** state the Brabners principle every time search fees come up: "a search fee is a disbursement only if you pass it through unused; if you interpret it in your advice it is part of your taxable supply, VAT applies". Note the postal concession ended 1 Dec 2020 (R&C Brief 6 (2020)), so post-vs-electronic no longer decides it. Treat CHAPS fees as standard-rated recharges. Do NOT state that all Land Registry/search fees are automatically VAT-free disbursements (that is the pre-Brabners error). Cross-ref §6, §7.

### 6.B Counsel's fees (locked, 2026-06-03)

**Position.** Barrister's (counsel's) fees are, in the normal case, a **supply to the firm** that the firm uses in making its own onward supply of legal services to the client: the firm **recovers the input VAT** on counsel's fee (if registered and making taxable supplies) and **charges output VAT** on its total fee (the counsel element included). Counsel's fees only qualify as a **disbursement** in the rarer case where counsel acts as the **client's** agent, in the client's name and on the client's instruction (e.g. some direct-access arrangements). There is a long-standing **HMRC concession** allowing a solicitor to treat counsel's fee as a disbursement and pass counsel's VAT-bearing fee note to the client, but only where the firm does not reclaim the input VAT itself; in modern practice most firms treat counsel as a supply to the firm and recover/charge the VAT. Overseas counsel can engage the **reverse charge**.

- **Statutory / authority hooks:** VATA 1994 (supply of services, input-tax recovery); VAT Notice 700 §25 (disbursements concession); the agency/principal test (§6 eight conditions).
- **HMRC manual anchors:** VTAXPER (agency/disbursements); VATSC (single/multiple supplies).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** default is "counsel's fee is a supply to the firm: recover the input VAT, charge output VAT on your fee". Flag the concession/disbursement route as the exception (client-instructed counsel) and warn that you cannot both treat it as a disbursement AND reclaim the input VAT. Cross-ref §6, §6.A.

### 6.C Partial exemption, the de minimis test, and interest as exempt income (locked, 2026-06-03, HP-lock gate)

**Position.** A law firm makes mostly taxable supplies (standard-rated legal services), but it can also have **exempt income**, most commonly **interest**: interest on the firm's own deposits and **interest the firm retains on client-account / designated-deposit balances** is an **exempt financial supply** (VATA 1994 **Schedule 9 Group 5**: Item 1 dealing with money, Item 2 making advances or granting credit, Item 8 operating a deposit/savings account). Exempt income can make a firm **partially exempt**, restricting recovery of input VAT on overheads attributable to the exempt activity. BUT input VAT linked to exempt supplies is **fully recoverable if it is within the de minimis limits**: exempt input tax is **no more than £625 per month on average (£1,875 per quarter, £7,500 per year) AND no more than 50% of total input tax** (a firm passes if it meets the original test or HMRC's simplified Test One or Test Two). Residual/overhead input VAT is apportioned by the **standard method** (value of taxable supplies divided by value of total supplies, **rounded up to the next whole percent** unless residual input tax exceeds £400,000 a month), with a mandatory **annual adjustment**. **Crucial carve-out:** HMRC treats **incidental** financial transactions, expressly including **interest received on a bank account that arises merely as a consequence of normal business activity**, as **excluded from the standard-method calculation** because their inclusion would distort it (VAT Notice 706). So for a typical firm, modest deposit/client-account interest is **incidental and does not restrict recovery**; partial exemption bites only where exempt income is **non-incidental** (actively generated, material in scale) or where exempt input tax breaches the de minimis limits. "Incidental" is not statutorily defined (facts and degree).

- **Statutory / authority hooks:** **VATA 1994 Schedule 9 Group 5** (finance/interest exempt) https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1994/23/schedule/9 ; partial exemption mechanics in the **VAT Regulations 1995 (SI 1995/2518) regs 99 to 111** (attribution, standard method, de minimis, annual adjustment); VAT Notice 706.
- **HMRC manual anchors:** **PE21500+** (de minimis), **PE30000+** (standard method), **PE60000+** (annual adjustment); Notice 706 §§4, 6, 9, 11 (incidental supplies).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** state both de minimis limbs together (**£625 per month average AND no more than 50% of total input tax**) and the standard-method round-up. Lead with the honest position: interest is usually **incidental and excluded**, so most firms are unaffected; do NOT overstate the restriction. Flag the live risk only where interest income is non-incidental or large, or de minimis is breached. Cross-ref §5 (the SRA Rule 7 fair-interest duty is a different point), §6, §6.E.

### 6.D Time of supply (tax point) for legal fees (locked, 2026-06-03, HP-lock gate)

**Position.** Under standard VAT accounting the firm accounts for output VAT at the **tax point**, NOT simply when it is paid. **VATA 1994 s.6** sets the rules for services: the **basic tax point** is when the services are **performed/completed** (s.6(3)); but an **actual tax point** arises earlier to the extent the firm **issues a VAT invoice or receives payment before** the basic tax point (s.6(4)); and the **14-day rule** (s.6(5)) makes the **invoice date** the tax point where a VAT invoice is issued within 14 days after the basic tax point (unless the firm elects out). Solicitor-specific practice: a **"request for payment" or "request for payment on account" is NOT a VAT invoice** and creates **no tax point** (no output VAT until the firm issues a VAT invoice or a final/interim **statute bill**, or receives payment, whichever is first); an **interim statute bill** (a final bill for the work it covers under the Solicitors Act 1974) **does** create a tax point. For continuous supply the tax point is the **earlier of invoice or payment**. The standard VAT rate has been **20%** since 4 January 2011. Only the **cash accounting scheme** (turnover under the scheme threshold) makes the tax point follow receipt of payment.

- **Statutory / authority hooks:** **VATA 1994 s.6** (time of supply) https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1994/23/section/6 ; continuous-supply rule VAT Regulations 1995 reg 90; VAT Notice 700 §§14 to 15; Solicitors Act 1974 (statute bills).
- **HMRC manual anchors:** **VATTOS** (time of supply); Notice 700 §14 (tax points), §15 (VAT invoices).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** state the tax point is **s.6** (basic tax point overridden by an earlier invoice or payment, plus the 14-day rule), NOT "when the client pays" (that is only cash accounting). Use the solicitor distinction: **request for payment = no tax point; statute bill / VAT invoice / payment = tax point**. Useful for managing VAT cash flow across interim billing. Cross-ref §6, §6.C.

### 6.E Option to tax and the Capital Goods Scheme on premises (locked, 2026-06-03, HP-lock gate)

**Position.** Supplies of an interest in **land or buildings are normally VAT-exempt**, which blocks recovery of input VAT on buying or refurbishing premises. An **option to tax (OTT)** under **VATA 1994 Schedule 10** makes the opter's supplies of that property **standard-rated**, so input VAT on the purchase/refurbishment becomes recoverable (relevant to a property-owning entity such as a landlord, a connected property company, or a SIPP/SSAS that holds the premises; a **tenant firm does not opt to tax its landlord's building**, the landlord does). The OTT must be **notified to HMRC within 30 days** (form VAT1614A), can be revoked in a **6-month cooling-off** period (VAT1614C), otherwise **lasts 20 years** (revocable after 20 years, VAT1614J; auto-revoked after a continuous 6-year period holding no interest). Where a partially exempt entity (§6.C) spends **£250,000 or more (VAT-exclusive) on land or a building** (or £50,000+ on a single computer item), the **Capital Goods Scheme (CGS)** spreads input-VAT recovery over a **10-interval adjustment period for land/buildings** (**5 intervals for computers**): each interval recovery is recalculated against that year's taxable-use percentage (which equals the partial-exemption recovery rate), clawing back or refunding VAT as the taxable/exempt mix changes; a final adjustment crystallises on sale or deregistration. So §6.C exempt-income shifts can claw back building VAT over the CGS intervals.

- **Statutory / authority hooks:** **VATA 1994 Schedule 10** (option to tax; paras 23 to 29) https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1994/23/schedule/10 ; VAT Notice 742A (opting to tax); **Capital Goods Scheme** in the **VAT Regulations 1995 regs 112 to 116** (£250,000 land/buildings 10 intervals; £50,000 computers 5 intervals); VAT Notice 706/2.
- **HMRC manual anchors:** **VATLP22000+** (option to tax); **PE67100+ / CA (CGS)** Notice 706/2.
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** use £250,000 (land/buildings, 10 intervals) and £50,000 (computers, 5 intervals); note the OTT lasts 20 years with a 6-month cooling-off and 30-day notification. Make clear a **tenant firm does not opt to tax premises it merely occupies** (the owner of the interest opts). Tie the CGS adjustment percentage to the §6.C partial-exemption recovery rate. Cross-ref §6.C, §8 (capital allowances on the same fit-out spend).

### 6.F VAT on legal aid work and the tax point (locked, 2026-06-03, HP-lock gate)

**Position.** Legal aid (Legal Aid Agency funded) work is a **standard-rated taxable supply** (no exemption). A VAT-registered firm submits its legal-aid bill **net of VAT** and the funder **adds VAT at source** per the firm's VAT mandate; the firm still **accounts output VAT** to HMRC. The tax point follows §6.D / HMRC VATTOS: the **basic tax point is on COMPLETION** of the case (VATTOS8560 / VATTOS8570); a standard monthly payment or payment on account relating to a **completed** case creates no fresh tax point, but the part offset against **ongoing or future** work is a pre-payment that **does** create a tax point. The LAA billing and payment cycle creates a cash-flow lag that interacts with lock-up (§4).

- **Statutory / authority hooks:** VATA 1994 (standard rate, supply of services); **HMRC VATTOS8560 / VATTOS8570** (legal-aid tax point on completion); §6.D (time of supply). Conductor cross-checked the brief's VATTOS quotes.
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** legal aid is **standard-rated**; the firm bills **net** and VAT is **added at source** (the firm still accounts output VAT); the tax point is **case completion**, not when the LAA pays. Cross-ref §6, §6.D, §4.

### 6.G VAT on inter-partes (recovered) costs (locked, 2026-06-03, HP-lock gate)

**Position.** On costs recovered from the other side (party-and-party / inter-partes), VAT is claimed against the paying party **only to the extent the receiving party's client cannot recover it as input tax**. If the receiving client is **VAT-registered and recovers** the input tax, costs are claimed **NET of VAT**. If the client **cannot recover VAT** (e.g. a private individual or an exempt body), costs are claimed **VAT-inclusive** and the paying party bears the VAT (and cannot itself reclaim it, the legal supply being to the receiving party's client, not to the payer). The firm issues its **tax invoice to its OWN client**, never to the paying party. This flows from the **indemnity principle**; the bill / costs schedule must state the receiving party's VAT-recovery position.

- **Statutory / authority hooks:** **HMRC VATSC11534** (the Law Society Revenue Law Committee agreement on VAT and recovered costs); **CPR Practice Direction 44** (VAT statement in the bill of costs); the indemnity principle. Conductor cross-checked the brief's VATSC11534 quotes.
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** registered receiving client recovering input tax → costs claimed **net of VAT**; non-recovering client → costs claimed **VAT-inclusive** (paying party bears it, cannot reclaim); the firm invoices **its own client**, not the payer. Cross-ref §6, §6.F.

### 6.H Referral and introduction fees (locked, 2026-06-03, HP-lock gate)

**Position.** A solicitor may pay or receive a **referral / introduction fee** in most work (e.g. conveyancing, probate) provided it is **disclosed**: SRA Code of Conduct **paragraph 5.1** requires the firm to tell the client of any financial or other interest an introducer has in referring them (5.1(a)), any relevant **fee-sharing arrangement** (5.1(b)) put **in writing** (5.1(c)), and bars referral payments for **criminal-proceedings** clients (5.1(d)); **paragraph 5.2** presumes a payment to an introducer is a referral fee unless the firm shows otherwise. The **SRA Transparency Rules** require firms to publish prices (including VAT and likely disbursements) for specified services including residential conveyancing and probate. Referral fees in **personal-injury (and death) claims are BANNED** (LASPO 2012 ss.56 to 60, "prescribed legal business", s.56(4)); **conveyancing is NOT caught**. A referral / introduction fee is consideration for a **standard-rated supply of introduction services** (VAT at 20% on fees paid AND received); it is **never a disbursement**.

- **Statutory / authority hooks:** SRA Code of Conduct **para 5.1 / 5.2**; **SRA Transparency Rules**; **LASPO 2012 ss.56 to 60** (s.56(4) PI/death scope); VATA 1994 (standard-rated). Conductor cross-checked the brief's sra.org.uk + legislation.gov.uk quotes.
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** referral fees are permitted-with-disclosure **outside** PI (SRA Code 5.1, in writing); **BANNED in PI/death** (LASPO s.56); a referral/introduction fee is **standard-rated VAT** (paid and received) and **never a disbursement**. Cross-ref §6, §1, §7.

### 6.I Supply of staff and secondment VAT (locked, 2026-06-03, HP-lock gate)

**Position.** Seconding a fee-earner (to a client, or between firms) is a **standard-rated supply of staff** for VAT: VAT at 20% is due on the **FULL charge**, including any salary, employer NIC and pension costs recharged. The old **"staff hire concession"** (which let VAT be charged only on the commission / mark-up) was **WITHDRAWN with effect from 1 April 2009**. There is **no taxable supply of staff** in two cases: a **genuine joint contract of employment** (the worker employed by both parties), and a secondment involving a **temporary suspension** of the original employment contract. The seconding firm normally **remains the employer for PAYE**; overseas secondment can engage the reverse charge.

- **Statutory / authority hooks:** HMRC **VATSC03540** / VAT Notice 700/34 (supply of staff; concession withdrawn **1 April 2009**); VATA 1994 standard rate. Conductor cross-checked the brief's VATSC03540 quotes.
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** secondment = **standard-rated supply of staff**, VAT on the **FULL charge incl. recharged salary**; the staff-hire concession is **gone (1 Apr 2009)**; the only no-supply cases are **genuine joint employment** or a **suspended contract**. Cross-ref §6, §3.

### 6.J VAT grouping and service-company recharges (locked, 2026-06-03, HP-lock gate)

**Position.** Intra-group supplies between members of a **VAT group** are **DISREGARDED** for VAT (**VATA 1994 s.43(1)**: the representative member is treated as carrying on each member's business; supplies between members are disregarded, so a service company's recharge to the LLP carries **no VAT and no VAT invoice**). Eligibility (**s.43A**): UK bodies corporate under common control; and since **Finance Act 2019 (effective 1 November 2019)** non-corporate entities (individuals and partnerships, including an **LLP**) can join a VAT group where they **control** the body corporate. So a law-firm LLP can VAT-group with its **service company** and the staff / premises / IT recharge carries no VAT. **WITHOUT** a VAT group, the service company's recharge is a **standard-rated supply (20%)**, recoverable by the LLP as input tax only to the extent it makes taxable supplies (subject to §6.C partial exemption). A service company taking a **genuine arm's-length cost-plus recharge** (TIOPA 2010 Part 4 transfer pricing + the wholly-and-exclusively test) is **NOT a profit-sharing corporate member** and is not caught by the §2.B mixed-membership rules (ss.850C-E); profit parked via a non-commercial recharge can be.

- **Statutory / authority hooks:** **VATA 1994 s.43 / s.43A** (VAT groups; FA 2019 non-corporate extension from 1 Nov 2019); VAT Notice 700/2; TIOPA 2010 Part 4 (transfer pricing); §2.B (mixed-membership). Conductor cross-checked the brief's s.43/s.43A quotes.
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** a **VAT group disregards** the service-company recharge (s.43); **without** a group it is **standard-rated 20%**; an **LLP can now join** a VAT group (FA 2019, from 1 Nov 2019); a **genuine arm's-length recharge is not a §2.B corporate member**. Cross-ref §6.C, §2.B, §3.

### 6.K Expert-witness and forensic-accountant fees: VAT and disbursement treatment (locked, 2026-07-09, HP-lock gate Wave 3)

**Position.** Fees of a forensic accountant or other expert instructed in litigation follow the **§6.B counsel analysis**. **Party-appointed expert (the firm contracts and instructs):** the default is a **supply to the firm**: the firm recovers input VAT and charges output VAT when it passes the cost on as part of its own supply; **disbursement treatment** (outside the scope, no VAT added) is available only where **all eight VTAXPER39000 conditions** are met, including that the client was responsible for paying the expert and authorised that specific payment, which is rarely satisfied where the firm selects and instructs the expert. **Single joint expert (CPR r35.7/r35.8):** the parties are **jointly and severally liable for the SJE's fees (r35.8(5))**, which makes the "client responsible for paying" condition materially easier to satisfy, so disbursement treatment is a stronger (but still conditions-dependent) case; check conditions 1, 7 and 8 on the facts. On **inter-partes recovery** the §6.G analysis applies (net vs VAT-inclusive by the receiving client's VAT-recovery position). Expert fees are **costs** within CPR r44.1(1), recoverable subject to proportionality; the court can cap expert fees at permission stage (r35.4(4)).

- **Statutory / authority hooks:** CPR **r35.3** (overriding duty), **r35.4(4)** (fee cap), **r35.6** (written questions), **r35.7/r35.8(5)** (SJE, joint and several fee liability), verified justice.gov.uk 2026-07-09; **VTAXPER39000** (eight disbursement conditions); §6.B (counsel), §6.G (inter-partes).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** never state a blanket "expert fees are disbursements"; run the agency/eight-conditions analysis and split party-appointed vs SJE. Cross-ref §6.B, §6.G.

## 7. SDLT, LBTT and LTT (the firm advises on it) (locked, 2026-06-03)

**Position.** The firm **advises clients on, and pays from client account**, the property transaction tax; the firm's own conveyancing fee is a **separate standard-rated VAT supply** (§6), and the tax paid for the client is a disbursement (§6). Three jurisdictions:
- **SDLT (England and Northern Ireland), HMRC.** Progressive (banded) residential rates. From **1 April 2025** the standard residential bands are: nil to **£125,000**; **2%** on £125,001 to £250,000; **5%** on £250,001 to £925,000; **10%** on £925,001 to £1,500,000; **12%** above £1,500,000. (The temporary **£250,000** nil-rate band and the **£425,000 / £625,000** first-time-buyer thresholds that ran from 23 September 2022 **ended on 31 March 2025**; do not use them for transactions on or after 1 April 2025.) **First-time-buyer relief (from 1 April 2025):** nil to **£300,000**, **5%** on the slice £300,001 to £500,000, **no relief** if the price exceeds £500,000. **Higher rates for additional dwellings (HRAD) surcharge: 5%** (raised from 3%) for effective dates **on or after 31 October 2024** (contracts exchanged on/before 30 October 2024 keep 3%). **Non-resident surcharge 2%** (from 1 April 2021). Residential SDLT return + payment due within **14 days** of the effective date. Non-residential/mixed-use bands: nil to £150,000, 2% to £250,000, 5% above.
- **LBTT (Scotland), Revenue Scotland.** Progressive (marginal-band), NOT slab; nil-rate **£145,000**, first-time-buyer relief lifts the nil rate to **£175,000**. **Additional Dwelling Supplement (ADS): 8%** (raised from 6%) for contracts entered into **on or after 5 December 2024**. Return + payment within **30 days**.
- **LTT (Wales), Welsh Revenue Authority.** Progressive (marginal-band); main residential nil-rate **£225,000**; **no** first-time-buyer relief. Additional/second-property purchases use **higher residential rate tables** (not a flat surcharge); the opening higher rate rose from **4% to 5% from 11 December 2024** (each band +1 point). Return + payment within **30 days**.

- **Statutory / authority hooks:** **Finance Act 2003 Part 4** (SDLT) + HRAD/non-resident surcharge provisions; **Land and Buildings Transaction Tax (Scotland) Act 2013** (LBTT) + ADS; **Land Transaction Tax and Anti-avoidance of Devolved Taxes (Wales) Act 2017** (LTT). Rate sources: SDLT HRAD 5% https://taxscape.deloitte.com/measures-autumn-budget-2024/stamp-duty-land-tax---higher-rates-for-additional-dwellings.aspx ; LBTT https://revenue.scot/taxes/land-buildings-transaction-tax/residential-property ; LTT https://www.gov.wales/land-transaction-tax-rates-and-bands .
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** jurisdiction-tag every property-tax statement (England/NI = SDLT/HMRC/14 days; Scotland = LBTT/Revenue Scotland/30 days; Wales = LTT/WRA/30 days). Use the **5% SDLT surcharge from 31 Oct 2024** (NOT 3%, NOT "from April 2025"). Use the **£125,000 SDLT nil-rate band and the £300,000 first-time-buyer threshold from 1 April 2025** (the temporary £250,000 / £425,000 thresholds ended 31 March 2025: do NOT state nil-rate £250,000 or FTB £425,000 for a current transaction). LBTT and LTT are **progressive/marginal**, NOT slab. LBTT ADS is **8% from 5 Dec 2024**; Wales uses higher-rate tables (opening 5% from 11 Dec 2024), not a flat 4% surcharge. Frame the firm's role as "you advise on and pay it; your fee is a separate VAT supply". Do NOT call LBTT or LTT a slab tax. Do NOT use the old 3% SDLT surcharge or the old 6% LBTT ADS for current advice. Cross-ref §6, §6.A.

### 7.A SDLT refund and overpayment claim windows (locked, 2026-06-03, HP-lock gate)

**Position.** Where a buyer paid the **5% higher-rate (additional-dwelling) surcharge** because they had not yet sold their previous main residence, they can **reclaim** the surcharge once the old home is sold, provided the old home is sold **within 3 years** of the new purchase (exceptional-circumstances extension possible). The refund claim must reach HMRC by the **LATER of 12 months after the sale of the previous main home OR 12 months after the filing date of the SDLT return**. Separately, a return can be **amended within 12 months of its filing date** (the filing date being 14 days after the effective date), and **overpayment relief** can be claimed where it is more than 12 months after the filing date but **within 4 years of the effective date**. England and Northern Ireland (SDLT / HMRC); LBTT (Scotland) and LTT (Wales) have their own refund routes and time limits.

- **Statutory / authority hooks:** Finance Act 2003 Sch 4ZA (higher rates + the replacement-of-main-residence refund); gov.uk "Apply for a refund of the higher rates of SDLT" + SDLT returns / overpayment-relief guidance. Conductor cross-checked the brief's gov.uk quotes.
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** higher-rate refund = the **later of 12 months from the sale or 12 months from the filing date**; old home sold **within 3 years**; **amend** within 12 months of the filing date; **overpayment relief** within 4 years of the effective date; England/NI only (LBTT/LTT differ). Cross-ref §7, §6.

### 7.B Corporate / non-natural-person SDLT (17%) and ATED (locked, 2026-06-03, HP-lock gate)

**Position.** A **single dwelling costing more than £500,000** bought by a **company, a partnership with a corporate member, or a collective investment scheme** (a "non-natural person") is charged SDLT at a **flat 17%** (raised from 15% to **17% for transactions with an effective date on or after 31 October 2024**), UNLESS a relief applies: a **property-rental business**, a **property developer/trader**, a trade making the property **available to the public**, a **financial institution lending**, **occupation by employees**, a **farmhouse**, or a **qualifying housing co-operative** (reliefs are **clawed back** if the qualifying use ceases within the control period). Where a relief takes the purchase out of the 17% flat rate, the **standard residential rates plus the 5% higher-rate surcharge and the 2% non-resident surcharge** apply instead. A company holding a UK dwelling worth more than £500,000 may also owe the **Annual Tax on Enveloped Dwellings (ATED)**, an annual charge; an ATED return (or a relief-declaration return) is required **even where the charge is nil**.

- **Statutory / authority hooks:** Finance Act 2003 s.55 / **Sch 4A** (higher-rate 15%/17% for non-natural persons); **Finance Act 2013 Part 3** (ATED); gov.uk "SDLT: corporate bodies" + ATED guidance. Conductor-verified the **17%** rate at gov.uk 2026-06-03.
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** the corporate / non-natural-person flat rate is **17%** over £500,000 (**from 31 October 2024; do NOT use 15%** for current transactions); list the reliefs and the clawback; flag the **ATED** follow-on (a return is due even when the charge is nil). Cross-ref §7, §7.A.

## 8. Capital allowances for law-firm fit-out and IT (locked, 2026-06-03)

**Position.** **Annual Investment Allowance (AIA) £1,000,000** per year (permanent), 100% first-year relief on qualifying plant and machinery, covering most office fit-out kit, IT/servers, furniture and equipment. A **fit-out** splits between **general plant and machinery** (main-rate pool, writing-down allowance **18%, reducing to 14% from 1 April 2026 (corporation tax) / 6 April 2026 (income tax)** per **FA 2026 s.28**) and **integral features** (special-rate pool, **6%** WDA, unchanged) such as electrical, cold/hot water, heating, air and ventilation systems; AIA is best directed at the special-rate (6%) items first. A new **40% first-year allowance** on **new** main-rate plant applies from **1 January 2026** (FA 2026 s.29) to companies and unincorporated businesses (excludes cars and second-hand). **Full expensing** (companies only: 100% FYA on new main-rate, 50% on new special-rate) remains permanent. On buying **a property including fixtures**, a **CAA 2001 s.198 election** fixes the fixtures value between seller and buyer (joint election, **2-year limit** under s.201; the s.187A fixed-value + pooling conditions apply); missing it forfeits the buyer's fixtures allowances. **SBA (Structures and Buildings Allowance) at 3%** may apply to qualifying construction/premises spend on/after 29 October 2018 (the building structure, not the plant).

- **Statutory / authority hooks:** **CAA 2001** s.38A/s.51A (AIA), s.56 (main-rate WDA 18% reducing to 14% per **FA 2026 s.28**), ss.104A to 104D (special-rate 6%), **FA 2026 s.29** (40% FYA), s.39/s.45S (full expensing), **s.187A** (fixed-value + pooling), **s.198/s.199** (fixtures election), **s.201** (2-year limit), Part 2A (SBA 3%).
- **HMRC manual anchors:** **CA23080+** (AIA), **CA22300+** (integral features), **CA26800+** (fixtures elections), **CA90000+** (SBA).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** use AIA £1m; date-tag the main-rate WDA (**18% to Mar/Apr 2026, then 14%**, FA 2026 s.28); special rate 6%. On a premises purchase flag the s.198 election and its 2-year deadline (a preventable loss). For a NEW main-rate asset compare AIA (100%) with the 40% FYA. Full expensing is companies-only. Do NOT state a flat 18% WDA for periods after April 2026. Do NOT claim AIA on cars or SBA on plant. Cross-ref §9 (asset price split on a sale).

## 9. Practice sale and succession (locked, 2026-06-03)

**Position.** A **partnership or LLP has no shares**, so its sale is an **asset/business sale** (the buyer buys goodwill, WIP, debtors and fixed assets; the member's capital account is settled). Only an **incorporated firm (company/ABS)** can do a **share sale**. **Goodwill** is the largest asset on most law-firm sales and is a **capital asset**: the seller's gain is charged to **CGT, not income tax** (report on the capital-gains pages). **WIP and debtors are trading income** (taxed at income-tax rates + Class 4 NIC if sold/billed as a trading receipt), so the sale agreement must clearly split goodwill (capital) from WIP/debtors (income), see §4.

**Business Asset Disposal Relief (BADR):** reduced CGT on qualifying gains up to a **£1m lifetime limit per individual**. Rate: **10%** to 5 Apr 2025; **14%** for disposals 6 Apr 2025 to 5 Apr 2026; **18%** from 6 Apr 2026. Conditions held throughout the **2-year period to disposal**: trading business/company; for an **asset sale** of a partnership/LLP interest, a genuine partner/member disposing of the whole or part of the business; for a **share sale**, at least **5% of ordinary share capital and 5% of voting rights**, plus officer/employee status, plus 5% economic entitlement. A member caught by the **salaried member rules (§2.A)** may be treated as not having a genuine BADR-qualifying interest.

**Standard (non-BADR) CGT rates for individuals (locked at the rewrite-session HP-lock eyeball, 2026-06-03):** **18%** on gains within the basic-rate band and **24%** on gains above it, applying to **all** chargeable assets (including goodwill) from **30 October 2024** (the former 10% / 20% rates on non-residential assets rose to 18% / 24% that day; residential was already 18% / 24%). **Annual exempt amount £3,000** (2025/26 and 2026/27). So a partner's gain **above** the BADR £1m lifetime limit, or any gain that does not qualify for BADR, is taxed at **18% / 24%**, not the BADR rate.

**Section 162 incorporation relief:** **TCGA 1992 s.162** defers CGT where the whole business is transferred as a going concern to a company wholly or partly for shares (cash consideration restricts the relief pro rata); used to roll an unincorporated practice into a company so a later share sale can access BADR.

**Buyer-side goodwill relief:** a buyer can claim **fixed-rate 6.5% per year** relief on goodwill/relevant assets acquired **on/after 1 April 2019** as part of a business acquisition that also includes qualifying IP (CTA 2009 Part 8 Ch 15A, s.879A+), capped at 6x the qualifying-IP expenditure; **no relief** on goodwill acquired 8 July 2015 to 31 March 2019. On a **self-incorporation** (partners transferring their existing practice to their own new company) the goodwill is acquired from a **related party**, which **fully restricts** the buyer-company relief where the third-party-acquisition condition is not met (effectively no 6.5% amortisation for the new company, HMRC **CIRD44065**), so do NOT assume the company gets goodwill relief on incorporation of an existing firm. **VAT:** a going-concern business sale can be a **TOGC** (no VAT) if the conditions are met; a share sale is outside the scope of VAT.

- **Statutory / authority hooks:** **TCGA 1992 ss.169H to 169S** (BADR) https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/12 ; **TCGA 1992 s.162** (incorporation relief); standard CGT rates 18% / 24% (gov.uk/capital-gains-tax/rates, from 30 Oct 2024) + annual exempt amount £3,000; **CTA 2009 s.879A+ / Part 8 Ch 15A** (goodwill relief, FA 2019 Sch 9); **ITTOIA 2005 ss.182 to 185** (valuation of work in progress / trading stock on cessation: WIP brought into account as an income receipt, not capital); BADR rate schedule https://www.gov.uk/business-asset-disposal-relief ; VATA 1994 + SI 1995/1268 (TOGC).
- **HMRC manual anchors:** **CG63950+** (BADR); **CG65700+** (s.162 incorporation relief); **CIRD44050+** (goodwill/relevant-assets relief); **VTOGC** (transfer of a going concern).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** state the BADR rate **with its exact date band** every time (the 6 Apr 2026 step to 18% is a live planning lever for late-2025/26 completions). State plainly that a partnership/LLP can only do an asset sale (no shares) and that goodwill is capital while WIP/debtors are income. Use 6.5% buyer goodwill relief only for post-1-April-2019 acquisitions and surface the qualifying-IP/related-party conditions. Do NOT state BADR is still 10%. Do NOT say a partnership/LLP can be sold by a share sale. Cross-ref §2.A, §4, §8.

### 9.A CGT on matrimonial transfers: the FA 2023 no-gain/no-loss window (locked, 2026-07-09, HP-lock gate Wave 3, verified legislation.gov.uk)

**Position.** Transfers of assets between spouses/civil partners in divorce follow **TCGA 1992 s.58 as substituted by Finance (No. 2) Act 2023 (subsections (1A)-(1D), effective 6 April 2023)**: **no-gain/no-loss treatment extends to the EARLIER of the end of the third tax year after the tax year of separation, or the date of the divorce/dissolution order**; transfers made under a formal divorce agreement/order get no-gain/no-loss **without time limit**. The receiving spouse takes the transferor's **base cost** (the latent gain travels with the asset), which is the core advisory point when a business or shares are transferred in a settlement: the "value" received net of latent CGT is less than the headline value. Business valuation in matrimonial proceedings sits under **MCA 1973 s.25(2)(a)-(h)** (resources including business interests) and s.25A (clean break); expert evidence under **FPR 2010 Part 25** (single joint expert default, jointly agreed instructions, joint and several fee liability, one round of written questions, PD 25B duties).

- **Statutory / authority hooks:** **TCGA 1992 s.58(1A)-(1D)** (F(No.2)A 2023, from 6 Apr 2023) verified legislation.gov.uk 2026-07-09; **MCA 1973 s.25/s.25A** verified; **FPR 2010 Part 25 + PD 25B** verified justice.gov.uk.
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** always distinguish headline value from value net of latent CGT (base-cost carry-over); date-split the s.58 rule at 6 April 2023 and state both limbs (3-tax-year window vs unlimited under a formal order). Case-law anchors must be verified before citation (White v White [2000] UKHL 54 is the verified sharing-principle cite; Versteegh unverified at Stage 1, re-source before use). Cross-ref §9, §6.K.

## 10. PII, IR35 for locum/consultant solicitors, and MTD for ITSA (locked, 2026-06-03)

**Position · Professional Indemnity Insurance (PII).** Every SRA-regulated firm must hold PII on the SRA **Minimum Terms and Conditions (MTC)**. Minimum sum insured per claim: **£3 million** for a **relevant recognised body or relevant licensed body** (LLPs, companies, ABSs), **£2 million** "in all other cases" (sole practitioners and partnerships); **no monetary limit on defence costs**. On cessation a firm must obtain **run-off cover for six years**. PII premiums are an **allowable trading expense** (wholly and exclusively, **ITTOIA 2005 s.34** / CTA 2009 for companies), deducted at firm level for a partnership/LLP (before allocation) and at company level for a company; a locum/consultant deducts their own premium (self-employment pages, or against the PSC's corporation tax). PII premiums are **VAT-exempt** (insurance), so no input VAT to reclaim. Apportion any mixed personal/professional cover.

**Position · IR35 / off-payroll for locum and consultant solicitors.** A locum operating through a **personal service company (PSC)** is within the off-payroll regime. From **6 April 2021**, where the engaging firm is **medium or large** (Companies Act 2006 size test: two of turnover > £10.2m, balance sheet > £5.1m, > 50 employees), the **client, not the PSC, determines status** and issues a **Status Determination Statement (SDS)**; if inside IR35 the fee-payer operates PAYE/NIC before paying the PSC (Chapter 10, Part 2 ITEPA 2003). If the engaging firm is **small**, the **PSC** decides its own status (the original IR35, Chapter 8, Part 2 ITEPA 2003). Status turns on substitution, control and mutuality of obligation (the actual working arrangement, not the label). A **sole-trader** locum is outside IR35 (it applies to intermediaries) but the engaging firm must still assess genuine self-employment.

**Position · MTD for Income Tax Self Assessment.** Phased by qualifying income (gross trading + property): **£50,000 from 6 April 2026**, **£30,000 from 6 April 2027**, **£20,000 from 6 April 2028**. Most full-time partners/sole practitioners exceed £50,000 and are in scope from 6 April 2026. MTD for ITSA does **not** apply to companies (it is income tax, not corporation tax); MTD for VAT already applies to all VAT-registered firms.

- **Statutory / authority hooks:** **SRA Indemnity Insurance Rules + MTC** (£3m recognised/licensed bodies, £2m others, 6-year run-off) https://www.sra.org.uk/solicitors/standards-regulations/indemnity-insurance-rules/ ; **ITTOIA 2005 s.34** (wholly and exclusively); VATA 1994 Sch 9 Group 2 (insurance exemption); **ITEPA 2003 (c.1) Part 2 Chapter 10** (off-payroll, client-led) https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2003/1/part/2/chapter/10 and **Chapter 8** (original IR35); Finance (No. 2) Act 2017 + SI 2021/1076 (MTD for ITSA).
- **HMRC manual anchors:** **BIM37000+** (wholly and exclusively); **ESM10000+** (off-payroll / IR35); MTD for ITSA guidance https://www.gov.uk/guidance/check-when-to-sign-up-for-making-tax-digital-for-income-tax .
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** PII minimum cover is **£3m for LLPs/companies/ABSs, £2m for sole practitioners/partnerships** (state it this way round; do NOT reverse it), run-off 6 years, premiums deductible and VAT-exempt. For IR35 state the SDS sits with the medium/large engaging firm from 6 Apr 2021 (small client = PSC decides). ## 11. Financial distress, insolvency and SRA intervention (locked, 2026-07-09, HP-lock gate Wave 3)

**Position.** A law firm in financial distress faces three interlocking regimes. **(1) Insolvency law by structure:** a **company/ABS** uses IA 1986 directly (CVA, administration, CVL); an **LLP** uses IA 1986 as applied by the **LLP Regulations 2001 (SI 2001/1090) Sch 3**, including **s.214A adjustment of withdrawals**: a liquidator can claw back a member's withdrawals (profit share, salary, loan interest/repayments or any other withdrawal) made **within the two years before winding up** where the member **knew or had reasonable grounds to believe** the LLP was or would become unable to pay its debts, and the court finds the member knew or ought to have concluded there was **no reasonable prospect of avoiding insolvent liquidation** (verified verbatim at legislation.gov.uk 2026-07-09); a **general partnership** is wound up under the **Insolvent Partnerships Order 1994 (SI 1994/2421)** with parallel petitions possible against the firm and the partners. **(2) SRA intervention:** grounds and powers are in **Solicitors Act 1974 Sch 1** (Pt I grounds, Pt II powers: vesting of client money, taking documents, agent appointment); intervention is the involuntary endpoint (practice money vests, practice effectively closes), and **intervention costs are recoverable from the solicitor**. Procedural/agency mechanics beyond the statute (SRA guidance pages) were NOT accessible at lock time: cite the statute, and WebFetch a live SRA source at write time before describing procedure. **(3) Run-off PII (§10):** cessation triggers **6-year run-off** on the MTC; in distress the run-off premium (typically a multiple of the annual premium) is itself a major creditor issue. Client money must remain ring-fenced per §5 throughout (distress does not relax the SRA Accounts Rules); partner **capital calls** and s.214A clawback exposure are the personal-finance dimension.

- **Statutory / authority hooks:** **Solicitors Act 1974 Sch 1 Pt I/II** verified legislation.gov.uk 2026-07-09; **SI 2001/1090 Sch 3 + IA 1986 s.214A** verified verbatim 2026-07-09; **SI 1994/2421** (IPO 1994, structure verified at contents level; fetch article text at write time before citing specific articles); **SRA MTC Annex 1 cls 5.3/5.4** (run-off, §10). SRA Code of Conduct for Firms **para 2.3** ("You remain accountable for compliance... where work is carried out through others, including third parties") verified sra.org.uk 2026-07-09; para 2.5 is material-risk management, NOT the outsourcing-accountability hook.
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** always split the analysis by structure (company/LLP/partnership); state s.214A exposure with its two-year lookback and knowledge conditions; treat intervention as statutory (Sch 1) and hedge procedure to a live SRA source; run-off premium is a distress cost, not an afterthought; client account rules never relax. Cross-ref §5, §10, §9 (orderly exit alternative), §2 (member liability context).

## 12. Barristers and chambers (locked, 2026-07-09, HP-lock gate Wave 3)

**Position.** A self-employed barrister is a trade for income tax. **Basis:** the old barrister early-years cash-basis rule (**ITTOIA 2005 s.160**) was **repealed by FA 2013 Sch 4 para 51** (from 2013-14, with transitional protection for those already using it); from **2024/25 the cash basis is the DEFAULT for all unincorporated trades (ITTOIA 2005 s.24A, inserted FA 2024 Sch 10)** with **accruals by election (s.25C)** and exclusions in s.25B (companies, LLPs etc.). A barrister with significant aged debt/WIP should actively consider the accruals election; the default cash basis taxes fees when received, which matches the bar's slow-payment reality. **VAT:** barristers have a special tax-point rule, **VAT Regs 1995 (SI 1995/2518) reg 92**: the tax point is the EARLIEST of fee receipt, VAT-invoice issue, or cessation of practice (verified legislation.gov.uk 2026-07-09). **Chambers expenses:** rent, clerks' fees and chambers contributions are deductible (wholly-and-exclusively, s.34); the VAT treatment of chambers shared expenses is governed by **VAT Notice 700/44 (VAT for barristers and advocates)**, which prescribes THREE methods (verified gov.uk 2026-07-09): **Method 1** (nominated member claims all input tax and charges output tax to the other members, who reclaim it; by concession no tax invoices needed), **Method 2** (input tax apportioned among members by contribution, each registered member claims their share directly, no output tax charged), **Method 3** (only where ALL members are VAT-registered: nominated member deducts all input tax and contributes the equivalent to the common fund). VIT13850 covers the input-tax side. Present chambers VAT by the three methods; do NOT assert a blanket no-supply position (Method 1 explicitly involves output tax). **NIC:** Class 4 6%/2% (LPL £12,570/UPL £50,270), Class 2 treated as paid at/above the SPT (£7,105 for 2026/27, per gov.uk).

- **Statutory / authority hooks:** **ITTOIA 2005 s.24A/s.25B/s.25C** (cash basis default, FA 2024 Sch 10, from 6 Apr 2024) verified legislation.gov.uk 2026-07-09; **s.160 repeal** (FA 2013 Sch 4 para 51) verified; **SI 1995/2518 reg 92** verified; **VAT Notice 700/44** (three chambers methods) + **VIT13850** verified 2026-07-09; BIM72010 (cash-basis exclusions list, barristers NOT excluded) verified.
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** never present the pre-2013 "first seven years" barrister rule as current; date-split at 2013-14 (repeal) and 2024/25 (default cash basis). State reg 92 as the barrister VAT tax point. Chambers VAT = the three Notice 700/44 methods; name the barrister's chambers' method as the determinant. Cross-ref §6.B (the solicitor-payer side), §3 (NIC), §10 (IR35 for employed/PSC arrangements).
