## 1. The intermediaries legislation: IR35 (Chapter 8) and off-payroll working (Chapter 10) (locked, 2026-06-12)

**Position.** Two separate but related code sets sit in **Part 2 ITEPA 2003**. The **original intermediaries legislation ("IR35")** is **Chapter 8** (in force from 6 April 2000): where it applies, the **PSC itself** assesses status and, if inside, calculates and operates a **deemed employment payment** on its own account (§4). The **off-payroll working rules** are **Chapter 10** (public sector from **6 April 2017**, extended to medium and large private-sector clients from **6 April 2021**): here the **end client determines status** (issues a Status Determination Statement, §3) and the **fee-payer** (usually the agency closest to the PSC) operates PAYE and NIC before paying the PSC (§4). Which chapter applies depends on the **size of the end client**: if the client is **small** (or wholly overseas with no UK connection), Chapter 10 does not apply and the **PSC stays responsible under Chapter 8**; if the client is medium or large, Chapter 10 applies and responsibility shifts to the client/fee-payer.

- **Statutory hooks:** **ITEPA 2003 Chapter 8, Part 2** (ss.48 to 61, IR35); **Chapter 10, Part 2** (ss.61K to 61X, off-payroll working). https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2003/1
- **HMRC manual anchors:** **ESM3000+** (Chapter 8 / IR35); **ESM10000+** (Chapter 10 / off-payroll working).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** never conflate the two regimes. "IR35" loosely means the whole topic, but precisely: **Chapter 8 = PSC decides and pays; Chapter 10 = client decides, fee-payer pays.** State that the dividing line is **client size** (small client = Chapter 8 stays with the PSC). Do NOT say the contractor "always decides their own status" (only true for Chapter 8 / small or overseas clients). Cross-ref §1.A (small-company exemption), §3 (SDS), §4 (deemed payment mechanics).

### 1.A The small-company exemption and ITEPA s.60A (locked, 2026-06-12)

**Position.** Chapter 10 does **not** apply where the end client is a **small** company; in that case the PSC remains responsible under Chapter 8. "Small" is defined by reference to the **Companies Act 2006 s.382** small-company conditions, imported by **ITEPA 2003 s.60A**: a company is small for a financial year if it meets **two or more** of three conditions. From **financial years beginning on or after 6 April 2025** those thresholds were raised by roughly 50%: **turnover not more than £15m** (was £10.2m), **balance sheet total not more than £7.5m** (was £5.1m), and **not more than 50 employees** (unchanged). A company normally has to meet (or cease to meet) the conditions in **two consecutive financial years** before its status changes (s.382(2)).

**Timing knock-on (state carefully).** A client's off-payroll obligation for a given **tax year** is set by reference to its **last financial year that ended before the start of that tax year** with sufficient time to file (the relevant-financial-year rule in s.60A and the linked Companies Act filing mechanics). Because of this lag plus the two-consecutive-years rule, the **earliest** a previously medium client can drop out of Chapter 10 scope on the back of the raised thresholds is **6 April 2027** (and for many year-ends 6 April 2028). So for 2026/27 most contractors should assume their medium/large clients are **still in scope** unless the client has confirmed otherwise.

- **Statutory hooks:** **ITEPA 2003 s.60A** (when a client qualifies as small for off-payroll); **Companies Act 2006 s.382** (small-company thresholds, raised for financial years beginning on/after 6 Apr 2025); **s.60B to s.60G** ITEPA (associated and connected entities, non-corporate clients). https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2006/46/section/382
- **HMRC manual anchors:** **ESM10008+** (conditions for the simplified / small-client treatment).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** use the **£15m / £7.5m / 50** thresholds and tag them "for financial years beginning on or after 6 April 2025". State the **two-or-more-of-three** rule and the **two-consecutive-years** rule. Critically, do NOT tell contractors the threshold rise takes them out of scope **now**: explain the **6 April 2027 earliest** timing because of the relevant-financial-year lag. For non-corporate clients (LLPs, partnerships, unincorporated bodies) the test is turnover-based; flag that as a separate limb and point to s.60C. Cross-ref §1, §3, §4.

## 2. Status tests and the leading cases (locked, 2026-06-12)

**Position.** There is **no statutory definition of employment** for tax. Status rests on the multi-factorial case-law test from ***Ready Mixed Concrete (South East) Ltd v Minister of Pensions* [1968] 2 QB 497**: an irreducible minimum of (1) **personal service** (the worker must do the work personally; a genuine, unfettered **right of substitution** points away from employment), (2) a sufficient degree of **control** by the engager (what, how, when, where), and (3) **mutuality of obligation (MOO)** (an obligation to offer and to accept/perform paid work), with the remaining terms then weighed in the round. Modern appellate authority (**Atholl House [2022] EWCA Civ 501**, **Kickabout [2022] EWCA Civ 502**, and the Supreme Court in **PGMOL [2024] UKSC 29**) confirms that MOO and control are **necessary preconditions but not sufficient**: even where both are present, the tribunal must stand back and ask, on the **whole picture**, whether the hypothetical contract is one of employment, including whether the worker is **in business on their own account**.

- **The three tests in a contractor setting:**
  - **Control:** the more the client directs how, when and where the work is done (set hours, line management, fixed location with no autonomy), the more employment-like. Genuine professional autonomy over method points to self-employment.
  - **Personal service / substitution:** a genuine and **unfettered right to send a substitute** (paying the substitute yourself, the client cannot unreasonably refuse) is a strong outside-IR35 pointer. A fettered or never-used "sham" substitution clause carries little weight (working practices over wording).
  - **Mutuality of obligation:** beyond the basic wage-work bargain, the question is whether there is an **ongoing obligation** to offer and accept work. **PGMOL** confirmed MOO can exist within a single engagement (once an appointment is accepted there are obligations on both sides), so the absence of an umbrella obligation between assignments does not by itself put a contractor outside; MOO is necessary, not decisive.
- **CEST house stance (grey area).** HMRC's **Check Employment Status for Tax (CEST)** tool (updated **30 April 2025**) tests substitution, control and financial risk and now has a dedicated MOO question and an upfront "is there a contract" gate. **House stance:** CEST is a useful **first screen and an audit-trail document**, and HMRC says it will **stand behind a CEST result where the inputs are accurate, consistent with the working practices, in line with the guidance, and there is no avoidance**, but that promise has real limits: (a) it does not bind a tribunal; (b) it is only as good as the answers, and a determination that does not match the **actual working practices** is worthless; (c) its treatment of MOO is **narrower than the case law**, so it can return "outside" or refuse to decide on facts a tribunal would view differently. **Never present a CEST "outside" result as a guarantee.** Treat it as supporting evidence to be backed by a contract review and a working-practices assessment. See §17.
- **Statutory / authority hooks:** no single statute; *Ready Mixed Concrete* [1968] 2 QB 497; *Atholl House* [2022] EWCA Civ 501; *Kickabout* [2022] EWCA Civ 502; *PGMOL* [2024] UKSC 29.
- **HMRC manual anchors:** **ESM0500+** (employment status, the factors and the irreducible minimum); **ESM7000+** / CEST guidance; **ESM11000+** (using CEST).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** lead with the three tests and "the whole picture governs". Cite the cases by name with the bracketed citation. State the **CEST stand-behind promise WITH its limits** every time CEST is mentioned. Stress **working practices over contract wording**. Do NOT cite a statutory section as "the employment test" (there is not one). Do NOT tell readers a substitution clause alone makes them outside-IR35 (it must be genuine and the whole picture still applies). Cross-ref §1, §3, §17.

## 3. Status Determination Statements, reasonable care and the disagreement process (locked, 2026-06-12)

**Position.** Under **Chapter 10** the **end client must issue a Status Determination Statement (SDS)** for each engagement: a statement of the client's conclusion (inside or outside IR35) **with the reasons for it**, taken with **reasonable care**. The SDS must be passed to the **worker** and to the **next party in the chain** (the agency/fee-payer). Two consequences flow from getting it wrong: (1) if the client **does not take reasonable care**, the SDS is **invalid** and the **client itself becomes the deemed employer** liable for the PAYE/NIC (even if there is an agency below it); (2) if the client fails to **pass the SDS down** the chain, the **liability sits with the client** until it does. A worker who disagrees can use the **client-led disagreement process**: the client must consider the representations and respond **within 45 days**, either confirming the SDS (with reasons) or issuing a new one. **Transfer of debt:** where a party in the chain fails to meet its obligations or cannot pay, HMRC can recover the PAYE debt from another party up the chain (ultimately the client), under the off-payroll debt-transfer provisions.

- **Statutory hooks:** **ITEPA 2003 s.61NA** (SDS, reasonable care, passing it on); **s.61T** (client-led disagreement process, 45 days); the off-payroll debt-transfer regulations (Income Tax (PAYE) Regulations 2003 as amended for Chapter 10). https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2003/1
- **HMRC manual anchors:** **ESM10013** (what makes a valid SDS); **ESM10014** (reasonable care); **ESM10015** (client-led disagreement process); **ESM10025+** (debt transfer / recovery up the chain).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** the **client issues the SDS, not the contractor**. State that lack of reasonable care or failure to pass it down moves the liability **to the client**. Give the **45-day** response deadline for the disagreement process and note it is **client-led** (the client decides, but must respond and give reasons). Flag **blanket determinations** (a client applying "inside" to a whole category of roles without individual assessment) as a likely **failure of reasonable care**, see §17. Do NOT say the contractor can self-determine when the client is medium/large. Cross-ref §1, §2, §4, §17.

## 4. The deemed employment payment: Chapter 10 fee-payer PAYE vs the Chapter 8 calculation (locked, 2026-06-12)

**Position.** What happens to inside-IR35 income depends on which chapter applies.

- **Chapter 10 (client medium/large).** The **fee-payer** treats the payment to the PSC, after deducting any VAT and the **direct cost of materials**, as a **deemed direct payment** and operates **PAYE and employee Class 1 NIC** on it, and pays **employer Class 1 NIC (15%)** and the Apprenticeship Levy on top, before paying the net to the PSC. There is **no 5% expenses allowance** under Chapter 10. The income arrives in the PSC having already been taxed, so it can generally be drawn by the worker without a second charge (the company tracks it to avoid double taxation), but the tax-efficient salary/dividend extraction normally available is lost on that income.
- **Chapter 8 (client small, or overseas / no UK fee-payer).** The **PSC self-assesses** and, if inside IR35, computes a **deemed employment payment** at year-end: take the total relevant engagement income, deduct (i) a flat **5% of that income** as an administrative expenses allowance (**retained under Chapter 8**), (ii) actual allowable employment-type expenses, (iii) employer pension contributions and certain other deductions, (iv) any salary already paid through PAYE, then gross-down for employer NIC and treat the balance as deemed salary subject to PAYE/NIC. The **5% allowance is the key Chapter 8 vs Chapter 10 difference**: it exists under Chapter 8 and is **gone under Chapter 10**.

- **Statutory hooks:** **ITEPA 2003 s.61N** (Chapter 10 deemed direct payment, fee-payer); **s.54 / s.58** (Chapter 8 deemed employment payment, the 5% deduction in the step calculation). https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2003/1
- **HMRC manual anchors:** **ESM10020+** (Chapter 10 calculation, deemed direct payment); **ESM3500+** (Chapter 8 deemed payment, the step-by-step computation and the 5% allowance).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** make the **5% allowance distinction explicit**: retained under Chapter 8, abolished under Chapter 10. Under Chapter 10 the fee-payer (not the PSC) operates PAYE and bears employer NIC at 15%. Note the **materials / VAT** deduction before the deemed payment. Do NOT apply the 5% allowance to a Chapter 10 engagement. Cross-ref §1, §3, §5 (offset where errors arise), §7 (employer NIC rate).

### 4.A The off-payroll set-off (offset) rules from April 2024 (locked, 2026-06-12)

**Position.** Before April 2024, if HMRC found a client/fee-payer had wrongly treated an engagement as outside IR35, it assessed the **full PAYE/NIC** on the deemed employer **without credit** for tax the worker and PSC had **already paid** on the same income (CT on PSC profits, income tax and employee NIC on salary, income tax on dividends), producing potential **double taxation**. From **6 April 2024** a statutory **set-off (offset)** lets HMRC reduce the deemed employer's PAYE liability by an estimate of those taxes **already paid** by the worker or the intermediary on the relevant income. The offset operates on a **trigger event** (typically a Reg 80 determination on or after 6 April 2024), where HMRC can identify the worker and intermediary and confirm returns were filed and tax paid/assessed. It applies to errors arising from **6 April 2017** (public sector) or **6 April 2021** (private sector), with the offset itself available from 6 April 2024.

- **Statutory hooks:** Income Tax (PAYE) Regulations 2003 as amended (the off-payroll set-off regulations, in force from 6 April 2024); the deemed-employer liability sits under ITEPA Chapter 10.
- **HMRC manual anchors:** **ESM10036+** (set-off / accounting for amounts already paid).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** state that the offset **reduces but does not eliminate** the deemed employer's exposure (it credits worker/PSC taxes already paid, not the employer NIC, which was never paid), and that it is an **HMRC-operated estimate on a trigger event**, not an automatic netting the contractor can rely on. Frame it as why the post-2024 cost of an incorrect "outside" determination, while still serious, is lower than the pre-2024 double-tax position. Cross-ref §3, §4.

## 5. Personal tax 2026/27: income tax, dividends and savings (locked, 2026-06-12)

**Position (rUK; tag the tax year on every figure).** **Personal allowance £12,570** (tapered by £1 for every £2 of adjusted net income above £100,000, nil at £125,140). Income tax bands: **basic 20%** £12,571 to £50,270; **higher 40%** £50,271 to £125,140; **additional 45%** above £125,140. These thresholds are **frozen to April 2031** (freeze extended at the November 2025 Budget), so **fiscal drag** is a live planning theme. **Dividend allowance £500.** Dividend rates (from 6 April 2026, FA 2026 s.4): **ordinary 10.75%**, **upper 35.75%**, **additional 39.35%** (additional unchanged). **Personal Savings Allowance:** **£1,000** for a basic-rate taxpayer, **£500** for a higher-rate taxpayer, **£0** for an additional-rate taxpayer; the **starting rate for savings** gives up to **£5,000** of savings income at 0% where non-savings income is low (tapered away once non-savings income exceeds £12,570, gone at £17,570). Dividends sit on **top** of other income and are taxed in their own bands after non-savings and savings income.

- **What FA 2026 changed:** it **raised the dividend ordinary and upper rates** (s.4, the change above) and **extended the threshold freeze** (Budget measure). It did **not** change the personal allowance figure, the income tax band rates, the dividend allowance (£500) or the savings allowances.
- **Statutory hooks:** **ITA 2007 s.8** (dividend rates, amended by **Finance Act 2026 (c. 11) s.4**); ITA 2007 ss.6 to 10 (main rates and bands); ITTOIA 2005 Part 4 (dividends; savings income). https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2026/11/section/4
- **HMRC guidance anchors:** https://www.gov.uk/income-tax-rates ; https://www.gov.uk/tax-on-dividends .
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** **never quote a dividend rate without its tax-year tag**; for 2026/27 use **10.75% / 35.75% / 39.35%** and note these rose from 8.75% / 33.75% on 6 April 2026. Use PA £12,570 and the frozen bands, and lean into **fiscal drag** (frozen to 2031) as a genuine planning point. Do NOT use the old 8.75%/33.75% dividend rates for 2026/27 advice. Cross-ref §8 (CT, the company side), §9 (extraction strategy), §7 (NIC).

## 6. National Insurance 2026/27 (locked, 2026-06-12)

**Position.** **Employee primary Class 1:** **8%** on earnings between the **primary threshold £12,570** and the **upper earnings limit £50,270**, then **2%** above. **Employer secondary Class 1:** **15%** on earnings above the **secondary threshold of £5,000** (the threshold was cut to £5,000 and the rate raised to 15% from 6 April 2025, carried into 2026/27); the **lower earnings limit (LEL) is £6,708**, which preserves the employee's contributory benefit/state-pension entitlement even where little NIC is actually paid. **Employment Allowance £10,500**, which offsets an employer's secondary Class 1 NIC, but is **not available to a company whose only employee is a single director** (the classic single-director PSC cannot claim it). For the **self-employed / sole trader:** **Class 4** at **6%** on profits between £12,570 and £50,270, then **2%**; **Class 2** is **no longer compulsory** above the **Small Profits Threshold (£7,105 for 2026/27)** (the self-employed are treated as having paid and keep state-pension entitlement), with **voluntary Class 2 at £3.65/week** available below the SPT to protect the NI record.

- **Statutory hooks:** **SSCBA 1992** s.8 to s.11 (Class 1 primary/secondary), s.11 (Class 2, reformed by the National Insurance Contributions Act 2024), s.15 (Class 4); **National Insurance Contributions Act 2014** (Employment Allowance, £10,500, single-director restriction).
- **HMRC guidance anchors:** https://www.gov.uk/guidance/rates-and-thresholds-for-employers-2026-to-2027 ; https://www.gov.uk/self-employed-national-insurance-rates .
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** use **employer NIC 15% above £5,000** (do NOT use the old 13.8%/£9,100). State the **single-director Employment Allowance exclusion** plainly (it is the reason single-director PSCs often pick a low salary, §9). For sole traders, state **Class 2 is no longer payable** above the SPT (do NOT tell a freelancer to pay a weekly Class 2 charge) and use **Class 4 6%/2%**. Cross-ref §9 (salary level), §11 (employer pension as the NIC-free extraction), §12 (umbrella, where employer NIC bites the assignment rate).

## 7. Corporation tax 2026/27 (locked, 2026-06-12)

**Position.** **Small profits rate 19%** where augmented profits do not exceed **£50,000**; **main rate 25%** where they exceed **£250,000**; between £50,000 and £250,000 the main rate applies with **marginal relief** (standard fraction **3/200**), producing an effective **marginal rate of about 26.5%** on profits in that band. The £50,000 and £250,000 limits are **divided by the number of associated companies** (so a contractor with several companies under common control sees the bands shrink). **FA 2026 made no change to the corporation tax rates** (they are the same for FY2025 and FY2026).

- **Associated companies:** two companies are associated where one controls the other or both are under common control; the limits are divided by the number of associated companies plus one (CTA 2010 s.18E onward). A contractor and their spouse each running a PSC can be associated if connected, shrinking each company's bands; flag it, do not assume it.
- **Statutory hooks:** **CTA 2010 Part 3, ss.18 to 18N** (small profits rate, marginal relief, associated companies). https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2010/4/part/3
- **HMRC guidance anchors:** https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/rates-and-allowances-corporation-tax/rates-and-allowances-corporation-tax .
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** use **19% / 25% / marginal relief 3/200** and the **£50k / £250k** limits, and mention the **26.5% marginal-band** effective rate where relevant. Always flag the **associated-companies** divisor when a contractor has more than one company or a connected spouse company. Confirm **FA 2026 left CT rates unchanged**. Cross-ref §9 (extraction), §15 (s.455, winding up).

## 8. Profit extraction strategy for a PSC director 2026/27 (locked, 2026-06-12)

**Position.** A PSC director extracts profit through some mix of **salary** (a deductible company expense, subject to PAYE/NIC) and **dividends** (paid from post-corporation-tax profit, taxed on the individual at dividend rates with no NIC). The standard framings for 2026/27:

- **Salary level.** Three common targets, and the right one depends on **Employment Allowance eligibility**:
  - **£5,000 to £6,708 (secondary threshold / LEL):** for a **single-director company with no other employees** that **cannot claim the Employment Allowance**, the usual targets are between the **secondary threshold (£5,000)** and the **LEL (£6,708)**. A salary at exactly £5,000 attracts no employer NIC, but it is **below** the £6,708 LEL, so to secure a **qualifying NI year** a single director typically sets salary at the **LEL £6,708**, accepting a small slice of employer NIC (15% on the £1,708 between £5,000 and £6,708) as the price of the qualifying year. State this nuance; do not present a single clean figure as universally optimal.
  - **£12,570 (the personal allowance / primary threshold):** for a company that **can** claim the Employment Allowance (eg a genuinely employed spouse on the payroll, or other staff), salary up to **£12,570** is usually optimal: the extra salary above the secondary threshold is relieved by the Employment Allowance (no net employer NIC) and saves corporation tax at 19% to 26.5%, outweighing the employee NIC/income tax cost. Even **without** the Employment Allowance, taking £12,570 rather than £6,708 can give a modest net benefit at 2026/27 rates because the corporation tax saving on the extra salary exceeds the extra employer NIC, so model it rather than asserting one answer.
- **Dividend taxation order.** Dividends are taxed **after** non-savings and savings income, in their own bands, using the **£500 dividend allowance** and **10.75% / 35.75% / 39.35%** (2026/27). Plan the split to use the basic-rate band efficiently and to manage the **£100,000 personal-allowance taper** and the **£50,270 higher-rate threshold**.
- **House stance on "optimal salary".** There is **no single optimal salary** that fits every PSC. The right number turns on (a) Employment Allowance eligibility (single director vs has-staff), (b) whether the director has other income using the personal allowance, and (c) the corporation tax marginal rate. **State the framework and the caveats; never publish a one-size-fits-all "the optimal salary is £X" without the EA caveat.** See §17.
- **Statutory hooks:** ITEPA 2003 (salary, PAYE); ITTOIA 2005 Part 4 + ITA 2007 s.8 (dividends, §5); CTA 2010 Part 3 (CT relief on salary as a deduction, §7); National Insurance Contributions Act 2014 (Employment Allowance, §6).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** present the **salary options with the EA fork** (single director vs has-staff) and the **LEL £6,708 / secondary threshold £5,000** nuance; tag dividend rates 2026/27 (10.75%/35.75%); always caveat "optimal depends on circumstances". Pair the dividend-rate rise (§5) with the message that the **incorporation advantage narrowed in 2026/27**. Do NOT publish a single universal optimal salary. Cross-ref §5, §6, §7, §11 (pension as the bigger lever), §17.

## 9. VAT for contractors (locked, 2026-06-12)

**Position.** Most contractor services are **standard-rated (20%)**. **Registration is compulsory** once **VAT-taxable turnover exceeds £90,000** in any rolling 12 months (or is expected to in the next 30 days); the **deregistration threshold is £88,000**; both have been **frozen since 1 April 2024**. Many contractors register **voluntarily** below the threshold to reclaim input VAT and because end clients (usually VAT-registered) can recover the VAT charged. The **Flat Rate Scheme (FRS)** (entry where expected taxable turnover is **£150,000 or less** ex-VAT) lets a business pay a fixed percentage of **VAT-inclusive** turnover instead of tracking input VAT, but a **limited-cost trader** (one spending less than **2% of turnover, or less than £1,000 a year**, on **goods**) must use the **16.5%** flat rate, which for a typical labour-only contractor wipes out most of the FRS benefit. The limited-cost test is applied **each VAT period**.

- **House stance on the FRS.** Since the 16.5% limited-cost-trader rate was introduced (April 2017), the FRS is **rarely worthwhile for a labour-only contractor** (most have negligible spend on goods and so fall into the 16.5% rate). It can still suit a contractor with genuine, regular goods spend above the 2%/£1,000 test, or who values administrative simplicity. **Model standard VAT accounting vs the applicable FRS percentage before recommending the FRS.**
- **Statutory hooks:** **VATA 1994 Schedule 1** (registration £90,000 / deregistration £88,000); the Flat Rate Scheme in the VAT Regulations 1995 (SI 1995/2518) and VAT Notice 733 (limited-cost trader 16.5%). https://www.gov.uk/vat-registration
- **HMRC guidance anchors:** https://www.gov.uk/vat-registration ; VAT Notice 733 (Flat Rate Scheme).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** use **£90,000 registration / £88,000 deregistration** and tag them "frozen since 1 April 2024" (do NOT use the old £85,000). For the FRS, always surface the **16.5% limited-cost-trader** rate and state it usually neutralises the FRS benefit for labour-only contractors. Apply the limited-cost test **per period**. Cross-ref §11 (expenses, since FRS interacts with input-VAT recovery).

## 10. Expenses for contractors (locked, 2026-06-12)

**Position.** Allowable PSC/sole-trader expenses are those incurred **wholly and exclusively** (and, for employees/inside-IR35, **necessarily**) for the business: **accountancy fees, business insurance (professional indemnity, public liability), equipment and software (often via capital allowances/AIA), a salary for genuine work, employer pension contributions (§11), business travel and subsistence, use of home, and phone/internet apportionment.** Three contractor-specific rules dominate:

- **The 24-month / 40% temporary-workplace rule.** Travel to a **temporary workplace** is deductible, but a workplace stops being temporary (and becomes a **permanent workplace**, so travel there is non-deductible ordinary commuting) once the contractor has spent, or **expects to spend, more than 40% of their working time there over a period exceeding 24 months**. The clock is about **expectation**: once it is known the engagement will exceed 24 months at one site, travel ceases to be allowable from that point, not from month 24. This is the single most-misclaimed contractor expense.
- **Travel and subsistence inside IR35.** For an engagement **caught by Chapter 10 (or inside under Chapter 8)**, the **home-to-client travel is generally NOT deductible**, because each separate engagement is treated as a separate employment so the client site is a **permanent workplace** for that engagement (the post-April-2016 restriction on T&S relief for workers under supervision, direction or control through an intermediary). Outside-IR35 contractors keep the temporary-workplace relief subject to the 24-month rule.
- **Mileage (AMAP).** For business motoring in the contractor's own car/van, the approved tax-free rate is **55p per mile for the first 10,000 business miles and 25p thereafter from 6 April 2026** (the first-10,000 rate rose from 45p; 25p is unchanged). Ordinary commuting (home to a permanent workplace) does not qualify.
- **Trivial benefits.** A close company can give the director small perks free of tax/NIC under the **trivial benefits exemption** (ITEPA s.323A): **£50 or less per benefit**, not cash/voucher, not a reward for services, not contractual, with a **£300 annual cap** for a director of a close company (s.323B).
- **Pensions as the big lever.** The genuinely large tax-efficient extraction is an **employer pension contribution** (§11), not day-to-day expenses.

- **Statutory hooks:** **ITEPA 2003 ss.337 to 339** (travel, permanent vs temporary workplace), **s.339(5)/(6)** (24-month and 40% rule), **ss.338A / 339A** (T&S restriction for workers through intermediaries under SDC / inside IR35); **ITTOIA 2005 s.34** (wholly and exclusively, unincorporated); **ITEPA s.323A/s.323B** (trivial benefits); AMAP rates under ITEPA ss.229 to 236.
- **HMRC manual anchors:** **EIM32000+** (travel, temporary workplace, 24-month rule); **EIM32125+** (the 40% test); **EIM31200+** (mileage / AMAP); **EIM21864+** (trivial benefits); BIM37000+ (wholly and exclusively).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** explain the **24-month rule as expectation-based** (relief stops when the 24-month/40% expectation is formed, not at month 24). State plainly that **inside-IR35 home-to-client travel is generally not deductible**. Use **55p/25p** mileage tagged from 6 April 2026 (45p only for 2025/26 and earlier). Keep trivial benefits to the £50/£300 cliff edges. Point to **pensions** as the real lever. Cross-ref §11 (pensions), §1 (status, since T&S relief turns on it).

## 11. Pensions 2026/27: the contractor's biggest tax lever (locked, 2026-06-12)

**Position.** An **employer pension contribution from the PSC** is the most tax-efficient extraction route: it is **deductible against corporation tax** (on a paid basis, wholly and exclusively, FA 2004 s.196 / BIM46035), carries **no employer or employee NIC**, and is **not taxed on the director as income** when paid in, subject to the annual allowance. The **annual allowance is £60,000** (2026/27), measured across all contributions (employer + personal). It **tapers** where **threshold income exceeds £200,000 AND adjusted income exceeds £260,000**, reducing by £1 for every £2 of adjusted income above £260,000, down to a **minimum of £10,000**. **Unused annual allowance can be carried forward from the previous three tax years** (current year used first), which lets a contractor make a large one-off employer contribution (eg in a high-profit year or before a sale) without an annual-allowance charge. The **money purchase annual allowance (MPAA) is £10,000** and is triggered if the director has flexibly accessed a DC pension, after which carry-forward is lost for DC contributions.

- **Employer vs personal contributions.** A **personal** contribution is limited to **100% of relevant UK earnings** (and salary, not dividends, is relevant earnings), so a low-salary/high-dividend contractor has little personal-contribution headroom; the **employer (company) contribution** is **not** capped by the director's earnings (only by the annual allowance and the wholly-and-exclusively test), which is why the **employer route is usually preferred** for a PSC.
- **Statutory hooks:** **Finance Act 2004 Part 4** (annual allowance ss.227 to 228ZA; tapered AA s.228ZA; MPAA s.227B onward); **s.196** (employer contribution deductible, paid basis). https://www.gov.uk/tax-on-your-private-pension/annual-allowance
- **HMRC manual anchors:** **PTM050000+** (annual allowance); **PTM055000+ / PTM056000+** (carry-forward, tapered AA); **PTM057000+** (MPAA); BIM46035 (employer contribution deductibility).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** lead with "the **employer pension contribution** is the contractor's biggest tax-efficient lever". Use **£60,000 / £200,000 / £260,000 / £10,000 floor** tagged 2026/27. Stress the **employer contribution is not limited by the director's salary** (only the annual allowance + carry-forward), unlike a personal contribution. Flag the **MPAA** trap for anyone who has flexibly drawn a pension. Do NOT use the old £40,000 allowance or the pre-2023 £240,000 adjusted-income threshold. Cross-ref §8, §10.

## 12. Umbrella companies and the April 2026 reform (locked, 2026-06-12)

**Position.** An **umbrella company** employs the contractor under an **overarching contract of employment**, operates **PAYE and NIC** on the assignment income and pays the worker a salary; it is the standard route for **inside-IR35** or low-margin assignments where running a PSC is not worthwhile. The contractor's pay is the **assignment rate less the umbrella's deductions**, which legitimately include **employer NIC (15%), the Apprenticeship Levy and the umbrella's margin** (these are funded from the assignment rate, not from the worker's gross salary, and a compliant umbrella sets this out transparently). Umbrella workers are entitled to **holiday pay** (which must be paid, not unlawfully retained) and must receive a **Key Information Document (KID)** before starting, showing the rate, deductions and expected take-home.

- **The April 2026 change (state precisely).** From **6 April 2026**, responsibility for **accounting for PAYE and NIC** on umbrella-worker pay is reinforced by a new **joint and several liability (JSL)**: where a worker is supplied through an umbrella, the **recruitment agency that contracts with the end client** (or the **end client itself where there is no agency** in the chain) becomes **jointly and severally liable** with the umbrella for any PAYE/NIC the umbrella fails to remit. **The umbrella remains the legal employer** (the employment relationship does not move); what changes is that **HMRC can pursue the agency or end client** for unpaid umbrella PAYE. This is a **tax-compliance** measure aimed at the non-compliant umbrella market (estimated £1bn of avoidance), not full umbrella regulation (a separate regulatory regime is expected later). Enacted by **Finance Act 2026 s.24**, inserting **new Chapter 11 (ss.61Y to 61Z2) into ITEPA 2003 Part 2**, with parallel NIC powers via SSCBA 1992.
- **House stance.** The reform makes **agencies and end clients far more careful which umbrellas they use**, so contractors should expect tighter PSL (preferred-supplier-list) controls and should themselves use a **compliant umbrella** (FCSA/Professional Passport accredited, transparent KID, no inflated "expenses" or mini-umbrella/skimming schemes). Avoid any umbrella promising take-home rates that imply tax is not being properly deducted (a marker of a disguised-remuneration / tax-avoidance scheme, §17).
- **Statutory hooks:** **ITEPA 2003 Chapter 11, Part 2 (ss.61Y to 61Z2)**, inserted by **Finance Act 2026 (c. 11) s.24**, effective 6 April 2026; SSCBA 1992 (parallel NIC JSL powers); Conduct of Employment Agencies and Employment Businesses Regulations 2003 (Key Information Document). https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2026/11/section/24
- **HMRC guidance anchors:** https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/paye-changes-for-the-umbrella-company-market .
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** state the JSL change as **"the agency/end client becomes jointly and severally liable; the umbrella stays the employer"** (do NOT say PAYE responsibility "moves to" the agency in a way that suggests the umbrella stops being the employer). Tag it **6 April 2026, FA 2026 s.24 / ITEPA Chapter 11**. Explain that **employer NIC + margin + Apprenticeship Levy come out of the assignment rate** (so the umbrella "day rate" is not the PSC equivalent), and that **holiday pay must be paid** and a **KID provided**. Cross-ref §1 (inside IR35 drives umbrella use), §6 (employer NIC), §17.

## 13. Managed Service Company legislation, Chapter 9 ITEPA (locked, 2026-06-12)

**Position.** The **Managed Service Company (MSC) legislation** (**Chapter 9, Part 2 ITEPA 2003**, s.61A onward, in force from 6 April 2007) is **separate from IR35** and can be more dangerous because it does **not** depend on a status test. Where a company is an MSC, **all** payments to the worker are treated as **employment income subject to PAYE/NIC** (there is no "outside" finding to win), and unpaid PAYE can be **transferred as a personal debt** to the worker, the company's directors, the **MSC provider** and certain others (s.688A / the MSC debt-transfer regulations). A company is an **MSC** broadly where: (1) its business is **wholly or mainly providing the services of an individual**; (2) payments to the worker equal **most of the company's income**; (3) those payments exceed what PAYE would give; and (4) an **"MSC provider" is involved with the company** (promotes/facilitates the use of companies to provide individuals' services and is involved in running them).

- **The accountancy-services boundary (the key point for choosing an accountant).** A person is **not** an MSC provider **merely by providing legal or accountancy services in a professional capacity** (**ITEPA s.61B(3)**). The danger zone is where an "accountant" goes **beyond advice** into **influencing or controlling** the company's finances, the way the worker is paid, or the company's structure, or provides a **packaged, standardised "your-company-run-for-you" product**. A genuine **independent accountant who advises** the client (the client makes the decisions and could move freely) is on the safe side of the line.
- **Case-law / current status (grey area, treat with care).** The Court of Appeal confirmed the **two-stage MSC-provider test** in *Christianuyi Ltd v HMRC* [2019] EWCA Civ 474. HMRC has since pursued the well-known accountancy firms **Churchill Knight and Boox**, arguing their packaged products made them MSC providers; those **test cases are listed for First-tier Tribunal hearings in June 2026 and November 2026 and are not yet finally decided**, with appeals likely beyond. **House stance:** the outcome is **genuinely unresolved**, so pages must NOT state that a particular accountancy model is or is not caught; instead, explain the **risk factors** and why choosing an accountant who clearly stays on the **advice** side of s.61B(3) matters.
- **Statutory hooks:** **ITEPA 2003 Chapter 9, Part 2** (s.61A definition of MSC; **s.61B** MSC provider, **s.61B(3)** accountancy/legal carve-out; s.61D deemed employment payment); MSC debt-transfer in **s.688A** and the MSC Regulations 2007 (SI 2007/2053). https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2003/1
- **HMRC manual anchors:** **ESM3500+ / ESM3510+** (MSC legislation, MSC provider, the involvement test, debt transfer).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** frame MSC as a **separate, status-test-free** risk with **personal debt transfer**. State the **s.61B(3) accountancy carve-out** and the **advice-vs-running-it** boundary as the practical decision rule for choosing an accountant. On Churchill Knight / Boox, say the **test cases are live and undecided (2026 hearings)** and do NOT assert an outcome or label any current provider's model as caught/safe. Cross-ref §17. **[UNVERIFIED — reviewer check]:** confirm at HP-lock date that no FTT judgment has landed; if one has, update the status line.

## 14. Director's loans, s.455 and winding up the company (locked, 2026-06-12)

**Position.** A PSC is a **close company**, so an **overdrawn director's loan account** (drawings taken ahead of declared salary/dividend/profit) triggers a **section 455 charge**: the company pays CT at the **dividend upper rate** on the loan still outstanding **9 months and 1 day** after the period-end. That rate is **33.75% on loans made before 6 April 2026** and **35.75% on loans made on or after 6 April 2026** (it tracks the FA 2026 s.4 dividend upper rate). The charge is **temporary**: **s.458 relief** repays it once the loan is repaid/released/written off, but the relief is **deferred** to 9 months and 1 day after the end of the accounting period in which repayment occurs. A loan **over £10,000** at any point in the year is also a **beneficial-loan benefit in kind** unless interest is paid at the official rate. **Closing the company:** a solvent **Members' Voluntary Liquidation (MVL)** lets retained reserves be distributed as **capital** (potentially within **BADR**, §15), but the **TAAR (ITTOIA 2005 s.396B)** re-characterises the distribution as an **income** dividend where its four conditions are met, notably **Condition C** (the individual carries on the **same or a similar trade or activity within 2 years** of the distribution) combined with a **main-purpose** of tax avoidance.

- **The TAAR four conditions:** A: the individual held **at least 5%** of the company immediately before the winding up; B: the company was a **close company** at some point in the **2 years** to the winding up; C: within **2 years** of the distribution the individual **continues or is involved in the same or a similar trade/activity**; D: it is reasonable to assume a **main purpose** of the winding up was to **avoid or reduce income tax**. All four must be met for the TAAR to bite. The "phoenix" pattern (liquidate, take capital, restart the same contracting business) is the classic trigger.
- **Statutory hooks:** **CTA 2010 s.455** (charge), **s.458** (relief), **s.464A** onward (extension to benefits/arrangements); **ITEPA 2003 ss.173 to 191** (beneficial loans, £10,000 threshold, official rate); **ITTOIA 2005 s.396B** (winding-up TAAR). https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2010/4/section/455 ; https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2005/5/section/396B
- **HMRC manual anchors:** **CTM61500+ / CTM61505** (loans to participators, s.455/s.458); **CTM36300+ / CTM36305** (winding-up TAAR).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** state the **s.455 rate with its date band** (33.75% pre-6-Apr-2026, **35.75% on/after**), the **9-months-and-1-day** timing, and that s.458 relief is **deferred**, not instant. On winding up, set out the **four TAAR conditions** and stress the **2-year same/similar-trade** trap for a contractor who liquidates and then keeps contracting. Do NOT describe s.455 as a permanent tax. Do NOT present an MVL as a clean capital exit for a contractor who intends to keep working in the same field within 2 years. Cross-ref §15 (BADR), §7 (CT).

## 15. Capital gains on exit: BADR (locked, 2026-06-12)

**Position.** On a qualifying disposal (selling the company or, more commonly for a contractor, **winding it up via an MVL** and distributing reserves as capital, subject to the §14 TAAR), **Business Asset Disposal Relief (BADR)** can reduce the CGT rate on gains up to a **£1m lifetime limit per individual**. The BADR rate is **14% for disposals 6 April 2025 to 5 April 2026** and **18% for disposals on or after 6 April 2026** (so the **2026/27 rate is 18%**); the £1m lifetime limit is unchanged. Conditions held throughout the **2-year period to disposal**: the company is the individual's **personal company** (**5% of ordinary share capital and 5% of voting rights**, plus a 5% economic entitlement), the individual is an **officer or employee**, and the company is **trading**. Where BADR does not apply, the main CGT rates apply (18% basic-rate band / 24% above for most assets, tag the year as these have moved).

- **House stance.** For a contractor closing a company, **whether BADR is worth it depends on the reserve size, the £1m limit, the 18% rate (now the same as the standard CGT rate for many), and crucially the §14 TAAR** (if they intend to keep contracting in the same field within 2 years the distribution may be taxed as income, not capital, and BADR is irrelevant). Always model the **income-dividend alternative** against the capital/BADR route and check the TAAR first.
- **Statutory hooks:** **TCGA 1992 ss.169H to 169S** (BADR, £1m limit, conditions). https://www.gov.uk/business-asset-disposal-relief
- **HMRC manual anchors:** **CG63950+** (BADR); **CG64174** (the April-2025 and April-2026 rate steps; anti-forestalling on unconditional contracts).
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** state the BADR rate **with its date band** (14% to 5 Apr 2026, **18% from 6 Apr 2026**) and the **£1m lifetime limit**. Always pair BADR with the **§14 TAAR** check for a contractor who will keep working in the field. Do NOT state BADR is still 10% (or 14% for 2026/27). Cross-ref §14, §8.

## 16. Making Tax Digital for Income Tax (locked, 2026-06-12)

**Position.** **MTD for Income Tax** mandates digital record-keeping and **quarterly updates** for **sole traders and landlords** in Self Assessment, phased by **qualifying income** (gross self-employment + property income): **£50,000 from 6 April 2026**, **£30,000 from 6 April 2027**, **£20,000 from 6 April 2028**. **It does NOT apply to limited-company directors or to company profits** (MTD for IT is an income-tax measure; companies file CT, not SA). This matters for contractors because **most operate through a PSC and file under Self Assessment only for their salary and dividends**, which are **not** qualifying income for MTD, so a typical PSC contractor is **outside MTD for IT**. A **sole-trader / freelance** contractor with trading income over the threshold **is** in scope and must keep digital records and file quarterly.

- **Statutory hooks:** **Finance (No. 2) Act 2017 s.60 / Sch A1** (MTD framework) and the Income Tax (Digital Requirements) Regulations 2021 (SI 2021/1076) as amended (thresholds and commencement). https://www.gov.uk/guidance/check-when-to-sign-up-for-making-tax-digital-for-income-tax
- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** use **£50k/6-Apr-2026, £30k/6-Apr-2027, £20k/6-Apr-2028** exactly, with qualifying income = **gross trading + property** income tested on the prior year. State clearly that a **PSC contractor (salary + dividends) is generally OUTSIDE MTD for IT**, while a **sole-trader/freelancer over the threshold is IN**. Do NOT say MTD applies to company profits or to a director's dividend income. Cross-ref §1 (PSC vs sole trader), §8.

## 17. House stances on contractor grey areas (locked, 2026-06-12)

The firm's locked positions on the recurring judgement calls. Every page touching these must reflect the stance below; do not soften or contradict.

- **17.A CEST reliability.** CEST is a **useful first screen and audit document**, and HMRC will **stand behind a CEST result given accurate, working-practices-consistent inputs in line with the guidance and absent avoidance**, **but** it does **not bind a tribunal**, its **MOO treatment is narrower than the case law**, and a result that does not match the real working practices is worthless. **Never present a CEST "outside" as a guarantee.** Always pair it with a contract review and a working-practices check. (See §2.)
- **17.B Contract review value.** A professional **contract review is worthwhile but not sufficient**: it fixes the paperwork, but **HMRC and tribunals look at the actual working practices**, so a clean contract with employee-like working practices will not hold. **Working practices over contract wording** is the firm's consistent line. Recommend reviewing **both** the written terms and how the engagement actually operates.
- **17.C Inside-IR35 umbrella vs PSC.** For a **genuinely inside-IR35** engagement, an **umbrella is usually the simpler and often the more economic** route (no PSC running costs, no double layer of PAYE admin), while keeping a **PSC** makes sense where the contractor also holds **outside-IR35** work, wants to retain profits, or values the company for pensions/structure. **Do not present the PSC as always superior**; the inside/outside mix drives the answer, and from April 2026 the umbrella-JSL reform (§12) makes umbrella compliance, not structure, the bigger watch-item.
- **17.D Working practices over wording.** Stated as a standalone locked principle because it recurs: substitution clauses, control language and MOO drafting only help if they **reflect reality**. The firm never advises relying on contract drafting alone.
- **17.E Blanket determinations.** A client that issues a **blanket "inside IR35" determination** across a whole category of contractors **without assessing each engagement individually** is very likely **failing the reasonable-care requirement** (§3), which can invalidate the SDS and move the liability to the client. The firm's stance: blanket determinations are **bad practice and legally risky for the client**, and a contractor faced with one can use the **client-led disagreement process** (§3) to seek an individual assessment.
- **17.F Tax-avoidance schemes / "90%+ take-home" umbrellas.** Any arrangement promising take-home pay materially above a compliant umbrella or PSC (loan schemes, "advances", offshore/disguised-remuneration structures, mini-umbrella fraud) is a **tax-avoidance scheme**; the firm's stance is an **unequivocal do-not-use** and a warning that HMRC will pursue the worker for the tax (often years later, with interest and penalties). Point readers to HMRC's Spotlights and the "Tax avoidance: don't get caught out" campaign.
- **17.G MSC / choice of accountant.** Because the MSC rules carry **personal debt transfer** and the Churchill Knight / Boox litigation is **undecided** (§13), the firm's stance is to use an accountant who clearly **advises** (the client makes the decisions and could leave freely) rather than one selling a **packaged, standardised, finances-controlled "company run for you"** product. Frame this as **risk management**, not a claim about any named provider.

- **Practical writing rule for sessions:** when a page raises any of these, state the **stance plainly** and keep the hedges (CEST not binding; contract review not sufficient; umbrella-vs-PSC depends on the inside/outside mix; blanket determinations are risky; avoidance schemes are a hard no; MSC favours advice-not-product). Do NOT overclaim certainty in any of these grey areas.
