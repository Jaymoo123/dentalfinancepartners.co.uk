<h2>The Problem with Most Sole Trader vs Limited Company Calculators</h2>

<p>If you search for a "sole trader vs limited company tax calculator" online, you will find dozens of them. Most compare basic income tax and National Insurance rates. A few factor in corporation tax and dividend tax.</p>

<p>Almost none of them account for a company car.</p>

<p>That is a problem if you drive a vehicle for your business. The tax treatment of a car differs radically between sole trader and limited company structures. A calculator that ignores benefit-in-kind (BIK) and fuel scale charges will give you a misleading answer. Potentially an expensive one.</p>

<p>In our experience working with business owners, we see structure decisions based on incomplete comparisons. This article fixes that. We will walk through the real numbers for a business with a company car, comparing sole trader and limited company structures side by side.</p>

<p>If you want a personalised calculation, our <a href="/calculators">tax calculators</a> can help, but read this first to understand what matters.</p>

<h2>The Core Difference: How Cars Are Taxed</h2>

<p>Under a sole trader structure, your vehicle is a business asset. You claim capital allowances on the purchase price and deduct running costs (fuel, insurance, repairs) as allowable expenses. There is no personal benefit charge because you are the business. The line between business and personal use is drawn through the capital allowance and expense claims, not through a separate tax charge.</p>

<p>Under a limited company, the car is owned by the company. If you have any personal use (including commuting), HMRC treats that as a benefit-in-kind. You pay income tax on the BIK value. Your company pays Class 1A National Insurance on it at 15% (from 6 April 2025). The company can claim capital allowances and deduct running costs, but the BIK charge is a real cost that must be factored into any comparison.</p>

<p>This is where most "sole trader vs limited company tax calculator" tools fall down. They compare the headline tax rates but ignore the BIK liability entirely.</p>

<h3>How Fuel Complicates Things Further</h3>

<p>If your company pays for fuel for personal journeys (including commuting), there is an additional fuel scale charge. This is calculated using the same BIK percentage as the car itself, applied to a fixed figure (currently £27,800 for 2025/26).</p>

<p>You can avoid the fuel scale charge by reimbursing your company for personal fuel at HMRC's advisory fuel rates. But many directors do not do this, and the charge adds up quickly.</p>

<h2>Worked Example: A Real Comparison</h2>

<p>Let us take a concrete scenario. A business owner in Bristol, trading through a consultancy, has taxable profits of £80,000 before any car costs. They want to buy a new car for the business. The car is a plug-in hybrid (PHEV) with CO2 emissions of 42g/km and an electric range of 40 miles. It costs £45,000.</p>

<p>We will compare the total tax and NI cost under both structures. All figures are for 2025/26.</p>

<h3>Sole Trader Structure</h3>

<p>As a sole trader, you can claim capital allowances on the car. A 42g/km PHEV enters the main rate writing-down allowance (WDA) pool. The 100% first-year allowance for cars with CO2 emissions of 1-50g/km was abolished from 6 April 2021; only zero-emission (0g/km) vehicles retain a specific FYA. Year-one WDA at 18% (2025/26 rate; reducing to 14% from 6 April 2026 per FA 2026 s.28): £45,000 × 18% = £8,100. Because 30% of use is personal, you restrict the allowance to the business proportion: £8,100 × 70% = £5,670.</p>

<p>Running costs (fuel, insurance, servicing) are fully deductible as business expenses. You apportion between business and personal use. For this example, assume 70% business use and 30% personal use. You claim 70% of running costs.</p>

<p>Your taxable profit after the car allowance: £80,000 minus £5,670 (WDA) = £74,330. You also claim 70% of running costs. Assume £4,000 total running costs, so you claim £2,800. Adjusted profit is £71,530.</p>

<p>Tax calculation for a sole trader (2025/26):</p>
<ul>
<li>Personal allowance: £12,570 (taxed at 0%)</li>
<li>Basic rate band: £12,571 to £50,270 (taxed at 20%)</li>
<li>Higher rate: £50,271 to £125,140 (taxed at 40%)</li>
</ul>
<p>Your profit of £71,530 spans both bands. Income tax = (£50,270 minus £12,570) × 20% + (£71,530 minus £50,270) × 40% = £7,540 + £8,504 = £16,044.</p>
<p>Class 4 National Insurance (2025/26): 6% on profits between £12,570 and £50,270, then 2% above that. On £71,530 that is (£50,270 minus £12,570) × 6% + (£71,530 minus £50,270) × 2% = £2,262 + £425.20 = £2,687.20.</p>
<p>Class 2 National Insurance was abolished from 6 April 2024. No charge applies.</p>
<p>Total tax and NI: £16,044 + £2,687.20 = <strong>£18,731.20</strong>.</p>
<p>You have no BIK charge. You have no employer NI. Simple.</p>

<h3>Limited Company Structure</h3>

<p>As a limited company, the company buys the car for £45,000. The same WDA rules apply: the PHEV enters the main rate pool at 18% WDA (2025/26 rate; reducing to 14% from 1 April 2026 per FA 2026 s.28). Unlike a sole trader, there is no private use restriction on the company's capital allowance for a car; the private use is separately accounted for through the BIK charge. Year-one WDA: £45,000 × 18% = £8,100.</p>
<p>Company profits before car: £80,000. After WDA: £80,000 minus £8,100 = £71,900.</p>
<p>Corporation tax on £71,900 falls within the marginal relief band (between £50,000 and £250,000). CT = 25% × £71,900 minus (£250,000 minus £71,900) × 3/200 = £17,975 minus £2,671.50 = £15,303.50 (effective rate 21.3%).</p>
<p>But now we add the BIK charge. The car's P11D value is £45,000. The BIK percentage for a 42g/km PHEV with 40-mile electric range is 12% for 2025/26. So the BIK value is £45,000 × 12% = £5,400.</p>
<p>You pay income tax on that £5,400 at your marginal rate. Assume you take a salary of £12,570 (no tax or NI) and dividends up to the basic rate band. Your total income from the company is salary plus dividends. If you take dividends of £37,700 (filling the basic rate band), your total income is £50,270. The BIK of £5,400 is added on top. That pushes £5,400 into the higher rate band (40%).</p>
<p>Income tax on BIK: £5,400 × 40% = £2,160.</p>
<p>Your company pays Class 1A NI on the BIK at 15% (from 6 April 2025). That is £5,400 × 15% = £810.</p>
<p>If the company also pays for fuel for personal journeys, you have a fuel scale charge. The fixed figure is £27,800. The BIK percentage (12%) applies. Fuel scale charge = £27,800 × 12% = £3,336. You pay income tax on that too: £3,336 × 40% = £1,334.40. Company pays Class 1A NI: £3,336 × 15% = £500.40.</p>
<p>Total company costs: corporation tax £15,303.50 + Class 1A NI on BIK £810 + Class 1A NI on fuel £500.40 = £16,613.90.</p>
<p>Total personal costs: income tax on BIK £2,160 + income tax on fuel £1,334.40 = £3,494.40.</p>
<p>Combined total: £16,613.90 + £3,494.40 = <strong>£20,108.30</strong>.</p>

<h3>The Comparison</h3>
<p>Sole trader total: £18,731.20</p>
<p>Limited company total: £20,108.30</p>
<p>The limited company structure costs £1,377.10 more in this scenario.</p>
<p>That is still a meaningful difference. And it is driven almost entirely by the BIK and fuel scale charges that most "sole trader vs limited company tax calculator" tools ignore. Both structures now carry similar capital allowance relief on the car; the limited company's disadvantage comes from the Class 1A NI and the personal income tax on the benefit.</p>

<h2>When Does a Limited Company Still Win?</h2>
<p>Our example shows that when both structures use 18% WDA on the car, capital allowances no longer strongly favour one side. The remaining disadvantage for the limited company is the BIK and Class 1A NI cost. If you buy a car with CO2 emissions above 160g/km, the car goes into the special rate pool at 6% WDA (far slower relief), adding a further capital allowance disadvantage under both structures while the limited company still bears the BIK charge on top.</p>
<p>Also, the example uses a relatively modest profit of £80,000. At higher profit levels, the limited company structure's ability to retain profits within the company (taxed at 19% to 25% rather than 40% or 45%) can outweigh the BIK cost. Especially if you choose a low-emission car with a low BIK percentage.</p>
<p>For example, a fully electric car (0g/km CO2) has a BIK percentage of 3% for 2025/26 (rising to 4% for 2026/27). On a £45,000 car, that is a BIK of £1,350. The income tax cost is much lower. The fuel scale charge for electric cars is also 3% applied to the £27,800 fixed figure, giving £834. That changes the maths dramatically.</p>
<p>And if you do not need the car for business at all, the comparison flips. A sole trader cannot claim any costs for a car used only personally. A limited company director can still have a company car, pay the BIK, and the company gets the capital allowances and running cost deductions. That can be tax-efficient if structured properly.</p>

<h2>What About VAT?</h2>
<p>VAT adds another layer. If your company is VAT registered, you can reclaim 50% of the VAT on the purchase of a car used for both business and personal purposes (the other 50% is blocked). You can reclaim 100% of VAT on business-only mileage for fuel if you keep detailed mileage logs. A sole trader can do the same.</p>
<p>But if you use the flat rate VAT scheme as a limited company, the VAT position changes again. The flat rate percentage varies by sector, and the limited cost trader rules add complexity. This is worth discussing with your accountant, as it can shift the comparison.</p>

<h2>How to Use a Sole Trader vs Limited Company Tax Calculator Properly</h2>
<p>If you use an online <a href="/fundamentals">sole trader vs limited company tax calculator</a>, follow these steps to get a realistic answer:</p>
<ol>
<li>Input your true profit before any car costs.</li>
<li>Add the car's P11D value and CO2 emissions.</li>
<li>Check the BIK percentage for your car (HMRC publishes these annually).</li>
<li>Estimate your personal fuel costs and whether the company will pay for them.</li>
<li>Factor in the fuel scale charge if applicable.</li>
<li>Include Class 1A NI on the BIK and fuel charge.</li>
<li>Compare the total tax bill, not just the headline rates.</li>
</ol>

<h2>Other Factors Beyond Tax</h2>
<p>Tax is not the only consideration. A limited company offers limited liability protection. If you have a car accident while driving for business, your personal assets are protected. A sole trader has unlimited personal liability.</p>
<p>Directors' loan accounts, pension contributions, and the ability to bring in a spouse as a shareholder (using alphabet shares) are all easier with a limited company. These can tip the balance even if the tax comparison is close.</p>
<p>On the other hand, a limited company has more compliance overhead. You must file annual accounts at Companies House, submit a corporation tax return (CT600), and manage payroll if you take a salary. The <a href="/blog/director-pay-and-dividends">director pay and dividends</a> structure needs careful planning.</p>

<h2>Summary: What the Numbers Say</h2>
<p>For a business with a company car, the "sole trader vs limited company tax calculator" comparison is not simple. The BIK and fuel scale charges can add thousands to the limited company tax bill. In our worked example, the limited company cost around £1,377 more than the sole trader structure, and that gap widens if the company pays for personal fuel too.</p>
<p>But that is not always the case. Low-emission cars, high profit levels, and specific personal circumstances can flip the result. The key is to use a calculator that includes all the relevant factors, not just the basic rates.</p>
