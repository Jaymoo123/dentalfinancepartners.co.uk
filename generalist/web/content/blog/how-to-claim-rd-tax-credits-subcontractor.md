<p>If you subcontract parts of your research and development work to third parties, you can still claim R&D tax credits on those costs. But the rules are different from when you use in-house staff. HMRC restricts the proportion of subcontractor costs you can include in your claim, and the restriction depends on whether the subcontractor is connected to your company.</p>

<p>Many business owners assume subcontractor costs are treated the same as direct employee costs. They are not. Get the classification wrong and you risk a full HMRC enquiry, repayment demands, and penalties. Get it right and you can claim legitimate relief on significant R&D spend.</p>

<p>This article covers how to claim R&D tax credits for subcontracted work under the current merged R&D scheme (from 1 April 2024) and the pre-April 2024 rules where relevant. We also cover the connected party rules, the 65% cap, and what records you need.</p>

<h2>What Qualifies as Subcontracted R&D Work</h2>

<p>Subcontracted R&D means you engage another party to carry out R&D activities on your behalf. You remain the company that is seeking to resolve the scientific or technological uncertainty. The subcontractor performs some or all of the work.</p>

<p>Common examples include:</p>

<ul>
<li>A software consultancy in Manchester's Northern Quarter building a prototype algorithm for your fintech product</li>
<li>A specialist engineering firm in Birmingham's Jewellery Quarter developing a new manufacturing process for your hardware company</li>
<li>A materials science lab in Bristol's Harbourside testing new composite formulations for your construction business</li>
<li>A freelance data scientist in Shoreditch building a machine learning model for your SaaS platform</li>
</ul>

<p>HMRC distinguishes between two categories of subcontractor: connected and unconnected. The rules for each are different.</p>

<h2>The 65% Cap on Unconnected Subcontractor Costs</h2>

<p>If your subcontractor is <strong>not</strong> connected to your company (meaning they are an independent third party with no ownership or control links), you can include only <strong>65% of the relevant costs</strong> in your R&D claim.</p>

<p>This applies to the merged R&D scheme for accounting periods starting on or after 1 April 2024. It also applied to the old SME scheme before that date.</p>

<p>Here is how it works with real numbers. Say a 4-employee software consultancy in Leeds turns over £420,000. They subcontract a specialist AI developer (unconnected) for £80,000 of work on a new recommendation engine. The qualifying R&D subcontractor cost is 65% of £80,000, which is £52,000. The remaining £28,000 is not claimable.</p>

<p>The 65% cap exists because HMRC assumes the subcontractor has their own overheads and profit margin built into their fees. The cap strips out that element, leaving only the direct R&D activity cost.</p>

<p>There is no cap on connected subcontractor costs. But the rules are stricter in other ways.</p>

<h2>Connected Subcontractors: No Cap but Different Rules</h2>

<p>A connected subcontractor is one where your company controls them, they control you, or both are under common control. For a limited company, control usually means holding more than 50% of the shares or voting rights.</p>

<p>If your subcontractor is connected, you can include <strong>100% of the qualifying costs</strong> you pay them. But the subcontractor cannot also claim R&D relief on the same work. HMRC prevents double counting.</p>

<p>This is common in group structures. A parent company might subcontract R&D work to a wholly owned subsidiary. The subsidiary performs the work and charges the parent. The parent claims R&D tax credits on the full amount paid. The subsidiary does not claim anything for that work.</p>

<p>The connected party rules also apply to individuals who are connected to your company. For example, a director who provides R&D services through their own personal service company. If the director controls both companies, the 100% rule applies. If they are unconnected, the 65% cap applies.</p>

<h2>What Costs Qualify Under Subcontracted R&D</h2>

<p>Not every cost in a subcontractor's invoice qualifies. HMRC looks at the substance of the work, not the label on the invoice.</p>

<p>Qualifying costs include:</p>

<ul>
<li>Direct staff costs of the subcontractor's employees working on the R&D project (salary, employer NI, pension contributions)</li>
<li>Consumable items used directly in the R&D work (materials, software licenses for the project)</li>
<li>Subcontracted costs paid to third parties by the subcontractor (but subject to the same 65% cap further down the chain)</li>
</ul>

<p>Non-qualifying costs include:</p>

<ul>
<li>General overheads and admin costs</li>
<li>Profit margin built into the subcontractor's fee</li>
<li>Capital expenditure (though capital allowances may be available separately)</li>
<li>Costs that are not directly attributable to the R&D project</li>
</ul>

<p>For unconnected subcontractors, the 65% cap is designed to automatically exclude the non-qualifying elements. You do not need to itemise the subcontractor's internal cost breakdown. You simply take 65% of the total relevant payment.</p>

<p>For connected subcontractors, you need to identify the actual qualifying costs incurred by the subcontractor. You cannot claim on the full invoice amount if it includes profit, overheads, or non-R&D costs.</p>

<h2>How to Claim R&D Tax Credits for Subcontractor Costs</h2>

<p>The claim process follows the same general route as any R&D tax credit claim. You include the qualifying costs in your corporation tax return (CT600) and submit a separate R&D claim report.</p>

<p>For accounting periods starting on or after 1 April 2024, the merged R&D scheme applies. You claim under either the enhanced R&D Intensive Scheme (ERIS) if you are an R&D-intensive loss-making SME, or the standard merged scheme for all other companies.</p>

<p>The key steps are:</p>

<ol>
<li><strong>Identify the R&D projects</strong> that meet HMRC's definition: work to resolve scientific or technological uncertainty, not routine development or improvement.</li>
<li><strong>Separate subcontractor costs</strong> from in-house costs. Track them separately in your accounting records.</li>
<li><strong>Determine connected or unconnected status</strong> for each subcontractor. Document the relationship.</li>
<li><strong>Calculate qualifying costs</strong> using the correct percentage (65% for unconnected, 100% of actual qualifying costs for connected).</li>
<li><strong>Prepare the R&D claim report</strong> with detailed narratives of each project, the technical uncertainties, and the costs incurred.</li>
<li><strong>Submit the claim</strong> with your CT600 within the normal filing deadline (12 months after the end of the accounting period for the merged scheme).</li>
</ol>

<p>We strongly recommend using specialist R&D software or working with an adviser who understands the subcontractor rules. HMRC has increased scrutiny of R&D claims, and subcontractor costs are a common trigger for enquiries.</p>

<h2>Common Mistakes with Subcontracted R&D Claims</h2>

<p>Here are the errors we see most often from UK business owners who attempt to claim without proper advice:</p>

<p><strong>Claiming 100% of unconnected subcontractor costs.</strong> This is the most common mistake. The 65% cap applies regardless of how detailed the invoice is. Even if the subcontractor itemises their staff costs, you still apply the cap.</p>

<p><strong>Claiming on connected subcontractor costs without identifying actual qualifying costs.</strong> You cannot simply take the full invoice amount. You must identify the subcontractor's direct R&D staff costs, consumables, and qualifying subcontracted costs (with their own 65% cap).</p>

<p><strong>Including costs that are not R&D.</strong> Just because a subcontractor works on a project does not mean all their costs qualify. Routine testing, standard software development, or non-technical work does not count.</p>

<p><strong>Failing to document the connected party relationship.</strong> HMRC will ask. If you claim 100% of connected subcontractor costs and cannot demonstrate the control relationship, your claim will be rejected.</p>

<p><strong>Double counting.</strong> If the subcontractor also claims R&D relief on the same work, both claims will be rejected. Ensure your subcontractor confirms they are not claiming.</p>

<h2>Record Keeping Requirements</h2>

<p>HMRC expects you to maintain contemporaneous records of your R&D activity. For subcontractor costs, you need:</p>

<ul>
<li>Contracts or engagement letters with each subcontractor</li>
<li>Invoices showing the work performed and costs incurred</li>
<li>Technical documentation of the R&D projects (design documents, test results, meeting notes, emails)</li>
<li>Evidence of the subcontractor's connected or unconnected status</li>
<li>Project time records if the subcontractor charges on a time basis</li>
</ul>

<p>If you use accounting software like Xero or FreeAgent, tag subcontractor costs to a specific R&D project code. This makes extracting the data for your claim much easier.</p>

<h2>Example: Subcontracted R&D Claim for a Bristol Engineering Firm</h2>

<p>A structural engineering company in Bristol's Harbourside employs 12 staff and turns over £1.2 million. They develop a new lightweight composite material for bridge construction. They subcontract specialist testing to an unconnected lab in Sheffield for £45,000. They also subcontract design work to a connected subsidiary company for £60,000.</p>

<p>For the unconnected lab: qualifying cost is 65% of £45,000 = £29,250.</p>

<p>For the connected subsidiary: the subsidiary's actual qualifying costs (staff, consumables, subcontracted costs) total £42,000. The parent claims £42,000 (not £60,000).</p>

<p>Total subcontracted R&D qualifying costs: £29,250 + £42,000 = £71,250. This is added to the company's in-house R&D costs (staff, consumables, software) to calculate the total R&D claim.</p>

<p>Under the merged scheme, the company would receive a payable credit (if loss-making) or a reduction in corporation tax (if profitable). The exact benefit depends on the company's tax position.</p>

<h2>What Happens If HMRC Opens an Enquiry</h2>

<p>HMRC has increased the number of R&D enquiries significantly since 2023. Subcontractor costs are a frequent focus because the rules are misunderstood.</p>

<p>If HMRC opens an enquiry into your claim, they will ask for:</p>

<ul>
<li>Detailed project narratives for each R&D project</li>
<li>Breakdown of costs by category (staff, consumables, subcontractors)</li>
<li>Evidence of the subcontractor relationship (connected or unconnected)</li>
<li>Supporting invoices and contracts</li>
<li>Technical evidence that the work involved scientific or technological uncertainty</li>
</ul>

<p>If your claim is incorrect, HMRC can withdraw the relief, charge interest, and impose penalties. In serious cases, they can open a criminal investigation for fraudulent claims.</p>

<p>The best defence is a well-documented claim prepared by someone who understands the rules. If you are unsure about any aspect of your subcontractor costs, speak to an adviser before submitting the claim.</p>
