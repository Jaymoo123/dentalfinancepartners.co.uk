<p>If you run a small limited company in the UK, you have a legal duty to file annual accounts at Companies House. Most small companies qualify as "small" under the Companies Act 2006 and can file abbreviated accounts. These are shorter, less detailed versions of your full statutory accounts. But the starting point for both abbreviated and full accounts is the same: the trial balance.</p>

<p>Getting the trial balance right matters because it is the foundation document. If the trial balance is wrong, every figure in your abbreviated accounts will be wrong too. HMRC and Companies House may not check every filing immediately, but errors compound over time and can trigger enquiries, penalties, or restatements.</p>

<p>This guide walks through what a trial balance is, how abbreviated accounts differ from full accounts, and the exact steps to prepare a trial balance that supports a clean abbreviated filing. We will use a worked example throughout so you can see the numbers in action.</p>

<h2>What Is a Trial Balance?</h2>

<p>A trial balance is a list of every nominal ledger account in your business, showing the balance at a specific date. Every debit and every credit. The total of all debits must equal the total of all credits. If they do not match, something is wrong in the bookkeeping.</p>

<p>Think of it as the raw data dump from your accounting software. Xero, FreeAgent, QuickBooks, Sage 50 - they all generate a trial balance report. You can run it at any time, but for year-end accounts you need it at your company's accounting reference date.</p>

<p>The trial balance is not the same as the balance sheet or profit and loss account. It contains every account, including control accounts, suspense accounts, and temporary accounts that get closed off before the final accounts are produced. It is the working document that your accountant uses to make adjustments, post accruals and prepayments, and then produce the statutory accounts.</p>

<h2>What Are Abbreviated Accounts?</h2>

<p>Abbreviated accounts are a simplified version of the full statutory accounts that small companies can file at Companies House. The key difference is what gets disclosed publicly.</p>

<p>For abbreviated accounts, you typically file:</p>

<ul>
<li>An abbreviated balance sheet (with reduced notes)</li>
<li>No profit and loss account (it stays private)</li>
<li>No directors' report (unless you choose to include one)</li>
</ul>

<p>The full accounts - including the profit and loss, detailed notes, and directors' report - still need to be prepared for the shareholders and for HMRC. But the public version is the abbreviated set. This keeps your turnover, cost of sales, gross profit, and net profit figures off the public record.</p>

<p>To qualify, your company must meet two of three criteria: turnover not more than £10.2 million, balance sheet total not more than £5.1 million, and average employees not more than 50. Most small Ltds qualify.</p>

<h2>Why the Trial Balance Matters for Abbreviated Accounts</h2>

<p>The trial balance is where your abbreviated accounts start. Your accountant takes the trial balance, makes year-end adjustments (depreciation, accruals, prepayments, stock, debtors, creditors), and then produces the balance sheet and profit and loss. From those, the abbreviated accounts are extracted.</p>

<p>If the trial balance is incomplete or unbalanced, the abbreviated accounts will be wrong. A common error is forgetting to include director's loan account transactions, or missing accrued expenses that were paid after the year-end. These small omissions can make the balance sheet misstate the company's position.</p>

<p>Because abbreviated accounts strip out the profit and loss detail, the balance sheet must be accurate. The trial balance for abbreviated accounts needs particular care around the items that appear on the balance sheet: debtors, creditors, cash, fixed assets, share capital, reserves, and the profit and loss reserve.</p>

<h2>Step-by-Step: Preparing the Trial Balance</h2>

<h3>Step 1: Complete All Bookkeeping to the Year-End</h3>

<p>Before you can run a trial balance, every transaction up to the accounting reference date must be entered. That means all sales invoices, purchase invoices, bank receipts, bank payments, credit card transactions, petty cash, and payroll journals.</p>

<p>Missing a single supplier invoice can throw off your creditors figure. Missing a customer receipt can understate your debtors. The trial balance is only as good as the data you put in.</p>

<p>For a typical small Ltd turning over £150,000 with 2-3 employees, this might mean 200-400 transactions per month. If you use cloud accounting software with bank feeds, most of this is automated. But you still need to code each transaction to the correct nominal account.</p>

<h3>Step 2: Reconcile All Control Accounts</h3>

<p>Reconciliation is non-negotiable. Reconcile your bank account to the bank statement. Reconcile your VAT control account to your VAT return. Reconcile your PAYE control account to your RTI submissions. Reconcile your director's loan account to the actual money owed to or by the director.</p>

<p>A common trap: the bank reconciliation shows a difference of £47 because of a bank charge you forgot to enter. That £47 sits in the suspense account. If you file abbreviated accounts with a suspense balance, the balance sheet will not balance properly and Companies House will reject the filing.</p>

<p>Clear all suspense accounts before running the trial balance. Every item should have a proper nominal code.</p>

<h3>Step 3: Run the Trial Balance Report</h3>

<p>In your accounting software, run the trial balance report for the period ending on your company's year-end date. Most software lets you choose a date range. Use the exact date, not a range that includes post-year-end transactions.</p>

<p>The report will show every nominal account with a balance. Debits on the left, credits on the right. The totals must match.</p>

<p>If they do not match, go back to step 2. The most common causes of an unbalanced trial balance are:</p>

<ul>
<li>A transaction entered as a debit when it should be a credit</li>
<li>A journal posted with only one side</li>
<li>A payment coded to the wrong account type</li>
<li>Opening balances not carried forward correctly from the previous year</li>
</ul>

<h3>Step 4: Post Year-End Adjustments</h3>

<p>The raw trial balance from your software is rarely the final version. You need to post adjusting journals for:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Depreciation</strong> on fixed assets (typically 18% reducing balance for plant and machinery, 6% for long-life assets, or straight-line over useful life)</li>
<li><strong>Accruals</strong> for expenses incurred but not yet paid (accountant's fees, utility bills, interest)</li>
<li><strong>Prepayments</strong> for expenses paid in advance (insurance, software subscriptions, rent)</li>
<li><strong>Stock and work in progress</strong> if your business holds inventory</li>
<li><strong>Bad debts</strong> if you have customers who are unlikely to pay</li>
<li><strong>Directors' loan account</strong> adjustments for dividends voted but not yet paid, or expenses paid personally</li>
</ul>

<p>Each adjustment must be posted as a journal with equal debits and credits. After posting, re-run the trial balance. It should still balance, but now with the adjusted figures.</p>

<h3>Step 5: Extract the Balance Sheet Figures</h3>

<p>For abbreviated accounts, you only need the balance sheet. Take the adjusted trial balance and identify which accounts go onto the balance sheet:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Fixed assets:</strong> cost minus accumulated depreciation</li>
<li><strong>Current assets:</strong> debtors, cash, prepayments, stock</li>
<li><strong>Creditors due within one year:</strong> trade creditors, accruals, VAT, PAYE, director's loan (if owed back to the company)</li>
<li><strong>Creditors due after one year:</strong> bank loans, hire purchase, director's loan (long-term)</li>
<li><strong>Capital and reserves:</strong> share capital, profit and loss reserve, other reserves</li>
</ul>

<p>The total of assets must equal total liabilities plus equity. This is the fundamental accounting equation. If it does not balance, the trial balance is wrong.</p>

<h2>Worked Example: A Small Ltd Filing Abbreviated Accounts</h2>

<p>Let us use a concrete example. ABC Consulting Ltd is a small limited company based in Manchester's Northern Quarter. It turns over £180,000, has two directors and no employees, and its year-end is 31 March 2025.</p>

<p>The raw trial balance at 31 March 2025 shows the following key balances:</p>

<ul>
<li>Bank: £24,500 debit</li>
<li>Trade debtors: £18,200 debit</li>
<li>Trade creditors: £6,800 credit</li>
<li>Director's loan account (owed by director): £4,200 debit</li>
<li>Share capital: £100 credit</li>
<li>Profit and loss reserve (brought forward): £32,400 credit</li>
<li>Sales: £180,000 credit</li>
<li>Cost of sales: £72,000 debit</li>
<li>Overheads: £58,000 debit</li>
<li>Corporation tax (paid): £9,500 debit</li>
<li>VAT control: £3,200 credit</li>
</ul>

<p>The trial balance totals: debits £186,400, credits £186,400. It balances.</p>

<p>Now the year-end adjustments:</p>

<ul>
<li>Depreciation on computer equipment: £1,200 debit to depreciation, £1,200 credit to accumulated depreciation</li>
<li>Accrued accountant's fee: £2,400 debit to professional fees, £2,400 credit to accruals</li>
<li>Prepaid insurance: £800 debit to prepayments, £800 credit to insurance</li>
<li>Corporation tax charge for the year: estimated at £14,720. Debit to tax charge, credit to corporation tax creditor</li>
</ul>

<p>After posting these, the adjusted trial balance still balances. The balance sheet for abbreviated accounts would show:</p>

<ul>
<li>Fixed assets (net book value): say £4,800</li>
<li>Current assets: bank £24,500, debtors £18,200, prepayments £800, director's loan £4,200 = £47,700</li>
<li>Creditors within one year: trade creditors £6,800, accruals £2,400, VAT £3,200, corporation tax £14,720 = £27,120</li>
<li>Net current assets: £47,700 minus £27,120 = £20,580</li>
<li>Total assets less current liabilities: £4,800 plus £20,580 = £25,380</li>
<li>Capital and reserves: share capital £100, profit and loss reserve (brought forward £32,400 plus retained profit for year £14,720 minus dividends?) - we need to factor dividends</li>
</ul>

<p>Assume the directors voted a dividend of £20,000 during the year. That reduces retained earnings. The final profit and loss reserve might be £32,400 plus £14,720 profit minus £20,000 dividends = £27,120. Total equity = £27,220. The balance sheet balances: assets £52,500, liabilities £27,120, equity £27,220. Total £52,500 each side.</p>

<p>The abbreviated accounts would show the balance sheet with these figures and minimal notes. The profit and loss account stays private.</p>

<h2>Common Mistakes When Preparing a Trial Balance for Abbreviated Accounts</h2>

<h3>Mistake 1: Including Post-Year-End Transactions</h3>

<p>Do not enter transactions dated after the year-end into the trial balance period. A payment made on 10 April should not appear in the 31 March trial balance. It distorts debtors and creditors. Use accruals and prepayments instead.</p>

<h3>Mistake 2: Forgetting the Director's Loan Account</h3>

<p>This is the most common error we see. Directors pay business expenses from their personal account, then forget to record the repayment or the expense. The director's loan account becomes a mess. For abbreviated accounts, the balance sheet must show the correct amount owed by or to the director. If it is wrong, the balance sheet is wrong.</p>

<h3>Mistake 3: Not Clearing Suspense Accounts</h3>

<p>A suspense account balance on the trial balance means something is miscoded. You cannot file abbreviated accounts with a suspense balance. It will not make sense on the balance sheet and Companies House may reject the filing.</p>

<h3>Mistake 4: Misclassifying Items Between Current and Non-Current</h3>

<p>Abbreviated accounts require a split between creditors due within one year and those due after more than one year. If you have a director's loan that is repayable in 18 months, it goes under "after more than one year". If it is repayable on demand, it goes under "within one year". Getting this wrong misstates the company's liquidity position.</p>

<h3>Mistake 5: Forgetting to Account for Dividends</h3>

<p>Dividends voted but not yet paid at the year-end are a liability. They reduce retained earnings and create a creditor (usually within the director's loan account or a dividends payable account). If you forget to post the dividend journal, the profit and loss reserve will be overstated and the balance sheet will not balance.</p>

<h2>Abbreviated Accounts vs Full Accounts: What Changes?</h2>

<p>If you prepare a full set of statutory accounts, you produce a profit and loss account, balance sheet, notes, and directors' report. For abbreviated accounts, you strip out the profit and loss and most of the notes.</p>

<p>The trial balance itself does not change. It is the same document. What changes is what you disclose publicly. The trial balance for abbreviated accounts still needs all the same adjustments. You just do not publish the detail.</p>

<p>This means you cannot take shortcuts on the trial balance just because you are filing abbreviated accounts. HMRC still receives the full accounts (through the corporation tax return, CT600, and supporting computations). If the trial balance is wrong, your CT600 will be wrong too, and HMRC will eventually notice.</p>

<h2>Filing the Abbreviated Accounts at Companies House</h2>

<p>Once the trial balance is adjusted and the abbreviated balance sheet is prepared, you file online through the Companies House WebFiling service or through your accountant's software. The filing fee is £13 for online filing (or £33 for paper).</p>

<p>The deadline is 9 months after your accounting reference date. For a 31 March year-end, that means filing by 31 December. Miss it and the late filing penalties start at £150 for a private company, rising to £1,500 if you are more than 6 months late.</p>

<p>Your abbreviated accounts must include:</p>

<ul>
<li>A balance sheet signed by a director</li>
<li>The director's name and signature</li>
<li>The date of approval</li>
<li>The statement that the company was entitled to exemption from audit (if applicable)</li>
</ul>

<p>If your company is also exempt from audit (most small companies are), you include a statement on the balance sheet confirming that exemption. The abbreviated accounts do not need an audit report, but they must still be true and fair to the best of the directors' knowledge.</p>
