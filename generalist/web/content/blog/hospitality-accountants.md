<h2>What Makes Hospitality Accounting Different?</h2>

<p>Hospitality businesses operate on a cash-intensive, high-volume, low-margin model. Your accounting needs to reflect that reality. Here are the key areas where hospitality accounting diverges from a standard service business or retailer.</p>

<h3>VAT: The Biggest Trap for the Unwary</h3>

<p>VAT in hospitality is not straightforward. The standard rate is 20%, but there are exceptions that catch business owners out regularly.</p>

<p>Food sold for consumption on the premises is standard-rated at 20%. But takeaway food (cold takeaway items, for example) is zero-rated. Hot takeaway food is standard-rated. Confused yet? So are many accountants who do not specialise in hospitality.</p>

<p>Alcoholic drinks are standard-rated. Soft drinks are standard-rated. But some catering supplies to schools or hospitals may be zero-rated. The point is this: if your <strong>accountant hospitality</strong> specialist does not know the difference between a pasty eaten in your café and one taken out the door, you could be overpaying VAT or under-declaring it.</p>

<p>Then there is the Flat Rate Scheme. Hospitality businesses using the Flat Rate Scheme pay a fixed percentage of turnover as VAT (currently 12.5% for restaurants and takeaways, 10.5% for hotels and boarding houses). That can simplify administration, but it is not always the best option. A good hospitality accountant will model both the standard scheme and the flat rate scheme for your specific business to see which saves you more.</p>

<p>And do not forget the VAT threshold. If your rolling 12-month turnover exceeds £90,000, you must register for VAT. Many hospitality businesses creep up to that threshold without realising, then face a penalty for late registration. We see this regularly with growing cafés and pop-up restaurants.</p>

<h3>Tips, Tronc and Service Charges</h3>

<p>This is one of the most misunderstood areas in hospitality accounting. How you handle tips, service charges and tronc (the system for distributing tips among staff) has significant tax and NI implications.</p>

<p>Cash tips given directly to staff are the staff's responsibility to declare on their self assessment. But tips added to a card payment and paid through your payroll are different. And service charges added to bills are treated differently again.</p>

<p>Since October 2024, the law changed. Under the Employment (Allocation of Tips) Act 2023, employers must pass on all tips and service charges to staff in full, without deduction. That includes any administrative fees. The distribution must be fair and transparent, and you must have a written policy.</p>

<p>For tax purposes, how you handle the money matters. If tips go through your payroll, you pay employer NI on them. If they go through a tronc scheme operated by a tronc master (who could be a senior employee), the employer NI liability may be reduced or avoided. This is a technical area where a generalist accountant can cost you thousands in unnecessary NI.</p>

<p>Specialist <strong>hospitality accountants</strong> know the difference between a discretionary service charge and a mandatory one, and how each is treated for VAT and payroll purposes. They also know how to set up a compliant tronc scheme that minimises your NI bill while keeping you on the right side of HMRC.</p>

<h3>Stock and Wastage: The Hidden Profit Killer</h3>

<p>In a standard service business, stock is often minimal. In hospitality, stock is your single biggest variable cost. And a lot of it goes in the bin.</p>

<p>Your accounts need to reflect wastage accurately. If you are not tracking stock properly, your cost of sales figure is wrong, your gross margin is misleading, and your tax return is built on sand.</p>

<p>We recommend monthly stocktakes for any hospitality business with more than £10,000 of stock on hand. Your accounting software (Xero, QuickBooks or FreeAgent) can handle stock adjustments, but you need to enter them properly. Many generalist accountants simply take the year-end stock figure from your annual accounts and call it done. That is not enough.</p>

<p>Wastage also affects your VAT calculations. If you buy stock, reclaim the VAT, then throw it away, you have reclaimed VAT on goods you never sold. That is fine for normal wastage (within reason). But HMRC can challenge excessive wastage claims, especially if your records are poor.</p>

<h3>Payroll: More Complicated Than You Think</h3>

<p>Hospitality payroll is not a simple monthly salary run. You have variable hours, zero-hour contracts, overtime, tips, tronc, service charges, and seasonal staff. You may have chefs on salary, waiting staff on minimum wage plus tips, and kitchen porters on hourly rates.</p>

<p>Then there is the National Minimum Wage. Hospitality is one of the sectors most commonly investigated for NMW underpayments. The rules are complex: you cannot count tips towards the minimum wage, but you can count some accommodation offsets. If you get it wrong, HMRC can issue a notice of underpayment, name you publicly, and fine you up to 200% of the arrears.</p>

<p>Your hospitality accountant should review your payroll at least quarterly to check you are meeting NMW obligations. They should also advise on whether to use the Employment Allowance (up to £10,500 off your employer NI bill) and whether it applies to your business.</p>

<h3>Gross Profit Margins and Menu Pricing</h3>

<p>This is not strictly accounting, but it is where a good hospitality accountant earns their keep. We help you understand your gross profit margin by menu item, by category (food, drink, events), and by outlet if you have multiple sites.</p>

<p>If your food GP is 65% but your drinks GP is 75%, you know where to focus. If your cost of sales is creeping up month on month, you need to investigate supplier pricing or wastage. A generalist accountant will give you a set of annual accounts that show your overall GP. A hospitality accountant will give you monthly management accounts that show the detail.</p>

<h2>What to Look for in Accountants for Hospitality</h2>

<p>Not every accountant who says they work with hospitality businesses actually understands the sector. Here is what to check when you are choosing <strong>accountants for hospitality</strong>.</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Do they know the VAT rules for food?</strong> Ask them to explain the difference between standard-rated, zero-rated and reduced-rate supplies in hospitality. If they hesitate, keep looking.</li>
<li><strong>Can they set up a tronc scheme?</strong> A good hospitality accountant should be able to advise on tronc structure and the NI implications. They do not need to run the tronc themselves, but they need to know how it interacts with payroll.</li>
<li><strong>Do they understand stock and wastage?</strong> Ask how they handle stock adjustments in your accounting software. If they say "we do it at year-end", that is a red flag.</li>
<li><strong>Do they know the licensing laws?</strong> Premises licences, personal licences, and the associated compliance requirements affect your business. Your accountant should at least be aware of them.</li>
<li><strong>Do they offer real-time management accounts?</strong> Monthly or quarterly management accounts are essential in hospitality. Annual accounts are history. You need forward-looking numbers.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Common Hospitality Accounting Mistakes (and How to Avoid Them)</h2>

<p>Here are the most common errors we see when we take on a new hospitality client who has been with a generalist accountant.</p>

<h3>Mistake 1: Incorrect VAT Treatment of Deposits</h3>

<p>If you take deposits for events, weddings or large bookings, the VAT point is when you receive the deposit, not when the event happens. Many accountants get this wrong and either account for VAT too late or too early. The correct treatment depends on whether the deposit is refundable and whether you issue a VAT invoice at the time of receipt.</p>

<h3>Mistake 2: Not Claiming Capital Allowances on Fit-Out Costs</h3>

<p>When you fit out a restaurant, pub or hotel, you spend money on fixtures, fittings, equipment and装修. A lot of that qualifies for capital allowances, including Annual Investment Allowance (AIA) at 100% on most plant and machinery up to £1 million per year. But some costs (like building alterations) are not plant and machinery and qualify only for Structures and Buildings Allowance at 3% per year. A good accountant will split your fit-out costs correctly to maximise your relief.</p>

<h3>Mistake 3: Misclassifying Staff as Self-Employed</h3>

<p>Hospitality businesses often use casual staff, event staff, or freelance chefs. HMRC is aggressive on employment status in hospitality. If you control when, where and how someone works, they are probably an employee, not a contractor. Misclassification can lead to backdated PAYE, NI and penalties.</p>

<h3>Mistake 4: Ignoring the Cash Flow Cycle</h3>

<p>Hospitality is a cash business, but the cash flow cycle is brutal. You pay suppliers weekly or monthly. You pay staff weekly or monthly. But your customers pay you immediately (card or cash). That sounds good, but it means you have little working capital buffer. A good hospitality accountant will help you forecast cash flow and plan for seasonal dips.</p>
