<p>If you're self employed and your taxable income is below the personal allowance of £12,570, you might be missing out on a straightforward tax break worth up to £252 per year. It's called the Marriage Allowance, and it lets you transfer up to £1,260 of your unused personal allowance to your spouse or civil partner.</p>

<p>This is one of the most underclaimed reliefs in the UK tax system. HMRC estimates that over 2 million eligible couples don't claim it. Many of them are self employed people who assume their income fluctuates too much to qualify. That's often wrong.</p>

<p>Let's walk through exactly how the marriage allowance self employed claim works, who qualifies, and how to apply.</p>

<h2>What Is the Marriage Allowance?</h2>

<p>The Marriage Allowance (sometimes called the Marriage Tax Allowance) lets one partner transfer 10% of their personal allowance to the other partner. For the 2025/26 tax year, that's £1,260. The receiving partner gets a tax credit worth 20% of that amount, which is £252.</p>

<p>It only works if one partner earns below the personal allowance threshold (£12,570) and the other pays tax at the basic 20% rate. The lower-earning partner must not be a higher or additional rate taxpayer either.</p>

<p>This is not a joint claim. You apply individually, and HMRC adjusts the tax code of the higher-earning partner. The relief then flows through PAYE or self assessment automatically.</p>

<h2>Who Qualifies for the Marriage Allowance Self Employed Claim?</h2>

<p>You qualify if all of the following apply:</p>

<ul>
<li>You are married or in a civil partnership</li>
<li>Your taxable income (after allowable expenses and trading allowances) is below £12,570</li>
<li>Your spouse or civil partner's taxable income is between £12,571 and £50,270 (the basic rate band)</li>
<li>Neither of you was born before 6 April 1935 (this rule is now largely obsolete but still on the books)</li>
</ul>

<p>For a self employed person, the key figure is your net profit after deducting allowable business expenses, capital allowances, and any trading allowance. It is not your turnover. A freelance graphic designer in Manchester might turnover £18,000 but have £6,000 in expenses, leaving a net profit of £12,000. That person qualifies for the marriage allowance because their taxable income is below £12,570.</p>

<p>Similarly, a sole trader builder in Birmingham who had a quiet year with net profits of £9,500 would qualify. So would a part-time self employed cleaner in Bristol earning £8,000 net from two days of work per week.</p>

<p>The critical point: your income can vary year to year. You need to check your eligibility each tax year based on your actual net profit.</p>

<h2>How Much Can You Save?</h2>

<p>The maximum saving is £252 per year. That's not life-changing money on its own, but over the course of a marriage it adds up. Claim it for five years and you've saved £1,260. Claim it for ten years and it's £2,520.</p>

<p>Here's a worked example:</p>

<p>Sarah is a self employed bookkeeper in Leeds. Her net profit for 2025/26 is £11,200. Her husband Tom is employed as a project manager earning £42,000 per year. Sarah's income is below the personal allowance, so she pays no income tax. Tom pays basic rate tax on most of his income.</p>

<p>Sarah transfers £1,260 of her unused personal allowance to Tom. Tom's personal allowance increases from £12,570 to £13,830. He saves 20% of £1,260, which is £252. Tom's tax bill drops by £252. Sarah's tax bill stays at zero because she still has £11,200 of her own allowance left.</p>

<p>The couple saves £252 for the year. It takes about five minutes to apply online.</p>

<h2>How to Apply for the Marriage Allowance as a Self Employed Person</h2>

<p>The application is done through GOV.UK. You do not need to wait until you file your self assessment return. You can apply at any point during the tax year.</p>

<p>Here's the step-by-step process:</p>

<ol>
<li>Go to GOV.UK and search for "Marriage Allowance".</li>
<li>Click the "Apply now" button.</li>
<li>You'll need your National Insurance number and your spouse's National Insurance number.</li>
<li>Confirm that your income is below the personal allowance for the current tax year.</li>
<li>Confirm that your spouse's income is within the basic rate band.</li>
<li>Submit the application.</li>
</ol>

<p>HMRC will send a confirmation letter and adjust your spouse's tax code. The change usually takes effect within a few weeks. If you're already in a tax year, the relief is backdated to the start of that year.</p>

<p>If your income changes mid-year and you become a taxpayer, you can cancel the transfer at any time. HMRC will adjust both partners' tax codes to reflect the change. There's no penalty for cancelling.</p>

<h2>Can You Backdate the Marriage Allowance Claim?</h2>

<p>Yes. You can backdate the claim for up to four previous tax years. This is a significant feature for self employed people who may not have known about the relief.</p>

<p>If you apply today, you can claim for the current tax year and the four previous tax years. That's five years total. At £252 per year, that's a potential refund of £1,260 in one go.</p>

<p>You do this through the same GOV.UK application. HMRC will calculate the backdated amount and issue a refund, usually as a tax code adjustment or a cheque if the amount is large enough.</p>

<p>For a self employed person who has had several low-income years, this backdating provision is valuable. It's worth checking your records for the last four years to see if you qualified.</p>

<h2>Common Misconceptions About the Marriage Allowance for Self Employed People</h2>

<h3>"My income varies too much to qualify"</h3>
<p>This is the most common reason self employed people don't claim. The allowance is assessed per tax year based on your actual net profit. If your profit was below £12,570 in a given year, you qualify for that year. It doesn't matter if you earned £30,000 the year before or £40,000 the year after. Each year stands alone.</p>

<h3>"I have to earn zero to qualify"</h3>
<p>No. You just need to earn less than £12,570. You can have an active self employed business with real income. As long as your net profit is below the threshold, you qualify.</p>

<h3>"My spouse has to be employed"</h3>
<p>No. Your spouse can be self employed too. The only requirement is that their taxable income is between £12,571 and £50,270. If both of you are self employed and one earns below the threshold while the other earns within the basic rate band, the marriage allowance still applies.</p>

<h3>"It's complicated to claim"</h3>
<p>It takes about five minutes on GOV.UK. You don't need an accountant to do it, though we can help if you're unsure about your income levels or want to check backdated years.</p>

<h2>What Happens If Your Income Changes Mid-Year?</h2>

<p>Self employed income is rarely predictable. If you start the tax year expecting low profits but then land a large contract, your net profit could exceed £12,570. In that case, you should cancel the Marriage Allowance transfer.</p>

<p>You can do this online through GOV.UK. HMRC will adjust both partners' tax codes from the point of cancellation. You won't owe any penalty. The relief simply stops applying going forward.</p>

<p>If you end the year with profits above £12,570 after all, the transfer was technically invalid for that year. HMRC will recalculate and may ask for the relief back. This is rare in practice, but it's worth monitoring your income if you're close to the threshold.</p>

<h2>Marriage Allowance and Self Assessment: What You Need to Know</h2>

<p>When you file your self assessment tax return, the Marriage Allowance is already factored into your tax code. You don't need to enter it separately on your SA100 or SA103 forms. The tax code adjustment from HMRC flows through to your calculation automatically.</p>

<p>If you're the lower-earning partner and you file a self assessment return, your tax calculation will show a zero or reduced liability because your personal allowance has been partially transferred. This is correct. You are not penalised for the transfer.</p>

<p>If you're the higher-earning partner and you file self assessment, your tax code will have increased by £1,260, reducing your overall tax bill. Again, this is automatic.</p>

<p>The key point: the Marriage Allowance is not something you report on your return. It's a tax code adjustment handled by HMRC. You just need to ensure the claim is active.</p>

<h2>Other Tax Reliefs for Self Employed Couples</h2>

<p>The Marriage Allowance is just one of several reliefs that can reduce your tax bill as a self employed couple. Others include:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Trading Allowance:</strong> If your self employed income is under £1,000, you don't need to declare it at all. This can keep you below the personal allowance threshold and make you eligible for the Marriage Allowance.</li>
<li><strong>Spouse Employment:</strong> If your spouse works for your business, you can pay them a salary that is tax-deductible for the business and falls within their personal allowance. This shifts income to a lower tax bracket.</li>
<li><strong>Business Partnership:</strong> If you run a business together, a formal partnership structure can allow you to split profits efficiently between you, keeping both partners within basic rate bands.</li>
</ul>

<p>These strategies work alongside the Marriage Allowance. A self employed couple in their 40s running a consultancy from home in the Northern Quarter of Manchester could combine all three to reduce their combined tax bill significantly.</p>
