<p>If you run a UK business that sells goods or services, you will need to handle VAT at some point. The standard rate is 20%. You either charge it to customers or reclaim it on your purchases. Getting the numbers wrong means overpaying HMRC or undercharging customers. Both hurt your cash flow.</p>

<p>A <strong>VAT calculator</strong> helps you add or remove VAT quickly. But you also need to understand when to register, how to file returns, and what the rules are for your specific trade. This guide covers all of that.</p>

<h2>How to Use a VAT Calculator</h2>

<p>A VAT calculator does two things. It adds VAT to a net price to give you the gross amount. Or it removes VAT from a gross price to show the net amount. The standard UK VAT rate is 20%.</p>

<p><strong>Adding VAT:</strong> Multiply the net price by 1.20. For example, £100 net x 1.20 = £120 gross. The VAT element is £20.</p>

<p><strong>Removing VAT:</strong> Divide the gross price by 1.20. For example, £120 gross ÷ 1.20 = £100 net. The VAT element is still £20.</p>

<p>For reduced rate (5%) or zero rate (0%), use 1.05 or 1.00 respectively. Most businesses use the standard rate, but check your specific supplies.</p>

<p>You can find free <a href="/calculators">VAT calculators on our site</a> that handle all rates automatically.</p>

<h2>When Must You Register for VAT?</h2>

<p>You must register for VAT if your total VAT taxable turnover exceeds £90,000 in any rolling 12-month period. This is not a fixed tax year. You check it every month. If your turnover hits £90,000, you must register within 30 days.</p>

<p>You can also register voluntarily if your turnover is below £90,000. This is common for businesses that buy from VAT-registered suppliers and want to reclaim input VAT. It can also make your business look more established to clients.</p>

<p>Once registered, you charge VAT on your sales (output tax) and reclaim VAT on your purchases (input tax). You file a VAT Return usually every quarter. Most businesses must keep digital VAT records and use software to submit VAT Returns <sup><a href="#ref-1" id="cite-1">[1]</a></sup><sup><a href="#ref-2" id="cite-2">[2]</a></sup>.</p>

<h2>Making Tax Digital for VAT</h2>

<p>Since April 2022, all VAT-registered businesses must follow Making Tax Digital (MTD) rules. This means you cannot use the old HMRC online portal to file your returns manually. You must use MTD-compatible software like Xero, QuickBooks, or FreeAgent.</p>

<p>You also need to keep digital records of your VAT data. Spreadsheets are allowed if you use bridging software to submit the return. But fully manual paper records are no longer acceptable.</p>

<p>If you are not yet registered for VAT, you will need to adopt MTD-compatible software when you do register. Our <a href="/blog/vat-and-making-tax-digital">VAT and Making Tax Digital guide</a> covers the software options and setup steps in detail.</p>

<h2>VAT Rates: Standard, Reduced, and Zero</h2>

<p>Most goods and services are standard-rated at 20%. But there are exceptions.</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Standard rate (20%):</strong> Most goods and services, including electronics, clothing, professional services, and consultancy.</li>
<li><strong>Reduced rate (5%):</strong> Domestic fuel, children's car seats, sanitary products, and some energy-saving materials.</li>
<li><strong>Zero rate (0%):</strong> Most food, children's clothing, books, newspapers, public transport, and prescription medicines.</li>
<li><strong>Exempt:</strong> Insurance, education, healthcare, and postal services. You do not charge VAT, but you also cannot reclaim input VAT on related costs.</li>
</ul>

<p>Getting the rate wrong can trigger HMRC penalties. If you sell a mix of standard and zero-rated goods, you need separate VAT codes in your software.</p>

<h2>Partial Exemption: When You Can't Reclaim All Input VAT</h2>

<p>If your business makes both taxable and exempt supplies, you are partially exempt. This means you cannot reclaim all the VAT you pay on purchases. You must apportion it.</p>

<p>The de minimis limit is where the total value of your exempt input tax is not more than £625 per month on average and half of your total input tax in the relevant period <sup><a href="#ref-3" id="cite-3">[3]</a></sup>. If you fall within this limit, you can reclaim all input VAT. If you exceed it, you must restrict your reclaim.</p>

<p>Partial exemption is common for businesses that let property, provide financial services, or offer certain types of education. For example, if you let facilities for playing any sport or for taking part in any physical recreation, these supplies are normally standard-rated <sup><a href="#ref-3" id="cite-3">[3]</a></sup>. But if the rental is for more than 24 hours or is for a series of 10 or more sessions, subject to conditions, then your supply may be exempt <sup><a href="#ref-3" id="cite-3">[3]</a></sup>.</p>

<p>Medical services provided by registered health professionals are normally exempt <sup><a href="#ref-3" id="cite-3">[3]</a></sup>. Therapists such as acupuncturists, psychotherapists, hypnotherapists and others who do not have statutory registers cannot currently exempt their services <sup><a href="#ref-3" id="cite-3">[3]</a></sup>. Where a medical report is produced solely to provide a third party with a necessary element for taking a decision for insurance or legal purposes, the supply is taxable at the standard rate <sup><a href="#ref-3" id="cite-3">[3]</a></sup>.</p>

<p>Partial exemption calculations are complex. If you think you might be partially exempt, speak to an accountant before filing your first return.</p>

<h2>VAT Payment Deadlines and Penalties</h2>

<p>Your VAT Return and payment are due one calendar month and seven days after the end of your accounting period. For most quarterly returns, this means you file and pay by the end of the month following the quarter end.</p>

<p>You cannot use the HMRC VAT payment deadline calculator if you make payments on account or use the annual accounting scheme <sup><a href="#ref-1" id="cite-1">[1]</a></sup><sup><a href="#ref-2" id="cite-2">[2]</a></sup>. Those schemes have different deadlines.</p>

<p>Late payment triggers interest and surcharges. The current Bank Rate is 3.75% <sup><a href="#ref-4" id="cite-4">[4]</a></sup>. HMRC charges late payment interest at Bank Rate plus 2.5%. So as of early 2025, that is 6.25% per year. Late filing penalties start at £50 per month for the first return, rising to £100 per month for subsequent defaults.</p>

<p>If you are struggling to pay, HMRC offers Time to Pay arrangements. You can set up a payment plan online if you owe less than £50,000.</p>

<h2>Flat Rate Scheme: Simpler but Not Always Cheaper</h2>

<p>The Flat Rate Scheme lets you pay a fixed percentage of your turnover as VAT, instead of calculating input and output VAT in detail. The percentage varies by sector, typically between 4% and 14.5%.</p>

<p>You cannot reclaim input VAT on most purchases under the Flat Rate Scheme. The exception is capital assets over £2,000 including VAT.</p>

<p>Limited cost traders must use a flat rate of 16.5%. You are a limited cost trader if your VAT-inclusive expenditure on relevant goods is less than 2% of your VAT-inclusive turnover, or less than £1,000 per year if the 2% test gives a higher figure.</p>

<p>The Flat Rate Scheme can save time on bookkeeping. But for many businesses, the standard scheme gives a better net result because you can reclaim input VAT on significant purchases.</p>

<h2>VAT and Advertising Claims: What the ASA Says</h2>

<p>If you advertise prices, you must show VAT correctly. The ASA upheld a complaint against 3D-Lipo Ltd regarding misleading earnings claims in an email sent on 26 May 2021 <sup><a href="#ref-5" id="cite-5">[5]</a></sup>. The email claimed potential earnings of £36,000 over 6 months based on one 60-minute laser hair removal treatment per day <sup><a href="#ref-5" id="cite-5">[5]</a></sup>. The email stated a treatment price of £300 per session and a total machine cost of £300 plus VAT <sup><a href="#ref-5" id="cite-5">[5]</a></sup>. The ASA considered that businesses would understand from the claims that £36,000 was representative of earnings achievable by clinics purchasing the product <sup><a href="#ref-5" id="cite-5">[5]</a></sup>. The ASA required data demonstrating that earnings figures in the ad were representative and did not overstate potential earnings <sup><a href="#ref-5" id="cite-5">[5]</a></sup>.</p>

<p>The lesson is clear. If you quote prices including or excluding VAT, be accurate. If you make earnings claims, back them with data.</p>

<h2>Common VAT Mistakes and How to Avoid Them</h2>

<p><strong>Mistake 1: Registering late.</strong> If your turnover hits £90,000 and you do not register within 30 days, HMRC can backdate your registration and charge you VAT on sales you made before registering. You cannot usually pass this cost on to customers after the fact.</p>

<p><strong>Mistake 2: Claiming input VAT on non-business purchases.</strong> You can only reclaim VAT on goods and services used for your business. Personal purchases, client entertaining (except staff entertaining), and certain motor expenses have restrictions.</p>

<p><strong>Mistake 3: Using the wrong rate.</strong> If you sell a product that you think is zero-rated but HMRC decides it is standard-rated, you owe the difference plus penalties. Check the VAT rate for your specific goods or services on gov.uk.</p>

<p><strong>Mistake 4: Not keeping digital records.</strong> Under MTD, you must keep digital records of all VAT data. If HMRC asks for your records and you only have paper, you face penalties.</p>

<p><strong>Mistake 5: Ignoring partial exemption.</strong> If you make exempt supplies and reclaim all input VAT, HMRC will eventually catch it. The correction plus interest and penalties can be significant.</p>
