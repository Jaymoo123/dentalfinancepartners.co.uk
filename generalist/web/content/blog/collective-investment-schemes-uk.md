<h2>What Exactly Is a Collective Investment Scheme?</h2>

<p>A collective investment scheme (CIS) is a fund where multiple investors pool their money together, and a professional fund manager invests that pooled money across a range of assets like stocks, bonds, or property <sup><a href="#ref-1" id="cite-1">[1]</a></sup>. The legal definition sits in section 235 of the Financial Services and Markets Act 2000 (FSMA) <sup><a href="#ref-1" id="cite-1">[1]</a></sup>.</p>

<p>For UK business owners, understanding CIS matters because your company pension, your personal investments, or even your business's surplus cash might sit inside one. The tax treatment differs depending on the type of scheme, and getting it wrong can cost you.</p>

<p>There are two main forms of UK collective investment schemes: Authorised Investment Funds (AIFs) and Unauthorised Unit Trusts (UUTs) <sup><a href="#ref-1" id="cite-1">[1]</a></sup>. AIFs split further into Authorised Unit Trusts (AUTs) and Open-Ended Investment Companies (OEICs) <sup><a href="#ref-1" id="cite-1">[1]</a></sup>.</p>

<h2>Authorised Investment Funds: AUTs and OEICs</h2>

<p>Authorised Unit Trusts are the older structure. Investors hold units in a trust, and the value of each unit reflects the underlying assets. AUTs are FCA-authorised and must comply with strict rules on diversification, borrowing, and pricing <sup><a href="#ref-1" id="cite-1">[1]</a></sup>.</p>

<p>Open-Ended Investment Companies are corporate structures. Investors hold shares in the company itself, and the company's assets are the investment portfolio. OEICs can only be set up in the UK as FCA-authorised collective investment schemes. There is no UK-resident unauthorised equivalent <sup><a href="#ref-1" id="cite-1">[1]</a></sup>.</p>

<p>Both AUTs and OEICs are "open-ended" meaning the fund creates or cancels units/shares as investors buy in or cash out. This keeps the unit price close to the net asset value of the underlying assets.</p>

<p>For stamp taxes, authorised investment funds are not treated differently from UUTs <sup><a href="#ref-1" id="cite-1">[1]</a></sup>. That means no stamp duty reserve tax on most transactions within the fund, which is a practical advantage for investors.</p>

<h2>Unauthorised Unit Trusts (UUTs)</h2>

<p>UUTs are collective investment schemes that are not FCA-authorised. They are typically used for specific purposes like employee share schemes or certain property arrangements. UUTs are not regulated in the same way as AUTs or OEICs, and the tax treatment can be less favourable.</p>

<p>A body corporate, other than an OEIC, is not a collective investment scheme for the purposes of section 235 FSMA <sup><a href="#ref-1" id="cite-1">[1]</a></sup>. That distinction matters because it determines whether the entity falls under CIS tax rules or standard corporate tax rules.</p>

<h2>Unregulated Collective Investment Schemes (UCIS)</h2>

<p>If a CIS is not authorised or recognised by the FCA, it is considered an unregulated collective investment scheme (UCIS) <sup><a href="#ref-2" id="cite-2">[2]</a></sup>. UCIS can only be promoted in the UK to specific types of investor: certified high net worth individuals, certified sophisticated investors, and self-certified sophisticated investors <sup><a href="#ref-2" id="cite-2">[2]</a></sup>.</p>

<p>UCIS are high risk. The FCA warns that you should be prepared to lose all your money <sup><a href="#ref-2" id="cite-2">[2]</a></sup>. Investors in UCIS may not have access to the Financial Ombudsman Service or the Financial Services Compensation Scheme (FSCS) if things go wrong <sup><a href="#ref-2" id="cite-2">[2]</a></sup>.</p>

<p>For business owners, the key point is simple. If someone offers you an investment in a UCIS, treat it with extreme caution. The lack of regulatory protection is a serious red flag.</p>

<h2>Tax Treatment of Collective Investment Schemes</h2>

<p>The tax treatment depends on the type of scheme and whether you invest personally or through your company.</p>

<p><strong>For individuals:</strong> Income from authorised funds (AUTs and OEICs) is taxed as dividend income or interest income depending on the fund's distribution policy. Capital gains on disposal of units or shares are subject to CGT at your marginal rate. The annual exempt amount (£3,000 for 2025/26) applies.</p>

<p><strong>For limited companies:</strong> Distributions from authorised funds are generally exempt from corporation tax under the dividend exemption rules. Gains on disposal are subject to corporation tax on chargeable gains, currently at 19% or 25% depending on profit level.</p>

<p><strong>For UUTs:</strong> The tax treatment is less straightforward. Income may be taxed as trading income rather than investment income, and capital gains may be treated as income rather than capital. Professional advice is essential before investing in a UUT.</p>

<p>HMRC's internal manual (CTM48105) previously covered authorised investment funds and collective investment schemes, but that guidance has now moved to the Investment Funds Manual <sup><a href="#ref-3" id="cite-3">[3]</a></sup>. The principles remain the same, but the location has changed.</p>

<h2>Why Business Owners Should Care</h2>

<p>If your company holds surplus cash, you might consider investing it in a collective investment scheme rather than leaving it in a low-interest bank account. The tax treatment matters because you want to maximise post-tax returns.</p>

<p>If you are a director extracting profits, you might invest personally through an ISA or a general investment account. Understanding whether the fund is authorised or unauthorised affects your tax position and your risk exposure.</p>

<p>If you are planning an exit from your business, the proceeds might be invested in a CIS. The timing of the investment and the type of scheme can affect your CGT position, particularly if you are using Business Asset Disposal Relief (BADR).</p>

<h2>Ponzi Schemes and the CIS Definition</h2>

<p>Not every pooled investment is a legitimate collective investment scheme. History is littered with examples of fraudulent schemes that look like CIS but are actually Ponzi schemes.</p>

<p>Between 1919 and 1920, Charles Ponzi cheated almost 40,000 investors and raised USD 15 million using a fictitious postal coupon investment scheme <sup><a href="#ref-4" id="cite-4">[4]</a></sup>. Bernard Madoff's Ponzi scheme involved USD 50 billion and collapsed in 2008 due to heightened fund redemptions <sup><a href="#ref-4" id="cite-4">[4]</a></sup>.</p>

<p>In China, there were about 6,000 Ponzi scheme cases in 2015 involving RMB 250 billion (USD 40.15 billion) <sup><a href="#ref-4" id="cite-4">[4]</a></sup>. The Ezubao scheme attracted about 900,000 investors and swindled RMB 50 billion (USD 8.14 billion) in less than two years <sup><a href="#ref-4" id="cite-4">[4]</a></sup>.</p>

<p>The FCA's guidance on UCIS is clear: if a scheme is not authorised or recognised, you have no regulatory protection <sup><a href="#ref-2" id="cite-2">[2]</a></sup>. That does not mean every UCIS is a fraud, but it does mean you are taking on significant risk without the usual safeguards.</p>

<h2>Practical Steps for Business Owners</h2>

<p><strong>Check the FCA register.</strong> Before investing in any collective investment scheme, confirm that the fund is FCA-authorised. The FCA register is free to search.</p>

<p><strong>Understand the fee structure.</strong> Authorised funds charge annual management charges (AMCs) and often have entry or exit fees. These eat into your returns. Compare total expense ratios (TERs) across similar funds.</p>

<p><strong>Know your tax position.</strong> If you invest through your company, the dividend exemption may apply. If you invest personally, your marginal income tax rate and CGT rate determine the net return. Use our <a href="/calculators">calculators</a> to model the numbers.</p>

<p><strong>Get professional advice.</strong> The rules around collective investment schemes are detailed and change regularly. HMRC's Investment Funds Manual runs to hundreds of pages. A qualified accountant can help you navigate the complexity.</p>
