<p>Yes, OnlyFans income is taxable. As soon as your subscription fees, tips and pay-per-view earnings exceed £1,000 in a tax year, you are required to register for self assessment and declare the income as trading profit. The question is not whether HMRC expects a return. It is whether you are claiming every legitimate expense, choosing the right business structure, and preparing for VAT before you cross the threshold rather than after.</p>

<p>This guide covers the specific tax and accounting issues that apply to UK OnlyFans creators: self assessment registration and the double-bill timing trap, the expenses that are genuinely allowable (and the ones HMRC will push back on), VAT registration, IR35 and agency arrangements, and a worked example comparing sole trader against limited company at a realistic profit level. Every figure is grounded in the rules current for 2025/26 and 2026/27.</p>

<h2>Self Assessment Registration: Who Must Register, and When</h2>

<p>HMRC treats OnlyFans income as trading income under <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2005/5/part/2" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">ITTOIA 2005 Part 2</a>. If your earnings from subscriptions, tips, custom content or pay-per-view messages exceed £1,000 in a tax year, you must register for self assessment. The registration deadline is <strong>5 October</strong> following the end of the tax year in which you first earned above that threshold. If you started earning in, say, October 2025 and passed £1,000 during 2025/26, the deadline is 5 October 2026.</p>

<p>Once registered, you file an SA100 return with the SA103 self-employment supplementary pages. These ask for your turnover, allowable expenses, and net profit. OnlyFans income from all sources (subscriptions, tips, PPV, referral income) goes into the turnover line.</p>

<p>The filing and payment deadline is <strong>31 January</strong> after the end of the tax year. A paper return is due by 31 October. The first year of self-employment creates a well-known cash-flow problem: on 31 January you pay both the balancing payment for the year just ended <em>and</em> a payment on account toward the following year's tax. That payment on account is 50% of the prior year's income tax and Class 4 NIC liability, per <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1970/9/section/59A" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">TMA 1970 s.59A</a>. A second payment on account falls due on 31 July. If your first year's tax bill is £6,000, you pay £6,000 plus £3,000 on 31 January and a further £3,000 on 31 July. Many creators are unprepared for this.</p>

<p>HMRC receives data from payment platforms including OnlyFans under its third-party data-gathering powers. Late registration carries an automatic £100 penalty on the return, then daily penalties after three months, then surcharges at 30 days, 6 months and 12 months after the payment deadline. A deliberate failure to register is treated more severely and can attract penalties of up to 100% of the tax unpaid.</p>

<h2>What Tax Does an OnlyFans Creator Pay? The 2025/26 Rates</h2>

<p>As a sole trader you pay income tax and Class 4 National Insurance on your trading profit. The <strong>personal allowance is £12,570</strong> (2025/26 and 2026/27). Income below this is tax-free. The allowance tapers by £1 for every £2 of adjusted net income above £100,000, disappearing entirely at £125,140.</p>

<p><strong>Income tax bands for 2025/26 (England, Wales and Northern Ireland):</strong> 20% basic rate on taxable income up to £37,700 (so profit of up to £50,270 after the personal allowance), 40% higher rate from £37,701 to the additional rate threshold, and 45% additional rate above £125,140.</p>

<p><strong>Class 4 NIC (2025/26):</strong> <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1992/4/section/15" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">SSCBA 1992 s.15</a> charges Class 4 at <strong>6%</strong> on profits between £12,570 and £50,270, then <strong>2%</strong> above £50,270. Class 2 NIC was abolished from 6 April 2024 for profits above the Small Profits Threshold: most creators no longer pay a weekly flat charge.</p>

<p>A worked example at £45,000 profit for 2025/26:</p>

<ul>
<li>Personal allowance: £12,570. Taxable profit: £45,000 minus £12,570 equals £32,430.</li>
<li>Income tax: £32,430 at 20% equals £6,486.</li>
<li>Class 4 NIC: £32,430 at 6% equals £1,946 (the whole taxable slice falls below £37,700).</li>
<li>Total tax and NIC: £8,432. Net after tax: £36,568.</li>
</ul>

Dual-use items (used partly for the business, partly for personal purposes) can be claimed only to the extent of the business proportion. The key is a contemporaneous usage log.</p>

<h2>OnlyFans VAT: When and How It Applies</h2>

<p>The VAT registration threshold is <strong>£90,000</strong> in taxable turnover over any rolling 12-month period. This was raised from £85,000 on 1 April 2024. Once your OnlyFans income (subscriptions, tips, PPV) in any consecutive 12-month window exceeds £90,000, you must register for VAT within 30 days of the end of the month in which you crossed the threshold.</p>

<p>Once VAT-registered, you charge 20% on your taxable supplies. For UK subscribers, your subscription price becomes inclusive of 20% VAT. If you were charging £15 per month and register for VAT, the subscriber effectively pays £15 but you hand over £2.50 of that to HMRC. Most creators raise their price to maintain net revenue, but the increase affects subscriber conversion rates. Plan before you hit the threshold, not after.</p>

<p>VAT returns are filed quarterly under <strong>Making Tax Digital for VAT</strong>, which has applied to all VAT-registered businesses since April 2022. You need MTD-compatible software (Xero, QuickBooks or FreeAgent are the most common) and must link your records digitally to your return. HMRC does not accept manual VAT returns from MTD-registered businesses.</p>

<p><strong>Should you register voluntarily below £90,000?</strong> Voluntary registration lets you reclaim input VAT on equipment purchases. A creator spending £5,000 on a camera rig, lighting and editing software each year recovers £833 in input VAT. The cost is the administrative burden of quarterly MTD returns and the obligation to charge VAT on all taxable supplies. Whether the reclaim outweighs the revenue impact depends on your margin and subscriber sensitivity to price changes. An <strong>OnlyFans accountant</strong> can model the break-even for your specific turnover and purchase profile.</p>

<h2>IR35 and Agency Arrangements</h2>

<p>IR35 (the off-payroll working rules under <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2003/1/part/2/chapter/8" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">ITEPA 2003 Chapter 8 and Chapter 10, Part 2</a>) is designed to catch disguised employment: a worker who provides services through their own company but would be an employee if they dealt directly with the end client. For most independent OnlyFans creators it simply does not apply. You set your own prices, own your content, manage your schedule, and carry the financial risk of fluctuating subscriber numbers. That is the profile of genuine self-employment, not employment.</p>

<p>The scenario where IR35 becomes live is an agency or production company that approaches you with an exclusive or semi-exclusive retainer. If that arrangement means the agency controls when, where and how you produce content, provides equipment, and pays a fixed fee regardless of output, HMRC can argue you are an employee of that intermediary. From <strong>6 April 2021</strong> the rules changed: for medium and large clients, the <strong>client determines your IR35 status</strong> and must issue a Status Determination Statement (SDS) with reasons. For small clients (broadly meeting the Companies Act small company test), you can determine your own status under the original Chapter 8 rules.</p>

<p>If you are approached by an agency with a guaranteed monthly arrangement: (1) ask for a written SDS before signing; (2) have your accountant review the actual working arrangements against the status factors (control, substitution, mutuality of obligation, financial risk, integration); (3) if the SDS puts you inside IR35, all income through that arrangement is subject to PAYE and NIC, with no ability to take dividends from that income. You lose a significant part of the tax efficiency of a limited company on that income stream.</p>

<p>The vast majority of OnlyFans creators are outside IR35 by the nature of what they do. If you are not working through a management company and you set your own prices and output, IR35 is unlikely to be a live issue. But document your working arrangements in a simple statement of business model in case HMRC enquires.</p>

<h2>Sole Trader or Limited Company? A Worked Comparison</h2>

<p>This is the most common structural question we see from creators whose profit has grown past £50,000. The answer depends on one thing above everything else: how much of your profit do you extract each year versus retain in the business?</p>

<p>The corporation tax rates for FY2025 and FY2026 are: <strong>19%</strong> (small profits rate) where augmented profits do not exceed £50,000; <strong>25%</strong> (main rate) where they exceed £250,000; and marginal relief tapers the effective rate between £50,000 and £250,000, with an effective marginal rate of approximately <strong>26.5%</strong> on the slice within the band. From 6 April 2026 (Finance Act 2026 s.4), dividend rates are: <strong>10.75%</strong> ordinary (basic), <strong>35.75%</strong> upper (higher), and <strong>39.35%</strong> additional. The dividend allowance remains £500.</p>

<p>The worked example below uses a creator with £80,000 of net profit after all expenses for 2026/27, comparing sole trader against a limited company where all available profit is extracted.</p>

<p><strong>Sole trader (2026/27):</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Taxable income after personal allowance: £80,000 minus £12,570 equals £67,430.</li>
<li>Income tax: first £37,700 at 20% equals £7,540. Remaining £29,730 at 40% equals £11,892. Total income tax: £19,432.</li>
<li>Class 4 NIC: £37,700 at 6% equals £2,262. £29,730 at 2% equals £595. Total Class 4: £2,857.</li>
<li>Total tax and NIC: £22,289. Net take-home: £57,711.</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Limited company, full extraction (2026/27):</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Director's salary at the secondary threshold (£5,000, no employer NIC for a single-director company): no income tax (within personal allowance), no employee or employer NIC.</li>
<li>Taxable company profit: £80,000 minus £5,000 salary equals £75,000.</li>
<li>Corporation tax with marginal relief: £9,500 (19% on first £50,000) plus £6,625 (26.5% on next £25,000) equals <strong>£16,125</strong>. Post-CT distributable profit: £75,000 minus £16,125 equals <strong>£58,875</strong> paid as dividends.</li>
<li>Personal tax on salary: £5,000 is within the personal allowance (£12,570), so £0 income tax on salary.</li>
<li>Remaining personal allowance: £12,570 minus £5,000 equals £7,570, which absorbs the first £7,570 of dividends tax-free. Next £500 within the dividend allowance at 0%. The £500 dividend allowance is a nil-rate band: it is taxed at 0% but still occupies basic-rate band space, so only £37,200 of the basic-rate band (not £37,700) remains available for dividends taxed at rate.</li>
<li>Dividends at basic rate: £37,200 at 10.75% equals £3,999. Higher rate dividends: £58,875 minus £7,570 minus £500 minus £37,200 equals £13,605 at 35.75% equals £4,864. Total dividend tax: £3,999 plus £4,864 equals <strong>£8,863</strong>.</li>
<li>Total tax (CT plus dividend tax): £16,125 plus £8,863 equals <strong>£24,988</strong>. Net personal take-home: £5,000 (salary) plus £58,875 (dividends) minus £8,863 (dividend tax) equals <strong>£55,012</strong>.</li>
</ul>

<p>At £80,000 profit with full extraction, the sole trader (£57,711 net) comes out ahead of the limited company (£55,012 net). The sole trader nets £2,699 more at this profit level. The company route carries a higher total tax burden because marginal relief pushes the effective CT rate on the £50,000 to £250,000 band to 26.5%, and the dividend rates from 6 April 2026 have narrowed the gap further. The case for a limited company at this profit level rests on <strong>retention and timing</strong>, not pure extraction parity. If the director retains £20,000 inside the company rather than extracting everything, that sum is taxed at 19% to 25% corporation tax rather than at 40% income tax plus 2% Class 4 NIC. Over several years the retained profit compounds at a materially lower initial tax cost.</p>

<h2>Making Tax Digital for Income Tax: What OnlyFans Creators Need to Know</h2>

<p>From <strong>6 April 2026</strong>, self-employed individuals (including OnlyFans creators) with qualifying income above <strong>£50,000</strong> must comply with Making Tax Digital for Income Tax. From 6 April 2027 the threshold drops to £30,000, and from 6 April 2028 to £20,000.</p>

<p>Under MTD ITSA you must keep digital records of income and expenses and submit quarterly updates to HMRC through compatible software (Xero, QuickBooks, FreeAgent), plus an end-of-period statement and final declaration. This replaces the single annual self assessment return for those within scope. HMRC will notify you if you need to comply, but you are responsible for checking your own obligations.</p>

<p>If your OnlyFans income crosses the £50,000 threshold before April 2026, set up MTD-compatible bookkeeping software now. Real-time digital records make the quarterly update process straightforward; trying to reconstruct a year's transactions from bank statements and screenshots is significantly harder. See our guide to <a href="/blog/sole-trader-and-self-employment/accountant-for-self-employed-near-me">finding the right accountant for self-employed income</a> for what to look for in an accountant who can support MTD compliance.</p>

<h2>Record-Keeping Requirements</h2>

<p>HMRC requires you to keep records for at least <strong>five years</strong> after the 31 January filing deadline for the relevant tax year. For a 2025/26 return filed by 31 January 2027, records must be kept until 31 January 2032.</p>

<p>For an OnlyFans creator this means: monthly payout statements downloaded as PDFs from the OnlyFans dashboard; bank statements showing those payments landing in your account; receipts for all claimed expenses (digital photos of paper receipts are acceptable); a mileage log if you claim travel; and VAT records if registered. If you are paid in US dollars or another currency, keep a record of the GBP exchange rate on the date of each payment or the method you use to convert.</p>

<p>Bookkeeping software connected to your bank account (Xero, QuickBooks, FreeAgent) categorises transactions in real time and makes the year-end process, and any HMRC enquiry, substantially more manageable. The cost of the software is itself a deductible business expense.</p>

<h2>Paying a Family Member: What HMRC Will and Will Not Accept</h2>

<p>A common planning point for creators who work with a partner on production, editing or administration: can you pay them a wage to shift income into a lower tax band? The answer is yes, subject to strict conditions. HMRC applies the wholly-and-exclusively rule (<a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2005/5/section/34" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">ITTOIA 2005 s.34</a>) and its guidance at BIM37700+: the wage must be paid for genuine work, the person must actually do it, and the rate must be commercially appropriate for the role. Paying a partner £15,000 a year for three hours of admin a month will be challenged. Paying a market-rate wage for genuine editing, bookkeeping or social media management that they carry out stands up.</p>

<p>If you operate through a limited company, your partner becomes an employee: you must set up PAYE, report in real time via RTI, and deduct income tax and employee NIC. The company also pays employer NIC at <strong>15%</strong> on wages above the secondary threshold of <strong>£5,000</strong> a year (from 6 April 2025). If the company has at least one genuine non-director employee, it may be able to claim the Employment Allowance of £10,500 to offset employer NIC.</p>
