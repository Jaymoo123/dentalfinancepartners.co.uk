<p>HMRC opens enquiries into corporation tax returns every year. If you have received a letter saying your company's return is being checked, you need to know what happens next. Especially if there is an error in that return.</p>

<p>The short answer is this: if HMRC finds your corporation tax return is incorrect during an enquiry, you will have to pay the extra tax due, plus interest, and you will face a penalty. The size of that penalty depends on whether the error was careless, deliberate, or deliberately concealed. In serious cases, HMRC can open a criminal investigation.</p>

<p>But the process is not automatic. You have rights. You can respond, correct mistakes, and in some cases reduce penalties significantly. Here is what actually happens when HMRC opens an enquiry into an incorrect corporation tax return, and how to handle it.</p>

<h2>How HMRC Opens a Corporation Tax Enquiry</h2>

<p>HMRC does not review every company return. They use a risk-based system. Their computers flag returns that fall outside expected patterns: unusually high expenses, low profit margins for your sector, large director's loan account movements, or claims for R&D tax credits that do not match your business profile.</p>

<p>Once flagged, an HMRC officer can open an enquiry under Schedule 18 Finance Act 1998. You will receive a formal letter known as a "notice of enquiry". This letter will specify which aspects of your return are being checked. It will not necessarily say HMRC suspects an error. It may just say they are checking certain figures.</p>

<p>The notice of enquiry must be issued within 12 months of the filing deadline. For a return filed on time, that means HMRC has until 12 months after the statutory filing date (usually 12 months after your company's year-end) to open an enquiry. If you filed late, the window extends.</p>

<p>Once you receive that letter, the clock starts. You typically have 30 days to respond. Ignoring it is the worst thing you can do.</p>

<h2>What Happens If HMRC Finds an Error</h2>

<p>During the enquiry, HMRC will ask for supporting documents: invoices, receipts, bank statements, contracts, timesheets if you claimed R&D relief. They will compare your return against these records. If they find a discrepancy, they will write to you with their findings.</p>

<p>If the error is straightforward, for example you claimed a capital allowance on an asset that does not qualify, HMRC will issue a "closure notice" amending your return. You will owe the extra corporation tax, plus interest from the date the tax was originally due.</p>

<p>If the error is more serious, for example you understated sales or claimed personal expenses as business costs, HMRC will raise the matter of penalties. This is where the real cost hits.</p>

<h3>Penalty Tiers for Incorrect Returns</h3>

<p>HMRC applies a standard penalty framework under Schedule 24 Finance Act 2007. The penalty percentage depends on the behaviour that caused the error:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Careless (mistake despite taking reasonable care):</strong> 0% to 30% of the extra tax due. If you tell HMRC about the error unprompted, the penalty range is 0% to 30%. If prompted (HMRC finds it first), it is 15% to 30%.</li>
<li><strong>Deliberate but not concealed:</strong> 20% to 70% unprompted, 35% to 70% prompted.</li>
<li><strong>Deliberate and concealed:</strong> 30% to 100% unprompted, 50% to 100% prompted.</li>
</ul>

<p>These are ranges. The actual penalty depends on how quickly you disclose the error, how cooperative you are, and whether you have a history of similar errors.</p>

<p>Take a concrete example. A Birmingham-based construction company under-declared profits by £40,000 over two years. The extra corporation tax due was £10,000 (25% rate). HMRC classified the error as careless. The penalty was 20%: £2,000. Plus interest on the £10,000 from the original due dates. The total bill came to roughly £12,600. Painful, but not catastrophic.</p>

<p>Now take a different case. A London consultancy deliberately inflated expenses by £150,000 over three years. The extra tax was £37,500. HMRC classified it as deliberate and concealed. The penalty was 70%: £26,250. Total bill: £63,750 plus interest. That kind of figure can break a small business.</p>

<h2>Interest on Late Corporation Tax</h2>

<p>Interest runs from the date the corporation tax was originally due. For most small companies, that is 9 months and 1 day after the year-end. HMRC charges late payment interest at the Bank of England base rate plus 4% (raised from base rate plus 2.5% on 6 April 2025), so currently around 8.25% to 8.5% per year. It adds up.</p>

<p>You cannot negotiate interest. It is statutory. The only way to reduce it is to pay the tax as quickly as possible once HMRC raises the assessment.</p>

<h2>Can HMRC Open a Criminal Investigation?</h2>

<p>Yes, but only in serious cases. HMRC reserves criminal prosecution for cases involving fraud, systematic evasion, or deliberate concealment with evidence of intent. For most small business errors, the outcome is a civil penalty, not a criminal prosecution.</p>

<p>That said, if HMRC believes you deliberately suppressed income, fabricated expenses, or hid assets, they can refer the case to their Criminal Investigation unit. The threshold is higher than most business owners realise. You would need to have taken active steps to conceal the error, such as destroying records or creating false invoices.</p>

<p>If you are worried about this, speak to a solicitor specialising in tax investigations. Do not try to handle it alone.</p>

<h2>Your Rights During a Corporation Tax Enquiry</h2>

<p>You are not powerless. You have the right to:</p>

<ul>
<li>Know exactly what HMRC is enquiring into. The notice of enquiry must specify the areas under review.</li>
<li>Appoint an agent (accountant or tax adviser) to handle all correspondence. HMRC will deal with them, not you directly.</li>
<li>Challenge HMRC's findings through a statutory review or tax tribunal if you disagree.</li>
<li>Request a closure notice if HMRC is dragging the enquiry out without good reason.</li>
</ul>

<p>We advise clients to cooperate fully but never to agree to anything without understanding the implications. If HMRC says you owe more tax, ask for a detailed breakdown. Check their calculations. They can make mistakes too.</p>

<h2>How to Respond to an HMRC Enquiry Letter</h2>

<p>Do not panic. Do not ignore it. Here is the practical sequence:</p>

<ol>
<li>Read the letter carefully. Note the specific areas under enquiry and the deadline for response.</li>
<li>Contact your accountant immediately. If you do not have one, find an ICAEW qualified firm that handles HMRC enquiries. This is not a job for a general bookkeeper.</li>
<li>Gather the supporting documents HMRC has requested. Do not destroy anything. Do not alter records.</li>
<li>Respond within the deadline. Even if you need more time, write to HMRC and ask for an extension. They usually grant one if you have a genuine reason.</li>
<li>Do not admit liability for errors you are unsure about. Let your accountant review the position first.</li>
</ol>

<p>If you discover an error during the enquiry, tell your accountant. They will advise on how to disclose it. Voluntary disclosure reduces penalties significantly. The difference between unprompted and prompted disclosure can halve your penalty.</p>

<h2>Can You Amend a Return After HMRC Opens an Enquiry?</h2>

<p>Yes, but only with HMRC's agreement. Once an enquiry is open, you cannot simply file an amended return. You must write to HMRC, explain the amendment, and ask them to include it in the enquiry. They may accept it or reject it.</p>

<p>If you discover an error that increases your tax liability, you should disclose it. HMRC will treat this as a prompted disclosure, but it is still better than them finding it themselves.</p>

<p>If the error reduces your tax liability, you can still claim the overpayment. HMRC will review the claim as part of the enquiry. If it is valid, they will amend your return and issue a repayment.</p>

<h2>How Long Does a Corporation Tax Enquiry Take?</h2>

<p>There is no fixed timeline. Simple enquiries close in 3 to 6 months. Complex ones can take 18 months or longer. HMRC aims to complete most enquiries within 12 months, but that is not guaranteed.</p>

<p>If HMRC is slow, you can ask for a closure notice. If they refuse, you can apply to the tax tribunal. This is rare in practice. Most enquiries resolve through negotiation.</p>

<h2>What Happens After the Enquiry Closes?</h2>

<p>HMRC will issue a closure notice. This document sets out the final amendments to your corporation tax return. You must pay any additional tax, interest, and penalties within 30 days. If you disagree, you can appeal within 30 days by requesting a statutory review or taking the case to the First-tier Tribunal.</p>

<p>Once the closure notice is issued, HMRC cannot reopen the same return for the same period unless they discover fraud or negligent conduct that was not previously known.</p>

<h2>Preventing Enquiries in the First Place</h2>

<p>The best way to handle an HMRC enquiry is to never trigger one. That means filing accurate, well-supported returns. Keep proper records. Use accounting software like Xero, FreeAgent, or QuickBooks to track income and expenses. Reconcile your bank accounts monthly. Keep invoices and receipts for at least 6 years.</p>

<p>If you claim R&D tax credits, make sure you have the technical and financial evidence to back the claim. HMRC scrutinises R&D claims heavily. A weak claim is an enquiry magnet.</p>

<p>If your company has unusual transactions, such as a large director's loan or a property sale, get professional advice before filing. A small fee upfront can save you thousands in penalties later.</p>

<h2>Final Thoughts</h2>

<p>An HMRC enquiry into an incorrect corporation tax return is stressful, but it is manageable if you respond correctly. The key steps are: cooperate, get professional help, disclose errors voluntarily, and pay what is due promptly. Penalties are not automatic. They depend on behaviour. Careless mistakes attract lower penalties than deliberate evasion.</p>

<p>If you receive an enquiry letter, do not try to handle it alone. Contact an accountant who understands HMRC investigations. The cost of advice is small compared to the potential penalties and interest.</p>
