<h2>R&D Tax Claim Software Companies: A Buyer's Guide for UK Businesses</h2>

<p>If you have spent time researching R&D tax credits, you will have come across the growing number of <strong>r&d tax claim software companies</strong> offering to handle your claim. Some promise a fully automated process. Others claim to maximise your qualifying expenditure. A few simply connect you with a specialist who does the work manually.</p>

<p>The question is: which ones actually deliver for a UK business owner who is not a tax specialist?</p>

<p>This guide covers the main <strong>r&d tax claim software companies</strong> operating in the UK market as of 2025/26, what each platform does well, where they fall short, and when you are better off working directly with an ICAEW qualified firm like Holloway Davies.</p>

<h2>What R&D Tax Claim Software Actually Does</h2>

<p>R&D tax credit software typically handles three things:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Technical narrative preparation.</strong> The software guides you through describing the technological advances or uncertainties your project addressed.</li>
<li><strong>Cost calculation.</strong> It identifies qualifying costs (staff salaries, subcontractor fees, consumables, software licences) and applies the correct relief rates.</li>
<li><strong>HMRC submission.</strong> It either generates the CT600 corporation tax return supplementary pages or exports data for your accountant to file.</li>
</ul>

<p>No software does all three perfectly for every business type. That is the first thing to understand.</p>

<h2>The Main R&D Tax Claim Software Companies in the UK</h2>

<h3>1. Catax (Now Part of Ryan)</h3>

<p>Catax has been around since 2008 and was acquired by Ryan, a global tax services firm, in 2022. Their platform is aimed at larger SMEs and mid-market companies. You typically get a dedicated advisor who uses the software to prepare your claim, rather than doing it yourself.</p>

<p><strong>Best for:</strong> Companies with R&D claims between £50,000 and £500,000. Manufacturing, engineering, and construction businesses where the technical narrative is complex.</p>

<p><strong>Limitation:</strong> Not a self-service tool. You are paying for the advisor as much as the software. Smaller claims below £20,000 may not justify the fee structure.</p>

<h3>2. MyR&DClaim (Formerly ForrestBrown)</h3>

<p>ForrestBrown rebranded to MyR&DClaim in 2024 and repositioned as a more accessible platform. It offers a guided online process where you answer questions about your projects and costs. The software then generates a technical report and cost calculation.</p>

<p><strong>Best for:</strong> Growing tech companies, software developers, and digital agencies with R&D claims from £10,000 to £100,000.</p>

<p><strong>Limitation:</strong> The software is strong on the technical narrative but weaker on cost optimisation. If you have complex subcontractor arrangements or overseas workers, you may miss qualifying costs.</p>

<h3>3. GovGrant</h3>

<p>GovGrant positions itself as a full-service R&D tax credit specialist with a software platform underneath. They employ in-house technical writers and tax specialists. The software is more of a workflow tool than a DIY platform.</p>

<p><strong>Best for:</strong> Life sciences, pharmaceuticals, and deep-tech companies where the technical narrative is highly specialised.</p>

<p><strong>Limitation:</strong> Not suitable for micro-businesses or sole directors. Their minimum claim value is typically £25,000+.</p>

<h3>4. GrantTree</h3>

<p>GrantTree is one of the older names in the R&D tax credit space, operating since 2011. They offer both R&D tax credits and grant funding services. Their software platform is proprietary and used by their in-house team rather than offered directly to businesses.</p>

<p><strong>Best for:</strong> Companies that also want grant funding support alongside R&D tax credits. Good for businesses in clean tech, AI, and advanced manufacturing.</p>

<p><strong>Limitation:</strong> You cannot use the software independently. You are buying a service, not a tool.</p>

<h3>5. Leyton</h3>

<p>Leyton is a large international firm with a UK R&D tax credit practice. They have their own software for tracking and claiming R&D expenditure. Like Catax and GrantTree, it is advisor-led rather than self-service.</p>

<p><strong>Best for:</strong> Larger SMEs and corporates with multi-year R&D programmes. Good for businesses that want ongoing R&D tracking rather than a one-off claim.</p>

<p><strong>Limitation:</strong> Their fee model can be opaque. Some clients report unexpected charges for additional project narratives.</p>

<h3>6. Claim Capital</h3>

<p>Claim Capital is a newer entrant, founded in 2018. Their platform is more self-service oriented. You upload your project descriptions and cost data, and the software generates the claim. They offer a free assessment tool on their website.</p>

<p><strong>Best for:</strong> Small tech startups and digital agencies with straightforward R&D projects. Claims from £5,000 to £50,000.</p>

<p><strong>Limitation:</strong> The software does not handle complex cost allocations well. If you have part-time R&D staff or shared resources, you will need to do manual adjustments.</p>

<h2>What the Software Companies Do Not Tell You</h2>

<p>Every R&D tax claim software company markets itself as the solution to a complex problem. But there are several things they rarely mention upfront.</p>

<h3>HMRC Is Scrutinising Claims More Closely</h3>

<p>Since 2023, HMRC has increased its compliance checks on R&D tax credit claims. The number of enquiries has risen significantly. If your claim uses a software-generated technical narrative that is generic or formulaic, HMRC may reject it or ask for further information.</p>

<p>The <a href="/r-and-d-credits">R&D tax credits</a> page on our site explains the current compliance landscape in more detail. The short version is that a human-written technical narrative tailored to your specific project is far more likely to pass HMRC scrutiny than a template-driven one.</p>

<h3>The Cost Calculation Is Only as Good as Your Data</h3>

<p>Software can only calculate qualifying costs from the data you feed it. If you do not track staff time against specific R&D projects, the software will guess. Guessing leads to overclaims or underclaims. Both are problems.</p>

<p>An overclaim triggers an HMRC enquiry, interest, and penalties. An underclaim means you leave money on the table.</p>

<p>Proper time tracking and cost allocation are essential. No software fixes bad data.</p>

<h3>The Software Companies Are Not Your Accountant</h3>

<p>Most R&D tax claim software companies are not ICAEW qualified accountants. They are specialists in R&D tax credits, which is a narrow field. They will not review your overall tax position, check your corporation tax return for other reliefs, or advise on dividend planning.</p>

<p>If you use a software company to prepare your R&D claim, you still need a qualified accountant to file your CT600 corporation tax return and ensure everything ties together.</p>

<h2>When R&D Tax Claim Software Makes Sense</h2>

<p>For certain businesses, using an R&D tax claim software company is a sensible choice.</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>You have simple, one-project R&D.</strong> A single software development project with clear start and end dates, and staff who spend 100% of their time on it.</li>
<li><strong>You have good time records.</strong> You use Xero Projects, Toggl, Harvest, or similar to track staff hours against specific R&D activities.</li>
<li><strong>Your claim is under £25,000.</strong> The fee for a specialist advisor may eat too much of the benefit. A software platform with a lower fee structure makes more sense.</li>
<li><strong>You want to test the process.</strong> If you have never claimed R&D tax credits before, a software platform can help you understand what qualifies and what documentation you need.</li>
</ul>

<h2>When You Should Avoid R&D Tax Claim Software</h2>

<p>In other situations, a software-only approach is risky.</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Your R&D is complex or multi-project.</strong> If you have several projects running simultaneously, with staff splitting time across them, software struggles to allocate costs accurately.</li>
<li><strong>You have subcontractors or externally provided workers.</strong> The rules around qualifying subcontractor costs changed in 2024. Software may not apply the correct restrictions.</li>
<li><strong>Your claim is over £50,000.</strong> The risk of HMRC enquiry increases with claim size. A professionally prepared claim with a robust technical narrative is worth the investment.</li>
<li><strong>You are claiming under the R&D Intensive Scheme (ERIS).</strong> This scheme applies to loss-making companies where R&D spend exceeds 30% of total expenditure. The eligibility rules are specific, and software may not flag them correctly.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Cost Comparison: Software vs Accountant</h2>

<p>R&D tax claim software companies typically charge between 10% and 25% of the claim value. Some have a minimum fee of £500 to £1,000. Others work on a fixed fee basis starting around £1,500.</p>

<p>A qualified accountant preparing your R&D claim as part of your annual accounts and tax return may charge a fixed fee of £500 to £2,000 depending on complexity. The key difference is that the accountant's fee covers the full tax return, not just the R&D claim.</p>

<p>If your claim is £10,000 and the software company charges 20%, that is £2,000. An accountant charging £1,500 to prepare the claim and file the return is cheaper and more comprehensive.</p>

<p>For larger claims, the maths changes. A £100,000 claim at 15% software fee is £15,000. An accountant may charge £3,000 to £5,000 for the same work. The saving is significant.</p>

<h2>Making the Right Choice for Your Business</h2>

<p>The decision comes down to three factors: claim size, complexity, and your existing relationship with an accountant.</p>

<p>If you already work with an ICAEW qualified accountant who handles R&D tax credits, there is rarely a reason to use a separate software company. Your accountant knows your business, your financials, and your tax position. Adding a software company into the mix creates duplication and potential for error.</p>

<p>If you do not have an accountant, or your current accountant does not handle R&D claims, then a software company may be a practical starting point. Just be aware of the limitations and the need for a qualified accountant to file the final return.</p>

<p>For businesses with straightforward R&D and a good accountant, the best approach is often to let your accountant handle the claim. The software companies are selling a service that your accountant can already provide, usually at a lower effective cost and with better integration.</p>

<h2>Frequently Asked Questions</h2>

<h3>Can I use R&D tax claim software without an accountant?</h3>
<p>Yes, you can use software to prepare the technical narrative and cost calculation. But you still need a qualified accountant to file the CT600 corporation tax return that includes the claim. The software companies typically do not file your full return.</p>

<h3>How much do R&D tax claim software companies charge?</h3>
<p>Most charge between 10% and 25% of the claim value, with minimum fees from £500 to £1,500. Some offer fixed fees for smaller claims. Always ask for a full breakdown of fees before agreeing to use a platform.</p>

<h3>Will HMRC accept a software-generated R&D claim?</h3>
<p>HMRC will accept a software-generated claim if the technical narrative is specific to your project and the cost calculation is accurate. Generic or template-driven narratives are increasingly rejected. HMRC has stepped up compliance checks since 2023.</p>

<h3>What is the difference between R&D tax claim software and an R&D tax credit specialist?</h3>
<p>Software provides a platform to prepare your claim. A specialist provides a human advisor who reviews your projects, writes the narrative, and calculates costs. Some software companies also offer advisor-led services. The main difference is the level of human input and the cost.</p>
