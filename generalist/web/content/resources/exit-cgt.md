<h2 id="badr-overview">Business Asset Disposal Relief (BADR)</h2>
<p>Business Asset Disposal Relief (BADR, formerly Entrepreneurs' Relief) taxes qualifying business disposals at a reduced CGT rate instead of the standard rate. The relief applies to the first £1,000,000 of qualifying lifetime gains. Gains above the lifetime limit, or gains that do not meet the qualifying conditions, are taxed at the standard CGT rate.</p>

<h2 id="the-rate-change">The rate change at 6 April 2026</h2>
<p>This is the most time-critical planning point for anyone approaching a sale:</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Disposals before 6 April 2026:</strong> BADR rate = <strong>14%</strong></li>
  <li><strong>Disposals on or after 6 April 2026:</strong> BADR rate = <strong>18%</strong></li>
</ul>
<p>On a £500,000 gain that qualifies in full for BADR, the difference is £20,000 in additional tax. On larger gains the difference is proportionately greater. The completion date (not exchange) determines which rate applies.</p>

<h2 id="standard-rates">Standard CGT rates for business disposals</h2>
<p>Gains that do not qualify for BADR (or the portion above the £1m lifetime limit) are taxed at:</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>24%</strong> for higher and additional rate taxpayers (the rate applying to business asset gains since October 2024)</li>
  <li>The annual exempt amount is <strong>£3,000</strong> in 2026/27</li>
</ul>
<p>Residential property disposals use different rates (18% basic, 24% higher) but are not the subject of this guide.</p>

<h2 id="qualifying-conditions">Who qualifies for BADR</h2>
<p>To qualify for BADR on the sale of a business, the conditions must be met for at least two years before disposal:</p>
<ul>
  <li>The business must have been a trading company (or holding company of a trading group), not mainly an investment company.</li>
  <li>The individual must be a director or officer, or an employee, of the company.</li>
  <li>The individual must own at least 5% of the shares and 5% of the voting rights in the company.</li>
  <li>Those shares must give the individual entitlement to at least 5% of the distributable profits and 5% of the assets on a winding up.</li>
</ul>
<p>Share dilution below 5% from a funding round can disqualify the relief unless an election was made before the dilution. This is a specialist area that should be reviewed before issuing new shares.</p>

<h2 id="share-sale-vs-asset-sale">Share sale vs asset sale</h2>
<p>The structure of the transaction changes the tax position materially:</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Share sale:</strong> you sell the shares in the company. The gain is at the shareholder level; BADR can apply to the whole gain if conditions are met. Buyers typically prefer asset sales (they acquire a clean entity without hidden liabilities).</li>
  <li><strong>Asset sale:</strong> the company sells its assets (plant, goodwill, contracts). The gain sits inside the company and is subject to corporation tax. Extracting the proceeds then involves a further tax charge (dividend, or MVL). BADR does not apply at the corporate level.</li>
</ul>
<p>Sellers usually prefer a share sale; buyers usually prefer an asset sale. The price negotiation often reflects this preference. A specialist models both scenarios before you enter heads of terms.</p>

<h2 id="60-day-reporting">60-day reporting for residential property</h2>
<p>This guide covers business disposals. If your exit involves selling a property that is not your main residence, you must report the disposal and pay CGT within <strong>60 days</strong> of completion. Missing this deadline triggers automatic penalties.</p>

<h2 id="mbo-and-earnouts">MBOs and earn-outs</h2>
<p>Management buyouts and deals with earn-out provisions (where part of the price is paid over future periods linked to performance) have their own CGT and timing rules. An earn-out paid in cash is generally assessed on receipt; one paid in securities may be assessed on different terms. These need specialist input before heads of terms are finalised.</p>
