<h2 id="the-true-cost-of-hire">The true cost of a hire</h2>
<p>When you offer someone a £30,000 salary, you are not paying £30,000. Before they arrive at their desk you will also pay employer NIC, an auto-enrolment pension contribution, and potentially payroll administration costs. The model calculates the statutory floor; real all-in cost including software, equipment and training is typically 10 to 20% higher.</p>

<h2 id="employer-nic">Employer National Insurance in 2026/27</h2>
<p>Employer Class 1 NIC (secondary NIC) is charged at <strong>15%</strong> on earnings above the secondary threshold of <strong>£5,000 per year per employee</strong>. Both the rate and the threshold changed at the Autumn Budget 2024 (from 13.8% and £9,100) and are unchanged for 2026/27.</p>
<p>At a £30,000 salary: (£30,000 - £5,000) x 15% = <strong>£3,750</strong> employer NIC.</p>

<h2 id="employment-allowance">Employment Allowance</h2>
<p>The Employment Allowance offsets up to <strong>£10,500</strong> of employer NIC per year in 2026/27 (it rose from £5,000 in 2024/25; the previous £100,000 NI bill cap was removed from 6 April 2025).</p>
<p><strong>Critical restriction:</strong> a company where the director is the only person paid above the secondary threshold does NOT qualify. You need at least one genuine, non-director employee. The cost-of-hire model shows an "Employment Allowance not applied" warning when you have only one employee, regardless of the toggle setting, to match the actual legal position.</p>
<p>Once you have two qualifying employees, the allowance covers the first £10,500 of your combined employer NIC before you pay anything further to HMRC.</p>

<h2 id="auto-enrolment-pension">Auto-enrolment pension</h2>
<p>If an employee earns more than £10,000 per year and is aged 22 to state pension age, you must auto-enrol them and contribute at least 3% of qualifying earnings. Qualifying earnings for 2026/27 are the slice between £6,240 and £50,270.</p>
<p>At a £30,000 salary: qualifying earnings = £30,000 - £6,240 = £23,760. Employer pension at 3% = <strong>£712.80</strong>.</p>
<p>Most employers offer higher contributions as part of a competitive package; the model calculates the statutory minimum 3% floor only.</p>

<h2 id="payroll-administration">Payroll administration</h2>
<p>You must run payroll through HMRC's Real Time Information (RTI) system, submitting a Full Payment Submission (FPS) on or before each pay day. If you have no payroll in a period, an Employer Payment Summary (EPS) may be needed. Late submissions can trigger automatic penalties. Most businesses use payroll software (Xero, QuickBooks Payroll, Sage Payroll, or a bureau) rather than doing this manually.</p>

<h2 id="what-else-to-budget">What else to budget for</h2>
<p>The statutory floor understates total employment cost. Also budget for:</p>
<ul>
  <li>Software licences per seat (Slack, Google Workspace, your internal tools)</li>
  <li>Hardware and equipment</li>
  <li>Training and professional development</li>
  <li>Recruitment costs (recruiter fees typically 15 to 25% of first-year salary)</li>
  <li>Any enhanced benefits: private health, life cover, higher pension match</li>
  <li>Statutory sick pay (SSP) exposure in periods of absence</li>
  <li>Payroll bureau or accountant cost if you outsource the processing</li>
</ul>

<h2 id="directors-payroll">Directors on payroll</h2>
<p>A director is treated as an employee for PAYE purposes. Even a nominal salary of £5,000 or £12,570 needs to go through RTI. A common mistake is to pay the salary and file the returns inconsistently; HMRC cross-checks both and penalties apply to late or missing submissions.</p>
