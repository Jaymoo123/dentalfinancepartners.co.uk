<p>A hospitality consultant fixes operations on a project basis: menu engineering, covers, brand, site selection and turnaround. A hospitality accountant runs the recurring financial spine: VAT and the Flat Rate Scheme, tips and tronc, payroll, management accounts, tax and cashflow forecasting. For anything money-shaped, start with the accountant. Bring a consultant when the problem is specifically operational and the numbers are already being tracked accurately.</p>

<h2>What a hospitality consultant actually does</h2>

<p>A hospitality consultant is typically engaged to solve a specific operational problem and then leave. The engagement is project-based rather than ongoing, and the work sits firmly in operations rather than finance.</p>

<p>Common consultant briefs include:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Menu engineering:</strong> reviewing dish mix, food cost per dish, pricing psychology and the balance between high-margin and high-volume items. A consultant may recommend dropping dishes, repricing, or restructuring the menu to shift the average spend.</li>
  <li><strong>Covers and throughput:</strong> analysing table turn, reservation patterns, front-of-house flow and kitchen capacity to identify where covers are being lost.</li>
  <li><strong>Kitchen efficiency:</strong> prep schedules, waste tracking, portion control and supplier consolidation to reduce food cost at source.</li>
  <li><strong>Brand and concept:</strong> positioning the offer relative to the local market, refreshing the brand, or advising on whether the concept is right for the site.</li>
  <li><strong>Site selection and new openings:</strong> demographic analysis, lease review from an operational perspective, and concept feasibility for a new location.</li>
  <li><strong>Turnaround:</strong> when a site is underperforming and needs rapid operational intervention to stabilise trading before any longer-term decisions are made.</li>
</ul>

<p>A consultant's value is specific and time-limited. They are not the right resource for the recurring financial and compliance work that every hospitality business carries every month.</p>

<h2>What a hospitality accountant actually does</h2>

<p>The accountant runs the financial spine of the business on an ongoing basis. For a hospitality operator, this is more complex than for most trades: the combination of mixed VAT rates, tips legislation, high staff turnover, zero-hours payroll and cash-handling creates a compliance and management-information workload that generalist accountancy is not equipped to handle well.</p>

<p>Core accountancy work for hospitality includes:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>VAT and the Flat Rate Scheme:</strong> hospitality operators have some of the most complex VAT positions in UK business, from the hot/cold and eat-in/takeaway distinctions on food VAT to the sector-specific FRS rates. The FRS rate for a pub (6.5%) is materially different from the rate for a restaurant or takeaway (12.5%), and the tradescode, not the operator's own description, determines which applies. Getting this wrong is expensive. See <a href="/services/hospitality-vat">hospitality VAT</a> for the detail.</li>
  <li><strong>Tips and tronc:</strong> since 1 October 2024, the <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2023/13/contents">Employment (Allocation of Tips) Act 2023</a> requires 100% of qualifying tips to reach workers without deduction, with a written policy and allocation records. The tax treatment differs depending on whether tips are paid direct to staff, through the employer's payroll, or via an independent tronc. A correctly structured tronc, run by a genuine troncmaster with no employer involvement in allocation, carries no NIC liability for either the employer or the worker; income tax via PAYE still applies. Any employer involvement in allocation destroys the NIC exemption. This is technical PAYE and NIC work. See <a href="/services/tronc-scheme-setup">tronc scheme setup</a> and <a href="/calculators/tronc-tips-paye-nic-calculator">the tronc and tips NIC calculator</a>.</li>
  <li><strong>Payroll and employer NIC:</strong> hospitality payroll carries high complexity: zero-hours and casual staff, multiple age-bands under the National Minimum Wage, high turnover, and the employer NIC rate of 15% above the secondary threshold of <a href="https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/rates-and-allowances-national-insurance-contributions/rates-and-allowances-national-insurance-contributions">£5,000 per year</a> from April 2025. The <a href="https://www.gov.uk/claim-employment-allowance">Employment Allowance of £10,500</a> offsets this for eligible businesses. The <a href="https://www.gov.uk/national-minimum-wage-rates">National Living Wage is £12.71 per hour for workers aged 21 and over</a> from 6 April 2026. Tips of any kind cannot count toward NMW or NLW. See <a href="/services/hospitality-payroll">hospitality payroll</a>.</li>
  <li><strong>Management accounts:</strong> a monthly pack covering revenue, GP%, labour percentage, overheads and net profit by site or trade type. This is the primary tool for understanding whether the business is moving in the right direction between year-end filings.</li>
  <li><strong>Cashflow forecasting:</strong> seasonality, VAT payment cycles and payroll timing create predictable cashflow pinch-points in hospitality. A forecast built on actual trading data (from the EPOS and payroll system) gives operators a working line of sight on their position.</li>
  <li><strong>Tax compliance and planning:</strong> corporation tax (19% on profits up to £50,000; 25% above £250,000), income tax for sole-trader operators, capital allowances on kitchen refits and equipment, and MTD for Income Tax for sole traders above the relevant threshold.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Where the two overlap and who leads</h2>

<p>The clearest overlap is in margin analysis: GP% and labour percentage sit at the intersection of financial measurement and operational performance.</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Measuring GP% and labour %:</strong> this is accountancy work. Accurate figures require correct sales data (usually from EPOS), correctly allocated costs (food purchases, supplier invoices, waste adjustments) and a consistent methodology. Without the financial infrastructure, GP% figures are guesses.</li>
  <li><strong>Acting on GP% and labour %:</strong> once the numbers are accurate, the question of whether to change a recipe, drop a dish, renegotiate a supplier or restructure a rota is operational. A consultant may be well placed to drive that work, particularly in a kitchen that has drifted over time.</li>
  <li><strong>Break-even analysis:</strong> the accountant produces the break-even model (fixed costs, variable costs, contribution per cover). A consultant may use that output to restructure the trading model. The model itself is accountancy; the operational response to it may not be.</li>
</ul>

<p>Neither discipline genuinely substitutes for the other in the overlap zone. Accountancy produces the measurement; operational expertise makes the intervention. The best outcomes come from both working from the same numbers. See <a href="/blog/hospitality-accounts/gross-profit-menu-pricing">gross profit and menu pricing</a> for the financial mechanics.</p>

<h2>When you genuinely need both</h2>

<p>Several situations reliably require both disciplines working together:</p>

<p><strong>Turnaround with a financial and operational angle.</strong> A site trading at a loss needs the accountant to establish the true financial position (debt, deferred VAT, payroll arrears, the actual run rate of losses) before any operational intervention makes sense. An operational turnaround that ignores a hidden VAT liability or a payroll error will fail. The consultant stabilises operations; the accountant ensures the financial structure can support recovery.</p>

<p><strong>Opening a second or third site.</strong> The financial work here is substantial: whether to trade as a single company or separate entities, whether a VAT group registration is appropriate, how the enlarged payroll structure works across sites, and how management accounts will be split to show per-site performance. None of this is operational; all of it needs to be resolved before the second site opens. The consultant's work on site selection, concept and operational model runs in parallel, not after.</p>

<p><strong>A major refit or rebrand.</strong> Capital allowances on kitchen equipment and fit-out can be material. The <a href="https://www.gov.uk/capital-allowances/annual-investment-allowance">Annual Investment Allowance covers up to £1,000,000</a> of qualifying plant and machinery. From 2026-27, the main pool Writing Down Allowance is 14% (reduced from 18% by Finance Act 2026), with a 40% First Year Allowance available for qualifying main-pool additions. Getting the allowance claim right is the accountant's job; the refit specification and contractor management is operational. See <a href="/services/business-rates-relief">business rates relief</a> for the rates position on the newly refitted premises.</p>

<p><strong>A structured exit or sale.</strong> Valuation methodology, tax position on disposal, any earn-out structure and the buyer's due diligence pack are all financial and legal work. The operational presentation of the business (trading history, staff structure, supplier relationships) feeds into the financial case but is assembled differently.</p>

<h2>What good financial control looks like month to month</h2>

<p>Whatever operational shape the business is in, the financial control function needs to be running reliably. For a hospitality operator, that means:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Management accounts by the 15th of the following month</strong>, covering revenue by type (food, wet, accommodation if applicable), gross profit by category, total labour cost including employer NIC, and net profit after overheads. Without this, decisions are being made on feel rather than data.</li>
  <li><strong>GP% and labour % tracked each period</strong> against a plan. These two ratios are the operational levers the business can actually move. Rent and rates are fixed; GP% and labour % are not. The <a href="/calculators/staff-cost-rota-margin-calculator">staff cost and rota margin calculator</a> helps model labour scenarios before committing to a rota.</li>
  <li><strong>VAT return reviewed each quarter</strong>, not filed by routine. Mixed VAT supplies (standard-rated eat-in, zero-rated takeaway, standard-rated drinks) require correct sales splitting from the EPOS system. An error runs for the life of the scheme without review.</li>
  <li><strong>Payroll reconciled each pay run</strong>, not at year end. Employer NIC at 15% above the <a href="https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/rates-and-allowances-national-insurance-contributions/rates-and-allowances-national-insurance-contributions">£5,000 secondary threshold</a> is a meaningful cost at hospitality staffing levels. The <a href="https://www.gov.uk/claim-employment-allowance">£10,500 Employment Allowance</a> should be claimed if eligible.</li>
  <li><strong>Tips and tronc allocation documented</strong> every pay period, with a written policy in place meeting the requirements of the <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2023/13/contents">Employment (Allocation of Tips) Act 2023</a>. Records must be kept and made available to workers on request.</li>
  <li><strong>MTD for Income Tax</strong> applies to sole-trader operators with self-employment and property income above <a href="https://www.gov.uk/guidance/check-if-youre-eligible-for-making-tax-digital-for-income-tax">£50,000 from 6 April 2026</a>, with the threshold dropping to £30,000 from April 2027 and £20,000 from April 2028. Quarterly digital updates require MTD-compatible software and accurate in-year records.</li>
</ul>

<p>A business with clean, timely management accounts is in a far stronger position when engaging any external adviser, whether an accountant providing an ongoing service or a consultant brought in to fix a specific problem. The data makes both more effective.</p>
