<p>Many cafes, coffee shops and small takeaways are paying business rates they do not legally owe. Small Business Rate Relief (SBRR) can eliminate the bill entirely for qualifying premises, yet large numbers of eligible operators never apply for it. This guide explains the thresholds, the taper, the one-property condition, and what to do next.</p>

<p>These figures apply in <strong>England only</strong>. Scotland and Wales operate separate rates and relief regimes with different thresholds. If your business is in Scotland or Wales, contact your local council or the relevant devolved agency for the applicable rules.</p>

<h2>The short answer: 100% relief up to £12,000 rateable value, tapering to zero at £15,000, and you must claim it</h2>

<p><strong>Small Business Rate Relief gives 100% relief on your business rates bill if your rateable value is £12,000 or less and the property is the only business premises your operation uses. The relief tapers between £12,001 and £15,000 before falling to zero. It is not applied automatically: you must claim it from your local council.</strong> Many cafes and small operators qualify and receive nothing because they have not submitted a claim.</p>

<p>Source: <a href="https://www.gov.uk/apply-for-business-rate-relief/small-business-rate-relief">gov.uk/apply-for-business-rate-relief/small-business-rate-relief</a>.</p>

<h2>What Small Business Rate Relief is and who it is for</h2>

<p>Business rates are a tax on non-domestic property. Like council tax on a home, your rateable value (an estimate of the annual rent the property could command on the open market) determines the starting point for your bill, which is then multiplied by a nationally set multiplier and adjusted for any reliefs you are entitled to.</p>

<p>SBRR is a relief scheme specifically for small businesses occupying lower-value premises. For a cafe, coffee shop or takeaway with a modest footprint and a rateable value in the qualifying range, it can mean paying nothing at all. It is available in England and administered by local councils. The scheme has no fixed end date and has not been withdrawn.</p>

<p>Typical qualifying premises include small high-street or market-town cafes, independent coffee shops, single-site takeaways, and small snack bars. If your premises have a modest floor area and are in a secondary location rather than a prime city-centre pitch, your rateable value is more likely to fall within the relief band.</p>

<h2>How rateable value decides your relief</h2>

<p>The table below shows the relief available at key rateable value points in England, based on the <a href="https://www.gov.uk/apply-for-business-rate-relief/small-business-rate-relief">government's published guidance</a>:</p>

<table>
  <thead>
    <tr>
      <th>Rateable value (England)</th>
      <th>SBRR available</th>
    </tr>
  </thead>
  <tbody>
    <tr>
      <td>Up to £12,000</td>
      <td>100% (full relief, no rates bill)</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>£13,500</td>
      <td>50% (example point from gov.uk)</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>£14,000</td>
      <td>33% (example point from gov.uk)</td>

    </tr>
  </tbody>
</table>

<p>The taper between £12,001 and £15,000 is set by your council using the statutory formula; the percentages at £13,500 and £14,000 are government-published example points and the intermediate values follow the same progressive reduction. Above £15,000 the relief disappears entirely, which creates a cliff worth understanding if your rateable value is in or near that range.</p>

<h2>The one-property condition</h2>

<p>SBRR normally applies only where the property is the <strong>only</strong> business property your business uses. If you have a second premises (a separate kitchen unit, a storage site, a second cafe location), the relief for the main property may be affected or lost.</p>

<p>There is a limited exception. Where a business has multiple properties and each additional property has a rateable value below £2,900, and the combined rateable value across all properties is below £20,000, it may still be possible to claim SBRR on the main property. The detail of this exception should be confirmed with your council before you assume you qualify. Do not claim SBRR on the assumption that multiple properties are fine without checking your specific situation.</p>

<p>For the majority of single-site independent cafes, coffee shops and takeaways, the one-property condition is straightforward to meet.</p>

<h2>How to find your rateable value</h2>

<p>Your rateable value is set by the Valuation Office Agency (VOA), not your local council. You can look it up using the <a href="https://www.gov.uk/find-business-rates">Find a business rates valuation service</a> on GOV.UK. Search by address; the result will show your current rateable value and the date it was last assessed.</p>

<p>If you believe your rateable value is wrong (for example because the VOA has used inaccurate floor area information, or because comparable premises nearby have lower values), you can challenge it through the Check, Challenge, Appeal (CCA) process on GOV.UK. A lower rateable value means a lower rates bill and a better chance of qualifying for full SBRR.</p>

<p>It is worth doing this check before assuming you do not qualify. Rateable values are set by the VOA based on their survey data; errors are not uncommon in smaller commercial premises.</p>

<h2>How to claim Small Business Rate Relief</h2>

<p>SBRR is <strong>not applied automatically</strong>. You must apply to your local council. The process varies slightly between councils but generally involves:</p>

<ol>
  <li>Finding your rateable value using the <a href="https://www.gov.uk/find-business-rates">gov.uk valuation service</a>.</li>
  <li>Confirming you meet the one-property condition (or the limited multiple-property exception).</li>
  <li>Contacting your local council's business rates team. Most councils have an online form; some require a written application.</li>
  <li>Providing your property address and confirming the number of properties your business occupies.</li>
</ol>

<p>Once granted, the relief is applied to your rates account. If your circumstances change (for example you take on a second property), you must inform the council promptly; continuing to claim relief you are no longer entitled to can result in a back-dated rates bill.</p>

<p>If you have been eligible for SBRR in previous years and have not claimed, it may be possible to back-date the claim. Contact your council to ask what periods are available for a retrospective claim.</p>

<h2>SBRR vs Retail, Hospitality and Leisure relief: two completely different things</h2>

<p>There is significant confusion between SBRR and the former Retail, Hospitality and Leisure (RHL) relief. They are not the same scheme and must not be conflated.</p>

  <thead>
    <tr>
      <th></th>

      <td>Revised rate multipliers for RHL properties</td>
    </tr>

      <td>England only?</td>
      <td>Yes (Scotland/Wales differ)</td>
      <td>Yes (England)</td>
    </tr>
  </tbody>

<p>RHL relief was a temporary annual discount applied to hospitality, retail and leisure properties in England. It ran at varying percentages and cash caps in different years and was subject to annual change by central government. From 1 April 2026, <a href="https://www.gov.uk/business-rates-relief/retail-discount">new claims for RHL relief are no longer accepted</a>. Instead, from that date, qualifying hospitality, retail and leisure properties in England benefit from lower rate multipliers rather than a discretionary discount. The multiplier mechanism is a permanent structural change, not a relief that needs to be claimed annually.</p>

<p>SBRR is unaffected by this change. If your rateable value is within the SBRR band, you still claim SBRR from your council in the normal way. The end of RHL relief does not affect your entitlement to SBRR.</p>

<p>If you were previously benefiting from RHL relief and your rateable value is above £15,000, the lower 2026-27 multipliers for RHL properties in England now apply to your bill instead. For the detail on multipliers and how they work for your type of premises, see our <a href="/services/business-rates-relief">business rates review service</a> page.</p>

<h2>What happens if your rateable value changes</h2>

<p>The Valuation Office Agency carries out periodic revaluations of all rateable values in England. Revaluations bring rateable values into line with current rental market evidence. The most recent revaluation took effect from 1 April 2023.</p>

<p>If a revaluation increases your rateable value beyond a threshold:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Crossing from below £12,000 to above £12,000 means your relief drops from 100% to a tapered amount.</li>
  <li>Crossing from below £15,000 to above £15,000 means your relief disappears entirely.</li>
  <li>Moving in the other direction (a reduction in rateable value after a successful challenge) can restore relief you were not previously entitled to.</li>
</ul>

<p>The cliff at £15,000 is worth planning around if your rateable value is close to that level. A small increase in rateable value at the margin can produce a significant step up in your rates bill. Monitoring the VOA's valuations for your premises and challenging errors promptly is a practical way to manage this risk.</p>

<p>If you have had a revaluation recently, check whether your rateable value now falls within the SBRR band and, if so, apply immediately. Relief is not backdated automatically; you need to trigger the claim.</p>

<h2>Getting your rates reviewed</h2>

<p>Many small cafes, coffee shops and takeaways are paying rates bills that could be reduced or eliminated. The reasons vary: some have never applied for SBRR; some had a rateable value that has since been revised downward; some have taken on a property without checking whether a previous occupier's claim lapsed.</p>

<p>A structured review of your rates position typically involves: confirming your current rateable value against the VOA register; assessing whether SBRR applies and has been claimed; checking whether your rateable value can be challenged; and reviewing whether any transitional arrangements affect your bill.</p>
