<p>Most restaurants need their books done weekly, not once a year at tax time. A bookkeeper reconciles daily EPOS takings, enters purchase invoices, tracks stock movements and gross profit, processes tips and tronc, and keeps VAT-ready records throughout the year. The accountant then takes those records, reviews the position, advises and files. You can run that bookkeeping function in-house or outsource it to a specialist. This page explains both the role and the decision.</p>

<h2>What a restaurant bookkeeper actually does</h2>

<p>The restaurant bookkeeper's job is to keep the business's financial records accurate and current. In a hospitality setting that is more involved than in most trades, because a restaurant generates a high volume of small transactions across multiple payment types, has mixed VAT rates on its sales, carries live stock that changes daily and processes tips that must be handled separately from turnover.</p>

<p>The core scope covers:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Daily EPOS takings reconciliation</strong>: total takings from the till or EPOS system are reconciled to actual card settlements and cash counted, split by payment type (cash, card, delivery apps) and, critically, by VAT rate. A restaurant selling eat-in meals (standard-rated) alongside cold takeaway items (potentially zero-rated) cannot lump all revenue together. The daily reconciliation is the point at which this split is captured.</li>
  <li><strong>Purchase invoice entry</strong>: supplier invoices are scanned or entered into the accounting system, coded to the correct nominal account and VAT treatment, and matched against the purchase order or delivery note. A food supplier invoice is mostly zero-rated input VAT on food ingredients, but may carry standard-rated VAT on cleaning products or packaging on the same invoice.</li>
  <li><strong>Supplier statement matching</strong>: the bookkeeper reconciles supplier statements against what is in the purchase ledger, chases discrepancies and ensures the business only pays what it owes.</li>
  <li><strong>Stock recording and wastage</strong>: deliveries received are recorded against expected costs. Wastage, spoilage and voids are logged so that the gross profit calculation reflects actual consumption, not theoretical cost. Without this, the GP% figure is meaningless.</li>
  <li><strong>Tips and service charge processing</strong>: tips must be separated from turnover (they are not the business's income) and processed correctly depending on the method used. See the tips and tronc section below.</li>
  <li><strong>Bank reconciliation</strong>: the bank statement is reconciled to the accounting system regularly, so that cash flow is accurate and unreconciled items are caught quickly rather than at year-end.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Bookkeeper vs accountant: the honest split</h2>

<p>These are two different jobs and, in most restaurants, two different relationships. Conflating them is the source of most bookkeeping neglect.</p>

<p>A bookkeeper records what happened: daily sales, supplier costs, stock movements, bank transactions, tips. They reconcile figures, chase discrepancies and maintain accurate ledgers. The output is a set of records that reflects the business's financial position accurately and in real time.</p>

<p>An accountant takes those records and does the higher-order work: reviewing the position, advising on VAT scheme, tax structure and planning, preparing statutory accounts, filing corporation tax returns and handling HMRC correspondence. An accountant operating from poor records spends most of their billable time reconstructing the basics. An accountant operating from clean books can focus on advice.</p>

<p>The line in practice: everything up to a balanced set of books is bookkeeping. Everything that requires professional judgement, regulated advice or a signed-off return is accounting.</p>

<h2>The daily and weekly bookkeeping cycle</h2>

<p>Restaurant bookkeeping runs on a short cycle. Transactions are too high-volume and too time-sensitive to batch for the month-end.</p>

<p><strong>Daily (or first thing next morning):</strong></p>
<ul>
  <li>Reconcile EPOS takings to card terminal settlement and cash in drawer</li>
  <li>Log delivery-app sales net of commission (the commission is your cost, not a reduction in turnover)</li>
  <li>Record cash-up and any voids or refunds</li>
  <li>Flag any discrepancy for investigation before it compounds</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Weekly:</strong></p>
<ul>
  <li>Enter purchase invoices received in the week</li>
  <li>Match deliveries to invoices and flag short deliveries or pricing errors</li>
  <li>Record stock movements and wastage</li>
  <li>Process tips and service charge (see below)</li>
  <li>Run a quick bank-to-ledger check</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Monthly:</strong></p>
<ul>
  <li>Full bank reconciliation</li>
  <li>Supplier statement matching and payment run review</li>
  <li>GP% calculation and review against assumptions</li>
  <li>VAT position check ahead of the return</li>
  <li>Draft management accounts</li>
</ul>

<h2>What a generalist bookkeeper misses in a restaurant</h2>

<p>A bookkeeper without hospitality experience can produce balanced books that are still wrong in ways that matter.</p>

<p><strong>VAT rate split on mixed takings.</strong> A restaurant can have standard-rated eat-in sales, zero-rated cold takeaway sales and standard-rated hot food sales in the same day. The VAT position on each sale is determined by the supply type, not the product alone. A bookkeeper who codes all food sales to a single VAT bucket will produce an incorrect VAT return. The <a href="/calculators/food-drink-vat-rate-checker">food and drink VAT rate checker</a> handles product-level queries; <a href="/services/hospitality-vat">hospitality VAT</a> covers the scheme and return work.</p>

<p><strong>Tips vs turnover.</strong> Tips are not the business's income. Money collected by the employer and paid out to staff is not turnover and must not be recorded as such. The method of distribution (employer-controlled payroll, independent tronc or direct tips) determines the tax and NIC treatment. Recording tips as revenue inflates turnover, VAT output tax and potentially triggers compulsory <a href="https://www.gov.uk/vat-registration/when-to-register">VAT registration</a> at £90,000 of taxable turnover before the business has actually hit that threshold.</p>

<p><strong>Delivery-app netting.</strong> Platforms pay out the customer price less their commission. A bookkeeper who records only the net payout is understating turnover, which affects VAT, the registration threshold and management information. The correct treatment is to record the gross sale and expense the commission separately. No percentage is stated here as commission structures vary by platform and contract.</p>

<p><strong>GP% tracking as a control.</strong> A generalist bookkeeper may not flag when the GP% figure is drifting because they are not benchmarking against assumptions. A hospitality bookkeeper builds the GP% check into the monthly routine and surfaces it as a management information figure, not just a number that falls out of the accounts.</p>

<h2>VAT-ready records and Making Tax Digital</h2>

<p>HMRC requires businesses to keep records that support every figure on a VAT return. For a restaurant this means: daily takings records split by VAT rate, supplier invoices with the VAT amount identified, and records distinguishing eat-in from takeaway sales where the same product crosses the line.</p>

<p>From <a href="https://www.gov.uk/guidance/sign-up-your-client-for-making-tax-digital-for-income-tax">6 April 2026, MTD for Income Tax</a> applies to sole-trader operators with income over £50,000, with further thresholds at £30,000 from April 2027 and £20,000 from April 2028. These operators must keep digital records and submit quarterly updates to HMRC using compatible software. <a href="https://www.gov.uk/simpler-income-tax-cash-basis">Cash basis is the default</a> for unincorporated businesses from April 2024, though stock-heavy restaurants often benefit from electing accruals instead. See the <a href="/blog/hospitality-accounts/cash-basis-vs-accruals-stock">cash basis vs accruals post</a> for that trade-off.</p>

<p>The practical consequence: clean books kept weekly are easier to make MTD-compliant than a year-end reconstruction. The bookkeeper's discipline is what creates the digital audit trail.</p>

<h2>Tips and tronc: why it sits with bookkeeping and payroll</h2>

<p>Tips processing is a bookkeeping task that sits at the boundary with payroll, and getting it wrong creates both VAT and NIC exposure.</p>

<p>The key rules under <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2023/13/contents">the Employment (Allocation of Tips) Act 2023</a>, in force since 1 October 2024, require employers to pass 100% of qualifying tips to workers, maintain a written tips policy and keep allocation records. Tips cannot count toward the <a href="https://www.gov.uk/national-minimum-wage-rates">National Living Wage</a> (£12.71 per hour for workers aged 21 and over from April 2026) under any circumstances.</p>

<p>For NIC treatment, the method matters:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Employer-controlled distribution</strong>: tips collected by the employer and paid out are employment income, processed through payroll, and subject to employer NIC at <a href="https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/rates-and-allowances-national-insurance-contributions/rates-and-allowances-national-insurance-contributions">15% above the £5,000 secondary threshold</a>.</li>
  <li><strong>Independent tronc</strong>: where a genuinely independent troncmaster controls allocation without any employer involvement, there is <a href="https://www.gov.uk/hmrc-internal-manuals/national-insurance-manual/nim02922">no employer or employee NIC liability</a>. PAYE income tax still applies. The NIC exemption is not automatic: any employer involvement in who receives what destroys it.</li>
  <li><strong>Direct tips kept by the worker</strong>: cash tips taken directly by a member of staff and not pooled are the worker's own income to declare. <a href="https://www.gov.uk/tips-at-work">GOV.UK tips guidance</a> confirms the employer's obligations on record-keeping even for direct tips under the 2023 Act.</li>
</ul>

<p>The bookkeeper records which tips were collected, through which route and in what amounts. The payroll function processes them accordingly. These two tasks need to be joined up. The <a href="/services/tronc-scheme-setup">tronc scheme setup service</a> and <a href="/services/hospitality-payroll">hospitality payroll service</a> cover the compliance side.</p>

<h2>In-house bookkeeper vs outsourced</h2>

<p>Both models work. The decision turns on transaction volume, whether you need someone physically present, and the employment cost vs the outsourced fee.</p>

<p><strong>In-house</strong>: the bookkeeper is on site, can query invoices with the purchasing team, spot discrepancies at source and handle the daily cash-up physically. The cost includes salary, holiday cover, employer NIC at 15% above £5,000 and any software or training. For a high-volume operation with complex stock management this is often the right model.</p>

<p><strong>Outsourced</strong>: a specialist bookkeeper works from a cloud accounting system, typically on a fixed monthly arrangement. Invoices are submitted digitally. The bookkeeper reconciles remotely and flags issues by exception. No employment overhead, but also no one on the floor. Works well for operators whose EPOS system exports clean data and whose purchase process is already digitised.</p>

<p>Hybrid arrangements are common: an in-house administrator handles scanning and daily cash-up; an outsourced specialist does the accounting system work and produces management information.</p>

<p>No fee or salary figures are stated here; both vary by location, transaction volume and scope.</p>

<h2>Restaurant bookkeeping checklist</h2>

<p>Use this as a minimum scope guide, not an exhaustive task list.</p>

<p><strong>Weekly tasks:</strong></p>
<ol>
  <li>Reconcile daily EPOS takings to card settlements and cash (split by VAT rate where the sales mix requires it)</li>
  <li>Enter and code purchase invoices received</li>
  <li>Match purchase invoices to delivery notes and flag discrepancies</li>
  <li>Record stock received, wastage and voids</li>
  <li>Process tips and service charge (record route and amount; confirm payroll coding where applicable)</li>
  <li>Reconcile bank to accounting system (at least a running check; full rec monthly)</li>
</ol>

<p><strong>Monthly tasks:</strong></p>
<ol>
  <li>Full bank reconciliation</li>
  <li>Supplier statement matching</li>
  <li>Payment run review (approve what is due, flag what is disputed)</li>
  <li>GP% calculation (food and, where relevant, wet sales separately)</li>
  <li>VAT position check against expected liability</li>
  <li>Draft management accounts for the operator</li>
</ol>
