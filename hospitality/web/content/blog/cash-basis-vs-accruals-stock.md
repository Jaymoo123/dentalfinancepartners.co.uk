<h2>The short answer: cash basis is now the default, but stock-heavy operators usually elect accruals</h2>

<p>From <strong>6 April 2024</strong>, <a href="https://www.gov.uk/simpler-income-tax-cash-basis">cash basis became the standard method</a> for unincorporated businesses: sole traders and partnerships without corporate partners. You do not need to opt in. If you have done nothing, you are already on cash basis.</p>

<p>For most hospitality operators who carry food and drink stock, this default is the wrong fit. Cash basis can distort your reported profit when stock sits on a shelf at period end. Accruals accounting is available by election and typically produces a more accurate picture of how the business is actually performing. This is an active decision worth taking before you file your first return under the new default.</p>

<h2>What each method actually means</h2>

<p><strong>Cash basis</strong> records income when money comes in and expenses when money goes out. There is no adjustment for what you are owed, what you owe others, or goods you have paid for but not yet sold. It is the closer of the two methods to a bank statement.</p>

<p><strong>Accruals accounting</strong> (sometimes called traditional accounting) matches income and costs to the period they relate to, regardless of when cash changes hands. If you sold meals in March but a corporate client pays in April, the income belongs to March. If you bought a delivery of beer in March and half of it is still in the cellar at 31 March, only the half you sold is an expense for March.</p>

<p>The difference matters most when stock, debtors or creditors are in play at the end of a period.</p>

<h2>Who this applies to: sole traders and partnerships only</h2>

<p>The cash basis regime applies only to sole traders and partnerships without corporate partners. <strong>Limited companies cannot use cash basis</strong> and are required to prepare accounts on an accruals basis.</p>

<p>If your restaurant, pub, cafe or takeaway trades as a limited company, this choice does not apply to you. If you trade as a sole trader or in a partnership of individuals, it does.</p>

<p>The relevant operator types in hospitality who are typically unincorporated include sole-trader <a href="/for/cafes-and-coffee-shops">cafe and coffee shop</a> owners, sole-trader <a href="/for/takeaways">takeaway operators</a> and smaller pub and restaurant partnerships.</p>

<h2>Why stock is the deciding factor</h2>

<p>Under cash basis, when you pay a supplier for food or drink, the full invoice amount is recorded as an expense immediately. There is no mechanism to carry forward the cost of unsold goods to the period in which they are actually sold.</p>

<p>This creates a timing distortion: if you buy a large stock of goods near your accounting period end and have not sold them by the last day of the year, your accounts show a higher cost (and therefore lower profit) than your business actually generated in that period. In the next period, when you sell those goods, the income appears but there is no matching cost, so profit looks artificially high.</p>

<p>For a hospitality business that holds meaningful stock at year end (a pub cellar, a restaurant larder, a cafe's dry goods), this swing can be significant. Accruals fixes it by requiring you to value unsold stock at period end and carry it forward as an asset.</p>

<p>Under accruals, your cost of sales for the period is: <strong>opening stock + purchases during the period - closing stock</strong>. Only the goods you actually sold are charged against this period's income. The rest sit on the balance sheet until the next period.</p>

<p>Neither method is wrong in isolation, but accruals gives a more stable and accurate view of underlying trading performance.</p>

<h2>What accruals adds beyond stock</h2>

<p>Accruals does not only address stock. It also recognises:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Debtors:</strong> income earned but not yet received (for example an invoice to a corporate client for a private dining event, sent on 28 March but unpaid at year end).</li>
  <li><strong>Creditors:</strong> costs incurred but not yet paid (for example a gas bill covering February and March received after year end).</li>
  <li><strong>Prepayments:</strong> costs paid in advance that relate to the next period (for example insurance or rent paid quarterly in advance).</li>
  <li><strong>Accrued costs:</strong> costs known to relate to the period but where an invoice has not yet been received.</li>
</ul>

<p>For a business that runs mostly on card receipts and pays suppliers promptly, the difference between the two methods on debtors and creditors may be small. Stock, for most hospitality operators, is the biggest variable.</p>

<h2>How to elect accruals</h2>

<p>Accruals is not the default for unincorporated businesses from April 2024, so you have to actively choose it. The election is made on your <a href="https://www.gov.uk/self-assessment-tax-returns">Self Assessment tax return</a> for the relevant tax year. There is no separate form to file and no HMRC approval needed.</p>

<p>If you have been on accruals under the old default (before 6 April 2024) and want to stay on accruals, you still need to make the election on your return, because the system default has changed. Doing nothing means moving to cash basis.</p>

<p>Switching from cash basis to accruals (or vice versa) in a later year requires transitional adjustments to make sure income and costs are not counted twice or missed entirely. These adjustments cover opening stock, debtors and creditors at the point of change. An accountant should handle this; getting the transition year wrong creates errors that can take two years to unwind.</p>

<h2>Cash basis and MTD for Income Tax</h2>

<p>Your accounting method and your Making Tax Digital obligations are separate. You can use cash basis while complying with MTD for Income Tax.</p>

<p><a href="https://www.gov.uk/guidance/check-if-youre-eligible-for-making-tax-digital-for-income-tax">MTD for Income Tax</a> applies to sole-trader operators with total self-employment and property income over £50,000 from <strong>6 April 2026</strong>, dropping to £30,000 from April 2027 and £20,000 from April 2028. MTD requires digital record-keeping and quarterly updates to HMRC, but it does not dictate whether those records are on cash basis or accruals.</p>

<h2>Which is right for your business</h2>

<p>Cash basis suits operators who:</p>

<ul>
  <li>hold minimal stock (a coffee cart, a small kiosk, a market trader with no significant closing inventory)</li>
  <li>have no meaningful debtors or creditors at period end</li>
  <li>prioritise simplicity over precision</li>
</ul>

<p>Accruals suits operators who:</p>

<ul>
  <li>carry a cellar, larder or dry-goods store that varies meaningfully at period end</li>
  <li>invoice corporate clients or cater events in arrears</li>
  <li>want accounts that reflect the true trading position of each period</li>
  <li>are planning to borrow, sell or bring in investors (lenders and buyers expect accruals accounts)</li>
</ul>

<p>The default cash basis is not a trap, but it is not a decision either. For most operators with food and drink stock, electing accruals produces a more accurate profit figure, a cleaner picture of gross margin, and a basis for the kind of <a href="/blog/hospitality-accounts/gross-profit-menu-pricing">gross profit and menu pricing analysis</a> that improves margins over time.</p>
